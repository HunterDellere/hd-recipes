# Latin American Cuisines — Reference Note

"Latin American cuisine" covers a continent and change — Mexico, seven Central American countries, the Caribbean islands, and a dozen South American nations. They share a Columbian-Exchange foundation (corn, beans, chilies, tomatoes, potatoes, cacao, vanilla, peanuts, squash all originated here), heavy Spanish and Portuguese colonial layers, and varying degrees of African, indigenous, and other immigrant influence. But the cuisines are dramatically different from each other — Mexican and Argentine cooking have less in common than French and German cooking do.

This note covers the four broad zones: **Mexico** (its own deep tradition, treated separately), **Central America and Caribbean**, **Andean South America** (Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, Colombia), and **Southern Cone** (Argentina, Uruguay, Chile, Brazil — though Brazil is its own world).

---

## Mexico

### What Mexican cuisine actually is

Recognized by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage. Built on the **Mesoamerican triad** of corn, beans, and squash, with chilies as the flavor architecture and a deeply regional structure. The major culinary regions:

- **Northern Mexico** — wheat (flour tortillas, originated here), grilled meats, asado, cabrito.
- **Central Mexico** — Mexico City and Puebla. Moles, mixiotes, barbacoa, the most famous national dishes.
- **Yucatán** — Mayan-rooted, distinctive: achiote, sour orange, banana leaf cooking (cochinita pibil), habanero, regional dishes barely overlap with central Mexico.
- **Oaxaca** — Land of the Seven Moles, complex regional cuisine, mezcal, distinct chili varieties.
- **Coastal** (Veracruz, Pacific coast) — seafood-heavy, Spanish-Caribbean influences.

The defining sensibility is **chili-driven complexity** — Mexican cooking uses dozens of distinct chili varieties (each with its own flavor profile, not just heat level), often combined in a single dish, with techniques like toasting, soaking, and blending creating layered sauces of remarkable depth.

### Pantry foundations

**Dried chilies** as their own category — each variety contributes specific flavor:
- *Ancho* (dried poblano — sweet, raisin-like, mild)
- *Guajillo* (bright, fruity, mild)
- *Pasilla* (rich, dark, slightly bitter, mild-medium)
- *Chipotle* (smoked dried jalapeño — smoky, medium-hot)
- *Mulato* (similar to ancho, more chocolate)
- *Árbol* (small, hot, clean spice)
- *Habanero* (very hot, fruity, especially in Yucatán)

These are not interchangeable. A mole calling for ancho + mulato + pasilla + chipotle has each chili doing specific work.

**Fresh chilies**: jalapeño, serrano, habanero, poblano (the fresh form of ancho).

**Corn** — the foundation. *Masa harina* (nixtamalized corn flour, like Maseca) is the everyday tortilla and tamale base; *fresh masa* from a tortilleria is dramatically better when available. Whole nixtamal kernels for *pozole*.

Other essentials: **Mexican oregano** (different species from Mediterranean — more citrusy, slightly licorice; not interchangeable), **epazote** (pungent herb used with beans — distinct, can't substitute), **Mexican cinnamon/canela** (true cinnamon, softer and more delicate than the cassia commonly sold as cinnamon in the US), **achiote/annatto** (red coloring and earthy flavor — central to Yucatán cooking), **piloncillo** (unrefined cone sugar, smoky-molasses depth), **Mexican chocolate** (with cinnamon and sometimes almonds — for mole and chocolate caliente), **lard** (manteca — the traditional cooking fat, especially for refried beans and tamales), **lime** (limón — used heavily as a finisher), **cilantro**, **white onion**, **avocado**, **tomatillo** (the tart green husk-tomato — base of salsa verde), **black, pinto, and other beans**, **queso fresco** and **queso Oaxaca**.

### Defining techniques

**Nixtamalization** — soaking dried corn in alkaline water (lime/cal), which softens the kernel, makes niacin bioavailable, and creates the masa flavor. Without nixtamalization, corn is a different food. This is the foundational technique of the entire Mesoamerican culinary tradition; all corn tortillas, tamales, sopes, gorditas, atole start here.

**Toasting and rehydrating chilies** — dried chilies are toasted briefly on a comal (until fragrant, not burnt — burnt = bitter), then soaked in hot water until soft, then blended into sauces. This unlocks their flavor.

**Comal cooking** — a flat dry griddle (originally clay) used to toast chilies, char vegetables for salsa, warm tortillas, cook quesadillas. The dry-heat charring it produces is essential to many salsas.

**Mole-making** — toasting chilies, nuts, seeds, spices separately; rehydrating; blending; frying the paste in lard until darkened; then adding stock and simmering for hours. A real mole is a multi-day project with 20-30 ingredients; the technique is the cuisine's most elaborate.

**Slow-roasted meats in earth ovens** — barbacoa (lamb, traditionally pit-cooked in maguey leaves), cochinita pibil (pork in achiote and sour orange, banana leaf wrapped). Modern home approximations use Dutch ovens or slow cookers.

**Salsa-making** — fresh (pico de gallo, salsa verde cruda) and cooked (salsa roja with toasted dried chilies + tomato + garlic + onion, blended). Salsa is not a condiment but a category — most Mexican meals include multiple salsas.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- "Mexican food" reduced to Tex-Mex (cheese-stuffed everything, ground beef, sour cream, yellow cheese melted on top) — Tex-Mex is a real and good cuisine, but it's not Mexican food. Authentic Mexican cooking uses cheese sparingly, rarely uses ground beef, and never uses the bright orange melted cheese.
- Cumin treated as a defining Mexican spice — cumin is used, but lightly, and is far more central to Tex-Mex than to most regional Mexican cooking. Real Mexican cooking is much more about chilies than about cumin.
- "Chili powder" (the McCormick blend of chili + cumin + oregano + salt) substituted for ground specific chilies — completely different flavor architecture.
- Mediterranean oregano substituted for Mexican oregano — different plant, different flavor profile.
- Cassia (the everyday "cinnamon" sold in US supermarkets) used in mole — should be true Mexican canela, much more delicate.
- Flour tortillas treated as default — flour tortillas are Northern Mexican; in central, southern, and Yucatecan cooking, corn is the standard.
- Lime juice + cilantro + onion treated as Mexican-by-default for any meat — this combo is real (in tacos al pastor garnish, for instance) but not universal.
- Skipping the chili-toasting step in salsa or mole — toasting unlocks flavor; raw blended dried chilies are flat and bitter.

## Central America and Caribbean

### What this cuisine actually is

The seven Central American countries (Guatemala, Belize, Honduras, El Salvador, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, Panama) share an indigenous Mesoamerican base with Mexico (corn, beans, chilies) but have diverged significantly. The Caribbean (Cuba, Puerto Rico, Dominican Republic, Jamaica, Haiti) layers African and indigenous Taíno influences on Spanish/French/British colonial bases; cuisine is rice-and-bean-and-pork-and-seafood-driven, with strong African contributions (callaloo, jerk, plantains).

Defining dishes by region:
- **Guatemala/El Salvador**: pupusas (stuffed thick corn tortillas), tamales (different from Mexican — banana leaf wrap common, often sweeter masa).
- **Honduras/Nicaragua**: baleadas, gallo pinto, nacatamales.
- **Costa Rica**: gallo pinto, casado plates, simpler/lighter than the rest.
- **Cuba**: ropa vieja, lechon asado, mojo (sour orange + garlic + cumin marinade — a defining sauce), Moros y Cristianos (black beans and rice cooked together).
- **Puerto Rico/Dominican**: sofrito-based (Caribbean sofrito = recao/culantro + cilantro + ají dulce + onion + garlic + bell pepper), arroz con gandules, mofongo.
- **Jamaica**: jerk (allspice + scotch bonnet + thyme + scallion + ginger + cinnamon + nutmeg, a complex spice paste, traditionally smoke-grilled over pimento wood).
- **Haiti**: epis (a green seasoning paste base), griot (fried marinated pork), pikliz (spicy pickled cabbage).

### Pantry foundations

**Sour orange** (naranja agria — citrus aurantium, central to Cuban and Yucatecan cooking; substitute is 50/50 lime + orange), **culantro/recao** (sawtooth herb, more pungent than cilantro, holds up in cooking), **ají dulce** (sweet small Caribbean pepper, distinct from bell pepper), **scotch bonnet** (very hot fruity chili — defining Jamaican heat), **allspice/pimento** (Jamaican berry, central to jerk), **plantains** (green for tostones/mofongo, ripe for sweet maduros), **yuca/cassava**, **black beans, red beans, pigeon peas (gandules)**, **rice** (long-grain), **coconut** (especially in coastal cooking), **annatto/achiote**, **culantro**, **Caribbean sofrito** (a green seasoning paste, often pre-made and refrigerated).

### Defining techniques

**Sofrito as a base** — Caribbean sofrito is green and herbal (recao + cilantro + ají dulce + garlic + onion + bell pepper, blended); Cuban sofrito leans more Mediterranean (onion, garlic, green pepper, tomato in olive oil). Used as the foundation of beans, rice, stews.

**Mojo marinade** (Cuban) — sour orange juice + garlic + cumin + oregano + olive oil. Used on pork, chicken, yuca.

**Jerk** — wet spice paste, marinade for hours to days, traditionally cooked low and slow over pimento (allspice) wood; a smoke-grilling technique as much as a flavor.

**Slow-braised meats** — ropa vieja (shredded beef in tomato sofrito), griot (Haitian twice-cooked pork — boiled with citrus, then fried).

**Fried then mashed** — mofongo and tostones both involve frying green plantains then smashing them.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- "Jerk seasoning" from a jar — passable but dramatically different from a freshly made wet jerk paste with fresh thyme, scotch bonnet, scallion, and allspice.
- Sour orange substituted with regular orange juice — too sweet, too floral. Use 50/50 lime + orange or fresh sour orange when available.
- Cilantro substituted for culantro/recao in Caribbean sofrito — different herb, different intensity. Recao holds its flavor in cooking; cilantro fades.
- Cuban "mojo" reduced to garlic + lime — needs the cumin and oregano and ideally sour orange.

## Andean South America (Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, Colombia)

### What this cuisine actually is

**Peru** has emerged in the past 20 years as one of the world's most exciting cuisines, blending indigenous Andean ingredients (potatoes — over 4,000 native varieties, quinoa, ají chilies, guinea pig/cuy), Spanish colonial layer, Japanese immigrant influence (Nikkei cuisine — ceviche, tiraditos), and Chinese immigrant influence (chifa cuisine — lomo saltado, chaufa).

**Colombia** — coffee country, regional diversity (coastal seafood vs Andean mountain food), arepas (corn cakes — flatter, denser than Venezuelan arepas), bandeja paisa.

**Ecuador, Bolivia** — share the Andean potato/quinoa/corn foundation with Peru but with their own distinct dishes (Bolivia's salteñas, Ecuador's encebollado, hornado, llapingachos).

### Pantry foundations

**Ají amarillo** (yellow Peruvian chili — fruity, moderately hot, defining Peruvian flavor — usually used as paste), **ají panca** (deep red, mild, smoky-sweet), **rocoto** (very hot, fruity), **purple corn** (chicha morada), **quinoa**, **potatoes** (yellow, purple, blue, fingerling, hundreds of varieties), **lime** (limón sutil — small, intensely sour Peruvian lime, central to ceviche), **cilantro**, **culantro**, **huacatay** (Peruvian black mint — strong, used in ocopa sauce), **chuño** (freeze-dried potato — Andean staple), **arepa flour** (precooked cornmeal, brand: P.A.N.).

### Defining techniques

**Ceviche** — Peruvian ceviche is short-cured (15-30 minutes, sometimes "leche de tigre" style with the lime juice as a drink), with raw fish + lime + ají chili + red onion + cilantro + sweet potato + corn. Texture is tender, not "cooked through" by acid as Mexican-style ceviche often is.

**Stir-fry technique from Chinese influence** (lomo saltado) — beef + onion + tomato + ají amarillo, stir-fried in a wok with soy sauce, served with rice and french fries.

**Ají paste making** — fresh or dried ají chilies blanched (multiple water changes to mellow), then blended with garlic and oil into a paste used as a foundation for many dishes.

**Arepa cooking** — corn flour + water + salt dough, formed into discs, griddle-cooked then sometimes finished in oven; split and stuffed.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- Mexican-style ceviche technique applied to Peruvian — Mexican ceviches "cook" the fish for hours; Peruvian is fresh and quick (the fish should still be translucent at center).
- Generic chili paste substituted for ají amarillo paste — ají amarillo has a specific fruity-spicy character that defines Peruvian food. Frozen pulp or jarred paste is widely available.
- Regular lime substituted for Peruvian limón — these limes are smaller and much more intense; use about 30% more juice if substituting Persian limes.

## Southern Cone (Argentina, Uruguay, Chile) and Brazil

### What this cuisine actually is

**Argentina and Uruguay** — beef-driven, Italian-immigrant-influenced (heavy pasta and pizza traditions). Asado (live-fire grilled meat) is the cultural institution. Chimichurri (parsley + garlic + oregano + red wine vinegar + olive oil + chili flakes) is the standard accompaniment. Mate (yerba mate tea) is the daily ritual beverage.

**Chile** — long thin country with seafood, wine, and indigenous Mapuche influences. Pebre (cilantro-onion-chili relish), cazuela (stew), curanto (pit-cooked seafood and meat).

**Brazil** — its own world, deserving its own note eventually. African-Portuguese-indigenous synthesis. Defining cuisines:
- **Bahian** (African influence — palm oil/dendê, coconut milk, dried shrimp, malagueta chili — moqueca, vatapá, acarajé).
- **Minas Gerais** — pork, beans, cheese (queijo Minas), pão de queijo.
- **Southern** (Rio Grande do Sul) — overlaps with Argentine asado, churrasco.
- **Amazonian** — distinct ingredients (jambu, tucupi, tambaqui).

National dishes that cross regions: feijoada (black bean and pork stew), pão de queijo (cheese bread), brigadeiro (chocolate truffle).

### Pantry foundations

**Argentina/Uruguay**: beef (huge cuts, grass-fed traditions), chimichurri ingredients (parsley, garlic, oregano, red wine vinegar, olive oil, chili flakes), provoleta cheese, dulce de leche, yerba mate.

**Brazil**: black beans, cassava/yuca and cassava flour (farinha), palm oil/dendê (bright red, distinctive flavor — central to Bahian cooking), coconut milk, dried shrimp (camarão seco — used in moqueca and vatapá), malagueta chili, cachaça (sugarcane spirit — for caipirinhas and cooking), guava paste, queijo Minas, pão de queijo dough (uses tapioca/cassava starch, not wheat).

**Chile**: seafood, merken (smoked Mapuche chili spice), pebre ingredients.

### Defining techniques

**Asado / churrasco** — live-fire grilling, with regional variations. Argentine asado uses parrilla (grate over coals) and a sequence of cuts (chorizo, morcilla, mollejas/sweetbreads, then larger cuts like asado de tira, vacío, tira de asado). Brazilian churrasco often skewers and rotates over fire (rodízio style).

**Moqueca** — Bahian fish stew in coconut milk + palm oil + tomato + onion + cilantro, cooked in a clay pot. The dendê/palm oil is structural — without it, it's not moqueca.

**Feijoada cooking** — black beans simmered for hours with multiple cuts of pork (often including ears, tail, and other less-glamorous cuts traditionally), served with rice, collard greens, orange slices, farofa (toasted cassava flour), and pepper sauce.

**Caipirinha technique** — muddle lime with sugar (not simple syrup) until the oils release, add cachaça and ice. The texture from the muddled lime peel oils is the point.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- Chimichurri made with cilantro — chimichurri is parsley-based. Cilantro chimichurri exists as a riff but is not the standard.
- Chimichurri blended in a food processor to a smooth paste — should be coarsely chopped, with visible texture.
- Olive oil substituted for dendê/palm oil in moqueca — the dish is fundamentally different. Use real palm oil (red dendê, available at Brazilian/African groceries).
- Caipirinha made with simple syrup — defeats the muddling step; use real sugar.
- Brazilian feijoada presented as a single national dish — there are major regional variations (Rio's heavier version, Bahian lighter, etc.).

## Cross-cutting notes for Latin American cooking

**Chilies are an entire flavor architecture, not a heat dial.** This is most obvious in Mexico and Peru. A recipe calling for ancho + guajillo + chipotle is using each chili for distinct flavor work; substituting "chili powder" or one variety for another flattens the dish.

**Corn cuisine here is nixtamalized.** Latin American corn cooking — Mexican, Central American, much of South American — runs through nixtamal. Cornmeal mush in Northern European or Italian polenta tradition is a different ingredient and produces different food.

**The Spanish/Portuguese sofrito/refogado base recurs across Latin America** but with different ingredients (Caribbean is recao-and-ají; Mexican is more typically tomato-onion-chili; Brazilian refogado is onion-garlic-tomato-coriander). The technique is shared; the contents vary.

**Lime/lemon/sour-orange acidity is structural.** Ceviche, mojo, mojito, caipirinha, taco garnish, salsas — citrus acidity isn't a finishing touch in most Latin American cooking, it's a primary flavor.

**African contributions are major and often invisible in Western framings.** Brazilian Bahian cuisine (palm oil, dendê, dried shrimp), Caribbean (callaloo, plantain technique), Cuban (much of the rice and bean tradition) — the African layer is foundational, not decorative.

**Rice and beans are not a single dish.** Cuban Moros y Cristianos, Brazilian arroz e feijão, Puerto Rican arroz con gandules, Costa Rican gallo pinto, Mexican refried beans + rice — each has its own preparation and standing.
