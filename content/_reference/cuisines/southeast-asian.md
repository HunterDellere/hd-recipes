# Southeast Asian Cuisines — Reference Note

Southeast Asia spans mainland (Thailand, Vietnam, Cambodia, Laos, Myanmar) and maritime (Indonesia, Malaysia, Philippines, Singapore, Brunei) traditions. They share fish sauce, rice, fresh aromatics (lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime, chili), and an unusual obsession with the simultaneous balance of salty / sour / sweet / spicy / bitter in a single bite. But the techniques and pantry compositions diverge meaningfully between countries — and even between regions within them.

---

## Thailand

### What Thai cuisine actually is

Four broad regional traditions: **Central** (the canonical "Thai food" of green/red curries, pad thai, tom yum — Bangkok and the central plains), **Northern** (Lanna — milder, herbier, sticky rice culture, dishes like khao soi and sai oua), **Northeastern/Isaan** (Lao-influenced — sticky rice, larb, som tum, grilled meats, more chili and fish funk), **Southern** (more turmeric, coconut, dried shrimp, fierce heat — Muslim influence in dishes like massaman).

The defining sensibility is the **balance of four flavors** in a single dish: salty (fish sauce, shrimp paste), sour (lime, tamarind), sweet (palm sugar), spicy (fresh and dried chilies). A good Thai cook is constantly tasting and rebalancing.

### Pantry foundations

**Fish sauce** (nam pla) is the salt of Thai cooking — look for two ingredients only: anchovies and salt. **Palm sugar** (nam tan pip) is the sweetener — not white sugar, not brown sugar; it has a butterscotch-caramel depth. **Tamarind paste** (or pulp soaked and strained) is the dominant sour element alongside lime. **Shrimp paste** (kapi) is the funk-forward base in many curry pastes and dipping sauces. **Coconut milk** (full-fat, not "lite") and **coconut cream** for curries.

Fresh aromatics are non-negotiable: **lemongrass**, **galangal** (not ginger — different flavor entirely; piney and citrusy), **kaffir lime leaves** (now often called makrut lime to drop the slur), **Thai basil** (anise-forward, not the same as Italian basil), **holy basil** (kaprao — peppery, used in pad kaprao), **bird's eye chili** (prik kee noo). Curry pastes use these fresh, pounded with shrimp paste and spices.

### Defining techniques

**Pounding curry paste in a stone mortar** (krok hin) — the texture and oil release from real pounding can't be replicated by a food processor; the paste has a different viscosity and flavor. **Cracking coconut cream** — heating thick coconut cream until the oil separates out and the milk solids brown, then frying the curry paste in that oil before adding the rest of the coconut milk. This is the soul of Thai curry. **High-heat wok stir-frying** for dishes like pad kaprao and pad see ew. **Pounding som tum** in a clay mortar (krok din) to bruise green papaya and meld dressing without crushing.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- Ginger substituted for galangal — they're not interchangeable. Galangal is piney and citrusy; ginger is warm and pungent. Use galangal even if frozen.
- Italian basil substituted for Thai or holy basil — Italian basil is sweet and clove-like, Thai is anise-forward, holy is peppery. They produce different dishes.
- Lite coconut milk in curries — Thai curries depend on the fat to crack the cream and carry flavor. Use full-fat.
- Brown sugar for palm sugar — works but flattens the caramel-butterscotch note.
- "Curry paste in a jar" treated as equivalent to fresh-pounded — passable for weeknights but the flavor is muted; many jarred pastes also under-include shrimp paste.
- Fish sauce omitted "for vegetarians" without replacement — needs a substitute that brings salt + funk (light soy + a little miso, or vegan fish sauce). Just leaving it out hollows out the dish.
- Pad thai treated as the central Thai dish — it's a relatively recent invention (1930s/40s national-identity project) and not what most Thai people eat day-to-day.

## Vietnam

### What Vietnamese cuisine actually is

Three broad regional traditions: **Northern** (Hanoi — subtler, less sweet, less spicy; pho originated here, refined and brothy), **Central** (Hue — imperial cuisine, more complex, spicier, smaller plates), **Southern** (Saigon — sweeter, more herbs, more coconut, French influence visible in banh mi and Vietnamese coffee).

The defining sensibility is **lightness, herbs, and contrast**: hot broth with cool herbs, crispy with soft, sweet with sour, all in dialogue rather than blended. Fresh herbs are not a garnish — they're a primary ingredient eaten by the handful.

### Pantry foundations

**Fish sauce** (nuoc mam) is again the salt — Vietnamese fish sauce tends to be a touch milder than Thai. **Nuoc cham** — the fish sauce dipping sauce of fish sauce + lime + sugar + garlic + chili — is the table condiment that touches almost every meal. **Rice paper** (banh trang) for fresh and fried rolls. **Rice noodles** in many widths (banh pho for pho, bun for cold noodle dishes, mien/glass noodles). **Hoisin and Sriracha** are common but specifically Southern; Northern cooking uses them less.

Fresh herbs are the pantry: **Vietnamese mint/rau ram** (peppery), **Thai basil**, **culantro** (saw-tooth herb, more pungent than cilantro), **mint**, **shiso**, **perilla**. Plus **bean sprouts**, **lime**, **bird's eye chili**.

### Defining techniques

**Long simmered broths** for pho — beef bones charred first, then simmered for hours with charred onion and ginger, star anise, cloves, cinnamon, cardamom. Clarity matters; a cloudy broth is a flawed broth. **Fresh roll assembly** (goi cuon) — softened rice paper, herbs and protein arranged on top, rolled tight without tearing. **Caramelization** (kho — meats braised in a caramel sauce of sugar cooked to deep amber, then fish sauce and water added — for dishes like thit kho).

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- Pho served with bean sprouts and hoisin in the bowl as default — that's a Southern Vietnamese-American convention; in Hanoi pho is served plain to let the broth speak.
- Sriracha treated as a default condiment — it's fine in the South but the broader Vietnamese flavor palette is much more about nuoc cham and fresh chili.
- Cilantro substituted for culantro in soups — different herb, different flavor (culantro is more pungent and holds up better in hot liquid).
- Banh mi made on a French baguette — should be a Vietnamese baguette (shorter, thinner crust, fluffier inside; rice flour is often blended into the dough).
- "Vietnamese" fish sauce dishes that omit the herbs — herbs aren't a garnish, they're the dish. A bowl of pho without a plate of herbs alongside is incomplete.

## Indonesia, Malaysia, Singapore (Maritime SE Asia)

### What this cuisine actually is

Indonesia spans 17,000+ islands and 300+ ethnic groups; "Indonesian cuisine" is a flag, not a single tradition. The major culinary regions are **Javanese** (sweeter, kecap manis, soto soups), **Sumatran** (Padang/Minangkabau — bold, spicy, coconut-rich, rendang), **Balinese** (suckling pig, base gede paste), and the **Spice Islands** (Maluku — actual spice provenance for clove and nutmeg).

Malaysian and Singaporean cuisine layer Malay, Chinese, Indian, and Peranakan (Straits-born Chinese) traditions. **Nyonya/Peranakan** cooking is its own coherent tradition — Chinese techniques applied to Malay ingredients.

### Pantry foundations

**Kecap manis** (Indonesian sweet soy sauce — thick, palm-sugar-sweetened) is the defining condiment of Javanese cooking. **Sambal** (chili pastes — sambal oelek raw, sambal terasi with shrimp paste, sambal matah Balinese raw) is at every table. **Belacan/terasi** (fermented shrimp paste — block form, toasted before use). **Coconut milk and grated coconut**. **Candlenuts** (kemiri — for thickening curry pastes, related to macadamia). **Tamarind**, **palm sugar**, **lemongrass**, **galangal**, **kaffir lime**.

For Malaysian/Singaporean cooking add **pandan leaves** (vanilla-grassy aroma in rice and desserts), **laksa leaf** (Vietnamese mint), **dried shrimp**.

### Defining techniques

**Spice paste pounding** (bumbu in Indonesian, rempah in Malay) — a wet aromatic paste of shallots, garlic, chilies, ginger/galangal, candlenuts, terasi, pounded and then **fried until the oil splits** (similar logic to the Thai cracking-coconut-cream technique — you need the paste to lose its raw edge and bloom in the fat before liquid is added). **Long-cook coconut reductions** — rendang famously cooks beef in coconut milk and spice paste for hours until the liquid completely dries and the meat fries in its own released coconut oil; the result is fundamentally different from a wet curry.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- "Rendang" sold as a wet curry — true rendang is dry, with the meat coated in a dark, intense paste. The wet version is *kalio*, an earlier stage of the same dish.
- Sambal substituted with sriracha — sambal oelek is a chili paste with little else; Indonesian sambals are an entire family of preparations with shrimp paste, lime, shallots, etc.
- Kecap manis treated as "Indonesian soy sauce" without the sweetness — it's much closer to a thick syrup than a pourable sauce.
- Belacan / terasi used raw — must be toasted (over flame, in foil, or dry-fried) before use; raw it tastes harshly fishy.
- Nasi goreng made without kecap manis — the dark sweetness is what defines it against other fried rices.

## Philippines

### What Filipino cuisine actually is

Distinct from the rest of Southeast Asia: heavy Spanish, Chinese, and American colonial layers on top of indigenous techniques. Less use of fresh herbs and chili than mainland SE Asia; more vinegar, more pork, more sour-savory braising.

The defining sensibility is **sourness as a primary flavor axis** — adobo, sinigang, kinilaw all build their identity around acid (vinegar, tamarind, kalamansi, green mango).

### Pantry foundations

**Soy sauce** (toyo — Filipino soy sauce is saltier and lighter-bodied than Chinese), **vinegar** (cane vinegar, coconut vinegar, sukang Iloko — palm vinegar), **fish sauce** (patis), **calamansi** (small citrus, lime-orange hybrid), **bagoong** (fermented shrimp/fish paste). **Coconut** (in Bicol especially). **Banana ketchup** (a real ingredient with real history, not a joke).

### Defining techniques

**Adobo** — meat (chicken, pork) braised in vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, bay leaves, peppercorns until the liquid reduces to a glaze. The acid-as-cooking-liquid is what makes it adobo, not the soy sauce (which is a later addition; pre-colonial adobo was vinegar only). **Sinigang** — sour soup soured with tamarind, kamias, green mango, or guava. **Kinilaw** — raw fish "cooked" in vinegar with ginger and chili (parallel to ceviche but vinegar-based).

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- Adobo described as "Filipino soy sauce stew" — the soul of adobo is the vinegar; soy sauce is a regional addition, not the foundation.
- Sinigang flavored with lemon juice instead of tamarind/kamias — completely changes the dish; Filipino sourness is a specific kind of fruity-tart.
- Kalamansi swapped for regular lime — kalamansi is sweeter and more orange-perfumed; in a pinch use a 50/50 lime-orange mix.

## Cross-cutting notes for Southeast Asian cooking

**Fish sauce is the salt of the region** — across Thailand, Vietnam, Cambodia, Laos, the Philippines, parts of Indonesia. Quality matters: 2-ingredient labels (anchovies + salt), 6-12 month aging, amber-to-mahogany color. Three Crabs and Red Boat are widely respected.

**Fresh aromatics differ from East Asia.** Galangal not ginger, kaffir/makrut lime leaves not regular citrus, lemongrass, Thai basil — these are not pantry items in Chinese/Japanese/Korean cooking and are non-substitutable here.

**Coconut is structural, not flavoring.** Fat from coconut milk is the cooking medium for many curries; it cracks, fries the paste, then dilutes back to sauce. Lite coconut milk does not work for this.

**The four-flavor balance** (salty/sour/sweet/spicy, sometimes with bitter as fifth) is the conceptual frame. A Thai cook tasting a curry is checking whether all four are present and in proportion, not whether one flavor dominates.

**Heat tolerance varies.** Northern Thai, Northern Vietnamese, and Filipino cooking is relatively mild. Isaan, Southern Thai, Padang, and many Indonesian dishes are seriously spicy by Western standards.
