# East Asian Cuisines — Reference Note

This note covers China, Korea, and Japan. They share rice culture, soy fermentation, chopstick eating, and a deep relationship with umami — but their pantries, technique vocabularies, and sensibilities diverge sharply once you go past those surface similarities. Treat this as a briefing before drafting any recipe in this region: it should orient you, not constrain you.

---

## China

### What "Chinese cuisine" actually is

There is no single Chinese cuisine. The conventional framing is the **Eight Great Traditions**: Sichuan, Cantonese (Yue), Shandong (Lu), Jiangsu (Su), Zhejiang, Fujian (Min), Hunan (Xiang), and Anhui. The four with the strongest identity in Western kitchens are Sichuan, Cantonese, Shandong, and Jiangsu — most other regional dishes can be located on the spectrum between them.

A useful mental model: **North = wheat, salt-forward, hearty (noodles, dumplings, breads)**. **South = rice, fresh, lighter (Cantonese steaming, dim sum)**. **West = bold, numbing-spicy (Sichuan, Hunan)**. **East = refined, sweet-savory, seasonal (Jiangsu, Shanghai)**.

### Pantry foundations

Soy sauce is not one ingredient — **light soy** (生抽, sheng chou) seasons; **dark soy** (老抽, lao chou) colors and adds molasses depth; they are not interchangeable. **Shaoxing wine** is the default cooking wine across most regions and is closer to dry sherry than to sake. **Black vinegar** (Chinkiang/Zhenjiang) is the standard dipping vinegar — fruity and malty, nothing like Western black vinegar. **Doubanjiang** (fermented broad bean and chili paste, especially Pixian) is the soul of Sichuan cooking. **Fermented black beans** (douchi), **oyster sauce**, **white pepper** (more common than black in most Chinese cooking), and **toasted sesame oil** (used as a finishing oil, almost never as a cooking oil) round out the core.

For Sichuan specifically: **Sichuan peppercorns** (huajiao) for the *má* numbing sensation, **dried chilies** (typically erjingtiao or facing-heaven), **Pixian doubanjiang**, and often a chili oil with toasted spices.

### Defining techniques

**Wok hei** — the elusive smoky char from a screaming-hot carbon steel wok — is the signature of Cantonese stir-frying and most southern Chinese cooking. It requires real heat, a dry wok surface, and ingredients added in stages so the pan never cools. **Velveting** (marinating protein in egg white, cornstarch, and sometimes Shaoxing wine, then blanching in oil or water before stir-frying) is what produces the silky texture in restaurant-style chicken and beef. **Red-cooking** (hong shao — slow-braising in soy sauce, sugar, wine, and aromatics) is the foundational eastern/Shanghai technique. **Steaming** is central to Cantonese cooking — fish, dumplings, buns, custards. Sichuan adds **dry-frying** (gan bian) and **fish-fragrant** (yu xiang) flavor profiles built on pickled chilies, garlic, ginger, and vinegar.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- "Soy sauce" used as a single undifferentiated ingredient — almost always light soy is meant, but recipes often skip the distinction.
- Brown sugar substituted for **rock sugar** (bing tang) in red-cooked dishes — works, but loses the cleaner sweetness and the gentle gloss rock sugar gives a braise.
- Five-spice powder treated as a default Chinese seasoning — it's actually used sparingly and regionally, not sprinkled across everything.
- Chinese cooking wine swapped for "any white wine" — Shaoxing is closer to dry sherry; Japanese sake is a reasonable substitute, dry white wine is not.
- "Sesame oil" added during cooking — toasted sesame oil burns and goes bitter; it's a finishing oil. Untoasted is fine for cooking but rarely called for.
- Sichuan peppercorns omitted "for heat reasons" — they're not for heat; they're for *má* (tongue-numbing tingle), which is the entire point of Sichuan flavor.
- Americanized dishes (orange chicken, General Tso's, sweet-and-sour pork with red sauce and pineapple) presented as authentic — these are Chinese-American inventions and worth labeling honestly.

## Japan

### What "Japanese cuisine" actually is

Japanese home and traditional cuisine — **washoku** — is built on restraint, seasonality, and the foregrounding of single high-quality ingredients. The philosophical frame is the **five principles**: five colors (white, black, red, yellow, green), five flavors (salty, sour, sweet, bitter, umami), five cooking methods (raw, simmered, steamed, grilled, fried), five senses, five mindsets. A meal is composed, not assembled.

A meaningful split: **washoku** (traditional Japanese — kaiseki, home cooking, sushi, soba, tempura) versus **yōshoku** (Western-influenced Japanese — tonkatsu, omurice, curry rice, hambagu). Both are real and beloved, but they're different traditions. Ramen sits in its own category — rooted in Chinese noodle soup but evolved into something distinctly Japanese, with strong regional identities (Sapporo miso, Hakata tonkotsu, Tokyo shoyu, etc.).

### Pantry foundations

The quartet that does most of the work: **shoyu** (soy sauce — usually koikuchi/dark; usukuchi/light is saltier and lighter in color, used in Kansai cooking), **miso** (white/shiro is sweet and mild, red/aka is aged and assertive, awase is a blend), **mirin** (sweet rice cooking wine — *hon-mirin* is real, *mirin-fu chōmiryō* is a sweetened substitute), and **sake**. Add **rice vinegar** (mild, almost sweet — nothing like Western vinegars) and you have the seasoning grammar of most washoku.

**Dashi** is the foundational stock and the heart of washoku. The standard combination is **kombu** (kelp) and **katsuobushi** (dried bonito shavings) — *ichiban dashi* (first dashi, briefly steeped, delicate) for clear soups and refined dishes; *niban dashi* (second dashi, simmered longer) for braises and miso soup. **Shiitake** dashi (dried mushroom) is the vegetarian equivalent. **Niboshi** (dried sardines) makes a stronger, more rustic dashi for miso soup and noodle broths.

Other essentials: short-grain Japanese rice (washed until water runs clear, then rested), **nori**, **wakame**, **panko**, **shichimi tōgarashi** (seven-spice blend), **yuzu** (citrus, increasingly available frozen).

### Defining techniques

**Dashi extraction** is the foundational technique — kombu cold-soaked or warmed gently to just below simmering (never boiled, which makes it slimy), then katsuobushi added off-heat and strained quickly. The whole process takes minutes; the discipline is in not overworking it. **Rice cookery** (washing, soaking, precise water ratio, resting after cooking) is treated with the seriousness Italians give pasta. **Knife work** is elevated — *sogigiri* (angled slicing), *katsuramuki* (rotary peeling) — because raw and lightly cooked presentations leave nowhere to hide. **Tempura** technique relies on cold batter, minimal mixing (lumps are correct), and oil temperature management. **Grilling** (yakimono — yakitori, yakiniku, fish) is a major mode, often with a salt-cure or tare glaze.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- Boiling dashi or steeping kombu past 60-65°C — produces bitter, slimy stock. Restraint is the whole point.
- "Mirin" that is actually mirin-fu chōmiryō (sweetened cooking liquid, not fermented) — it works but has none of the depth of real hon-mirin.
- Rice cooked without washing — leaves surface starch that turns the grains gluey rather than separate-but-tender.
- Soy sauce treated as the dominant seasoning — in most washoku it's a supporting note; dashi and salt do more of the work.
- Sushi rice seasoned wrong — the vinegar/sugar/salt ratio matters and varies regionally (Edo-style is more vinegar-forward, Kansai sweeter).
- Wasabi paste from a tube treated as real wasabi — almost all Western "wasabi" is horseradish, mustard, and green dye. Real wasabi (*hon-wasabi*) is grated fresh from the rhizome and tastes floral and gentle, not nasal-burn aggressive.
- Hibachi-style teppanyaki presented as traditional Japanese — it's a Japanese-American restaurant format.

## Korea

### What "Korean cuisine" actually is

Korean cuisine is built around the **rice + soup + banchan** structure: a bowl of rice, a soup or stew, and an array of small side dishes (banchan), all eaten together rather than in courses. The defining sensibility is **fermentation** — kimchi, the *jangs* (fermented pastes and sauces), and the practice of building deep flavor from time rather than from heat or fat.

Regional variation exists (Jeolla is famous for the most elaborate banchan spreads, Gyeongsang for spicier and saltier dishes, Pyongan/North for milder, beefier flavors) but is less pronounced than in China — Korean cuisine reads more unified to outsiders, and the fermentation traditions are nationwide.

### Pantry foundations

The **three jangs** are the holy trinity: **ganjang** (Korean soy sauce — multiple types, with *guk-ganjang*/soup soy being lighter and saltier than the everyday *jin-ganjang*), **doenjang** (fermented soybean paste — funkier, earthier, and less sweet than Japanese miso), and **gochujang** (fermented red chili paste with glutinous rice and soybeans — sweet, savory, and slow-burning).

Beyond the jangs: **gochugaru** (Korean red chili flakes — coarse for kimchi and stews, fine for gochujang and seasoning; bright red, fruity, moderate heat — not interchangeable with cayenne or generic red pepper flakes), **toasted sesame oil** and **toasted sesame seeds** (used heavily as finishers), **garlic** (lots — Korean cuisine is among the most garlic-heavy in the world), **green onions**, **Asian pear** (for marinades — its enzymes tenderize meat and add subtle sweetness), **soy sauce**, **fish sauce** (*aekjeot* — used in kimchi and many banchan), **dried anchovies and kelp** (for the foundational *yuksu* stock), **glutinous rice flour**, **perilla leaves and oil** (distinct from sesame — minty and grassy).

Kimchi is its own pantry — baechu (napa cabbage) is the default but there are dozens (kkakdugi/radish, oi sobagi/cucumber, baek/white non-spicy, mul/water).

### Defining techniques

**Fermentation** is the foundational technique and the source of most of the cuisine's depth. Making jangs traditionally takes months; kimchi ferments for days to years depending on style. **Marinating with fruit** (Asian pear, kiwi, pineapple) is standard for grilled meats — bulgogi and galbi rely on it for tenderness and a backnote of sweetness. **Tabletop grilling** (gogigui) is a defining mode of eating, not just a cooking technique. **Stews** (jjigae and guk) are built on yuksu stock with jangs, often finished with soft tofu, seafood, or pork. **Banchan-making** is a distinct craft — most are quick-pickled, stir-fried, or seasoned vegetables made in batches and kept refrigerated.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- Gochujang treated as a hot sauce or substituted with sriracha — it's a paste with funk, sweetness, and slow heat; sriracha is a vinegar-forward chili sauce. Not the same job.
- Generic red pepper flakes substituted for gochugaru — gochugaru is fruitier, less sharply hot, and brick red. Cayenne or pizza-shop flakes will overheat and underflavor a dish.
- Doenjang substituted with Japanese miso — works in a pinch but loses the funk. Doenjang is to miso what aged blue cheese is to a young brie.
- Korean soy sauce flattened to "any soy sauce" — for soups specifically, *guk-ganjang* (lighter color, saltier) keeps broths clear; everyday soy darkens them.
- Kimchi treated as a single ingredient — fermentation age changes everything. Fresh kimchi is for eating raw; aged/sour kimchi is for stews and pancakes.
- Bulgogi marinade made without Asian pear — works, but the texture and sweetness shift noticeably.
- Banchan-as-appetizers in Western restaurants — they're not appetizers, they're part of the meal eaten alongside everything else.

## Cross-cutting notes for East Asian cooking

**Soy sauce is not soy sauce is not soy sauce.** Chinese light/dark, Japanese koikuchi/usukuchi/tamari, Korean jin-ganjang/guk-ganjang — these are functionally different ingredients with different salt levels, colors, and flavor profiles. When a recipe says "soy sauce," specify which.

**Rice is treated with cuisine-specific discipline.** Long-grain jasmine for southern Chinese, short-grain for Japanese and Korean, glutinous (sticky) for desserts and some Southeast Asian dishes. Washing, soaking, water ratio, and resting all vary.

**The umami architecture differs by cuisine.** Japanese builds it through dashi (clean, precise). Korean builds it through fermentation and yuksu (deep, funky). Chinese builds it through soy, fermented bean pastes, dried seafood, and aromatics layered in stir-fry (varied by region).

**MSG is normal here.** Across all three cuisines, monosodium glutamate (or natural glutamate-rich ingredients like kombu, fermented pastes, and aged soy) is part of the flavor architecture. Western squeamishness about MSG is a cultural artifact, not a culinary judgment.

**Heat levels vary widely.** Cantonese and most Japanese cooking is gentle. Sichuan, Hunan, and most Korean cooking lean spicy — but Korean spice is fruity-warm, Sichuan is numbing-burning, and they are not interchangeable.
