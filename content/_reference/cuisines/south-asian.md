# South Asian Cuisines — Reference Note

South Asian cuisine is enormous and deeply regional. The biggest mistake outside the subcontinent is treating "Indian food" as a single tradition — North and South India share a flag and a few spices but cook in fundamentally different ways. Pakistani, Bangladeshi, Sri Lankan, and Nepali cuisines have their own distinct characters, often closer to a neighboring Indian state than to each other.

The unifying technique across the entire region is **layering of toasted whole and ground spices** in oil or ghee — usually as a *tadka* / *tarka* / *bagar* / *chaunk* (regional names for the same idea: tempering whole spices in hot fat to bloom their aromas, then either using that fat as the cooking base or pouring it over a finished dish). Get tempering right and almost any South Asian dish improves; skip it and even the best ingredients taste flat.

## North India

### What North Indian cuisine actually is

The "Indian food" most familiar in Western restaurants — Punjabi, Mughlai, Kashmiri, and the broader Hindi belt. Wheat-based (roti, naan, paratha) more than rice. Heavier use of dairy (paneer, ghee, cream, yogurt). The Mughal influence brought slow-cooked meats, nuts and dried fruit in savory dishes, and elaborate biryanis.

### Pantry foundations

**Whole spices** for tempering: cumin seeds, mustard seeds (less common than in the South), cardamom (green and black), cloves, cinnamon, bay leaf, dried red chilies. **Ground spices**: coriander, cumin, turmeric, Kashmiri red chili (mild, deep red color — used for color more than heat), garam masala (a finishing blend, not a base spice — added late). **Ghee** (clarified butter) as a primary cooking fat. **Yogurt** (dahi) for marinades, cooling, and curry bases. **Onion-ginger-garlic paste** as the foundation of most curries. **Tomato** (since the Columbian exchange — now structural in dishes like butter chicken and rajma). **Cream and cashew paste** for richness in dishes like korma and butter chicken. **Paneer** (fresh non-melting cheese), **basmati rice** (long-grain, aromatic).

### Defining techniques

**Bhuna** — the slow frying of onion-ginger-garlic-tomato-spice base until the oil separates out (you can see it pool at the edges of the pan). This is the "cooking the masala" step; rushing it produces raw, harsh curries. **Tandoor cooking** — clay oven at very high heat, used for breads (naan), marinated meats (tandoori chicken, seekh kebab), and paneer dishes. The smoky char is hard to replicate at home but a hot oven + cast iron + brief broil approximates it. **Dum cooking** — slow steaming in a sealed pot, used for biryani — rice is layered with parcooked meat, the pot sealed with dough or a tight lid, and finished on low heat so the flavors meld. **Slow simmer of legumes** — dishes like dal makhani are cooked for hours, sometimes overnight, to develop depth.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- "Curry powder" used as a default — there is no such thing in Indian cooking. Curry powder is a British invention. Real Indian cooking uses individual spices and dish-specific blends (garam masala for finishing, sambar powder for sambar, etc.).
- Garam masala added at the start with the other spices — it's a finishing spice, added in the last few minutes so the volatile aromatics don't cook off.
- Skipping the bhuna step to save time — produces a curry that tastes thin and raw, no matter how good the ingredients are.
- Using regular long-grain rice for biryani — basmati is structural; the long, fluffy, separate grains are part of what biryani *is*.
- Substituting Greek yogurt for Indian dahi in cooking — fine flavor-wise but Greek yogurt is more prone to splitting in heat; dahi is looser and more stable, or whisk Greek yogurt with a little water and a pinch of cornstarch.
- Tikka masala or butter chicken treated as ancient Indian classics — both are 20th-century inventions (tikka masala likely Glaswegian, butter chicken Punjabi-Delhi). They're real dishes but not "traditional" in the way they're often presented.

## South India

### What South Indian cuisine actually is

A fundamentally different cuisine from North India. Rice-dominant rather than wheat. Lighter, often vegetarian, built on rice + lentil ferments (idli, dosa, vada), tamarind-soured stews (sambar, rasam), and coconut. Includes Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Kerala, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana — each with its own variations (Chettinad is fierier, Kerala is more coconut-and-seafood, Andhra is famously the spiciest).

### Pantry foundations

**Mustard seeds** (the defining tempering spice — popping them in hot oil is the start of countless dishes), **curry leaves** (fresh, fragrant, not interchangeable with bay leaves — a different plant entirely), **dried red chilies**, **urad dal** and **chana dal** (used as both lentils and as crunchy tempering spices when fried whole), **asafoetida/hing** (sulfurous powder used in tiny amounts to add savory depth, especially in vegetarian cooking — replaces the umami other cuisines get from meat or fermented pastes), **tamarind**, **coconut** (fresh grated, dried, milk, oil), **rice flour** and **rice + urad dal batter** (for dosas and idlis).

Spice blends: **sambar powder** (for sambar), **rasam powder** (for rasam — different from sambar), **chettinad masala**.

### Defining techniques

**Tadka / tempering** is even more central in the South than the North. Mustard seeds, urad dal, dried red chili, curry leaves, sometimes asafoetida — all bloomed in hot oil or ghee for 10-30 seconds, then either used as the cooking base or poured over a finished dish (dal, rasam, vegetables) just before serving. **Fermentation** — idli and dosa batters are fermented overnight; the rise and slight sourness are what define the texture. **Steam cooking** for idli. **Tamarind extraction** — soaking tamarind pulp in warm water, then squeezing and straining for the sour liquid that bases sambar and rasam.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- Curry leaves swapped for bay leaves — completely different plant and flavor. Curry leaves are floral, citrusy, faintly nutty. Bay leaves are camphorous and bitter. Use frozen curry leaves if fresh aren't available.
- Asafoetida omitted — small amount, big function. Replaces the savory depth of onion+garlic in vegetarian cooking; a pinch in oil at the start of a dal completely changes its character.
- Sambar made with garam masala — wrong spice blend; sambar powder is its own thing (coriander-forward, includes fenugreek and roasted lentils).
- Dosa batter made without fermentation — produces a flat crepe, not the airy-crisp dosa with the characteristic sour note.

## Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal

### Pakistan

Closely tied to North Indian Punjabi cuisine but with stronger Mughal/Persian influences and a heavier hand with meat (especially lamb and beef). Defining dishes: nihari (slow-cooked beef stew), haleem (wheat and meat porridge), Karachi-style biryani (spicier than Lucknowi). Stronger emphasis on grilled meats (seekh kebab, chapli kebab, bihari kebab).

### Bangladesh

River-delta cuisine — fish (especially hilsa/ilish), mustard oil, panch phoron (the five-spice tempering blend of nigella, fenugreek, cumin, fennel, mustard — a signature of Bengali and Bangladeshi cooking), green chilies, and a lighter, more delicate hand than its South Asian neighbors. Sweets are a major category (mishti). Mustard oil is a primary cooking fat — pungent when raw, mellower when smoked first.

### Sri Lanka

Distinct from South Indian Tamil cuisine despite proximity. Heavier use of coconut milk and Maldive fish (a smoked, dried tuna), unique spice blend (Sri Lankan curry powder is dark-roasted, much more aromatic and toasty than Indian versions), pandan leaves and rampe. Defining techniques: **kiri hodi** (mild coconut curry), **pol sambol** (raw coconut chili relish), **string hoppers** (steamed rice noodle nests).

### Nepal

Lighter, less spicy than Indian cuisine. Defining dish is **dal bhat** — lentils and rice with vegetables and pickle, eaten daily by most Nepalis. **Momos** (Tibetan-influenced steamed dumplings) are nationally beloved. Less use of cream and rich masalas; more reliance on cumin, coriander, turmeric, and timur (Sichuan-pepper-relative).

## Cross-cutting notes for South Asian cooking

**Tempering is the master technique.** Whether called tadka, tarka, bagar, chaunk, or chhonk, the act of blooming whole spices in hot fat is the move that defines South Asian cooking more than any single ingredient. Get the oil hot, add spices in the right order (slow-bloomers like mustard seeds first, fast-burners like curry leaves last), and the foundation is set.

**There is no "Indian curry powder."** Spice blends are dish-specific and often regional. When a recipe calls for a generic "curry powder," it's a Western shorthand that the dish itself wouldn't use.

**Ghee vs. oil matters.** Ghee (clarified butter) has a distinct nutty richness; mustard oil is pungent; coconut oil is sweet; refined vegetable oil is neutral. The choice shapes the dish — Bengali fish curry in mustard oil tastes different from the same recipe in vegetable oil.

**Yogurt is a cooking ingredient, not a topping.** It bases marinades, splits curries (in a good way — adding tang), and tempers heat. Indian dahi is looser than Greek yogurt; whisk thicker yogurts thin if substituting.

**Heat is regional, not pan-South-Asian.** Andhra and Chettinad cooking are seriously hot. Mughlai, Kashmiri, and Bengali cooking are often quite mild. Heat is not a marker of "authenticity"; it's a regional preference.

**Vegetarianism is structural in much of the region.** A huge proportion of Indian cuisine is vegetarian by default, not by adaptation. The dishes are designed around vegetables, lentils, dairy, and grains — not built as meat dishes with the meat removed.
