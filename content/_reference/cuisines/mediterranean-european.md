# Mediterranean European Cuisines — Reference Note

The Mediterranean European countries share a foundation: extra-virgin olive oil as the primary cooking fat, wheat (bread and pasta) as the staple grain, wine as table beverage and cooking liquid, tomatoes and chilies (post-Columbian additions that became structural), and a deep respect for seasonal, often humble vegetables. The unifying technique is the slow-cooked aromatic base — *soffritto* in Italian, *sofrito* in Spanish, *sofregit* in Catalan — onion (and often garlic, celery, carrot, tomato) sweated in olive oil until it melts into a sweet savory foundation.

But each country has distinct sensibilities, and within each there's strong regional variation. "Italian food" and "Spanish food" are simplifications that obscure most of what's interesting.

## Italy

### What Italian cuisine actually is

Italy is the textbook case of regional cuisine masquerading as national cuisine. The 20 regions cook differently enough that an Italian from Liguria and one from Sicily share fewer dishes than they have distinct ones. A useful split:

**North** (Piedmont, Lombardy, Veneto, Emilia-Romagna): rice (risotto), corn (polenta), butter as primary fat, fresh egg pasta, cured meats (prosciutto, mortadella, culatello), aged cheeses (parmigiano, grana padano, gorgonzola). Cooler climate, more dairy, more meat.

**Central** (Tuscany, Umbria, Lazio, Marche): bread-and-bean cooking (ribollita, panzanella), pecorino, wild herbs and fennel, hearty meat dishes (bistecca fiorentina, cinghiale), simpler tomato sauces.

**South** (Campania, Puglia, Calabria, Sicily): tomatoes (San Marzano), olive oil over butter, dried pasta over fresh, more vegetables, seafood, citrus. Sicilian cuisine has Arab influences (couscous, sweet-and-sour caponata, citrus and pistachios in savory dishes).

The defining sensibility is **restraint and ingredient quality**: a great Italian dish has 4-6 ingredients, all excellent, treated simply. Recipes are remembered as ratios and gestures, not measured to the gram.

### Pantry foundations

**Extra-virgin olive oil** — multiple bottles for different uses (cheaper for cooking, expensive single-estate for finishing). **Tomatoes** — whole peeled San Marzano (look for DOP), passata, tomato paste (concentrato di pomodoro), sun-dried. **Pasta** — bronze-die-extruded (rough surface holds sauce; smooth Teflon-extruded is inferior). **Parmigiano-Reggiano** (24-month minimum, the rind goes into soups and braises). **Prosciutto, pancetta, guanciale** (each different — pancetta is cured belly, guanciale is cured jowl with much more fat and gelatin, used in carbonara and amatriciana). **Anchovies** (in oil, used as a salt-and-umami builder, not as a "fish flavor"). **Capers**, **olives** (regionally specific — Castelvetrano, Cerignola, Taggiasca), **garlic**, **fresh herbs** (basil, parsley, rosemary, sage, oregano in the south). **Dry white wine** (for risotto and braises), **Marsala** (Sicilian fortified wine), **balsamic vinegar** (real aged is from Modena/Reggio Emilia and expensive; commodity "balsamic" is a different product).

### Defining techniques

**Soffritto** — onion (or onion + carrot + celery, the *battuto* / mirepoix) slowly sweated in olive oil until soft and translucent, never browned. The base of countless dishes. **Pasta cooking** — water salted "like the sea" (1-2% salt by weight), pasta cooked al dente, finished in the sauce in the pan with starchy pasta water to emulsify. The sauce *coats* the pasta, doesn't sit on top. **Risotto** — toast the rice in fat, deglaze with wine, add hot stock one ladle at a time, stirring; finish off heat with cold butter and parmigiano (*mantecatura*) for the creamy texture. **Pizza napoletana** — high-hydration dough, long fermentation, very hot oven (450-500°C / 850-900°F traditional), 60-90 second bake. **Acquacotta and ribollita** — bread as a structural ingredient, not a side.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- "Italian seasoning" treated as a default — Italian cooks use individual fresh herbs in specific dishes; a generic dried blend appears nowhere in Italy.
- Garlic browned to deep gold or beyond — garlic in most Italian cooking is sweated to fragrant pale gold; deeply browned garlic is bitter. Burnt garlic is restarted, not pushed through.
- Cream added to carbonara — carbonara is egg yolks, pecorino romano, guanciale, black pepper, pasta water. No cream. The creaminess is from the emulsified eggs and pasta water.
- Pancetta substituted for guanciale in carbonara/amatriciana — works in a pinch but the dish is meaningfully different. Guanciale renders more fat and adds gelatinous richness.
- Pasta rinsed after cooking — washes off the surface starch the sauce needs to cling to.
- Pasta and sauce served separately on the plate — pasta is finished in the sauce in the pan; "spaghetti with sauce on top" is an Italian-American convention.
- "Alfredo sauce" with cream and garlic — real Alfredo is butter + parmigiano + pasta water emulsified into a sauce. Cream-based Alfredo is American.
- Olive oil drizzled on pizza dough or used in pasta water — neither is Italian practice.

## Spain

### What Spanish cuisine actually is

Spain has stronger regional identity than most Western Europeans realize: **Basque** (pintxos, seafood, beef, cider), **Catalan** (Mediterranean + Pyrenees, sofregit-based, fideuà, escudella), **Andalusian** (Moorish-influenced, fried fish, gazpacho, jamón ibérico), **Galician** (Atlantic seafood, octopus, empanadas), **Castilian** (roasts, lamb, suckling pig). Regional languages still align with culinary identity.

The defining sensibility: **bold simplicity** — great olive oil, great salt, great pork, great paprika, used directly. Many famous Spanish dishes have very short ingredient lists.

### Pantry foundations

**Spanish extra-virgin olive oil** (Picual is the dominant variety — bold, peppery, heat-stable). **Pimentón** (smoked paprika — dulce/sweet, agridulce/medium, picante/hot — a defining Spanish spice; Hungarian paprika is not the same). **Saffron** (real saffron threads, not powder). **Sherry vinegar** and **Sherry wine** (Jerez — fino for cooking and aperitif, oloroso for braising, Pedro Ximénez sweet). **Jamón ibérico** and **chorizo** (Spanish chorizo is cured and ready-to-eat; Mexican chorizo is fresh ground sausage — completely different). **Bomba/Calasparra rice** (short-grain, absorbs liquid without going mushy — for paella). **Manchego cheese**, **piquillo peppers**, **boquerones** (white anchovies in vinegar — different from oil-cured anchovies), **canned tuna and seafood** (a serious category — preserved bonito and mussels are gourmet items).

### Defining techniques

**Sofrito** — slow-cooked tomato + onion + garlic + olive oil base, often with green pepper. The foundation of paella, fideuà, and many stews. **Paella** — short-grain rice cooked in a wide flat pan with no stirring, building the **socarrat** (the crust of caramelized rice on the bottom). Heat distribution and rice-to-liquid ratio are everything. **Tortilla española** — slow-cooked potato + onion + egg, slid out, flipped, finished — texture should be just-set, not dry. **Cured pork tradition** — jamón, lomo, chorizo, morcilla — entire culinary subculture.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- Mexican chorizo substituted for Spanish chorizo — different products. Mexican is fresh, Spanish is cured. Not interchangeable.
- Hungarian or generic paprika substituted for pimentón — the smoke is the point. Use real pimentón de la Vera.
- Long-grain or basmati rice used for paella — bomba or calasparra absorbs differently and produces the right texture; long-grain produces wet, gummy paella.
- Paella stirred during cooking — never. The whole point is the socarrat.
- Saffron skipped or substituted with turmeric — turmeric gives color, not flavor. Saffron's musk is irreplaceable; if you can't afford it, pick a different dish.
- Sangria treated as quintessentially Spanish — it's mostly drunk by tourists. Tinto de verano (red wine + lemon soda) is the actual Spanish summer drink.

## Portugal

### What Portuguese cuisine actually is

Often lumped with Spanish but quite distinct. Heavily Atlantic — bacalhau (salt cod) is the national obsession, with hundreds of preparations. Cilantro/coriander is used extensively (unusual in Iberian cooking). African and Brazilian influences via colonial routes (piri-piri chili, malagueta). Olive oil and lard both used.

### Pantry foundations

**Bacalhau** (salt cod — must be soaked 24-48 hours, water changed several times, before use), **piri-piri** (small hot chili, fresh or in sauce form), **cilantro/coentros**, **garlic**, **olive oil**, **paprika** (often sweet, not smoked like Spanish), **port wine** and **Madeira** (for cooking too, not just drinking), **white wine vinegar**, **massa de pimentão** (red pepper paste, a foundational seasoning).

### Defining techniques

**Bacalhau preparation** — desalting via long cold-water soak before any cooking; never boiled in fresh water (poached gently or shredded raw). **Cataplana cooking** — clamshell-shaped copper pan that traps steam, used for seafood stews. **Frango piri-piri** — butterflied chicken, marinated and grilled with piri-piri marinade.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- Bacalhau cooked without soaking — inedibly salty.
- Portuguese piri-piri sauce treated as the same as Nando's commercial product — Nando's is an interpretation; traditional piri-piri is fresher and brighter.

## Greece

### What Greek cuisine actually is

Greek cooking is built on **lathera** (literally "with oil") — vegetables slow-cooked in generous olive oil with herbs, often served at room temperature. This is the everyday cooking of Greece, not the moussaka-and-souvlaki taverna menu. Add **wild greens** (horta, foraged), **legumes** (gigantes, fava — which in Greek means split yellow peas, not the bean), **filo pastry traditions** (spanakopita, tiropita), **lamb and goat** more than beef, and **dairy** (feta, yogurt, kasseri, graviera).

### Pantry foundations

**Greek extra-virgin olive oil** (Koroneiki olive — fruity, peppery, very high in polyphenols), **feta** (real feta is sheep's milk PDO; cow's milk "feta-style" is a different product), **Greek yogurt** (strained, much thicker than American "Greek yogurt"), **dried oregano** (the wild rigani is more pungent than Italian oregano), **lemon** (used heavily — lemon juice is a finishing seasoning more than a marinade), **garlic**, **mint and dill**, **bay leaves**, **kalamata olives**, **bulgur wheat**, **filo pastry**, **honey** (Greek thyme honey is special).

### Defining techniques

**Lathera cooking** — vegetables cooked low and slow in a lot of olive oil with tomato, onion, herbs; served warm or room temp. Green beans (fasolakia), okra (bamies), peas (arakas) all get this treatment. **Avgolemono** — egg-and-lemon sauce/soup base, tempering eggs with hot broth then lemon, used to finish soups and stews. **Slow-roast lamb** (kleftiko, traditionally cooked in a sealed clay pot). **Filo work** — layered with melted butter or olive oil, baked until shatteringly crisp.

### Common Western drift to be aware of

- "Greek salad" with lettuce — horiatiki is tomato, cucumber, onion, olives, feta, oregano, olive oil. No lettuce. Lettuce is American.
- Cow's milk feta substituted for sheep's milk PDO feta — different texture (crumblier), different flavor (less tangy-funky). Use real feta.
- Greek yogurt thinned to drinking consistency for dressings — Greek yogurt is structural; if a recipe calls for it thick (tzatziki, dips), don't thin it.
- Olive oil used sparingly in lathera dishes — these dishes are *defined* by being cooked in a lot of oil. Cutting the oil cuts the cuisine.
- Italian oregano substituted for Greek rigani — Greek is more pungent and floral; Italian is milder. In a pinch, use 1.5x the amount of Italian.

## Cross-cutting notes for Mediterranean European cooking

**Olive oil is a cooking ingredient, not just a finisher.** The notion that EVOO is "too good to cook with" is a recent American idea. Across Spain, Italy, Greece, and Portugal, EVOO is the everyday cooking fat, used generously and at heat. Buy good oil and use it; reserve the most expensive single-estate stuff for raw applications.

**Tomatoes vary in quality dramatically.** Out-of-season fresh tomatoes are usually worse than good canned. San Marzano DOP for sauces, fresh only when in season.

**Bread is a structural ingredient.** Across the Mediterranean, stale bread isn't waste — it's the base of soups (ribollita, sopa de ajo, açorda), salads (panzanella), and pastes (skordalia). Don't throw it out.

**Cheese is regional and rules-bound.** Real DOP/PDO cheeses (Parmigiano-Reggiano, Pecorino Romano, real feta, Manchego) have legal definitions for a reason. The non-DOP imitations are different products.

**The aromatic base differs by country but follows the same logic.** Italian soffritto, Spanish sofrito, Catalan sofregit, French mirepoix — all are slow-cooked aromatic vegetables in fat as a foundation. The specifics (with or without tomato, with or without bell pepper, sweated vs. browned) define the cuisine.

**Wine is a cooking ingredient.** Dry white for delicate dishes, red for braises, fortified (sherry, Marsala, port) for specific traditional dishes. "Cooking wine" with added salt is not a thing serious cooks use.
