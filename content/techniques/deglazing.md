about: |
  After a sear, the pan holds fond — the brown crust of protein and sugar residue stuck to the bottom. Fond is concentrated Maillard product. Pour liquid in, scrape, and the fond dissolves into the liquid, transferring those flavor compounds out of the pan and into what becomes the sauce. Skip the deglaze and you discard most of the flavor you spent the sear creating.

  Acid helps. Wine, vinegar, citrus, even tomato — the acid attacks the proteins binding fond to metal and the residue lifts faster and more completely than with plain stock or water. A splash of dry white before the stock goes in is a common move for exactly this reason.

  The cue is visual and tactile. The brown layer lifts cleanly under a wooden spoon, the liquid darkens within seconds, and as it reduces it thickens and gains body. If the liquid stays pale and thin, either the pan wasn't hot enough to mobilize the fond or there wasn't enough fond there to begin with — go back and brown harder next time.

  Failure modes are sharp. If the pan was too hot when liquid hit, the fond burns instantly to bitter and the sauce is unrecoverable. Pull the pan briefly off heat before adding cold liquid. The other failure is upstream: if the fond burned during the sear (black, not mahogany), there's nothing to recover — start over with a fresh pan. Use stainless or carbon steel so you can see the fond. Non-stick deflects the whole technique; nothing meaningful sticks, so nothing meaningful lifts.
