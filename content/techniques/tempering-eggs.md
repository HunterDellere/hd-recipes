title: 'Tempering eggs'
desc: 'Equalize egg temperature with a hot liquid in stages so the proteins set smoothly instead of curdling.'
metaDesc: 'Tempering eggs: the denaturation kinetics, the staged-addition ratio, the sensory cue for a smooth custard, and what scrambled curd looks like.'
tags: ['egg', 'custard']
updated: '2026-04-26'

about: |
  Egg proteins — primarily ovalbumin in the white, livetins and lipovitellins in the yolk — denature in a tight band: ~62°C for yolks, ~65°C for whites. Hit that band gradually and the proteins unfold and link into a smooth gel. Hit it as a shock (raw egg dumped into 90°C milk) and the unfolding outpaces the linking; the proteins clump into discrete curds while the surrounding liquid stays watery. That's curdling, and once the curds form they don't redissolve.

  The fix is staging. Whisk a ladle (~60g) of the hot liquid into the beaten eggs, then another, then another, raising the egg temperature in steps before returning the mixture to the pot. A working ratio is 1 part hot to 1 part egg per addition for the first three additions, then larger pours once the egg is above 50°C. The cue is texture: the mixture thickens to coat the back of a spoon (nappé) at ~82°C and a finger drawn across leaves a clean line. Pull immediately; the carry-over will push you another 2-3°C, which is fine, and another 30 seconds on heat will scramble it.

  Failure mode: scrambled custard. Visible yellow curds suspended in thin liquid. The only fix is a fine-mesh sieve, which removes the curds but also removes most of the body, so you end up with sweetened milk. A heavy saucepan, a flat-edged wooden spoon (to scrape the corners where heat concentrates), and an instant-read thermometer turn this from a guess into a process. Off the heat, strain into a cold bowl set over ice to stop the cook.
