  Comal charring is the Mexican technique of blackening vegetables, dried or fresh chilies, tomatoes, tomatillos, whole onion halves, garlic cloves, or corn tortillas on a dry, very hot, flat metal cooking surface. The traditional vessel is the comal ; a flat round griddle of clay, cast iron, or carbon steel, sized 25 to 40 cm in diameter, used over an open burner or in the embers of a wood fire. In a modern home kitchen, a heavy cast iron skillet, a carbon-steel paella pan, or a flat-top griddle accomplishes the same chemistry; the only critical traits are flatness, heavy thermal mass (at least 3 mm of metal), and the ability to hold 230 to 320 °C surface temperature for the duration of the char without scorching the vessel itself. Thin aluminum, non-stick, and enameled cast iron are not appropriate; thin aluminum spikes-and-drops in temperature, non-stick coatings degrade above 230 °C, and enamel chips off under the direct radiative heat.

  The chemistry. Comal charring works because the absence of water or fat as an intermediary lets the vegetable surface climb past the Maillard reaction window (140 to 200 °C) and into the pyrolysis window (200 to 320 °C) where carbohydrate and protein molecules in the surface tissue thermally decompose into pyrazines, furans, and nitrogen-containing aromatic compounds. In wet or oily cooking, the surface temperature is held near the boiling point of the medium (100 °C for water, 180 to 230 °C for typical cooking oils) and the surface develops light browning at most. On a dry hot comal, the surface dries within seconds (most vegetables have only a few percent surface moisture once placed on a hot pan), the temperature climbs into pyrolysis range, and the resulting char is structurally different from a sauté or roast. The black-mottled surface of a comal-charred tomatillo or chili is not "burnt" in the failure-mode sense; it is the intentional product of controlled pyrolysis.

  Surface temperature targets by ingredient. Whole tomatillos and small tomatoes: 260 to 290 °C surface, 8 to 12 minutes total with rotation, until the skin is mostly black-mottled and the flesh has begun to soften (gentle pressure with tongs yields). Whole chilies (jalapeño, serrano, habanero, poblano): 230 to 260 °C, 6 to 10 minutes total with rotation, until the skin is black-and-blistered and easily peelable. Dried chilies (ancho, guajillo, pasilla, chipotle, mulato, árbol): 130 to 160 °C, 30 to 60 seconds per side, until the chili is fragrant and slightly pliable; this is far cooler than the fresh-vegetable char because dried chilies pyrolyze quickly and turn acrid past 90 seconds. Whole garlic cloves (skin on): 200 to 230 °C, 12 to 18 minutes with frequent rotation, until the skin is fully blackened and the clove is soft. White onion halves: 230 to 260 °C, 10 to 14 minutes cut-side-down, until the cut face is black-and-mahogany and the onion is tender. Nopal paddles: 230 to 260 °C, 4 to 5 minutes per side, until the surface develops the dark mottled lacquer that the mucilage-and-Maillard chemistry produces. Corn tortillas: 230 to 260 °C, 30 to 45 seconds per side, until the tortilla puffs slightly and develops a few small charred spots.

  Constant motion is not the rule. Unlike dry-toasting (where the small high-aroma ingredients require constant motion to prevent localized scorching), comal charring of larger vegetables requires deliberate stillness for periods of 2 to 4 minutes per face. The contact face needs uninterrupted time at the pan surface to develop the deep blackening; tossing or rotating too early breaks the seal between vegetable and pan and produces a pale, patchy char rather than the deep mahogany-and-black target. Rotate vegetables only after the contact face has reached its target color; for chilies and tomatillos, this is typically 2 to 4 minutes per face; for nopal paddles, 4 to 5 minutes per face; for garlic and onion, 3 to 4 minutes per face. The exception is dried chilies, which pyrolyze quickly and require the constant-motion approach of dry-toasting.

  Smoke is the working condition, not a failure. Comal charring at 230 to 320 °C produces visible smoke; the smoke is the volatilized pyrolysis products (a mix of pyrazines, furans, phenolic compounds from the lignin in chili stems and pepper skins, and traces of cyanide-related compounds at very high temperatures that are below toxicological concern at culinary scale). The smoke is the dish's flavor argument, not a problem; ventilate the kitchen aggressively (open windows, run the hood at maximum) and accept the smoke as part of the cooking. A comal char with no smoke means the surface is too cool and the char is incomplete. Genuine failure is acrid black smoke (pyrolysis past the pyrazine window into pure carbon, which produces bitter rather than savory flavor) or sustained flame (vegetable juice ignition, which can happen with high-sugar tomatoes and tomatillos at the upper end of the temperature range); pull the ingredient off the heat at the first hint of either.

  Inflection points and failure modes. The window between properly charred and burnt is roughly 60 to 90 seconds at the upper temperature range, narrower at the lower range. For tomatillos and tomatoes, 60 seconds late and the skin moves from intentional pyrolysis to a bitter carbon char that contaminates the salsa; the cue is the smell turning from charred-vegetable to acrid-smoke. For chilies, 30 seconds late and the chili turns acrid; the cue is the smoke turning from gray-vegetable to white-acrid. For dried chilies, the window is 15 to 20 seconds, which is why the lower temperature and constant motion are required. For garlic, the cue is the cloves yielding to gentle pressure; the skin can blacken further safely as long as the inside is soft. For onion halves, the cue is the cut face going beyond mahogany to deep black; pull at the first hint of acrid smoke.

  Pan choice and preheat. The single biggest source of failed comal charring at home is insufficient preheat. The pan should preheat for 4 to 6 minutes over medium-high heat before any ingredient touches it; a flick of water should snap and evaporate within 2 seconds. A cold pan plus cold vegetables means the system spends 5 to 8 minutes climbing toward Maillard temperature, the surface moisture from the vegetables steams off rather than charring, and the result is a pale, patchy, sad version of the technique. The cast iron or carbon steel skillet wins over the comal-clay vessel in a modern kitchen because the higher thermal mass of the cast iron buffers the temperature spike-and-drop when room-temperature vegetables hit the surface; the clay comal, while traditional, requires a more practiced cook to manage the temperature.

  Post-char handling. Charred chilies and tomatillos go into a sealed bag or covered bowl for 5 to 10 minutes immediately after pulling from the heat; the trapped steam loosens the blackened skin from the flesh and lets it peel off cleanly (or be left on for rustic-style salsas; both are legitimate). Charred garlic cloves are squeezed out of their burnt skins like soft paste; the inside is sweet, rich, and fully cooked. Charred whole onion halves are roughly chopped or pulsed in a food processor; the cut face often retains the deepest char. Charred nopales rest 3 minutes on a cutting board and are then sliced; no peeling is needed because the lacquered surface is the dish's intended flavor. Charred dried chilies are immediately rehydrated in hot water for 15 to 20 minutes before grinding into pastes for adobos, mole, and salsas roja.

  Applications by category. Mexican salsas (salsa verde with charred tomatillo, garlic, and serrano; salsa roja with charred tomato, garlic, and onion; salsa morita with charred chipotle morita; the "salsa quemada" subcategory specifically built around the char). Mexican mole bases (charred dried chilies, charred garlic, charred onion as the foundation of the spice paste). Mexican ensaladas (ensalada de nopales with charred paddles; ensalada de pepino with charred jalapeño). Mexican tortilla warming (the same comal at lower temperature for warming corn tortillas before they hit the table). Mexican adobo and pibil pastes (charred dried chilies and aromatics ground with vinegar and citrus). Mexican-American Tex-Mex grilling (charred poblano for chiles rellenos, charred jalapeño for taco-bar condiments). The technique is regionally Mexican and southwestern American; analogous techniques exist in other cuisines (Indian charred-tomato chutney, Levantine charred-eggplant baba ghanoush, Iranian charred-tomato kuku) but with different aromatic targets and different vessels.
