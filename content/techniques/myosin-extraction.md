about: |
  Myosin extraction is the technique that gives a ground-meat preparation a springy, snappy bite instead of a crumbly one. It is the difference between a Chinese pork meatball or a Teochew fish ball, which bounce, and a loose Italian-American meatball bound with soaked bread, which falls apart on the fork. The two are built on opposite principles: the Western meatball uses a panade (starch and dairy) to interrupt the protein network and stay tender, while the bouncy-school preparation deliberately builds that network as strong and continuous as it can.

  The mechanism is salt acting on muscle protein. Myosin is the dominant contractile protein in muscle, and it is salt-soluble: at the ionic strength produced by roughly 1 to 1.5% salt by weight of the meat, myosin dissolves out of the muscle fibers into a sticky, extractable form. Mechanical work, stirring or kneading the salted mince hard in a single direction, shears those freed myosin molecules into aligned strands. When the mass then hits heat, the strands denature and cross-link into a continuous three-dimensional gel that traps water and fat in its matrix. That gel is what holds the bounce, the gloss, and the juice; an under-worked or under-salted mince has no network and renders dry and loose.

  The visible cue is the only one that matters: work the mince until it turns tacky and visibly pulls against the spoon or your hand, a drag that was not there when you started. Stirring in one direction (rather than folding randomly) aligns the strands and builds the network faster. Two to four minutes of hard work by hand is typical for a 500 g batch; a stand mixer with the paddle does it in under two. Stop when the mass is cohesive and sticky enough to hold a peak.

  Salt level and timing are the primary controls. Below about 0.8% salt the myosin does not solubilize enough to bind; above about 2% the texture turns rubbery and the seasoning reads harsh. Salt the meat first and work it before adding fat or liquid seasonings, because fat coats the protein and water dilutes the ionic strength, both of which blunt extraction. Cold is the second control: the mince should stay below roughly 12C / 54F throughout, since warm fat smears and the emulsion of fat-in-protein-gel breaks, leaving a greasy, grainy result. Chill the bowl, work fast, and rest the bound mince in the fridge before shaping.

  Baking soda is a common partner and does a different job. A small dose (around ¼ tsp per 450 g) raises the meat's pH, which both increases water-holding capacity and keeps the actin-myosin proteins from ratcheting as tightly during cooking, so the network reads springy rather than dense and tight. It is optional; salt and work alone build bounce, but the soda widens the margin between snappy and rubbery.

  The technique spans cuisines and formats. Chinese pork meatballs and lion's-head meatballs rely on it for their characteristic Q texture; so do Teochew and Cantonese fish balls and beef balls, where surimi-style fish or beef paste is salted and slapped against the bowl until it can be squeezed into a bouncing sphere. Vietnamese gio lua (pork sausage) and cha ca (fish cake) are the Southeast Asian expression of the same principle. Western fresh sausage and the bind in a fine emulsion sausage (frankfurter, mortadella) use identical chemistry: salt extracts myosin, the work builds the bind, and cold keeps the fat emulsion intact. Across all of these, the move is the same, salt then work then keep cold, even though the protein and the dish change.

  Where it does not apply: any preparation where tenderness and crumb are the goal rather than bounce. A meatloaf, a Bolognese, a soft Italian polpette, and a burger patty are all better off under-worked, salted late or at the table, and bound (if at all) with a panade. Working salt into ground beef for a burger and then cooking it produces the dense, springy, hot-dog texture of a supermarket "seasoned" patty, which is exactly the wrong result for a burger and exactly the right one for a bouncy ball.
