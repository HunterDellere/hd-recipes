about: |
  Yogurt is a casein gel suspended in whey. Casein micelles are stabilized by surface kappa-casein and by their negative charge at the gel's natural pH (around 4.4). Heat does two destructive things to that gel. Above roughly 85 °C the kappa-casein layer denatures and the micelles aggregate into visible curds, exactly the response you want when making cheese and exactly the response that ruins a sauce. And the same heat drives the whey out as a thin grey-yellow liquid, which separates from the curds and leaves the dish broken: chunks of casein sitting in a watery medium. A cooked yogurt sauce is the set of techniques that prevent this aggregation while still allowing the yogurt to integrate into a hot dish at simmer temperatures.

  Three stabilizers, used singly or in combination. The first is starch. A 1–2% cornstarch slurry (10–20 g per 1 kg yogurt) hydrates as the sauce heats, forming a gel matrix around the casein micelles that physically blocks aggregation. Cooks with access to cornstarch at all use this; rice flour and arrowroot work similarly. The second is egg yolk. Yolk lecithin and lipoproteins coat the casein micelles and act as an additional stabilizing emulsifier, the same logic that makes yolk-thickened sauces (avgolemono, hollandaise) hold. One yolk per 250 g of yogurt is the working dose. The third is hydrated whey or pre-fermented yogurt protein in concentrated form, which is what jameed (see ingredients/jameed) brings already built in: the casein has partially hydrolyzed during the long air-ferment, so the protein is more heat-tolerant from the start. Live yogurt without one of these stabilizers will curdle regardless of how careful the cook is.

  Tempering is non-negotiable. Cold yogurt (whether fresh or reconstituted from jameed) dropped into a hot sauce shocks the casein into immediate aggregation at the contact zone, no matter what stabilizer is in the yogurt. The fix is to ladle hot cooking liquid into the yogurt in 100–200 ml increments, whisking constantly, until the yogurt has reached 60–70 °C and roughly doubled in volume. Only then does the warmed yogurt go back into the pot. This thermal-bridge step is identical in mechanism to tempering eggs (techniques/tempering-eggs); the casein simply has a wider failure window and demands more increments to clear it.

  Stir constantly, in one direction, until the sauce comes back to a tremor. The mechanical motion keeps the surface from forming a casein skin and keeps any localized hot spots from passing the curdle threshold while the bulk catches up. The "one direction" rule that appears in regional traditions (clockwise in Jordanian mansaf practice, for instance) has no chemistry behind direction per se; it is a practical instruction to keep the cook from reversing motion and creating shear zones where curdling can nucleate. A wooden spoon flat-bladed against the pot floor and walls is the right tool. Whisks aerate, which is the wrong move once the sauce is integrated.

  Hold at a tremor, never a boil. The sauce should bubble lazily, with surface ripples and an occasional break, at roughly 88–92 °C. Above 95 °C the casein starts to fail even with starch and yolk in place; a hard boil will break the sauce in seconds. This is the same temperature window as a slow lamb braise, which is convenient: yogurt-stewed lamb dishes simmer at the right temperature for both the meat and the sauce.

  Acid timing. The acid in many yogurt-cooked dishes (lemon, vinegar, sometimes sour green grape verjuice) goes in after the yogurt has been integrated and the sauce has come back to a tremor. Adding acid into raw yogurt before stabilization drops the pH further and accelerates aggregation; adding it into the integrated stable sauce simply seasons. The exception is when the recipe calls for acid as part of the pre-blended yogurt (some kadhi traditions, some Turkish yogurt soups), in which case the cook is relying on the starch matrix to hold against the lower pH and the dose has been calibrated against that.

  Where the technique applies. Yogurt-stewed lamb across the Levant: Jordanian mansaf with reconstituted jameed (recipes/mansaf), Levantine and Syrian shakriyeh with fresh yogurt and cornstarch, similar dishes in Iraq and the Gulf. Yogurt soups: Turkish yayla çorbası (rice and mint with yogurt), Persian ash-e mast, Greek avgolemono (egg-yogurt-lemon finish on chicken stock). Indian kadhi and Punjabi karhi (yogurt thickened with chickpea flour, which serves both as starch stabilizer and primary thickener). Pakistani and Mughal yogurt-cooked curries (some korma styles). Anywhere fresh yogurt or jameed is meant to be the simmering medium rather than a finishing dollop, the cook is doing some version of this technique.

  Failure modes and recovery. Sauce broken into curds and watery whey at the start of integration: the yogurt was added too cold or too fast, or the pot was too hot. Off heat, take a stick blender to the broken sauce until smooth; it will not be perfect but it is rescuable. Sauce thinned and weeping after a long hold: the bond between casein and water has failed at the molecular level and a blender will not fix it. The dish is still edible, the texture is wrong. Sauce reading floury and gluey: too much starch. Cut with a small splash of stock or water and stir hard. Sauce reading thin and curdled at the surface only: skin formation, not full failure. Skim the skin, stir, drop the heat, continue.

  When NOT to use this technique. Drizzling cold yogurt onto a finished dish at the table (Turkish manti, Persian khoresh-e bademjan as served, Indian raita) is not a cooked yogurt sauce; the yogurt is a finishing condiment and stays raw. Beating yogurt into a marinade for tandoor-style cooking is also outside the scope; the marinade does not go through a sauce-integration step. Reach for this technique only when the recipe calls for yogurt to simmer as a structural component of the dish.
