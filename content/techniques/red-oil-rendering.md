  Red oil rendering (or "blooming the doubanjiang") is the structural starting move of most Sichuan stir-fries and dry-pot dishes. Doubanjiang (Pixian fermented chili-bean paste) is bloomed in oil at controlled temperature; the chili-pigment carotenoids and aromatic compounds dissolve into the oil and turn it brilliant brick-red. That red oil is then the cooking medium for everything that follows: the doubanjiang is no longer a separate flavor on top of the dish but a base color and base aroma running through every bite. Skip this step or shortcut it, and the same dish reads under-flavored, paler in color, and structurally hollow.

  The mechanism. The carotenoid pigments in dried chilies (capsanthin, capsorubin, beta-carotene) and the aromatic compounds in fermented broad beans (free amino acids, methyl ketones, pyrazines) are fat-soluble. They are concentrated in the doubanjiang paste in a tightly-bound state; gentle heat in oil dissolves them out of the paste and into the oil. Once in the oil, they distribute evenly across whatever cooks in the wok next, contributing color and flavor to every component of the dish. The temperature window is narrow: hot enough to extract (140 to 160°C / 285 to 320°F), but not so hot that the chili pigments scorch into bitterness (above 175°C / 350°F).

  Method. Heat a wok or wide skillet over medium heat. Add neutral oil (the volume depends on the dish: 30 to 50 ml / 2 to 3 tbsp for a stir-fry, 80 to 120 ml / ⅓ to ½ cup for a dry-pot or mapo tofu base). When the oil is shimmering but not smoking, visually around 140°C, add the doubanjiang. Break it up with a spatula immediately so the paste disperses through the oil rather than sitting in one chunk. Cook 90 seconds to 2 minutes, stirring continuously, until: (1) the oil has turned brilliant brick-red, distinct from the duller red of the paste itself; (2) the kitchen smells distinctly of toasted chili and bean paste, with a slight savory-sweet edge; (3) tiny bubbles form at the edges of the paste pieces as residual moisture cooks off. At this point, add dried chilies (broken into 2 cm pieces, seeds in or out per preference) and Sichuan peppercorns if the dish calls for them. Cook 30 seconds; the chilies will darken slightly and become aromatic. Then add aromatic alliums (ginger, garlic, scallion whites) and cook 30 seconds before adding any wet ingredients.

  Temperature is the entire technique. Below 140°C / 285°F: the paste sits in the oil but does not bloom. The oil stays a dull yellow-orange, the doubanjiang flavor stays inside the paste rather than extracting into the medium, and the dish reads under-flavored. Visual cue for too cold: oil looks the same color it did before the paste went in. Solution: raise the heat slightly and continue rendering until the oil clearly turns red. Between 140 and 160°C / 285 to 320°F: the sweet spot. Oil turns red, paste flavor extracts, no scorching. The kitchen smells of chili, bean paste, and a faint cocoa-roast note. Above 175°C / 350°F: the chili pigments scorch into bitter compounds and the oil goes brown rather than red. The kitchen smells acrid and burnt. The dish is unsalvageable; start over.

  Doubanjiang quantity scales with dish format. A stir-fry for 4 people typically needs 25 to 40 g doubanjiang. A dry-pot needs 30 to 50 g. Mapo tofu needs 35 to 50 g. Twice-cooked pork needs 25 to 40 g. The oil quantity scales with the doubanjiang: roughly 2 mL oil per gram of doubanjiang for a stir-fry (so 50 mL oil for 25 g paste), or up to 3 mL per gram for dry-pot and mapo (so 100 mL for 35 g paste). The higher oil ratios for dry-pot are because the rendered oil is the entire liquid component of the dish; there is no broth or sauce diluting it later.

  When to add the dried chilies. Chinese cooking typically adds whole or broken dried chilies during the rendering step, after the doubanjiang has bloomed for 60 to 90 seconds. The chilies contribute additional color (especially for da hong pao) and an aromatic top-note that complements the deeper doubanjiang base. Alternative: render the doubanjiang and the chilies separately and combine; this gives more control but is more steps. Default to combined rendering for routine cooking.

  When the technique applies vs when it does not. Red oil rendering is the right opening move for: Sichuan dry-pot of any protein, mapo tofu, twice-cooked pork, fish-fragrant eggplant or pork (yu xiang), Sichuan boiled fish (shui zhu yu), Sichuan-style braised tofu, and any dish where doubanjiang is a structural component. It is not the right move for: Sichuan dishes that don't use doubanjiang (kung pao chicken, dan dan noodles in some traditions, tea-smoked duck), non-Sichuan Chinese cooking (Cantonese stir-fries, Northern dumpling fillings), or any dish where the doubanjiang is added at the end as a finishing flavor rather than the cooking medium.

  Failure modes. Doubanjiang piece sitting whole in the oil without breaking up: the oil reddens unevenly and the dish has hot-spots of paste with bland zones around them. Solution: break up the paste with the spatula immediately as it goes in. Too-hot oil scorching the paste before extraction completes: the dish goes bitter from burnt-chili compounds. Solution: lower heat, start over. Too-cold oil with paste sitting unmoving: nothing extracts. Solution: raise heat carefully. Adding wet ingredients (soy, vinegar, water) into the rendering step before the oil has reddened: the moisture drops the temperature and arrests the extraction. Solution: complete the render fully before any wet ingredients enter the wok.
