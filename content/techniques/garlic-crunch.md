about: |
  Sliced garlic in hot oil cooks in two phases. First the water in the cells boils off — the slices bubble vigorously, the oil sounds like rain on a tin roof, and nothing changes color for 60-90 seconds. Once the water is gone, the cell-wall sugars (mostly fructans) and the few free amino acids hit the dehydrated surface temperature of the oil and Maillard browning starts. The slices go pale gold, then straw, then amber in quick succession. Amber is the target: maximum nutty-sweet flavor, full crunch, no bitter edge.

  The window from straw to burnt is roughly five seconds, which is the entire skill of the technique. Pull the pan off the heat at first amber — the residual heat in the oil and the slices themselves will carry the cook another shade darker, which is exactly the color you want on the plate. If you wait until the slices look right in the pan, they will be black by the time you get them out. Lift the slices onto a paper towel with a slotted spoon or a fine spider; leaving them in the cooling oil keeps cooking them.

  Failure mode: burnt garlic. The slices go from amber to mahogany to black in single-digit seconds, and the bitterness is not subtle — it's the dominant flavor of the oil and any dish that touches it. The bitter compounds (acrylamide and Maillard end-products taken too far) are heat-stable and not rescuable; the batch is done, oil included. Use a wide, light-colored skillet so you can see the color shift through the bubbling oil — a dark cast-iron pan hides the cue. Slice the garlic uniformly thin (1-2mm); thicker slices and slivers brown unevenly and the thin edges burn while the centers are still pale.
