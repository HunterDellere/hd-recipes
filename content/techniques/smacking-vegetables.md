about: |
  A clean knife cut slices between cells; a smack with the broad side of a cleaver bursts them. Bursting matters because most of a vegetable's water and dissolved solutes sit inside the central vacuole of each cell, a membrane-bound sac that a clean cut leaves intact. Rupturing those vacuoles does two things at once. It dumps cell water onto the surface, where it can be drawn off by salt. And it creates a torn, irregular surface area that's many times larger than a cut face, with pockets and fissures that a sauce can physically lodge in. A smashed cucumber holds chili crisp the way a sliced one cannot.

  The sequence after the smack is osmotic. Salt the broken vegetable and let it sit fifteen to thirty minutes; the salt creates a concentration gradient that pulls more water out of the partially ruptured cells, concentrating the vegetable's own flavor and leaving the tissue ready to absorb dressing instead of dilute it. The cue is pooled liquid in the bowl and a slight slump in the vegetable's structure: cucumbers go from rigid to pliable, the visible surface looks glossy with exuded water. Drain that liquid before saucing or the dressing will be diluted into broth.

  Failure mode is under-smacking — a few timid taps that crack the skin but leave most cells intact. The vegetable then sits in the dressing without absorbing it, salt water pools at the bottom of the bowl, and the dish tastes like sauce next to vegetable instead of vegetable carrying sauce. Use a cleaver or the flat of a chef's knife and commit to the strike; the vegetable should split audibly and visibly into rough chunks, not slices. Tear by hand from there rather than re-cutting with a blade; the torn edges hold sauce better than clean ones.
