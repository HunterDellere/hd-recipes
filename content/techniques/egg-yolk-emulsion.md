about: |
  Egg-yolk emulsions are the most stable kitchen emulsions short of industrial homogenization. They are the structural mechanism behind mayonnaise (cold, vinegar-based), aioli (cold, garlic-and-olive-oil-based), hollandaise (warm, butter-based, acid-tightened with lemon), béarnaise (hollandaise plus tarragon-shallot reduction), and Caesar dressing (cold to warm, anchovy-and-lemon-based). All five sauces are the same emulsion engineering problem solved with different fat, different acid, and different aromatic load. A cook who understands the yolk-emulsion mechanism can write any of them from scratch and rebuild them when they break.

  Lecithin is the active ingredient. Egg yolk is roughly 30 percent fat, 16 percent protein, and 50 percent water by weight; the fat fraction includes about 10 percent lecithin (a phospholipid) plus cholesterol and triglycerides. Lecithin is the canonical biological surfactant: each molecule has a hydrophilic phosphate head and two hydrophobic fatty-acid tails, so it sits at the interface between water droplets and oil droplets and stabilizes the boundary against coalescence. One yolk contains roughly 1.5 g of lecithin, which is enough to stabilize a vast quantity of dispersed oil: a single yolk can hold 200 to 400 g of oil in stable mayonnaise emulsion if the build is paced correctly. By comparison, dijon mustard (the standard vinaigrette emulsifier) holds about a tenth as much oil per unit and breaks within an hour; a yolk emulsion holds for a day to several days, refrigerated.

  The water phase is built first, complete, before any oil enters. Yolks plus salt plus acid (vinegar, lemon, mustard) plus any dissolved aromatics (garlic, anchovy, herbs). The salt and acid serve two functions simultaneously: they season the sauce and they partially denature the yolk proteins, which exposes more emulsifying sites and produces a more stable colloid. A mayonnaise built without acid in the yolk-and-salt phase is a thinner, less stable sauce than one built with the lemon or vinegar already integrated. Mustard, when present, multiplies the emulsion power by adding mucilage and Pickering particles to the yolk lecithin alone.

  The oil stream is the second phase. Add the oil in a thin thread at first; the slowest part of the build, the part most often rushed. Each new droplet of oil entering the yolk-and-acid base must be fully coated by lecithin before the next droplet arrives, or the new droplet coalesces with the old one and the emulsion stays as visible oil layers rather than a dispersed colloid. Whisking provides the mechanical shear that breaks the incoming oil into small droplets; small droplets stay suspended longer and cream less than large ones. Once the emulsion has visibly tightened (about a third of the oil incorporated), the cook can switch from a thread to a slow drizzle and the emulsion accepts oil faster as the population of stabilized droplets grows.

  Yolk-to-oil ratios. The classic mayonnaise ratio is one yolk per 200 to 240 g of oil; pushing past 240 g per yolk produces a thinner sauce that breaks faster. Hollandaise is leaner on yolk-to-fat by mass but warmer (warmth thins the sauce, which means the cook does not need as much yolk to read as luxurious): one yolk per 60 to 80 g of melted butter. Caesar dressing sits between the two: two yolks for 90 to 120 g of olive oil, plus a substantial water phase of lemon and worcestershire that lightens the body. The yolk-to-fat ratio determines body; the water-phase mass determines pourability.

  Coddling: warm-yolk versus raw-yolk emulsions. Raw yolk produces a workable emulsion but a riskier one (salmonella concerns) and a slightly less viscous sauce. A coddle (90 seconds in barely simmering water at 88 °C / 190 °F, or alternatively 75 seconds at 70 °C / 158 °F sous vide) partially denatures the yolk proteins, which thickens the yolk before any oil enters and makes the dressing feel richer at the same fat load. Coddling is also the kitchen-practical pasteurization step. The 1924 Caesar Cardini recipe coddled the whole egg in shell for 1 minute; the modern adaptation coddles two yolks for 90 seconds for a richer result. For mayonnaise and aioli, raw yolks are the standard (refrigerate to slow bacterial growth and consume within 3 days); for hollandaise the gentle warm cooking over a bain-marie is the coddle, integrated into the build itself.

  Failure modes. Broken emulsion (visible oil and water layers separating, sauce looks oily-streaked or curdled): caused by adding oil too fast, by the oil being too cold to disperse, or by the acid load overwhelming the lecithin. Rebuild: do not whisk harder, that just folds in air. Instead, start fresh in another bowl with a teaspoon of warm water plus a teaspoon of dijon (or another yolk for a fully-yolk rebuild) and slowly whisk the broken sauce into the new water phase in dribbles. The fresh emulsifier coats the released oil droplets and the sauce reassembles. Curdled hollandaise (yolk over-cooked into scrambled lumps): rebuild over fresh yolk in a bain-marie, whisking the broken sauce in slowly off-heat. Greasy mayonnaise (the sauce reads as oil sitting on top of yolk-water rather than as a unified colloid): the cook over-shot the yolk-to-oil ratio; rebuild on a second yolk or add a teaspoon of acid to thin the water phase and re-suspend.

  Applications by category. Cold sauces: mayonnaise (neutral oil base), aioli (garlic and olive oil), tartare (mayonnaise plus chopped pickle and capers), remoulade (mayonnaise plus mustard, anchovy, herbs), Caesar dressing (anchovy and parmigiano on a coddled-yolk base). Warm sauces: hollandaise (clarified butter, lemon), béarnaise (hollandaise plus tarragon-shallot reduction), maltaise (hollandaise plus blood-orange juice). Pan-sauce thickeners: yolk tempered into the off-heat reduction of a pan sauce thickens it into a velouté or a yolk-bound sauce without scrambling, the same technique as carbonara. Pasta finishes: carbonara is a yolk emulsion built around hot pasta water and rendered guanciale fat, no acid; cacio e pepe with yolk added is a non-canonical hybrid that drifts the dish toward carbonara.

  Salt and acid timing. Both go in the water phase before the oil. Salt added at the end clumps and reads as gritty hot spots in the sauce. Acid added at the end thins the emulsion past its tight point and can break it. Build the water phase fully (yolk plus salt plus mustard plus acid plus any aromatics) and taste it for seasoning, then start the oil. The water phase tastes aggressively salty and aggressively acidic at this stage; the oil dilutes both back toward the right finished balance.

  Storage. Refrigerated cold-yolk emulsions hold 3 days for raw-yolk sauces (mayonnaise, aioli, raw-yolk Caesar), up to 5 days for coddled-yolk Caesar. Hollandaise and béarnaise do not hold; they are à la minute sauces and break or curdle on the second reheat. The viscosity drops over time as the lecithin slowly migrates and the sauce thins; sauces that read full on day 1 read slightly looser on day 3 even when stable.
