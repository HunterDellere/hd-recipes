about: |
  A vinaigrette is a temporary emulsion of oil suspended as droplets in vinegar, with an emulsifier (most commonly mustard, also egg yolk, honey, or shallot mucilage) doing the chemistry that makes the suspension hold for the 20 to 40 minutes before plating. The technique is one of the most fundamental in Western cooking and most often taught wrong. The cook's goal is not to "shake until combined" but to engineer a colloid that delivers oil and acid in a single stable droplet to each lettuce leaf or salad component, without breaking back into separated phases.

  Oil and water do not mix on their own. Olive oil molecules are hydrophobic; vinegar molecules are hydrophilic; suspending one in the other requires an amphiphilic molecule (one end loves oil, the other end loves water) to coat each oil droplet and prevent the droplets from coalescing back together. Mustard is the canonical kitchen emulsifier: dijon mustard contains substantial mucilage (a polysaccharide from the seed coat that swells in water and surrounds oil droplets), plus surface-active proteins from the ground seed, plus suspended seed-coat particles (a Pickering emulsion mechanism: solid particles can stabilize emulsions like surfactants do). One teaspoon of dijon in 30 g of vinegar plus 90 g of olive oil produces a lush, body-having vinaigrette that holds 40 minutes; the same vinegar-and-oil without mustard breaks within 30 seconds.

  Ratios are the second lever. The classical French ratio is 1 part vinegar (or other acid) to 3 parts oil, by volume; in metric and by weight that runs 30 g vinegar to 90 g oil. The 1:3 ratio targets a salad where the dressing reads as a discreet partner to the leaves; the dressing is bright but not sharp. Cooks who want a sharper salad pull this to 1:2 (heavier on the acid); cooks who want a milder, more luxurious plate push to 1:4 (heavier on the oil). The acid load also depends on the acid: red wine vinegar at 6 percent acetic, lemon juice at 5 percent citric, sherry vinegar at 7 percent acetic, balsamic at 4 percent: bright vinegars can hold a 1:3 ratio while milder ones (rice vinegar, champagne vinegar) often need 1:2 to read.

  The build sequence determines whether the emulsion holds. Wrong: shake oil and vinegar together in a jar, the oil dispenses in large droplets that re-coalesce immediately. Right: put the acid (vinegar plus any dissolved seasonings: salt, mustard, minced shallot, honey) in a bowl, and stream the oil in slowly while whisking vigorously. The slow oil addition gives the emulsifier time to coat each newly-introduced droplet before the next one arrives; the vigorous whisking shears the oil into smaller droplets (smaller droplets stay suspended longer than larger ones). A cook with practiced wrist can build a vinaigrette in 60 seconds; the technique scales from a teaspoon for one plate to a quart for a banquet.

  Salt timing matters. Salt dissolves in vinegar fast and fully; it does not dissolve in oil at all. The cook adds salt to the vinegar first, dissolves, and then streams in the oil. Salt added at the end sits in pockets in the dressing and reads as gritty, salty hot spots on the salad. The same logic applies to dissolved sweeteners (honey, maple) and to mustard: build the water-phase first, complete, then introduce the oil.

  Emulsion stability is graded. A "broken" vinaigrette has visible oil and vinegar layers separating in the bowl. A "tight" emulsion holds without visible separation for 30 to 60 minutes. A "stable" emulsion (achievable with a powerful emulsifier like egg yolk or with mechanical homogenization) holds overnight; mayonnaise is the extreme version and is technically a vinaigrette engineering problem solved by yolk lecithin and high-shear mixing. For everyday salad service, tight is enough; the dressing meets the leaves before it has time to break.

  Rebuilding a broken vinaigrette. If the emulsion breaks during prep (oil and vinegar separated, the dressing looks oily-streaked), do not whisk harder; whisking a broken emulsion just folds in air and does not rebuild the colloid. Instead, transfer to a small bowl, add another teaspoon of mustard to a fresh tablespoon of warm water, whisk into the broken dressing in dribbles, and the emulsion rebuilds. The water gives the emulsifier room to redistribute and re-coat the droplets.

  Variations and other emulsifiers. Egg-yolk vinaigrette (Caesar dressing): one yolk per half cup of dressing, more powerful and longer-holding emulsifier. Honey or maple vinaigrette: the sugars contribute viscosity and minor surface-activity; lighter-bodied than mustard versions but acceptable. Shallot vinaigrette without mustard: the shallot mucilage provides modest emulsifier activity, the dressing breaks quickly but the flavor is bright and elegant: works on hot composed plates where the dressing is poured immediately rather than holding. Tahini vinaigrette: tahini contributes both fat and emulsifier (the same surface-active proteins as in any seed paste); produces a creamy, near-permanent emulsion.

  Failure modes. Pre-acidified mustard (commercial, jar-aged): the mustard's acid load is already high and adding more vinegar can over-tighten the dressing past the cook's intent: taste before adding more acid. Cold ingredients straight from the fridge: vinegar and especially mustard don't blend cleanly when cold; bring components to room temperature before building. Storing dressed salad: even a tight vinaigrette breaks down lettuce within an hour; dress at the table, not in the bowl ahead of time.
