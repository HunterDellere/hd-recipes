  Pounding in a mortar is a controlled mechanical disruption, heavy, repeated impact from a pestle that fractures the structure of an ingredient rather than slicing or shearing it. There are two regimes that look superficially similar but produce different results: grinding (continuous circular motion that reduces ingredients to a smooth paste, curry pastes, spice blends, garlic-salt pastes) and bruising (discrete vertical strikes that crack and tear without homogenizing, som tum, Mexican molcajete salsas, Genoese pesto in its traditional form). The same mortar can do both; the cook chooses which by adjusting the motion.

  Cell-wall rupture is the mechanism. Vegetable, fruit, and herb tissue is built of cells with rigid pectocellulosic walls and a central vacuole holding water and dissolved solutes (sugars, organic acids, glutamates, color compounds). A clean blade slices between cells; a pestle strike compresses and bursts them. The burst dumps cell contents onto exposed surfaces and creates jagged microtopography that a sauce or dressing can lodge in. This is why a pounded basil-pine-nut-cheese pesto reads silkier and more integrated than a food-processed one (which slices), why a molcajete-built salsa carries more depth than a blender salsa, and why som tum dressing migrates into pounded papaya in a way it does not migrate into julienned papaya tossed with the same dressing.

  Clay mortars (krok din in Thai) versus stone mortars (krok hin in Thai, molcajete in Mexico, Genoese marble mortar in Liguria). Clay or wood-walled mortars are softer-walled, larger, and almost always paired with a wooden pestle. They are calibrated for the bruise, pounding moist ingredients (papaya, long bean, tomato, fresh herbs) where the goal is fracture rather than homogenization. Stone mortars (granite, basalt, marble) have hard walls and stone or wooden pestles; they handle dry pounding (curry paste, spice blend, dry-roast peanut crumb) and grinding (continuous-motion paste work) where the cook needs friction against the wall to break tough fibrous material. Using the wrong mortar produces predictable failures: a clay mortar cannot grind a hard curry paste smooth; a granite mortar pounded the same way as a clay one for som tum yields papaya mush.

  Applications across cuisines. Thai som tum is the canonical bruise application, green papaya, long bean, tomato, garlic, chili, peanuts (in the dressing build, not the salad), all pounded in a clay mortar against the dressing liquids; the dressing is built in the mortar as the pounding progresses. Thai red, green, and yellow curry pastes are the canonical grind application, in granite mortars, with the pestle moving in steady circles for 15 to 30 minutes until the lemongrass, galangal, shallot, garlic, chili, and shrimp paste reduce to a uniform wet paste with no visible fiber. Indonesian sambals and Malaysian rempah pastes work the same way as Thai curry pastes. Mexican molcajete salsa is a bruise application, chili, onion, garlic, tomato pounded with salt against the lava-stone bowl, the textured surface tearing the ingredients into a coarse rustic salsa with the lava-stone iron contributing trace minerality. Genoese pesto in its traditional Ligurian form is also a bruise, basil, pine nuts, garlic, salt, and parmigiano pounded with olive oil against a marble mortar, the slow rupture preserving basil aromatics that a steel-blade food processor heats and oxidizes. Indian wet masalas (the "ginger-garlic-green chili paste" of countless North Indian recipes) sit between bruise and grind: traditionally pounded in a stone mortar (sil-batta) into a coarse-textured paste.

  Failure modes. Over-pounding the bruise reduces the dish to puree, the cue is loss of textural distinction between ingredients (papaya disappears into tomato into bean). Under-pounding the grind leaves visible fiber and oily droplets that signal incomplete cell rupture in lemongrass and galangal, the curry paste then needs longer cooking to break down further, and the volatile aromatics drift off in that extended heat. Wrong mortar is the third failure: clay for hard pastes, granite for moist vegetables. The cook learns by feel and sound, the bruise is dull thud; the grind is steady stone-on-stone scrape; the over-pound is wet slap.

  Building dressings in the mortar. The strongest argument for mortar work in salads and salsas is that the dressing is built against the vegetables progressively. Garlic and chili pound first into a coarse paste; sugar and salt-functioning ingredients (fish sauce, dried shrimp) join and are pounded into the paste; liquid acids (lime, vinegar) come in last and dissolve the paste into a dressing. The vegetables are then pounded into that built dressing. The cook can taste from the pestle at every stage and adjust before the next ingredient locks in, a pour-it-in-the-bowl-and-toss approach does not allow the same iterative correction. This is why the four-flavor balance of a great som tum is essentially a tasting curriculum, not a recipe.
