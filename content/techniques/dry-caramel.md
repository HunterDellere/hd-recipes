  Dry caramel is the technique of melting granulated sugar in a pan over heat with no added water, letting it liquefy and then brown progressively from pale gold through amber to deep mahogany, at which point it carries a complex sweet-bitter-roasted flavor that raw sugar does not. The dry method (sugar alone) is faster and reaches deep color sooner than the wet method (sugar dissolved in water first), but it is less forgiving because there is no water buffer slowing the temperature climb. The technique underlies caramel candy and sauce, crème caramel and flan, croquembouche glue, praline, and savory applications such as the Vietnamese nuoc mau (the amber-sugar base of caramelized braises like thit kho) and Filipino and Latin American caramel-darkened sauces.

  The chemistry, and why it is not Maillard. Caramelization is the pyrolytic (heat-driven) decomposition of sugar itself; it requires only sugar and heat, no protein. As sucrose passes its melting point (around 160 to 186 °C / 320 to 367 °F) it first melts clear, then begins to break down: the sucrose inverts to glucose and fructose, which dehydrate and fragment and recombine into hundreds of new compounds, including diacetyl (buttery), furans and maltol (sweet-toasted, caramel), and at the dark end bitter, roasted, slightly acrid molecules. This is chemically distinct from the Maillard reaction, which is a reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars and produces a different (savory-roasted) flavor family; a seared steak browns by Maillard, a melted sugar browns by caramelization. The two can run together (in a baked good with both sugar and protein) but the dry-caramel technique is pure caramelization.

  The color-to-flavor spectrum. Color is the cook''s only real-time gauge of flavor depth, and the progression is fast at the end. Pale straw (around 160 °C) is barely caramelized, mostly sweet. Light amber (170 °C) carries the first toasted note and is right for delicate caramel sauces. Medium amber (175 to 180 °C) is the workhorse for candy and most sauces, balanced sweet-and-toasted. Deep amber, the color of an old penny (180 to 188 °C), carries a pronounced bitter edge that balances sweetness and is the target for savory caramels (nuoc mau), bitter-edged caramel sauces, and anywhere the caramel must hold its own against salt or fat. Past that, at the first wisp of smoke, the caramel tips into burnt: acrid, thin, and bitter past usefulness. The difference between deep amber and burnt is often 10 to 15 seconds, so the deep-amber target is cooked right to the edge and then stopped fast.

  Crystallization, the dry-method failure mode. Sugar wants to re-crystallize back into grains rather than stay a smooth melt, and a single seed crystal can cascade the whole pan into a grainy, lumpy mass. Two disciplines prevent it. First, do not stir a dry caramel with a utensil once it begins to melt; stirring drags undissolved crystals up the pan walls where they seed crystallization. Swirl the pan by its handle instead to even the heat. Second, keep the pan walls clean: a few drops of water brushed down the sides early, or a covered start that lets steam wash the walls, dissolves stray crystals before they fall back in. If a dry caramel seizes into lumps, it can sometimes be rescued by continued gentle heat that re-melts the crystals, but the safer move with sticky savory caramels is the wet method.

  Stopping the caramel, and the steam hazard. A caramel keeps cooking and darkening from residual heat after the pan leaves the burner, so it must be stopped at the moment it reaches the target color, one shade lighter than the goal if anything. Stopping means adding liquid: cream for dessert caramel, water and fish sauce for nuoc mau, water for a simple syrup-stop. Adding liquid to caramel at 180 °C is violent: the liquid flashes to steam instantly, the caramel sputters and bubbles up the pan, and the steam is hot enough to cause a serious burn. Stand back, add the liquid in a steady stream away from your hand, and expect the caramel to seize into a hard lump that re-dissolves with a minute of gentle stirring over low heat. Have the stopping liquid measured and standing at the stove before the sugar ever reaches color; there is no time to fetch it once the caramel is amber.

  Pan and quantity. Use a heavy-bottomed pan with a light-colored interior (stainless, not dark non-stick or cast iron) so the caramel color reads accurately; you cannot judge amber against a black pan. The heavy bottom buffers hot spots that would scorch one patch while the rest is still pale. Keep the layer of sugar thin and even; a deep pile melts unevenly, the bottom caramelizing while the top is still crystalline. Medium heat is right for control; high heat reaches color faster but the window to catch deep amber before burnt shrinks to almost nothing.

  Applications by category. Dessert and confection: caramel sauce and candy (caramel stopped with cream and butter), crème caramel and flan (caramel poured into the mold, custard baked on top), croquembouche and spun sugar (caramel used as glue and decoration), praline and brittle (caramel poured over nuts and set hard), caramelized-sugar tops on crème brûlée (a thin sugar layer caramelized under a torch). Savory and cross-cuisine: Vietnamese nuoc mau (sugar to deep amber, stopped with water and fish sauce, the base of thit kho and ca kho), Filipino and Latin American caramel-darkened braises and sauces, the caramel notes built into some barbecue and glaze reductions, and the deep-amber caramel tossed with fried vegetables (the fish-sauce-caramel brussels sprout). Across all of these the same rule holds: the depth of the amber is the depth of the flavor, and the caramel is stopped the instant it reaches its target.
