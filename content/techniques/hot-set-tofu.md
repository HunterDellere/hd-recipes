about: |
  Hot-set tofu is the home-kitchen technique for making silken tofu in the serving vessel rather than buying it pre-set. The home cook takes plain unsweetened soy milk, warms it to a controlled temperature, whisks in glucono delta-lactone (GDL) at 0.25 to 0.30% by weight, pours the mixture directly into the serving bowl, and lets the bowl sit undisturbed for 15 to 25 minutes. The GDL hydrolyzes slowly into gluconic acid in the warm soy milk, the rising acidity drops the pH past the soy protein isoelectric point (around pH 4.5), and the proteins coagulate into a smooth custard-soft curd that holds the shape of the bowl. The result is the silkiest possible silken tofu — fresher and softer than any commercially available product — and the technical foundation of Chinese douhua (豆花, "tofu flower"), the poured-set mapo tofu variant, and Japanese silken-tofu desserts (kuromitsu silken, gomadōfu).

  Why GDL and not nigari or gypsum. Three coagulants are used in commercial silken tofu, GDL, nigari (magnesium chloride), and gypsum (calcium sulfate), but only GDL is practical for home pour-and-set work. Nigari and gypsum are direct-acting coagulants that begin to coagulate the soy milk within seconds of contact; for them to set silken tofu in a vessel, they must be incorporated extremely fast (under 5 seconds, usually with specialized tools) and the curd structure they produce is sensitive to disturbance during setting. GDL is unique in that it is a slow-release acidulant: the lactone form is stable in soy milk, but it slowly hydrolyzes to gluconic acid over 20 to 30 minutes at 70 to 90°C. The slow hydrolysis means the home cook has plenty of time (1 to 2 minutes is common) to whisk the GDL evenly through the soy milk, pour it into the bowl, and disturb-free settle before the coagulation begins. The resulting curd is also more uniformly fine than nigari or gypsum curds because the acidification is gradual and even rather than sudden and local.

  Method. The home protocol is five steps. (1) Soy milk: use plain unsweetened soy milk (no flavorings, no calcium fortification, calcium throws off the coagulation). 500 mL fills two ordinary 250 mL serving bowls or one large 500 mL bowl. Commercial soy milk works (Silk plain unsweetened, Westsoy organic plain) but homemade fresh soy milk produces a noticeably better curd. (2) Heat: warm the soy milk in a saucepan to 80°C / 175°F (just below simmer; small bubbles forming at the edges, no rolling boil). Boiling soy milk before GDL addition is fine and is preferred by some recipes for cleaner flavor; the only requirement is that the milk reaches 80°C and is then cooled if necessary back to the 70 to 90°C window. (3) GDL: weigh 1.25 to 1.50 g GDL for 500 mL soy milk (0.25% to 0.30% by weight). Dissolve the GDL in 1 tbsp / 15 mL of warm water before adding to the soy milk; this prevents lumping. (4) Whisk and pour: whisk the dissolved GDL into the warm soy milk for 5 seconds, long enough to distribute evenly, short enough not to introduce excessive air. Immediately pour the mixture into the serving bowl(s); if any topping is being placed in the bowl ahead of time, the topping should be at the bowl bottom and the soy milk poured over it. (5) Set: cover the bowl loosely with plastic wrap or a lid (prevents skin formation on the surface) and leave at warm room temperature (or in a turned-off oven that was warmed to 80°C and then turned off) for 15 to 25 minutes. Test by gently tilting the bowl; the surface should not move and a finger pressed at the edge should leave a clean indent that fills with clear whey. The tofu is set; serve within 30 minutes.

  Temperature is everything. Below 70°C / 160°F: the GDL hydrolyzes too slowly and the tofu sets unevenly or not at all over a reasonable timeframe. Above 95°C / 205°F: the soy proteins denature too aggressively before the GDL has had time to acidify the milk evenly, producing a clumpy or grainy curd. The 70 to 90°C window is genuinely narrow and worth a thermometer for the first few attempts.

  Disturbance during setting. The 15 to 25 minute setting period must be undisturbed. Bumping the bowl, moving it, or even setting it on a surface that vibrates (near a running dishwasher, on a counter where someone is chopping) will fracture the developing curd and produce a layered, broken texture rather than the smooth wobble. Set the bowl on a stable counter and leave it.

  Bowl selection. The serving bowl must be heat-tolerant (the soy milk goes in at 80°C) and ideally pre-warmed (running hot water over the empty bowl for 30 seconds, drying, then using). A cold bowl will pull heat out of the soy milk faster than the GDL can work and will cause uneven setting at the bottom. Wide shallow bowls (10 to 14 cm / 4 to 6 inch diameter) produce the best texture because the heat is retained more evenly through the setting time; very deep narrow bowls can have temperature gradients that produce a layered set.

  Applications. Chinese douhua (豆花), sweet version with brown sugar syrup and ginger; savory version with soy sauce, chili oil, scallion, dried shrimp, and pickled mustard greens. The poured-set mapo tofu variant, soy milk + GDL set in the bowl, then hot mapo sauce ladled over (the sauce should be at near-boiling temperature; the heat penetration is part of the dish). Japanese kuromitsu silken, set the silken with kinako-flavored soy milk, top with kuromitsu (black sugar syrup) and roasted soy flour. Gomadōfu, sesame-paste-and-soy-milk silken tofu set with a kuzu starch (a different setting mechanism, but the pour-and-set logic is the same). The technique extends naturally to any preparation where freshly-set silken tofu in a serving vessel reads better than pre-cubed tofu.

  Failure modes. Tofu fails to set: GDL was insufficient, soy milk was too cool, soy milk was calcium-fortified or sweetened (which interferes), or the bowl was disturbed during setting. Solution: weigh GDL precisely, use a thermometer for the first attempts, use plain soy milk, set on a stable surface. Tofu sets but is grainy or curdled: soy milk was too hot when GDL was added, or the soy milk had cream solids that separated. Solution: stay in the 70 to 90°C window. Tofu sets at the top and is liquid at the bottom: the bowl was cold or the setting time was insufficient. Solution: pre-warm the bowl, extend setting time to 30 minutes, check temperature retention. Tofu has a faint sour or yogurt-like flavor: GDL was too high a percentage (above 0.35%), or the setting time was too long (past 40 minutes the gluconic acid concentration goes past the silent zone and starts to read on the palate). Solution: weigh GDL carefully, serve within 30 minutes of setting.

  Buying GDL. GDL is sold as a fine white powder, food-grade, at Asian groceries (look for "tofu coagulant" or "豆腐凝固剂"), at homebrew supply stores (used as a clean acidulant in some beverage preparations), and online (Modernist Pantry, Amazon). It is shelf-stable at room temperature in a sealed container for 2 years. Standard quantity needed for home use is small (a 100 g bag at 1.25 g per batch is enough for 80 batches); buying once is enough for years.
