about: |
  Atta is the South Asian whole wheat flour used for almost every flatbread in the region: chapati (roti), paratha, kulcha, puri, naan-style breads. Three structural differences distinguish atta from American whole wheat flour. First, the wheat varietal: atta is milled from durum-class hard wheat (Triticum durum or hard winter wheat), which has higher protein and harder kernels than the soft red wheat used for American whole wheat flour. Second, the milling: atta is ground extremely fine using stone mills or chakki mills that crush rather than shear, retaining all the bran and germ but pulverizing them into a powder fine enough to produce smooth dough. American whole wheat flour from steel roller mills retains larger bran particles that interrupt gluten formation and produce coarser, denser breads. Third, the gluten development: atta has slightly less gluten than American whole wheat (around 10 to 12% protein vs 13 to 14%), but the high protein quality and the fine particle size mean atta dough produces notably more pliable, elastic flatbreads.

  Why atta is structural for chapati. Chapati dough is just atta + water + a pinch of salt, sometimes with a small amount of oil or ghee. The dough is rested 20 to 30 minutes for gluten development, divided into balls, rolled thin (2 to 3 mm), and cooked on a dry hot tawa (cast-iron griddle) for 30 to 45 seconds per side, then briefly puffed over an open flame. The chapati must be flexible enough to fold without cracking, soft enough to scoop curry, and developed enough to puff with internal steam. American whole wheat flour produces chapati that is denser, more brittle, and less puff-prone; the substitution is recognizably wrong. The right tool is atta.

  Cooking applications. Roti (the everyday flatbread of North India and Pakistan, eaten with curry and dal), chapati (the same as roti, sometimes thinner), paratha (layered flatbread cooked with ghee), kulcha (slightly leavened with yogurt), puri (deep-fried puffed flatbread), bhature (deep-fried leavened flatbread). For naan, the textbook Indian recipe is half atta + half all-purpose flour + yogurt + a small amount of yeast or baking powder; some restaurant versions use entirely all-purpose flour and read more bread-like.

  Buying notes. Indian groceries (Patel Brothers, smaller regional groceries) reliably stock atta in 5 to 10 kg bags. Major brands: Aashirvaad (the Indian household standard), Pillsbury Atta (a widely-available Indian-market brand from the same conglomerate, distinct from American Pillsbury), Sujata, Robinhood. American supermarket atta is rare; if found, the brands above are reliable. "Multigrain atta" or "fortified atta" are marketing variants — read labels for actual composition. Bob''s Red Mill "whole wheat pastry flour" is sometimes proposed as an atta substitute but is fundamentally different (soft wheat, lower protein) and produces wrong-textured chapati.

  The chakki-fresh upgrade. Some specialty Indian groceries operate a chakki (stone mill) on-site and sell freshly-ground atta. Fresh-milled atta is dramatically more aromatic and produces better chapati than bagged atta that has sat for months. If accessible, this is the upgrade. The standard bagged atta from a high-turnover Indian grocery is also excellent and is the everyday default.

storage: |
  Sealed at room temperature in a cool dry pantry, 6 months for peak flavor; up to 1 year usable. The whole wheat bran contains oils that go rancid faster than refined flour; refrigeration extends shelf life to 1 year and freezing to 2 years. Smell-test: fresh atta has a clean wheat-grain smell; rancid atta smells faintly like wet cardboard and produces wrong-tasting flatbreads. Off-cues are mealy bugs (Indian groceries occasionally have weevil-introduction issues; freeze the bag for 48 hours after purchase to kill any), or rancid smell; discard.
