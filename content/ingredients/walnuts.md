about: |
  Walnuts are the seed of Juglans species, sold as halves or pieces in shells or shelled. English walnut (Juglans regia) is the standard supermarket walnut, with a thin shell and mild, slightly tannic flesh. Black walnut (Juglans nigra) is native to North America, with a thick rugged shell, a stronger, more aggressive flavor, and a much shorter season; black walnuts are a specialty ingredient in American Southern baking and not interchangeable with English walnuts in most recipes. Default to English unless a recipe specifies black.

  Composition and the rancidity problem. Walnuts are about 65% fat, with an unusually high proportion of polyunsaturated fats (omega-3 alpha-linolenic acid in particular) compared to other nuts. The high polyunsaturated content makes walnuts the most rancidity-prone of all common nuts: they oxidize quickly at room temperature, develop bitter and stale flavors that read as "tastes like an old walnut," and are essentially ruined by the time most cooks notice. Buying small quantities, storing in the freezer, and tasting before use is the entire technique of walnut sourcing. A bright, buttery walnut and a stale, bitter walnut are functionally different ingredients.

  Toasting. Toasting walnuts at 175°C / 350°F for 6 to 8 minutes (or in a dry skillet over medium heat for 4 to 5 minutes, stirring) develops their flavor dramatically: the slight bitterness recedes, sweetness comes forward, and the nuts smell distinctly roasted. Almost every preparation that uses walnuts benefits from toasting first; raw walnuts in baked goods are acceptable because the bake itself toasts them, but raw walnuts in salads, pasta, or cheese plates read flat. The cue is smell, fresh-toasted walnuts smell sweetly nutty and buttery; over-toasted walnuts smell acrid and burnt, and the flavor is lost.

  Cooking behavior. Whole halves: cheeseboards, salads, baked-goods topping. Coarsely chopped: pasta with gorgonzola, salads, pesto Trapanese (Sicilian), Persian fesenjan (walnut-pomegranate stew). Finely chopped or ground: tarator and muhammara (Levantine sauces and dips), Greek baklava, Persian rice pilafs. Walnut paste (toasted walnuts ground with oil): the base of muhammara, of some Italian pesto-style sauces, and of the Turkish/Levantine tarator yogurt sauce. The walnut's high oil content means even short grinding produces a paste; this is the basis of muhammara's velvety texture.

  Pairings. Cheese: gorgonzola, brie, blue cheese family, aged cheddar, the high fat and slight tannin of walnuts complement aged dairy. Fruit: pear (the canonical pairing in Italian cooking), apple, fig, persimmon, pomegranate. Greens: arugula, radicchio, endive (the bitter-edge greens that walnuts cut). Honey: especially in Greek and Levantine baking, where toasted walnuts and honey form the foundational filling for baklava and walnut-honey cake. Coffee and chocolate: brownies, banana-bread-with-walnuts, walnut-and-coffee tortes. The pairing reach is wide.

  Cross-cuisine reach. Italian: walnut sauce for pasta (salsa di noci, Ligurian), walnut pesto, walnut-ricotta filling for ravioli, gorgonzola-and-walnut combinations across northern Italy. French: walnut bread, walnut-and-roquefort salads, Périgord walnut tarts. Persian/Iranian: fesenjan (chicken or duck stew with walnut and pomegranate), walnut-stuffed eggplant. Turkish/Levantine: muhammara (walnut-and-roasted-pepper dip), tarator (walnut-yogurt sauce), walnut-stuffed dolma. American: walnut brownies, walnut-and-banana bread, Southern black-walnut pies. Korean: walnut-stuffed pastries (hodu gwaja). The walnut's role spans appetizer, main, and dessert across the cuisine map.

  Buying notes. Look for walnut halves or pieces that are pale tan or golden (deep brown means oxidized), without visible mold or shriveling. The package should smell sweetly nutty when opened; a stale or bitter smell means the walnuts are past prime regardless of the date on the bag. Buy from sources with high turnover, supermarket bulk bins are usually too slow and the walnuts in them are often rancid. The best North American sources are the harvest-time fall harvest from California; walnuts bought between October and January are at peak quality and decline through the year. Once rancid, walnuts cannot be rescued; replace the supply.

storage: |
  Walnuts in shells, in a cool dry pantry: 2 to 3 months. Shelled walnuts at room temperature, sealed: 1 to 2 months at most. Refrigerated: 6 months. Frozen, sealed in a zip-top bag with as much air pressed out as possible: 1 year (or longer; freezing is the only reliable long-term storage for walnuts). Toasted walnuts go rancid faster than raw; freeze any toasted batch you don't use immediately. The cue for past-prime is smell-and-taste: a single piece tasted from the bag will tell you within 5 seconds whether the supply is good. Do not assume "it should still be fine", walnuts go bad faster than expectations suggest.
