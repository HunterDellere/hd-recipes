about: |
  Rau ngổ (literally "ngổ vegetable" in Vietnamese; also called om in Cambodian Khmer, phak khayaeng in Lao, and rice paddy herb in English) is Limnophila aromatica, an aquatic-to-semi-aquatic herb in the Plantaginaceae family. The plant grows in rice paddies and slow-moving freshwater across Southeast Asia, harvested by clipping the top 10 to 15 cm of the leafy stems. The leaves are small (2 to 4 cm), pointed, slightly serrated, and arranged in opposite pairs along a slender, slightly hairy stem; the overall appearance is similar to a delicate mint or thin watercress. The stems are tender enough to eat alongside the leaves; only the bottom 2 to 3 cm of woody stem is typically discarded.

  Aromatic profile and chemistry. Rau ngổ's defining feature is its complex volatile-oil profile, dominated by limonene (the bright citrus top note also present in citrus peel) and cuminaldehyde (the spicy back-note of cumin seed), plus secondary terpenes including alpha-pinene and beta-caryophyllene. This combination produces a flavor that reads as cumin-citrus-grassy in a way no other common kitchen herb does. The closest aromatic comparison is a faint cumin note layered onto a bright cilantro-or-shiso top, but neither cilantro nor shiso captures the full profile. The herb is added off-heat (the volatile oils evaporate above 80°C / 176°F over a few minutes), so it goes into the bowl at the end of cooking, not into the simmering pot.

  Why it matters. Rau ngổ is the signature finishing herb of southern Vietnamese canh chua; the dish's identity is built on the three-stratum structure of tamarind broth, pineapple-tomato-bạc hà vegetable bed, and the rau ngổ + culantro herb top. Replace the rau ngổ and you have lost the most distinctive aromatic top of the dish; the broth and vegetables still work, but the bowl no longer reads as canh chua specifically. The herb also appears in some Khmer sour soups (samlor machu), Lao or-lam-style stews, and as a fresh-eaten herb in Vietnamese herb-plates served alongside grilled meats and bún bowls.

  Cooking applications by category. **Vietnamese (southern, Mekong delta)**: canh chua cá and canh chua tôm (the structural finishing herb); occasionally in fresh-herb plates served with grilled fish and pork; rarely in northern Vietnamese cooking, which leans on dill and Vietnamese coriander instead. **Cambodian**: in samlor machu yuon (a fish-based sour soup closely related to canh chua); in fresh-herb plates with prahok-based dips. **Lao and Thai (Isan)**: in fish-based soups and stews, less common than in Vietnamese cooking but present in border regions.

  Buying notes. Fresh rau ngổ at Vietnamese markets in major US metropolitan areas (Houston, Orange County, San Jose, Boston, NYC Chinatowns): sold in small bundles (50 to 100 g) tied with a rubber band, with the leaves typically still attached to the slender stems. The bundle should look fresh-green, with no yellowed or limp leaves and no slimy patches at the cut ends. The herb wilts within 3 to 4 days off-store, so the bundle should look freshly harvested. Frozen rau ngổ is occasionally available at large Asian groceries (H Mart, 99 Ranch) in 50 to 100 g bags; the frozen version is acceptable for cooked applications but reads slightly muted compared to fresh. Outside Vietnamese-population areas, rau ngổ can be hard to find; Bangkok-Asian markets occasionally stock it, and some specialty herb suppliers ship overnight.

  Common Western drift. The substitution problem is large: rau ngổ has no exact substitute in Western herb pantries, and US-published canh chua recipes commonly suggest dill or cilantro alone. Dill plus a small fraction of cilantro is the closest visual and aromatic approximation (the dill's anethole captures the bright aromatic note, and cilantro adds the green-stem dimension), but the cumin back-note is lost. Acknowledging this loss is more honest than pretending dill is rau ngổ.

storage: |
  Fresh rau ngổ, stems trimmed and the bundle wrapped in slightly damp paper towel inside a sealed plastic bag, refrigerated: 3 to 5 days, with quality declining steadily. The herb wilts visibly before it spoils; once the leaves bend rather than crisp under pressure, the volatile oils have largely dissipated and the herb is no longer at peak. Frozen pre-cut rau ngổ: 3 to 6 months at peak quality, suitable for cooked applications. Failure modes: slimy patches at the cut ends (bacterial breakdown, discard), yellow-brown leaves (oxidation, trim or discard if extensive), strong fermented smell (advanced spoilage, discard).
