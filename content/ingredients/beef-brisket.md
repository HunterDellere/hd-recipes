about: |
  Beef brisket is the breast or lower chest of the steer, sitting below the chuck and forward of the plate. It comes in two retail subdivisions: the flat (a leaner, more uniform rectangle good for slicing) and the point (a fattier, more marbled section that shreds well). Whole brisket combines both; pre-trimmed flat is the more common retail cut in North America. Composition is roughly 56% water, 18% protein, 26% fat, with very high collagen content in the connective tissue that runs through the muscle.

  Functional category: structural slow-cook and braise cut. Brisket needs 4 to 8 hours of moist heat at temperatures between 80 and 95°C / 175 to 205°F to convert its high collagen content into gelatin. Cooked fast or at higher temperatures, the meat reads as a tough fibrous mass; cooked properly at the long-slow window, the same muscle reads as silky tender, easily shredded or sliced against the grain. The point cut shreds more easily and is preferred for shredded preparations (Cuban ropa vieja, smoked barbecue burnt ends); the flat slices cleanly and is preferred for sliced preparations (pastrami, corned beef, sliced barbecue brisket).

  Applications by category. Cuban: ropa vieja (literally old clothes, the brisket simmered then shredded into a tomato-pepper sauce). North American: smoked Texas barbecue brisket (the canonical low-and-slow application), New England boiled dinner, corned beef. Jewish: pot roast brisket with onions, brisket for Passover. Vietnamese: bo kho (spiced beef stew). Korean: galbitang (boiled brisket and short rib soup), some bulgogi preparations.

  Sizes. Whole brisket weighs 5 to 7 kg / 11 to 16 lb (mostly bought by barbecue specialists). Flat-only retail cuts weigh 1.5 to 3 kg / 3 to 7 lb and are the standard for home cooking. Cuban ropa vieja, pot roast, and braised dishes typically use 1 to 1.5 kg / 2 to 3 lb of flat brisket; the leftovers freeze and reheat exceptionally well.

  Buying. Look for brisket with a uniform fat cap on one side (5 to 10 mm thick is ideal; thicker can be trimmed, thinner produces dry meat) and visible marbling through the lean. Bright red color with no gray or brown patches; the surface should be firm to the touch with no slime. Prime-grade brisket is more expensive but the additional marbling makes a real difference in long-cooked dishes; choice grade is the standard for home cooking and works well; select grade is leaner and produces drier final dishes.

storage: |
  Refrigerated, in original packaging, by the sell-by date (typically 3 to 5 days from packaging). Once opened, use within 2 days. Frozen, vacuum-sealed: 6 months at peak; usable for 12 months with mild texture degradation. Cooked: refrigerated in cooking liquid, 5 days; frozen in cooking liquid, 6 months. Reheating: in the original cooking liquid, covered, at gentle heat; high-heat reheating dries the meat. Failure modes: gray-green discoloration (oxidation, edible after trimming), slimy texture or off smell (bacterial spoilage, discard).

Use the same weight, expect a 30 to 60 minute longer cook time for chuck and 30 minutes shorter for shank.'
