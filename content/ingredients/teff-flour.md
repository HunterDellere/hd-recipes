about: |
  Teff is the smallest cultivated grain in the world, roughly 1 mm long and harvested by the millions per square meter from a tall grass native to the Ethiopian highlands. Because the kernel is so small, the bran and germ stay with the endosperm in any practical milling, which is why teff flour is whole-grain by default and richer in iron, calcium, and resistant starch than any common cereal flour. Two color varieties dominate, ivory (more delicate, more expensive, the prestige version) and brown (more robust, earthier, the everyday version); both work for injera and both ferment readily.

  Functionally teff flour does one thing better than any other gluten-free flour: it ferments cleanly. Mixed with water and left at room temperature, the wild yeasts and lactic-acid bacteria on the kernel surface drive a 1 to 3 day sour fermentation that produces injera, the bubbly tangy flatbread that is plate and utensil in Ethiopian eating. The fermentation is more vigorous than wheat sourdough; the bubbles in the finished injera (the "eyes") come from CO2 trapped in a batter loose enough to spread thin.

  Outside injera, teff flour bakes well into quick breads and porridges, contributes a malty earthy note to gluten-free pancake and muffin recipes, and ferments into the Ethiopian beer t'ella. It is not interchangeable with rice flour or sorghum flour in injera; only teff carries the right wild-ferment microflora and the right starch profile for the characteristic crumb.

storage: 'Airtight container in a cool dry pantry, 2 to 3 months. The whole-grain bran fraction makes teff flour prone to rancidity faster than refined wheat flour; refrigerate or freeze for longer storage (6 months refrigerated, a year frozen). Failure mode is rancid-oil aroma; if the bag smells of paint or stale nut, the bran has oxidized and the flour will read off in the finished injera.'
