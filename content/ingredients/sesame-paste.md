about: |
  Chinese sesame paste (芝麻酱, zhima jiang) is produced by roasting white sesame seeds heavily, almost to the brink of bitterness, before stone-grinding them with their oil into a thick, dark amber-brown paste. The roasting is the whole story. At the toasting temperatures used, sesame seeds undergo extensive Maillard reaction between their proteins and the trace sugars in the seed coat, generating pyrazines, furans, and pyrroles that drive the deep nutty-roasted character. Volatile sesamol and sesamolin are also liberated and partially oxidized to compounds that carry the faintly bitter, faintly smoky finish that defines the paste. The flavor that results sits a full register darker than tahini, which is made from raw or only lightly toasted seeds and reads creamy, light, and sweet by comparison.

  Functional role: Chinese sesame paste serves as a concentrated source of nutty depth, body, and emulsifying capacity in a single ingredient. The ground seeds release oil and protein that together form a paste capable of binding water-based sauces (vinegar, soy, chili oil) into a creamy emulsion that coats noodles or dressings without breaking. It carries roasted aromatics into both cold and hot applications, and its bitterness balances the sweetness of soy and the heat of chili oil in ways that cleaner pastes cannot.

  Across regional Chinese and adjacent Asian cooking, zhima jiang shows up in: cold sesame noodle preparations (Sichuan-style cold noodles, Beijing zhima jiang ban mian, Hong Kong-style sesame chicken salad); hot sesame noodle traditions (Sichuan dan dan mian, ma jiang mian, Beijing-style za jiang mian when the sauce is sesame-rich); hot pot dipping sauces (the canonical Beijing-style mutton hot pot dip is sesame paste base plus fermented bean curd, leek flowers, and chili oil); cold-dressed vegetables (sesame-and-garlic spinach, smashed cucumbers); steamed and cold buns and breads (sesame paste filling in fang gao, sesame swirl bread); and porridge sweeteners and fillings (zhima hu, the ancient Cantonese sesame soup; sesame-paste-filled tang yuan). Across Korean cooking, the closest analog is roasted sesame oil rather than paste; across Japanese cooking, kuro goma (black sesame) paste plays a similar role in goma-ae and sweet preparations like kuro goma pudding.

  How it differs from tahini: tahini is the Levantine-Mediterranean cousin made from raw or lightly hulled sesame seeds, ground without significant pre-roasting. Tahini is paler (cream to light tan), looser-bodied, sweeter, and dramatically less roasted on the palate. Substituting tahini directly for Chinese sesame paste produces a dish that reads flat and sweet, missing the toasted depth that the recipe was built around. The fix is to toast tahini-suitable seeds further or to compensate with toasted sesame oil added to the tahini, which is the working substitution when zhima jiang is unavailable.

  How it differs from black sesame paste: black sesame paste, a traditional Chinese and Japanese ingredient, is made from black sesame seeds (a different cultivar with darker, more mineral-edged flavor). It reads sweeter, smokier, and more dessert-leaning, and shows up primarily in sweet preparations (sesame paste mochi, sesame ice cream, black sesame pudding, lunar-new-year tang yuan filling). It is not interchangeable with white sesame paste in savory applications.

  How it differs from peanut butter: roasted natural peanut butter shares the textural role and the toasted-fat depth, and substitutes acceptably in Sichuan-style cold sauces and dan dan mian when the paste is just one of several aromatic components. It carries a distinctly different flavor that reads through; not a clean substitute when the sesame character is meant to lead.

  Working with the paste: oil rises to the top of the jar. Stir vigorously before measuring or pour off and reincorporate; an unstirred jar gives an inaccurate ratio. To loosen the paste into a pourable sauce, whisk in warm water, noodle water, or the recipe''s acid component (vinegar, lemon) in small increments. Adding all the liquid at once causes the paste to seize into a thick clump before relaxing; small increments produce a smooth emulsion. Acid added after some water has been integrated keeps the emulsion stable.

  Buying notes: Lan Chi (兰花) and Wang Zhi He are common reliable Chinese brands. The label should list only sesame seeds (and possibly salt); brands that include hydrogenated oils, stabilizers, or sugar are downgraded products. The paste should be dark amber to brown, not greasy-looking, and the released oil on top should be clear and faintly amber. Storage at the back of an Asian-grocery shelf for too long darkens the surface oil toward rancidity; sniff before buying when possible.

storage: 'Pantry before opening, away from heat and light. After opening, refrigerate to slow oxidation of the unsaturated sesame oil. Keeps 6+ months refrigerated; the surface oil can go rancid before the paste below it does, so check by smell before each use. Failure mode is a sharp, crayon-like or paint-thinner aroma in the surface oil signaling rancidity; if the rest smells clean after stirring, the paste is usable, but if the rancid note persists through, discard. Stir vigorously before each use to redistribute the oil that has separated.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'Chinese sesame paste (cold sesame sauces, noodle dressings, hot pot dips)'
    use: 'tahini + 1 tsp toasted sesame oil per 3 tbsp tahini'
    note: 'The sesame oil compensates for the missing toasted depth. Closest practical substitute when zhima jiang is unreachable. Whisk thoroughly to integrate the oil before adding other sauce components.'
  - for: 'Chinese sesame paste (dan dan mian, dishes where chili oil leads)'
    use: 'unsweetened natural peanut butter + 1 tsp toasted sesame oil per 3 tbsp peanut butter'
    note: 'Peanut and sesame share roasted fat character; in dishes where the chili oil and aromatics dominate, the swap reads correct. Wrong in delicate cold sesame applications where the sesame character itself is the lead.'
  - for: 'Chinese sesame paste (sweet applications, fillings, dessert preparations)'
    use: 'black sesame paste'
    note: 'Different cultivar (black sesame); sweeter, smokier, more dessert-leaning. Use 1:1 in tang yuan fillings, sesame mochi, and dessert porridges. Wrong in savory applications where the white-sesame profile is meant.'
  - for: 'Chinese sesame paste (savory applications, allergy-driven)'
    use: 'sunflower seed butter + toasted sesame oil'
    note: 'Sunflower is the closest seed-allergy-friendly base; loses sesame flavor entirely but retains the textural and emulsifying role. Add 1 tsp toasted sesame oil per 3 tbsp sunbutter to fake some of the aroma.'
  - for: 'Chinese sesame paste (homemade, fresh)'
    use: 'toasted sesame seeds, ground in a blender with neutral oil'
    note: 'Toast 100 g white sesame seeds in a dry pan over medium heat until deeply golden and fragrant (10–12 minutes, watching carefully). Cool, then blend with 30 g neutral oil until a coarse paste forms. Closer to commercial paste than any substitute and adjustable to taste.'
