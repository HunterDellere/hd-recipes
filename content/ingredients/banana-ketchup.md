about: |
  Banana ketchup originated during World War II when tomato shortages and abundant Philippine banana harvests pushed food technologist Maria Orosa to formulate a ketchup-style condiment from mashed saba (cooking banana), vinegar, sugar, garlic, onion, and spices, tinted red with food coloring to look like tomato ketchup on the table. The improvisation outlasted the shortage; banana ketchup is now a legitimate Filipino staple sold globally under Jufran and UFC Banana Sauce.

  Flavor is sweet-front with the soft, fruity backbone of banana, a vinegar tang sharper than tomato ketchup, and a faint warm-spice (cinnamon-clove-allspice) profile. Texture is glossy and slightly thicker than American tomato ketchup. The red food coloring is cosmetic; nothing in the actual recipe is red, and "natural" banana ketchups (sold by smaller brands) are an opaque tan-yellow.

  Two real applications. As a table condiment for Filipino fried foods (lumpia, longanisa, tocino, fried chicken). And as the structural sweet acid in Filipino spaghetti, the sweet, hot-dog-and-ground-beef pasta sauce that defines Filipino-American birthday parties; the banana ketchup replaces some or all of the tomato sauce and gives Filipino spaghetti its distinct sweet-tang flavor that no Italian spaghetti shares.

  Brand standard: Jufran (the global export brand) is sweeter and brighter, UFC is slightly more vinegary. Both are widely available in Filipino groceries and increasingly in mainstream Asian groceries.

storage: 'Pantry indefinitely unopened. After opening, refrigerate; lasts 6 months. The high sugar and vinegar content prevents spoilage; the color fades over time but the flavor holds.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'banana ketchup (in cooking)'
    use: 'tomato ketchup + a pinch of cinnamon + 1 tbsp mashed ripe banana per cup'
    note: 'Approximates the warm-spice and banana notes. The texture is close; the flavor is not quite right but workable for Filipino spaghetti or as a fried-food dip.'
  - for: 'banana ketchup (as table condiment)'
    use: 'tomato ketchup + a splash of apple cider vinegar'
    note: 'A non-Filipino kitchen substitute that gets the sweet-tart axis without trying to mimic the banana note. Acceptable for non-essential applications.'
