about: |
  Shrimp flesh is dense myosin-rich muscle laced with a small amount of glycine and free amino acids; that's the source of the sweet, almost candy-like backnote in well-cooked shrimp. The window between "translucent and raw" and "rubber-tight" is narrow because shrimp protein begins to denature aggressively around 60°C and the muscle fibers contract on themselves once they cross ~65°C, squeezing out water. The cook's job is to land them at 60–63°C internal and pull immediately.

  Buy IQF (individually quick frozen), shell-on if possible. "Fresh" shrimp at most US fish counters is previously frozen and thawed by the store; you'll do a better job thawing them yourself. Look for **U/15** through **21/25** counts (shrimp per pound) for sautéing; smaller shrimp lose the heat window and turn rubbery before they sear.

  Wild Gulf shrimp and Argentine red shrimp have noticeably more flavor than farmed white shrimp; pink shrimp from the Atlantic and Argentine reds also cook a touch faster (softer flesh).

  Always devein along the back. The "vein" is the digestive tract; not dangerous, but gritty and bitter. Shell-on cooking gives more flavor and protects the flesh; remove shells before serving unless the dish is a peel-and-eat.

  A 0.5% by weight saline brine (or a 1% baking-soda dust) for 15 minutes before cooking changes the surface protein chemistry, holds water, and yields a noticeably snappier bite. This is the same principle behind Cantonese velveting.

storage: 'Frozen at -18°C indefinitely. Thaw in cold water (15 min) or overnight in the fridge; never at room temp. Once thawed, cook within 24 hours.'
