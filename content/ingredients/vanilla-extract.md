about: |
  Real vanilla extract is cured vanilla beans steeped in ~35% ethanol. The alcohol pulls out vanillin (the dominant aroma) plus 250+ minor compounds — guaiacol, anisaldehyde, hydroxybenzaldehyde, and a long tail of esters and phenolics. Those minor compounds are the difference between vanilla that smells round and vanilla that smells flat.

  Imitation vanilla is synthetic vanillin alone (often from lignin or guaiacol). It nails the headline note and misses the chorus. Fine in a brownie where chocolate dominates; obvious in a custard or buttercream where vanilla is the lead.

  Heat strips the volatile top notes. In baked goods this matters less — the structure traps enough of the heavier compounds. In stovetop applications (custard, pastry cream, ice cream base), add the extract off-heat after cooking, or you're paying for aroma that boiled off.

  Double-strength extract (2x) exists for commercial use; halve the quantity if you've got it.

storage: 'Dark cupboard, tightly capped. Indefinite shelf life — alcohol preserves it. Light and air slowly oxidize the aromatics, so don''t leave the bottle open or on a sunny shelf.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'vanilla extract'
    use: 'vanilla bean paste'
    note: '1:1 swap. Same flavor, plus visible seeds. Better in anything where you see the vanilla (ice cream, panna cotta, frosting).'
  - for: 'vanilla extract'
    use: 'whole vanilla pod'
    note: '~1 pod per tablespoon of extract. Split, scrape seeds into the dish, steep the pod in any liquid component, then pull it.'
  - for: 'vanilla extract'
    use: 'imitation vanilla'
    note: '1:1, but only in recipes where vanilla is a background note (chocolate cakes, brownies). Skip in custards, buttercreams, ice cream.'
