about: |
  Bun (Vietnamese) is a thin, round rice-flour noodle, about 1 mm in diameter, sold dried in folded nests of 250 to 500 g. Made from rice flour and water, sometimes with a small fraction of tapioca starch for chew; the dried noodle is brittle and ivory-white, the cooked noodle is soft, slick, and faintly translucent. Differs from banh pho (the flat rice noodle of pho) in shape and from glass noodles (mien) in composition (mien is mung bean starch, not rice).

  The defining preparation is the cold or room-temperature noodle base. Boil 2 to 3 minutes until just tender, drain, rinse aggressively under cold water to stop the cook and wash off surface starch, then drain again and toss with a few drops of neutral oil to prevent clumping. The cooked noodle holds at room temperature for 2 to 3 hours without quality loss; this is what makes bun the platform noodle for room-temperature compositions (bun cha, bun bo Hue at home, bun thit nuong, goi cuon).

  Critical cooking note. Bun overcooks fast. The window between al dente and mushy is about 30 seconds; pull a strand at 2:30 and bite-test before draining. Soggy bun ruins the dish; the noodle should have a faint resistance, not the soft-paste texture of overcooked rice noodle. The cold rinse is also non-negotiable, both to stop the cook and to remove the surface starch that turns gummy at room temperature.

  Brand standard: Three Ladies (Vietnamese, the green-package vermicelli), Sailing Boat (Thai). Both are widely available in Asian groceries. Avoid generic "rice stick noodle" without the round-vermicelli specification; flat rice noodles look similar in package and cook differently.

storage: 'Pantry indefinitely in the original sleeve. Cooked vermicelli holds 2 to 3 hours at room temperature, or refrigerated 24 hours (re-rinse briefly in warm water before using to loosen).'
