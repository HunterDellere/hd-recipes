about: |
  Most commercial light brown sugar is white granulated sugar with refinery molasses sprayed back on — roughly 3.5% by weight. It is not an "unrefined" sugar in any meaningful sense. The molasses is the active ingredient.

  That molasses changes three things at once. It adds water (~10% of the molasses by weight), so a brown-sugar cookie has more free moisture than a granulated one, meaning more steam in the oven, more spread, a chewier set. It adds acid (pH ~5), which weakens gluten and accelerates Maillard browning, deepening color and crust flavor. And it adds the molasses itself (toffee, raisin, faint smokiness), which reads as "caramel" even though no caramelization has happened yet.

  Pack-by-volume is the crime scene of brown-sugar baking. A loose cup and a packed cup can differ by 30–40%. American recipes usually mean "firmly packed" but rarely say so. The fix, again, is grams: 1 cup packed is ~213g.

storage: 'Airtight container at room temperature. Failure mode is moisture loss: exposed to air, the molasses dries and the sugar hardens into a brick. Revive by sealing it overnight with a slice of bread or a damp paper towel; the sugar pulls moisture back from the bread.'
