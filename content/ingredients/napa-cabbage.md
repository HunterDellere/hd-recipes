about: |
  Napa cabbage (Brassica rapa subsp. pekinensis) is a leafy heading brassica native to the area near Beijing (the name "napa" derives from the Japanese word for vegetable leaves, "nappa"; the Chinese name is dà bái cài or "big white vegetable"). The head is long and oblong (15 to 30 cm tall, 10 to 15 cm wide), the leaves are pale green with broad white-to-cream colored ribs (the white portion is the structural midrib of each leaf, much more substantial than in European head cabbage), and the texture is delicate and water-rich. A whole head weighs 1 to 2 kg; smaller heads sold as "baby napa" are 300 to 600 g and have proportionally more leaf and less rib.

  Composition and water content. Napa is roughly 95% water by weight, with a sugar content slightly higher than European cabbage (the sweet character is why napa kimchi reads sweeter than sauerkraut even before fermentation) and a much lower glucosinolate content (the sulfur compounds that give cabbage its "cabbagey" smell are present in napa at a fraction of the level in round cabbage). The high water content drives all the cooking behavior: napa wilts dramatically in heat (a head that fills a wok at the start collapses to a third of its volume after 3 minutes), releases significant water during cooking that becomes part of the dish's liquid, and absorbs flavors readily once the cellular structure breaks down.

  Leaf vs rib structure. The leafy green portion and the white rib portion of each napa leaf cook at different rates and are sometimes separated by careful Chinese cooks. The leaves are delicate and tender, wilting in 30 to 60 seconds of high heat; the ribs are crunchier, more water-rich, and take 2 to 4 minutes to soften to the same texture. For stir-fries where the cook wants a layered texture (tender leaves, slightly crunchy ribs), the two are separated and the ribs added first (giving them a 1 to 2 minute head start before the leaves). For braises and soups where both are cooked to full tenderness, the separation is unnecessary; the whole leaf is cut crosswise into 2 to 3 cm strips and added together.

  Salting for water release. The Korean kimchi tradition and the Chinese dumpling-filling tradition both rely on salt-rest-rinse to manage napa's water content. The vegetable is roughly chopped, tossed with salt (about 2% by weight), and left to rest 30 to 60 minutes; the salt draws water out of the cells via osmosis (visible as a pool of brine at the bottom of the bowl), and the cabbage softens and reduces in volume. For kimchi, this brine is then rinsed off (the salt has done its work, both drawing water and seasoning the cabbage) and the cabbage is dressed with the fermentation paste. For dumpling filling, the salted cabbage is squeezed dry by hand or in a clean towel, releasing 30 to 50% of its initial weight as brine; this prevents the dumpling from rupturing during steaming or frying as the cabbage gives off water. The salt-and-squeeze step is the structural move that separates a tight, juicy dumpling from a soggy, leaking one.

  Cooking applications by category. **Korean**: kimchi (baechu kimchi, the canonical fermented cabbage; the sweet character of napa is what gives Korean kimchi its complex sour-sweet-spicy register, and the rib structure is what holds shape after months of fermentation), various jjigae and guk soups. **Chinese (northern)**: dumpling and bun fillings (jiaozi, baozi, the most common Chinese filling base), lion''s head braises (where the cabbage cushions the meatballs and absorbs the braising stock), hot pot (the canonical green leafy hot pot vegetable, added in the last minutes), stir-fries with dried shrimp or wood ear mushroom, vinegar-glazed cabbage as a banquet side. **Chinese (southern, Cantonese)**: silkier preparations including napa with cream sauce or with crab roe, congee garnishes. **Japanese**: shabu-shabu and sukiyaki (the canonical leafy vegetable in both hot-pot styles), gyoza fillings (Japanese dumpling tradition borrowed the cabbage base from China), pickled tsukemono. **Vietnamese, Thai (limited)**: in some northern Vietnamese stir-fries and pickled preparations; less common than mustard greens, water spinach, or yu choy in Southeast Asian cooking. **Western adoption**: increasingly common in American supermarket cooking as the "Asian salad" base (with sesame oil, rice vinegar, peanuts), as a hot-pot ingredient, and as a slaw component (more delicate and faster-wilting than green cabbage, so dress just before serving).

  Buying notes. Asian markets (H Mart, 99 Ranch, Patel Brothers, Vietnamese groceries) carry napa year-round at low cost; supermarket napa (Whole Foods, Trader Joe''s, mainstream chains) is widely available but typically 2 to 3x the price of the Asian-market version. Choose heads that are firm and heavy for their size (loose, light heads have lost water and will wilt limply rather than crisply), with bright pale-green leaves and pure white ribs (yellow or brown discoloration is age), and tight ribs at the base (loose splayed bases suggest the head is past peak). A good head should feel substantial in the hand and the leaves should crackle slightly when squeezed at the base. Baby napa (small whole heads) is excellent for hot pot where the whole leaf-rib structure is wanted and the cabbage cooks faster. Pre-cut napa in plastic bags is acceptable for stir-fry and soup but loses water and quality fast (use within 2 days of purchase).

  Common Western drift. (1) Treating napa as a direct substitute for green cabbage in coleslaw or sauerkraut; the higher water content and more delicate leaves make napa unsuitable for long-aged sauerkraut (it goes mushy in days, where green cabbage holds for months) and it weeps water in coleslaw within an hour of dressing. The two cabbages are not interchangeable for those preparations. (2) Overcooking napa in stir-fries until it goes limp and grey; the right cook is a fast wilt (30 to 90 seconds), still bright green with the ribs slightly tender but retaining some bite. (3) Skipping the salt-and-squeeze step in dumpling fillings, producing dumplings that leak water and rupture during cooking.

storage: |
  Whole head, refrigerated in the crisper drawer wrapped loosely in a damp paper towel inside a perforated bag: 1 to 2 weeks (napa keeps significantly longer than most leafy vegetables). Cut head, wrapped in plastic with the cut surface against the wrap: 4 to 6 days. Once the outer leaves begin to discolor or wilt, peel them off; the inner head is typically fine for another several days. Failure modes are slimy or sticky leaf surfaces (advanced spoilage, often starting in the leafy tips before the ribs), brown spots on the rib bases (oxidation, trim away), and a sulfurous off-smell (advanced decay, discard). Frozen napa loses all structural integrity (the cells burst when ice crystals form) and is only usable for braises and soups where the texture loss is invisible.

substitutions:
  - for: 'napa cabbage (Chinese braises, lion''s head, hot pot)'
    use: 'savoy cabbage or green cabbage'
    note: 'Savoy is the closest substitute structurally (similar leaf-rib distinction, slightly tougher) and works in any braise or hot pot application. Green cabbage is denser, more sulfurous, and reads as a different vegetable; works in lion''s head and hot pot but the dish feels heavier. Both substitute 1:1 by weight; bump the cook time 30 to 50% to compensate for the denser leaves.'
  - for: 'napa cabbage (dumpling and bun filling)'
    use: 'green cabbage (with the salt-and-squeeze step extended), or bok choy'
    note: 'Green cabbage works in dumpling filling with a longer salt-rest (60 to 90 minutes instead of 30 to 45) to break down the denser leaves; the filling reads slightly tougher but is recognizable. Bok choy (finely chopped, salt-rested, squeezed) is the Cantonese variant and produces a more tender filling with a more pronounced green flavor.'
  - for: 'napa cabbage (kimchi base)'
    use: 'green cabbage, daikon radish, or cucumber'
    note: 'Green cabbage kimchi is a legitimate variation but reads less sweet and more sulfurous; daikon (kkakdugi) and cucumber (oi sobagi) are recognized non-cabbage kimchi varieties in the Korean canon. None of these are 1:1 substitutes for napa baechu kimchi specifically; each is its own kimchi style.'
