about: |
  Sodium alginate is the sodium salt of alginic acid, harvested from the cell walls of Laminaria and other brown algae. In water it dissolves into a viscous neutral solution. When that solution contacts calcium ions, the sodium swaps for calcium and the alginate chains cross-link instantly into a flexible gel, the membrane that holds a popping-boba sphere together while the inside stays liquid.

  Two spherification methods exist. Basic spherification drops alginate-flavored liquid into a calcium bath; the membrane forms outward into the bath, and the spheres must be eaten quickly because gelation continues until the whole interior sets. Reverse spherification drops calcium-flavored liquid into an alginate bath; the membrane forms inward, then the sphere is rinsed and can be held for hours with the liquid center stable. Popping boba production uses basic spherification at industrial scale, with stabilizers; the homemade version using reverse spherification gives the best amateur results for held service.

  Hydration is the failure point. Sodium alginate clumps aggressively when sprinkled into water; the dry particles get a gel skin that traps the powder inside. Two solutions: (1) blend the powder in slowly with an immersion blender, or (2) pre-mix the powder with sugar or another dry ingredient so the particles disperse before water hits. Let hydrated solutions rest 4 to 12 hours refrigerated before use; trapped air bubbles otherwise lift the spheres in the calcium bath instead of letting them sink and form clean rounds.

  Calcium chloride is the standard cross-linker for the bath; calcium lactate gives a milder taste and is preferred for reverse spherification where the calcium is in the liquid being eaten.

  Acidic liquids (pH < 3.5) below set time pull alginate out of solution as alginic acid, which doesn't gel cleanly. Buffer with sodium citrate (1 g/L) for citrus juices.

storage: 'Airtight container, cool dry pantry. Two years dry. Once hydrated in solution, refrigerate and use within a week; viscosity slowly decreases as polymer chains degrade.'
