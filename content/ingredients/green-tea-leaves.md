about: |
  Green tea is Camellia sinensis leaf that has been prevented from oxidizing. The defining processing step is "kill-green" (sassha in Chinese, killing the enzyme): within hours of plucking, the leaf is heated, by steaming in the Japanese tradition or by pan-firing or tumbling in the Chinese, to denature polyphenol oxidase, the enzyme that would otherwise brown the leaf and convert its catechins into the theaflavins and thearubigins of black tea. Halt that enzyme early and the leaf stays green, retains its catechins (chiefly EGCG), and brews into a grassy, vegetal, faintly astringent cup. Let the enzyme run and you get black tea. The plant is identical; the divergence is entirely a matter of how fast and how hard the fresh leaf is heated after plucking.

  Two roles, one leaf. As a beverage, green tea is brewed and discarded: 2 to 3 g per 240 ml of water held at 70 to 80°C for 1 to 2 minutes, deliberately below boiling because the catechins and caffeine that drive bitterness extract faster at high temperature than the sweet, savory L-theanine does. Steep green tea with boiling water and you pull a harsh, drying cup; steep it gently and the amino-acid sweetness comes through. As food, the tender top leaves and buds are the eating substrate of Burmese lahpet (pickled tea), where steamed young leaf is packed under pressure and lacto-fermented for weeks into a soft, sour, faintly bitter green mass that is then dressed and eaten, not drunk. This is one of very few traditions on earth that eats the tea leaf rather than infusing it.

  Leaf grade and form. For the cup, leaf style is the main variable: Japanese sencha (steamed, needle-shaped, marine-vegetal), gyokuro (shade-grown, intensely umami), and bancha (later-harvest, coarse, mild); Chinese gunpowder (rolled into pellets that unfurl), dragonwell/longjing (pan-fired flat, chestnut-sweet), and the green base of genmaicha. For eating as lahpet, the relevant material is whole tender young-leaf-and-bud picked specifically for fermentation, traditionally from Shan State; outside Myanmar it is bought ready-fermented in vacuum packs rather than made from raw leaf, because raw unprocessed fermentation-grade leaf is not exported. A cook making lahpet from scratch outside Myanmar works from fresh or frozen tender green tea leaf, or, as a practical proxy, from good loose-leaf sencha rehydrated and fermented (the [[lahpet-fermented-tea-leaves]] recipe covers both routes).

  Why temperature governs everything. The catechin fraction (astringent, bitter, antioxidant) and the L-theanine fraction (sweet, savory, calming) extract on different curves: catechins and caffeine rise steeply with temperature, theanine far more gently. Every decision in green tea, brewing temperature, steep time, the shading that raises theanine in gyokuro and matcha, is a lever on that ratio. The same logic governs the fermentation of lahpet, where weeks of anaerobic lactic acidification convert the raw leaf's harsh astringency into a mellow sourness the way time, rather than heat, tames the catechins.

seasonality: 'First-flush spring leaf (shincha in Japan, pre-Qingming in China) is the prized harvest, sweeter and lower in catechins. Fermentation-grade leaf for lahpet is picked in the rainy-season flushes. Dry leaf is a year-round pantry good.'

storage: |
  Dry green tea leaf is far more perishable than black tea: the retained chlorophyll and catechins oxidize on contact with air, light, heat, and moisture, and a green tea past its prime goes flat, hay-like, and dull-yellow in the cup. Store airtight in an opaque tin away from heat and light, ideally refrigerated for unopened premium leaf, and use within 4 to 6 months of opening. The cue for staleness is smell: fresh green tea smells of cut grass and seaweed; dead green tea smells of nothing, or of dusty hay. Fermented lahpet is a different keeper, sealed under its oil and brine it holds for weeks refrigerated and only deepens, but exposed to air it molds; keep it submerged and capped.
