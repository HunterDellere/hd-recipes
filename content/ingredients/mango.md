about: |
  Mango (Mangifera indica) is the fleshy stone fruit of a large tropical evergreen tree native to South Asia and cultivated across the tropics for at least 4,000 years. The fruit ranges from 150 g (small Ataulfo, Indian Kesar) to 600 g (large Kent, Tommy Atkins), with a thin tough skin (green, yellow, red, or mottled depending on cultivar), a fibrous or smooth orange flesh, and a flat fibrous central pit. Ripe mango flesh is roughly 84% water, 15% carbohydrate (mostly fructose and sucrose, with smaller amounts of glucose), 0.8% protein, and 0.4% fat; it carries a complex aromatic profile dominated by lactones (the canonical "mango" smell), terpenes (myrcene, ocimene), and esters that vary widely by cultivar. Unripe mango is firmer, paler, and aggressively tart from citric and malic acid; the carbohydrate fraction is mostly starch that has not yet converted to sugar.

  Functional role across ripeness states. Ripe mango is a sweet fruit, used in lassi, sorbet, salsa, fresh-eating, batido, smoothie, and sweet preparations. Unripe (green) mango is structurally a vegetable: firm, tart, faintly grassy, and used as the principal acid carrier and crunch source in Thai som tam (green papaya is interchangeable in many versions), Vietnamese goi xoai (green mango salad with dried shrimp and Thai chili), Indian aam panna (a salted-and-sweetened green mango cooler), and Indian kachumber-style raw salads. The cultivar matters less than the ripeness state for green-mango applications; for ripe applications, the cultivar matters enormously.

  Cultivar map. Alphonso (Indian Hapus): the most aromatic and most expensive cultivar, available frozen pulp year-round in Indian groceries and fresh for a 6-week April-to-June window; lactone-and-honey aromatic, smooth fiberless flesh. Kesar (Indian Gujarati): similar window, slightly less aromatic, slightly tarter; the canonical mango for shrikhand and kulfi. Ataulfo / Champagne / Honey (Mexican): small (150 to 200 g), yellow skin, kidney-shaped, smooth flesh, low fiber, mild-sweet; the most accessible high-quality mango at North American supermarkets and the right choice when no Indian cultivar is available. Kent (Mexican, Floridian): large, red-shouldered, smooth flesh, sweet-mild, the most accessible large cultivar. Tommy Atkins (Mexican, Floridian): the most-produced cultivar globally; fibrous, mild, often shipped underripe; reads as generic "American supermarket mango." Keitt (Mexican, late season): green-skinned even when ripe, low fiber, sweet, good for slicing.

  Ripeness cues and chemistry. A ripe mango yields slightly to gentle thumb pressure at the stem end, the skin gives off the lactone aromatic at the stem when sniffed, and the color (for cultivars that color-shift, not Keitt) has reached the genetic peak. Underripe mango has high starch and high acid; the fruit can be ripened on the counter at 18 to 22 °C for 2 to 4 days until soft, during which amylase enzymes convert starch to sugar and the malic acid degrades. Refrigeration arrests ripening; ripe mangoes can be refrigerated for 5 to 7 days but should not be refrigerated when underripe. Overripe mango begins to ferment and develops alcoholic-acetic off-notes; the texture goes from firm-yielding to mushy-stringy.

  The urushiol caveat. Mango is in the Anacardiaceae family alongside cashews, pistachios, and poison ivy. The skin, leaves, and sap of the mango tree contain urushiol-related compounds (specifically cardol and resorcinol derivatives) that can cause contact dermatitis in poison-ivy-sensitive individuals; the reaction is localized to skin contact with the peel or sap, not to eating the flesh. Cooks with known poison-ivy sensitivity should peel mangoes wearing gloves or have someone else peel them. The flesh itself is safe and eaten without restriction.

  Heat and acid behavior. Cooked ripe mango (in chutneys, jams, salsas heated briefly): the sugars caramelize and the lactones partly volatilize, producing a deeper amber-honey flavor at the cost of the fresh top notes; for chutneys and jams this is the point, for salsas it is a trade-off. Cooked green mango (in pickles, curries): the firm flesh holds shape well under brief cooking but softens steadily in long simmers; for the canonical Goan and South Indian green-mango curries, the mango is added in the last 5 to 8 minutes to preserve some texture. Acid behavior: ripe mango holds its color and texture in acidic dressings for several hours but begins to leach water after 6 to 8 hours; for make-ahead grain salads with diced ripe mango, dress shortly before serving or accept some dressing dilution.

  Applications by category. Sweet preparations: Indian mango lassi (Alphonso pulp + yogurt + sugar + cardamom), Indian aamras (puréed ripe mango served with puris), Mexican mango con chile y limón (cubed fresh mango with chili-lime salt and lime juice as the canonical Mexico City street snack), Thai khao niao mamuang (ripe mango with coconut sticky rice), Filipino mango sago. Savory preparations: Thai green mango salad (yum mamuang) and Vietnamese green mango salad (goi xoai) using shredded unripe mango, dried shrimp, fish sauce, chili, lime; West African and Caribbean diced-mango salsas with red onion, lime, scotch bonnet, cilantro; Indian aam ka achaar (green mango pickle in mustard oil and spices); Indian aam panna (salted-sweetened-spiced green mango cooler).

  Cutting. The pit runs flat through the long axis of the fruit; the canonical cut is to stand the fruit on end and slice down each side of the flat pit to yield two large "cheeks." Each cheek can be score-cubed (cut a grid into the flesh down to but not through the skin, then invert the skin so the cubes pop outward) for diced applications, or peeled and free-sliced for thinner cuts. Yield from a 300 g fresh-weight mango: roughly 180 to 220 g of flesh after pit and skin, 60 to 75% by weight. Indian frozen pulp (Alphonso, Kesar) is sold in 850 g cans at most Indian groceries and is the cleanest source for lassi and ice cream when fresh Alphonso is out of season.

storage: |
  Unripe mangoes (firm to the thumb, no aromatic at the stem) store at room temperature, 18 to 22 °C, on a counter out of direct sun, for 2 to 5 days to ripen. Ripening accelerates in a paper bag with a banana or apple (ethylene off-gassing). Ripe mangoes (yielding to thumb, aromatic) refrigerate in the produce drawer for 5 to 7 days; the skin may develop dark blotches without flavor impact. Cut mango refrigerates 3 to 4 days in an airtight container, or freezes 6 months in 1-cm cubes on a parchment-lined sheet pan (flash-freeze, then bag for storage). Mango pulp in cans (Alphonso, Kesar) refrigerates 5 to 7 days after opening; transfer to a non-metal container. Discard any mango that smells alcoholic-fermented, leaks brown fluid, or shows surface mold.

substitutions:
  - for: 'mango (ripe, fresh-eating or diced into salad)'
    use: 'ripe peach, or ripe nectarine, or ripe papaya'
    note: 'Peach and nectarine are the closest North American summer analogs; the lactone-honey aromatic is different but the structural role (sweet, firm, juicy, holds in a dressing) is preserved. Papaya is the closest tropical analog; similar texture and similar moderate-sweetness, with a slightly muskier aromatic that some find divisive.'
  - for: 'mango (unripe, green, shredded for salad)'
    use: 'green papaya, or tart firm apple (Granny Smith), or jicama'
    note: 'Green papaya is the canonical Thai-equivalent substitute and the recipe in question (som tam, goi) is often built on either ingredient interchangeably. Green apple gives the crunch and tartness but reads as fruit rather than as vegetable; jicama gives the crunch but is too sweet and needs a tartness boost from extra lime.'
  - for: 'frozen Alphonso pulp (for lassi, kulfi, sorbet)'
    use: 'puréed ripe Ataulfo mango plus a pinch of cardamom and a small amount of saffron'
    note: 'Ataulfo is the closest accessible North American analog to Alphonso; the puréed flesh of 2 to 3 ripe Ataulfos plus the cardamom-saffron correction reads close to canned Alphonso for most purposes. No fresh mango outside of India reproduces Alphonso exactly.'
