about: |
  Allspice (called pimento in Jamaica, its native island) is the dried unripe berry of Pimenta dioica, an evergreen tree in the myrtle family. The name allspice comes from the aroma, which carries notes of cinnamon, clove, and nutmeg simultaneously; the principal aromatic compound is eugenol (also the dominant compound in clove), with myrcene, methyl eugenol, and beta-caryophyllene as supporting notes.

  Jamaica produces over 70% of the world supply and Jamaican allspice is broadly considered the highest-quality source (the volatile oil content is highest in berries from the island's particular growing conditions). The whole berry is about 5 mm across, dark brown, slightly rough-skinned; ground allspice oxidizes quickly (3 to 4 months at peak aroma) so whole berries plus on-demand grinding is the practical workflow for any cook who uses it regularly.

  Functional category: warming aromatic. Allspice carries the largest aromatic load of any single warming spice (clove, cinnamon, nutmeg, allspice); the eugenol concentration is high enough that it dominates blends unless dosed carefully. Toasting whole berries briefly in a dry pan (30 to 45 seconds, until fragrant) and grinding fresh is the structural prep step that distinguishes a deep jerk paste from a flat one.

  Applications by category. Caribbean: Jamaican jerk paste (the structural spice, traditionally smoked over allspice wood as well), Jamaican curry powder, sorrel drink. Middle Eastern: Lebanese seven-spice (bahārāt with allspice as one component), Turkish meat dishes. European baking: gingerbread, Christmas spice blends, pickling spice, mulled wine. North American: pumpkin pie spice (a modern blend with allspice as one component), Cincinnati chili. Mexican: mole poblano (a structural background spice).

storage: |
  Whole berries: sealed glass jar, cool dark pantry, 2 years at peak aroma. Ground: 3 to 4 months. Frozen whole berries: 4 years (overkill for most cooks; the pantry life is already long). Failure mode: ground allspice that smells dusty rather than aromatic (oxidized; replace).

substitutions:
  - for: 'whole allspice berries'
    use: 'a blend of 4 parts ground cinnamon, 2 parts ground clove, 1 part ground nutmeg'
    note: 'The classic substitution. Use 1 tsp of the blend per 1 tsp ground allspice. Works for baking; less convincing in Jamaican jerk where the single-berry character of true allspice carries.'
