about: |
  Sherry (Jerez, Xérès) is a fortified wine from the Sherry Triangle in Andalusia (Jerez de la Frontera, Sanlúcar de Barrameda, El Puerto de Santa María). The two dry styles structurally relevant to cooking are Fino (aged under flor yeast, pale, bone-dry, around 15 percent ABV) and Manzanilla (aged at Sanlúcar de Barrameda specifically, where the coastal humidity produces a thicker flor and a saltier, more savory wine). Both styles spend their aging period under a layer of indigenous Saccharomyces yeast that consumes residual sugar, protects the wine from oxidation, and contributes a distinctive nutty-briny character (the result of acetaldehyde and other yeast byproducts). The result is a wine with no sweetness, a strong savory backbone, and an aromatic profile of green apple, almond, and sea salt.

  Functional category: structural Spanish deglazing liquid and savory accelerant. In Spanish cooking, dry sherry is the canonical deglazing liquid for shrimp dishes (gambas al ajillo), garlic chicken (pollo al ajillo), kidneys in sherry sauce (riñones al jerez), Spanish meatballs (albóndigas en salsa de almendras), and Iberian braises. The high acid and savory yeast character cut through fat and protein, build complexity, and the rapid evaporation of alcohol over high heat concentrates the wine's flavor into a glaze. Outside Spain, dry sherry is the secret weapon in Cantonese cooking (the closest accessible substitute for Shaoxing wine), in French onion soup (a small splash added at deglazing brings depth no other wine produces), and as an under-recognized finishing splash in stocks, soups, and mushroom dishes.

  Fino vs Manzanilla vs others. Fino is the workhorse for cooking, widely available and the canonical Spanish kitchen sherry. Manzanilla is slightly more delicate and slightly saltier and is the Andalusian preference for seafood-driven dishes. Both are bone-dry and interchangeable in most recipes. Amontillado (aged longer, partially oxidized, deeper nutty character) is the next step up; Oloroso (fully oxidized, no flor, dark amber, deeply nutty) is the richest dry style and is excellent for braises and game dishes. Pedro Ximénez (PX) and Cream Sherry are sweet styles and are NOT substitutes for dry sherry in savory cooking; they are dessert wines.

  Cooking sherry vs drinking sherry. The labeled "cooking sherry" sold next to vinegar at American supermarkets is salted (to evade alcohol-sales tax) and is structurally inferior to drinking sherry. Use a real dry Fino or Manzanilla (a 750 ml bottle at $12 to $20 is the right price point); the open bottle keeps refrigerated for 3 to 4 weeks (longer than dry white wine because of the flor character, but shorter than aged oxidized styles).

  Buying. Look for sherry at any wine shop or larger liquor store. The label should say Fino or Manzanilla; the producer should be from the Sherry Triangle (Tio Pepe, La Gitana, Lustau, Hidalgo are reliable and widely distributed). Avoid "cooking sherry" and avoid bottles labeled "Cream" or "PX" for savory cooking. The wine should be pale straw to gold; if it is dark amber, it is an oxidized style (Amontillado or Oloroso) and is a different ingredient.

storage: |
  Unopened: cool dark pantry, 12 to 24 months. Once opened: refrigerated with the cork or a wine stopper, 3 to 4 weeks at peak quality (the flor character degrades after that point but the wine is still usable for cooking for another 2 to 3 months). Fino and Manzanilla are particularly sensitive to oxidation; once opened, the flor character (the bone-dry briny note) softens and the wine starts to read more like Amontillado. For long-term cooking storage, decant into a smaller bottle so the wine sits in less air contact. Failure modes: vinegar smell (severely oxidized, discard), browning to deep amber (oxidized; usable but reads as a different style now), corked / wet-cardboard smell (TCA contamination, discard).
