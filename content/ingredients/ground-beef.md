about: |
  Ground beef is whole-muscle beef passed through a meat grinder with a plate of specified hole diameter (most commonly 4.5 mm for "medium grind" supermarket ground beef, 3 mm for "fine grind" used in pâtés and sausage, 6 mm for "coarse grind" used in chili and ragu). The two variables that determine which dish the grind belongs in are the fat percentage and the cut origin. Both are usually printed on the package as a fraction like 80/20 (80% lean, 20% fat) or as a cut name like "ground chuck."

  Fat percentage. The fat content drives texture, flavor depth, and structural behavior in cooking. **70/30 ground beef** (typically from short rib, brisket, or fatty chuck) is rich, decadent, and self-bastes in the pan; the 30% fat creates the fond and the in-mouth richness that makes a great cheeseburger and a deeply savory ragu. The trade is that 70/30 is too greasy for any application where the beef is not the central textural focus — it overwhelms the sauce, slicks the noodle, and reads as fat rather than as meat. **80/20 ground chuck** is the canonical workhorse: enough fat to render and brown, enough lean for clean meat flavor, and the fat-lean balance the most American beef recipes were calibrated around (burgers, meatballs, taco filling, chili, basic mapo tofu). **90/10 ground sirloin or round** is the lean choice; the meat browns harder and faster because there's less fat to render, the resulting cooked grind reads more "serious" and clean-flavored, and the dish tastes leaner. The trade is that 90/10 dries out under aggressive cooking and reads tough if overworked. **95/5 extra-lean** is the fitness-targeted product and is generally a cooking liability — almost no fat to render, no browning depth, and a tendency to pebble into hard granules. Avoid for most applications.

  Cut origin. Ground from chuck (shoulder) is the supermarket default and is what "ground beef" typically means without further specification; it has good fat content, full beef flavor, and a balance that fits most applications. Ground from sirloin (loin) is leaner, more concentrated in beef flavor, and better suited to dishes where the meat is the focus (a classic American burger, a pâté). Ground from round (rear leg) is the leanest standard cut and reads slightly tough; it benefits from longer cooking that hydrolyzes the connective tissue. Ground from short rib (chest) is rare in supermarkets but excellent at butchers; it is fattier, more deeply beefy, and the structural choice for restaurant-style burgers and the most decadent ragus. Custom ground blends (chuck-and-short-rib at 70/30, brisket-and-sirloin at 80/20) are what good butchers will make on request.

  Role in Sichuan cooking. The canonical Sichuan mapo tofu is built on ground beef, not pork, the original Chen Mapo Tofu in Chengdu's Wenshu Yuan district uses beef, the Sichuan classics codify beef, and the substitution of pork is a Cantonese and overseas-Chinese adaptation rather than the source recipe. Use 80/20 ground chuck for the standard mapo, where the beef is rendered until the moisture cooks off and the meat granules brown to deep mahogany; this is the textural and flavor anchor of the dish, and it cannot happen with extra-lean grinds (no fat to render) or with overly coarse cuts (won't break into the fine granular texture that distributes through the silken tofu). The browning step is non-negotiable: at least 5 to 7 minutes in the wok over high heat with continuous breaking-up of the grind, until the beef is dry, granular, and aromatic. This is "ma chao" technique, and the dish hinges on doing it right.

  Cooking behavior. Ground beef releases water in the first minute or two of high-heat cooking, and the cook's choice is whether to drain that water (pour off after the meat is broken up, common in American taco-meat preparations) or cook it off in the pan (let the water evaporate before browning, common in Sichuan and Italian preparations). The Italian-and-Sichuan approach produces deeper browning and more complex Maillard flavor; the American-drain approach produces faster cleanup and a slightly leaner-tasting result. Either is valid for the appropriate dish. The browning itself happens only after the water has evaporated; cooks who skip the dry-it-out step and try to brown wet ground beef end up with grey, steamed meat that has none of the depth.

  Buying notes. Supermarket ground beef in tube packaging or in plastic-wrapped trays is fine for everyday cooking; aim for a label date within 2 days of purchase. Butcher-ground beef (ordered from the meat counter with a specified cut) is dramatically better in flavor than supermarket ground because the cut is fresher, the fat is integrated, and the grind is fresher (oxidation begins as soon as beef is ground; same-day grinds are ideal). For high-stakes applications (a special burger, a recipe where the beef is the focal protein), order from a butcher and use the same day. The bright cherry-red color of fresh-ground beef is from oxygenated myoglobin; the brown-grey color of older ground beef is from oxidation and is not a sign of spoilage but is a sign of age (typically 2 to 3 days post-grind).

storage: |
  Refrigerated in original packaging at the back of the fridge (coldest spot): 1 to 2 days from grind date. Once opened, store sealed in a non-reactive container (glass or food-grade plastic) and use within 24 hours. Frozen in airtight packaging or vacuum-sealed: 3 to 4 months at 0°F / -18°C. Defrost overnight in the fridge, never at room temperature or in warm water (food safety hazard). Cooked ground beef refrigerates 3 to 4 days and freezes 2 to 3 months. Off-cues are sour smell, slimy surface, or grey-green discoloration past the normal age-grey; discard.
