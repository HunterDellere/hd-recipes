about: |
  Jalapeño (Capsicum annuum) is a medium-heat green chile pepper, 5 to 9 cm long, with a thick wall and a moderate seed-and-membrane load. Heat sits at 2500 to 8000 Scoville units; the spread is wide and individual fruits from the same plant can vary by a factor of three. Flavor is grassy-green, slightly grassy-sweet when ripe red, with a clean direct heat that hits the front of the tongue and dissipates fairly quickly (unlike serrano, which lingers, or habanero, which builds). The same fruit smoked over wood and dried becomes chipotle morita (mahogany-brown, sweet-smoky, the canonical Mexican smoked chile).

  Functional category: everyday Mexican heat. Raw and finely diced into salsa fresca, pico de gallo, and guacamole (the structural fresh-heat element); pickled in escabeche to top tacos and tortas (the canonical jarred jalapeño); roasted and peeled for blendable salsas; stuffed (in the form of poppers and jalapeños rellenos) when oversized; smoked and dried for chipotle. In Tex-Mex and American cooking, the jalapeño is the everyday chile that does the work that serrano does in Mexican cooking, slightly milder and slightly more grassy.

  Seed-and-membrane removal as the heat dial. The capsaicin in any chile concentrates in the white inner membranes (the placental tissue), with secondary heat from the seeds (which sit against the membrane and absorb capsaicin by contact). Removing both drops the heat by 60 to 80 percent; leaving both in delivers the full Scoville hit. Standard technique for medium heat: cut the pepper in half, scrape out the seeds and the white membrane with a small spoon, dice the remaining wall. For full heat: dice the whole pepper. For mild heat (suitable for children or heat-sensitive diners): use only the wall, discard everything inside.

  Raw vs cooked. Raw jalapeño reads sharp-grassy-and-direct, with the heat front-loaded on the first bite. Cooked (sautéed, roasted, simmered into a sauce), the heat mellows by 40 to 50 percent and the flavor turns slightly sweet and rounded. The pickled jalapeño (escabeche) sits between the two: the vinegar tempers the heat but the texture stays crisp.

  Buying. Look for firm jalapeños with smooth, deep-green skin (or fully red for ripe sweetness) at any supermarket year-round. Small white striations on the skin ("corking") indicate a hotter fruit and is generally a positive sign; smooth-skinned glossy jalapeños tend to be milder. Avoid wrinkled fruits (dehydrated), soft spots (rot), or any pepper with brown patches (heat damage in storage). Red ripe jalapeños are sweeter and slightly hotter than green; the same fruit, picked at full ripeness, becomes the chipotle starting point.

storage: |
  Refrigerated in a perforated plastic bag or paper bag in the crisper, 1 to 2 weeks at peak. The skin dehydrates slowly; usable past 2 weeks for cooked applications, but the snap-fresh quality is lost. Pickled jalapeños keep 6 to 12 months refrigerated. Frozen whole, 8 months (collapses on thaw; suitable only for cooked applications and salsas). Failure modes: soft spots (rot, cut around or discard), wrinkled skin (dehydration; usable cooked), white or pink mold (discard).
