about: |
  Shallots are a member of the allium family that grow as small clustered bulbs (multiple cloves per shallot, similar in growth pattern to garlic but from a different species). Botanically they sit between onions (Allium cepa) and garlic (Allium sativum), with their own characteristic chemistry: the same alliinase enzyme that produces sulfur compounds in cut onions and crushed garlic, but acting on different precursor molecules to give a more complex, wine-like, sweet aromatic profile. Where a yellow onion brings forward sulfur pungency and a garlic clove brings allicin sharpness, a raw shallot brings a layered, slightly sweet, slightly nutty allium complexity that sits more elegantly on cold dishes.

  Sugar content is the technical differentiator. Shallots run 4 to 6 percent sugar by weight (vs 2 to 4 percent in yellow onions), which means they caramelize faster and read sweeter when raw. The sulfur fraction is also lower; raw shallot is meaningfully less aggressive on the palate than raw yellow onion, which is why French cooking uses raw minced shallot in vinaigrettes, mignonette (the classic oyster condiment of shallot, vinegar, and pepper), and steak tartare, where raw onion would dominate. The same logic explains the Southeast Asian use of fried shallots: the high sugar content drives faster Maillard browning and produces the crispy sweet-savory crumb that crowns Vietnamese, Thai, Indonesian, and Malay dishes.

  Cut decides character. A microplane-grated shallot dissolves into a vinaigrette and contributes only chemistry, not texture; this is the niçoise application. A finely minced shallot (1 to 2 mm dice) sits visible in the dressing or on a tartare plate, contributing texture and aromatic complexity together. A sliced shallot (lengthwise julienne, 2 to 3 mm thick) holds its structure through a quick sauté and contributes a soft sweet allium presence to a pan sauce. A whole shallot, halved and slow-roasted, turns into a sweet jammy bulb that sits alongside roasts as a side. The same ingredient covers four functional roles depending on the cut.

  Heat behavior tracks onion behavior. Sulfur compounds break down within 30 seconds of contact with hot fat (the raw bite disappears), residual sugars caramelize between 110 and 140 °C (the sweet-savory development point), and excessive heat past golden-brown produces bitterness from polyphenol degradation. Fried shallots (the Southeast Asian condiment) work because the cut is thin (sliced 1 mm crosswise), the oil is held at 140 °C, and the cook pulls them at deep golden, not brown. Past brown they turn bitter immediately.

  Applications by category. Cold raw applications (vinaigrettes, mignonettes, tartare, ceviche garnish) are the primary French-Mediterranean role. Sauce reductions (red-wine-and-shallot bordelaise, beurre blanc with white wine and shallot, classical bistro pan sauces) use lightly sweated shallot as the aromatic base. Slow-roasted whole (alongside chicken or beef in a hot oven) gives a side. Fried crisp (Southeast Asian dishes, Vietnamese pho topping, Indonesian sambal goreng, Malay nasi lemak garnish) is a texture-and-flavor garnish.

  Buying. The two main commercial types: the French gray shallot (cuisse de poulet, Allium oschaninii) is the gold standard with the most complex flavor; widely available in French markets, harder in the US. The standard pink-brown shallot (Allium cepa var. aggregatum) dominates US groceries and is a fine substitute. Look for firm bulbs with tight papery skin, no green sprouts (sprouting bulbs are bitter when cooked), and no soft spots. The smaller bulbs in a cluster are more flavorful per gram than the large ones; for raw applications, prefer smaller bulbs.

storage: 'Cool, dark, dry, with airflow. Whole bulbs keep 3 to 4 weeks at room temperature in a mesh bag or open ceramic crock. Refrigeration accelerates sprouting and softening; only refrigerate broken-open bulbs, and use within a week. Failure modes: green sprouts (still usable but progressively bitter, halve and remove the central green core); soft or rubbery skin (water loss, still usable; the flavor concentrates); dark spots or mushy texture (discard the affected portion); strong sulfur off-smell (discard the bulb).'

substitutions:
  - for: 'shallot (raw, vinaigrette or mignonette)'
    use: 'red onion, finely diced and rinsed under cold water'
    note: 'Rinsing strips the most aggressive sulfur compounds so the onion does not dominate. Sweeter Vidalia or Walla Walla onion also works without rinsing. Yellow onion is too aggressive raw.'
  - for: 'shallot (sauce reduction, sweated aromatic base)'
    use: 'yellow onion, finely diced'
    note: 'Sweat slightly longer (5 to 6 minutes vs 3 to 4) to land the same sweetness. The result is slightly more onion-forward and less aromatic-complex; acceptable in most pan sauces.'
  - for: 'shallot (fried crisp, Southeast Asian garnish)'
    use: 'thinly sliced red onion'
    note: 'Lower sugar content means slower caramelization; pull at the same color rather than the same time. Results in a slightly less sweet but functionally identical garnish.'
