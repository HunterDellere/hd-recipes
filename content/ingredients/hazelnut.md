about: |
  Hazelnuts are the seed of Corylus species, sold whole in shells, shelled and skin-on, shelled and blanched (skin removed), or processed into pastes (praline, gianduia, hazelnut butter). The European hazel (Corylus avellana, the "filbert" in older US usage) is the standard culinary hazelnut and what every European recipe means; American hazel (Corylus americana) is smaller and rarely sold commercially. The major production regions are Turkey (about 70 percent of world supply, mostly destined for industrial chocolate-and-praline), Italy (Piedmont in particular, source of the famous Tonda Gentile delle Langhe variety used in fine confectionery and gianduia), and the US Pacific Northwest (Oregon and Washington, primarily Barcelona variety, the source of most US-domestic hazelnuts). Variety matters at the high end of confectionery; for everyday cooking, supermarket Pacific Northwest hazelnuts are fine.

  The skin problem. Raw shelled hazelnuts come with a thin papery brown skin (technically the seed coat) that contains bitter and tannic compounds (proanthocyanidins and other polyphenols). The skin is acceptable in raw applications where the bitter edge is wanted (some salads, some health-food contexts), but it reads as harsh in any cooked or toasted application; the bitter compounds become more pronounced when the nut is heated. The skin-removal technique is the structural step in hazelnut cooking: toast the skin-on hazelnuts at 175 °C / 350 °F for 10 to 12 minutes (or in a dry skillet over medium heat for 5 to 6 minutes) until the skins crack visibly and the flesh below is golden; transfer the hot nuts to a clean kitchen towel, fold the towel over them, and rub vigorously for 30 seconds; the skins slough off in flakes. Some residual skin is fine; the bulk skin is what carries the bitter, and getting 80 to 90 percent off is the practical goal. Pre-blanched hazelnuts (sold with the skins already removed) skip this step but are more expensive and slightly less aromatic (the skin contributes some toasted-nut character even after removal).

  Composition and rancidity. Hazelnuts are about 60 percent fat, primarily monounsaturated (oleic acid, the same fat that dominates olive oil) with smaller amounts of polyunsaturated. The high monounsaturated content makes hazelnuts more storage-stable than walnuts (which are highly polyunsaturated and oxidize quickly), but they still go rancid eventually; the cue is a stale or soapy smell rather than the sweet-buttery smell of fresh nuts. Toasted hazelnuts go rancid faster than raw; toast small batches and use within a week, or freeze toasted batches for longer storage.

  Cooking behavior. Whole toasted: salads, cheese plates, grain bowls, the modern North-American autumn-salad register (paired with squash, kale, farro, blue cheese). Roughly chopped toasted: pesto Trapanese variations, granola, baked-good toppings, the Italian Piedmontese cookies (brutti ma buoni). Finely chopped or ground: macarons, Italian torta di nocciole (hazelnut cake), Turkish baklava, dacquoise (French nut meringue cake). Pastes (toasted hazelnuts ground with sugar to a smooth paste): gianduia (the Piedmontese hazelnut-and-chocolate paste that became Nutella), praline (caramelized sugar around toasted hazelnuts, ground to a paste), Turkish helva variants. Hazelnut oil (cold-pressed from toasted hazelnuts): finishing oil for salads, vinaigrettes, drizzled over fish; intensely aromatic, spoils quickly (refrigerate after opening).

  Pairings. Chocolate (the canonical pairing; gianduia, Nutella, hazelnut-and-chocolate everything). Coffee (the affogato-leaning hazelnut latte, the hazelnut-coffee tortes of Northern Italy). Vanilla and brown butter (the foundational baking pairings; hazelnut-brown-butter financiers, vanilla-hazelnut shortbread). Pear and apple (the Italian fall fruit-and-nut pairings). Aged blue cheese (gorgonzola, stilton; the high fat of the nut and the salt of the cheese balance). Honey (Turkish baklava, Italian torrone). Brown sugar and maple syrup (American autumn baking). Roasted winter vegetables, especially squash and brassicas (the modern grain-bowl pairing; the toasted nut amplifies the Maillard depth of the roasted vegetables). Dried cherries and cranberries (the canonical American salad-with-nuts pairing).

  Cuisine origin and reach. The hazelnut is European and ancient; archaeological records of hazelnut consumption in Europe date to 7000 BCE. The Italian Piedmont region developed the canonical use cases through the Renaissance and beyond: gianduia, torta di nocciole, brutti ma buoni cookies. Turkish cuisine uses hazelnuts heavily in baklava and confectionery (the country produces most of the world's supply by volume). French cuisine adopted hazelnuts via the dacquoise, the praline, and the various nut-based patisseries of the 19th century. The American Pacific Northwest developed its own hazelnut culture in the 20th century (Oregon's hazelnut industry is the second-largest source of US-grown nuts after almonds), with applications in baking, granola, and the modern farm-to-table salad register.

  Buying notes. Whole shelled skin-on hazelnuts are the default purchase; skin-on stores better (the skin is a barrier against oxidation), and the toast-and-rub step is fast. Pre-blanched hazelnuts (skin removed, sometimes labeled "filberts") skip the rub step but are more expensive and store less well; useful when the toast-and-rub step is not feasible (large-batch baking, time pressure). Hazelnut flour (finely ground blanched hazelnuts) is a specialty product for macarons and gluten-free baking; expensive and short-shelf-life. Avoid bulk-bin hazelnuts at low-turnover supermarkets; the slow turnover means rancidity is common. Buy from sources with high turnover; Italian-imported Tonda Gentile is a luxury upgrade for confectionery and rarely worth the price for everyday cooking.

storage: |
  Whole shelled skin-on hazelnuts in a sealed container in a cool dry pantry: 2 to 3 months at peak quality. Refrigerated, sealed: 6 months. Frozen, sealed in a zip-top bag with as much air pressed out as possible: 1 year. Toasted hazelnuts (skin removed) at room temperature, sealed: 1 to 2 weeks at peak quality; refrigerate or freeze for longer storage (1 month refrigerated, 6 months frozen). The cue for past-prime is smell-and-taste: a single nut tasted from the package will reveal rancidity within seconds (stale, soapy, slightly bitter); fresh nuts taste sweetly buttery. Pre-blanched hazelnuts go rancid noticeably faster than skin-on; buy small quantities and use quickly. Hazelnut flour is the most rancidity-prone form (large surface area exposed to air); refrigerate or freeze immediately after opening, use within 1 month.

  - for: 'hazelnuts (Italian gianduia, hazelnut-chocolate desserts)'
    use: 'no good direct substitute, see almonds with caveats'
    note: 'Hazelnut-chocolate is the structural identity of gianduia and Nutella; substituting another nut produces a different dessert. Almonds (toasted, ground to a paste with sugar) produce a workable almond-chocolate spread that is structurally similar but flavor-different; the dish is no longer gianduia. Skip the recipe rather than substitute when authenticity matters.'
  - for: 'hazelnuts (Turkish baklava, nut-and-honey pastries)'
    use: 'walnuts or pistachios'
    note: 'Walnuts are the most common alternative in Turkish baklava and produce a slightly more bitter, more rustic dessert. Pistachios produce a brighter, sweeter, more luxurious dessert and are common in Persian and Levantine variants. Both are legitimate variations rather than substitutions.'
  - for: 'hazelnuts (American autumn baking, granola, grain salads)'
    use: 'pecans (toasted)'
    note: 'Pecans are the closest American autumn-coded swap; the flavor is sweeter, less tannic, and reads similarly seasonal. The dish loses the slight European-Italian hint that hazelnuts carry but gains American coherence.'
