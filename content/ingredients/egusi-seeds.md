about: |
  Egusi (also spelled agushi or akatoa, depending on language and region) are the shelled, dried seeds of several West African gourd species. The seed itself is flat, oval, ivory-colored, about the size of a pumpkin seed but with most of the dark seed coat removed. Nutritionally the seed is roughly 50% oil, 30% protein, and the rest fiber and carbohydrate; the high fat content is what gives ground egusi its structural thickening power in soups.

  Functionally egusi does two jobs simultaneously in West African cooking. Ground to a coarse paste with water, it acts as the body of egusi soup, the Nigerian and Cameroonian stew built on palm oil, scotch bonnet, ground egusi, meat or fish, and bitter leaf or ugu greens; the seeds clump into soft pebble-textured masses as they cook in the hot palm oil, giving the soup its distinctive grainy mouthfeel and visible white-yellow clumps. Toasted and ground, the same seed becomes the snack and seasoning called agushi in Ghana, used as a topping for many one-pot grain dishes.

  Buy egusi pre-shelled from a West African or African-Caribbean grocery; the dark seed-coat shells are bitter and tedious to remove by hand. Whole seeds keep months in airtight storage but ground egusi goes rancid quickly (the high fat content oxidizes); grind only what you need for a recipe, or grind a small batch and refrigerate.

storage: 'Whole seeds in an airtight jar, cool dry pantry, 6 months. Refrigerated or frozen, 12 to 18 months. Ground egusi 1 to 2 weeks refrigerated, 3 months frozen. Failure mode is rancidity; rancid seeds smell of stale nuts and read bitter and harsh in the finished soup.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'egusi seeds (in egusi soup)'
    use: 'raw shelled pumpkin seeds (pepitas), ground in a spice grinder or food processor'
    note: 'The closest substitute by far. Pepitas carry similar fat content and nutty register, grind to a similar paste, and clump in hot palm oil similarly to egusi. The flavor is slightly cleaner and less earthy than real egusi; the dish reads as recognizably egusi soup but slightly polished. Quantity: 1:1 by weight.'
  - for: 'egusi seeds (for general thickening)'
    use: 'raw cashews ground to a paste, or sunflower seeds'
    note: 'Cashew paste is the closest mouthfeel match for cooks without pepitas; sunflower seeds work but read greener and less rich.'
