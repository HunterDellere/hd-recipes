about: |
  Ocimum tenuiflorum (also called tulsi in Indian contexts, sacred basil in English). The leaves are smaller, more pointed, and visibly serrated compared to Thai basil; stems can be green or purple. The aroma is peppery, clove-forward, almost camphor-like; the dominant aromatic is eugenol, the same compound that defines clove and allspice, with much less of the methyl chavicol that gives Thai basil its anise note.

  The defining application is pad kaprao (gai pad kaprao, moo pad kaprao), a Thai street-food stir-fry of meat with chili, garlic, fish sauce, and a large handful of holy basil added at the very end. The peppery-clove note is what makes pad kaprao read as pad kaprao; a version with Thai basil reads as a different dish (closer to drunken noodles without the noodles). The substitution is the single most common reason Western pad kaprao tastes off.

  Heat behavior is similar to Thai basil, more heat-stable than Italian basil but still added late (last 10 to 15 seconds of stir-fry). The leaves wilt slightly into the sauce and release their oils into the residual fat.

  Sourcing is the real obstacle. Holy basil is uncommon outside Thai groceries and Southeast Asian farmers' markets; most Thai restaurants in the West use Thai basil and call it kaprao. When you find it, buy several bunches and freeze the leaves; the aromatic load survives freezing better than refrigeration.

storage: 'Fresh: stems in a glass of water on the counter, loosely covered, lasts 4 to 5 days. Refrigeration browns the leaves. For longer storage, pluck leaves and freeze in a single layer, then transfer to a bag; usable straight from frozen in stir-fries.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'holy basil (in pad kaprao)'
    use: 'Thai basil + a pinch of ground clove + extra cracked black pepper'
    note: 'The clove and pepper approximate the eugenol note that defines holy basil. The dish will read closer to authentic pad kaprao than Thai-basil-alone but the floral-herbal top is still slightly off.'
  - for: 'holy basil (emergency)'
    use: 'Italian basil + ¼ tsp ground clove per cup of leaves'
    note: 'Last resort. Italian basil wilts faster and lacks the heat stability; add only in the last 5 seconds of cooking. The eugenol from added clove carries most of the flavor identity.'
