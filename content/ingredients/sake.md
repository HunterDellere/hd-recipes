about: |
  Sake is brewed from rice via koji enzymatic conversion plus yeast fermentation, landing around 14–16% ABV. In cooking, sake does three jobs: it dissolves alcohol-soluble flavor compounds (volatile esters, certain amino acids), it tenderizes protein (the alcohol partially denatures surface proteins, and trace organic acids do additional work), and it provides a clean, dry backbone that doesn't fight the rest of the seasoning.

  For cooking, you do **not** need a premium ginjō or daiginjō: those are tasting sakes, and the delicate aromatics are wasted in heat. A standard junmai or futsūshu (table sake) is right. **Avoid "cooking sake" sold in US supermarkets that has added salt and corn syrup**, which are seasoned cooking liquids and behave differently.

  Pairs natively with mirin (sake provides dryness, mirin provides sweetness), soy (umami stack), dashi (everything), and ginger (volatile carry). The Japanese "yū-shio" pairing of soy + sake + mirin is the trinity behind teriyaki, simmered fish, and most nimono.

  If you can't get sake at all, **dry sherry** is the closest functional substitute (similar ABV, similar dry character, oxidative depth). Shaoxing wine is workable but reads more Chinese in profile.

storage: 'Cool dark place, 6–12 months unopened. Once opened, refrigerate; flavor stays good for 2–3 weeks, fine for cooking up to 2 months.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'sake (washoku cooking: glazes, simmered dishes, dashi-based)'
    use: 'dry sherry, fino or manzanilla'
    note: 'Best 1:1 functional substitute. A touch nuttier and oxidative; the difference is mostly invisible once soy and mirin enter.'
  - for: 'sake (deglazing, protein tenderizing marinades)'
    use: 'dry vermouth or dry white wine'
    note: 'Covers the alcohol-driven volatile carry and the dry character. Adds a touch of fruit; fine in non-purist applications.'
  - for: 'sake (red-cooked or Chinese-leaning braises)'
    use: 'Shaoxing wine'
    note: 'Closer in fermentation style but more Chinese in flavor. Right when the dish is already cross-pollinated; pulls washoku off-profile.'
