about: |
  A Dungeness crab is a large brachyuran (true crab) of the Cancridae family, native to the Pacific coast from Alaska to central California. The body is roughly oval, 15 to 25 cm across the carapace at legal size, with a smooth pale-brown shell that darkens to red-orange when cooked. The anatomy a cook sees: the carapace (the top shell, lifted off in cleaning to expose the body cavity); the gills (the soft feathery "dead man's fingers" lining the body cavity, pulled out and discarded); the hepatopancreas or "crab butter" (the yellow-cream to greenish-tan paste filling the body cavity, prized in Asian preparations and used as a sauce-enrichment in Italian-American cioppino, though some American cooks discard it from squeamishness); the body meat (the lumpy white meat in the body chambers between the legs); the leg and claw meat (the long, fibrous, sweet meat inside the legs and claws); and the swimmerets and mouthparts (discarded). A live Dungeness in the 900 to 1100 g range yields 200 to 250 g of cooked picked meat, roughly 25 percent meat by live weight.

  Functionally, Dungeness crab is a glutamate, glycine, and AMP-IMP source with an unusually high free-amino-acid load that reads as concentrated sweetness; the meat is sweeter than blue crab, less mineral than stone crab, and structurally distinct from snow or king crab (which are spider crabs, with longer, looser leg meat). The brown butter (hepatopancreas) is a fat-and-glutamate concentrate that emulsifies into a broth or pasta sauce and adds dimensional depth; this is the canonical Asian use (Cantonese ginger-scallion crab, Singaporean chili crab, Vietnamese bun rieu) and the unstated secret in any Italian-American cioppino that tastes deeply of crab beyond the white meat. Crab meat pH is around 7.0 to 7.2, slightly alkaline; the flavor pairs cleanly with butter, garlic, lemon, sherry, white wine, tomato, gochugaru, ginger, scallion, and Old Bay seasoning.

  Transformation. Live Dungeness is the gold standard; the meat texture and sweetness degrade audibly within hours of death, so the standard West Coast home protocol is buy live, cook the same day. Killing protocol: chill the live crab on ice 20 to 30 minutes to slow metabolism (the crab is poikilothermic and "anesthetizes" in the cold); then either dispatch with a stab through the central nerve cluster under the apron (the quick humane death) or boil directly from the ice (the traditional shortcut). Cook time: 12 to 14 minutes at a rolling boil for a 900 to 1100 g crab; the carapace turns bright red-orange and the legs detach cleanly from the body at the joints when twisted. Cool in an ice bath 5 minutes to stop residual cooking, then clean and pick. Pre-cooked frozen Dungeness (Costco, H Mart) is acceptable for cioppino and crab cakes where the meat is reheated in a sauce; the texture is firmer than fresh-cooked and the brown butter is usually gone (rinsed in processing), but the meat-and-shell stock made from the cleaned shells still adds depth.

  Applications by category. In North-American Italian cooking, Dungeness is the structural protein under San Francisco cioppino (the dish was invented by Genovese fishermen in North Beach in the late nineteenth century around whatever the day's catch produced, but Dungeness in the shell is the symbolic centerpiece); the legs and body sections cook in the tomato-fennel-saffron broth and the eater cracks the shells at the table. In American Pacific Coast preparations, Dungeness shows up cracked-and-cleaned over romaine in crab Louie (a Hangtown-era San Francisco salad with hard-cooked egg and a thousand-island-like dressing), in crab cakes (similar to but distinct from Maryland blue crab cakes, with looser meat), and in crab boils with potato and corn (the Pacific Coast answer to the Lowcountry shrimp boil). In Cantonese cooking, whole crabs are stir-fried with ginger and scallion (jiang cong chao xie), and the brown butter is the unstated thickener of the sauce. In Singaporean and Malaysian cooking, chili crab and pepper crab both use Dungeness as a sub for the smaller mud crab when mud crab is unavailable. In Vietnamese cooking, bun rieu uses small freshwater crabs ground whole, but Dungeness shell stock fortifies any seafood broth in a Western kitchen. In Japanese cooking, Dungeness reads as a sub for snow crab in kani sushi and salads; the meat is sweeter and lumpier than snow crab so the dishes shift slightly.

  Buying notes. Dungeness is best in November through May on the West Coast; the Pacific Fishery Management Council regulates the season tightly and the crabs are sweetest and meatiest in winter when feeding is most active. Look for live crabs that are heavy for their size (light crabs have not refilled their shells after the molt and the meat yield is poor), with bright movement and aggressive claw response when prodded (sluggish crabs are dying or already dead). The store should be holding them in chilled saltwater tanks; crabs piled in melting ice for days are deteriorating. Pre-cooked Dungeness (whole, cracked-and-cleaned, or picked meat in plastic containers) is available year-round at Costco, H Mart, Whole Foods, and Asian seafood markets; pre-cooked is acceptable for any cooked preparation but the difference between live-cooked and pre-cooked is audible in a side-by-side taste. Frozen pre-cooked is a step down again, but acceptable for cioppino in regions where fresh is unavailable. Avoid pasteurized "crab meat" in supermarket cans or tubs (Phillips, Chicken of the Sea); the pasteurization step ruins the texture for any dish where the meat carries the plate.

  Pacific Coast vs. Atlantic options. Dungeness is structurally the closest in flavor and meat texture to West Coast blue crab (Callinectes sapidus, the Atlantic species used in Maryland-style boils and crab cakes), but the meat is larger-flaked, sweeter, and less mineral; the Asian preparations that work for Dungeness work for blue crab with reduced cook time (smaller body). King crab (Paralithodes camtschaticus) and snow crab (Chionoecetes opilio) are taxonomically distant (they are king-crab and snow-crab families, not true crabs); their meat is longer, looser, and sweeter, and they substitute for Dungeness only in preparations where the leg meat is the focus. Stone crab claws (Florida) are entirely different in texture (firm, dense, the meat reads more like lobster claw).

storage: 'Live: ideally use the day of purchase; refrigerate up to 24 hours in a paper bag (not a sealed plastic bag, the crab suffocates) over an ice tray to drain melting water. Cooked, in the shell: 2 days refrigerated. Picked meat: 2 days refrigerated in an airtight container, or 3 months frozen in vacuum-sealed bags; frozen meat texture is somewhat firmer. Cleaned shells make excellent stock; freeze in zip-top bags and combine with shrimp and fish shells for a seafood-stock base.'

    note: 'Closest in lump size and sweetness. Pasteurized canned meat reads mushy and stringy and should be avoided for any dish where the meat is the texture; only acceptable for fillings inside dumplings or wontons where the texture is masked.'
  - for: 'Dungeness crab (entirely unavailable, any dish)'
    use: 'large peeled shrimp (head-on if possible) plus lump blue crab meat'
    note: 'For cioppino or a seafood stew specifically, the dish can be made without crab by doubling the shrimp and folding in 200 g of lump crab meat at the end. The signature crab-shell presentation is lost; the broth reads slightly less crab-forward. A working compromise, not a perfect substitute.'
