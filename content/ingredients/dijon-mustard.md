about: |
  Dijon mustard is a smooth pale-yellow paste of ground brown mustard seeds (Brassica juncea) suspended in an acidic liquid (traditionally verjuice, the unfermented juice of unripe grapes; modern production uses white wine vinegar or white wine and water). The Dijon style originated in 13th-century Burgundy; the city held a near-monopoly on mustard production through the medieval period. The "Dijon" name carries no AOP protection; mustard made anywhere in the world can use the label. The actual quality marker is the seed (Brassica juncea, not B. nigra or B. alba) and the acid base (verjuice or white wine, not water with citric acid).

  Pungency comes from glucosinolate chemistry, the same family that drives capers, horseradish, and wasabi. Brown mustard seeds contain sinigrin, a glucosinolate held in compartmentalized cells. When the seeds are crushed in water, the enzyme myrosinase meets sinigrin and produces allyl isothiocyanate, the pungent volatile compound that gives mustard its sinus-clearing heat. This reaction is fast: within 10 to 15 minutes of mixing crushed seeds with cold water, the mustard reaches peak pungency, then progressively mellows over hours as the volatile isothiocyanates evaporate. Acid (vinegar, wine, verjuice) slows the breakdown; this is why traditional Dijon is mixed with verjuice rather than plain water: the cure preserves the pungency longer.

  The vinaigrette emulsion is the canonical French application. Dijon mustard contains substantial mucilage from the seed coat (a polysaccharide that swells in water) plus the surface-active proteins from the ground seed. Both act as emulsifiers; a teaspoon of Dijon whisked into a vinegar-and-shallot base, then olive oil streamed in, produces a temporary emulsion that holds 20 to 40 minutes (long enough for the salad to be tossed and eaten, not long enough for an overnight dressing). Without the Dijon, a vinaigrette breaks within seconds because olive oil and vinegar are immiscible. The Dijon also contributes acidity, sharpness, and body; a salad dressing without it reads thin and watery.

  Other applications. Pan sauces (Dijon stirred into a chicken pan sauce after deglaze, the standard French bistro chicken-with-mustard-sauce). Marinades (Dijon plus olive oil plus herbs as a coating for roast pork loin or chicken thighs; the mustard sticks to the protein and seals in moisture). Sandwich condiment (the European spread, less sweet than American yellow mustard, with more pungent backbone). Hollandaise variation (a teaspoon of Dijon stirred into a finished hollandaise transforms it into a sauce moutarde for fish and asparagus).

  Heat behavior. The volatile isothiocyanates that drive pungency are heat-sensitive; sustained heat above 60 °C drives them off within minutes. Practical effect: Dijon used as a marinade or cooked into a long sauce contributes only its emulsifier and acid character, not its pungent finish. To preserve the pungent bite, stir Dijon in at the last 30 seconds of cooking or at the table.

  Buying. Maille (now owned by Unilever) and Edmond Fallot (still independent in Burgundy) are the two reputable French brands. Maille is widely available in US supermarkets and is honestly good — the Dijon Originale is the standard kitchen jar. Fallot's stone-ground varieties (the Tradition Familiale or the Burgundy AOC mustard with Burgundy white wine) are a clear quality step up; available at specialty groceries and online. Avoid generic supermarket "yellow mustard" or "deli mustard" — these are made from B. alba seeds (yellow mustard, much milder) plus turmeric for color; they are a different ingredient and fail in vinaigrette and pan-sauce roles.

  Whole-grain mustard (Dijon's textured cousin) is a distinct ingredient: same seed and acid base, but the seeds are not fully ground, leaving visible whole and cracked seed in the paste. Use whole-grain where texture is wanted (in a glaze, on a charcuterie board); use smooth Dijon where the mustard needs to dissolve (vinaigrette, pan sauce, hollandaise).

storage: 'Refrigerated after opening, 6 to 12 months sealed jar. Shelf-stable in pantry before opening. Failure modes: dried-out crust on the surface (scrape off and discard, the rest is fine if it smells right); flat-tasting mustard with little pungency (volatiles have escaped, still functional in cooked applications); off smells (rare; discard).'
