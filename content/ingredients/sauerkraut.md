about: |
  Sauerkraut is the simplest possible vegetable ferment, shredded green cabbage salted at roughly 2% by weight and pressed under its own brine, where the salt selects for the right microbes and the cabbage juice provides the liquid medium. The first phase is dominated by Leuconostoc mesenteroides, which produces lactic acid, acetic acid, and a small amount of CO2; as the pH drops below about 4, Lactobacillus plantarum takes over and pushes the lactic acid concentration higher, finishing the ferment with a clean sour tang and dropping the pH below 3.5 for shelf stability. The whole process takes 1 to 4 weeks at room temperature.

  Functionally sauerkraut is a flavor base, not a condiment. The German and Polish tradition is to drain the raw ferment, then cook it with fat (lard, bacon, or duck fat), aromatics (onion, caraway, juniper, bay), and often a splash of wine or beer for at least 30 minutes, sometimes hours. The long cook softens the texture, mellows the sharp acid into a rounded tang, and lets the kraut absorb the fat and aromatics that make it taste of more than just sour cabbage. Eating raw kraut straight from the jar on a hot dog is an American move; it is not how Central European kitchens use the ingredient.

  Buy refrigerated raw sauerkraut from the dairy case (Bubbies, Wildbrine, Farmhouse Culture), not the shelf-stable canned or jarred product that has been pasteurized and tastes of brine and metal. The live ferments retain the microbial complexity and the cleaner sour note that the long cook will concentrate. Homemade kraut is genuinely better than any commercial product and takes 30 minutes of active work plus a 2 to 3 week wait.

storage: 'Refrigerated in its own brine, 6 months or longer. The ferment keeps slowly progressing in the cold, getting more sour and softer over time; year-old kraut is acceptable but past peak. If the brine surface develops fuzzy mold (kahm yeast is acceptable, true mold is not), skim and continue, or discard if extensive.'
