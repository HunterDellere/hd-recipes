about: |
  Kosher salt is just NaCl with a coarse, irregular crystal. The name comes from its use in koshering meat — the crystal size is what pulls moisture. In cooking, the irregular shape is why a pinch behaves predictably between fingers.

  The two American brands are not interchangeable. Diamond Crystal is hollow, flaky, and roughly half as dense as Morton by volume — a teaspoon of Diamond is ~3g, a teaspoon of Morton is ~6g. A recipe written for one and seasoned with the other lands at half or double the salt. This is the single most common reason a recipe "tastes off." If a recipe gives salt in volume without naming the brand, treat it as suspect and salt to taste.

  The fix is grams. Sodium chloride doesn't care about crystal shape on the tongue — 5g of either brand is 5g of salt. Once you weigh, the brand question disappears.

  Kosher salt is a cooking salt, not a finishing salt. The crystals dissolve fully into anything wet. For finishing — on a steak, on a chocolate cookie — you want a flake salt (Maldon) or a coarse sea salt that crunches and signals salt in distinct bursts.

storage: 'Airtight container, dry. Keeps indefinitely — salt doesn''t spoil. Failure mode is humidity: kosher salt clumps and turns into a brick if exposed to damp air, especially near a stove.'
