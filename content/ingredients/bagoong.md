about: |
  Bagoong covers a family of Filipino fermented seafood pastes. The two most common are bagoong alamang (paste of fermented krill or tiny shrimp, wet, pink-to-purple, salt-forward) and bagoong isda (paste or sauce of fermented small fish, brown, deeper-funky). A third major regional version, bagoong monamon (anchovy-based, from Ilocos), is closer in profile to Thai or Vietnamese fish sauce but thicker.

  Bagoong alamang is the form most Western recipes encounter. The flavor is salt-front with a deep fermented-shrimp umami; the texture is wet paste with visible flecks of crustacean shell. Used raw as a dip for green mango (the canonical pairing, a salty-sweet-sour-tart contrast), or sauteed with garlic and pork fat to make ginisang bagoong, the standard accompaniment to kare-kare (Filipino oxtail-and-peanut stew).

  Distinct from Indonesian terasi and Malaysian belacan in that bagoong is wet rather than block-dried, and is more often eaten raw rather than toasted. The fermentation goes longer (often 6 to 12 months in clay jars), producing a more aggressive funk that mellows on the tongue.

  Brand standard: Barrio Fiesta (the orange-label bagoong alamang, lightly sauteed in pork fat), Rufina (raw bagoong alamang in a glass jar). Both are widely available in Filipino groceries.

storage: 'Refrigerate after opening; lasts a year. The high salt content (15 to 20 percent by weight) prevents spoilage. Smell-check before using; spoiled bagoong smells sharply ammoniac rather than salty-funky.'

    note: 'For the green-mango pairing. The Korean saeujeot is the closest wet-fermented-shrimp analog to bagoong; the fish sauce thins it to a dipping consistency.'
