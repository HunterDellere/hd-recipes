about: |
  Tandoori masala is a Punjabi spice blend developed for cooking in the tandoor, the cylindrical clay oven that heats to 400 to 500 °C and that defines a category of North Indian and Pakistani cooking (tandoori chicken, paneer tikka, seekh kebab, tandoori naan). The blend's defining purpose was to season meat surfaces that would face brief, intense heat; the proportions are built around two structural choices: a high dose of Kashmiri chili powder for color and gentle heat, and a roasted-and-ground whole-spice base (cumin, coriander, fennel, mace, black pepper) for depth that survives the tandoor.

  Distinction from garam masala. Garam masala is the all-purpose finishing blend of North Indian cooking, built around warm aromatic spices (cinnamon, clove, cardamom, nutmeg) added at the end of cooking to perfume the dish. Tandoori masala is the marinade-and-rub blend, built around heat-stable spices and a high color-driver (chili) that holds shape under intense heat. Substituting one for the other changes the dish category: tandoori masala in a finished curry reads underspiced and tinny; garam masala on a tandoori marinade reads sweet and confused, with the cinnamon and clove notes fighting the chili-and-cumin base. Many home pantries stock both; recipes that call for "tandoori masala" mean specifically this blend, not "any Indian spice powder."

  Canonical proportions vary by maker but cluster around: 30 percent Kashmiri red chili powder (the color and the gentle heat), 15 percent roasted ground cumin, 12 percent roasted ground coriander, 8 percent ground ginger, 8 percent garlic powder, 6 percent garam masala, 5 percent dried fenugreek leaves (kasuri methi, the structural Punjabi flavor), 5 percent paprika, 4 percent black salt or kala namak, 3 percent ground mustard seed, 2 percent ground cardamom, 1 percent ground mace, 1 percent black pepper. The Kashmiri chili is the structural ingredient, its high color-strength (10,000 to 15,000 SHU and a deep red anthocyanin profile) is what gives tandoori dishes the brick-orange-red color that defines them visually, and its mild heat (compared to cayenne or Thai chili) lets the cook use it at high doses without crippling the dish.

  Functionally, tandoori masala sits across four roles. As a yogurt-marinade base for grilled or roasted protein (the canonical tandoori chicken application), the blend is whisked into thick yogurt (1 to 2 tablespoons per 500 ml yogurt) with ginger-garlic paste, lemon juice, and salt, then used to coat the protein for 4 to 24 hours; the lactic acid in the yogurt tenderizes the protein while the spice adheres and stains. As a dry rub for high-heat grilling or oven-roasting, the blend is dusted directly onto oiled meat (1 teaspoon per 250 g) and used immediately; the tandoor is the canonical heat source but a 230 °C oven on a sheet pan, a hot grill, or a hot cast-iron pan all work. As a flavor base for tandoori-mayo or tandoori-yogurt finishing sauces (sandwiches, salads, dips), the blend is whisked into the cool emulsion (1 to 2 teaspoons per 250 g mayo or yogurt) and rested 10 minutes for the flavors to integrate. As a quick curry-base spice for one-pan dishes (tandoori chickpeas, tandoori cauliflower), the blend is bloomed in fat at the start of cooking with ginger-garlic, then liquid and protein are added and the dish builds from there.

  Buying. Pakistani and Indian groceries stock the better blends, Shan, Verka, MDH, National, Mehran are the recognized brand names. Supermarket "tandoori seasoning" at the spice aisle is often heavy on paprika and salt and light on Kashmiri chili and the structural spices; readable through a thin and underspiced finished dish. The bench-standard upgrade for home use is to buy a basic blend and supplement: add 50 percent more Kashmiri chili powder, double the kasuri methi, and adjust salt at the marinade stage. The further upgrade is to grind the blend at home, start with whole spices, dry-roast them lightly, grind in a spice mill, mix in the powders that don't toast (chili powder, salt, kasuri methi). Home-ground tandoori masala holds aroma for 4 to 6 weeks against the 6 to 12 months of commercial blends.

  Color and appearance. Genuine tandoori masala produces a brick-orange-red color when applied to yogurt or meat surface; the color comes from Kashmiri chili anthocyanins and the small fraction of paprika. Commercial "tandoori masala" labeled as bright fluorescent red on the packaging often contains added Red 40, Tartrazine, or Sunset Yellow food coloring; check the ingredient label and avoid the dyed product. The genuine red is brick-orange and somewhat muted; the dyed red is fluorescent and unnaturally bright. Some traditional restaurants do use food coloring for visual effect; home cooking does not need to.

storage: 'Airtight container in a cool dark cupboard, away from heat and direct light. Six to twelve months at room temperature for commercial blends; 4 to 6 weeks for home-ground. Failure modes: aroma loss (the spices have oxidized; usable for cooking but underwhelming, replace at the next opportunity), surface clumping (humidity penetration; dry the clumps in a 90 °C oven for 5 minutes and re-grind to recover), discoloration (the chili red has dulled to brown; the blend is past its useful life). Refrigeration is unnecessary and may introduce humidity; freezer storage in vacuum-sealed packets extends the home-ground life to 6 months but is rarely worth the trouble.'
