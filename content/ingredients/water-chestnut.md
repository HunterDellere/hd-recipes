about: |
  The water chestnut (Eleocharis dulcis) is the corm, the underground swollen stem, of an aquatic sedge that grows in flooded paddies across southern China, Vietnam, Thailand, and other Southeast Asian regions. The plant is not a true nut, and is unrelated to the unrelated North American "water caltrop" (Trapa natans) which is sometimes also called water chestnut and is a different species with a different culinary profile. The corms are roughly 2 to 4 cm across, dark brown to nearly black on the exterior with a slightly rough peel, and pure white inside with a crisp, juicy, faintly sweet flesh that reads more like a crisp Asian pear than like a starchy tuber. Despite the white interior, water chestnut is technically a starchy vegetable (about 75% water, 20% carbohydrate, mostly as starch and free sugars).

  The persistent crunch. Water chestnut's defining culinary feature is that it stays crisp through cooking. Where most starchy vegetables (potatoes, taro, jicama) soften when heated as the cell walls hydrolyze and the starch granules gelatinize, water chestnut retains its crunch through long braises and even canning. The mechanism is unique to the Eleocharis genus and a small handful of related plants: the cell walls are cross-linked by ferulic acid (a phenolic compound) which forms heat-stable bonds that resist hydrolysis at cooking temperatures. The cells stay rigid even as the starch inside gelatinizes; the cooked water chestnut reads cooked-tender on the inside but maintains a structural snap when bitten. This property is the entire reason water chestnut is used in Chinese cooking; it's a textural contrast tool that no other vegetable can substitute for cleanly.

  Cooking applications by category. **Chinese (Cantonese, Shanghai, Sichuan)**: dumpling and bun fillings (the canonical Cantonese pork-and-shrimp dumpling almost always includes finely chopped water chestnut for crunch), lion''s head meatballs (the Yangzhou braised pork balls; the water chestnut keeps the dense meat mixture from reading as a uniform paste), congee garnishes (matchstick cuts on top of plain congee), stir-fries with pork or chicken (sliced thin and tossed in at the end for crunch), egg foo young filling, lotus-leaf rice (zongzi adjacent). **Cantonese dim sum specifically**: water chestnut is in roughly half of the steamed dumpling fillings and most of the fried dumpling fillings; it''s the canonical textural element. **Vietnamese, Thai (limited)**: in some Vietnamese spring roll fillings (gỏi cuốn variations) and Thai larb and stir-fries; less central than in Chinese cooking. **American Chinese restaurant**: in stir-fries (kung pao chicken, sweet and sour pork), in soup garnishes, occasionally in egg rolls. **Western adoption (limited)**: as a salad component (water chestnut salad with bacon, a 1960s American party dish), as a stir-fry vegetable in home cooking.

  Fresh vs canned. Fresh water chestnuts (sold at Asian markets year-round, peak in autumn-to-winter) are dramatically better in flavor and texture than canned. Fresh corms have a juicy, sweet, faintly nutty flavor closer to a crisp Asian pear; canned water chestnuts (the universal American supermarket version, sliced or whole, packed in water with citric acid) lose most of the flavor in processing and read primarily as a textural element with little taste of their own. Fresh corms require peeling (a small paring knife, working under cold water; the peel is thin but the corms are slippery), and the peeled flesh oxidizes quickly to a beige color (toss with cold water plus a teaspoon of vinegar to hold for an hour). Canned is the right pick when peeling fresh is impractical (a 60-corm Chinese dumpling session is a different proposition than a 6-corm stir-fry); for any focal application where the water chestnut is a primary textural feature (lion''s head, dim sum filling), fresh is meaningfully better and worth the trip to the Asian market.

  Cutting and prep. For meatball and dumpling fillings, water chestnut is finely diced (3 to 4 mm dice; small enough to incorporate into the mixture without large lumps, large enough to provide a discrete crunch on the bite). For stir-fries, slice into 3 to 4 mm coins or quarters. For salads and cold preparations, julienne into matchsticks. Whole water chestnuts (canned or fresh) work as a textural component in stir-fries where bite-sized chunks are wanted. The corm has a slightly grainy texture under the knife and cuts cleanly without crumbling; a dull knife is the only thing that produces a ragged cut.

  Buying notes. Fresh water chestnuts at Asian markets (H Mart, 99 Ranch, Chinatown groceries) typically run $3 to $6 per pound depending on season; choose corms that are firm and heavy with intact unbroken skin and no soft spots or mold; the larger corms (3 to 4 cm) are easier to peel than tiny ones. Canned water chestnuts (La Choy, Roland, Maesri) at any supermarket; the sliced version is more convenient than whole, the whole version is fresher-tasting (less surface area exposed to the brine). Avoid canned water chestnuts that smell strongly metallic when opened (suggests low-quality canning), and rinse all canned water chestnuts under cold water before use to remove the brine taste.

storage: |
  Fresh water chestnuts at room temperature: 3 to 5 days if cool and dry (the corms shrivel slightly but stay edible). Refrigerated in a paper bag in the crisper: 2 to 3 weeks. Soaked submerged in water in the refrigerator (changing the water daily): 2 to 4 weeks; this is the traditional Chinese storage method and produces the best texture preservation. Peeled fresh water chestnuts in water in the fridge: 2 to 3 days before the cut surfaces soften. Canned water chestnuts unopened: 2 to 3 years at room temperature (check the printed date). Canned, opened, in their own brine in a sealed container in the fridge: 5 to 7 days. Failure modes are softness or shriveling past the point of restoration (fresh corms), and a slimy or fermented surface (advanced spoilage, discard). Freezing destroys the cell structure and the characteristic crunch; do not freeze.
