about: |
  Lime in Western kitchens almost always means one of two species: Persian (Tahiti) lime, Citrus latifolia, the larger seedless green lime that is the supermarket default; or Key (Mexican) lime, Citrus aurantiifolia, the smaller, rounder, more aromatic and seedier lime that defines key lime pie and most Mexican and Cuban cooking. The two are not interchangeable in fine work, Persian lime is more uniform and slightly less aromatic; Key lime is more floral, more bitter, and noticeably more acidic. Mexican cooks specify limón (Key lime) where it matters; Thai cooks use the Persian-equivalent makrut lime relatives or specify lime simply as manao. There is also makrut lime (Citrus hystrix), the bumpy, dark-green lime whose leaves and zest define Thai and Cambodian cooking but whose juice is rarely used (too acidic, too bitter); makrut is a flavoring ingredient via leaves and zest, not a juicing lime.

  Chemistry. Lime juice is roughly 6 to 8% citric acid by weight (pH 2.0 to 2.5), with smaller fractions of malic acid and ascorbic acid. The aromatic profile comes from the peel oil, limonene (the dominant terpene, citrus-pine character), geranial and neral (the citrus-aldehyde top notes), and traces of bergamotene and other terpenoids. The juice itself carries far less aromatic compound than the peel; this is why lime zest reads dramatically more "lime" than the equivalent volume of juice and why bruising spent lime shells in dressings (Thai practice) extracts the peel oils that the juice alone misses. Citric acid is heat-stable but the aromatic terpenes are volatile; lime added to a hot dish loses the aromatic top within a minute and survives only as acid. This is why lime is a finishing ingredient in Southeast Asian and Mexican cooking, the cook adds it off heat or at the table, not during simmering.

  Role across cuisines. As the primary souring agent in Vietnamese nuoc cham, where lime juice balances fish sauce and palm sugar into the foundational dipping sauce of the cuisine. As the primary acid in Thai som tum dressing, paired with fish sauce, palm sugar, and chili in the four-flavor balance. As the cooking acid in Mexican aguachile and Peruvian ceviche, where lime juice denatures fish proteins through the acid-cure mechanism (parallel to but not identical to heat denaturation, the acid causes protein unfolding and aggregation that produces an opaque firm texture without thermal cooking). As the finishing acid on Mexican tacos, Indian curries (where it replaces tamarind in some regions), Thai noodle soups, Vietnamese pho, and Caribbean stews. As the structural acid in cocktails (margarita, gimlet, mojito, daiquiri) where its aromatic-citric balance is irreplaceable by lemon. As a flavoring zest in Mexican pozole, Thai green curry paste, and Filipino kinilaw.

  Lime juice versus lemon juice. Lemon juice (Citrus limon) is roughly 5 to 6% citric acid (slightly less acidic than lime), with a different aromatic profile, citral and limonene-dominant but with a sweeter, less floral character. The two are not interchangeable in fine work. Mexican and Southeast Asian cooking specifies lime; French and Mediterranean cooking specifies lemon. Substituting lemon for lime in nuoc cham produces a dressing that reads "off", the same acid concentration but a missing floral top note. Substituting lime for lemon in a French sauce reads similarly wrong. The cook treats them as related but distinct ingredients.

  Buying signals. Choose limes that feel heavy for their size (more juice), with thin smooth skin (older limes have thick puckered skin) and a slight give when pressed (rock-hard limes have less juice). Persian limes should be uniformly dark green; yellowing is not a defect (limes ripen to yellow in Mexico and Latin America, where the yellow-ripe lime is the eating preference) but indicates an older fruit and less acidic juice. Key limes are smaller (3 to 5 cm), often with thin yellow-tinged skin, and noticeably more aromatic than Persian when the skin is rubbed. Makrut limes are unmistakable: dark green, bumpy, with a strong perfumed scent from the peel and leaves. Avoid limes with brown soft spots (chill damage, common from refrigerated supermarket display) or wrinkled puckered skin (dehydration).

  Juicing technique and yield. A medium Persian lime (about 60 g) yields 25 to 35 g / 5 to 7 tsp of juice depending on freshness and ripeness. A Key lime (about 25 g) yields 10 to 15 g / 2 to 3 tsp. Roll the lime under your palm against the cutting board for 10 seconds before cutting to break interior membranes, this increases juice yield by 20 to 30%. Cut across the equator (not pole-to-pole), juice with a citrus reamer or a Mexican lime squeezer (which inverts the half and uses the natural shape of the rind to express juice), strain seeds. Microwaving cold limes for 8 to 10 seconds also increases yield. The juice oxidizes quickly, within 15 minutes the pH stays the same but the aromatic top notes degrade; within 2 hours the juice tastes flat and slightly bitter. Juice limes within 30 minutes of using; do not "prep ahead" lime juice except for very short windows.

  Shopping convention in this site. Recipes specify "lime" (Persian/Tahiti default) unless they call out Key lime or makrut explicitly. The grams-of-juice math assumes Persian lime; Key lime substitution requires roughly twice as many limes by count for the same juice yield.

storage: |
  Whole limes: 1 week at room temperature in a fruit bowl; 2 to 3 weeks refrigerated in the crisper drawer. Refrigerated limes hold longer but the skin develops chill damage (brown soft patches) and the juice loses some aromatic top. For best flavor, buy what you will use within a week and keep on the counter. Cut limes: cut-side down on a small plate, refrigerated, 2 to 3 days; the cut surface dries out but the interior holds. Fresh-squeezed lime juice: refrigerated in a sealed glass jar, 24 hours for best flavor, 3 days workable but increasingly flat. Frozen lime juice: 6 months in ice-cube trays or sealed containers, acceptable for cooking applications and cocktails but loses aromatic top notes; not equivalent to fresh for raw applications. Lime zest: freezes well in sealed containers for 6 months, retains aromatic compounds. Bottled "lime juice" (the supermarket plastic-bottle product) is shelf-stable through pasteurization and added preservatives; the citric acid is correct but the aromatic compounds are essentially gone. Acceptable for marinades that will cook off; not acceptable for raw applications. Spoilage cues on whole fruit: soft mushy patches, mold (especially blue-green Penicillium at stem end), fermented smell, hollow lightweight feel (interior has dried out).

    use: 'lemon juice'
    note: 'The closest substitute by acid concentration but the aromatic profile is meaningfully different. Lemon reads sweeter and less floral. The dish will be acid-correct but flavor-shifted. Better than no acid; clearly inferior to fresh lime where lime is structural to the cuisine.'
  - for: 'lime juice (cocktails, raw cures)'
    use: 'kalamansi or yuzu juice'
    note: 'Kalamansi (Filipino calamansi) is the lime-orange hybrid native to the Philippines and reads as a sweeter more aromatic lime; excellent substitute. Yuzu is more lemon-grapefruit and less lime; works in cocktails but shifts the dish toward Japanese profile.'
  - for: 'lime juice (general cooking acid)'
    use: 'rice vinegar plus a drop of lime zest or essential oil'
    note: 'Distant emergency substitute. The acid is correct but the aromatic profile is wrong. Use only when no fresh citrus is available; the dish will read flat.'
  - for: 'makrut lime leaves (Thai/Cambodian cooking)'
    use: 'lime zest plus a bay leaf'
    note: 'Makrut leaves carry a uniquely floral-citrus aroma that has no clean substitute. Lime zest provides the citrus character; a bay leaf adds the slight herbaceous depth. The combination is approximately right but identifiably not makrut. Frozen makrut leaves (available at Thai markets) are the only real solution.'
