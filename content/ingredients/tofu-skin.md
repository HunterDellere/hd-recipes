about: |
  Tofu skin is the film that rises on hot, freshly-made soy milk as soybean proteins coagulate and trap fat at the air-liquid interface. Each successive film is lifted off with a chopstick or rod, hung over a rack to drain, and either eaten fresh on the day it was made or dried into a stable form. The material is concentrated soy: roughly 50% protein, 25% fat, with a chewy meat-adjacent texture that has made it the default vegetarian protein across Chinese Buddhist cuisine, where it is shaped into mock chicken, mock duck, mock fish, and mock pork preparations that go beyond simple substitution into their own canon.

  The forms to know:

  - **Fresh tofu skin (xian zhu sheng / xian fu pi):** thin sheets sold in plastic bags from the refrigerated case at Chinese groceries. Already pliable, already cooked. Used immediately in stir-fries, hot-pot, and noodle bowls; cut into ribbons or wrapped around a filling like a delicate egg roll. Keeps 5 to 7 days refrigerated.

  - **Dried sheets (fu pi):** large, brittle sheets sold flat in plastic bags. Soak in warm water 5 to 10 minutes to rehydrate to pliable; the result is close to fresh tofu skin in behavior. Used for spring-roll-style wraps and stir-fries.

  - **Dried sticks (fu zhu, 腐竹):** the most commonly available form in the West. Long, hollow, ivory-tan sticks made by lifting the wet film and drying it twisted into a long bar. Soak in warm water 1 to 2 hours (or in cold water 4 to 6 hours / overnight for a better, more even rehydration), then cut into 4 cm lengths or angled slices. The hollow structure makes them aggressively absorbent in sauces and braises; this is the form used in Sichuan dry-pot, Cantonese braised tofu skin, and Korean and Japanese hot-pot. The texture rehydrated is chewy with a faint bite.

  - **Knots (fu pi jie):** sheets folded and tied into small knots, then dried. Soak 30 to 60 minutes; same uses as sticks, more decorative shape, holds sauce in the central knot. Often substituted for sticks in dry-pot.

  - **Sweet tofu sheets (tian zhu):** thin sheets briefly cooked in sugar syrup before drying. Used in dessert preparations and sweet-savory braises (especially Cantonese vegetarian dim sum).

  Cooking behavior. Rehydrated tofu sticks have a chewy, dense texture and a near-neutral soy-sweet flavor that takes on whatever sauce it meets. In dry-pot or stir-fry, the sticks contribute structural bite and absorb red oil from doubanjiang-based sauces. In soups, they give body and a meat-adjacent chew that vegetarian preparations rely on. In braises, they soften further and the hollow channels fill with broth, the inside of a properly braised tofu stick is sometimes more flavorful than the meat next to it.

  Soaking is the entire technique. Cold water is slower (4 to 6 hours) but produces a more even rehydration with no soft spots; warm water is faster (1 to 2 hours) but the outer layer can soften before the core does, leading to outside-mush-inside-crunch results in larger sticks. Hot water above 60°C / 140°F damages the protein and produces a slimy, broken texture; do not shortcut with boiling water. Once rehydrated, the tofu skin is ready to cut and cook; do not over-soak past pliable (the texture goes mushy).

  Buying notes. Look for sticks that are pale ivory to gold (deep tan or brown means old or oxidized), straight or evenly twisted (warped pieces indicate uneven drying), and aromatic when the bag is opened (faintly sweet, soybean-leaning; sour or musty means rancid). The soybean oil in tofu skin is the rancidity vector; older product reads stale and grassy. Buy from stores with high turnover and store sealed in a cool, dry pantry.

storage: |
  Sealed in the original bag or transferred to an airtight container, in a cool dry pantry: 6 to 8 months. Refrigerated: 1 year. Freezer: 18 months. Once rehydrated, refrigerate sealed and use within 24 hours; rehydrated tofu skin sours quickly and grows mold within 2 to 3 days. Off-cues are sour or rancid smell, visible mold, or a sticky surface; discard.
