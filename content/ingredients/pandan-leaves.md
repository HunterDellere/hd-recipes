about: |
  The defining aromatic of Southeast Asian sweets. The fragrance comes primarily from 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline, the same compound responsible for the aroma of jasmine rice and freshly baked bread crust; in pandan it's much more concentrated, giving a vanilla-meets-grass-meets-jasmine perfume that has no Western analog. Used across Thai, Vietnamese, Indonesian, Malaysian, and Filipino kitchens to scent rice, custards, jellies, syrups, and coconut milk; in Indonesia and Vietnam the juice extracted from blended leaves also tints preparations a natural pale green.

  Fresh leaves are sold in long ribbons (60 to 90 cm), often folded in plastic sleeves, increasingly available frozen in Asian groceries when fresh is out of season. Frozen retains 80% of the aromatic load; dried pandan (sold as small leaf fragments or powder) loses almost all the 2-acetyl-pyrroline volatiles within months and is barely worth using. Pandan extract sold in tiny bottles (popular brand: Koepoe Koepoe) is usually a synthetic-aroma + green-food-dye blend; it gives color but not the floral depth of real leaves.

  For aroma alone, bruise 2 to 3 fresh leaves with the back of a knife and knot them, then steep in hot liquid (coconut milk, sugar syrup, rice cooking water) for 15 to 30 minutes off heat. Strain before use. For color and aroma both, blanch leaves 10 seconds, blend with 30 ml water per leaf, strain through a fine cloth, the deep green juice is what tints pandan chiffon cakes and chè ba màu jelly.

  Pandan loses aroma fast under sustained high heat. Add to milk or syrup off the boil, or pull leaves out before bringing a coconut milk reduction to a hard simmer. The flavor blooms in fat (coconut cream) and dissolves in water; recipes that pair pandan with coconut milk are working with both extraction media at once.

storage: 'Fresh: damp towel in produce drawer, 5 to 7 days, or freeze whole in a sealed bag for 6 months. Frozen leaves rehydrate texturally during cooking and are functionally equivalent to fresh.'
