about: |
  Filo (also transliterated phyllo, from the Greek φύλλο, "leaf") is an unleavened dough of wheat flour, water, a small amount of oil, and often a splash of acid (vinegar or lemon), stretched and rolled to near-transparency, then stacked as dry sheets typically 0.5 mm thick or less. It is the structural pastry of the Eastern Mediterranean and the Balkans: the layered shell of spanakopita and tiropita in Greece, börek in Turkey, baklava across the whole region, and the savory and sweet pies of the Levant. Unlike puff pastry, filo contains no laminated butter and relies on no leavening; the flake and shatter come entirely from the fat the cook brushes between the sheets at assembly, and from the steam that lifts each ultra-thin layer in the oven.

  The mechanism. A single filo sheet, brushed with melted butter or olive oil and stacked on another brushed sheet, bakes into two crisp planes separated by a film of set fat. Stack six to twelve sheets this way and the oven turns the assembly into a multi-layered crust that shatters cleanly because each thin sheet of baked dough is mechanically isolated from its neighbors by the fat film, no continuous gluten network to chew through. The dough itself is mostly developed gluten (high-protein wheat flour stretched until the sheet is translucent), so an unbrushed, unstacked sheet bakes into a tough, leathery cracker; the fat is not a flavor garnish but the entire structural reason the pastry works. Olive oil produces a slightly coarser, more savory, faintly chewy crust suited to Greek savory pies; clarified butter or ghee produces a finer, more brittle, richer crust suited to baklava and tiropita; a blend is common.

  The dry-out failure mode is the single thing to manage. Filo is so thin that exposed sheets dry to brittle, cracking shards within 2 to 3 minutes of air contact. Once dried, a sheet crumbles instead of folding and is unusable for wrapping. The non-negotiable handling rule: keep the unused stack fully covered at all times with a sheet of parchment or a clean kitchen towel laid lightly over the top (a barely-damp towel for long assemblies, but never wet, which glues the sheets together). Work one sheet at a time, re-covering the stack immediately. A torn sheet is not a problem in a layered pie, the next brushed sheet bonds over the tear, so cooks should patch and proceed rather than discard.

  Thaw and temperature. Filo is sold frozen in tightly rolled or folded sheets. It must thaw slowly: overnight in the refrigerator, then 1 to 2 hours at room temperature still sealed in its plastic before unrolling. Thawing too fast (microwave, warm room from frozen) leaves the outer sheets thawed and tacky while the inner core is still ice-bonded; the sheets then tear and stick into an unusable block. Thawed filo that was never opened keeps a few days refrigerated; refreezing thawed filo degrades it badly (ice crystals rupture the gluten sheets and it shatters on unrolling). Bring the working portion to cool room temperature before assembly, cold-from-fridge sheets are brittle and crack on the first fold.

  Applications by category. The Greek savory canon: spanakopita (spinach and feta), hortopita (mixed wild and cultivated greens), tiropita (cheese), kreatopita (meat), in large pan-baked sheets cut into squares or rolled into individual triangles and coils. The Greek sweet canon: baklava (nuts and syrup), galaktoboureko (semolina custard). The Turkish canon: börek in its many forms (su böreği, sigara böreği), and baklava. The Levantine and broader Eastern Mediterranean canon: warbat, knafeh-adjacent pastries, and savory cheese and spinach fatayer-style pies. Modern Western cooking borrows filo for quick canapé cups, en-croute fish, and apple strudel (strudel dough is filo's close Austrian cousin, hand-stretched rather than machine-rolled). Filo is not interchangeable with puff pastry (which leavens and puffs from laminated butter), shortcrust (which is a tender crumbly fat-and-flour paste), or spring-roll wrappers (which are thinner, often rice or wheat-and-egg, and fry rather than bake into the same shatter).

  Buying. Sold frozen in 450 g (1 lb) boxes containing 18 to 24 sheets, usually in two plastic-wrapped rolls, at most supermarkets (Athens and Apollo are the dominant US brands; Greek and Middle Eastern groceries carry better, fresher stock and sometimes the thicker "country style" sheets used for rustic village pies). Sheet dimensions vary by brand (commonly around 30 by 35 cm or larger); check the box, because a recipe scaled to one brand's sheet size needs adjusting for another. Thicker "horiatiko" (country-style) filo is more forgiving for the home cook (it tears less and handles like sturdier dough) at the cost of a less delicate final crust; standard thin filo gives the finest shatter but demands faster, more careful handling. Look for boxes frozen solid with no sign of thaw-refreeze (a misshapen box or frost inside the wrap signals the sheets have fused).

storage: 'Frozen, in the sealed box, up to the printed date (typically 12 months); freeze rock-solid and avoid any thaw-refreeze cycle, which fuses and shatters the sheets. Thawed but unopened: refrigerated in its sealed plastic, 2 to 3 days; do not refreeze. Opened and partially used: re-roll the unused sheets tightly in their original plastic, seal in a zip-top bag pressing out air, refrigerate, and use within 2 to 3 days (the cut edges dry fastest). Failure modes: sheets fused into a solid block that tears rather than separating (thaw-refreeze damage, discard); brittle sheets that crumble to powder on unrolling (dried out from a torn wrapper or too-long storage, discard); a sour or off smell (rare, but discard). Baked filo pastry holds its crisp 1 to 2 days at room temperature loosely covered; refrigeration softens the crust (the fat sets hard and the crumb goes leathery), so store baked pies at room temperature and re-crisp in a hot oven rather than chilling.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'filo pastry (layered savory pie: spanakopita, hortopita, börek)'
    use: 'thicker country-style (horiatiko) filo, or thawed yufka'
    note: 'Country-style filo is the more forgiving direct swap, sturdier sheets that tear less, at the cost of a slightly less delicate shatter. Turkish yufka is the same dough family in larger rounds and works identically for börek and savory pies. Both handle like standard filo: keep covered, brush every sheet with fat. Use the same number of layers.'
  - for: 'filo pastry (when only puff pastry is available)'
    use: 'all-butter puff pastry'
    note: 'A real change in character, not a clean swap. Puff pastry leavens and puffs from its own laminated butter, so the pie reads richer, breadier, and taller rather than shatter-thin and crisp. It needs no fat brushed between layers (it brings its own). Use a single rolled sheet top and bottom rather than many brushed layers, and expect a different, heavier pastry. Acceptable when filo is unavailable and a quick savory pie is the goal.'
  - for: 'filo pastry (individual hand pies or wraps)'
    use: 'spring roll wrappers'
    note: 'Workable for small triangles and cigars where the filling is the point and the crust is a thin crisp shell. Spring roll wrappers (wheat-based) brush and fold like filo but bake to a thinner, less layered crisp and brown faster, so drop the oven temperature 10 to 15 degrees and watch closely. Wrong for large pan-baked layered pies, where the multi-sheet filo structure is the dish.'
