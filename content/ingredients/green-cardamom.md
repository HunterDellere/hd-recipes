about: |
  Green cardamom is the dried fruit pod of Elettaria cardamomum, a plant in the ginger family native to the Western Ghats of India. The pale-green pods (1 to 2 cm long, papery, slightly oval) contain 8 to 12 sticky black-brown seeds clustered in three internal chambers. The signature aroma is concentrated almost entirely in the seeds — fragrant, complex, with notes of eucalyptus, citrus, and a faint pine-camphor edge from the high terpinyl acetate and 1,8-cineole content. The pod itself contributes only a faint papery contribution. This is why "ground cardamom" sold pre-ground in supermarkets is often disappointing: the grinding includes the bland pod material along with the aromatic seeds, diluting the flavor, and the volatile aromatics oxidize quickly once ground.

  Pods vs seeds vs ground. Three forms exist and they are functionally different. Whole pods (used in savory dishes — biryani, korma, pilaf, chai) are added to the cooking medium and infuse during simmering; the cook fishes them out before serving or warns diners not to bite them. Crushed or split pods (the most common form for home cooking) crack the pod with the side of a knife or in a mortar, releasing the seeds while keeping them tied to the pod for easy removal. Whole seeds (extracted from the pod, used for sweets and finishing applications) are pounded in a mortar to release the volatile oils right before use. Pre-ground cardamom (a Western convenience product) is acceptable for baking applications but loses most of its character within 2 to 3 months of grinding; for any dish where cardamom is structural, whole pods or fresh-ground seeds are the standard.

  Cooking applications. South Asian sweets, kheer, ras malai, gulab jamun, ladoo, halwa, typically depend on cardamom as the dominant flavoring, often paired with rose water or saffron. North Indian biryani and pilaf, 4 to 6 whole pods are added to the rice or the meat marinade. Korma, shahi paneer, and other rich Mughlai curries, typically 3 to 4 pods. Masala chai, cardamom is the structural top note of the spice blend, 4 to 6 pods cracked for a 4-cup pot. Scandinavian baking, Swedish kardemummabullar (cardamom buns) use freshly ground cardamom seeds at significantly higher quantities than Indian sweets (1 to 2 tbsp per batch). The reach is enormous and the cooking grammar varies by tradition.

  Black cardamom is a different ingredient. Black cardamom (badi elaichi, Amomum subulatum) is a larger, dark-brown, smoky-aromatic pod from a different plant entirely; it is used in savory Mughlai cooking (biryani, nihari, garam masala) and is recognizably different from green cardamom. The two are not interchangeable. Recipes that say "cardamom" without specifying typically mean green cardamom; recipes that call for black cardamom say so explicitly.

  Buying notes. Indian groceries sell green cardamom in 100 g bags. Look for plump, firm, pale-green pods with no shriveling or browning. Pre-bleached white cardamom (pods bleached white for cosmetic uniformity) is the same plant but visually paler; the aroma is essentially the same. Whole pods keep their aroma 1 to 2 years sealed; fresh-ground cardamom seeds keep 6 to 8 weeks sealed.

storage: |
  Whole pods: sealed in glass at room temperature, 1 to 2 years. The intact pods protect the seed aromatics. Ground cardamom (commercial or freshly ground at home): sealed in glass, 2 to 3 months for peak; 6 months usable. Refrigeration unnecessary; cool dry pantry. Off-cues are pale color, brittle pods that crumble at touch, or loss of aroma when crushed; discard or grind into a mass-application dish where staleness reads less.
