about: |
  Kecap manis is fermented soy sauce reduced with palm sugar (gula jawa) until thick and syrupy, then aromatized with star anise, galangal, and sometimes salam leaf. The result is closer to molasses in viscosity than to Chinese or Japanese soy sauce, sweet-front with a deep fermented soy backbone and a faint smoky-anise note from the aromatics. It pours in a slow ribbon, not a stream.

  The brand standard is Bango (Indonesian, owned by Unilever) and ABC. Both are widely available in Asian groceries and reasonably authentic. Avoid generic American "sweet soy sauce" sold by mainstream brands, which is usually corn-syrup-thickened light soy sauce without the palm-sugar fermentation depth.

  Functionally it is three ingredients in one bottle: salt, sweet, and umami. In nasi goreng it provides the dark caramel glaze and the dominant flavor; in satay marinades it browns the meat aggressively on the grill (high sugar means fast Maillard); in sambal kecap (chopped chili, shallot, lime, and kecap manis) it is the entire sauce. A teaspoon over a fried egg with rice is a complete Javanese breakfast.

  Reduces further on heat but burns fast. Add late in stir-fries (last 30 seconds) to coat without bitter charring, or whisk into a finishing glaze off heat. The palm sugar content means it caramelizes and blackens at around 175°C / 350°F; satay grills hot, so brush with kecap manis between turns, not before.

storage: 'Pantry indefinitely unopened; refrigerate after opening to slow fermentation drift. Lasts a year in the fridge. Crystallization at the bottom is normal palm sugar; warm the bottle in hot water to redissolve.'
