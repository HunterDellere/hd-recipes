about: |
  Leek (Allium ampeloprasum var. porrum) is a long, cylindrical allium grown for the thickened bundle of overlapping leaf bases that forms the edible white-and-pale-green shaft. Unlike onion (a true bulb), the leek does not swell into a globe at the base; it builds a 25 to 40 cm column 3 to 6 cm thick, with the bottom 15 to 20 cm blanched white through deliberate hilling of soil during cultivation, the middle few centimeters fading through pale green, and the top 10 to 15 cm of dark-green flat leaves that read tougher and more grassy. The blanched white-and-pale-green portion is the prime culinary section; the dark-green tops are tougher but flavor-dense and ideal for stocks. The species is the same as elephant garlic and pearl onion (A. ampeloprasum) but selected for the leaf-base column rather than the cloves or small bulbs. Leek is a winter and spring vegetable in the Northern hemisphere; the plant tolerates frost and improves in flavor after a cold snap, when the leaf bases convert starch to sugar.

  Functionally, leek is a sweet, mellow allium. The dominant sulfur compounds are alliinase-released propanethial S-oxide and dipropyl sulfides (the same family that makes onions tear-inducing and garlic pungent), but at substantially lower concentration than yellow onion or garlic; the result is a milder, more vegetal, slightly grassy onion flavor with a noticeable sweet undertone (sucrose at 2 to 3 percent of fresh weight, plus inulin, a low-glycemic fructan that contributes a delicate sweetness when slow-cooked). The flavor sits between onion (sharper, more tear-inducing) and scallion (greener, more vegetal); leek occupies the sweet-mellow corner of the allium space and is the canonical aromatic when an onion would read too aggressive (delicate fish broths, white-wine cream sauces, vichyssoise). Raw, the leek has a mild bite and a noticeable crunch; cooked low and slow in butter (the French foundational technique called étuver), the leaf bases collapse into a silky, sweet, golden mass that reads as a structural building block of the dish rather than an identifiable allium.

  Transformation. The single operational fact every cook learns the hard way is that the leek's overlapping leaf bases trap soil during cultivation; a leek that has been hilled with garden soil will have grit between every layer of the white shaft if not properly cleaned. The standard technique is to trim off the root and the tough dark-green tops (reserve the green tops for stock), split the white shaft lengthwise into halves or quarters, fan the layers under cold running water until no grit appears, and then slice or chop. The reverse approach (slice first, soak the slices in cold water, lift them out leaving the grit behind) is faster for a high volume but produces less control over slicing geometry. Heat transformations: 60 to 80 °C (étuver in butter, lid on, low heat) collapses the leaf bases in 15 to 20 minutes into a sweet golden mass with no browning, the foundational technique for soubise and for the French chicken-and-leek braise; 130 to 160 °C (sauté in butter or oil, lid off) caramelizes the leaf-base sugars in 8 to 12 minutes into a darker, nuttier "fried leek" reading; 200 °C (oven roast, halved leeks, charred-cut-side-down) produces the smoky-sweet caramelized leek that has become a modern restaurant side. The dark-green tops do not soften in the same way (more cellulose, less sugar) and are best dropped into stock for 30 to 45 minutes and strained out, where they contribute structural alliumness without taking up texture in the dish.

  Applications by category. In French cooking, leek is the second pillar of the white mirepoix (with onion or shallot, fennel, and celery) used in fish stocks (fumet, court bouillon) and in white-wine cream sauces; it is the foundation of vichyssoise (the cold leek-and-potato cream soup), the body of cock-a-leekie's Anglo-Scottish cousin poireaux à la française, and the structural sweet aromatic in Provençal bouillabaisse and bourride (leek replaces or complements yellow onion when the dish wants sweetness without sharpness). In British cooking, leek is the canonical winter aromatic in Welsh cawl (lamb-and-leek stew), in leek-and-potato pie, and in Scottish cock-a-leekie soup. In Belgian and Northern-European cooking, leek pairs with potato in soups and gratins. In modern American restaurant cooking, charred halved leeks are a side dish (with romesco or vinaigrette, the Catalan calçots template), and leek tops are a stock workhorse. Across all categories the same operational rule applies: clean rigorously, choose the heat treatment to match the role (étuver for sweetness, char for smoky depth, stock for background allium), and use the green tops rather than discarding them.

  Buying notes. A good leek is firm, with a tightly bundled white shaft that has no soft, slimy, or yellowed leaf bases at the cut end; the dark-green tops should be vivid and crisp, not wilted or dried out. Size: a "medium" leek is 250 to 350 g whole-weight (about 100 to 150 g trimmed white-and-pale-green portion). Fresh-cut leeks are sold in plastic-wrapped bundles of 2 to 3; the cut ends should still be moist and green. Avoid leeks with woody, fibrous-feeling shafts (they will be tough no matter the cook), with brown spots in the layers, or with extensive yellowing in the green tops. Available year-round but at peak quality from October through April. Trader Joe's and Whole Foods carry consistent stock; farmers' markets in winter months often have larger, sweeter leeks that have been hit by frost. Baby leeks (the spring "ramp"-adjacent immature plants, 1 to 2 cm thick, sold in small bunches) are a different, more delicate product; they are eaten whole, lightly grilled or charred, and do not need cleaning.

  Common Western drift. The most common home-cook mistake is to trim off and discard the entire dark-green portion as inedible. The dark-green tops are tougher than the white shaft, but they carry concentrated allium flavor and the highest fructan content; they belong in the stock pot or the freezer-bag for future stock. The second most common mistake is to skip the slit-and-rinse and end up with grit in the dish; rinse aggressively, every time. The third is to substitute leeks 1:1 by weight for onion in a recipe and end up with too much volume; leeks are bulkier than onions for the same flavor contribution, so reduce the volume by roughly 25 percent or accept a sweeter, more textural dish.

storage: 'Refrigerator, in the crisper drawer, loosely wrapped in plastic or paper towel: 7 to 10 days for fresh whole leeks. The cut end will start to dry out and yellow after a few days; trim off the affected slice before use. Cleaned and chopped leek (white portion only): 3 to 4 days in a sealed container in the refrigerator. Frozen leek: chop and freeze in zip-top bags for 3 to 4 months; use directly from frozen in stocks, soups, and braises (texture suffers if defrosted and used in a sauté). The dark-green tops freeze well for stock; collect in a freezer bag along with onion peels, fennel stalks, and parsley stems for a future stock.'
