title: 'Lemongrass'
desc: 'Tall, fibrous grass stalk with a lemon-pine aroma from citral. The structural aromatic of mainland Southeast Asian cooking; bruised in broths, pounded into spice pastes.'
tags: ['tropical']
updated: '2026-05-17'

about: |
  Cymbopogon citratus, a tropical grass whose woody stalk is layered like a leek and packed with citral, the same aldehyde that gives lemon zest its perfume. The aroma is lemon-forward with a piney-grassy backbone that no citrus alone can replicate; the texture is fibrous and inedible whole, which dictates how it gets used.

  Two preparations. For broths and braises (Vietnamese pho, Thai tom yum, Malaysian rendang), bruise the white-and-pale-green lower 10 to 15 cm with the back of a heavy knife or pound flat with a pestle, then tie in a knot and simmer. The bruising fractures the cell walls and releases the volatile oils into the liquid; pull and discard before serving. For spice pastes (Thai curry pastes, Indonesian bumbu, Malaysian rempah), peel away the dry outer layers, trim to the pale tender heart of the lower third, slice paper-thin against the grain, then pound or blend smooth. The thin slice plus aggressive pounding breaks the fibers small enough to disappear into the paste; left in larger pieces it stays unpleasantly stringy.

  The upper green stalk (about two-thirds of the plant) is too fibrous and aromatically weaker; discard it or use it bruised in stocks. Only the bottom third is worth pounding.

  Fresh stalks should feel heavy and firm with tight, glossy outer layers; bendy or hollow stalks are dried out and have lost most of their volatile oils. The aroma should be sharp lemon at the cut end; faint or musty means the stalk is past prime.

  Frozen lemongrass (whole stalks or pre-sliced) retains 80 percent of the aromatic load and is structurally identical for paste-making. Dried lemongrass and lemongrass paste in tubes are aromatic shadows; usable for emergency seasoning but not for serious work.

storage: 'Fresh: wrap in damp paper towel, refrigerate for 2 to 3 weeks, or freeze whole in a sealed bag for 6 months. Slice frozen with a sharp knife (the freeze makes the fiber easier to cut). Dried: 6 months in an airtight jar but progressively weaker.'

  - for: 'lemongrass (in spice paste)'
    use: 'omit and add 1 tsp finely grated lemon zest at the end'
    note: 'A real lemongrass-free paste tastes structurally different (no piney aromatic, no fibrous bulk). The zest is a top-note approximation; the paste loses body and aromatic depth.'
