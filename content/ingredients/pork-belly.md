about: |
  Pork belly is the cut from the underside (ventral surface) of the pig, between the rear of the ribs and the front of the back leg, typically including the skin (if not removed during butchering) and the alternating layers of subcutaneous fat and muscle that give the cut its distinctive striped cross-section. A whole pork belly weighs 4 to 7 kg, runs 30 to 50 cm long and 20 to 30 cm wide, and is roughly 30 to 60% fat by weight depending on the breed and the feeding (heritage breeds like Berkshire and Mangalitsa run fattier, modern industrial pork is leaner). The cut is sold skinless or skin-on; skin-on belly is the canonical Asian-cooking version, skinless is the standard American supermarket version.

  Structure and rendering behavior. The defining feature of pork belly is the alternating fat-and-lean layers, typically 4 to 6 layers visible in the cross-section, with each fat layer rendering at a different rate during cooking. The subcutaneous fat (closest to the skin) is denser and renders slower; the intramuscular fat is finer and renders faster. Under low slow heat (braising at 85 to 95°C for 2 to 4 hours, sous vide at 75°C for 12 to 24 hours), all the fat layers slowly render into the cooking liquid or out through the surface, leaving lean-meat strands suspended in a silk-textured cooked-fat matrix; this is the structural mechanism behind Chinese hong shao rou and Japanese kakuni, where the finished cut is so tender it disintegrates under chopsticks. Under high dry heat (roasting at 220°C+, grilling, deep-frying), the surface fat renders out into a crackling crisp layer while the interior fat remains relatively intact; this is the mechanism behind Cantonese siu yuk (roasted pork belly) and Filipino lechon kawali. Different applications target different rendering profiles.

  Skin-on vs skinless. The skin (rind) is a structural feature in Asian and Latin pork-belly preparations: it contains collagen that converts to gelatin under long cooking and produces a uniquely tender chew, and it crisps dramatically under high dry heat (the source of pork crackling and chicharon). Cantonese siu yuk and Filipino lechon kawali both rely on the skin to produce their crispy-shell-soft-meat eating experience. American supermarket pork belly is almost always skinless (the skin is removed at the packing plant and sold separately as pork rind), where Asian-market pork belly is typically skin-on. For most American cooks targeting Chinese, Korean, or Filipino preparations, the trip to an Asian market for skin-on belly is structurally important; skinless American pork belly works for Japanese chashu and braised preparations where the skin is sometimes removed anyway.

  Cooking applications by category. **Chinese (multi-regional)**: hong shao rou (red-braised pork belly with caramelized sugar and soy, the most famous Mao Zedong dish), Dongpo pork (Hangzhou specialty, slow-braised cubes), siu yuk (Cantonese roasted pork belly with crispy crackling), lu rou fan (Taiwanese braised pork rice), pork-and-vegetable stir-fries, char siu (Cantonese roast pork, sometimes made with belly instead of shoulder). **Japanese**: chashu (the ramen pork topping, slow-braised pork belly rolled and tied), kakuni (Nagasaki-style braised pork cubes), buta no kakuni, butadon variants. **Korean**: samgyeopsal (grilled pork belly, the canonical Korean BBQ cut), bossam (boiled pork belly served with kimchi and ssam wraps), spicy stir-fries (dwaeji bulgogi). **Filipino**: lechon kawali (deep-fried pork belly), sisig (chopped pork-belly hash), various adobo applications. **American (mainstream and BBQ)**: bacon (the cured-and-smoked product, an entire culinary tradition built around pork belly), modern restaurant "crispy pork belly" applications, BBQ "pork belly burnt ends." **Vietnamese**: thịt kho tàu (caramelized pork-and-egg braise), bún chả (grilled pork belly with vermicelli), banh mi variants. **Italian (pancetta)**: pancetta is salt-cured pork belly, the Italian counterpart to bacon; lardo is the cured fat layer alone. **Western European**: many traditional charcuterie applications (rillettes, terrines, pancetta, guanciale, bacon variants).

  Buying notes. Asian markets (H Mart, 99 Ranch, Patel Brothers, Vietnamese groceries) carry skin-on pork belly in 2 to 4 kg slabs at the meat counter year-round, typically at lower per-pound cost than American supermarkets. American supermarkets carry skinless pork belly in 1 to 2 kg slabs, often sold whole or sliced into 5 to 10 cm-wide strips for Korean BBQ. Butcher-cut pork belly (any quality butcher) is meaningfully better than supermarket pork belly; ask for skin-on if you want it, and ask the butcher to leave the cut larger rather than cubed (you can cube at home for whichever application). Choose pork belly with thick, even fat layers (suggests a well-finished pig), pink-and-white-and-pink stripes in the cross-section (sign of clean butchering), and a mild slightly-sweet smell (any sour or off-smell indicates age or improper handling, do not buy). Whole pork belly (skin-on, 3 to 5 kg) is the right purchase for ambitious cooking; smaller pre-cut pieces are fine for stir-fries and braised dishes.

  Common Western drift. (1) Treating pork belly as a generic fatty pork cut; the specific fat-lean structure is the entire point, and treating it like ground pork or pork shoulder mismatches the technique. (2) Cooking pork belly to a single doneness target; the dish requires either long-low or short-high cooking, and intermediate doneness (medium roast at 175°C) produces unrendered fat that reads greasy rather than silky. (3) Buying lean pork belly thinking that "lean is better"; the fat is the structural feature and an over-lean cut produces a dry, disappointing dish.

storage: |
  Fresh pork belly, refrigerated in original packaging at the back of the fridge: 3 to 4 days from purchase. Wrap tightly in plastic and a layer of aluminum foil for slightly longer shelf life (5 to 6 days). Frozen, double-wrapped: 4 to 6 months at 0°F / -18°C; thaw overnight in the refrigerator before use, never at room temperature. Cooked pork belly: refrigerated 4 to 5 days, frozen 2 to 3 months. Cured pork belly (bacon, pancetta): 1 to 2 weeks refrigerated unopened, 1 week opened. Failure modes are sour or off smell (advanced spoilage, discard), slimy surface (bacterial growth, discard), and grey-green discoloration past the normal aging-gray (discard).

substitutions:
  - for: 'pork belly (Chinese hong shao rou, Japanese chashu, slow-braise applications)'
    use: 'pork shoulder (Boston butt), pork ribs, or beef short rib'
    note: 'Pork shoulder is the leaner substitute; produces a less rich braise but still recognizable, and the texture is dense-tender rather than silken. Cooking time is similar. Pork ribs (country-style or baby back, well-trimmed) work for shorter braises with a different shape on the plate. Beef short rib produces a structurally different dish (beef rather than pork) but is the right substitute for cooks avoiding pork; cooking time may double.'
  - for: 'pork belly (Korean samgyeopsal, grilled application)'
    use: 'pork shoulder steaks, pork loin, or thick-cut bacon'
    note: 'Pork shoulder steaks (1.5 to 2 cm thick) work with the same grilling technique, less fatty and slightly less tender; the dish reads recognizable but less luxurious. Pork loin is leaner still and dries out under aggressive grilling. Thick-cut bacon is the Western-supermarket substitute that produces a similar smoky-fatty character but is significantly saltier (drop any salt seasoning).'
  - for: 'pork belly (American bacon, breakfast use)'
    use: 'pancetta, guanciale, or turkey bacon'
    note: 'Pancetta (Italian salt-cured pork belly) is the closest Italian-tradition substitute, less smoky and more delicate. Guanciale (cured pork jowl) is the canonical Roman pasta cured pork and is fattier and more deeply pork-flavored. Turkey bacon is a dietary substitute that lacks the fat and the structural Maillard browning; produces a meaningfully different dish.'
