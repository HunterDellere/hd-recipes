about: |
  Nori is sheets of red algae (Pyropia tenera and Pyropia yezoensis, formerly classified as Porphyra) that have been chopped into a slurry, pressed flat onto bamboo screens like papermaking, and dried into thin, dark-green-to-black sheets. The standard sheet (zenkei) is roughly 19 × 21 cm and weighs about 3 g. Despite being one of the lightest things in the pantry by mass, nori carries a remarkable umami load ,  free glutamate, alanine, and several nucleotides ,  that hits the tongue almost instantly because the sheet dissolves on contact with saliva. There is more umami per gram of nori than per gram of almost any other pantry ingredient.

  Toasted vs. untoasted. Raw nori (sushi-nori, hoshi-nori) is dark-green to black, slightly papery, and unsuitable for direct eating; it has a faint marine smell and chewy texture. Yaki-nori (toasted nori) has been briefly heated to 170 to 180°C, which shifts the color toward green, crisps the texture, and develops the sweet, faintly nutty top notes that define the eating experience. Most retail nori sold for sushi and snacking is yaki-nori; the toasting can also be done at home over a low flame for 5 to 10 seconds per side to revive sheets that have lost crispness from humidity. Ajitsuke-nori (seasoned nori) has been toasted and brushed with soy sauce, mirin, and sometimes sugar ,  the small snack-size sheets sold in flat boxes for eating with rice. These are not interchangeable with sushi-grade yaki-nori; the seasoning makes them break down too fast in a roll.

  Heat and moisture behavior. Nori is hygroscopic ,  it absorbs moisture from air the moment it leaves a sealed package, and a sheet that has gone limp loses its snap permanently unless re-toasted. For sushi rolls and onigiri, this is a clock: the sheet must be wrapped around rice and eaten within minutes (a freshly-rolled maki has a snap to the wrapper that the second-day version cannot recover). For applications where nori is meant to soften (chazuke, ramen toppings ladled with hot broth), the wilting is the point. The Japanese onigiri convention of wrapping the rice triangle in plastic with the nori sheet in a separate compartment, joined only when the eater opens it, is purpose-built around this constraint: the nori stays crisp until the moment of eating.

  Grades. Nori is graded by appearance (color uniformity, sheen, hole-free sheets) and by harvest timing (early-harvest "shinnori" from Ariake Bay is prized ,  sweeter, crisper, more tender; later harvests are darker and tougher). Grade A nori is uniformly dark green-black with a glossy sheen; lower grades have visible holes, color streaks, or dull surfaces. For sushi making, a grade A or B sheet matters; for tearing into pieces over rice or noodles, lower grades work. Beware of nori that is brown or has a grey cast ,  it is old or was poorly stored.

  Where it fits. Wrapping makizushi (sushi rolls) and temaki (hand rolls); wrapping onigiri (rice triangles, often with a small rectangle pressed against the bottom); ribbon-cut over a bowl of ramen, donburi, or chazuke; toasted and crumbled into furikake (the rice seasoning); shredded fine over yakisoba and okonomiyaki; eaten as snack sheets (ajitsuke-nori). The same sheet does multiple jobs depending on whether it is being used as a wrapper, a topping, or a flavoring.

  Buying. Yaki-nori sold in stacks of 10 sheets, vacuum-sealed with a small desiccant packet, is the standard. Brands like Hakuyuken, Nagai, and Yamamotoyama are widely available. Look for sheets that are uniformly dark green-black, not brown, and check the desiccant packet inside for moisture indication. Premium "Ariake-nori" grades from southern Japan are noticeably better for sushi than generic Pacific-nori. Open packs lose crispness within a week if not resealed; once limp, the sheets can be re-toasted briefly over a low flame, but repeated re-toasting eventually fails.

storage: 'Sealed in the original pack with the desiccant, yaki-nori keeps 6 to 12 months at room temperature. Once opened, transfer to an airtight container or zipper bag with a fresh desiccant packet (silica gel from a vitamin bottle works) and use within 4 to 6 weeks. The failure mode is humidity, not spoilage ,  the sheets pick up water from air, lose snap, and become unpleasantly leathery. Refrigerator storage helps in humid climates; the freezer extends life indefinitely (wrap tightly to prevent freezer odors). Sheets that have gone limp can be re-toasted over a low flame for 5 to 10 seconds per side to restore crispness, but the trick only works two or three times before the flavor flattens.'
