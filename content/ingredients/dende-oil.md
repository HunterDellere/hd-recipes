about: |
  Dendê (Brazilian Portuguese for the West African oil palm tree, Elaeis guineensis) is unrefined red palm oil pressed from the fleshy mesocarp of the palm fruit. The color is bright orange-red from carotenoid content (palm oil is the richest natural source of beta-carotene, with about 500 mg per kg, twenty times the concentration in carrots). The flavor is savory and slightly nutty, the aroma is distinctive and pungent, and the structural character of any dish built on dendê is recognizable from the color alone.

  Dendê is structurally different from refined palm oil (the bleached, deodorized, pale-yellow oil used industrially in processed foods). Refined palm oil has none of the color, none of the flavor, and none of the structural character of dendê; the two are functionally different ingredients despite both being palm oil. Bahian cooking demands unrefined dendê (sometimes labeled azeite de dendê, palm fruit oil, or red palm oil); refined palm oil substituted into moqueca produces a dish that is not moqueca.

  Functional category: structural pigment-and-flavor fat. In Bahian cooking, dendê is the fat that defines moqueca, vatapá, caruru, acarajé (the bean fritters), and bobó de camarão. The fat carries the color into the dish, releases its aromatic compounds during cooking, and acts as a flavor carrier for the dried shrimp, coconut milk, and chilies that complete the Bahian flavor architecture. In West African cooking (where the oil originates), it is the fat of palm soups, palm butter stews, and many of the heavy braised dishes of the Niger Delta and the Ivory Coast.

  Smoke point is relatively high (about 235°C / 455°F) but dendê is rarely used at high heat; the canonical applications use the oil as a flavor base in slow-simmered stews where the temperature stays at or below a simmer. High-heat frying degrades the carotenoids and dulls the color.

  Sourcing. Brazilian groceries (especially in cities with Brazilian populations: Miami, New York, Boston, Newark, Toronto), West African groceries, and online (Nutiva, Spectrum, Yoko brands sell sustainably-sourced palm oil; some Brazilian importers sell traditional dendê). Sustainability is a real concern: industrial palm oil production drives deforestation in Indonesia and Malaysia, though Brazilian and West African dendê production is largely smallholder and lower-impact. Look for RSPO-certified or smallholder-sourced product when available.

storage: |
  Pantry at cool room temperature, 12 to 18 months. The oil solidifies below about 24°C / 75°F, which is normal; warm gently to liquefy before measuring. Refrigerated: indefinite, but solidifies hard and requires warming for use. Avoid direct sunlight (the carotenoids degrade and the color fades). Failure modes: rancid smell (oxidized fats, discard), color shift from orange-red to dull brown (carotenoid loss, still usable but degraded), separation with a clear oil layer on top (normal in cool storage, re-emulsify by gentle warming and stirring).

    note: 'The desperate substitute when no unrefined palm oil is available. For moqueca specifically, this substitution produces something more like a Spanish-Brazilian fusion than a true moqueca; honest acknowledgement is better than pretense.'
