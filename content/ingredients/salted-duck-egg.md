about: |
  Salted duck egg is a raw duck egg cured in saturated salt brine (or, traditionally, packed in a salted clay paste) for roughly thirty to forty days at room temperature. The cure is purely osmotic and salt-driven, with no alkaline component, which is the central point of difference from century egg. Sodium ions migrate through the porous shell and into the white, drawing free water out of both white and yolk; the white reaches a salt concentration high enough to denature its proteins partially but not fully, leaving the cooked white slightly thickened, glassy, and aggressively saline. The yolk transforms more visibly. As water leaves, the lipid network in the yolk consolidates, the carotenoids and xanthophylls (lutein, zeaxanthin) concentrate into a deep red-orange color, and the proteins set into a dense, fudgy, oil-bleeding mass that is the entire point of the ingredient.

  Salted duck eggs are sold raw and cooked. Raw eggs in their cure are taken home and either steamed or boiled (about ten minutes) before eating; the cooked white sets to a slightly tough opaque solid and the yolk firms to its characteristic fudgy red-orange. Pre-cooked eggs are sold ready to halve and eat. The yolks alone, separated and vacuum-packed, are the form most cooks reach for as a baking and stuffing ingredient.

  Functional role: the cooked salted duck egg has three positions in the canon. As a side dish on its own, halved and eaten with plain rice or congee at breakfast, where the salt-fat punch of the yolk makes a pot of rice into a meal. As a filling, where the whole yolk is the buried lava center of a Cantonese mooncake or a savory Chinese rice dumpling (zongzi), and where the yolk's structural integrity through baking or boiling is the technique that mooncake bakers spend a career perfecting. And as a sauce base, blended into hot oil and butter into the salted-egg-yolk sauce that has gone from a Cantonese seafood pairing for crab and prawns into a global ingredient over the past decade, finding its way into pasta, popcorn, and croissants.

  Heat and time matter. The yolk holds its fudgy lava texture only when it has been cured to roughly the right degree (ideally a thirty-five-day cure) and then briefly cooked to set the surrounding white. Over-cured yolks turn chalky and crumble in the hand; under-cured yolks read closer to a normal hard-boiled yolk and lack the dense red-orange character. When cooks blend yolks into a salted-egg-yolk sauce, they steam the yolks first for ten minutes, mash through a sieve, and only then cook them in butter or oil; raw blending into the fat phase produces a granular, broken sauce.

  Buying notes: the canonical salted duck egg is a duck egg, not a chicken egg, and the duck egg's larger yolk and richer fat fraction (about thirty percent versus chicken's twenty-seven) is part of why the cured yolk has its character. Salted chicken eggs exist, sold as a cheaper substitute, but the yolk reads less dense and less red. Look for raw eggs with a clean shell and no visible cracks; pre-cooked eggs sold individually wrapped should have intact whites and bright orange yolks visible through the shell at the wide end. Lee Kum Kee, Eaglobe, and various Hong Kong and Filipino brands (the Filipino itlog na maalat tradition is closely related) all offer reliable cured eggs at North American Asian groceries. The pure-yolk vacuum packs are increasingly available and are the best buy for any cook who wants the yolk specifically as an ingredient and does not want to discard the salty white.

  Common Western drift to avoid: conflating salted duck egg with century egg. They are different ingredients made by different processes (brine cure versus alkaline cure) and they look, taste, and behave differently. A century egg is dark, savory-pungent, and pre-cooked by the alkaline pH; a salted duck egg is bright orange, salt-forward, and needs cooking. Restaurants serving Western audiences sometimes elide the difference, offering one when a recipe asks for the other; the cook who substitutes loses the dish.

storage: 'Raw cured eggs (in their original brine or sealed shell): refrigerator, four to six weeks beyond the cure end-date printed on the package. Once cooked, eat within five days refrigerated. Vacuum-packed pure yolks: refrigerator three months unopened, two weeks once opened and air-exposed. Failure mode for raw eggs is a strong ammoniated or sulfur-spoiled smell on cracking, or visible mold on the shell, both signals to discard. Cooked yolks that have dried into a chalky crust have lost their textural character and are best repurposed mashed into a sauce rather than eaten whole.'
