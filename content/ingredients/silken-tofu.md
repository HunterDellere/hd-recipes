about: |
  Silken tofu is soy milk coagulated directly in its serving container without pressing. The curd traps essentially all of the whey, which is why the water content sits at 88 to 92% by weight (versus 70 to 80% for firm tofu) and why the texture reads as custard rather than cheese-curd. The product is sold in 12 oz / 340 g aseptic packs (Mori-Nu, House Foods Premium Soft) or in refrigerated tubs (Pulmuone Soondubu, Sun Luck), and the difference between brands comes down almost entirely to the coagulant.

  Coagulant chemistry. Three coagulants are used in commercial silken tofu and they produce structurally different products.

  - **Glucono delta-lactone (GDL):** a slow-release acidulant that hydrolyzes into gluconic acid in warm soy milk over 20 to 30 minutes at 70 to 90°C / 160 to 195°F. It produces the smoothest curd of any coagulant — uniformly fine, slightly sweet, with no mineral edge — and is the basis of nearly all Japanese-style hot-set silken tofu and the modern Chinese pour-and-set "tofu pudding" (douhua, 豆花). GDL is the only coagulant that lets a home cook reliably set silken tofu in a serving bowl, because the slow hydrolysis gives time to pour and disturb-free settling. Standard usage is 0.25 to 0.30% by weight of the warm soy milk (2.5 to 3 g GDL per 1000 g soy milk).

  - **Magnesium chloride (nigari):** the traditional Japanese coagulant for both silken and firm tofu. Sets faster than GDL (under 10 minutes at 75°C / 165°F), produces a slightly denser, springier curd with a faintly bitter mineral note that Japanese cooks prize. Most refrigerated Japanese-brand silken tofu (House Foods, Hodo, Pulmuone Soondubu) uses nigari. The fast set means nigari is harder to use as a pour-and-set coagulant at home; it's typically used to set silken tofu in bulk and then cut into serving portions.

  - **Calcium sulfate (gypsum):** the traditional Chinese coagulant. In silken tofu it produces a softer, looser curd than nigari with a faintly sweet, mineral profile. Used in Chinese-style tofu pudding (lao doufu) and as the base for Sichuan mapo tofu when made with silken (rather than firm) tofu. Slightly slower than nigari, slightly faster than GDL.

  Cooking behavior. Silken tofu is structurally too delicate to stir-fry, deep-fry, or otherwise agitate; it will fall apart instantly on contact with a spatula. The cooking applications are five:

  1. **Eaten raw or barely warmed,** as in Japanese hiyayakko (cold cubed silken tofu with bonito, soy, ginger, scallion). The tofu is the dish; the toppings season it. The quality of the tofu is everything; this is when to buy fresh refrigerated nigari-set silken from a Japanese grocery, not aseptic-pack.

  2. **Hot-set in a serving bowl,** by mixing GDL into warm soy milk and pouring directly into the bowl that will be served from. The curd sets in 15 to 25 minutes undisturbed, in the bowl, at the right serving temperature. The technique is the basis of Chinese douhua, the user-requested poured-set mapo tofu variant, and Japanese silken-tofu desserts. See techniques/hot-set-tofu.

  3. **Topped with hot sauce,** as in Sichuan mapo tofu's silken variant, where cubes of silken tofu are blanched briefly in salted water (90 seconds at 80°C / 175°F) to firm the surface and warm them through, then transferred to a serving bowl and the hot mapo sauce is ladled over. The blanch-and-top protocol is the authentic Sichuan mapo move when silken tofu is the choice; the tofu is never tossed in the wok.

  4. **Folded into stews at the very end,** as in Korean sundubu jjigae, where extra-soft silken tofu is broken into chunks and dropped into the simmering broth in the last 60 to 90 seconds. The tofu warms through and absorbs broth without disintegrating; longer cooking dissolves it.

  5. **Blended into smooth sauces, custards, and desserts**, silken tofu purées into a vegan cream substitute that holds emulsions well. Used in vegan chocolate mousse, dairy-free quiche fillings, and creamy salad dressings.

  Hot-set vs cold-pack. The aseptic-pack silken tofu (Mori-Nu) on shelf-stable shelves is GDL-set, cooled, and packed in nitrogen; it is fully set when bought and behaves like a delicate cold custard. The same brand also makes a "tofu pudding" kit that sells the soy milk and GDL separately for the cook to set fresh; this is the home-version of the hot-set technique. Refrigerated silken tofu in water-filled tubs (Pulmuone, House Foods Premium Soft, Sun Luck) is mostly nigari- or gypsum-set in tub and behaves like a delicate fresh cheese, wobblier, milkier, more flavor variation by brand.

  Buying notes. For raw or barely-cooked applications (hiyayakko, the topping for cold mapo), buy fresh refrigerated nigari-set silken from a Japanese or Korean grocery; the difference from aseptic-pack is striking. For hot-set or mapo-topping applications (where the texture will be set or gently warmed in a different vessel), aseptic-pack GDL silken (Mori-Nu Extra-Firm Silken or Soft Silken) is the standard and is genuinely interchangeable with the more expensive options. For the hot-set technique specifically, you need either a tofu-pudding kit (soy milk + GDL packets) or you need to buy plain unsweetened soy milk and food-grade GDL separately; see techniques/hot-set-tofu for the protocol.

storage: |
  Aseptic-pack silken tofu (Mori-Nu) is shelf-stable in the original sealed box for 6 to 8 months at room temperature. Once opened, transfer to a sealed container, cover with cold water, refrigerate, and use within 3 days. Refrigerated tub silken tofu lasts 2 to 3 weeks unopened past purchase, 3 to 5 days opened (sealed in fresh water, water changed daily). Hot-set silken tofu (made fresh from soy milk + GDL) is best within 6 hours of setting and degrades visibly past 24 hours; do not make ahead. Off-cues are sour smell, slimy surface, or yellow-pink discoloration; discard. Never freeze silken tofu, the curd structure breaks irreversibly into a watery sponge.
