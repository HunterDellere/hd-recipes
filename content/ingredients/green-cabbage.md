about: |
  Green cabbage is the workhorse cabbage of European and American cooking, a tightly packed head of pale-green waxy leaves with a dense white core. It is mild, slightly sweet, and high in sulfur compounds that read pleasantly in short cooks and develop the recognizable cabbage-sulfur funk in long cooks (the smell that warns a kitchen something cabbage-y is happening). Texturally it is dense and crisp raw, softening to silken in a long braise, and yielding the pliable leaves that wrap a stuffed-cabbage roll.

  Functionally green cabbage does several distinct jobs in European cooking. Raw and shredded, salted briefly, it is the base of coleslaw and similar salads. Salted at 2% by weight and packed under brine, it is sauerkraut. Wilted in hot water for 2 to 3 minutes, the leaves become pliable enough to roll around a meat-and-rice filling for Polish golabki, Romanian sarmale, or Ukrainian holubtsi. Cut into wedges and braised in stock with bacon or smoked sausage, it is the structural vegetable in a German or Alsatian one-pot meal. Cut into ribbons and slow-cooked with onions and bacon for an hour, it transforms into something silky and sweet that reads almost confit.

  Choose a head that feels heavy for its size with tight, waxy outer leaves; the heavier the head, the more densely packed and the less internal browning. Avoid heads with split or yellowing outer leaves, which indicate age or shipping damage. Savoy cabbage (the crinkled-leaf variety) is a softer, more delicate substitute for stuffed-cabbage preparations and many cooks prefer it for golabki specifically because the leaves are more pliable raw.

storage: 'Whole head, crisper drawer of the refrigerator, 2 to 3 weeks. Cut cabbage, wrapped tightly in beeswax wrap or in a fitted lid container, 5 to 7 days. The cut surfaces oxidize to a faint brown over time; trim before use. Failure mode is sliminess on the cut surfaces or a sulfurous off-smell.'
