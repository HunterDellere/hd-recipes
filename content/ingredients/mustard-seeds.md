about: |
  Mustard seeds are the small round seeds of several Brassica species. Three types appear in cooking: **black mustard** (Brassica nigra, 1 to 1.5 mm, very dark brown-black, aggressively pungent, the canonical South Indian tempering spice), **brown mustard** (B. juncea, 1.5 to 2 mm, reddish-brown, slightly milder than black, the primary commercial mustard-seed crop worldwide and the species found in most Indian grocery stores labeled "black mustard"), and **yellow/white mustard** (Sinapis alba, 2 to 3 mm, pale yellow, milder, the species used in American yellow mustard and British mild mustard). In South Asian cooking, "black mustard seeds" refers almost always to the brown mustard (B. juncea), which is what reaches most North American markets labeled as black mustard; the genuinely black B. nigra is harder to source but closer to the canonical flavor profile. For practical purposes they are interchangeable in cooking, with black/brown reading sharper and more aggressive than yellow.

  The pungency mechanism is the glucosinolate-myrosinase enzymatic reaction. Whole intact mustard seeds are essentially pungency-inert; the glucosinolates (sinigrin and sinalbin are the dominant compounds) and the myrosinase enzyme are stored in separate cellular compartments. When the cell walls rupture (through grinding, crushing, or heat-induced steam pressure rupture), the compounds mix, and myrosinase cleaves the glucosinolates into glucose, sulfate, and the volatile isothiocyanate (ITC) that produces the sharp mustard pungency. In wet preparations (ground mustard with water or vinegar), this reaction peaks within 15 minutes at room temperature and produces maximum pungency; acid (vinegar) stabilizes the ITC and keeps the pungency longer, which is why prepared mustard stays sharp in the jar. In dry tempering (whole seeds dropped into hot fat), the steam pressure inside each seed ruptures the coat at about 165 to 175 °C, producing a sharp pop and releasing the ITC into the fat — but the ITC is volatile and at cooking temperatures it dissipates quickly, leaving behind the now-nutty fried-seed character and the mild sulfurous aromatic that reads as "mustard" in a tempered South Indian context rather than the sharp pungency of raw ground mustard.

  The key operational fact is that tempered mustard seeds taste completely different from ground raw mustard seeds. The same compound, through a different preparation, produces two radically different sensory results: the hot-oil tempered seed is nutty, mildly bitter, and aromatic; the raw-ground-with-water version is sharp, hot, and pungent. South Indian cooks use tempered whole seeds for tadka; Bengali cooks use both ground-paste (for mustard fish) and tempered whole; the two preparations should not be swapped.

  **Applications by category.** In South Indian cooking, mustard seeds are the first spice into the pan in virtually every dish: the tadka for dal, sambar, rasam, vegetable stir-fries, and chutneys opens with mustard seeds in hot oil or ghee popping for 15 to 20 seconds before anything else is added. The dish takes on a characteristic nutty-aromatic base note from the bloomed seeds. In Goan cooking, mustard seeds temper the fat for fish curry (alongside curry leaves and dried chilies) and appear in vindaloo bases. In Sri Lankan cooking, mustard seeds and curry leaves in coconut oil open the tempering for fish curries and kiri hodi. In Bengali and Bangladeshi cooking, panch phoron (the five-spice whole-seed blend including mustard, nigella, fenugreek, cumin, and fennel) is a regional tadka signature used in fish preparations (whole mustard-paste fish like Shorshe ilish) as well as in pickles and vegetable dishes. In North Indian cooking, mustard seeds are less central than in the South (cumin seeds dominate northern tadkas) but appear in Punjab-region mustard preparations and Rajasthani dal recipes. In European and American cooking, yellow mustard seeds are pickled (for bread-and-butter pickles and American dill pickles) or ground into prepared mustard; brown mustard seeds appear in whole-grain mustard and in some charcuterie preparations.

  **Buying notes.** Black or brown mustard seeds are available at any South Asian grocery (Indian, Sri Lankan, Bangladeshi specialty stores), at many natural food stores, and at H Mart. The seeds should be uniform in size, clean, and dark brown-to-black for the South Asian cooking type. Avoid the yellow variety for South Asian tadka applications (the flavor profile is milder and the color is wrong). Store in an airtight container; whole mustard seeds keep their pungency potential for 2 to 3 years since the glucosinolate-myrosinase system is intact and stable in the dry whole seed.

storage: 'Pantry, airtight container, dark and cool: 2 to 3 years for the whole dry seeds (the glucosinolate system remains intact). Ground mustard powder: 6 to 12 months, after which the volatile isothiocyanates have largely dissipated and the powder reads flat; buy in small quantities and replace often. Prepared mustard (in vinegar): 1 to 2 years refrigerated after opening; the acid stabilizes the ITC and the pungency diminishes slowly.'
