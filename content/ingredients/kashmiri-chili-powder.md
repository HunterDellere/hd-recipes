about: |
  Kashmiri chili powder is a mild, deeply red, low-heat ground chili made from Kashmiri or Byadgi cultivars (Capsicum annuum varieties grown in Kashmir, Karnataka, and increasingly in other Indian states). The Scoville heat is low (1500 to 2500 SHU, comparable to a poblano), and the spice is used primarily for color rather than for heat. The pigment density is dramatically higher than supermarket cayenne or paprika; a small amount of Kashmiri chili powder produces a vivid red color that defines tandoori chicken, butter chicken, rogan josh, and most North Indian and Mughlai curries that read as visually red.

  Why color, not heat. Indian cooking separates color and heat across two different chili sources: Kashmiri chili powder for color (added in larger quantity, 1 to 3 tsp per curry), and a smaller amount of hotter chili (cayenne, ground green chili, or fresh chopped chilies) added separately for heat. This separation gives the cook independent control over color and heat, which a single cayenne-or-paprika approach cannot. The visual red of a butter chicken or a rogan josh is structural to the dish identity; substituting a less-pigmented chili produces a paler dish that reads as wrong even at correct heat level.

  Deggi mirch vs Kashmiri chili. "Deggi mirch" (degi mirch) is a commercial blend of Kashmiri chili powder with paprika and sometimes a small amount of hotter chili, designed to provide the same color profile at a lower price point. The major Indian brands (MDH Deggi Mirch, Everest Tikhalal Lal Mirch) are standard at home and in restaurant kitchens. Pure Kashmiri chili powder is slightly more expensive and is the upgrade for applications where color depth matters most (tandoori, rogan josh).

  Cooking applications. Tandoori marinades (the brilliant red color that defines tandoori chicken and seekh kebab is structural — yogurt + Kashmiri chili powder + garam masala + ginger-garlic), butter chicken, rogan josh, paneer tikka, chicken tikka masala, dal makhani (small amount for color depth), and any North Indian curry where the visual red is part of the dish. Standard ratio: 1 to 2 tsp Kashmiri chili powder per 4-serving curry, often paired with ¼ to ½ tsp cayenne for heat.

  The "color, not heat" principle extends. Some restaurants and home cooks add tomato paste or beet juice to deepen the red color of tandoori marinades. Acceptable but unnecessary if Kashmiri chili powder is fresh and used at the right quantity. The color should come from the chili.

  Buying notes. Indian groceries reliably stock Kashmiri chili powder. MDH and Everest are standard; Burlap & Barrel and Diaspora Co. carry small-batch versions for the spice-quality conscious. The powder should be brilliant deep red, not dusty maroon. Heat level varies slightly by brand and harvest year; if the color reads right but the dish reads under-spiced, add a small amount of cayenne separately.

storage: |
  Sealed in glass at room temperature, 9 months for peak color; 1 year usable. Cool dry pantry, away from light (the pigment fades under UV). Heat and humidity accelerate degradation; do not store next to the stovetop. Off-cues are dull color (potency lost) or musty smell (humidity-driven mold, rare); discard.
