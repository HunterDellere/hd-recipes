about: |
  A large US egg is around 50g out of shell: 30g white (mostly water plus ovalbumin), 18g yolk (lecithin, fat, more protein), 6g shell.

  The two halves cook differently. Whites begin to set around 62°C and finish firm by 80°C. Yolks stay fluid until around 65°C, set creamy at 70°C, and turn chalky past 75°C. Custards, dun nai, soft scrambles, and tempered sauces all live in the gap between those numbers.

  Yolks are the emulsifier in mayo, hollandaise, ice cream, and most cake batters — that's the lecithin doing the work. Whites trap air and give meringues, soufflés, and angel cakes their lift.

  Older eggs whip into taller meringues (the whites have thinned and unfold faster), but fresher eggs poach better (tighter whites hold their shape).

storage: 'Refrigerator, in the original carton, on a middle shelf, not the door. Last 4–5 weeks past pack date. Bring to room temperature before baking unless a recipe specifies cold.'
