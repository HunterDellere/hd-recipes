about: |
  Wood ear (Auricularia heimuer, also widely sold as A. auricula-judae) is a tree-growing jelly fungus, harvested fresh and almost always sold dried as small, brittle, dark-brown to black chips. Rehydrated in cool water for 30–60 minutes it expands roughly 5x by weight to a translucent, slightly rubbery sheet with a distinct snap-against-jelly texture. The structural component is a chitin-glucan-polysaccharide matrix that holds its shape against high heat, long simmering, and acid in ways no cap mushroom can match. This persistence is what cooks buy it for.

  Functional role: wood ear is a texture and visual ingredient first, a flavor ingredient barely at all. The fungus itself contributes a faint earthy umami undertone but reads as almost neutral; what it brings to a dish is dark color contrast, ribbon shapes that catch on a spoon, and a yielding crunch that survives ten minutes in a wok or thirty minutes in a soup. Treat it the way a savvy cook treats fresh bean sprouts or jicama: a structural carrier that absorbs the surrounding seasoning while contributing its own physical signature.

  Across regional Chinese cooking, wood ear shows up wherever a dish needs dark texture against soft components: in soups it provides the slippery ribbons that distinguish hot-and-sour soup from any other thickened broth; in stir-fries it adds bite to soft proteins (mu shu pork, mooshu rou si, yu xiang dishes, scrambled-egg-and-tomato in some northern variants); in cold dishes it stands in for a vegetable with structure (cold-dressed wood-ear salad with chili oil and vinegar, a Northeast Chinese standard); in steamed dishes it lines the bottom under fish or pork and reads as a visual frame. In Korean cooking it appears in japchae and as a topping on bibimbap; in Japanese cooking it shows up as kikurage in tonkotsu ramen and in salads. Across all of these the role is identical: structural texture with neutral flavor.

  How heat changes it: almost not at all. The polysaccharide matrix is thermally stable past most home-kitchen temperatures, and wood ear retains its bite after long simmers that would reduce a cap mushroom to mush. This is part of why it is the canonical fungal ingredient in soups and braises that also include softer vegetables; everything else cooks down around it while the wood ear stays intact.

  Soaking is non-negotiable but easy to mishandle. The food-safety rule is that wood ear should be rehydrated in cool water for under two hours, or in the refrigerator for longer soaks. Wood ear left rehydrating in warm water at room temperature for 12+ hours has caused multiple documented cases of bongkrekic acid poisoning from Burkholderia gladioli, a soil bacterium that can grow on the wet fungus and produce a heat-stable toxin. The toxin survives cooking. The fix is procedural, not chemical: soak short and cold, and refrigerate any rehydrated stock that is not used immediately.

  Two close varieties shouldn''t be confused. 木耳 (heimuer / black wood ear) is the standard. 银耳 (yin er / snow ear / silver ear, Tremella fuciformis) is white, more delicate, more gelatinous when cooked, and used almost exclusively in sweet preparations (Chinese sweet snow-ear and lotus seed soup, snow-ear pear stews, dessert congees). The two have similar structure but different aesthetic and culinary roles, and they are not 1:1 swaps. Some packages also sell yun er (cloud ear, smaller and thinner-petaled), a different cultivar of wood ear that rehydrates faster and stays more delicate; useful in salads where you want less chew.

  Buying notes: look for whole pieces, not pre-shredded. Whole pieces let the cook decide cut size; the shredded product is often older stock and rehydrates unevenly. Quality wood ear pieces are uniform black-brown without dusty residue, snap rather than bend when dry, and rehydrate to translucent rather than opaque sheets. The fresh form, occasionally available at well-stocked Asian markets, is firmer and more delicate but functionally interchangeable.

storage: 'Dried, in an airtight container, dry pantry, away from light. Holds 1–2 years. Failure mode is dampness: any moisture that gets in causes mold inside the bag within weeks. Once rehydrated, refrigerate in fresh water and use within 2 days; do not re-dry rehydrated pieces (they will not rehydrate again the same way and the moisture history is a food-safety risk).'
