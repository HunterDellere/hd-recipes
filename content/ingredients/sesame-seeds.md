about: |
  Sesame seed is the small flattened oilseed of Sesamum indicum, one of the oldest cultivated oilseeds in human food history. The seed coat is thin, the body is composed of roughly 50 to 55% lipid (predominantly oleic and linoleic acid), 20% protein, and a notable concentration of lignans (sesamin, sesamolin, sesamol) that act as natural antioxidants and contribute to the seed's exceptional shelf stability and the slow oxidation of sesame oil compared to other vegetable oils. The seed comes in white (hulled) and black (unhulled) forms; white sesame is the hulled version, milder and sweeter, the standard for Korean and Japanese garnishes and for tahini production; black sesame is the unhulled version, faintly more bitter and notably more pigmented, the standard for Chinese sweet pastes, Japanese gomashio, and visual contrast in plating. The hulled white seed and the unhulled black seed are not different cultivars in most cases; they are processing distinctions on the same plant material.

  Functional role. Sesame seeds are a fat-and-flavor delivery vehicle in three modes: as a whole textural garnish (raw or toasted, scattered across a finished dish for crunch and nutty aroma), as a ground paste (tahini in Levantine cooking, neri goma in Japanese cooking, dou jiang in Chinese sesame paste), and as a base for sesame oil (cold-pressed for light white sesame oil, dark-toasted and pressed for toasted sesame oil). Across all three modes, the value is the unique aromatic profile of toasted sesamol and sesamolin (the lignans that develop characteristic roasted-sesame aromatics during toasting) layered over the high lipid content (the seed coats the palate and carries fat-soluble flavors). The umami contribution is minor (low free glutamate), but the texture and aroma contribution is structural to Korean banchan, Japanese rice toppings, Chinese cold dishes, and Middle Eastern dips.

  Transformation. The defining transformation is dry-toasting. Raw white sesame seeds are pale beige, dense, and faintly grassy in aroma. Heated in a dry skillet over medium heat with constant agitation, the seeds undergo a predictable sequence: at 60 to 90 seconds the seeds release a soft popping sound (water flashing in the seed body, similar to the popcorn mechanism but at much smaller amplitude), at 2 to 3 minutes the color shifts from beige to pale golden, at 3 to 4 minutes the color reaches the correct light caramel-bronze and the aroma is unmistakably toasted-sesame, and past 4 minutes the seeds darken and turn bitter as sesamol breaks down past its useful window. The correct pull is the moment the popping slows and the color reaches caramel-bronze; the residual pan heat continues to toast for another 15 seconds after pulling, so pull slightly under and rest the seeds on a cool plate. Black sesame seeds are harder to read for color and rely on aroma alone; the same time window applies. Toasted sesame seeds keep their aroma for 1 to 2 weeks at room temperature in a sealed jar; past that, the lignans degrade and the toasted flavor flattens. Toasting in small batches (2 to 4 tbsp) as needed produces dramatically better results than buying pre-toasted seeds, where the toast is days or weeks old and the aroma has flattened.

  Applications by category. In Korean cooking, toasted white sesame seeds are scattered over nearly every banchan (oi-muchim, kongnamul, miyeok-muchim, gimchi-jjim), sprinkled across rice bowls and bibimbap, and ground into tare for kalbi and bulgogi marinades; the seed appears in roughly 80% of finished savory Korean dishes as the visual and aromatic signature. In Japanese cooking, white sesame is the body of furikake rice toppings, the nutty layer in horenso no goma-ae (spinach with sesame), the garnish on inari-zushi, and the seed for gomashio (toasted seeds with salt); black sesame appears in goma tofu, kuromame, and as a contrast garnish on white-rice plating. In Levantine cooking, hulled white sesame is the structural component of tahini, which then becomes hummus, baba ghanoush, halva, and the dressing base for most Israeli salads. In Chinese cooking, sesame seeds appear on shaobing (sesame flatbreads), bao buns, mantou, the surface of red-cooked pork, in chilled noodle dressings, and as the sesame paste in Sichuan dan dan mian and Beijing-style hot pot dipping sauce. In Mexican cooking, sesame seeds are an essential element of mole poblano (toasted and ground into the spice paste).

  Buying notes. Look for white sesame seeds at any Asian grocery (Korean, Japanese, and Chinese markets carry them in bulk at the best prices), Middle Eastern groceries, or the bulk bins at well-stocked supermarkets. The seeds should be uniform in color, dry, and free of broken or chipped seeds (broken seeds indicate old stock or rough handling). Pre-toasted seeds in jars or vacuum packs lose aroma rapidly after opening; raw seeds toasted at home are dramatically better. Hulled white sesame is preferred for toasting and garnish use; unhulled white seeds have a slightly bitter coat that is fine for tahini but not for garnish. Black sesame quality varies; the Japanese kuro-goma standard is the best widely available.

storage: 'Raw sesame seeds: airtight jar in the pantry, 6 months. Refrigerator, 1 year. The high oil content makes the seeds prone to rancidity past those windows; rancid sesame smells acrid and cardboard-like. Toasted sesame seeds: airtight jar at room temperature, 1 to 2 weeks (the toast flavor fades fast). For longer storage, toast in small batches as needed. Toasted sesame oil: refrigerator after opening, 6 months. Ground sesame paste (tahini): refrigerator after opening, 3 months; oil separation is normal and reincorporates with stirring.'
