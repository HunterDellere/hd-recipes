about: |
  Cucumber is the elongated fruit of Cucumis sativus, a cucurbit grown commercially in dozens of cultivars distinguished primarily by skin thickness, seed-cavity development, and bitterness. Across cultivars the flesh is roughly 95 percent water held in a thin-walled parenchyma, with the structural firmness coming from turgor pressure rather than fiber: a cucumber crisps because the cells are turgid and snaps because they rupture cleanly under shear. The cool sensation in the mouth is partly real (the high water content cools by evaporation as it warms) and partly the chemical effect of trace methylated cucurbitacins on cold-receptor neurons.

  Cultivars and the right one for a given job. Persian cucumbers (4 to 6 inches, thin skin, small seeds) are the gold standard for raw salads — they have the highest flesh-to-seed-cavity ratio, the skin is tender enough to leave on, and the texture stays crisp 20 minutes into a salted dressing. English cucumbers (12 to 14 inches, sold shrink-wrapped to protect the thin skin) are nearly seedless and slice cleanly into long ribbons; the texture is slightly less crisp than Persian. Slicing cucumbers (the standard American supermarket product, waxed for shelf life) have the thickest skin and the largest seed cavity; the wax should be peeled off and the seeds scraped if the cucumber will not be salted. Kirby (pickling) cucumbers are short, knobby, and bred for thicker skin and crisper texture under brine; they make the best raw smashed-cucumber salads but are less commonly stocked outside summer.

  The bitterness gene and salting. Cucurbitacin C (the bitter compound concentrated in the skin and the stem-end of the fruit) is produced by a single dominant gene; well-bred cultivars have the recessive non-bitter version, but field stress (heat, water stress, root damage) can re-express the gene even in non-bitter varieties. The traditional fix — slicing off the stem end and rubbing the cut surfaces together until foam forms ; does extract some cucurbitacin into the foam and is not pure superstition; the foam is a saponin-and-cucurbitacin mixture. Salting cut cucumbers and draining the released water also removes a fraction of the bitter compounds; this is the secondary benefit of the salting step that's primarily about firming the texture.

  Salting the texture question. Cucumber flesh holds its water in turgid cells; a 1 percent salt application to cut cucumber draws water out of the cells via osmosis over 15 to 30 minutes, the released water can be drained off, and the resulting cucumber is firmer (the cell walls stay intact) and more concentrated in flavor. This is canonical for Asian smashed-cucumber salads, Greek tzatziki, and any dish where the cucumber would otherwise weep into the bowl over the meal. Unsalted cut cucumber retains more crispness in the very short term (5 to 10 minutes) but releases water steadily afterward; salting trades initial crunch for sustained structure.

  Applications by category. Raw salads across cuisines (fattoush, Greek horiatiki, Lebanese tabbouleh, Thai yam taeng, Korean oi muchim, Sichuan smashed-cucumber salad, English tea sandwiches). Yogurt sauces (Greek tzatziki, Indian raita, Persian mast-o-khiar, Turkish cacık) ; usually grated and drained because the surface area releases water faster than diced. Quick pickles (rice-vinegar quickles for Vietnamese banh mi; salt-and-vinegar refrigerator pickles for Eastern European applications). Lacto-ferments (full-sour deli pickles, Kirby cucumbers in 3 to 5 percent salt brine for 1 to 4 weeks). Aguas frescas and gin garnishes for the high-water-content cooling effect.

  Buying. In summer, look for unwaxed Persian or Kirby cucumbers from a farmer's market ; the difference in crispness is dramatic compared to wax-coated supermarket slicers. Year-round, English cucumbers in shrink-wrap are the most reliable supermarket option; the wrap protects the skin and the seedless interior holds well. Skin should be deep green with no soft spots or yellow patches (yellowing indicates over-ripeness and seed development). The cucumber should feel heavy for its size (a sign of full water content); a light cucumber has begun to lose turgor.

storage: 'Refrigerated in the high-humidity drawer 4 to 7 days; never wash before storing (surface moisture accelerates skin breakdown). Store away from ethylene producers (apples, tomatoes, bananas) ; cucumbers are ethylene-sensitive and turn yellow and soft within 24 hours of exposure. Failure modes: soft spots (chill injury below 7 °C / 45 °F or end-of-life; cut around if minor, discard if widespread); slimy skin (bacterial breakdown, discard); bitter taste throughout (cucurbitacin re-expression, cannot be fixed; salt-and-drain only helps marginally); hollow center (overgrowth on the vine; texture is compromised but the fruit is safe to eat).'

    note: 'Persian holds crunch nearly as well as Kirby in a 24-hour quick pickle; the slightly thinner skin softens faster in long ferments, so Persian-based pickles are best eaten within 1 to 2 weeks rather than aged.'
