about: |
  Yuzu (Citrus junos, sometimes Citrus × junos) is a small to medium East Asian citrus, originally a wild hybrid of Citrus reticulata (mandarin) and Citrus cavaleriei (Ichang papeda), domesticated in China and Korea over a thousand years ago and now most strongly identified with Japan. The fruit is small (60 to 90 g, golf-ball to tennis-ball size), thick-skinned (the rind makes up 35 to 50% of the fruit weight, much higher than lemon), with a yellow-to-green skin (depending on harvest timing; green yuzu is harvested in autumn and prized for the most aromatic rind, yellow yuzu is harvested in early winter and has more juice), large prominent oil glands in the rind, and a small amount of acidic juice (typically 10 to 20 g of juice per 90 g fruit, far less than lemon at 30 to 40 g per fruit). The flesh is segmented like a regular citrus and contains many seeds; the seeds are large enough to be removed easily but plentiful enough that juicing is a strain-required step.

  The aromatic chemistry. Yuzu's defining character is in the rind, not the juice. The rind oil is dominated by D-limonene (similar to other citrus, the bulk of the volatile fraction), but the aromatic profile that makes yuzu read as yuzu rather than as generic citrus comes from a specific mix of sesquiterpenes (β-farnesene, β-bisabolene, others), oxygenated terpenes (linalool, α-terpineol), and characteristic minor compounds (yuzunone, the ester thymol methyl ether). The combined effect is a bright citrus top note with floral, faintly spicy, and pine-like undertones; yuzu reads as bergamot crossed with grapefruit and mandarin, with a quality of cleanness that lemon and lime lack. The rind is the highest-leverage part of the fruit; a small amount of grated zest carries the aromatic signature of the entire fruit. The juice contributes acid (5 to 7% citric acid by weight, similar to lemon at 5 to 6%) plus a small fraction of the rind aromatics that have migrated into the juice during juicing, but the juice alone is a less complete experience of yuzu than the zest.

  Cooking applications by category. Ponzu (Japan): yuzu juice (or a blend of yuzu, lemon, and lime) steeped 24 hours with kombu and katsuobushi in soy sauce, mirin, and rice vinegar; the canonical citrus-soy dipping sauce of washoku, used with grilled fish, chanko nabe hot pot, shabu-shabu, gyoza, agedashi tofu. Yuzu kosho (Japan): fresh green yuzu rind, salt, and green chilies pounded together and aged into a paste; an intense seasoning for grilled meat, sashimi, soup, and noodle broths. Yuzucha (Korea): yuzu peel and juice preserved in honey or sugar syrup, the standard Korean breakfast tea (a spoonful in hot water; the Korean name is yuja-cha and the product is sold widely at Korean markets). Yuzu sherbet, sorbet, ice cream (Japan, contemporary Western): a single squeeze of yuzu juice and a strip of zest carry a small batch of dessert; the aromatic intensity outpaces lemon at the same volume. Marinades, dressings, finishes (contemporary Western adoption since the 2000s): a small amount of yuzu juice or zest in vinaigrettes, mayonnaise-based sauces, and grilled-protein finishes brings a brightness that the dish would otherwise need a citrus blend to approximate.

  Pairings and finish strategies. Yuzu and soy: the canonical washoku pairing, the basis of ponzu and many simmered fish dishes. Yuzu and grilled or seared oily fish: the citrus-acid cuts the lipid load, and the floral aromatic register matches the rich fish; saba, sanma, salmon, yellowtail. Yuzu and dashi: the floral citrus brightens the umami stack without overpowering the kombu-katsuobushi delicacy; common in clear soups and refined nimono. Yuzu and chili (yuzu kosho): the heat and the citrus reinforce each other; the combination lifts grilled meats and noodle broths. Yuzu and honey or sugar (yuzucha, yuzu marmalade): the bitter-aromatic rind balances the sweetness and the result is far more complex than a lemon marmalade.

  Fresh versus frozen versus bottled. Fresh yuzu in the US is rare and expensive (often $4 to $8 per fruit at Japanese specialty grocers, and only available October to January); when available, fresh whole yuzu is the best possible source for both juice and zest. Frozen whole yuzu, sold at most Japanese grocers and increasingly at Korean markets (H Mart in particular), is the best year-round option; the freezing preserves both the juice acid and the rind aromatics well, and the thawed fruit is comparable to fresh for most applications. Bottled yuzu juice (Yuzuya Honten, Mitoku, several Japanese brands) is widely available and acceptable for cooking applications where the juice is being heated or combined with other strong flavors (ponzu, marinades, simmered dishes); the bottled juice loses some of the volatile top notes during pasteurization and has typically been pressed from yellow yuzu (less aromatic than green). Avoid yuzu products that list "yuzu flavoring" or "yuzu juice from concentrate"; these are usually citric acid plus aromatic isolates and bear little resemblance to real yuzu. Yuzu zest is essentially impossible to find dried or preserved at acceptable quality; if a recipe needs zest, it needs whole fresh or frozen fruit.

  Common Western drift. The most common drift is "yuzu = lemon-lime mix"; this approximates the acid balance and gets a small fraction of the floral character, but misses the specific aromatic signature that defines yuzu. The standard Western substitute (3 parts lemon juice + 1 part lime juice + a strip of grapefruit peel or a few drops of bergamot extract) is acceptable for cooking applications where yuzu is one note among many; for applications where yuzu is the foreground (yuzu sorbet, plain yuzu salt over fish, yuzu kosho), only real yuzu works. The other common drift is treating bottled yuzu juice as fresh; the bottled juice covers most cooking uses but is not adequate for the highest-aromatic applications (sashimi yuzu salt, finishing yuzu over ice cream).

storage: |
  Fresh whole yuzu: 2 weeks at room temperature (the rind dries slightly but the juice stays usable), 4 to 6 weeks in the refrigerator (in a paper bag, not plastic, to prevent surface mold). Frozen whole yuzu (in original vacuum pack from H Mart or Japanese grocer): 12 months at full quality, longer acceptable; thaw in the refrigerator overnight before use, juice immediately upon thaw, and do not refreeze. Bottled yuzu juice, unopened: 12 months in the pantry; opened: 2 to 3 months in the refrigerator (the volatile aromatics fade over time). Fresh yuzu juice (squeezed and strained from a fresh or thawed fruit): 1 week refrigerated, 6 months frozen in ice-cube trays. Yuzu zest, fresh: use immediately; loses aromatic intensity within hours. Failure modes for fresh yuzu: surface mold (fungal growth on the rind, often in spots; trim aggressively if minor and use, discard if extensive), soft spots that smell fermented (over-ripe, the fruit has begun to ferment internally, discard), juice that smells flat or musty (oxidation, the juice is past peak; discard if for raw applications, use for cooked applications if otherwise acceptable).
