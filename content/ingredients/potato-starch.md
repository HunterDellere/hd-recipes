about: |
  Potato starch is the pure starch fraction extracted from potato tubers by wet milling: the potatoes are ground, the starch granules wash free of the protein and fiber, and the dry powder is collected and air-dried. The result is a pure white, slightly granular powder composed of roughly 20% amylose and 80% amylopectin packed in unusually large granules (50 to 100 microns, compared to 5 to 10 microns for rice starch and 15 to 25 microns for cornstarch). The granule size is the single most important property: large granules absorb water rapidly, swell to many times their dry volume, and produce a shell that fries crisper, lighter, and with more shatter than cornstarch or wheat starch. The gelatinization temperature is also unusually low at 60 to 65°C; potato starch begins to thicken liquids at much cooler temperatures than cornstarch (75 to 85°C), which makes it the preferred thickener for clear glazes that should not break under prolonged heat.

  Functional role. In Korean and Japanese frying, potato starch is the structural coating that produces the characteristic glass-shatter crisp on yangnyeom chicken, karaage, and dakgangjeong; the large granules absorb surface moisture from the protein, dehydrate in the oil to a network of dry crystalline shells, and produce a thinner, lighter, more delicate crust than wheat flour or cornstarch. In batter applications (Korean haemul pajeon, jeon, Japanese tempura when blended with low-gluten flour), potato starch dilutes the gluten formation in the batter, raises the crisp factor of the finished shell, and produces a lighter mouthfeel. In Chinese cooking, potato starch (or its close cousin, sweet potato starch) is the standard for velveting (the cornstarch-egg-white coating on stir-fry proteins) and clear glazing on sweet-and-sour dishes, where the low gelatinization temperature and clarity of the gel are preferred over the slightly cloudier cornstarch finish.

  Transformation. Potato starch undergoes a sharp transition from dry powder to swollen gel at the gelatinization temperature: at 60 to 65°C in water, the granules absorb water, swell to 5 to 10 times their dry volume, and the amylose chains hydrogen-bond into a translucent gel network. Above 90°C the network begins to break down and the gel thins; this is the "retrogradation" problem with starch-thickened sauces, where a sauce thickens during simmer and then thins back out under prolonged hold. Cornstarch retrogrades faster than potato starch, which is why potato starch is preferred for sauces and glazes that need to hold thickness through service. In frying, the gel network is partially formed by the surface moisture of the food and then dehydrated by the hot oil before it can break down; the result is a dry, brittle, glass-like shell. The crisp factor is highest at 175 to 185°C oil temperature; below 160°C the starch absorbs oil and goes greasy, above 200°C the surface burns before the shell sets.

  Applications by category. In Korean cooking, potato starch is the coating for yangnyeom chicken, dakgangjeong, twigim (Korean tempura), and a 30 to 50% blend in the batter for haemul pajeon and pajeon; the high crisp factor is the defining textural element. In Japanese cooking, potato starch (katakuriko) is the standard coating for karaage chicken, the structural thickener for ankake glazes (clear thick sauces over rice or noodles), and the binder in dango and mochi-adjacent confections. In Chinese cooking, potato starch is the velveting coat for stir-fry proteins (slightly slicker and clearer than cornstarch), the body of clear glazes on sweet-and-sour pork, and the thickener for guo bao rou and similar dishes. In European baking, potato starch substitutes 1:1 for cornstarch in gluten-free cakes and shortbread (where it produces a more tender, less powdery crumb than wheat-only or cornstarch blends).

  Buying notes. Look for plain potato starch at any Asian grocery (Korean and Japanese markets are the best price, often half the cost of supermarket potato starch), or in the gluten-free baking section of well-stocked supermarkets. The packaging should specify "potato starch" not "potato flour"; the two are different products. Potato flour is whole dried potato ground to a powder; it is yellow-tan, dense, and contains the fiber and protein along with the starch, and behaves entirely differently in cooking (it does not crisp, and it thickens by absorption rather than gelatinization). Potato starch should be bright white, dust-fine, and odorless; any beige or off-white tint or earthy smell indicates contamination with potato flour or stale stock.

  Common Western drift. Cornstarch is often substituted for potato starch in Korean and Japanese fried-coating applications; the result is workable but textually different. Cornstarch produces a denser, more powdery shell with less shatter and shorter crisp retention (cornstarch-coated fried chicken goes soft within 10 minutes; potato-starch-coated chicken holds crisp for 30+ minutes). Tapioca starch is the closer functional substitute (similar large granule size, similar crisp factor) and is the standard in some Korean fried chicken shops as an alternative. The 1:1 substitution rule applies in thickening but not in frying.

storage: 'Pantry, airtight, indefinite. Potato starch is a refined dry powder with no fat content and no perishable elements; properly stored it lasts years without degradation. Watch for moisture absorption (clumping indicates the starch has taken on water and should be sieved or replaced if heavily clumped). Keep away from strong-smelling spices; the starch absorbs odors readily.'
