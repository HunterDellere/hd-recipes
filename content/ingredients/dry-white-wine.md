about: |
  Wine, in chemical terms, is a complex aqueous solution of ethanol (11 to 13 percent in most dry table wines), water, organic acids (tartaric, malic, lactic, succinic, citric), sugars (in a dry wine, less than 4 g/L of residual sugar), volatile aromatic compounds (esters from fermentation, terpenes from the grape, thiols, lactones from oak if used), and trace minerals. For cooking purposes, the operational variables are: acidity (the dominant flavor effect at the heat range of a kitchen, because most ethanol burns off in the first 5 to 10 minutes of a simmer), the volatile aromatic profile that survives the burn-off, and the absence of off-flavors (over-oaked, overly tannic, or oxidized).

  Dry white wine, the cooking standard, is a wine with residual sugar under 4 g/L (the technical "dry" threshold; most are under 2 g/L), and pH between 3.0 and 3.4. The classic cooking white-wine choices: unoaked Italian whites (Pinot Grigio, Soave, Vermentino, Verdicchio, Trebbiano) for general Italian preparations; Sauvignon Blanc (New Zealand, Loire Valley Sancerre or Pouilly-Fume, or California) for French dishes that want brightness; Picpoul de Pinet or other Mediterranean French whites for bouillabaisse and Provencal cooking; dry Riesling (Alsace) for Northern European preparations; Vinho Verde (Portuguese) for Iberian seafood. All are 11 to 13 percent alcohol, all carry pronounced acidity, all carry the volatile esters that flavor the dish even after the alcohol cooks off.

  Functionally, dry white wine plays three roles in cooking: acid (the deglaze role, the same chemistry as vinegar or lemon juice but with secondary aromatic complexity); aromatic vehicle (the esters, terpenes, and thiols are lipid-soluble and partition into fat during cooking, carrying through the dish; this is why a pan sauce or risotto tastes of "wine" even after the alcohol is gone); and tenderizer (the acidity in the marinade chemistry, similar to lemon juice or yogurt, weakening surface proteins on long-marinated meats and fish). The acid role is the most universal: a 60 to 120 ml splash of dry white into a hot pan, followed by 2 to 3 minutes of vigorous reduction, deglazes the fond, evaporates most of the alcohol, and leaves a concentrated, slightly fruity, gently acidic base that lifts whatever protein follows. In bivalve cookery (mussels, clams), the wine is the steaming liquid that the bivalves open into, and the resulting wine-and-bivalve-liquor mixture is the sauce; this is the entire chemistry of moules mariniere, vongole in bianco, and the wine-step of cioppino and bouillabaisse.

  Transformation. Heat the wine and the volatile compounds drive off in a predictable order: ethanol (boiling point 78 °C) leaves first, with most evaporating in the first 5 to 10 minutes of a simmer at 90 to 100 °C; the lighter esters (ethyl acetate, fruity-acetone notes) burn off shortly after; the heavier esters and aromatic acids (succinic acid, butyric esters) hold longer and contribute the "wine" flavor that lingers in the finished dish. The acid (tartaric, malic) is non-volatile and stays in the pan, concentrating as the wine reduces; a 250 ml glass of wine reduced to 50 ml is 5x as acidic by concentration and reads aggressively sharp. The classic deglaze technique is to add the wine to a smoking hot pan (rapid evaporation, fast burn-off) and reduce to syrupy consistency before adding any further liquid, which preserves the aromatic complexity while concentrating the acid; the slow simmer technique used in bivalve steams and braises keeps the wine at 90 to 95 °C below the simmer threshold for longer to extract the bivalve liquor while burning off only the alcohol.

  Applications by category. In French cooking, dry white wine is the deglaze for fish and chicken pan sauces (beurre blanc, sauce vin blanc), the braising liquid for chicken fricassee and pork in white wine, and the canonical pairing for moules mariniere, coq au Riesling (Alsatian), and beurre blanc proper. In Italian cooking, dry white is the deglaze for chicken piccata and chicken Marsala (Marsala is sweet-fortified, a different category), the steaming liquid for vongole and seafood, the simmer in osso buco and risotto Milanese, and the braising liquid for veal and chicken cacciatore. In Spanish cooking, dry white deglazes garlic shrimp (gambas al ajillo) and steam-opens clams in almejas a la marinera; in Portuguese cooking, the equivalent role belongs to Vinho Verde. In North-American Italian cooking, dry white wine is one of two structural acid-aromatics in cioppino (the other being the canned tomato), reducing in the broth before the fennel-onion base sweats. In all categories, the rule is consistent: use a wine you would drink, not one labeled "cooking wine."

  Buying notes. The single most important rule is the only-cook-with-wine-you-would-drink principle. "Cooking wine" (Holland House, Pompeian, the salted bottles in the vinegar aisle) is salted (1 to 1.5 percent sodium chloride added so the wine cannot be sold as beverage, evading liquor tax) and produced from inferior bulk juice; the salt unbalances any dish using more than 60 ml and the inferior wine carries no aromatic complexity. A drinkable wine in the $8 to $14 range is the right price point for cooking: a Trader Joe's or supermarket Pinot Grigio, Sauvignon Blanc, or Vermentino. Avoid heavily oaked Chardonnays for cooking (the oak gets sharp and resinous when reduced); avoid sweet Rieslings, Moscatos, and dessert wines (the residual sugar reads cloying); avoid heavily aromatic Gewurztraminer (the lychee-rose nose dominates the dish). For specific dishes: bouillabaisse and Provencal seafood want Picpoul or Vermentino; cioppino wants Pinot Grigio or Sauvignon Blanc; coq au Riesling wants dry Alsatian Riesling; vongole wants Italian unoaked white. Most home pantries are well-served by a single bottle of dry Pinot Grigio or Sauvignon Blanc kept for cooking; the bottle keeps in the fridge for 4 to 5 days after opening, and any wine past that should be used for vinegar production rather than cooking.

  Western drift. The widespread habit of using "cooking wine" from a non-refrigerated grocery aisle is the single biggest reason home cooks dislike "wine" in their cooked dishes; the salted bulk wine carries off-flavors and adds aggressive saltiness that distorts every dish. The recovery is to switch to a drinkable bottle, and the cooking world opens up. Cooking with a bottle that has been open more than a week is the second most common failure mode; oxidized wine reads dull and slightly bitter when reduced.

storage: 'Unopened: in a cool, dark place (10 to 15 °C ideal, but room temperature acceptable for cooking wine that will be used within a few months), for 1 to 3 years depending on the wine. Once opened: refrigerated, tightly recorked or with a wine-saver vacuum stopper, 3 to 5 days for cooking purposes. Past 5 days the wine starts to oxidize and reads dull; use for vinegar production or discard. Frozen in 30 to 60 ml ice cube blocks: 3 months for cooking applications, never for drinking; the freezing degrades structure but keeps the flavor profile usable for deglazes and braises.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'dry white wine (deglaze, pan sauce)'
    use: 'dry vermouth (extra dry) plus a splash of water'
    note: 'Closest functional substitute: dry vermouth (Noilly Prat, Dolin Dry) is wine-based with similar acidity and a more concentrated aromatic profile (the botanical infusion). Use about 2/3 the volume of vermouth (it is stronger and more concentrated) plus 1/3 water. Vermouth keeps in the fridge for months unlike open wine, so it is the practical pantry default for many cooks.'
  - for: 'dry white wine (bivalve steam, vongole or moules)'
    use: 'dry vermouth plus a squeeze of lemon, or hard cider'
    note: 'Vermouth is the closest sub for the steam role; hard cider (dry, not sweet) is the regional alternative in Normandy and Brittany cooking for moules and works well, contributing a faint apple aromatic. For Korean or Asian-influenced bivalve dishes, dry sake is the regional equivalent.'
  - for: 'dry white wine (cioppino, bouillabaisse, broth-based fish stew)'
    use: 'dry vermouth plus a splash of water'
    note: 'Same logic as the pan-sauce sub; vermouth is the workhorse pantry substitute. Use 2/3 vermouth plus 1/3 water, plus an extra squeeze of lemon at the end to compensate for the slightly lower acid level once reduced.'
  - for: 'dry white wine (alcohol-free cooking)'
    use: 'white grape juice plus white wine vinegar (3 to 1 ratio) or non-alcoholic dry white wine'
    note: 'For alcohol-free cooking: 90 ml white grape juice plus 30 ml white wine vinegar approximates 120 ml of dry white wine in flavor and acid level; the result reads slightly sweeter and benefits from a small reduction in any sugar or honey elsewhere in the dish. Non-alcoholic dry whites (Surely, Giesen 0%) are improving and are a good direct sub for the wine-flavor case; they cost more than the wine they replace.'
