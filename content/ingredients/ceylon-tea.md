about: |
  Ceylon refers to black tea from Sri Lanka, grown across three elevation bands. **High-grown** (above 1200 m, regions like Nuwara Eliya and Dimbula) is the brightest and most floral, with a slight citrus edge. **Mid-grown** (600 to 1200 m) is balanced, full-bodied, and what most "Ceylon black" blends in supermarkets are built on. **Low-grown** (below 600 m, Ruhuna) is the most robust and tannic, exported heavily to the Middle East and Russia for strong sweet tea preparations.

  Compared to Assam, Ceylon is brighter, less malty, and finishes cleaner. It picks up milk well without disappearing into it, which is why it dominates milk-forward formats: Hong Kong-style silk-stocking tea, Thai iced tea, and many "English Breakfast" blends (most of which are an Assam-Ceylon combination).

  CTC (crush-tear-curl) Ceylon produces small pellet leaves that extract fast and full; the orthodox whole-leaf version brews slower and more nuanced. For milk-tea applications CTC is the right choice, speed of extraction matters more than nuance, and the brisk astringency cuts through dairy fat. For straight drinking, whole-leaf orthodox is better.

  Brewing: 5 g per 240 ml at full boil for 4 minutes (orthodox) or 3 minutes (CTC). Longer pulls bitter tannins; shorter under-extracts and yields a thin brew that milk overwhelms.

storage: 'Airtight tin, dark cupboard. 12 months at peak; usable for two years with gradual loss of brightness.'
