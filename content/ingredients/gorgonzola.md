about: |
  Gorgonzola is one of the four canonical European blue cheeses (with Roquefort, Stilton, and Cabrales), produced under DOP protection in Lombardy and Piedmont in northern Italy. The cheese is made from cow's milk (where Roquefort is sheep's and Cabrales is mixed cow-sheep-goat) inoculated with Penicillium roqueforti spores; the mold develops in the interior of the wheel during 2 to 12 months of aging in cool, humid caves, producing the blue-green veining that defines the cheese. The flavor, sharp, salty, mineral, mushroomy, faintly metallic, comes from the mold's enzymatic breakdown of milk fats and proteins into free fatty acids, methyl ketones, and amino acid derivatives.

  The two grades are functionally different ingredients. Gorgonzola dolce ("sweet") is aged 2 to 3 months and is creamy, soft, spreadable, with mild blue-cheese character that reads more rich-and-tangy than sharp. Gorgonzola piccante ("sharp," sometimes labeled "naturale" or "stagionato") is aged 6 to 12 months, firmer, drier, with assertive blue-cheese character that reads sharp, salty, and mineral. Italian recipes specify dolce or piccante by name; substituting one for the other changes the dish significantly. As a rough rule: dolce for melting and spreading, piccante for crumbling and aging-cheese flavor punch.

  Cooking behavior. Dolce melts smoothly into a creamy sauce with very little heat; this is the basis of the classic Italian gorgonzola pasta sauce, where 100 g of dolce melted into 50 g of warm cream and a splash of pasta water becomes the entire sauce. Piccante crumbles cleanly and holds shape under brief heat; this is the form for pizza topping (gorgonzola e noci pizza, gorgonzola e radicchio), salads with shaved cheese, and the tartine format where the cheese is dolloped onto warm toast and softens slightly without fully melting. Both grades pair classically with pear, walnut, balsamic, and honey, but the pairing logic shifts: dolce is the spread on a tartine with sliced pear and walnut on top, piccante is the crumble over a pear-and-walnut salad.

  Pear-gorgonzola pairing. The classic combination across northern Italian cooking. The sweetness and slight acidity of a ripe Bosc or Anjou pear cuts the cheese's salt and richness, the cheese's umami amplifies the pear's fruit notes, and the textural contrast (firm fruit against soft cheese) produces a complete bite. The pairing extends across formats: raw on a cheeseboard, in a salad, melted on a tartine, in a risotto al gorgonzola e pere, in a stuffed-pear preparation. The proportions matter: roughly 1 part cheese to 2 parts pear by weight is the right ratio in most preparations; more cheese overwhelms the fruit, less cheese reads as a pear dish with cheese accents.

  Buying notes. Look for cheese with even blue-green veining throughout (not concentrated in one part of the wedge), a clean white-to-pale-yellow paste between the veins (no brown or pink discoloration), and a firm rind (for piccante) or no visible rind (for dolce, which is often sold rind-removed). The wedge should smell distinctly of blue cheese (mushroom, mineral, slight ammonia) but should not smell aggressively ammoniac, pronounced ammonia means the cheese is past prime. Italian-imported gorgonzola at delis and high-end groceries is the standard; domestic American "gorgonzola" is usually a less complex mold-blue cheese that approximates but does not replicate the original. The label should say "Gorgonzola DOP" if Italian.

  Cross-cuisine reach. Italian risotto al gorgonzola, gnocchi al gorgonzola, gorgonzola e pere pasta, gorgonzola e noci pizza. French gorgonzola-and-walnut salad, where the cheese serves as the Italian alternative to Roquefort in the same role. American steakhouse blue-cheese-crumble-on-burger and wedge salad use gorgonzola and Roquefort interchangeably. The cheese travels well across northern European and American cooking; less common in Asian, Latin American, or Middle Eastern traditions.

storage: |
  Wrapped in parchment or wax paper (not plastic, which traps moisture and accelerates over-ripening), then loosely covered with foil, in the cheese drawer of the refrigerator: 2 to 3 weeks for piccante, 1 to 2 weeks for dolce. Once the cheese starts to ammoniate strongly, it is past prime; mild ammonia is normal and dissipates with 30 minutes of room-temperature exposure before serving. Mold growth on the cut faces (white or blue) is normal for blue cheese and can be scraped off; only colored mold (orange, pink, black) means the cheese has gone bad and should be discarded. Bring to room temperature 30 to 45 minutes before serving for the full flavor.
