about: |
  Hoisin (haixian, "sea-fresh" in Cantonese, despite containing no seafood) is a thick, glossy sauce of fermented soybean paste, sugar, vinegar, garlic, sesame, chili, and five-spice. The texture is the viscosity of ketchup; the flavor is sweet-front with a deep fermented-bean umami, a touch of vinegary brightness, and the distinct anise-and-cinnamon top of five-spice.

  The Cantonese application is glazes and dips: brushed on char siu pork during the last 5 minutes of roasting, served as a dip for siu yuk (crispy roast pork), spread on the pancake under Peking duck. The Vietnamese application, distinctively Southern, is the pho garnish: thinned with a splash of broth and a squeeze of sriracha into a personal dipping sauce for the boiled brisket and tendon. In Hanoi (Northern Vietnam) hoisin is not served with pho at all; the broth is meant to speak alone.

  Functionally three ingredients in one bottle: salt, sweet, and umami, with five-spice as the aromatic signature. The sugar content makes it caramelize and burn fast over direct heat; brush onto roasting meats only in the final stretch.

  Brand standard: Lee Kum Kee (the red-label bottle) is the global default, Koon Chun is denser and slightly more fermented-forward, Wing Fai is a Hong Kong premium option. Avoid generic supermarket "hoisin" labeled as such but actually a sweetened plum-sauce facsimile; the fermented-bean depth is what makes hoisin work.

storage: 'Pantry indefinitely unopened. After opening, refrigerate; lasts a year. The high sugar and salt content prevents spoilage but the aromatic top notes fade after 6 months.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'hoisin sauce (cooking)'
    use: 'sweet bean paste + sugar + rice vinegar + a pinch of five-spice'
    note: 'Approximates the texture and flavor. Real hoisin has a depth from long fermentation that quick blends do not capture; acceptable but noticeable.'
  - for: 'hoisin sauce (for pho dipping)'
    use: 'oyster sauce + a pinch of five-spice + ½ tsp sugar'
    note: 'For Southern Vietnamese pho dipping use only. Lacks the sweet-bean note but covers the umami; acceptable.'
