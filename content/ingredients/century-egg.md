about: |
  Century egg is a raw duck egg (most commonly; chicken and quail also exist) preserved by alkaline cure rather than by salt brine or smoke. The traditional cure is a paste of slaked lime, wood ash, sodium carbonate or potassium carbonate, salt, rice husks, and sometimes black tea, packed around the shell and held at room temperature for six weeks to four months. Modern industrial production substitutes a sodium hydroxide solution and reduces the cure time. Either way, hydroxide ions migrate through the porous shell, raise the egg's interior pH from neutral to roughly twelve, and drive a series of irreversible transformations the cook would not get from heat alone. The names "thousand-year egg" or "hundred-year egg" or "millennium egg" are evocative marketing; the actual cure runs weeks to months.

  What the cure does: at pH twelve, egg-white proteins (mainly ovalbumin, ovotransferrin, and ovomucoid) denature and coagulate not into the rubbery white cooks know from a hard-boiled egg but into a translucent amber-to-black gel, the color a product of slow Maillard-like and Strecker degradation reactions on the white's amino acids producing melanoidin pigments. The yolk transforms more dramatically. The outer ring sets to a dark grey-green from iron-sulfide and porphyrin compounds, the middle is dark olive-green and fudgy, and the central core stays creamy and almost lava-textured. Sulfur-containing amino acids (cysteine, methionine) liberate hydrogen sulfide and ammonia, producing the dish's signature savory-pungent aroma; tannins from tea or rice husks moderate that pungency in higher-quality versions. The whites and yolks read as if they had been somehow custarded and cured at once, which is functionally what has happened.

  Functional role: pidan is a savory, glutamate-rich protein source used in cold appetizers and as a savory accent in cooked dishes. The two canonical applications are Cantonese pork-and-century-egg congee, where wedges of pidan steeped briefly in the hot rice porridge bloom their aroma into the broth and provide a contrasting jammy-creamy texture against the rice; and the cold appetizer of silken tofu draped with pidan, scallion, soy, and sesame oil, where the bland tofu carries the pidan's pungency to a wider audience than the egg alone could. Pidan also appears chopped into stir-fried greens (especially with chrysanthemum greens or amaranth), in fillings for cold dumplings, and as a savory topping over plain rice or porridge. Across all of these applications the egg is functionally cooked already; cooks heat it briefly to bloom aroma and warm the yolk, never to "set" it.

  Buying notes: better pidan reads cleaner, with pine-resin or earthy notes carrying the sulfur rather than ammonia dominating. The traditional cure produces a faint snowflake-shaped pattern of crystallized amino acids inside the white, sometimes called a "pine-needle" pattern (松花, songhua), and pidan with this visible crystalline structure (held up to light or seen in cross-section) signals a long, careful cure. Modern industrial pidan tends to be smoother and more uniformly black, slightly less complex in flavor. Lead-cured pidan was an older industrial practice now banned across major producing regions; reputable brands explicitly advertise lead-free (无铅, wuqian) on the box. Eggs are usually sold individually wrapped in plastic film or in cardboard six-packs at Chinese groceries; the shell may be cleaned smooth or still coated in a thin grey paste residue, which is harmless and washes off.

  Common Western drift to be aware of: the pidan is sometimes substituted with a hard-boiled egg in adapted recipes, or omitted entirely from congee in restaurants serving Western audiences. Neither swap preserves what the dish is built around. The pidan's sulfurous-savory aroma and the textural contrast of the green-jammy yolk against the creamy white rice are the entire reason the dish exists; without it, the congee is plain rice porridge with pork. Cooks meeting pidan for the first time often want to cook it harder, expecting it to "finish"; the egg is already as cooked as it gets, and additional heat only flattens the aroma.

storage: 'Unopened pidan: room-temperature pantry, six months from production date, since the alkaline cure has already neutralized any pathogenic risk. Once shelled, refrigerate, and eat within forty-eight hours; the gel surface oxidizes and the yolk dries into a chalky outer crust beyond that window. Failure mode is sharp ammonia smell that overwhelms the savory backbone, or visible mold on a cracked shell; either signals discard. Properly cured pidan smells assertive but pleasantly savory at the right side of fermented, not aggressively chemical.'
