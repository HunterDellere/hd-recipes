about: |
  Banh trang is a thin sheet of rice flour, tapioca starch, salt, and water, steamed in a single layer over a cloth-covered cauldron and dried on a bamboo mat (the diamond-pattern imprint on the finished sheet is the mat's signature). The result is a brittle, translucent disc, 16 to 22 cm in diameter, that softens to pliable, sticky, slightly chewy wrap in seconds when dipped in warm water.

  Two main applications. For goi cuon (fresh rolls), each sheet is dipped briefly in warm water (about 40°C / 105°F for 4 to 6 seconds), laid on a damp work surface, filled with shrimp, pork, rice noodles, and herbs, and rolled tight. For cha gio (fried rolls), the sheets are dipped slightly longer (8 to 10 seconds), filled with ground pork and vegetables, rolled, and deep-fried until shatteringly crisp; the tapioca content puffs and crackles in hot oil.

  The defining rookie mistake is over-dipping. Banh trang continues to hydrate after it leaves the water, drawing moisture from the filling and the work surface; a sheet dipped for 10 seconds will be limp, soggy, and tear in seconds. Pull the sheet from the water while it still feels stiff at the edges; by the time you have laid out the filling and started rolling, the sheet is fully pliable. If the sheet is already floppy when it leaves the water, the roll will tear.

  Common ratios: pure rice flour banh trang is more brittle and roll-friendly; rice-and-tapioca blend banh trang is more pliable and shrimp-roll friendly. Vietnamese-made (Three Ladies brand, Bamboo Tree brand) is the standard; Thai-made rice paper is also acceptable. Cassava-only sheets sold as "spring roll wrappers" are a different product entirely (different texture, different rehydration behavior); not interchangeable.

storage: 'Pantry indefinitely in the original plastic sleeve. Once opened, reseal tightly and store in a dry cupboard; humidity makes the sheets stick together into a brick.'
