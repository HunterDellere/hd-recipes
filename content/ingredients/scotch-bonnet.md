about: |
  Scotch bonnet is Capsicum chinense, the same species as habanero, but a distinct cultivar with a wider squat lantern shape and a fruitier aroma. Heat level is intense (100,000 to 350,000 SHU, on par with habanero and ten times hotter than jalapeño). The flavor profile is the differentiator from habanero: scotch bonnet reads tropical-fruity (mango, apricot, tomato), habanero reads more citrus-floral. In Jamaican and Haitian cooking the bonnet is non-substitutable; habanero is the closest interchangeable chili but the dish reads slightly different.

  The capsaicinoid that drives the heat is concentrated in the placental tissue (the white pith inside, not the seeds as folk wisdom suggests, though seeds get coated in capsaicin during cutting). Removing the pith and seeds reduces the heat by roughly 75% while preserving most of the fruity flavor; this is the standard technique for cooks who want the chili character without the full burn.

  Functional category: fruity heat. Used whole-and-pierced in stews (where it perfumes the broth without releasing seeds), minced raw in pikliz (Haitian pickled cabbage relish, where the heat infuses the vinegar), pounded into wet pastes (Jamaican jerk paste, where it carries the heat through the marinade), and chopped into sauces (West African pepper sauces, where the heat is the point). Treat the cut chili with respect: capsaicin sticks to skin oils and migrates to eyes hours after handling. Wear nitrile gloves when seeding or cutting more than one bonnet.

  Applications by category. Caribbean: Jamaican jerk paste (canonical), Jamaican rice and peas (whole and pierced, removed before serving), Haitian pikliz, Haitian sauce ti malice, Trinidadian pepper sauce. West African: Nigerian pepper sauces, Senegalese yassa marinade variants. South American: Bahian moqueca occasionally uses scotch bonnet though malagueta is more traditional.

storage: |
  Counter at room temperature for 5 to 7 days; refrigerated in the crisper drawer for 2 to 3 weeks. Freeze whole (unpierced) in zip-top bags for 6 months; thawed pods are softer but retain heat and flavor for cooked applications. Failure modes: soft spots through the skin (rotting; cut away or discard), darkening past wine-red toward brown (over-ripe and starting to ferment, still usable but check for off smells), white fuzzy mold (discard).
