about: |
  Pigeon peas are the seeds of Cajanus cajan, a tropical legume distinct from the common bean (Phaseolus vulgaris). The dried seed is small (about 4 mm), mottled beige with brown speckles; cooked, they soften to a creamy-yellow interior with a nutty, slightly earthy flavor closer to split peas than to black beans. Composition is typical for a legume: 9% water, 21% protein, 63% carbohydrate, 1.5% fat.

  Pigeon peas are the structural legume of much of the Caribbean rice-and-bean tradition. Puerto Rican arroz con gandules, Jamaican rice and peas (where peas refers specifically to red kidney beans or pigeon peas depending on region), Trinidadian pelau, and Dominican moro de guandules all build the dish on this specific bean. Substituting black beans or kidney beans changes the flavor character recognizably.

  Commercial forms. Canned (Goya is the dominant US brand; Iberia, La Cena are alternatives) is the daily kitchen reality for most Caribbean home cooks; pre-cooked, ready to add to rice in the last 20 minutes of cooking. Dried: requires 6 to 8 hours of soaking and 90 to 120 minutes of simmering, produces meaningfully better texture and broth. Frozen: par-cooked, the middle ground, available at some Caribbean and Latin American groceries.

  Functional category: structural rice-pairing legume. The bean cooks alongside rice in the same pot, taking on the sofrito and tomato flavors of the rice base while contributing its own nutty depth and starch to the final dish. The texture is firmer than a kidney bean and softer than a chickpea; the bean holds its shape through the rice cook without breaking down.

storage: |
  Canned (unopened): pantry, by the printed date (typically 3 years). Once opened, drain and transfer to a sealed container, refrigerated 4 days. Dried: cool dry pantry, 18 months at peak. Cooked from dried: refrigerated 5 days, frozen 6 months. Failure modes: canned with a swollen or dented can (discard), dried with white powdery dust or insect activity (discard).
