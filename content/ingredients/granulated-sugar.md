about: |
  Granulated sugar is ~99.9% sucrose: a disaccharide of glucose and fructose. In baking it's not a flavor ingredient first; it's a functional one. Pull it out of a cookie recipe and the cookie collapses structurally before it tastes wrong.

  Creaming is mechanical. When you beat butter and sugar, the sharp edges of the crystals tear holes in the fat, and air gets trapped in those holes. That's the aeration that lifts a cake or cookie. Smaller crystals (caster / superfine) cream faster and finer: better for tender cakes. Larger crystals leave bigger air cells. Powdered sugar can't cream at all; it has no edges.

  Sucrose is hygroscopic: it binds water. In a baked good, that bound water can't evaporate as easily, which is why a cookie made with more sugar stays softer longer. It also raises the boiling point and slows starch gelatinization, giving a wider window before the structure sets. That's part of why low-sugar baked goods often turn out tough or dry.

  Browning is two reactions. Maillard is sugar plus protein (amino acids) above ~150°C: the brown crust on a cookie edge, the top of a cake. Caramelization is sugar alone, no protein, above ~160°C: the amber on a flan, the bottom of a tarte Tatin. Granulated sucrose does both, depending on what's around.

storage: 'Airtight container at room temperature, dry. Doesn''t spoil, but it pulls humidity and clumps into rocks if the seal is loose. A tight lid is the whole game. Break clumps with a fork; they''re fine to use.'
