about: |
  Basil (Ocimum basilicum) is a genus of aromatic annual herbs with a chemistry built around volatile essential oils. The dominant compounds are linalool (citrusy-floral, 30 to 50 percent of the oil in most cultivars), eugenol (clove-like, 5 to 15 percent), methyl chavicol or estragole (anise-like, dominant in Thai basil and tarragon), and 1,8-cineole (eucalyptus-like, minor). The exact ratio defines cultivar character: Genovese basil leans linalool with moderate eugenol (the floral-clove balance of Italian pesto); Thai basil leans methyl chavicol (the anise-licorice cut that anchors Thai green curry); holy basil (kraprao, used in Thai stir-fries) is even more clove-eugenol-forward; lemon basil and lime basil push citral toward the citrus end.

  Heat fragility decides almost every application. The volatile oils that define basil's character evaporate fast in hot fat or hot water; sustained heat above 60 °C drives off the linalool fraction within 30 seconds, and most of the eugenol within 2 minutes. Basil cooked into a long-simmered tomato sauce contributes nothing perceptible at the finish; the chemistry is gone. The functional rule: basil goes in at the end. In an Italian tomato sauce, torn fresh basil is folded in at the table or in the last 30 seconds. In Thai green curry, the basil is added in the final 10 seconds of stir-frying or off heat. Pesto works because the cure is cold: olive oil and salt extract the volatiles into a stable suspension without ever heating the leaves; the pasta is cooked separately, and the pesto meets it at 60 to 70 °C briefly, which preserves most of the chemistry.

  Oxidation is the second failure mode. Basil leaves cut with a sharp knife oxidize visibly within minutes; the cut surface turns black-brown as polyphenol oxidase enzymes meet the air. Tearing the leaves by hand limits the cell damage to a smaller area and slows the oxidation; this is why traditional Italian and Provencal cooks tear basil rather than chop it. For pesto, the cure happens fast enough (and the oil seals the leaves quickly enough) that knife chopping is fine. For a finishing application where the leaves stay visible (caprese, niçoise, panzanella), tear and add at the last second.

  Cultivars by cuisine. Genovese (Italian) is the standard Mediterranean basil: large light-green leaves, sweet-floral, balanced linalool and eugenol. Italian large leaf or Italian classico are mass-market versions; Pesto Perpetuo and Genovese Compatto are home-garden cultivars closest to the Pra (the Ligurian region around Genoa with PDO designation for Pesto Genovese basil). Thai basil (also called horapha, anise basil, or sweet Thai basil): narrow purple-stemmed leaves, intense anise-licorice flavor, used in green curry, pho, and Vietnamese rolls. Holy basil (kraprao or Thai holy basil): fuzzy, smaller, pungent clove-pepper flavor, the only correct choice for pad kraprao. Lemon basil: citrusy, used in Indonesian and Lao soups. They are not interchangeable: Thai basil in pesto produces an off-flavored anise-floral paste that does not read as Italian; Genovese in pad kraprao reads sweet-floral rather than punchy-savory.

  Buying. Fresh-bunch basil from a farmers' market or specialty store is dramatically better than the plastic clamshell at a supermarket; the bunch is picked closer to use and the volatile profile is intact. Look for vibrant green (or purple-stemmed for Thai and holy), no yellowing, no wilting, no black spots on cut stems. Genovese basil is widely available; Thai and holy basil at Asian groceries (often labeled as "horapha" and "kraprao" respectively). Hydroponic basil sold in plastic boxes is a tier above plastic clamshells but a tier below fresh-bunch from a market. Dried basil is functionally a different ingredient; the aromatics are mostly gone. Use dried only in long-cooked applications where the basil is meant to read as background herb (a spaghetti meatball sauce), not where it should carry flavor.

storage: 'The cut-stem-in-water method works best: trim 1 cm from the stem ends, place the bunch upright in a jar with 2 cm of water at the base, cover loosely with a plastic bag, leave on the counter (not in the fridge: fridge cold blackens basil within 24 hours). Lasts 3 to 5 days at peak quality, gradually weakens through 7 days. Failure modes: blackening leaves at the edges (cold damage or oxidation, pull off affected leaves and use the rest immediately); wilting / limp (water loss, sometimes recoverable with a 10-minute cold-water soak); fermented or rotten smell (discard). Freezing basil leaves whole produces black mush; freeze instead as pesto (the oil-and-salt cure stabilizes) or as pureed leaves blended with olive oil into ice cubes.'
