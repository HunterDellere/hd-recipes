about: |
  Cornstarch is pure starch extracted from the endosperm of corn (maize) kernels by wet-milling: the kernels are steeped in warm sulfurous water to soften, then ground and centrifuged to separate the starch from the protein (which becomes corn gluten meal), the germ (corn oil), and the bran. The resulting starch is washed, dried, and milled into a fine white powder. Cornstarch is roughly 73% amylopectin (the branched-chain starch) and 27% amylose (the linear-chain starch); the high amylopectin fraction is what produces the glossy, translucent gel characteristic of cornstarch-thickened sauces, distinct from the more opaque, looser gel of high-amylose starches like wheat flour or tapioca.

  Gelatinization chemistry. Cornstarch granules are insoluble in cold water but begin to absorb water and swell at around 62 to 70°C / 144 to 158°F. As temperature rises through the gelatinization range, the granules swell to many times their original size, the amylose leaches out into the surrounding liquid, and the slurry transitions from cloudy suspension to translucent gel. The thickening peak occurs at full gelatinization (around 95°C / 200°F for cornstarch); cooking past this point briefly is necessary to dissolve any remaining raw-starch flavor (the chalky-mouth-feel of under-cooked starch), but extended cooking at high temperature breaks the gel structure and thins the sauce (a phenomenon called "starch retrogradation breakdown"). The practical rule: bring a cornstarch-thickened sauce just to a hard simmer for 30 to 60 seconds, then pull from heat. Cornstarch is also acid-sensitive; sauces with strong acid (vinegar, citrus, tomato) can break the gel during long cooking. Add the cornstarch slurry late in cooking when working with acidic liquids.

  The slurry vs the dry dredge. Cornstarch is used in two structurally different ways depending on the application. For sauce thickening (Chinese stir-fries, Western gravies, fruit pie fillings), the cornstarch is dissolved in cold water at roughly 1:2 cornstarch-to-water by volume to make a slurry, then whisked into a hot liquid; the slurry prevents the granules from clumping into glue-pockets that would otherwise form if dry cornstarch were added directly to hot liquid. For frying applications (Chinese velvet coating, Korean fried chicken, Taiwanese popcorn chicken), the cornstarch is used as a dry dredge or as a wet batter; on contact with hot oil, the surface starch instantly gelatinizes and then dehydrates into a thin, crackly, characteristically Chinese-restaurant fried crust. The crust is thinner and crispier than a wheat-flour crust because the high amylopectin content means less structural amylose to absorb oil, producing the famous shatter-crisp texture.

  The velveting marinade. The Cantonese "velveting" technique uses cornstarch (often combined with egg white, soy sauce, and Shaoxing wine) as a marinade coating on raw protein (sliced chicken, beef, shrimp, fish) for 15 to 30 minutes before stir-frying. The cornstarch forms a thin protective layer around the protein surface; in the hot wok, this layer gelatinizes before the protein interior reaches its denaturation temperature, sealing in moisture and producing the characteristic silky, slippery texture of Chinese-restaurant stir-fried protein. The same technique, scaled up to a thicker batter, produces the crackly crust of sweet-and-sour pork and General Tso's chicken. The cornstarch is doing double duty: surface protection during cooking, and starchy gloss in the final sauce coating.

  Cooking applications by category. **Chinese (all regions)**: stir-fry sauce thickening (every wok dish at every Chinese restaurant uses cornstarch slurry at the finish), velvet marinades, breaded fried foods (sweet-and-sour pork, salt-and-pepper shrimp, kung pao chicken), congee texture adjustment. **American (Southern, broader)**: gravy thickening (chicken-fried steak gravy, biscuits and gravy), fruit pie filling thickening (apple, cherry, blueberry), pudding and pastry-cream thickening, fried chicken coating component. **Korean**: yangnyeom chicken batter (the double-fried sweet-and-spicy fried chicken), japchae glass noodle thickening, tteokbokki sauce body. **Japanese (limited)**: katakuriko (potato starch) is preferred in Japanese cuisine over cornstarch, but cornstarch substitutes 1:1 in most home-cooking applications. **Filipino, Vietnamese, Thai**: various stir-fry thickeners and fried-coating applications mirroring Chinese practice. **European baking (limited)**: shortening agent in sugar cookies, lemon curd thickener, custard powder base.

  Cornstarch vs other starches. Cornstarch (high amylopectin) produces a glossy translucent gel and is the default for stir-fry sauces and Chinese frying. Potato starch (katakuriko, even higher amylopectin) produces an even more glossy and slippery gel, and is the Japanese preference; substitutes 1:1 for cornstarch and is generally interchangeable. Tapioca starch (high amylopectin, slightly different molecular weight) produces a stretchier gel and is used in boba, mochi, and some Southeast Asian desserts; for sauce thickening, substitutes 1:1 for cornstarch with a slightly different mouthfeel. Arrowroot (high amylopectin, very acid-tolerant) is the right choice for acidic sauces and clear glazes where cornstarch might break; substitutes 1:1. Wheat flour (mostly amylose, with gluten protein) produces an opaque, looser, more starchy-tasting gel and requires roughly 2x the weight to match cornstarch's thickening power.

  Buying notes. Cornstarch is a universal supermarket staple (Argo, Clabber Girl, store brands) in the baking aisle; quality is essentially uniform across brands. Asian groceries sometimes carry imported Chinese cornstarch in larger bags at lower per-gram cost; the product is identical. Non-GMO and organic cornstarch (Bob's Red Mill) is available for cooks who prefer it. Modified food starch (sometimes labeled "modified cornstarch") is cornstarch chemically treated for enhanced freeze-thaw stability or acid tolerance; useful for canning and frozen pie fillings but unnecessary for everyday cooking.

storage: |
  Cornstarch is extremely shelf-stable. Unopened in original packaging at room temperature in a dry pantry: indefinite (the printed best-by is conservative; cornstarch a decade past its date is generally usable if it has stayed dry). Once opened, transfer to an airtight container with a tight seal to prevent humidity absorption and pantry-pest entry. Failure modes are clumping or hardening (humidity exposure; the cornstarch is still usable if broken up but may need to be re-sifted), and visible mold or off smell (water contamination; discard).
