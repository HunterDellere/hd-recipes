about: |
  Red onions are Allium cepa cultivars selected for the anthocyanin pigments (cyanidin glycosides) that color the outer skin and the inner ring tissue red to deep purple. Raw, they are milder and noticeably sweeter than yellow onions, with a higher residual sugar fraction (~7% by weight in storage onions, vs ~5% in standard yellow) and lower allyl-propyl-disulfide pungency at the same maturity. The combination is why red onion is the canonical onion for raw applications across the world: kachumber and kachumbari, fattoush and tabbouleh, ceviche and aguachile, pico de gallo and salsa fresca, Greek salad, sumac onion as kebab garnish, the British pickled onion in pub sandwiches.

  The red color depends on cell pH. Anthocyanins are color-shifting molecules ; red below pH 7, blue-violet at neutral pH, yellow-green in alkaline conditions. Raw red onion in a vinegar or lime dressing keeps and slightly intensifies the red; the same onion sautéed in a pan lacking acid turns brown like any caramelized onion within minutes. This is why "pickled red onions" stay vivid pink-red while "sautéed red onions" produce no visible color difference vs yellow ; the application is built around either keeping the cells intact and acidic or accepting that the color goes once heat hits.

  Cell rupture is the flavor switch. The pungency-and-tear chemistry of any onion runs through alliinase, the enzyme that converts s-alkyl cysteine sulfoxides (intact, odorless) into propanethial S-oxide (the lacrimator) and downstream allyl propyl disulfide (the pungent volatile) when cells are cut. Sharper-cut onions release more; a duller knife crushes more cells and produces more pungency. Slicing pole-to-pole (along the grain, vertical cuts from root to tip) cuts fewer cells than slicing equator-to-equator (across the rings); pole-to-pole-cut red onion in a kachumber or salsa reads cleaner and less aggressive than equator-cut. Diced onion is hardest to control; aim for a sharp knife and quick cuts.

  Cold-water soak as a moderator. Aggressive raw onion (older storage onions, or warm-storage onions where allyl-propyl-disulfide has accumulated) can be moderated by a 5- to 10-minute soak in cold water after cutting. The water-soluble sulfur compounds leach out and the lacrimator content drops by roughly 50 to 70%, but the textural crunch is preserved. Over-soaking past 15 minutes leaches sugar and texture as well, leaving washed-out onion. The soak is for the recipe-specific case where the onion is too hot, not for every recipe.

  Where red onion is the right choice. Raw applications where the color matters and the eat is a single chew: salsas, kachumber, fattoush, ceviche, Greek salad, tacos, sliced onion on burgers and sandwiches. Quick pickles where the bright pink is the visual signature. Avoid for slow caramelization (no color advantage; standard yellow caramelizes more reliably), for soup/stock bases (browning destroys the color, and yellow has more bulk), and for any deeply browned application where the higher residual sugar burns before structural caramelization completes.

  Buying notes. Look for red onions that are heavy for their size with dry, papery skin and no visible green sprout from the top or soft brown spots near the root plate. Sprouting onions are usable but the green shoot has drawn sugar and moisture from the bulb, leaving a pithy, less-sweet onion. The "Red Burgundy" and "Red Bull" cultivars sold at most North American supermarkets are mid-pungency storage varieties; sweet red onions (Texas Sweet, Vidalia-derived reds) are seasonal and noticeably milder, suitable for raw eating with no soak.

storage: |
  Whole, unpeeled, cool dark pantry: 4 to 8 weeks. Red onions store slightly less well than yellow because the anthocyanin-rich tissue is more prone to microbial spoilage at the root plate; check weekly for soft spots and remove. Refrigeration is unnecessary for whole onions and shortens shelf life. Cut onion: refrigerated in a sealed container, 4 to 5 days at peak; usable but increasingly sulfurous past day 5. The off-cue is a soft, moist outer layer that smells acridly sulfurous (vs the clean sharp note of a fresh onion); discard.
