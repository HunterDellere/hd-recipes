about: |
  Pork shoulder is the upper foreleg of the pig and divides into two retail cuts: Boston butt (the upper, blockier portion with the shoulder blade running through it) and picnic (the lower, leg-end portion with the foreleg bone and a tougher external skin). Both are interchangeable for slow-cooking applications though Boston butt is the more commonly stocked retail cut in North America. Composition is roughly 60% water, 18% protein, 22% fat (the fat distributed as intramuscular marbling rather than as a single cap layer), with significant collagen content from the connective tissue and the working-muscle fascia.

  Functional category: structural braising and slow-cooking cut. The high collagen content is what makes pork shoulder uniquely suited to long, slow, moist cooking; the collagen converts to gelatin over 4 to 6 hours of cooking at temperatures between 80 and 95°C / 175 to 205°F, producing the characteristic silky tender texture of pulled pork, carnitas, and slow-braised pork dishes. Cooked fast or at temperatures above the boiling point, the collagen does not have time to convert and the meat reads tough and stringy; cooked too low (below 75°C / 165°F), the collagen never starts to break down.

  The fat is structural. Pork shoulder is one of the few common cuts where the fat-to-lean ratio is high enough that the fat self-bastes the meat through the long cook, keeps the meat moist when shredded, and contributes flavor as it renders. Trimming the fat down before cooking dries out the finished dish; leave the external fat cap and any visible marbling in place, and let the cook render what needs to render.

  Applications by category. Mexican: carnitas (confit-braised in its own fat with citrus), cochinita pibil (banana-leaf braised with achiote and sour orange). North American: pulled pork (slow-smoked or oven-roasted, shredded). Cuban: lechón asado (whole roasted pork shoulder with mojo marinade), ropa vieja variants (though ropa vieja is more typically beef). Italian: porchetta (rolled with herbs and roasted). Chinese: char siu (marinated and roasted in a hot oven), red-cooked pork (hong shao rou, simmered in soy and sugar). Filipino: lechon kawali (boiled then deep-fried for crackling skin). The cut travels everywhere slow cooking and braising are central to the cuisine.

  Sizes. Whole bone-in Boston butt weighs 2.5 to 4.5 kg / 5 to 10 lb; whole picnic shoulder weighs 3 to 5 kg / 7 to 12 lb. Most home cooks buy bone-in shoulder in the 3 to 4 kg range; the bone contributes flavor and gelatin to the cooking liquid and is easy to remove after cooking. Boneless shoulder is also widely available and is convenient when scaling down (cut to size as needed) though produces a slightly less flavorful broth.

storage: |
  Refrigerated, in original packaging, by the sell-by date (typically 3 to 5 days from packaging). Once opened, use within 2 days. Frozen, vacuum-sealed: 6 months at peak; usable for 12 months with mild texture degradation. Cooked: refrigerated in cooking liquid, 5 days; frozen in cooking liquid, 6 months. Failure modes: gray-green discoloration on the surface (oxidation, edible after trimming but flavor is degraded), slimy texture (bacterial overgrowth, discard), off smell (sulfur or sour, discard).

substitutions:

    use: 'pork shoulder (picnic) or pork country-style ribs'
    note: 'Both cuts come from the same general area of the pig and behave identically in slow cooking. Picnic has a tougher skin; country-style ribs are pre-cut into smaller pieces and cook somewhat faster. Use the same weight.'
  - for: 'pork shoulder'
    use: 'pork belly (for richer-fatter applications) or beef chuck (for non-pork applications)'
    note: 'Pork belly is much fatter and produces an oilier final dish; use it when the application is structurally fatty (carnitas variants, banh mi fillings). Beef chuck has comparable collagen content and behaves similarly in braises though the flavor profile is entirely different; use it for non-pork dietary substitution.'
