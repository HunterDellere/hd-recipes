about: |
  Chemically, rock sugar is just sucrose — the same molecule as granulated white sugar. The difference is crystal size and purity. Rock sugar is grown by slow recrystallization from a saturated syrup over days, producing chunks that can be fingertip-sized to fist-sized. The slow crystal growth excludes most of the molasses impurities, which is why the clear variety is purer than refined white sugar.

  In braises like hong shao rou (red-cooked pork), the slow dissolution matters. Granulated sugar dumps into solution immediately and caramelizes as a unit; rock sugar releases sucrose gradually as the braise reduces, so the final glaze sets at a consistent thickness without scorching. That gradual release is also why it's the canonical sweetener in tea-egg brines and pear soups: the sugar concentration tracks the reduction rather than spiking.

  Two varieties show up at Chinese groceries: clear (单晶冰糖) and amber/yellow (老冰糖). Clear is purer and gives a glassier finish. Amber is less refined, carries trace molasses, and is preferred for medicinal soups and for braises where you want a slightly deeper color. Either works in cooking; pick by what you can find.

storage: 'Airtight, dry pantry. Indefinite; sucrose does not spoil. The chunks can fuse into a single mass in humid kitchens; break apart with the back of a heavy knife.'
