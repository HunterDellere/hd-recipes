about: |
  Hominy is dried maize kernels treated with an alkaline solution (calcium hydroxide, called cal or pickling lime, in the Mexican tradition; lye in the US Southern tradition). The alkaline soak dissolves the pericarp (the tough outer seed coat), gelatinizes the surface starches, releases bound niacin into a bioavailable form (which is why corn cuisines built on nixtamalization avoid pellagra while those that did not historically suffered from it), and transforms the flavor of the kernel through Maillard chemistry on the bound amino acids.

  The treated kernel is called nixtamal in the Mesoamerican tradition. Ground wet, it becomes masa (the dough for tortillas, tamales, sopes). Left whole, dried, and rehydrated, it becomes hominy as sold commercially. The kernels swell to roughly twice the dry-corn size, soften through, and develop a chewy-tender texture distinct from cornmeal porridge or corn-on-the-cob.

  Commercial hominy comes three ways in North America. Canned (Juanita's, Bush's, Goya): pre-cooked, ready to use, soft and slightly mushy; the standard for quick pozole and Southern hominy dishes. Dried (Rancho Gordo, some Mexican groceries): requires 4 to 6 hours of simmering to soften through, produces a meaningfully better texture and broth, the traditional preparation for restaurant-quality pozole. Frozen (some Mexican groceries, especially in the Southwestern US): par-cooked, the middle ground between canned convenience and dried quality.

  Functional category: structural grain. Hominy is the corn-as-grain (rather than corn-as-flour) form of the Mesoamerican corn tradition; the whole kernel carries texture and substance that masa-based dishes cannot. It is the structural grain of pozole (Mexican stew with hominy and pork or chicken), menudo (tripe stew with hominy), Southern grits when ground (though most modern grits are made from non-nixtamalized corn), and posole verde (Pueblo Native American hominy stew).

  The chewy texture and faint slaked-lime flavor of hominy is unique to the nixtamalization process; non-nixtamalized whole corn kernels do not produce the same dish. Substituting plain canned corn for hominy in pozole produces a dish that is recognizably wrong; the kernels are softer, sweeter, and lack the structural lime-treated character.

storage: |
  Canned (unopened): pantry, by the printed date (typically 3 years). Once opened, drain and transfer to a sealed container with fresh water, refrigerated 5 days. Dried hominy: cool dry pantry, sealed glass jar, 18 months at peak. Cooked from dried, drained and cooled: refrigerated 5 days, frozen 6 months. Failure modes: canned hominy with a swollen or dented can (botulism risk, discard); dried hominy with insect activity or musty smell (discard).
