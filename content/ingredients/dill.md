about: |
  Dill is an annual umbellifer, related to fennel, anise, caraway, and parsley. The feathery green fronds carry a bright grassy aroma anchored by carvone (the same molecule that dominates caraway, at a different concentration and ratio) and limonene, producing the characteristic dill profile that reads as slightly anise-warm, slightly citrus, and unmistakably grassy. The herb does not handle long heat well; its volatile aromatics dissipate within minutes of going into a hot pan.

  Functionally dill is a finishing herb in most preparations and a primary herb in many. In Polish, Russian, Ukrainian, and Baltic cooking, fresh dill is the standard green on soups (zurek, borscht, mushroom soup), on boiled new potatoes with butter, in cucumber salads, and folded into sour cream as a dip or sauce base. In Nordic cooking it is the herb in gravlax, in dill new potatoes, and in many pickled-fish preparations. In Middle Eastern cooking (Iranian, Levantine) it appears in herb-heavy rice dishes (baghali polo) and in yogurt sauces. It is not a garnish in any of these traditions; it is the herb.

  Buy fresh dill in bunches with bright green fronds and no yellowing or limpness; the volatile oils are at their peak when the herb is recently cut. Dried dill weed has its uses but is a distant approximation of fresh; the volatile aromatics that define dill do not survive drying well, and the dried product reads more like grassy dust than like the herb. Dill seed (the actual fruit of the plant) is a different ingredient with a more concentrated caraway-leaning flavor, used in pickling.

storage: 'Loosely wrapped in a damp kitchen towel inside a fitted-lid container, refrigerator crisper, 4 to 7 days. The fronds wilt and yellow as they age; trim and use the still-green portions. Freezing is possible (chopped, in ice cubes with water) for long storage but the texture is lost and the result is only useful in cooked applications. Drying captures little of the original aroma.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'fresh dill (in a finishing application)'
    use: 'fresh tarragon, fresh fennel fronds, or fresh chervil'
    note: 'Tarragon and fennel fronds carry the closest anise-warm register; chervil is gentler and grassier. Quantity 1:1 by weight. None reproduce dill exactly.'
  - for: 'fresh dill (in cooked applications)'
    use: 'dried dill weed at one-third the quantity, added in the last 5 minutes of cooking'
    note: 'A real shortcut, not a true substitute. The aromatic load is much shallower and the dish reads recognizably without fresh dill. Acceptable in emergencies.'
