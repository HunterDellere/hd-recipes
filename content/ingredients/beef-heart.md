about: |
  Beef heart is a working muscle, not an organ in the digestive or filtering sense; structurally it is muscle tissue (more similar to a steak than to a liver or kidney) with much higher iron, zinc, B12, and CoQ10 content per gram than skeletal muscle. Composition is roughly 73% water, 17% protein, 4% fat, 5% other (minerals, glycogen). The flavor is mildly beefy with a faint mineral-iron note; the texture is dense and chewy when undercooked, tender and beef-like when prepared correctly.

  The key handling step is trimming. A whole beef heart (roughly 1.5 to 2 kg / 3 to 4 lb) has a network of silver-skin fascia, large blood vessels, valves, and fat caps that all need removal before cooking; the trimmed yield is roughly 60% of the whole-heart weight. Failure to trim aggressively produces a tough, chewy final dish; properly trimmed and cut against the grain into 2 to 3 cm cubes, the heart cooks quickly (3 to 4 minutes total on a hot grill) and reads as tender as a good steak.

  Functional category: lean working-muscle protein. The unusual texture (denser and finer-grained than skeletal muscle, the result of the heart's continuous low-intensity contraction over the animal's life) is what makes beef heart the canonical anticucho protein. Marinated overnight in ají panca, vinegar, garlic, and oil, then skewered and grilled hot and fast, the heart takes on a slight char on the surface while remaining tender at the center; the marinade penetrates the dense muscle better than it would a typical steak cut.

  Applications by category. Peruvian: anticuchos (canonical, the iconic Peruvian street food, beef heart skewers marinated in ají panca and grilled over coals). Mongolian and Central Asian: skewered grilled heart in various nomadic traditions. Filipino: pork heart in arroz caldo and balbacua. The Peruvian anticucho is the dish that has elevated beef heart from offal-tradition to celebrated cuisine; in fine-dining Peruvian restaurants (Astrid y Gastón, Central) it appears on the menu unbroken from the street vendor preparation.

  Sourcing. Mexican and Latin American butchers (almost always available, often pre-trimmed and pre-cubed). Halal and kosher butchers (commonly stocked). Specialty butchers and farm-direct programs in North America and Europe (call ahead). Generic supermarket meat counters usually do not carry it; request an order if needed.

storage: |
  Refrigerated, in original packaging, 2 to 3 days from purchase. Once opened or trimmed, use within 24 hours. Frozen, vacuum-sealed: 4 months at peak (offal cuts freeze less gracefully than skeletal muscle; the texture degrades faster). Cooked: refrigerated 3 days, freeze poorly (the lean dense muscle dries out when frozen cooked). Failure modes: gray or green discoloration (oxidation, edible after trimming but flavor compromised), slime or off smell (bacterial spoilage, discard).

substitutions:
  - for: 'beef heart (for anticuchos)'
    use: 'beef sirloin or flat-iron steak'
    note: 'A standard skeletal muscle substitute when heart is unavailable or culturally challenging for the cook or guests.
