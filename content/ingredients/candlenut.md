about: |
  Candlenut (Aleurites moluccanus, called kemiri in Indonesia, buah keras in Malaysia) is a waxy, oil-rich nut about the size of a macadamia. The kernel is roughly 60 percent fat by weight, soft and crumbly rather than crunchy, with a mild buttery flavor closer to raw cashew than to peanut. In Southeast Asian cooking it is functionally the thickener and fat carrier of bumbu and rempah spice pastes, ground with shallot, chili, garlic, galangal, and terasi to make the wet aromatic base that fries off in oil at the start of curries and rendang.

  Always cook candlenut before eating. Raw candlenuts contain toxic saponins and a mild cyanogenic compound; both break down with heat. The pounded-into-paste-and-fried application that defines its use also fully detoxifies it; standalone snacking is not a tradition.

  Sold dried in vacuum-packed bags or jars in Asian groceries, usually halved or quartered. Color is pale beige to off-white; any browning suggests rancidity (the high oil content turns) and the nut should be discarded. Fresh candlenuts have a faint waxy scent; rancid ones smell like old paint.

  The defining functional contribution is body. A spice paste built without candlenut runs thin and watery; with candlenut it grinds to a thick, almost creamy mass that fries into a brown, oil-cracked base. The 60 percent fat content means the paste carries flavor into oil dramatically faster than a candlenut-free paste, and the ground nut acts as a starch-like thickener in long-cooked curries.

storage: 'Airtight container in the fridge for 6 months, or freezer for a year. The high oil content makes pantry storage risky beyond 2 months. Smell-check before using; rancid candlenut ruins a curry paste.'

    note: 'The closest substitute by fat content (70 percent) and texture (waxy, crumbly). Slightly sweeter than candlenut; the difference disappears once fried into a spice paste.'
  - for: 'candlenut (emergency)'
    use: 'raw cashew, by equal weight'
    note: 'Lower fat content (45 percent) so the paste is slightly thinner. Reads close enough in finished curries; the texture of the raw paste is the main giveaway.'
