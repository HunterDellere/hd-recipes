about: |
  Pompano is the market name for several deep-bodied, laterally flattened jacks in the genus Trachinotus, family Carangidae. Two species dominate US kitchens. Florida or common pompano (Trachinotus carolinus) is the Gulf and South Atlantic fish that carries the Creole and Lowcountry reputation, line-caught, expensive, and seasonal, running 350 to 700 g whole. Golden or snub-nose pompano (Trachinotus blochii) is the aquacultured Indo-Pacific species that fills the iced cases at H Mart, 99 Ranch, and most Asian grocers, usually 300 to 600 g whole, sold under names like "golden pompano," "oval fish," or the Cantonese cang yu. The two are close enough in flesh structure and fat character that they cook interchangeably; the farmed golden pompano is the one a US home cook will actually find on ice, at a fraction of the wild Florida price, which is why it anchors this page.

  The body shape drives the economics. Pompano is a thin, near-circular disc, deep top-to-bottom and narrow front-to-back, with a small head and a compact skeleton of fine, soft pin-bones rather than a heavy rib cage. The practical consequence is yield: a whole pompano returns roughly 45 to 55% of its weight as boneless fillet, high for a round-bodied fish, and the two fillets lift off the central bone plate cleanly because the bones are short and laterally arranged rather than threaded through the meat. The flesh itself is bright white, fine-grained, and dense, with short muscle fibers separated by thin connective sheets, so it sets into firm, large flakes rather than the soft small flakes of cod or the coarse flakes of a true mackerel.

  The defining feature for cooking is fat. Pompano runs 8 to 12% intramuscular and subcutaneous fat, well above lean whitefish like cod, snapper, or branzino (2 to 5%) but below the oily pelagics like mackerel (12 to 18%). Crucially the fat is monounsaturated-leaning and distributed evenly through the muscle rather than concentrated in a dark lateral band, so the flesh reads clean and buttery rather than strong or oily. That even lipid load is the entire reason pompano suits sealed-packet and whole-steam methods: as the muscle proteins denature and the fibers contract above 55°C, the rendered intramuscular fat coats the fibers and buffers moisture loss, so the flesh stays succulent across a much wider doneness window than lean fish. A branzino fillet held two minutes too long goes chalky; a pompano fillet at the same overshoot is still moist. The fish is, in effect, self-basting, which is why it is nearly impossible to dry out.

  Heat behavior and inflection points. The collagen sheets between the muscle blocks gelatinize at 55 to 60°C and the flesh turns from translucent to opaque and lifts cleanly from the bone; the practical doneness target is 54 to 58°C at the thickest point, where the fat is rendered and mobile but not yet driven off. The skin is thin and renders well: under a hard sear or a hot grill the subcutaneous fat layer crisps fast and the skin lifts from the flesh as a crackling sheet, while in a steam or a parchment packet the skin stays soft and slips off if unwanted. Because the fat buffers the flesh, pompano tolerates the high, fast heat of a fully preheated oven (200 to 220°C) better than lean fish; the packet method exploits exactly this, trapping the rendered fat and any added wine or butter as a steam-and-fat emulsion around the fillet.

  Applications by category. On the Gulf Coast and Lowcountry the canonical preparations are pompano en papillote (the New Orleans Creole parchment-packet dish, fillets steamed in butter, wine, and a little shrimp or crab) and whole grilled or pan-seared pompano dressed with brown butter and lemon, the fat carrying high heat without drying. Across Cantonese, Vietnamese, and broader Southeast Asian markets the same fish goes in whole: steamed Cantonese-style over ginger and scallion with a hot-oil and soy finish (the snub-nose golden pompano is a default banquet steaming fish in southern China), or scored and deep-fried whole until the skin blisters and served with a tamarind, fish-sauce, or sweet-and-sour dressing in Vietnamese and Thai kitchens. The thread across both traditions is that pompano's even fat lets the cook apply an aggressive finish, smoking oil, hard fry, hot grill, without sacrificing interior moisture.

  Sourcing notes. At H Mart and most Asian seafood counters the golden pompano sits whole on crushed ice, often labeled "golden pompano" or "pompano" with the Trachinotus blochii origin (frequently farmed, Taiwan, China, or Southeast Asia). Look for clear, domed, shiny eyes (cloudy or sunken means past peak), bright silver flanks with the characteristic gold wash along the belly and fins, tight intact scales, no gummy slime (a thin clear sheen is normal), firm flesh that springs back from a finger press, and a clean ocean smell with no ammonia. The bright gold belly fades to dull gray as the fish ages, a useful at-a-glance freshness read specific to this species. Buy it whole and have the counter scale, gut, and fillet it for you, reserving the frame; the whole fish is the better value per gram of meat and the carcass makes a quick, clean fumet. A 450 to 500 g whole fish yields two fillets of roughly 110 to 130 g each, the right portion for one person.

storage: |
  Whole pompano, gutted and on ice in the refrigerator: best the day of purchase, acceptable within 24 hours. The moderate fat fraction oxidizes faster than lean whitefish but slower than mackerel; set the fish on a wire rack inside a rimmed pan under a bag of crushed ice (so meltwater drips below the fish) in the coldest part of the fridge. Fillets, fresh: 24 to 36 hours on ice. Flash-frozen whole fish or fillets, vacuum-sealed: 3 months at peak quality, 6 months acceptable; thaw overnight in the refrigerator in the bag, never at room temperature. Failure modes: any ammonia or sour note (microbial and lipid breakdown, discard and do not cook); the gold belly wash fading to flat gray and the silver flanks dulling (oxidizing surface fat, the fish is past peak and should go only into a heavily seasoned fry or braise); gummy rather than clear slime on the skin (bacterial surface load, rinse and cook immediately or discard); soft flesh that holds a finger dent without springing back (protein breakdown, the fish is too far gone for any clean preparation); yellow-brown discoloration or a rancid-oil smell at the cut surface of a fillet (advanced lipid oxidation, trim back to clean white flesh or discard).

substitutions:
  - for: 'pompano (en papillote / baked in a sealed packet)'
    use: 'black sea bass, branzino, or rainbow trout fillets'
    note: 'The packet method forgives a slightly leaner fish because the trapped butter and wine supply external fat, so branzino and black sea bass both work; add 1 to 2 minutes of bake time for a thicker bass fillet and pull a touch earlier since these lean fish dry faster than pompano. Trout is oilier and closest to pompano in self-basting behavior. Avoid thin flatfish fillets, which overcook before the packet puffs.'
  - for: 'pompano (whole steamed, Cantonese style)'
    use: 'branzino, black sea bass, or whole golden trevally'
    note: 'Branzino and black sea bass are the standard whole-steaming whitefish and carry the ginger-scallion treatment well, though both are leaner so hold to the lower end of the steam window. Golden trevally (a close Carangidae relative) is the nearest match in flesh density and fat. Match the steam time to the thickest point, not the species.'
  - for: 'pompano (grilled or pan-seared whole)'
    use: 'butterfish (Pacific harvestfish), small bluefish, or jack/trevally'
    note: 'Butterfish (the true Peprilus, also a flat deep-bodied fish) is the closest in shape and buttery flesh for a whole grill, just smaller and more delicate, so cut the heat slightly. Trevally and other jacks share pompano fat character and grill well. Bluefish is oilier and stronger; pair with extra acid.'
