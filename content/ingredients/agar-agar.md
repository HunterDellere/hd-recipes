about: |
  Agar is a mix of two polysaccharides (agarose and agaropectin) extracted from Gelidium and Gracilaria red algae. Where gelatin (animal-derived collagen) melts at body temperature and sets soft, agar sets at 32 to 40°C and only re-melts above 85°C, meaning agar jellies hold their shape in a tropical kitchen, on a hot day, and in a warm bowl of chè coconut sauce. This thermal stability is why agar dominates Southeast Asian dessert work where gelatin would slump.

  Texturally agar is brittle-firm at the standard 1% dilution; it cuts cleanly, snaps on the bite, and feels more like a soft candy than a custard. Below 0.6% it sets too soft to handle; above 1.5% it turns rubbery. The Vietnamese rau cau (jelly used in chè ba màu) is typically 0.8%, set enough to slice into colored cubes that hold in a sweetened coconut bath.

  Activation requires a hard boil. Bloom agar powder in cold liquid, then bring to a rolling boil and hold for 1 minute. Sub-boil temperatures do not fully dissolve the polymer; the result is a weak, grainy set. Acid (lemon juice, calamansi, citric acid) below pH 4.5 cleaves the polymer chains and weakens the gel, add acids only after the agar has set, or compensate with extra agar.

  Sold as powder, flakes, or bars (kanten). Powder is the most predictable; 1 tsp / 4 g sets 240 ml liquid firm. Flakes are roughly half the strength by weight; bars vary by brand. Always weigh, never volume-measure between forms.

storage: 'Airtight container, cool dry pantry. Indefinitely stable as a dried polymer.'
