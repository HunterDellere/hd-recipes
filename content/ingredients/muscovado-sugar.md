about: |
  Muscovado (also called Barbados sugar or moist sugar) is whole cane juice that has been clarified, evaporated, and crystallized without separating out the molasses. Standard "brown sugar" sold in supermarkets is the inverse: white refined sugar with molasses added back. The two differ in flavor depth, muscovado carries minerals (iron, magnesium, calcium) and aromatic compounds that the refine-and-recombine process strips out, giving a butterscotch-rum complexity rather than the flat sweetness of recombined brown sugar.

  Dark muscovado (the standard form) is roughly 8 to 10% molasses by weight; light muscovado is around 3 to 5%. The dark version is what hits the deep mahogany color and intense caramel-rum profile that brown-sugar boba syrups (the Tiger Sugar style) depend on. Substituting standard dark brown sugar gives a recognizable but flatter result.

  In syrup work, the molasses contributes both color and Maillard browning behavior. A muscovado syrup hits a deeper amber faster than a brown-sugar syrup at the same temperature; pull it 5°C earlier to avoid scorching. The high mineral content also raises smoke and burn point slightly compared to refined sugar.

  Texture is sticky and clumpy by design, the moisture is the freshness. A dried-out, sandy muscovado has lost the molasses oils that carry its character and isn't worth using; rehydrate with a slice of apple or bread in the container for 24 hours.

  Common brands worth seeking: Billington's (UK, widely exported), India Tree (US), and any "Mauritian dark" or "Filipino muscovado" labeled as single-origin.

storage: 'Airtight container with a slice of apple or a square of damp paper towel to keep moisture in. Two years; the molasses oils slowly oxidize and flatten the flavor past that point.'
