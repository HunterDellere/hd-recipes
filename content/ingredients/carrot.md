about: |
  The carrot is the cultivated taproot of Daucus carota subsp. sativus, descended from the wild carrot native to Central Asia and the eastern Mediterranean. The modern orange carrot is a relatively recent development (16th to 17th century Netherlands), bred from yellow and purple ancestral stocks; today supermarket carrots are almost exclusively the orange Nantes, Imperator, or Chantenay types, with heirloom rainbow varieties (purple, white, yellow, red) available at farmers markets and specialty grocers. The root is 80 to 90% water by weight, 5 to 10% sugar (sucrose, glucose, fructose), 2 to 3% pectin and other soluble fibers, and is the most concentrated dietary source of beta-carotene (the orange pigment, hydrolyzed to vitamin A in the gut).

  Sugar and caramelization chemistry. Carrots are one of the sweetest common vegetables, with sugar content rising during cold storage (the plant converts starch to free sugars as a cold-protection mechanism, making winter-stored carrots noticeably sweeter than fresh-pulled summer carrots). Under high dry heat (roasting at 220°C+, dry-pan caramelizing, grilling), the free sugars Maillard with the small protein fraction and the surface sugars also undergo direct caramelization, producing the deep brown-gold color and concentrated sweet-savory flavor of well-roasted carrots. The caramelization peak is around 160 to 175°C surface temperature; lower than that and the carrot reads steamed-soft rather than caramelized, higher and the sugars scorch into bitterness. Under long wet heat (braising, simmering in soup), the pectin softens and the carrot reads silky and sweet without caramelization; this is the right behavior for soups, stews, and the mirepoix base of French cooking.

  Mirepoix and soup base. The French mirepoix (two parts diced onion, one part diced carrot, one part diced celery, slowly sweated in butter) is the foundational aromatic base of French stocks, soups, stews, and braises; the carrot contributes sweetness and color, the onion contributes sulfurous depth, and the celery contributes a slightly bitter green-mineral aromatic. The Italian soffritto is structurally equivalent. The Spanish sofrito sometimes substitutes red pepper for the carrot; the Cajun trinity (onion, celery, green pepper) replaces the carrot entirely with bell pepper for a different aromatic register. The Chinese "yi cai" base for stocks similarly uses scallion and ginger rather than carrot. Outside the French-Mediterranean tradition, the carrot is more often used as a featured ingredient (Japanese root-vegetable simmers, Moroccan tagines, Indian gajar halwa) than as a background aromatic.

  Cooking applications by category. **French (mirepoix base)**: stocks, mother sauces, daube, pot au feu, every braise that starts with onion-carrot-celery. **Italian (soffritto)**: ragu bolognese, minestrone, bean and lentil soups. **English and American (Sunday roast)**: glazed carrots alongside roast meat, carrot-and-parsnip mash, carrot cake (the Anglo dessert tradition). **Moroccan and North African**: carrot salad with cumin and harissa, tagines with chickpea and lamb. **Indian (especially northern)**: gajar halwa (carrots cooked in cream and ghee with cardamom, a classic dessert), gajar matar (carrots and peas), carrot pickle. **Japanese**: nimono (simmered root vegetables in dashi and soy), kenchinjiru and other vegetable miso soups, namasu (vinegared carrot-and-daikon salad). **Korean**: julienned carrot in bibimbap, kimchi components, namul side dishes. **Vietnamese**: pickled carrot-and-daikon (đồ chua) for bánh mì, raw julienne in spring rolls. **Pan-Asian roasted-vegetable applications**: roasted with five-spice, roasted with miso glaze, roasted with sesame oil and soy.

  Cut shape and cooking time. The carrot's hardness and cook-time response are tightly linked to cut shape. **Rondelles (coins)**: 3 mm coins cook through in 8 to 10 minutes of simmering, 15 minutes of braising. **Half-moons**: same as rondelles. **Batonnets (matchsticks)**: 5 mm × 5 mm × 5 cm sticks cook in 6 to 8 minutes simmering, are the right cut for stir-fries and glazed vegetable preparations. **Brunoise (fine dice)**: 3 mm cubes cook in 4 to 5 minutes; the canonical mirepoix dice. **Roll-cut (rangiri)**: irregular wedge shape produced by rotating the carrot 90° between angled knife cuts; the irregular faces caramelize more aggressively than uniform cuts and the shape is canonical for Japanese nimono and Chinese braised carrots. **Whole**: a whole baby carrot (or a halved larger carrot) roasted at 220°C+ takes 25 to 35 minutes and produces a dramatic single-vegetable presentation.

  Buying notes. Supermarket carrots in plastic bags (Imperator type, the long uniform orange) are bred for shelf life and visual uniformity; flavor is acceptable but less complex than smaller varieties. Bunched carrots with greens attached (sold at farmers markets and better grocers) are typically the Nantes type and have meaningfully more flavor; the greens are edible (pesto, soup garnish, herb component) though slightly bitter. Heirloom rainbow carrots cost 2 to 3x more but visually transform a simple dish; the purple-skinned varieties are sweeter than orange, the white varieties are less sweet but more mineral. Baby carrots in supermarket bags are not actually small varieties; they are larger carrots whittled down to small cylinders, with a slightly waxy surface (food-grade chlorine wash residue) and noticeably less flavor than whole carrots. Choose firm carrots with smooth skin, no soft spots, no green sprouting at the stem end (sprouting indicates age and the sugar has begun converting back to starch), and greens that are bright and unwilted if attached.

  Common Western drift. (1) Substituting baby carrots for cut full-size carrots in any application where flavor matters; the baby carrots cook faster and read blander. (2) Discarding carrot greens; they're edible and flavorful (the slight bitterness is a feature for chimichurri-style sauces and bean soups). (3) Boiling carrots in salted water then dressing with butter and salt; the technique is fine but throws away the caramelization that high-heat roasting would have delivered. Roasting carrots at high temperature with a small amount of oil produces a meaningfully more complex result than boiling.

storage: |
  Whole carrots (no greens), refrigerated in a sealed bag in the crisper drawer with a paper towel to absorb excess moisture: 3 to 5 weeks. With greens attached, remove the greens (they wick moisture from the root) and store as above; greens themselves last 1 to 2 days in a damp paper towel. Carrots submerged in water in a sealed container in the fridge (the Korean traditional storage method): up to 2 months with weekly water changes. Cut carrots in a sealed container: 5 to 7 days. Frozen, blanched first (2 to 3 minutes in boiling water, then ice bath, dried, frozen on a sheet pan, transferred to a bag): 8 to 12 months; the texture is degraded but suitable for soups and stews. Failure modes are softness or flexibility (the carrot has lost moisture, still usable but degraded; partial recovery is possible by submerging in ice water for 30 minutes), white "blush" on the cut surface (oxidation and surface dehydration, cosmetic only), and slimy or moldy surface (advanced spoilage, discard).

substitutions:
  - for: 'carrot (French mirepoix, Italian soffritto, soup base)'
    use: 'parsnip, celeriac, or sweet potato (in small amounts)'
    note: 'Parsnip is the closest substitute for the sweet-aromatic role in mirepoix; the soup or stock reads slightly more sweet-anise and less orange-pigmented. Celeriac substitutes the textural component but lacks the sweetness; adjust by adding a tsp of sugar to the base. Sweet potato (in small dice) provides similar sweetness with a different color and a faster cook time.'
  - for: 'carrot (Japanese nimono, root-vegetable simmer)'
    use: 'daikon, sweet potato, or kabocha squash'
    note: 'Daikon is the canonical co-vegetable with carrot in Japanese root-vegetable simmers and works alone; the dish reads cleaner and more subtle. Sweet potato substitutes the sweetness with a faster cook. Kabocha squash provides both sweetness and a denser texture; substitute 1:1.'
  - for: 'carrot (raw application, salads and pickles)'
    use: 'daikon, jicama, or kohlrabi'
    note: 'Daikon is the most common substitute in Asian pickled-vegetable applications (đồ chua, namasu); reads white and slightly peppery rather than orange and sweet. Jicama provides crunch and a faintly sweet flavor for Mexican and Southeast Asian salads. Kohlrabi (the swollen stem of a brassica) provides crunch and a faintly mustardy flavor.'
  - for: 'carrot (roasted vegetable, glazed application)'
    use: 'parsnip, turnip, or sweet potato'
    note: 'Parsnip behaves nearly identically under roasting and glazing; the dish reads cream-colored and slightly more aromatic. Turnip is less sweet and more peppery, requires a touch of added sugar or honey in the glaze. Sweet potato roasts to a similar caramelized surface with a softer interior and faster cook time.'
