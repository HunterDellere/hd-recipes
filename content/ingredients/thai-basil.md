about: |
  Ocimum basilicum var. thyrsiflora, a basil cultivar that diverged from the Mediterranean lineage in Southeast Asia. The plant is recognizable by its purple-tinted stems, narrow lance-shaped leaves, and small purple flower spikes. The aromatic profile is dominated by methyl chavicol (estragole), the same compound that gives tarragon its anise-licorice note, with very little of the linalool that makes Italian basil sweet and clove-like.

  Functionally Thai basil is heat-stable in a way Italian basil is not. The tougher leaf structure holds up to 30 seconds of high-heat wok contact (pad kee mao, pad krapow stir-fries) without wilting to a black slick; Italian basil added the same way browns and turns bitter. In Vietnamese cooking the leaves go raw on the herb plate beside pho, where the anise note cuts through the rich beef broth; the cook adds whole sprigs to the bowl just before eating so they wilt gently in the steam without losing their aromatic top.

  Distinct from holy basil (Ocimum tenuiflorum, kaprao), which is peppery and clove-forward and the canonical herb for pad kaprao. The two are not interchangeable; pad kaprao made with Thai basil is structurally a different dish (and is what most Western restaurants serve, holy basil being harder to source).

  Fresh sprigs are the only worthwhile form; dried Thai basil is largely flavorless within weeks. Look for firm, glossy leaves with no black spots; the herb wilts fast in plastic bags.

storage: 'Stems in a glass of water on the counter, loosely covered with a plastic bag, lasts 4 to 5 days. Refrigeration browns the leaves; the cold-sensitivity is structural. For longer storage, pluck leaves and freeze in a single layer, then transfer to a bag; the texture suffers but the aroma keeps for 3 months.'

substitutions:

    use: 'Italian basil plus a pinch of crushed fennel seed'
    note: 'Italian basil added at the last 10 seconds (later than Thai basil) keeps it from wilting completely; the fennel seed approximates the missing anise note. The dish will read close but not identical.'
  - for: 'Thai basil (raw garnish for pho)'
    use: 'Italian basil torn at the last second'
    note: 'Standard Vietnamese-American restaurant substitution. The aromatic axis shifts from anise to clove-sweet but works in context.'
