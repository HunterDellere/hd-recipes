about: |
  Shimeji come in a tight cluster, sold most often as **buna-shimeji** (brown beech) or **bunapi-shimeji** (white beech). They are aggressively bitter when raw; a defense compound called fasciculol that breaks down with heat. Never eat them raw. Once seared above ~60°C the bitterness is gone and what's left is a clean, slightly seafood-sweet umami with a snappy bite (firm cell walls, low water content compared to button mushrooms).

  The cooking key is **dry heat first, fat second**. Trim off the woody base, separate the cluster into individual stems, dry-sear them in a hot pan with no oil for 60–90 seconds to drive off surface moisture and concentrate flavor. Add fat only after they've caught some color. Crowding the pan steams them; they need single-layer contact or you lose the texture and the bitterness lingers.

  Their glutamate content (a dashi-style umami) makes them a natural pairing with miso, soy, butter, and dashi-based sauces. They hold structure better than enoki or shiitake in stir-fries and soups.

storage: 'Refrigerated in their original pack or a paper bag (never sealed plastic; they sweat and rot). 5–7 days.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'shimeji (dry sear, sheet-pan roast, structural bite)'
    use: 'maitake (hen of the woods), pulled into clusters'
    note: 'More earthy, less briny-sweet. Crisps even better than shimeji under dry heat; the cluster architecture handles the same way.'
  - for: 'shimeji (stir-fries, soups, mixed mushroom dishes)'
    use: 'oyster mushroom, torn into small pieces'
    note: 'Different flavor (more anise-floral) but similar single-piece architecture. Holds up in liquid better than shiitake.'
  - for: 'shimeji (dashi-based soups, where umami matters more than texture)'
    use: 'rehydrated dried shiitake'
    note: 'Stronger umami, softer texture. Reserve the soaking liquid as part of the dashi.'
