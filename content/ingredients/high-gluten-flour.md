about: |
  High-gluten flour is wheat flour milled from hard red spring or hard red winter wheat to a protein content of roughly 13.5 to 14.5%, the highest commonly available outside of pure vital wheat gluten. The two reference grades in American baking are King Arthur''s Sir Lancelot (~14.2%, malted) and Sir Galahad equivalents in the professional channel; bromated and unbromated versions exist, with the bromated form historically favored in commercial bagel and pizza shops for extra oven spring. The protein figure is the entire point of the flour. The two storage proteins glutenin and gliadin, present in proportion to total protein, are what hydrate and cross-link into gluten, and a flour with two to four percentage points more protein than all-purpose builds a denser, stronger, more elastic network from the same hydration.

  Functional role: high-gluten flour is a structure flour, chosen when the finished crumb needs to be chewy, dense, and resistant to tearing rather than tender. When water hits the flour and the dough is worked, glutenin contributes elasticity (the pull-back) and gliadin contributes extensibility (the stretch); together they form a continuous viscoelastic matrix that traps fermentation gas and sets into the chew. More protein means more of this matrix per gram of flour, so the dough holds a tighter, less open crumb under the same mixing. This is why the flour defines a New York bagel: the bagel is a low-hydration, densely worked, briefly boiled, then baked dough whose entire identity is the tight chew and glossy blistered skin that only a 14% protein flour delivers. It is the same reason it anchors hand-tossed pizza built for a thin, foldable, leopard-spotted edge, chewy bagel-adjacent breads like bialys and pretzels, and any bread where structural integrity under heavy handling matters more than a delicate crumb.

  How heat, hydration, and working change it. The gluten matrix only forms with both water and mechanical work; dry flour has no gluten, only the precursor proteins. High-gluten dough demands more hydration to fully develop and more kneading energy to reach window-pane, and it resists shaping harder than weaker flours because the elastic glutenin fights back. Rest periods (autolyse, bench rest) let the network relax through enzymatic and physical slackening so the dough stops snapping back. In the oven, two thresholds matter: starch gelatinizes from about 60°C as it absorbs water and swells, and gluten denatures and sets permanently from roughly 70 to 80°C, locking the expanded structure in place. The high protein load means the set walls are thicker and stronger, which is what gives the dense chew and also why these doughs tolerate boiling (bagels, pretzels) without collapsing: the surface starch gelatinizes in the bath and the strong gluten beneath holds shape through to the bake.

  Where it fits, by category. Dense chewy breads are the core use: bagels, bialys, pretzels, Montreal-style and New York-style rounds, and Detroit and New York pizza doughs that want pull and snap. It also strengthens enriched or laminated doughs that would otherwise slacken under fat and sugar, and bakers blend a portion of it into weaker flours to raise the protein of a whole-grain or rye dough that needs more backbone. It is the wrong flour for anything that should be tender: cakes, biscuits, pie crust, muffins, and pancakes all want low protein, and high-gluten flour makes them tough.

  Buying notes. In the US the practical retail option is King Arthur Sir Lancelot, sold in larger bags through baking suppliers and some grocers; restaurant-supply channels carry it and unbranded high-gluten flour by the 50 lb sack. Check the protein on the nutrition panel: roughly 4 g protein per 30 g serving signals a true high-gluten flour, versus about 3 to 3.5 g for bread flour and 3 g for all-purpose. "Malted" on the label means a small amount of diastatic malt is included to feed the yeast and aid browning, which is desirable for bagels. Bromated flour (potassium bromate) is banned in the EU, UK, and several US states and is increasingly avoided; unbromated high-gluten flour is the default and bakes nearly identically for home use.

  Common substitution drift. The reflex swap is bread flour, and it is the right one: bread flour runs 12 to 13% protein, one to two points below high-gluten, so a bread-flour bagel reads slightly softer and less aggressively chewy but is entirely good. The genuine error is reaching for all-purpose (10 to 12%), which cannot build enough network for a true bagel chew and produces a bready, rounded result. To push bread flour toward high-gluten, a baker can add vital wheat gluten at roughly one tablespoon per cup of bread flour, which lifts the effective protein into the 13.5 to 14% range.

seasonality: 'None; a milled, shelf-stable dry good available year-round. Hard red spring wheat (the highest-protein North American class) is harvested late summer through autumn, but milling and blending make the retail flour consistent across the calendar.'

storage: |
  Store in an airtight container in a cool, dark, dry pantry. Unbromated white high-gluten flour keeps 6 to 12 months at peak; the higher protein and bran content of high-extraction or whole-grain versions shortens that. Flour''s failure modes are rancidity and infestation. Rancidity announces itself as a sharp, crayon-like, or musty-stale smell once the small fraction of wheat-germ oil oxidizes; fresh flour smells faintly sweet and neutral, so any oily or paint-like note means discard. Infestation appears as fine webbing, clumping, or tiny moving weevils, most common in warm pantries and in flour stored more than a few months; a sealed container and a cooler shelf prevent it, and freezing a new bag for 48 hours kills any eggs present at purchase. Clumping with no smell or webbing is usually just absorbed humidity and is safe if the flour breaks apart dry; clumping that stays damp or smells off means moisture has set in and the bag should go. For storage beyond a few months, an airtight container in the freezer roughly doubles shelf life.

    note: 'Add about 1 tablespoon vital wheat gluten per cup of bread flour to lift effective protein into the 13.5 to 14% range. Mix the gluten thoroughly into the dry flour before hydrating so it disperses evenly.'
  - for: 'high-gluten flour (hand-tossed or New York pizza dough)'
    use: 'bread flour, or 00 flour for a softer Neapolitan edge'
    note: 'Bread flour is closest for the chewy, foldable New York style. 00 flour is lower in protein and finer milled, giving a more tender, less chewy crust suited to high-heat Neapolitan baking rather than the dense pull high-gluten provides.'
  - for: 'high-gluten flour (general bread where extra structure helps but a true bagel is not the goal)'
    use: 'all-purpose flour plus vital wheat gluten'
    note: 'All-purpose alone (10 to 12% protein) is too weak for bagel chew, but adding roughly 1 to 2 teaspoons vital wheat gluten per cup raises it into bread-flour territory. Plain all-purpose works for ordinary loaves and rolls where chew is not the point.'
