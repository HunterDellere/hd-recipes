about: |
  Culantro is Eryngium foetidum, a biennial herb in the Apiaceae family (same as cilantro, parsley, dill, fennel, and carrots), native to the Caribbean and tropical Central and South America and naturalized across Southeast Asia through colonial-era trade routes. The plant grows as a low rosette of long (10 to 25 cm) lance-shaped leaves with serrated, almost sawtooth edges (giving the English common name "sawtooth herb"), pale-green to slightly bluish in tone, with a thick midrib running the length of each leaf. The leaves are tougher than cilantro and require thin slicing to release the aromatics; whole leaves do not chew as easily.

  Aromatics and chemistry. The defining aromatic compound is (E,E)-2,4-dodecadienal, the same aldehyde responsible for the characteristic flavor of cilantro, but present in culantro at 3 to 5× the concentration. This is why culantro reads as "cilantro turned up": more intense, more pungent, with a slightly bitter back-note that cilantro lacks. The secondary aromatics include alpha-bergamotene, beta-caryophyllene, and several other terpenes, contributing a slight earthy and faintly anise dimension. The same genetic variation that makes some people experience cilantro as "soapy" (a sensitivity to the dodecadienal aldehyde, linked to variants in the OR6A2 olfactory receptor gene) applies more intensely to culantro; cilantro-haters typically dislike culantro even more.

  Heat tolerance. Unlike cilantro (whose volatile aromatics dissipate quickly under heat), culantro retains much of its aromatic profile through simmering; this is one reason it appears in the broth of pho (added during the stock simmer in some Vietnamese kitchens, or chopped into the bowl as a finishing herb in others), in Caribbean rice-and-beans (cooked into the pot), and in long-simmered stews. The aldehyde and terpene profile is more thermally stable than the lighter top notes of cilantro, so culantro carries through cooking where cilantro would fade.

  Cooking applications by category. **Caribbean (Puerto Rican, Dominican, Cuban, Trinidadian)**: in sofrito (the aromatic base of Puerto Rican cooking, alongside cilantro and ají dulce); in sazón (often as a pre-blended spice with culantro powder); in pelau (Trinidadian rice-and-meat); in stews and braises. **Vietnamese**: as a finishing herb in phở (chopped into the bowl alongside Thai basil, mint, and bean sprouts); in canh chua (chopped into the bowl as a structural herb); in several Mekong-delta soups. **Thai, Lao, Cambodian**: in tom yum and tom kha variations; in some sour soups; in fresh-herb plates served with grilled meats. **South Asian (Bangladeshi, Bengali Indian)**: occasionally in fresh chutneys and as a finishing herb for fish curries, though less common than in the other traditions.

  Buying notes. Fresh culantro at Latin American markets (Mexican, Puerto Rican, Dominican, Cuban) in major US metros, and at Vietnamese, Thai, and Cambodian markets across the country. Sold in small bundles (50 to 100 g) of long whole leaves tied with a twist tie or rubber band. Choose leaves that are bright green-to-bluish (avoid yellowed or limp), firm with no soft brown patches, and with intact sawtooth edges. The herb is sometimes labeled with multiple names on the same package (culantro, recao, ngò gai) reflecting the Spanish-Vietnamese-English market crossover. Frozen pre-cut culantro is occasionally available at large Asian groceries; the frozen form retains the aromatics well due to the thermally stable profile.

  Common Western drift. Two patterns. (1) US home cooks confuse culantro with cilantro (the names are nearly identical, the family is the same, and the dodecadienal aldehyde overlaps), substituting one for the other without adjusting quantity; culantro at the same weight as called-for cilantro will overwhelm the dish, and cilantro at the same weight as called-for culantro will read thin. The conversion is roughly 1 part culantro to 2 to 3 parts cilantro by weight for similar aromatic intensity. (2) Latin-Caribbean culantro and Vietnamese ngò gai are the same plant, but US recipes from each tradition use slightly different cuts (Caribbean: chopped fine; Vietnamese: thin-sliced crosswise) and amounts; following the cut and quantity from the originating tradition matters.

storage: |
  Fresh culantro, wrapped in slightly damp paper towel inside a sealed plastic bag, refrigerated: 7 to 10 days; the tougher leaves keep substantially longer than cilantro. The herb is past peak when leaves yellow and lose their firm structure. Pre-chopped or pre-sliced culantro in a sealed container: 2 to 3 days, the cut surfaces oxidize and the aromatics dissipate faster. Frozen pre-cut culantro: 3 to 6 months at peak quality; the frozen form is well-suited to culantro due to the thermally stable aromatic profile. Failure modes: yellow-brown leaves (oxidation, trim or discard), slimy patches at the cut ends (bacterial, discard), strong fermented smell (advanced spoilage, discard).

    use: 'cilantro at 2 to 3× the weight'
    note: 'The most direct substitute and the workable workhorse. The aromatic profile is similar (the same dodecadienal compound) but at lower concentration and without the slight bitter back-note. The dish reads slightly thinner aromatically but is recognizably present.'
  - for: 'culantro (Caribbean sofrito, sazón)'
    use: 'cilantro at 2× the weight plus a pinch of dried oregano'
    note: 'For Caribbean applications where the slight earthy back-note of culantro matters, adding a small pinch of dried oregano alongside the doubled cilantro approximates the depth. Acceptable substitute for sofrito and rice-and-bean applications when culantro is unavailable.'
  - for: 'culantro (cooked applications, long simmer)'
    use: 'cilantro added in two passes (half during cooking, half at the end)'
    note: 'Because cilantro fades faster under heat than culantro, splitting the substitution into a cooked portion plus a finishing portion produces a longer-lasting aromatic top than a single late addition. The dish still loses some depth but holds up better through simmering.'
