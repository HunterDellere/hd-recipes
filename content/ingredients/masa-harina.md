about: |
  Masa harina is dried, finely ground nixtamalized corn. The production sequence: whole dried corn kernels are simmered with calcium hydroxide (cal), steeped overnight, drained and rinsed, ground wet into fresh masa, then dehydrated and reground into a fine flour. The result is shelf-stable corn flour that reconstitutes into a workable dough by adding water (roughly 1:1 by volume, slightly less by weight).

  Masa harina is structurally different from cornmeal and from corn flour. Cornmeal is dry-ground non-nixtamalized corn (used for cornbread, polenta, cornmeal mush) and produces a granular gritty texture. Corn flour is finely ground non-nixtamalized corn and produces a less granular but still non-tortilla product. Only nixtamalized corn flour (masa harina) produces tortillas that hold together, fold without cracking, and carry the structural corn-and-lime flavor of Mexican cooking. Substituting cornmeal or corn flour in a tortilla recipe produces an unworkable dough that crumbles on the comal.

  Commercial brands. Maseca is the global supermarket default and produces a good everyday tortilla; the flour is fine, slightly bland, and reconstitutes consistently. Bob's Red Mill masa harina is comparable. Specialty brands (Masienda, made from heirloom Mexican corn varieties like blue Bolita, white Olotillo, yellow Conico) produce meaningfully better tortillas with deeper corn flavor, available online and at specialty grocers at 3 to 5 times the supermarket price.

  Hydration is the critical variable. Add water gradually (start at 1.1 times the flour weight) and knead 2 to 3 minutes; the dough should hold together when squeezed without crumbling and without sticking to the bowl. Too dry: the tortilla cracks at the edges when pressed and tears when handled. Too wet: the dough sticks to the press and the cooked tortilla turns gummy. Rest the dough 15 to 30 minutes covered with a damp towel; the rest hydrates the flour particles fully and produces a more pliable dough that presses thinner without tearing.

  Applications by category. Mexican: corn tortillas (the canonical use), tamales (mixed with lard and broth for the thicker dough), sopes (thick masa rounds with pinched edges, fried, topped with beans and meat), gorditas (split open and stuffed), huaraches, tlacoyos. Salvadoran: pupusas (thick masa rounds stuffed with cheese or pork). Atoles and champurrado: thinned masa drinks. Nixtamalized corn flour is the structural foundation of Mesoamerican cuisine.

storage: |
  Sealed bag or jar, cool dry pantry, 6 to 9 months at peak. The flour does not spoil dramatically; it loses aromatic freshness over time and produces slightly flatter tortillas. Refrigerate or freeze for longer storage (12 to 18 months); the flour absorbs refrigerator odors if not sealed well, so double-bag. Failure modes: musty smell (oxidized fats, replace), insect activity (discard).
