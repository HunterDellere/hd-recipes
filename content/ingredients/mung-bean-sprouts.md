about: |
  Mung bean sprouts are the green-yellow mung bean (*Vigna radiata*) germinated for 4–6 days at warm room temperature in the dark, harvested when the white shoot is 3–5 cm long and the cotyledons are still attached but pale. The sprout is dramatically different from the seed it came from: germination has hydrolyzed roughly half of the starch into simple sugars and half of the storage protein into free amino acids, raised the vitamin C from undetectable to 13 mg per 100 g, and pumped the water content from 12% to 90%. The result is a structurally fragile, mildly sweet, faintly grassy crunch carrier with almost no fat, almost no calories, and a shelf life measured in days.

  The functional role is texture. Across the cuisines that use mung sprouts heavily — Filipino lumpia and ukoy, Vietnamese phở garnish and gỏi cuốn, Cantonese chow mein and beef chow fun, Thai pad thai, Indonesian gado-gado, Korean sukju namul — the sprout is doing snap-and-juice work that no other ingredient does the same way. The cell walls are thin and rigid, packed with water at high turgor pressure, and they audibly break under a tooth before flooding the bite with cool, slightly sweet liquid. Once the cell walls collapse, that effect is gone for good, which is why the sprout tolerates only seconds of high heat before it has to come off, and why it is added at the end of a stir-fry, garnished raw onto pho, or folded into batter for ukoy where the brief frying flash-sets the surrounding starch shell before the interior overcooks.

  Soybean sprouts (the thicker yellow-headed sprout used in Korean kongnamul) are a different ingredient. They tolerate longer cooking and have a starchier bite. Mung sprouts and soy sprouts are not interchangeable in either direction; recipes specifying "bean sprouts" without qualification almost always mean mung in Southeast Asian and Chinese contexts and soy in Korean contexts.

  The food-safety profile of fresh sprouts is genuinely worse than that of most produce. The warm humid germination conditions are also ideal for Salmonella, *E. coli* O157:H7, and Listeria, and outbreaks linked to commercially produced sprouts run into the dozens of cases per year in the US alone. Cooking past 70 °C internal kills the pathogens; this is why a stir-fried or ukoy-fried application is uniformly safe and why raw garnishing on pho is a calculated risk that the cuisine has historically tolerated and the modern food-safety guidance flags. Buyers with compromised immunity should cook them; everyone else should at least buy from a refrigerated case rather than a room-temperature display.

storage: 'Refrigerator, in a vented container or loosely-bagged in a paper towel that absorbs sweat. 2–4 days at 4 °C. Failure mode is sliminess and an ammonia smell as the sugars and amino acids feed the surface microbiome; once that starts, the sprouts are out, since rinsing does not undo the population. A blanching step (10 seconds in boiling water, ice bath, drained) extends usable life by 2–3 days at the cost of crunch. Never freeze: the cell walls rupture and what thaws is wet pulp, not crunch.'
