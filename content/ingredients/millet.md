about: |
  Millet is the umbrella name for several unrelated small-seeded cereal grasses, the three most commercially important being pearl millet (Pennisetum glaucum, the staple of the West African Sahel and northern India where it is called bajra), foxtail millet (Setaria italica, the principal millet of East Asia, called xiaomi in Mandarin and used for congee), and finger millet (Eleusine coracana, called ragi in southern India, used for flour and porridge). All three are gluten-free, roughly 11% protein, 73% carbohydrate, 4% fat, and rich in magnesium, phosphorus, and B vitamins. The kernels are 2 to 3 mm across (pearl), 1 to 2 mm (foxtail), or smaller still (finger), and carry a thin bran that the cook can choose to leave on or polish off. The flavor of cooked plain millet is mild and faintly sweet, with a corn-popcorn aromatic edge that intensifies on toasting.

  Functional role. Millet is a starch base. Cooked with a 1:2 grain-to-water ratio it produces a fluffy, separated, couscous-like crumb that the cook can fork-fluff and toss with a dressing (the canonical West African thiakry preparation); cooked with a 1:3 ratio it produces a soft porridge or polenta-style mass (Chinese xiaomi zhou, South Indian ragi mudde, West African ogi). The structural component is the kernel starch, mostly amylopectin in pearl and foxtail millet, which gelatinizes at 65 to 75 °C and absorbs water steadily over the cook. Because millet contains no gluten, the cooked grain has none of the chew or cohesion of wheat-based grains; it reads light and crumbly when cooked dry, smooth and creamy when cooked wet. The lipid fraction is higher than rice or wheat, which is why millet flours and pre-cooked millet have a shorter shelf life and a tendency to turn rancid faster.

  Heat and time. The defining inflection points: a 1 to 2 minute dry-toast in oil or in a dry pan before adding water develops the popcorn-aromatic pyrazines that distinguish a pilaf-style millet from a boiled-in-water millet; the same toasted-grain chemistry that drives rice pilaf and farro pilaf applies directly. Cooked covered at a bare simmer for 18 to 22 minutes (pearl or foxtail), the kernels fully hydrate and split. A 10-minute rest off heat with the lid on lets the grain steam-finish and the crumb separates cleanly when forked. Cooked uncovered or over too-high heat, the water escapes before the grain finishes and the result is gritty undercooked grain. Overcooked, the kernels burst entirely and the dish slumps into porridge; for couscous-crumb applications, 22 minutes total cook is the upper bound.

  Applications by category. West African grain salads and porridges: Senegalese thiakry (steamed millet couscous tossed with sweetened yogurt or, in savory adaptations, with herbs and fruit), Gambian fura (millet porridge with a fermented-milk sauce), Hausa kunun zaki (sweetened spiced millet drink). South Asian flatbreads and porridges: Indian bajra roti (rolled pearl millet flatbread cooked on a tava, paired with garlic chutney and ghee), ragi mudde (steamed finger millet balls eaten with sambar in Karnataka), bajra khichdi (Rajasthani millet-and-lentil one-pot). East Asian congees and side dishes: Chinese xiaomi zhou (foxtail millet rice porridge eaten at breakfast or for convalescence), millet-rice blends in everyday rice cookery, glutinous millet (Setaria italica var. glutinosa) for Korean ogokbap and Chinese dim sum fillings. Western health-food applications: millet pilaf, millet-and-bean bowls, millet flour in gluten-free baking. The grain is shared; the cuisine frames change.

  Toasting. Raw millet benefits from a 90 to 120 second dry-toast in a film of oil or in a bare dry pan over medium heat before adding water; the kernels darken one shade and the smell turns distinctly popcorn-grain. The toast is the difference between a pilaf-style millet that reads aromatic and complex and a boiled-in-water millet that reads cleanly cooked but flat. The same technique applies to West African thiakry preparation (where the steamed millet is sometimes briefly toasted first), Indian bajra (where bajra is dry-toasted in ghee before adding water for khichdi), and Chinese xiaomi (where rinsed grains can be toasted in oil before water for a more aromatic congee).

  Buying notes. Pearl millet at North American supermarkets is most commonly labeled simply "millet" and sold in 1-lb (450 g) bags in the bulk or grain section; the kernels are golden-yellow, roughly 2 mm across. Foxtail millet is sold under the Chinese name (xiaomi) at East Asian groceries; the kernels are smaller and a paler yellow. Finger millet (ragi) is sold whole or as flour at South Asian groceries; the whole kernels are reddish-brown and smaller than pearl. The three are not direct interchanges: pearl gives the lightest crumb, foxtail is slightly stickier, finger is denser and earthier. For a thiakry-style grain salad, pearl is the correct choice; for porridge, any of the three works with a water-ratio adjustment.

  Gluten-free reliability. Millet itself is gluten-free, but cross-contamination during milling and packaging is common in facilities that also process wheat. For celiac diners, buy certified gluten-free millet (Bob's Red Mill, Arrowhead Mills, or any package marked "certified gluten-free"). The grain is not a high-allergen ingredient otherwise.

storage: |
  Raw whole millet kernels store best sealed in glass or zip-top bags in a cool dry pantry. Pantry: 6 to 9 months; the lipid fraction begins to go rancid faster than wheat or rice. Refrigerated: 1 year. Frozen: 18 months. The cue for rancidity is smell; fresh millet smells faintly sweet and grain-clean, rancid millet smells of old paint or stale crayons (the same volatile compounds as rancid nuts). Cooked millet refrigerates 3 to 4 days in an airtight container; freezing cooked millet works in 1-cup portions for up to 2 months, with the texture holding well to a 45-second microwave reheat. Millet flour is even more lipid-vulnerable; refrigerate or freeze and use within 3 months. Discard any millet, whole or flour, that smells off.

substitutions:
  - for: 'millet (couscous-crumb application, grain salad, pilaf)'
    use: 'fonio, or quinoa, or coarse semolina couscous'
    note: 'Fonio (the West African ancient grain) is the closest cultural and textural analog and cooks in 5 minutes; quinoa is the most accessible Western substitute and reads similarly fluffy and gluten-free; coarse semolina couscous is wheat-based and the wrong choice for celiac diners but the correct choice for cooks who want the lightest, most particulate crumb. The dish remains structurally honest with any of the three.'
  - for: 'millet (porridge or congee application)'
    use: 'short-grain rice, or polenta, or oat groats'
    note: 'For a thicker porridge that holds its shape; cook at 1:4 ratio for 25 to 30 minutes. The flavor shifts (rice is cleaner, polenta is corn-sweet, oats are nuttier) but the structural role is preserved. For Chinese xiaomi zhou specifically, no substitute reproduces the corn-popcorn aromatic of foxtail millet; the dish becomes a different congee.'
  - for: 'pearl millet flour (bajra flour, for roti)'
    use: 'jowar (sorghum) flour, or a 50/50 blend of jowar and millet flours, or atta flour'
    note: 'Jowar flour is the closest gluten-free analog and the canonical alternative in Indian flatbread cookery; the texture of the rolled flatbread is slightly more delicate. Atta flour (whole wheat) introduces gluten and produces a more pliable roti at the cost of celiac safety and the distinctive earthy flavor of bajra.'
