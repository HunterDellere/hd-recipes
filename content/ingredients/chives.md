about: |
  Chives are the youngest leaves of Allium schoenoprasum, the smallest cultivated species in the onion genus. The plant grows as a clump of thin (2 to 4 mm diameter) hollow tubular green leaves rising 15 to 30 cm from a bulb cluster; the cook harvests the leaves at 10 to 20 cm length and discards the bulb (the bulb is too small to be useful as a cooking onion). The chemistry is the same allium-sulfur volatiles family as yellow onion and garlic (alliinase enzymes liberate sulfenic acids when the leaf is cut, releasing the characteristic mild oniony aroma), but the concentration is dramatically lower than in onion bulb tissue, which is why chives read as a clean delicate allium note rather than the pungent assault of raw chopped onion.

  The aromatic compounds are volatile and water-soluble; chives lose roughly half their aroma within 10 minutes of cutting at room temperature, and the aroma is fully eliminated by sustained heat above 60 °C. This is structural to how chives are used: never cooked into a long simmer (the aromatic disappears and the green color goes army-drab), always added at the finish or as a raw garnish. The cut chive should hit the plate within 5 to 10 minutes of being cut for peak aromatic delivery; bulk-prepped chives held in the refrigerator for hours read flat and grassy.

  Cooking applications by category. Finishing herb: thin-sliced chives (1 to 2 mm rounds) scattered across the finished plate as a final layer; canonical on baked potatoes with sour cream, on potato gratin, on omelets, on roasted vegetables, on cold soups (vichyssoise, gazpacho). German potato salad and any cream-or-vinegar-based composed salad: chives sliced into the dressing at the finish, never cooked in; they read against the sharper onion and vinegar with a brighter, fresher allium register. Compound butter and herb sauces: chopped chives folded into softened butter (with parsley, tarragon, lemon zest) to produce the canonical French fines herbes butter for fish and poultry; the chives must be added off-heat. Crème fraîche / sour cream / yogurt dressings: 2 to 5 g chives per 100 g dairy produces the canonical "chive sour cream" for blini, latkes, potato pancakes; the dairy lipids hold the volatile aromatics longer than water-based dressings. Garnish for cold preparations: smoked salmon, gravlax, tuna tartare, raw oysters with mignonette — chives are the canonical herb across the Northern European and French cold-preparation register.

  Chives vs green onion vs garlic chives. These three are commonly confused at the supermarket and not interchangeable. Green onion / scallion (Allium fistulosum or young A. cepa) has thicker (5 to 10 mm) tubular green leaves and a small white bulb; the aromatic is closer to fresh onion, more pungent, and the bulb can be cooked into stir-fries or grilled. Chives are smaller, more delicate, and exclusively a leaf herb; substituting scallion for chives produces a coarser, more aggressive dish (the volume of vegetable matter is wrong and the flavor reads too oniony). Garlic chives / Chinese chives (Allium tuberosum) are flat (not hollow) green leaves with a distinctly garlic-and-onion-hybrid aromatic; canonical in Chinese dumpling fillings and Korean buchu pancakes, not a substitute for chives in Western applications. The hollow tube and the mild onion aromatic distinguish A. schoenoprasum chives from both.

  Buying. Fresh chives are sold in supermarket produce sections in small plastic clamshells or tied bunches, 15 to 30 g per pack; runs $2 to $5. Look for chives that are uniform deep green, not yellowing at the cut end, and have not gone slimy at the base (water damage in the pack). The bunch should smell faintly oniony when handled; chives with no smell are old. Specialty markets and farmers' markets sell larger bunches at lower per-gram cost and the chives are dramatically fresher; a 50 to 75 g farmers' market bunch is often $3 and lasts a week. Growing chives at home is the right move for any cook who uses them regularly; the plant is hardy, perennial, and tolerates almost any garden or container with sun, providing fresh chives 9 months of the year in temperate climates.

  Substitution with scallion green tops is acceptable in informal cooking and approximates the chive function reasonably well when the scallion is sliced thinly (1 to 2 mm rounds) and used only the green tops. The dish reads slightly coarser and more onion-forward; in delicate French and Northern European applications the substitution is noticeable, in casual home cooking it works.

storage: 'Refrigerator, in the original clamshell or in a damp paper towel rolled in a zip bag with air, in the produce drawer. Fresh chives keep 5 to 10 days at 4 °C; the cut ends begin to yellow first and the slime at the base develops past 7 days. Failure modes: yellowing tips (trim and use the green portion), slime at base (discard the slimy portion), uniform yellow-green throughout (past use, discard). Freezing is acceptable for cooking applications: cut chives into 2 to 3 mm rounds, lay flat on a parchment-lined sheet pan, freeze 30 minutes, transfer to a zip bag and freeze up to 4 months; use directly from frozen in cooked applications. Frozen chives read flatter than fresh; the volatile aromatics partially survive freezing but the texture goes limp on thawing. Drying chives is not effective; the dried product has almost no aromatic and reads as green grass dust.'

    use: 'green onion / scallion tops, very thinly sliced (1 mm rounds), green portion only'
    note: 'The closest available everyday substitute and an acceptable swap in casual cooking. The dish reads slightly more onion-forward and the scallion tops are textually coarser; for delicate French or Northern European applications the substitution is meaningfully noticeable. Use 75 percent of the chive mass to compensate for the stronger aromatic.'
  - for: 'chives (when the cook wants a milder herb finish)'
    use: 'fresh parsley, finely chopped, plus a small dab of garlic confit'
    note: 'Different aromatic register entirely; the parsley provides the green herbal finish and the garlic confit provides the mild allium note without the chive sharpness. Works in French and Mediterranean register; not a substitute in dishes where chives are structural (chive sour cream for blini, German potato salad finish).'
  - for: 'chives (when fresh is unavailable)'
    use: 'frozen chives, used directly from frozen'
    note: 'Frozen chives retain most of the aromatic but go textually limp on thaw. Acceptable in cooked-into-sauce applications and dairy dressings; less successful as a visible-garnish scattering where the limp texture reads as wilted. Avoid freeze-dried chives; the aromatic does not survive.'
