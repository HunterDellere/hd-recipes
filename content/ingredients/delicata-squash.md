about: |
  Delicata's signature is its thin, edible skin; unlike butternut or kabocha, you don't peel it. The skin contributes a slight savory caramelization when roasted that the flesh alone doesn't carry. Cut lengthwise, scoop seeds with a spoon, then slice into 1 cm half-moons.

  Flavor sits in a sweet-chestnut zone, with a faintly vanilla-floral top note when fully roasted. The flesh is drier and starchier than butternut and browns harder under high heat; a 230°C / 450°F oven for 18–22 minutes, or a hot dry pan for 4–5 minutes per side, is the sweet spot. Sugar concentration in the flesh is high enough that you'll get visible Maillard browning at the edges within 90 seconds of pan contact.

  Choose squashes that feel heavy for their size, with cream-colored skin and dark green stripes; avoid any with soft spots or wrinkly skin (those have lost moisture and will be cottony). Sometimes called "sweet potato squash"; it's not, but it's in that flavor neighborhood.

  Available roughly September through February in temperate climates; the flesh sweetens during 1–2 weeks of curing post-harvest, so very fresh-picked delicata can taste flat compared to one that's sat on a counter for 10 days.

seasonality: 'Late summer through early winter (Sept–Feb US). Cures and sweetens during storage; supermarket stock late in the season is often the best-eating.'
storage: 'Cool dry pantry, 4–6 weeks whole. Once cut, refrigerated, 4 days.'

    note: 'Closest flavor (sweet, chestnut-y) but the skin is too tough to leave on. Peel before slicing; the Maillard depth still comes through at the cut surface.'
  - for: 'delicata squash (purées, soups, where texture matters less)'
    use: 'butternut squash, peeled and cubed'
    note: 'More water, less concentrated sweetness. Fine for blending; underwhelming in roasts where edge-char is the goal.'
  - for: 'delicata squash (general winter-squash sweetness, fall menus)'
    use: 'red kuri or honeynut squash'
    note: 'Both are sweeter and denser than butternut. Honeynut is the closest one-to-one in flavor; red kuri runs nuttier and starchier.'
