about: |
  Jameed (جميد) is sheep or goat yogurt that has been strained, salted heavily, and air-dried into rocks or cones until it loses essentially all of its free water. Goat-milk jameed is the Bedouin and southern-Jordanian standard; sheep-milk is the broader Levantine norm; cow-milk versions exist commercially but read flatter on the palate. The drying takes one to several weeks under sun and breeze, during which lactic and propionic fermentation continues at a slow temperature-limited rate, deepening the sourness and generating short-chain volatile acids that read funky-cheesy on the nose. Salt cures alongside the dehydration, ending up in the 8–12% range by mass once the stones are dry.

  The chemistry of why it does what it does. Live yogurt is roughly 88% water; jameed is around 5–10%. Removing free water concentrates lactose-derived lactic acid (so the pH drops well below fresh yogurt, into the 3.5–4.0 range when reconstituted), concentrates whey proteins and casein fragments, and concentrates the glutamate liberated by enzymatic protein breakdown during the long drying ferment. The functional consequence is that a small mass of reconstituted jameed delivers more sourness, more salt, and more savory depth than any live yogurt at any concentration. It also behaves differently in heat: the casein has already partially hydrolyzed during the slow ferment, so it tolerates simmering far better than fresh yogurt, which curdles aggressively above ~85 °C without stabilizers.

  Where it fits in cooking. Jameed exists for one canonical role: the sauce that bathes lamb mansaf, Jordan's national dish and the wider Bedouin and Palestinian tradition. It also appears in shakriyeh-style yogurt-stewed lamb across the Levant, in some Iraqi and Bedouin pilafs as a sour-savory broth component, and as a stand-alone broth (in southern Jordan, jameed broth is sometimes served on bread as a humble meal of its own). Outside this narrow regional band it is essentially unused, which is one reason it has no clean Western analog.

  Reconstitution. The stones are too hard to break with a knife. Two approaches. Cold soak: cover the stones with water (roughly 4 parts water to 1 part stone by mass) and refrigerate 12–24 hours. The stones soften from outside in, dissolving fully into a chalky off-white slurry by the end. Hot reduction: simmer the stones in water until they break down completely (45–90 minutes depending on size), stirring and crushing with a wooden spoon. Either way, blend the slurry smooth and pass through a fine-mesh sieve to catch grit and any undissolved fragments. The strained liquid is the working ingredient. Yield is roughly 1.2–1.5 L of finished sauce base per 250 g of dry jameed.

  Working with the reconstituted liquid. It looks and smells startling on first encounter: pasty white, sharply sour, deeply funky, salty enough that it should not be tasted straight. That is correct. The flavor lands when it is integrated into a hot lamb-stock sauce, where the funk reads as savory depth rather than off-note and the salt seasons the whole pot. Always temper jameed liquid into a hot dish gradually with constant stirring (see techniques/cooked-yogurt-sauce); even though the casein is far more heat-stable than fresh yogurt, dumping cold jameed slurry into a rolling boil can still cause the proteins to clump.

  Buying notes. Karawan, Halaby, Al Wadi al Akhdar, and AlAmir are the brands most reliably found at Middle Eastern groceries in North America and Europe. Jordanian-origin product (the village of Al-Karak is the center of southern Jordanian production) is the benchmark, with Bedouin goat-milk jameed considered the highest grade. The stones should be off-white to tan, dry to the touch, faintly chalky, with no soft spots and no mold. Black or pink discoloration is rejection-worthy. Powdered jameed exists as a convenience product and works in a pinch, but it has been milled from already-dried stones and tends to read flatter than rehydrating the whole stones from scratch.

  How it differs from fresh yogurt and labneh. Fresh yogurt is roughly 88% water and pH 4.4; jameed is 5–10% water and pH ~3.7 reconstituted, with several times the salt and a fundamentally different volatile profile from the long air-ferment. Labneh (strained yogurt) sits between them functionally but stops well short — it is still hydrated, still mild, still subject to immediate curdling in heat. None of these substitute cleanly for jameed in a mansaf-style sauce. Doctored fresh yogurt with cornstarch and lemon and salt approximates the texture and the sourness but never the funk; this is a working compromise for cooks who cannot source jameed, not an equivalence.

storage: 'Sealed in a dry pantry away from heat and light. The unopened bag keeps a year or more. Failure mode is a soft, damp, or off-color spot on a stone, which signals moisture intrusion and mold; discard the affected stone, inspect the rest. Once reconstituted into a slurry or broth, refrigerate in a sealed container; the liquid keeps 4–5 days at 4 °C and freezes well for 3 months. Frozen jameed broth thaws clean and re-emulsifies on a gentle warm whisk.'
