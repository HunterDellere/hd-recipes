about: |
  Cow''s milk reduced to about 40% of its original volume, no sugar added, then sterilized inside the sealed can. The faint cooked-caramel flavor comes from Maillard reactions between the lactose and milk proteins during that in-can sterilization — it''s a feature, not a defect, and it''s why evaporated milk reads richer than fresh milk reduced at home.

  In Thai iced tea it carries fat and dairy body without the sugar load that condensed milk would bring. The cook controls sweetness separately. This is also why it shows up in pumpkin pie, tres leches, and many Filipino and Mexican desserts where fresh cream wasn''t historically available; it does cream''s job at room-temperature shelf life.

  Reconstitutes 1:1 with water for "regular" whole milk in a pinch. The result is slightly cooked-tasting but functional in baking.

  Don''t confuse with sweetened condensed milk. Same starting material, very different ingredients — evaporated has no sugar, condensed is ~45% sugar by weight.

storage: 'Pantry indefinitely unopened. Once opened, transfer to a sealed jar and refrigerate; keeps ~5 days. The cooked flavor intensifies after opening as it oxidizes.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'evaporated milk'
    use: 'half-and-half'
    note: 'Closest match in fat behavior and body. Slightly more fat (~10–12% vs ~7–8%) and lacks the cooked-milk flavor: fine for sauces, slightly different in Thai iced tea.'
  - for: 'evaporated milk'
    use: 'whole milk reduced by 50% on the stovetop'
    note: 'Same reduction the can does, minus the in-can sterilization. Lacks the Maillard depth: closer to plain concentrated milk than to canned evaporated.'
