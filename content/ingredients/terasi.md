about: |
  Terasi (Indonesian) and belacan (Malay) are functionally identical: tiny shrimp or krill salted and fermented for weeks to months, then sun-dried and compressed into dense blocks. The raw paste smells aggressively fishy and ammonia-tinged; toasting flips it into a deep, savory-funky umami that bears no resemblance to the raw block. The transformation is the entire reason to use it; raw terasi tastes like ruined seafood.

  Sold in 100 to 250 g bricks wrapped in paper or plastic. Color ranges from pinkish-grey (younger, milder) to deep purple-brown (older, more pungent). Indonesian brands favor a denser, more compressed block; Malaysian belacan is often slightly looser and more pungent. Either works across the maritime Southeast Asian canon.

  Always toast before use. Three methods. Wrap a teaspoon-sized piece in foil and dry-fry over medium heat 2 to 3 minutes per side until fragrant and slightly puffed. Or toast directly on a piece of foil over an open flame, 30 seconds per side. Or pound it into a spice paste and fry the entire paste in oil, the terasi cooks out as the paste blooms. The toasted version contributes deep umami with a faint smoky-cured note; the raw never integrates and stays harsh.

  Functions as the glutamate engine of Indonesian bumbu and Malaysian rempah spice pastes. A pea-sized piece (about 3 g) seasons a full curry for 4 people; the goal is background depth, not foreground fish flavor.

storage: 'Wrap tightly in foil or place in an airtight jar after opening; refrigerate for up to a year. Smells strong enough to perfume an entire fridge, double-bag it. Freezes indefinitely.'
