about: |
  Chaat masala is a North Indian and Pakistani finishing spice blend, named for its primary application on chaat (the family of Indian street snacks ; dahi puri, papdi chaat, aloo tikki, fruit chaat). The blend is built around two structural components that together produce its signature flavor: kala namak (Himalayan black salt) and amchur (dried unripe mango powder). Kala namak is rock salt mined in the Sulaiman range and traditionally fired with charcoal-and-harad-fruit-and-bark, which converts trace iron and sodium into sulfur compounds (predominantly hydrogen sulfide and pyrite-derived iron sulfides) that lend the salt a faintly sulfurous-egg aroma. Amchur is sour and citric/malic-acid-forward, made from dried green mango ground to a tan powder. Together they produce a sulfurous-saline-sour profile that no Western spice blend approximates ; chaat masala is, functionally, the molecular reason chaat tastes like chaat.

  The supporting cast varies by region and brand but typically includes toasted cumin and coriander seeds (the warm-earthy backbone), ajwain or carom seeds (a thymol-bearing seed close to dried oregano in aroma), ginger powder, asafoetida (hing, the sulfurous-savory umami enhancer), ground black pepper, and a small amount of mild ground chili. Some blends add tartaric acid (anardana, dried pomegranate seed) for additional sourness or mango-fruity sweetness from extra amchur. The common axis across all legitimate chaat masala blends is the kala-namak-and-amchur backbone; a "chaat masala" without kala namak is mislabeled.

  Chaat masala is a finishing spice, not a cooking spice. The volatile aromatics ; kala namak's hydrogen sulfide, amchur's malic acid, ajwain's thymol ; all degrade rapidly above 80 to 100 °C and are gone within 2 minutes of being added to a hot pan. The blend is added off-heat or sprinkled over already-plated food, the same way you would treat a finishing salt or a furikake. Cooking chaat masala into a curry destroys its identity and wastes the spice; for in-cooking applications, garam masala (a different blend, with cinnamon, cloves, cardamom, bay, and sometimes black cardamom) is the right tool.

  Where chaat masala does its work. Sprinkled on chopped fruit (mango, guava, pineapple, watermelon ; the classic Indian fruit chaat) where the kala namak and amchur amplify the natural sweet-and-sour of the fruit. On lassi and yogurt drinks (a pinch in a savory lassi gives the chaat-shop kick). Folded into kachumber and other raw chopped salads as the structural seasoning. On corn-on-the-cob brushed with butter, on fries dressed with cilantro chutney, on boiled chickpeas, on roasted papad. As the finishing spice on aloo tikki, dahi vada, papdi chaat, bhel puri, sev puri ; the entire chaat family. Across Pakistani cooking it appears on grilled mango slices and in chaat-style salads served alongside biryani.

  The Western drift. Many Western recipe writers translate chaat masala as "Indian spice blend" or substitute garam masala, which is wrong on both counts. Garam masala is a warm-cooking blend (added during the bhuna step or as a finishing pinch in a curry); chaat masala is a sour-sulfurous finishing blend for raw or near-raw applications. The two are not interchangeable, and no recipe that calls for one is improved by the other. The substitution shorthand below covers what to do when chaat masala is genuinely unavailable.

  Buying notes. At any Indian grocer, chaat masala is sold by brands like MDH, Everest, Shan, Badshah, and Catch, in 100 g packets and 200 g jars at the $2 to $5 price point. The kala namak content is the differentiator across brands; MDH and Shan lean heavier on kala namak (more sulfurous, more aggressive), Everest is milder and more cumin-forward. All commercial blends lose their volatile top notes within 6 to 9 months of grinding; the homemade version (toasted whole spices ground fresh, see content/recipes/chaat-masala.md) has a vivid sulfur-sour aromatic that no store-bought tin can match.

storage: |
  Sealed in a glass jar at room temperature, away from heat and light: 6 to 9 months for the kala namak and amchur top notes, 12 to 18 months for the cumin-and-coriander backbone. The blend's volatile aromatics escape the powder steadily; after a year the hydrogen-sulfide note from kala namak is noticeably muted. Refrigeration is unnecessary and introduces moisture risk that clumps the powder. Off-cue is loss of the sulfurous-egg note; a chaat masala that smells only of cumin has lost its identity and should be replaced.
