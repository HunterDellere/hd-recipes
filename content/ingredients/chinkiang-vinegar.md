about: |
  Chinkiang vinegar is fermented from glutinous rice and aged for months (sometimes years) in earthenware jars. The result is dark like soy sauce, with a complex malty smokiness and only modest acidity (around 4–5%, less sharp than white wine vinegar).

  It''s the canonical dipping vinegar for Chinese dumplings (often paired with ginger threads), the acid in dandan noodles, and the brightener in cold dishes like smashed cucumbers. It does not work as a 1:1 sub for rice vinegar — chinkiang is much more flavorful and should be used where you want that depth.

  Look for "Gold Plum" brand at any well-stocked Chinese grocery. The label often reads "Chinkiang Vinegar" or "镇江香醋."
