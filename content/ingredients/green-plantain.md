about: |
  Green plantains are the unripe fruit of Musa paradisiaca, a cooking banana cultivar distinct from the dessert banana (Musa acuminata). Composition is roughly 65% water, 32% carbohydrate (almost entirely starch, only 2 to 3% sugar at the green stage), 1.3% protein, 0.4% fat. The skin is thick, fibrous, and adheres tightly to the flesh; the flesh is pale yellow, dense, and astringent from tannins that fade as the fruit ripens.

  Functional category: structural starch. Green plantains cook like a starchy root vegetable (potato, yuca, malanga) rather than like a fruit. Frying converts the surface starches through Maillard browning while the interior stays dense; smashing while hot and re-frying (tostones, mofongo) produces a two-stage crust where the second fry crisps the broken surface area exposed by the smash. The starch is also high in resistant starch (12 to 15% by weight at the green stage), which survives cooking and feeds colonic fermentation; this is why plantain-heavy diets correlate with distinctive gut microbiota profiles in West African and Caribbean populations.

  Ripeness as a continuum. Plantains progress from solid green (deep starchy, no sweetness, hard flesh) to yellow with green tips (transitional, some sweetness emerging) to yellow with brown speckles (sweet, softer) to nearly black (deeply sweet, soft, caramelized when fried, the maduros stage). Green-stage cooking is structurally different from ripe-stage cooking and the dishes are not interchangeable; tostones and mofongo demand green, maduros demand near-black, and the in-between stages produce neither dish well.

  Peeling green plantains is the kitchen friction point. Unlike bananas, the skin will not peel back from a stem cut. The reliable method: cut off both ends, score the skin lengthwise in 3 or 4 places (only through the peel, not into the flesh), soak in warm salted water 5 minutes, then pry the skin off in strips with a paring knife or thumbnail. Without the soak the skin tears in patches; with the soak it lifts cleanly.

  Applications by category. Caribbean: tostones (twice-fried green plantain rounds), mofongo (fried green plantain mashed with garlic and chicharrón), patacones (Colombian/Venezuelan tostones). West African: kelewele, ampesi, plantain chips. South American: chifles (Ecuadorian fried plantain chips).

storage: |
  Counter at room temperature; do not refrigerate green plantains (the cold halts ripening and produces black skin discoloration without sweetness conversion). Green plantains hold 1 to 2 weeks at room temperature; they ripen slowly to yellow over that window. To slow ripening (keep them green for tostones or mofongo), refrigerate only after the fruit has been peeled and cooked; raw green refrigeration ruins the texture. Failure modes: soft mushy spots through the peel (rotting; cut away the affected area and use the rest immediately, or discard if extensive); mold at the stem end (discard).
