about: |
  Smoked paprika is dried, smoked, and ground red pepper. The defining production region is La Vera in Cáceres, Spain; pimentón de la Vera carries Protected Designation of Origin (D.O.P.) status, meaning only paprika produced in La Vera using the traditional method can use the name. The traditional method: ripe red bola or jaranda pepper varieties (Capsicum annuum) are harvested in autumn, hung in smokehouses called secaderos over slow oak (typically holm oak, Quercus ilex) fires, smoke-dried for 10 to 15 days at low temperature (40 to 50 °C), then stone-ground to a fine powder. The slow oak smoking is the entire technique; faster industrial drying or non-oak smoking produces a different and lesser product.

  The smoke compounds are the flavor. Slow oak-smoking infuses the pepper with phenolic compounds (guaiacol, syringol, 4-methylguaiacol, eugenol, vanillin) that are the same compounds responsible for the smoke aromatic in chipotle peppers, smoked salt, and smoked meats. The pepper itself contributes the deep red color (carotenoids: capsanthin and capsorubin, the same compounds that give all paprika its red color), the sweetness from residual fructose in the dried pepper, and a small to large capsaicin load depending on the grade.

  Three main grades. Pimentón dulce (sweet) uses non-spicy pepper varieties and reads as the smoke-aromatic-and-sweet-pepper register without heat; it is the most versatile grade and the workhorse of Spanish cooking. Pimentón agridulce (bittersweet, also "semi-spicy") uses a mix of varieties and reads slightly hot. Pimentón picante (hot) uses spicy pepper varieties and reads moderately hot in addition to smoky-sweet. For most applications, dulce is the right default; picante is used when the dish wants both smoke and heat from a single ingredient. Hungarian paprika, by contrast, is typically not smoked (most Hungarian production uses sun-drying or hot-air drying, not wood smoke) and contributes a sweet pepper note without the smoke; using Hungarian paprika in a recipe that calls for smoked paprika produces a dish that reads thinner on the smoke axis.

  Spanish vs Mexican smoke chemistry. The smoke in pimentón de la Vera and the smoke in Mexican chipotle in adobo come from similar wood-smoke chemistry but different woods (oak vs mesquite/pecan/hickory in Mexican production) and different application methods (powder dried over fire vs whole peppers smoke-cured to dehydration). The aromatics align but are not identical; pimentón reads slightly more European-savory and chipotle reads slightly more piney-resinous. The two work well together in dishes that want layered smoke (chorizo-and-chipotle stew, smoked-paprika-and-chipotle vinaigrette).

  Cooking behavior. Smoked paprika should be added to a dish in fat over moderate (not high) heat; the carotenoids that carry the color and flavor are fat-soluble, and they bloom into the cooking oil within 30 to 60 seconds at 150 to 175 °C. High heat (above 200 °C) scorches the powder and produces bitter, acrid flavors; this is the same scorch curve as ground cumin and most powdered spices. The standard Spanish technique for sofrito or paella base is to bloom the paprika in olive oil after the onion has softened but before any liquid is added, agitating constantly for 15 to 20 seconds, then immediately adding the next wet ingredient (tomato, broth) to halt the cook. The dish takes on a deep red-orange color from the bloom and a pronounced smoke-aromatic.

  Applications by category. Spanish: chorizo (the cured sausage that defines Spanish charcuterie, colored and flavored almost entirely by pimentón), paella (in the sofrito base), patatas bravas (in the bravas sauce), pulpo a la gallega (sprinkled over octopus with olive oil), romesco (the Catalan red pepper sauce). North African: in some Moroccan tagines and dry rubs, especially in dishes influenced by the Moorish-Spanish cross-cultural exchange. American adoptions: barbecue rubs and sauces (where the smoke-aromatic supplements actual smoking), deviled eggs, vinaigrettes, roasted vegetables, grilled and roasted chicken. Mexican-American: as a layer in chipotle-and-paprika dressings (this recipe), as a stand-in for chipotle in lower-heat applications. The reach is wide; smoked paprika is one of the most cost-effective pantry purchases for adding smoke without owning a smoker.

  Buying. Pimentón de la Vera is widely distributed through US supermarkets in tin cans (250 g) and small jars; La Chinata, La Dalia, and Carmencita are common authentic Spanish brands and all are reliable. Avoid generic "smoked paprika" without the D.O.P. designation; some industrial-smoked products use liquid smoke or shorter smoking times and produce a thinner aromatic. Premium small-producer Spanish brands at specialty groceries are higher quality but the difference is more pronounced in dishes where the paprika is the dominant flavor (paella, romesco) than in dishes where it is one layer among several.

storage: |
  Sealed in the original tin or in glass at room temperature, 1 year for peak flavor; 18 months usable but progressively muted. Cool dry pantry; refrigeration is unnecessary and introduces moisture risk. The carotenoid pigments fade with light exposure; tin storage is preferable to clear glass for long-term flavor preservation. Failure mode is loss of aromatic and color (smell-test before use; the powder should smell pronouncedly smoky-and-sweet-pepper, not flat-and-dusty). The powder does not go rancid but loses culinary value as the volatile compounds escape.
