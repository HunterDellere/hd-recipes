about: |
  Almonds (Prunus dulcis, the sweet almond; the bitter almond is a separate cultivar with toxic levels of amygdalin and not the same culinary ingredient) are the seed of the almond tree. Composition is roughly 50 percent fat (mostly oleic and linoleic acid), 21 percent protein, 22 percent carbohydrate (mostly fiber), with significant vitamin E, magnesium, and calcium. The skin is bitter-tannic; for refined applications it is removed by blanching (60 seconds in boiling water, then slipping the skin off by hand).

  Functional category: structural sauce-thickener and textural-component nut. In Catalan and Spanish cooking, ground almonds are the body of picada (the dry-toasted nut-and-bread thickener stirred into stews at the end) and salsa romesco (the canonical Catalan red-pepper-and-almond sauce). In Andalusian cooking, almonds are the structural ingredient in ajoblanco (the cold white almond-and-garlic soup) and the body of salsa de almendras (the almond-tomato-saffron sauce for albóndigas). In Middle Eastern cooking, almonds are the canonical garnish for rice pilafs and Persian khoresht. In Mughlai cooking, almond paste is the structural thickener for korma and pasanda. In Mediterranean baking, ground almonds are the structural body of frangipane, financiers, and macarons.

  Form-by-application. Whole raw almonds (skin-on) for snacking and garnish; the skin adds tannic complexity. Whole blanched almonds (skin-off) for sauce thickeners where bitterness would intrude; toasting deepens flavor. Slivered or sliced for textural garnish (rice pilafs, salads, baked goods). Almond flour (finely ground, skin-off) for structural baking; the fat content makes it tender but it lacks gluten and cannot replace wheat flour 1:1 in most bread or pastry. Almond meal (coarser, often skin-on) for rustic baking and as a coating for cutlets. Marcona almonds (Spanish variety, rounder and slightly sweeter, sold roasted in oil with salt) as a tapas almond.

  Toasting. Almonds bloom dramatically when dry-toasted: 350°F oven for 8 to 12 minutes (until pale golden and intensely aromatic), or in a dry skillet over medium heat 4 to 6 minutes with constant agitation. Skin-on almonds toast slightly more aromatically; skin-off blanched almonds toast to a more refined nutty flavor without the tannic background. Toasted almonds are the structural baseline for most savory applications; raw almonds in a sauce read flat and chalky.

  Buying. Look for almonds at any supermarket; bulk-bin almonds from a high-turnover store are typically fresher than packaged. Whole raw with the skin on (the most versatile form), blanched whole (the second most useful), and slivered (specifically for textural garnish) are the three forms most worth keeping in the pantry. Almond flour is widely available at supermarkets and is essential for gluten-free baking. The fresh-almond smell should be clean and faintly sweet; rancid almonds smell of cardboard or old paint.

storage: |
  Sealed container in the pantry, 6 months at peak; the high oil content means almonds go rancid relatively quickly. Refrigerated, 12 months. Frozen (in a zip-top bag, no need to thaw before use), 24 months. Sliced and slivered almonds go rancid faster than whole almonds due to the higher surface-area-to-volume ratio; buy in small quantities and store in the freezer. Toasted almonds (cooled, sealed) keep 2 weeks at room temperature; the toasting accelerates oxidation slightly. Failure modes: rancid cardboard smell (oxidized, discard), visible mold (discard), bitter aftertaste (rancidity, discard).
