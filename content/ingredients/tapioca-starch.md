about: |
  Tapioca starch is nearly 100% amylopectin (branched starch) with very little amylose (linear starch). Amylopectin gelatinizes into a clear, glossy, slightly stretchy gel rather than the opaque, sliceable gel cornstarch produces. This is why a cornstarch-thickened sauce sets soft and cloudy while a tapioca-thickened one stays glassy and ropy, and why tapioca pearls bite chewy where a cornstarch pearl would crumble.

  Gelatinization begins around 62°C and is essentially complete by 72°C, but the chewy "Q" texture characteristic of Taiwanese boba and Vietnamese banh lot only develops when hydrated starch is then kneaded into a dough using boiling water. The hot water pre-gelatinizes a fraction of the starch on contact, which acts as a binder during kneading; the rest gelatinizes during the boil. Cold-water doughs do not work for boba.

  Sold as fine white powder under names "tapioca starch," "tapioca flour," and (confusingly) "cassava starch", all identical for cooking purposes. Distinguish from "cassava flour" (whole ground root, includes fiber, will not behave the same way), which is a separate ingredient.

  As a sauce thickener it is shear-thin: vigorous whisking after gelatinization breaks the network and the sauce thins. Stir gently once thickened. Freeze-thaw is poor; tapioca-thickened sauces weep on thaw where cornstarch holds.

storage: 'Airtight container, cool dry pantry. Indefinitely stable but absorbs ambient odors aggressively, keep away from spices and onions.'
