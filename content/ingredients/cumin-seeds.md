about: |
  Cumin seeds are the dried fruit of Cuminum cyminum, an apiaceae herb cultivated since the Bronze Age. The seeds are small (3 to 5 mm), elongated, ridged, and gray-brown to tan; the aroma when fresh is warm, earthy, slightly bitter with a faint citrus top note from the cuminaldehyde and other terpenoid compounds in the volatile oil. Cumin is the structural tempering spice of South Asian, Middle Eastern (especially Levantine), and Latin American (especially Mexican) cooking. It is not a finishing spice in any of these traditions; cumin earns its work in the first 15 to 30 seconds when the whole seeds hit hot fat or oil and the aromatic compounds dissolve into the cooking medium.

  Whole vs ground. Cumin seeds whole and cumin powder are functionally different ingredients. Whole seeds bloomed in hot fat (a tadka or tempering) release their flavor into the oil, the seeds themselves contributing additional textural and bursting-aroma notes when bitten. Ground cumin behaves more like a base seasoning, contributing background flavor and color without the discrete tempered-seed pop. Many South Asian recipes use both — whole seeds in the tadka at the start, ground cumin in the masala at midpoint — and the two are not interchangeable. Western-grocery-shelf "cumin" is almost always pre-ground; Asian, Mexican, and Middle Eastern groceries reliably stock whole seeds.

  Tempering technique. Heat 1 to 2 tbsp oil or ghee in a small pan or the bottom of the cooking pot until shimmering (around 175 to 200°C / 350 to 390°F). Add 1 to 2 tsp whole cumin seeds. They will hiss, foam slightly, darken from tan to medium-brown, and release a pronounced toasted-cumin aroma within 15 to 25 seconds. Pull immediately when the aroma peaks; another 5 to 10 seconds at this temperature scorches the seeds into bitterness. Cumin seeds in oil go from raw to perfect to scorched in about 30 seconds total; this is the entire tempering window and worth standing over.

  Dishes that depend on cumin. North Indian dal (almost any preparation), pilaf and biryani, jeera rice (the simplest application, cumin tempered in ghee, basmati cooked in the cumin-oil), Pakistani nihari and haleem, Mexican carne adovada and pozole, Lebanese kibbeh and meat fillings, Egyptian dukkah, North African ras el hanout, Greek dolmades. The reach is vast.

  Buying notes. Indian groceries sell cumin in 100 g and 500 g bags, and turnover is high. Whole seeds keep their aroma 1 to 2 years sealed; pre-ground cumin loses half its potency within 6 months. Look for plump tan-brown seeds with a clear cumin smell when the bag is opened. Black cumin (Bunium persicum, also called shahi jeera or kala jeera) is a different and more expensive spice with a smoker, more delicate flavor used in some Mughlai and Kashmiri dishes, not interchangeable with regular cumin.

storage: |
  Whole seeds: sealed in a glass jar at room temperature, 1 to 2 years for peak flavor; usable beyond but progressively muted. Ground cumin: sealed in glass, 6 months for peak; 9 to 12 months usable. Cool dry pantry; refrigeration is unnecessary and introduces moisture risk. Off-cues are loss of aroma (smell-test before use).
