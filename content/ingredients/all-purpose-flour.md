about: |
  All-purpose flour is milled from a blend of hard and soft wheats, sifted to remove the bran and germ, and standardized to a protein content that reads as neither cake nor bread. US bags run 10–12% protein by mass; King Arthur sits near the top of the band (~11.7%), Gold Medal toward the lower end (~10.5%), and supermarket store brands wherever the spec sheet falls. Bleached versions get an oxidative treatment (chlorine gas or benzoyl peroxide) that whitens the flour and weakens gluten, producing a marginally tenderer crumb in cakes; unbleached uses time and air to do the same chemistry slower and reads more wheaty in flavor. European "type 55" is the rough analog and runs in similar territory.

  Protein is the single variable that decides what the flour will do. Two of the proteins, glutenin and gliadin, are dormant in dry flour. Add water and work the mixture, and they cross-link into gluten, a viscoelastic network that traps gas, holds shape, and gives bread its chew. More protein plus more hydration plus more mechanical work means a stronger, chewier final product; less of any of those means a softer, more crumb-tender one. This is why the same bag of flour produces both flatbreads (low hydration, moderate work) and brioche (high hydration, heavy enrichment, long mechanical work) without changing flour, just changing the rest of the recipe.

  Functional role across categories: all-purpose is the spine of nearly every Western baked good that does not specifically demand a single-purpose flour. It thickens roux and béchamel via starch gelatinization (which begins around 60 °C and completes by ~95 °C); it dredges proteins for a Maillard-friendly crust before frying; it dusts work surfaces and laminates pastry; it builds the structure of cookies, quick breads, biscuits, pancakes, batters, and the great middle of cakes. Across Latin American cooking it shows up in tortillas de harina and empanadas; across South Asian cooking it appears as maida in samosas, parathas, and naan-adjacent breads; across Italian and French cooking it is the working flour for fresh pasta, gnocchi, and pâte brisée.

  Mixing technique is half of flour''s behavior. For cookies, quick breads, and any tender bake, mix until the dry streaks just disappear and stop; overmixing develops gluten that the recipe does not want, tightening the crumb. For bread doughs, knead long enough that a stretched piece forms a translucent windowpane without tearing. For laminations and pie crusts, keep the flour cold and the mixing brief; the goal is distinct fat layers in a barely-hydrated dough, not a homogeneous one.

  Measurement integrity matters. A "scoop and level" cup of all-purpose flour can vary ±20% by mass depending on whether the bag is freshly opened, packed from sitting, or fluffed. Recipes calibrated to one density and executed at another are the single most common cause of dry cookies, gummy cakes, and bread that will not rise. Weigh; the conversion is 1 cup ≈ 125 g spooned and leveled, or 130–140 g if you dipped the cup straight into the bag.

  Storage and pest behavior: all-purpose stores well at room temperature for 6–9 months in airtight containers. Whole-wheat and high-extraction flours go rancid in 2–3 months at room temperature because the germ oils oxidize; refrigerate or freeze those. The pest of concern across all flours is the Indian meal moth and the flour weevil; a freezer pass for new bags (48 hours, then back to pantry) sterilizes any eggs that arrived in the bag.

storage: 'Airtight container in a cool dry pantry, away from heat and light. Six to nine months at room temperature. Failure modes: musty smell from oxidation (ditch it; the off-flavor reads through), or live insects (visible webbing, tiny moths flying out when you open the container). Freeze new bags for 48 hours before pantry storage to kill any pest eggs that traveled in the flour.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'all-purpose flour (cookies, brownies, dense bakes)'
    use: 'cake flour'
    note: 'Lower protein (~7–9%); produces a tenderer, more crumbly result. Substitute 1:1 by weight if a softer cookie is desired.'
  - for: 'all-purpose flour (yeasted breads, pizza dough)'
    use: 'bread flour'
    note: 'Higher protein (~12.5–14%); chewier, more elastic, holds gas longer. Substitute 1:1 by weight; expect a longer rise and a tighter crumb. For pasta, swap to 00 flour instead for the silkier hand.'
  - for: 'all-purpose flour (delicate cake, fine crumb)'
    use: 'cake flour with a starch boost'
    note: 'For 1 cup AP, use 1 cup cake flour minus 2 tbsp + 2 tbsp cornstarch. Approximates a slightly lower-protein, higher-starch profile that yields a finer crumb.'
  - for: 'all-purpose flour (gluten-free general purpose)'
    use: '1:1 gluten-free flour blend with binder'
    note: 'Bob''s Red Mill 1:1 or King Arthur Measure for Measure include xanthan or guar gum to mimic gluten elasticity. Functional in cookies, cakes, and quick breads; less reliable in laminated and yeasted bread doughs, where the gluten network is doing structural work.'
  - for: 'all-purpose flour (whole-grain swap, partial)'
    use: 'whole-wheat flour, ≤25% replacement'
    note: 'Adds bran and germ; reads nuttier and absorbs more water. Replacing more than a quarter of the AP without recipe adjustment produces a dense, dry result. For full whole-wheat baking, follow recipes designed for it.'
  - for: 'all-purpose flour (thickening, roux, gravies)'
    use: 'cornstarch'
    note: 'Use half the volume; thickens at lower temperatures (~85 °C vs ~95 °C) and produces a clearer, glossier sauce. Wrong for roux-based sauces where the cooked-flour flavor is part of the dish.'
