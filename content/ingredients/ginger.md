desc: 'Rhizome of Zingiber officinale. Sharp, peppery heat from gingerol when raw; mellower and sweeter (zingerone) when cooked.'

updated: '2026-04-26'

about: |
  Fresh ginger gets its heat from gingerol, a compound that converts to milder zingerone when heated and to sharper shogaol when dried. That''s why fresh, ground, and crystallized ginger taste different even from the same root.

  For raw applications (dressings, ginger tea, salsas) grate finely on a microplane to break the fibers. For braises and stocks, smash a 1-inch knob with the side of a knife and add whole — easier to fish out later.

  Skin is thin and edible. If it bothers you, scrape it off with the edge of a spoon — wastes less than peeling with a knife. Younger ginger (pale skin, pink-tipped, found in Asian groceries) needs no peeling and is milder.

storage: 'Refrigerator, in a paper bag in the crisper, 2–3 weeks. Freezes well: peel, slice, freeze in a bag; grate from frozen straight into pots.'
