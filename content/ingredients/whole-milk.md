about: |
  Whole cow''s milk is roughly 87% water, 3.25% fat, 3.3% protein, and 4.8% lactose, plus trace minerals and fat-soluble vitamins. The protein fraction breaks into casein (about 80%, organized into colloidal micelles that scatter light and give milk its opacity) and whey (about 20%, soluble globulins like lactalbumin and lactoglobulin). Each fraction does a different job in cooking, which is why whole milk shows up in so many dishes that on the surface have nothing in common.

  Fat is the flavor carrier. Vanillin, capsaicin, the terpenes in cinnamon and cardamom, the curcuminoids in turmeric: all are fat-soluble, and infusing them into milk distributes their aroma evenly through whatever the milk is later folded into. This is the mechanism behind steeped milks for custard, panna cotta, and ice cream base; behind milk-and-spice infusions in chai, atole, and Mexican-style horchata; and behind béchamel''s ability to read mild while still carrying nutmeg and bay.

  Casein binds polyphenols. Tannins from over-steeped tea, bitter compounds from coffee, the astringent edge of cocoa: casein wraps around each and softens the bite without actually neutralizing the flavor underneath. The same mechanism explains why milk in tea works, why a splash of cream tames espresso, and why tres leches and pumpkin pie absorb spice without ever tasting harsh.

  Casein also coagulates under acid or heat. Add lemon juice or vinegar to warm milk and the casein micelles destabilize as pH drops below ~4.6, separating into curds (the protein-fat solid) and whey (the watery liquid). This is the mechanism for paneer, ricotta, and queso fresco; reverse it (rennet plus controlled acidification) and you get every aged cheese. Heat alone above 75 °C nudges whey proteins to denature and form a skin on the surface, the same skin a cook scrapes off scalded milk and the same skin that becomes yuba on simmering soy milk.

  Lactose drives dairy browning. The reducing sugar reacts with milk proteins under heat to push Maillard browning, which is why milk-rich doughs (brioche, milk bread, dinner rolls) crust deeper than water-based ones, why dulce de leche develops its caramel from sweetened condensed milk and time, and why a long-simmered béchamel goes ivory rather than staying paper-white.

  Skim and lowfat are not 1:1 substitutes in cooking. Removing the fat removes the flavor carrier, so spiced custards taste flatter and chocolate ganache reads thinner. The proteins set chalkier without the fat lubricating the casein network, so puddings and pastry creams turn dry. The acid-binding effect persists, but the mouthfeel does not.

  Buying notes: ultra-pasteurized (UHT) milk is heated to ~135 °C for a few seconds, which kills bacteria and partially denatures whey proteins; it tastes faintly cooked and shelf-stabilizes for weeks unopened. Standard pasteurized (HTST) milk is heated to ~72 °C for fifteen seconds and lasts about a week refrigerated. UHT works fine for cooking but can falter when whipping or when you want fresh-milk brightness in a custard.

storage: 'Refrigerator at or below 4 °C. UHT keeps 30+ days unopened; standard pasteurized 7–10 days from purchase. Once opened, both run on the standard week. Failure mode is sour or off-smell, with visible curdling once it has tipped. Freezes if you must (texture suffers slightly on thaw and the fat layer separates; fine for cooking, less so for drinking).'

substitutions:
  - for: 'whole milk (custards, pastry cream, set desserts)'
    use: 'half-and-half'
    note: 'Slightly higher fat (~10–12%); richer body, similar setting behavior. Reduce any added cream by an equivalent amount or the dish runs heavy.'
  - for: 'whole milk (cooking, sauces, batters)'
    use: 'evaporated milk + water (1:1 by volume)'
    note: 'Reconstitutes to roughly whole-milk fat ratio with a faint cooked note from the canning sterilization. Works in most savory and sweet applications.'
  - for: 'whole milk (light dilution, beverages, cereal)'
    use: '2% or 1% lowfat milk'
    note: 'Acceptable when the milk is a vehicle, not the flavor. Loses fat-soluble carry; drinks and cereals taste thinner.'
  - for: 'whole milk (bread doughs, milk-rich enriched dough)'
    use: 'water + 2 tbsp / 30 g milk powder per 240 ml'
    note: 'Reconstructs the proteins and lactose that drive browning and softness. Common bakery move when fresh dairy is not reliable.'
  - for: 'whole milk (vegan applications)'
    use: 'oat or soy milk'
    note: 'Soy carries proteins close to dairy and curdles or browns similarly. Oat milk runs sweeter and thinner. Neither carries fat-soluble flavor as well as dairy; expect a flatter spice infusion.'
