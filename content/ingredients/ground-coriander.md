about: |
  Ground coriander is dried coriander seeds (the fruit of Coriandrum sativum, the same plant whose leaves are sold as cilantro in North America and dhania in South Asia) toasted briefly and ground to a fine powder. The aroma is warm, slightly citrusy, faintly floral, and structurally body-building rather than top-note bright. In South Asian cooking, ground coriander is typically used at twice the volume of cumin and three times the volume of garam masala in a base masala; it provides the body that lets chili and cumin and turmeric land without harshness.

  Why coriander is structural, not background. Coriander seeds have a high content of linalool and geraniol, the same monoterpenes that give roses, basil, and bay leaves their floral character. In a curry, these compounds bridge the chili heat (capsaicin), the cumin earthiness, and the turmeric color into a unified flavor; without coriander, the dish reads as separate strident notes layered on top of each other rather than as a coherent flavor. This is why "Indian curry tastes flat" home-cooking results often correct dramatically with more ground coriander; cooks under-use it because it has no obvious leading flavor.

  Whole vs ground. Whole coriander seeds can be toasted and freshly ground; the difference vs supermarket pre-ground is significant. The toasted-fresh-ground powder is more aromatic, slightly floral, and integrates faster into a sauce. Pre-ground coriander loses 50% of its volatile aromatics within 6 months. Indian groceries sell whole seeds and ground; both are valid, but the toasted-fresh-ground option is the upgrade for serious cooking.

  Cooking applications. Bhuna masala (the slow-fried onion-ginger-garlic-tomato base of most North Indian curries) — 2 to 3 tsp ground coriander is typical for a 4-serving curry. Korma and shahi paneer (richer Mughlai curries). Pakistani nihari and haleem (in larger quantity, 1 tbsp+). Mexican adobo (in slightly smaller quantity, alongside cumin and oregano). North African ras el hanout. Garam masala blends typically include coriander as one of the larger components.

  Buying notes. Indian groceries (Patel Brothers, Patel''s, smaller regional groceries) sell ground coriander with high turnover; the powder should be fresh-aromatic when you open the bag. MDH and Everest are the standard pre-ground brands. Whole seeds keep significantly better than ground; if you have a spice grinder, buying whole and grinding in 50 g batches is the ideal arrangement.

storage: |
  Whole seeds: sealed in glass at room temperature, 1 to 2 years. Ground coriander: sealed in glass, 6 months for peak flavor; 1 year usable. Cool dry pantry; refrigeration unnecessary. Off-cues are loss of aroma (smell-test before use); discard or use up in dishes where coriander is one of many flavors.
