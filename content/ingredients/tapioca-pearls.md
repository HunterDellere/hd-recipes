about: |
  Commercial tapioca pearls are made by tumbling small portions of pre-gelatinized tapioca dough into rolling drums; centrifugal motion compacts them into spheres, which are then dried for shelf stability. The two common forms are black tapioca pearls (colored with caramel coloring or brown sugar, 8 mm) and white/clear pearls (uncolored, smaller). "Quick-cooking" pearls (the 5-minute kind in supermarket aisles) are partially pre-gelatinized and have a soft, less chewy bite; traditional pearls take 30 to 45 minutes of active boiling plus 30 minutes of rest.

  The hydration curve matters. Dropped into cold water they harden and never properly cook. Dropped into vigorously boiling water they expand to roughly 2.5 times their dry weight as starch granules swell and the amylopectin network gelatinizes from outside in. The center is the slowest to hydrate; a 30-minute rest off-heat after the boil lets residual heat penetrate the core without overcooking the perimeter to mush. This rest is non-negotiable for the proper QQ texture (Mandarin: chewy-springy).

  Cooked pearls hold for 4 to 6 hours at room temperature in a sugar syrup that prevents the surface from drying and cracking. Refrigeration is fatal, starch retrogrades, water leaves the network, and the pearls turn hard and chalky within hours. There is no good fix; recook fresh.

  The black coloring of standard boba is cosmetic. Brown sugar pearls (the foundation of brown-sugar milk teas) are cooked further in brown sugar syrup after the boil, which both flavors and tints the surface a glossy mahogany.

storage: 'Sealed bag, cool dry pantry. Dried pearls last 12 months. Cooked pearls are good for one service window, discard after 6 hours.'
