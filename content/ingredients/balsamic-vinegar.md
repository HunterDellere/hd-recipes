about: |
  Balsamic vinegar is fundamentally different from wine vinegar despite the shared name. Red wine vinegar starts from finished wine and is soured by acetobacter into a thin 5-to-7-percent acetic acid solution. Traditional balsamic starts one stage earlier, from grape must: the whole pressed juice of the grape, skins and seeds and all, cooked down over open flame to a concentrated, sweet, syrupy mosto cotto before any fermentation begins. That cooked must is then both alcoholically and acetically fermented and aged, so the finished product carries the caramelized sugar of the cooked juice alongside the acetic acid. The result is a condiment that is simultaneously sweet and sour, dark, and viscous, with a density (around 1.13 g/ml) noticeably higher than wine vinegar's near-water 1.01. That sugar load is why balsamic behaves completely differently in the pan: it reduces to a glaze, where a wine vinegar just evaporates to nothing.

  The grading gap is enormous and almost entirely hidden by the label word "balsamic." There are three tiers. At the top, Aceto Balsamico Tradizionale di Modena (or di Reggio Emilia) DOP is made only from cooked local grape must, no wine vinegar and no additives, aged a minimum of 12 years (and up to 25+) in a graduated battery of wooden barrels, and sold in a tiny bottle for the price of a good wine. It is a finishing drop, used by the half-teaspoon on parmigiano, strawberries, or a finished risotto, never cooked. In the middle and far more common, Aceto Balsamico di Modena IGP is the supermarket and restaurant standard: a blend of cooked grape must with wine vinegar, legally required to age only 60 days, often colored and sometimes sweetened with caramel (E150). Quality within IGP varies wildly with the must-to-wine-vinegar ratio and the aging; a good IGP carries real cooked-fruit depth, a cheap one is thin acid plus caramel color. At the bottom, "balsamic glaze" or "glaze di balsamico" is IGP already reduced and thickened with added sugar or thickeners, sold pre-syruped for drizzling. For cooking, the workhorse is a mid-grade IGP; for finishing raw, a better IGP or the real Tradizionale.

  Why it reduces to a syrup, and the cook's leverage there. Because balsamic carries dissolved sugar from the cooked must (plus any added caramel in cheaper grades), simmering it concentrates both the acid and the sugar. As water boils off, the sugar fraction climbs toward a glaze that coats a spoon and, on cooling, sets to a sticky lacquer; the acetic acid concentrates in parallel, so the reduction stays sharp rather than cloying. A 3-to-1 reduction (simmer 120 g down to roughly 40 g) of a decent IGP makes a pourable glaze in 5 to 8 minutes over medium heat; the visual cue is the same nappant drag-line test used for pan sauces, and it thickens substantially more as it cools, so pull it while it still flows or it will set hard in the pan. This reduction is the entire trick behind "balsamic-glazed" roasted vegetables and meats: the syrup clings, the sugar caramelizes against the heat for a second Maillard-and-caramel layer, and the acid cuts the richness underneath. Add a knob of butter or a spoonful of honey to round a too-sharp reduction; add a pinch of salt to lift a flat one.

  Pairing logic. Balsamic's sweet-sour axis makes it the canonical acid-and-sugar finish for things that are rich, fatty, charred, or earthy: roasted Brussels sprouts and other caramelized brassicas, seared mushrooms, roast pork and duck, aged hard cheeses, ripe tomatoes and strawberries, and good olive oil. The sweetness is what lets it stand in where a plain vinegar would read too austere; against the bitter-sweet char of a hard-roasted vegetable it completes the sweet-sour-bitter triad in a single ingredient. It is wrong where you want clean bright acid with no sugar (a crisp green salad usually wants red wine vinegar or lemon, not balsamic, unless the salad deliberately leans sweet) and it is wrong as a high-heat cooking acid added early (the sugar scorches; add balsamic at the end as a glaze or a finish, not at the start of a long cook).

  Buying. Read past the front label. On the back, the best IGP lists "grape must" first or high in the ingredients, ages longer, and skips the caramel color (E150) and added thickeners. A bottle that lists "wine vinegar, concentrated grape must, caramel color" in that order is the thin-and-colored end; a bottle that leads with "cooked grape must" and adds only wine vinegar is the better end. The IGP seal (a numbered consortium label) guarantees Modena origin but not quality tier; the must ratio inside the IGP band is what varies. For finishing raw, it is worth stepping up to a small bottle of aged Tradizionale DOP or a high-density "invecchiato" IGP; for reducing into a glaze, a mid-grade IGP is the right value, since the reduction concentrates whatever depth is already there and a too-cheap base reduces to sweet acid with little character.

storage: 'Cool dark pantry, tightly capped, for years. The acetic acid and high sugar concentration are self-preserving and balsamic does not spoil in the bacterial sense. Failure modes are slow and cosmetic: sediment or a gelatinous "mother" can form in less-filtered bottles (harmless, strain it); over many years the volatile aromatics fade and a once-complex bottle reads flatter and more one-dimensionally sweet-sour. A homemade or store-bought reduction/glaze keeps several weeks refrigerated in a sealed jar and will thicken further cold, loosen it with a few drops of water or a brief warm if it sets too stiff. Refrigeration of the unopened bottle is unnecessary.'
