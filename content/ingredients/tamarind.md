about: |
  Tamarind is the fruit of Tamarindus indica, a legume-family tree native to tropical Africa and naturalized across the Indian subcontinent, Southeast Asia, the Caribbean, and Mexico through colonial-era trade. The fruit itself is a brown pod (10 to 15 cm long, knobbly, brittle outer shell) containing a sticky brown pulp wrapped around 6 to 10 hard glossy seeds and fibrous filaments. The pulp is what's eaten and cooked with; the shell and seeds are discarded, the fiber is strained out. In the US the most common forms on the market are: seedless block pulp (300 g compressed bricks sold at Indian, Southeast Asian, and Latin grocers, the highest-quality option; the pulp is moist, dark brown, and slightly fibrous, requires soaking and straining); concentrated tamarind paste (jarred or tubbed, often Thai or Indian brands, a smooth dark-brown paste that is essentially pre-soaked-and-strained block pulp at 2 to 3× concentration); and tamarind powder or "tamarind concentrate" sold in some Indian groceries (lower-quality dried form). Avoid "tamarind sauce" products which are typically sweetened, vinegar-cut, and a different ingredient.

  Acid profile and chemistry. Tamarind is unusual among culinary acids for being primarily tartaric (4 to 8% by dry weight; the same primary acid in grape and wine) rather than the citric acid that dominates citrus or the acetic acid in vinegars. Tartaric acid has a slightly different sensory profile from citric: it reads deeper, more rounded, and with a longer finish on the palate. Combined with the secondary malic acid (1 to 2%) and the very high natural sugar load (40 to 60% of the pulp by weight, primarily glucose and fructose), tamarind delivers a sour-sweet curve that no single acid replaces. The roasted-fruit back-note comes from low concentrations of pyrazines and furans formed in the pulp's natural enzymatic maturation. This is why lime juice is not interchangeable with tamarind in canh chua, sambar, or pad thai; the citric-acid brightness reads as a different flavor entirely.

  Cooking applications by category. **Indian (South Indian especially)**: sambar (the lentil-vegetable stew that anchors South Indian breakfast and lunch); rasam (the spiced thin tamarind-tomato broth); tamarind chutney (the dark sweet-sour dipping sauce for chaat); puli kuzhambu (Tamil tamarind stew); fish curries (Goan, Mangalorean). **Thai**: pad thai (the structural sour-sweet element of the sauce); tom kha (some preparations); massaman curry (the sour balance against the rich coconut); the dipping sauce for crispy whole fish (pla rad prik). **Vietnamese**: canh chua (the southern Vietnamese sour soup); the sour base for some Mekong-delta dishes. **Mexican**: agua de tamarindo (the sweet-sour drink), tamarind candy (often with chili-salt coating), tamarind-paste-based salsas. **West African (Senegalese, Nigerian)**: bissap-style drinks, sour stews. **Middle Eastern (Iraqi)**: tamarind-based shorbat and sour soups.

  Soaking and extraction. The block-pulp form requires soaking and straining before use; this is the technique that produces the broth-ready acid. Standard protocol: break off the needed weight of pulp (50 to 100 g for most recipes), place in a heatproof bowl, cover with hot (not boiling) water at roughly 4× the weight of the pulp, soak 15 to 20 minutes. Mash with a fork to break up the pulp clumps. Push the slurry through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean bowl, scraping the underside of the sieve to capture the fruit purée, discarding the seeds and fibers. The resulting liquid is medium-thick, dark-brown, and roughly 4 to 5% tartaric acid (still 2 to 3× the acidity of lemon juice). Use directly in the broth. Concentrated paste skips this step; substitute at roughly 0.6 to 0.7× the weight (35 g paste for 50 g pulp).

  Buying notes. Block pulp at Indian, Southeast Asian (Thai, Vietnamese, Filipino), and Latin grocers, typically 300 to 400 g compressed bricks in plastic packaging, the highest-quality option and the form most South and Southeast Asian home cooks use. Look for moist, dark-brown, slightly oily-feeling pulp; dried-out, lightened pulp is past peak. Concentrated paste in jars: Thai brands (Thai Kitchen, Maesri, Por Kwan) and Indian brands (TRS, Swad) are reliable; the smooth dark-brown paste should be evenly textured, not separated or crystallized. Avoid US-supermarket "tamarind sauce" or "tamarind chutney" products which are sweetened, vinegared, and a different ingredient. The "tamarind concentrate" powder sold in some shops is acceptable for emergency use but reads slightly flatter and saltier.

  Common Western drift. Two patterns. (1) US cooks substituting lime juice for tamarind in recipes that call for the deep tartaric-acid curve (canh chua, sambar, pad thai); the dish reads brighter and shorter on the palate, recognizable as a different acid entirely. (2) US home cooks using "tamarind sauce" or "tamarind chutney" jar products as cooking tamarind, producing dishes that read overly sweet and slightly vinegared; the structural cooking ingredient is the unsweetened block pulp or concentrated paste, not the sauce.

storage: |
  Unopened block pulp: 1 to 2 years at room temperature in a sealed dry pantry, sometimes longer; the pulp is preserved by its high sugar load (osmotic stability) plus its own organic acids. The pulp may darken slightly over time; this is normal and does not affect performance. Opened block pulp: wrap tightly in plastic film, refrigerate, 6 to 12 months. The pulp dries out at the cut surface but the interior remains usable; cut off any hard dried surface and use the moist interior. Concentrated paste in a jar: refrigerated after opening, 6 to 12 months. Some natural separation is normal; stir before measuring. Failure modes: visible mold growth (rare in the dry pulp; possible in concentrated paste left at room temperature), fermented-sour off-smell (the pulp has spoiled, discard), white powdery surface on block pulp (this is sugar crystallization, not mold, and is harmless; the pulp is still good).
