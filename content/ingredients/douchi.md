about: |
  Douchi are whole black soybeans that have been steamed, inoculated with Aspergillus (the same koji mold used for soy sauce, sake, and miso), salt-cured, and aged in sealed jars for two to six months. The result is the most concentrated soy ferment in the Chinese pantry — each bean carries the aroma of aged soy sauce, the umami density of miso, and a faintly funky fermented-bean note that is distinctly douchi and reads in any dish even at very small quantities. Three or four grams of douchi will season a wok-full of mapo or steamed fish; this is not an ingredient you measure in tablespoons.

  Two production styles produce structurally different products. **Dry-salted douchi (干豆豉, gan douchi)** are the Sichuan and Hunan style, made with less moisture and a longer ferment, producing dry, wrinkled, individual beans that you scoop out with a spoon. The Yongchuan (永川) and Liuyang (浏阳) brands are the canonical reference points; Yongchuan douchi are the standard for Sichuan mapo tofu. **Wet-brined douchi (湿豆豉)** are the Cantonese style, made with more moisture and shorter fermentation, producing softer, plumper beans that come in jars with their own dark salty liquid. Cantonese black bean sauce, steamed fish with black beans, and clams in black bean sauce all use the wet-brined style. The two are not interchangeable: dry douchi added to a Cantonese steamed-fish sauce reads under-seasoned and the texture is wrong; wet douchi added to mapo tofu reads salty-soft and loses the textural contrast of distinct beans against the silken tofu.

  Functional role. Douchi works as a glutamate concentrator and an aromatic finisher rather than as a primary heat source. The fermentation breaks soybean proteins down into free amino acids — primarily glutamate, with smaller contributions of aspartate and the inosinate-like nucleotides that compound umami beyond simple addition, and the salt cure preserves the result at concentrations near 12 to 15% sodium by weight. Crushing the beans (with the side of a knife or in a mortar) before adding releases the cell-bound aromatics dramatically; the difference between whole douchi and crushed douchi in a finished dish is roughly the difference between whole peppercorns and freshly cracked.

  Cooking applications. In Sichuan mapo tofu, 4 to 6 g of crushed Yongchuan dry douchi is added to the wok with the doubanjiang during red-oil rendering; the douchi crisps slightly and seasons the rendered oil. In Cantonese steamed fish with black beans, 10 to 15 g of wet-brined douchi is mashed with garlic and ginger into a paste, scattered over the fish, and steamed; the beans rehydrate and dissolve into the steaming juices. In Hunan smoked-pork-and-douchi stir-fry (la rou chao douchi), whole dry douchi are stir-fried with cured pork and chilies and the beans are eaten as a primary ingredient. In Cantonese spareribs with douchi, wet-brined douchi mashed into a marinade flavors the ribs through low steaming for 90 minutes. The reach is wide but the rule across applications is the same: small amount, crushed not whole, added during the aromatic phase rather than as a finishing scatter.

  Buying notes. For Sichuan and Hunan dry-salted style, look for "Yongchuan douchi" (永川豆豉) or "Liuyang douchi" (浏阳豆豉) on the label; these come in cellophane bags of loose beans, are deep matte black, very wrinkled, and dry to the touch. For Cantonese wet-brined style, look for "Yangjiang douchi" (阳江豆豉) or jarred Pearl River Bridge "Black Bean Sauce" (which is wet-brined douchi processed with a bit of garlic and oil); these come in glass jars, are plumper, glossier, and sit in dark salty liquid. Both styles are sold at Chinese groceries; supermarket "fermented black beans" of unspecified provenance are usually wet-brined Cantonese style and are acceptable for that purpose but wrong for Sichuan dishes. The shelf-stable jarred "black bean garlic sauce" (Lee Kum Kee, etc.) is a processed, sweetened condiment built on douchi but containing significant sugar, garlic, and oil; it is not interchangeable with whole douchi for any application that names douchi as the ingredient.

  Salt and pre-rinsing. Both styles arrive heavily salted, and the question of whether to rinse before use depends on the dish. For Sichuan mapo and dry-pot, do not rinse, the salt is part of the calculation, and you should reduce other salt sources (soy sauce, salt) to compensate for the douchi contribution. For Cantonese steamed fish where the douchi is the primary seasoning, do not rinse. For preparations where douchi is added as a small finishing crunch and the rest of the dish is otherwise seasoned, give the beans a quick rinse and pat dry to bring the salt level into balance with the rest of the seasoning.

storage: |
  Dry-salted douchi: sealed in an airtight container at room temperature in a cool dry pantry, indefinitely (years). The salt cure is the preservative; the beans do not spoil under shelf-stable conditions. Cellophane-bag douchi can be transferred into a glass jar with a tight lid for better long-term storage. Off-cues are mold (white or green fuzz on the surface, which is contamination from a wet utensil or humid storage), or a rancid-oil smell from oxidized fat (rare but possible after many years). Wet-brined douchi: sealed jar refrigerated after opening, 6 to 8 months. The dark liquid in the jar protects the beans; do not pour it off. Crushed or mashed douchi (prepped ahead) can be stored sealed in the fridge for 1 week.
