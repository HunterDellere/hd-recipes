about: |
  Poblano (Capsicum annuum, poblano, sometimes mislabeled "pasilla" in California markets) is a wide, dark-green, gently heart-shaped chile pepper grown across central Mexico, with Puebla as the canonical origin. Heat sits at 1000 to 2000 Scoville units (mild, comparable to bell pepper at the low end and to a very mild jalapeño at the high end); flavor is grassy, slightly smoky, with a thick wall that holds shape under heat. Dried, the poblano becomes the ancho chile (the most-used dried chile in Mexican cooking); the fresh-versus-dried duality is structural.

  Functional category: structural stuffed-chile vessel and mole base. The thick wall and large cavity make the poblano the canonical chile for chiles rellenos (stuffed and batter-fried), chiles en nogada (stuffed with picadillo, sauced with walnut crema), and rajas (sliced and sautéed with onion and crema). Sliced raw into thin strips, the poblano is the structural component of rajas con crema, the Mexican comfort side. Dried as ancho, the same fruit becomes the body of mole poblano, mole negro, and most red enchilada sauces.

  Roast and peel before any application. The poblano's thick skin is bitter and waxy; eating it raw or with the skin on is wrong. Standard technique: char the whole pepper on a gas burner, in a dry comal, or under a broiler until the skin blackens and blisters across the surface (4 to 8 minutes depending on heat); transfer to a covered bowl or a paper bag for 10 minutes to steam-loosen the skin; rub off the blackened skin with paper towels (do not rinse, the water washes off the smoky flavor); slit the pepper to remove the seed cluster and the membrane. The roasted-and-peeled poblano is the structural starting point for every application.

  Heat variability. Poblano heat is unpredictable; the same plant can produce mild and unexpectedly hot fruits from one harvest to the next, driven by water stress and ripeness. A poblano can be 500 Scoville (truly mild) or up to 4000 Scoville (genuinely spicy, comparable to a hot jalapeño). Taste a small piece before committing to a stuffed-chile preparation; if the chile reads aggressively hot, remove all seeds and membranes (the heat concentrates there) and serve with crema to cool the dish.

  Buying. Look for poblanos at Mexican groceries (year-round, often best), Latin sections of large supermarkets (year-round, variable), and farmers markets (peak summer through fall). The skin should be deep dark-green to almost black-green, smooth, with no soft spots or wrinkles. Avoid pale-green poblanos (under-ripe, more vegetal and less complex), shriveled poblanos (dehydrated, the dried version is the ancho and is a different ingredient), or any pepper with light-brown patches (heat damage or rot beginning).

storage: |
  Refrigerated in a perforated plastic bag in the crisper, 1 to 2 weeks at peak. The skin slowly dehydrates over time; usable for cooked applications past 2 weeks but the stuffed-chile structural quality is lost. Roasted-and-peeled poblanos: refrigerated in olive oil in a sealed jar, 5 to 7 days; frozen (whole or sliced), 6 months. Failure modes: soft spots (rot beginning, cut around or discard), wrinkled skin (dehydrated, use for sauce not stuffing), white or pink mold (discard).
