about: |
  Nicoise olives are small, dark, brine-cured olives from the Cailletier cultivar (also called Taggiasca on the Italian side of the same growing region in Liguria), produced in the Provençal counties of Var and Alpes-Maritimes around Nice. The Cailletier is harvested fully ripe, when the drupe has turned from green through brown to deep purple-black, and cured in brine with bay leaves, thyme, and sometimes rosemary or fennel for 6 to 12 months. The result is a small olive (3 to 4 g, with a substantial pit) that reads winey, nutty, and herbal rather than the assertive briny salt-bomb of a Greek kalamata or the buttery green of a Castelvetrano.

  Curing chemistry is different from a Spanish or Greek olive. Raw olives are inedibly bitter from oleuropein, a phenolic glucoside that has to be broken down or leached out before the fruit becomes edible. Spanish olives are typically lye-cured (sodium hydroxide hydrolyzes oleuropein quickly, the cure runs in days). Greek and Italian olives are typically brine-cured (salt water draws oleuropein out by osmosis over months, slower but flavor-preserving). The Provencal Cailletier curing process is brine-only, often with herbs added to the brine, and the long contact time produces the distinctive layered savory-bitter-herbal finish that is the entire reason to use them on a niçoise rather than a substitute.

  Flavor profile splits across applications. On a composed plate (niçoise, bagna cauda crudités, charcuterie), the small size and pit-in service is the right footprint; the cook eats around the pit and the olive reads as a discrete component. In tapenade, you pit them and chop coarsely, the wine-like notes carry. In long-cooked sauces (Provencal lamb shoulder with olives and tomato, daube niçoise, pissaladière), they go in pit-in (a strainer is the cook's tool) and contribute olive flavor without disintegrating the way a softer kalamata can. The pit-to-flesh ratio is high enough that a recipe calling for "cup of olives" needs 50 percent more nicoise by weight than kalamatas to land the same plated volume.

  Buying. Authentic nicoise olives carry the Olives de Nice AOP designation since 2001 (Appellation d'Origine Protegee). The bag or jar should name the AOP and ideally a specific producer in the Var or Alpes-Maritimes; commodity "nicoise-style" olives sold in olive bars or supermarket packs are often Moroccan or Greek black olives mislabeled. The genuine product runs $15 to $30 per pound stateside; cheaper "nicoise" is almost certainly something else. The Italian Taggiasca olive (same Cailletier cultivar grown in Liguria) is functionally identical and often more accessible in US markets.

  Pits in or out. Plate service: pits in, always. The cured-flesh-pulled-from-pit experience is part of the format. Cooking: pits in for braises (the cook removes them at table), pits out for tapenade and pasta sauces (the eater should not encounter pits in a smooth purée). Pit-out commercial nicoise olives are often inferior; the pitting machinery damages the flesh and accelerates oxidation, so the olives turn flat-flavored and slightly metallic. Pit them yourself with the side of a chef's knife (smash, then pull) when smooth integration is the goal.

storage: 'Submerged in their brine in a sealed glass jar, refrigerated, 6 to 12 months. Top off with a 1:10 salt-water solution if the brine evaporates below the olive line; exposed olives oxidize and turn flat-flavored. Failure modes: surface mold (skim off and discard the affected olives, the rest are usually fine if the brine smells clean); fermented sour smell beyond the normal cure (discard); soft or mushy texture (discard). Olives in oil (rather than brine) keep 1 to 2 months but lose the cured-bright finish faster; brine is the canonical storage medium.'
