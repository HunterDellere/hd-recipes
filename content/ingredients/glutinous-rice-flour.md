about: |
  Glutinous rice (Oryza sativa var. glutinosa) is misleadingly named: it contains no gluten. The "glutinous" refers to the high amylopectin content (close to 100%, versus ~80% for jasmine) which produces a sticky, chewy gel when cooked. Milled into flour, it hydrates into a dough that holds shape under boiling and steaming and bites with the springy resilience characteristic of mochi.

  Two production methods exist. Standard glutinous rice flour is dry-milled; the more traditional Vietnamese and Cantonese version, water-milled (Mandarin: shui mo) glutinous rice flour, is soaked rice ground with water then dried. Water-milled is finer, less starchy on the tongue, and produces a smoother dough, worth seeking for tang yuan and banh troi where dough quality matters. The most common brand on Western shelves is Mochiko (Koda Farms, Japan); Erawan (Thailand) and Three Ladies (Vietnam) are also widely available.

  Hydration is forgiving but mixing is critical. Cold water produces a dough that resists kneading; warm water (~50°C / 120°F) hydrates the starch granules without pre-gelatinizing, yielding a smooth, workable mass in 30 seconds of kneading. Dough should feel like soft modeling clay, not stiff like bread dough.

  Do not confuse with regular rice flour, which is non-glutinous and produces brittle, crumbly results in any application calling for chew. The packaging often looks identical; check that the label says glutinous (sometimes "sweet rice flour" in the US).
