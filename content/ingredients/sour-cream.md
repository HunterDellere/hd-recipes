about: |
  Sour cream is cultured cream: cream (typically 18 to 20% milkfat in standard American product) inoculated with lactic acid bacteria (Lactococcus lactis subsp. lactis and L. cremoris) and held warm for 14 to 16 hours until the bacteria ferment lactose into lactic acid. The pH drops from ~6.7 to ~4.5, which causes the casein proteins to coagulate into a smooth, spoonable gel. The fat content is what gives sour cream body; the pH is what gives it the tang and the functional behavior in cooking.

  In baking, sour cream does two things at once. The acidity (pH 4.5) softens gluten by interfering with the protein bonds that toughen a cake crumb, producing a tender, fine-grained texture. The fat (a stable, well-emulsified ~18% by weight) lubricates the crumb and holds moisture, so cakes made with sour cream stay tender for several days where milk-only cakes go dry overnight. It also reacts with baking soda directly: the lactic acid neutralizes the soda and produces CO2 lift that complements the gas from baking powder. Recipes that use sour cream at meaningful quantities almost always pair it with at least some baking soda for this reason.

  In sauces and emulsions, sour cream is more stable than plain cream under acidic or hot conditions because the casein gel has already coagulated. It will still break under hard simmer (above 80°C / 175°F), so the standard rule is to stir it in off-heat or at the very end. The fat ratio also makes it forgiving in dips, dressings, and finishing on chili or borscht where pure heavy cream would feel slick rather than rich.

  Distinction from adjacent products. Crème fraîche is European-style cultured cream at higher fat (30 to 40%) with a milder, less tangy culture; it tolerates higher heat and reduces without breaking, which is why French sauce work uses it. Greek yogurt is cultured milk (not cream), strained to remove whey, with similar tang but lower fat (typically 5 to 10% in full-fat versions); it tenderizes baked goods similarly but contributes less richness. Mexican crema is a regional cousin closer to crème fraîche than American sour cream — looser, less tangy. Substituting one for another shifts both flavor and structural behavior; treat them as related but not interchangeable.

  Buying. Standard American sour cream lists "cultured cream" and sometimes a stabilizer (modified food starch, gelatin, locust bean gum) on the label. The stabilizer-free versions taste cleaner and behave better under heat; brands vary widely. "Light" sour cream at 12 to 14% fat will work in dips but loses the structural fat that bakes need; "fat-free" is functionally a different ingredient, mostly thickened milk solids, and should not be substituted in any recipe that calls for sour cream as a structural component.

storage: |
  Refrigerate at 4°C / 40°F or colder, in the coldest part of the fridge (back wall, not the door). Once opened, 7 to 10 days. The container will separate over time: a clear, thin layer of whey on top is normal and stirs back in. Pink, gray, or fuzzy patches mean spoilage organisms have outcompeted the lactic-acid bacteria; discard. The smell shifts from clean-tangy to sharply sour or ammoniac when the product has gone past use.
