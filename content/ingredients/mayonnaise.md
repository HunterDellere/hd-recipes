about: |
  Mayonnaise is a stable oil-in-water emulsion: roughly 70 to 80 percent neutral oil suspended as fine droplets in a water phase that contains egg yolk, lemon juice or vinegar, and salt. The emulsion is held together by lecithin, a phospholipid molecule abundant in egg yolk that has both a water-loving and an oil-loving end and that bridges the two phases at every droplet surface. The dispersed-oil-droplet state scatters light, which is why mayonnaise is opaque white despite being made from clear or pale-yellow ingredients; the water-trapped fat is the entire reason mayonnaise reads as creamy rather than greasy.

  Functionally, mayonnaise sits across three roles that home cooking often combines and that the cook should distinguish. As a sandwich and salad binder (chicken salad, tuna salad, egg salad, BLT), mayonnaise provides fat, salt, acid, and the textural moisture that holds the diced solids together; the recipe tunes each variable through additions but the mayonnaise base is structural. As a condiment finish (burgers, fries, sandwiches), mayonnaise is the carrier for flavor compounds, garlic in aioli, sriracha in sriracha-mayo, gochujang in Korean fusion, and the recipe is essentially "good mayonnaise plus the flavor at correct dilution." As a sauce starter (remoulade, tartar sauce, Russian dressing, Big Mac sauce), mayonnaise is the body of the sauce and other ingredients are added in measured ratios; getting the mayonnaise quality right matters because the whole sauce is built on it.

  American baseline: Hellmann's / Best Foods. The two brands are the same product sold under different names east-of-Rockies (Hellmann's) and west-of-Rockies (Best Foods). The recipe runs around 75 percent soybean oil, 8 percent water, 8 percent egg yolk, 5 percent vinegar, sugar, salt, and lemon juice; the result is a slightly tangy, slightly sweet, neutral-flavored mayo that works across nearly every American mayo-application. Duke's mayonnaise (the Southern American brand) is paleolithic in formulation, no sugar added, and reads more savory and lemon-forward; preferred in classic Southern recipes (deviled eggs, pimento cheese, biscuit-and-tomato sandwich) and recommended for any application where the sweetness of Hellmann's is unwelcome. Avoid "olive oil mayo" labels at the supermarket; the olive oil is typically a small fraction of the total fat and the product reads as a worse Hellmann's.

  Japanese mayo: Kewpie. Kewpie is the bench-standard Japanese mayonnaise, distinct from American product in three ways: (1) made with rice vinegar and apple-cider vinegar instead of distilled white, (2) uses only egg yolks rather than whole eggs (denser, richer, more yellow), (3) contains MSG at functional dose (the source of the umami-amplified flavor that makes Kewpie taste different from Hellmann's). Kewpie is the right choice for any Japanese application (okonomiyaki, takoyaki, ebi mayo, tonkatsu, Japanese potato salad), Korean fusion, and any dish where the umami-and-sweetness depth of Kewpie reads correctly. The plastic squeeze bottle with the red cap is the canonical packaging.

  Homemade mayonnaise. Two egg yolks, 250 ml neutral oil, 15 ml lemon juice, 5 g Dijon mustard, 3 g salt, optional 1 clove minced garlic and a pinch of cayenne, whisked together in 90 seconds with an immersion blender or by hand with a balloon whisk. Homemade mayo reads markedly different from commercial: more eggy, more aromatic, more lemony, more aggressive. The textural difference is significant, homemade is looser and more glossy; commercial is firmer and more spoon-holding because of stabilizers and a higher oil ratio. Homemade is the upgrade for finishing dishes (a small bowl of homemade aioli for crudités, a spoonful in a salad dressing); commercial is the canonical choice for chicken salad and sandwich applications where the looser texture of homemade would not bind well.

  Storage and food safety. Commercial mayonnaise is shelf-stable until opened due to the acid and salt levels; once opened, it requires refrigeration and keeps 2 to 3 months at 4 °C. Homemade mayonnaise made with raw egg yolks is a food-safety question, the salmonella risk is low (1 in 20,000 American eggs at the wholesale level) and the acid in the mayo inhibits bacterial growth, but the mayo should be made the day of use and refrigerated immediately. Pasteurized eggs (sold in cartons or as in-shell pasteurized eggs) sidestep the salmonella question entirely and are the right choice for homemade mayo destined for elderly, immunocompromised, or pregnant eaters.

  The temperature window. Mayonnaise breaks (the emulsion separates back into oil and water phases) at temperatures above 60 °C and below 0 °C. Recipes that fold mayonnaise into hot or warm dishes (a warm potato salad, a sandwich filling that just came off the stove) should bring the dish to room temperature first or accept a slight texture compromise. Frozen mayonnaise is permanently broken and cannot be reconstituted; do not freeze a mayonnaise-bound dish and expect the texture to recover on thawing.

storage: 'Unopened jar at pantry temperature for the printed expiry, typically 12 to 18 months. Once opened, refrigerator at 4 °C; lasts 2 to 3 months. Failure modes: oil separation visible at the surface (still safe; whisk to recombine if used immediately, discard if separated more than a thin film), pink or yellow surface discoloration (microbial; discard the whole jar), off-smell (rancid oil; discard). Homemade mayonnaise: refrigerator only, eat within 3 to 4 days, do not freeze.'
