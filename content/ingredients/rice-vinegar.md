about: |
  Rice vinegar (米酢, *komezu*) is brewed from rice or rice wine, landing around 4–5% acetic acid; noticeably lower acid than the 6–7% standard for white wine vinegar or apple cider vinegar. The result is softer, rounder, and free of the harsh top-note bite that dominates Western vinegars. There's a faintly sweet, almost lactic backnote from residual rice sugars and amino acids.

  Two things to watch for at the store. **"Seasoned rice vinegar"** has sugar and salt added; designed for sushi rice, fine for that purpose, wrong for everything else (including this dish). Get plain. Brown rice vinegar (genmaisu) is a different product; funkier, more aged, used in Korean and macrobiotic cooking. Japanese "kome-su" or Chinese "white rice vinegar" is the default.

  Use as a finishing acid in stir-fries (a teaspoon at the end brightens without souring), in vinaigrettes (the gentle acid lets you taste other ingredients), in pickle brines, and as the acid in sushi rice (with sugar and salt). It pairs cleanly with ginger, sesame oil, miso, soy, and citrus.

storage: 'Pantry, sealed, indefinitely. The acidity self-preserves. Color may darken over years; flavor stays usable.'
