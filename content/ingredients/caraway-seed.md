about: |
  Caraway is the seed (technically a fruit) of Carum carvi, an umbellifer related to parsley, fennel, and dill. The dominant aromatic compound is carvone, which sits across anise-licorice and warm-bitter registers; the same molecule shows up in spearmint at a different chirality and reads completely different. Caraway is the structural spice of German and Austrian rye bread, the warm-bitter undertone in sauerkraut and other cabbage ferments, a key player in Hungarian goulash and stews, and the distinctive note in aquavit and several Central European liqueurs.

  Functionally caraway does best when toasted dry and then ground or cracked, rather than used as whole seed for crunch alone. The volatile oils are released by heat and grinding; whole caraway sprinkled on top of bread carries less aromatic load than the same quantity toasted and folded into the dough. In braises and stews, whole seeds added at the start of the cook give up their flavor slowly over the long simmer; in quick preparations (sauerkraut dishes, schnitzel coatings), a pinch of cracked or ground seed reads more clearly.

  The flavor is divisive in American kitchens (the licorice-warm-bitter register is unfamiliar to palates that did not grow up on rye bread) but structural in Central and Northern European cuisines. A goulash without caraway reads recognizably wrong to a Hungarian cook; an Eastern European cabbage roll without caraway reads similarly thin.

storage: 'Airtight jar, dark cupboard, 1 year whole, 6 months ground. Whole seeds preserve volatile oils dramatically better than pre-ground; buy whole and grind small quantities as needed. Failure mode is aromatic flattening, the seed will not smell of anything in particular long before it spoils.'
