about: |
  Tomatillo (Physalis philadelphica, husk tomato, miltomate) is a small green to pale-yellow fruit in the nightshade family, enclosed in a papery calyx (the husk) that splits as the fruit ripens. Despite the English name, it is not a tomato, it is structurally closer to the cape gooseberry. The flesh is dense, seedy, and gel-forming when cooked; the flavor profile is sharply tart (pH around 3.8), faintly herbal-green, with no tomato sweetness.

  Functional category: green-salsa structural fruit. The high acid carries the pungency of raw onion and garlic in salsa verde cruda; the gel-forming pectin gives green moles and enchilada sauces their characteristic glossy clinging texture without added thickeners; the green-vegetal notes pair structurally with poblano, jalapeño, and serrano (the green-chile family) and with cilantro and Mexican oregano. Sour cream or crema is the structural counter to the acid in cooked sauces; raw, the tomatillo wants only salt and chile.

  Three preparations dominate. Raw blended for salsa verde cruda (the brightest, most acidic, most herbaceous expression). Boiled for salsa verde cocida (5 to 8 minutes in water, the flesh softens to puree-ready, the acid mellows). Charred on a comal or under a broiler for salsa verde asada (the canonical preparation; the surface blisters and blackens in spots, the flesh collapses, the dish picks up smoke and Maillard depth that distinguishes it from the raw and boiled versions). Charred is the default in central Mexican cooking; raw is the brighter summer version; boiled is the gentler everyday option.

  Buying. Look for tomatillos at Mexican groceries (year-round) and large supermarkets (peak summer through fall). The husk should be tight, dry, and pale green; the fruit underneath should be firm and bright green to pale yellow, with no soft spots. A purple or red blush is fine and indicates a slightly riper, sweeter fruit (Physalis ixocarpa, the milpero variety, runs purple). Avoid bruised fruit, soft fruit, or fruit with the husk already split open and dirty.

storage: |
  Refrigerated in the husk in a paper bag, 2 to 3 weeks at peak. Husks help moisture retention; the fruit deteriorates faster with husks removed. Once husked and rinsed, use within 5 days. Failure modes: soft spots (the fruit is going; cut around or discard), wrinkled skin (dehydrated; usable for cooking but flavor is duller), white or gray mold on the husk surface (discard the affected fruit; check the rest of the bag). Tomatillos can be frozen whole, husked but unblanched, in zip-top bags for 8 months; they collapse on thaw and are suitable only for cooked applications (salsa verde cocida, enchilada sauce, mole verde).

    note: 'Canned tomatillos are the standard pantry backup; the canning process collapses the texture to a soft puree-ready state similar to boiled fresh tomatillos. The flavor reads slightly tinned but is fully recognizable. Drain well; the canning liquid is bitter and tinny. Use the same weight as fresh.'
