about: |
  Sui mi ya cai is the tender stem of a mustard-green cultivar (Brassica juncea var. tumida) that's been salt-cured, sun-dried, fermented in jars with sugar and spices for months, then minced fine. The result is dark brown, almost black, and reads as a concentrated savory paste with a slight sweet-musty finish from the long anaerobic ferment. It is dry-textured, not wet like a pickle — closer in handling to a furikake than a kraut.

  Its job in cooking is to deliver glutamates and a fermented vegetal funk in a form that does not water down a hot pan. Dandan noodles (the canonical use) depend on it for the brown specks that catch on the noodles and for the salt-glutamate base under the chili oil and sesame paste. Skipping it and substituting any other pickle gives you a dish that is recognizably wrong even if you can't name what's missing.

  Do not confuse with zha cai (榨菜), which is the wet, knobbly, chili-coated pickle made from a different part of the same plant. Zha cai is wetter, hotter, and used differently — usually as a side or sliced into congee. Yibin Suimiyacai (宜宾碎米芽菜) is the reference brand; the small yellow-and-red foil packet is what you want at the Chinese grocery.

storage: 'Unopened packets at pantry temperature, indefinite. Once opened, refrigerate in an airtight jar; the residual moisture will mold within 2 months at room temp despite the salt load.'
