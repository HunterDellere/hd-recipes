about: |
  Pepitas (Spanish for "little seed") are the hulled inner seeds of pumpkins and other Cucurbita pepo squash, separated from the white fibrous outer hull. The seeds are flat, oval, olive-green to dark green from chlorophyll pigment, roughly 1 cm long, with a pronounced raphe (a faint ridge along the long axis where the seed coat once attached to the placenta). Composition (raw): roughly 5% water, 30% protein, 49% fat, 11% carbohydrate (mostly fiber), with high concentrations of magnesium (~550 mg per 100 g, one of the highest of any seed), zinc, and tryptophan. The fat is mostly unsaturated (oleic and linoleic acids dominant) with a small fraction of polyunsaturated linolenic acid that gives toasted pepitas their distinctive deep-green oil character.

  Hulled vs unhulled. Unhulled pumpkin seeds (the white seeds you scoop out of a jack-o-lantern) have an edible but tough white hull around the green inner seed; they require longer roasting to crisp the hull and the hull-to-seed ratio dilutes the seed's flavor. Hulled pepitas are the inner seeds with the white hull mechanically removed at processing; they are what is sold in Mexican groceries and bulk-bin sections as "pepitas" and what virtually every Mexican recipe specifies. Some pumpkin varieties (especially the Mexican Cucurbita pepo cv. Styrian) produce naturally hull-less seeds that need no mechanical hulling; these are the highest-quality pepitas and command a premium at specialty groceries.

  Toasting chemistry. Raw pepitas read grassy, slightly chalky, and faintly bitter; toasted pepitas develop a register dominated by pyrazines (Maillard products from the seed proteins and sugars) plus the aromatic compounds of the pumpkin seed oil (especially nonadienal and related polyunsaturated oxidation products that read as toasted-pumpkin-seed-oil, the same compound that gives Austrian pumpkin seed oil its color and flavor). Dry-toast in a small skillet over medium heat for 3 to 5 minutes, agitating every 30 seconds. The seeds will pop audibly (like miniature popcorn) as the residual moisture in the seed core flashes to steam, turn from olive-green to mottled tan-brown, and release a pronounced toasted-pumpkin-seed aroma. Pull immediately when the popping subsides and the color is set; the window from perfect to scorched is about 30 seconds, and over-toasted pepitas read bitter and harsh.

  Structural role in Mexican cooking. The pre-Hispanic Mexican use of pepitas is as the thickening-and-flavor base of pipián (and mole verde, a green pipián variant): toasted pepitas blended with green tomato (tomatillo), serrano chili, cilantro, epazote, and chicken or turkey broth into a thick green sauce. The pepitas provide the body (they thicken the sauce on simmering as the proteins denature and the seed pulp emulsifies into the broth fat) and the savory-toasty depth. Pipián verde is one of the foundational mole-family sauces and predates Spanish contact; the seed-thickened sauce technique is shared with West African groundnut soups and Indian seed-and-nut curries (kaju, badam) but developed independently in the Americas.

  Beyond pipián, pepitas function as a finishing crunch across modern Mexican and Mexican-American cooking: scattered across roasted-vegetable plates, sweet potato salads, charred-corn salads, ceviches, sopes, beans, and grain bowls. Toasted with salt and chili powder (sometimes called "spiced pepitas" or "Mexican-spiced pumpkin seeds"), they function as a snack or a beer accompaniment. Ground into flour and added to baked goods (pan dulce, certain tamales), they contribute a green-seed body and color. The reach is wide; pepitas are one of the most versatile seeds in Mexican pantry.

  Buying. Mexican groceries sell pepitas in 250 g and 500 g bags at substantially lower prices than supermarket bulk-bin sections. Look for plump uniformly green seeds with no shriveled or discolored individuals; the smell when the bag is opened should be clean nutty-and-grassy with no rancid-oil notes. Pre-toasted-and-salted pepitas are also available but lose flavor faster than raw seeds; buy raw and toast as needed. Premium hull-less Styrian pepitas are available at specialty stores at substantially higher prices and are worth the difference for finishing applications where the seed flavor is the point.

storage: |
  Raw pepitas: sealed in a glass jar or zip-top bag in the refrigerator (cold storage prevents the unsaturated fat from oxidizing) for 6 months at peak; usable beyond 9 months but progressively rancid-smelling. Pantry storage at room temperature is acceptable for 2 to 3 months but rancidity develops faster in warm weather. Freezer storage extends shelf life to 12 months. Toasted pepitas: in a sealed container at room temperature, 1 week for peak crispness; refresh by re-toasting in a dry skillet for 1 to 2 minutes if they soften. Failure mode: rancid smell (oily-paint-thinner notes, the unsaturated fat has oxidized; discard, the rancidity does not cook out and produces unpleasant aldehyde flavors); insect activity (pantry moths are the main risk, transfer to glass or freeze for 48 hours to kill any larvae present at purchase).
