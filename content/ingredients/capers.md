about: |
  Capers are the unopened flower buds of Capparis spinosa, a low spiny shrub native to the dry rocky slopes of the Mediterranean basin. The buds are picked by hand at first light (the plants reopen flowers fast in heat), graded by size, and cured by either salt-packing (the traditional Mediterranean method) or brining in vinegar-salt solution (the dominant commercial format). The cure is necessary: raw capers are intensely bitter from glucosinolates and a fresh sap that the cure mellows by partial enzymatic breakdown.

  Pungency comes from glucocapparin, a glucosinolate. Intact bud cells hold glucocapparin and the enzyme myrosinase in separate compartments, similar to the alliin-alliinase chemistry in garlic. When the cook chews a caper or a knife crushes one, the two meet and myrosinase cleaves glucocapparin into methyl isothiocyanate, the pungent mustardy compound that defines the caper's signature flavor. The same family of compounds drives mustard, horseradish, wasabi, and arugula bite; capers sit at the milder end of the family because the cure has already partially degraded the precursors. A whole intact caper read on the tongue tastes briny and herbal; only when bitten does the mustard pungency hit.

  Salt-packed versus brined. Salt-packed capers (sold in jars or paper sacks, coated in coarse sea salt) preserve more of the floral and pungent chemistry. The cure is gentler on the bud structure; the texture stays firm and the flavor reads layered (floral-mustard-savory). They need a 10-minute cold-water soak with one or two water changes before use to leach off the surface salt. Brined capers (in vinegar-salt solution, the dominant format) are more accessible and cost half as much; the vinegar dominates the flavor and the texture turns slightly softer. For finishing applications where the caper reads as a discrete component (niçoise plating, butter-caper pan sauce, vitello tonnato, salsa verde), salt-packed is meaningfully better. For dispersed applications (chopped into a tartar sauce, blended into a tapenade), brined works fine and the price difference is unjustified.

  Size grades matter. The grading runs from smallest (most prized) to largest. Non-pareille (under 7 mm) are the tightest, most floral, most expensive — French market preference, ideal for plate service. Surfines (7 to 8 mm), capucines (8 to 9 mm), capotes (9 to 11 mm), and fines (11 to 13 mm) descend in price and in textural elegance. Beyond capers proper, "caperberries" are the larger fertilized fruit of the same plant after the bud has flowered; they are a different ingredient (1 to 2 cm long, with stems, served whole on antipasti boards) and not interchangeable with capers in cooked applications.

  Applications by category. Finishing on plate (niçoise, vitello tonnato, slow-roasted salmon, smoked fish): scatter whole, kept structurally intact. Pan sauces (chicken piccata, butter-caper sauce for fish): added in the last 30 seconds so the buds soften without dissolving. Cold sauces (tartar, salsa verde, tapenade, gribiche): chopped and folded in. Pasta puttanesca: roughly chopped, cooked into the tomato base for 5 minutes so the capers contribute pungency to the sauce. Fried capers (a chef's-table garnish): salt-packed capers, rinsed, dried, fried in 180 °C oil for 30 seconds, drain — they bloom into crispy little flowers, intensely flavored, sit on top of a fish or pasta dish.

  Buying. Pantelleria (a small island off Sicily) and Salina (an Aeolian island) are the two most reputable Italian sources, both with PDO/IGP status; the Pantelleria capers carry IGP (Indicazione Geografica Protetta) designation. Spanish capers from Murcia and Mallorca are also high quality. For salt-packed, look for Cucina & Amore (Italian), Capraia (Italian), or anything labeled Pantelleria IGP or Salina IGP. For brined, Mediterranean Organic and Bonavita are mid-tier brands; the supermarket house brand is usable for cooked applications.

storage: 'Salt-packed: airtight in a cool dry pantry, 1 to 2 years. Brined: refrigerated after opening, 9 to 12 months, submerged in their own brine. Failure modes: dry crusty surface on salt-packed (still fine, soak before use); soft/mushy texture (overripe at packing or cure breakdown, discard); fermented sour smell beyond the brine (discard); cloudy brine with sediment (microbial activity, discard).'
