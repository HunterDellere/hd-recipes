about: |
  Poppy seeds are the mature seeds of *Papaver somniferum*, the opium poppy, harvested after the seed pod has dried and the alkaloid content of the sap has degraded. Two distinct types are sold: **blue-grey poppy seeds** (European variety, sometimes called Dutch or Canadian blue, 1 to 1.5 mm, kidney-shaped, the dominant variety in North American supermarkets, used in baking (mohnstrudel, poppy-seed bagels, lemon-poppy-seed muffins)) and **white poppy seeds** (South Asian variety, called *khus khus* or *posto* in Hindi/Bengali, creamy ivory to pale grey, slightly smaller, with a milder flavor and higher starch content, the dominant form in Indian cooking). The two varieties come from the same species but are distinct in flavor profile and cooking function; white khus khus is used in South Asian curry pastes and korma bases where the blue variety would taste wrong.

  Composition (white poppy seeds): ~40 to 45 percent fat (predominantly oleic acid at 30 percent, linoleic acid at 60 percent of the fat fraction: the unsaturated fatty acid profile that makes the seeds rich and prone to rancidity), ~20 percent protein, ~25 percent carbohydrate (including a significant starch fraction, which is the functional basis for their thickening use in curry pastes). Trace alkaloid content (morphine, codeine, noscapine) in the seeds is extremely low and food-safe — the alkaloids are concentrated in the seed pod sap (latex) and not in the mature dry seeds. A food sensitivity to poppy seeds exists (contact dermatitis, some cross-reactive allergy with latex) but is uncommon; the seeds are safe for the general population.

  **Cooking function in South Asian pastes.** In South Asian cooking, white poppy seeds (khus khus) serve two functions: flavoring and thickening. Whole dry-roasted khus khus develops a deeper nutty quality from Maillard reactions on the seed surface. When soaked in water for 15 to 30 minutes and then blended into a paste (alone or with other spices, grated coconut, or garlic-ginger), the starch granules in the seed hydrate and the fat emulsifies, producing a thick, slightly grainy paste that adds body to korma sauces, xacuti curry bases, and mughlai-style gravies. The paste contribution is structural: a korma sauce thickened with a poppy seed and cashew paste has a distinct velvety-without-cream texture that no other ingredient reproduces. In Goan xacuti, khus khus is one component of the roasted spice paste that forms the curry base, contributing body alongside grated coconut and whole spices. In Bengali cooking, *aloo posto* (potato and poppy seed) is a defining preparation where the white seeds are soaked, ground with green chili, and cooked with potato.

  **Baking function (blue-grey variety).** In European and North American baking, the blue-grey poppy seeds are used as a topping (on bagels, bread, muffins) for visual contrast and mild crunch, or as a ground filling in Central European pastries (mohnstrudel, poppy roll, mohntorte) where the seeds are ground with sugar and milk into a dense, sweet, nutty paste. For baking applications, the blue-grey variety is preferred; the khus khus would work but reads slightly different and is not canonical.

  **Applications by category.** In North Indian Mughlai cooking, white poppy seeds (khus khus) are soaked, ground, and incorporated into korma and shahi paneer bases alongside cashew paste, creating the thick, ivory-colored sauce that defines these dishes. In Goan cooking, khus khus is part of the xacuti masala paste (roasted with grated coconut, cloves, cinnamon, coriander seeds, and Kashmiri chilies, then wet-ground). In Bengali cooking, posto (poppy seed paste) bases a whole sub-category of preparations: aloo posto (potato), begun posto (eggplant), macher posto (fish). In Maharashtra, white poppy seeds appear in curry pastes for chicken and lamb preparations. In Central European baking, mohn (German and Yiddish for poppy) is the filling ingredient for strudel, hamantaschen, and poppy seed roll. Across all categories, toast or soak before using; whole raw poppy seeds have muted flavor compared to the toasted or soaked versions.

  **Buying notes.** White poppy seeds (khus khus) are available at South Asian grocery stores (Indian specialty shops, some H Mart and 99 Ranch locations). They are sold in small spice bags or bulk; look for ivory-to-pale-grey, clean, uniformly sized seeds with a faintly nutty smell. Blue-grey poppy seeds are available at any supermarket in the baking or spice aisle. Both varieties are prone to rancidity due to the high unsaturated fat content; buy in small quantities from stores with high turnover.

storage: 'Pantry, airtight container, dark and cool: 6 months for whole seeds. Refrigerator or freezer (sealed bag): 12 to 18 months; the high fat content (primarily linoleic acid, highly prone to oxidation) means the seeds go rancid faster than most dried spices, especially in warm pantry conditions. Rancid poppy seeds smell paint-like or fishy; discard. Soaked-and-ground paste: refrigerator, 2 days maximum; does not freeze well.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'white poppy seeds (khus khus in curry paste thickener)'
    use: 'raw cashews soaked 15 minutes in warm water, or hemp seeds'
    note: 'Soaked raw cashews produce a similar thick, creamy paste when blended; the flavor is richer and more cashew-forward, less nutty-neutral. Hemp seeds have a similar fat content and produce a comparable paste texture. Both are acceptable substitutes for the thickening function; neither reproduces the mild distinctive flavor of khus khus exactly.'
  - for: 'blue-grey poppy seeds (baking topping)'
    use: 'sesame seeds (white or black)'
    note: 'For the visual and textural topping function (bagels, bread): sesame seeds are the closest substitute in color (white sesame) and read similarly in baking. The flavor is distinctly sesame rather than poppy; acceptable for most baking contexts.'
