about: |
  Dark brown sugar is the same construction as light brown (refined sucrose plus refinery molasses, sprayed and tumbled) but with the molasses share roughly doubled, around 6.5% by weight. Like light brown, it is not an unrefined product despite frequent marketing language; the molasses is added back to white sugar after refining. The 6.5% molasses share, however, is enough to push every behavior of light brown sugar further along the same axis.

  Functional role: each thing molasses contributes to a baked good shows up at higher amplitude in dark brown. More water (the molasses is ~10–15% water by weight) means more steam pressure and more spread in cookies, more moisture retention in cakes, more chew everywhere. More acid (molasses sits around pH 5) means more aggressive Maillard browning and faster gluten relaxation, which translates to deeper crust color and tenderer crumbs. More molasses sugars (fructose and glucose alongside trace minerals) mean caramelization kicks in earlier in the bake, so the surface develops the toffee-bitter edge sooner. And the flavor itself moves from background to foreground; where light brown reads as a hint of caramel, dark brown reads as molasses straight, with toffee, raisin, and faint smoky-bitter notes that are unmistakable.

  This is why dark brown sugar is the default sugar in any application where molasses character is the point. Across baked goods: gingerbread, molasses cookies, sticky toffee pudding, certain styles of pecan pie, dark fruitcakes, and the full-flavored chewier school of chocolate chip cookies that prize maximum spread and depth. Across savory cooking: barbecue rubs and sauces, baked beans, bourbon glazes for ham and pork, mole sauces (in Mexican cooking, where the closest functional equivalent is piloncillo), Caribbean jerk marinades, Filipino adobo with brown sugar, and the long-cooked tomato-based barbecue traditions of the American South. It is not the right call in delicate cakes, white-flour quick breads, or anything where a clean sweetness is desired; the molasses will color the crumb and shout over subtler flavors.

  How it differs from genuinely unrefined sugars: muscovado is the closest unrefined cousin and has molasses fully integrated through the crystal rather than coated on the outside, which means the molasses is more evenly distributed and the texture is slightly stickier. Muscovado runs slightly more mineral-edged and more complex. Panela and piloncillo (Latin American) and jaggery (South Asian) are unrefined cane sugars that play a similar functional role in their respective cuisines but carry distinct regional flavor profiles. Substituting any of these for dark brown gives a slightly different character but the same structural job in a recipe.

  Swap math from granulated: dark brown sugar can be reconstructed by adding molasses to white sugar at the right ratio. For 1 cup packed (213 g): 193 g granulated sugar + 20 g (~1 tbsp) molasses, mixed until evenly coated. The cook should also reduce another liquid in the recipe by ~15 g to compensate for the water in the added molasses. The resulting sugar behaves identically to commercial dark brown.

  Pack-by-volume is the same trap as with light brown sugar. A loose cup is ~165 g; a firmly packed cup is ~213 g, a 30% difference that shows up loudly in cookie texture and cake rise. Recipes that produce inconsistent results between bakers almost always have a packing convention as a hidden variable. Weigh.

storage: 'Airtight container at room temperature, away from heat. The higher molasses load actually holds moisture better than light brown sugar, so dark brown stays soft longer; but once dried out, it sets harder. Failure mode is bricking from moisture loss; revive by sealing the container overnight with a slice of bread or a damp paper towel until the sugar pulls moisture back. Avoid humid storage; excess ambient moisture can drive slow secondary darkening and stickiness.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'dark brown sugar (gingerbread, sticky toffee, molasses-forward bakes)'
    use: 'muscovado sugar'
    note: 'Genuinely unrefined; molasses is integral to the crystal rather than sprayed on. Slightly more moisture, more complex flavor with mineral edges. Use 1:1 by weight. Often a subtle upgrade in any application where dark brown is the right tool.'
  - for: 'dark brown sugar (chocolate chip cookies, blondies, chewy bakes)'
    use: 'light brown sugar'
    note: '1:1 by weight. Cookie spreads less, browns less, tastes noticeably milder and less complex. Acceptable in most recipes; missed in anything where molasses is featured (gingerbread, sticky toffee, dark fruitcake).'
  - for: 'dark brown sugar (any baking application, rebuilt from scratch)'
    use: 'granulated sugar + molasses'
    note: 'For 1 cup (213 g): 193 g granulated + 20 g (~1 tbsp) molasses. Mix until evenly coated; reduce other recipe liquid by ~15 g to compensate for molasses water. Behaves identically by construction.'
  - for: 'dark brown sugar (Latin American applications, mole, dulce de leche)'
    use: 'piloncillo or panela'
    note: 'Unrefined sugarcane in cone or block form. Closer to muscovado than to refined dark brown. Grate or dissolve in liquid. Use 1:1 by weight; expect a slightly more mineral profile.'
  - for: 'dark brown sugar (South Asian and Caribbean preparations)'
    use: 'jaggery (gur) or dark muscovado'
    note: 'Jaggery (cane or palm sugar block) is the regional default for chai, kheer, and Bengali sweet preparations. Dark muscovado approximates it closely. 1:1 by weight; the flavor will read more pronounced.'
  - for: 'dark brown sugar (BBQ rubs, savory glazes)'
    use: 'turbinado or demerara'
    note: 'Turbinado has a coarser crystal and less molasses, so the rub stays drier and crusts more cleanly under heat. Use 1:1 by weight; expect less moisture in the rub and a slightly cleaner caramel character on the meat surface.'
