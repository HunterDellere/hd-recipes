about: |
  Japanese short-grain rice (uruchimai, 粳米) is a cultivar group of Oryza sativa japonica grown for the round, opaque-when-raw, plump-when-cooked grains that define Japanese rice eating. The grains are roughly 5 mm long and 2.5 mm wide ,  visibly stubbier than the long-grain Indica rices used in Indian and Southeast Asian cooking ,  and their internal structure is dominated by amylopectin (~83% of starch) over amylose (~17%). The amylose-amylopectin ratio is the single most important variable in cooked rice texture: high-amylose long-grain rices cook to fluffy, separate, dry-feeling grains; high-amylopectin short-grain rices cook to tender, slightly sticky, gently clumping grains that hold together when picked up with chopsticks. This is what people are responding to when they say "Japanese rice."

  Distinct from glutinous rice. Uruchimai is short-grain but not glutinous. Glutinous rice (mochigome) is a separate cultivar group with virtually no amylose (~99% amylopectin) ,  it cooks to a distinctly sticky, opaque, chewy texture and is reserved for mochi, sekihan, and specific sweets. The two are not interchangeable; uruchimai is the everyday rice, mochigome is for mochi. Western shoppers often see "sushi rice" or "Calrose" labels that mean uruchimai, and "sweet rice" or "glutinous rice" that mean mochigome.

  Cultivars and grades. Within uruchimai, the premium Japanese cultivars are Koshihikari (sweetest, most aromatic, the Toyota Camry of Japanese rice ,  what most home cooks in Japan eat), Akitakomachi (slightly firmer bite, grown in Akita prefecture), Sasanishiki, Hitomebore, and Yumepirika. In the United States, Calrose (a California-grown japonica) is the everyday workhorse ,  grown by Tamanishiki, Nishiki, and Kokuho Rose brands; not as nuanced as Japanese-grown Koshihikari but a real japonica and produces good results. Beware "sushi rice" labels on long-grain rice or Calrose blended with cheaper varieties; the package should say "short-grain Calrose" or "Koshihikari" by cultivar name.

  The four-step protocol ,  wash, soak, cook, rest. Japanese rice cookery is treated with the seriousness Italians give pasta. (1) Wash: place the rice in a bowl, fill with cold water, swirl with a hand, and pour off the cloudy water. Repeat 4 to 6 times until the water runs nearly clear. The cloudy starch on the surface, if not washed off, gelatinizes during cooking and turns the cooked rice gluey rather than separately tender. (2) Soak: drain and let the washed rice sit in fresh cold water for 30 minutes (40 in winter) before cooking. Soaking lets water penetrate the grain core fully so the rice cooks evenly inside-out rather than overcooking the outside before the center hydrates. (3) Cook: standard ratio is 1 cup rice : 1.1 to 1.2 cups water by volume (180 g rice : 200 to 220 g water by weight). Rice cooker or covered saucepan; bring to a boil, drop to lowest heat, cover tightly, cook 12 to 15 minutes until water absorbed. (4) Rest: this is the step Western cooks skip. Leave the lid on, off heat, for 10 to 15 minutes after cooking. The internal moisture redistributes, the bottom layer firms slightly, and the texture moves from "just cooked" to "Japanese rice." Then fluff gently with a wet rice paddle, lifting and folding rather than stirring.

  Where it fits. The base of nearly every washoku meal ,  rice plus soup plus banchan/okazu is the structural pattern. The medium of sushi (sushi rice is uruchimai mixed warm with a vinegar-sugar-salt seasoning, fanned to glossy sheen). The structural element of onigiri (uruchimai cooked, lightly salted, formed into triangles with cold-water-and-salt hands). The cushion of donburi (oyakodon, gyudon, tendon ,  all ladled over fresh-cooked uruchimai). The vehicle of chazuke (rice with hot tea or dashi poured over). The rice in fried-rice and risotto-style preparations is sometimes uruchimai too, but Japanese omurice and Korean kimchi-fried-rice often use longer-grain rice for clearer separation; uruchimai's amylopectin makes it tend to clump in stir-fries.

  Buying. Look for uruchimai sold under cultivar names (Koshihikari, Akitakomachi) or by reputable brands (Tamanishiki, Nishiki, Kokuho Rose, Tamaki Gold) at Japanese or well-stocked Asian grocers. The package should specify "short-grain" and ideally identify the cultivar. Buy in 5 to 10 lb / 2 to 5 kg bags from Japanese grocers ,  the turnover is high and the rice is fresher than the small bags at Western supermarkets. Sell-by dates matter; rice loses flavor measurably 6 months past milling and noticeably 12 months past.

storage: 'Sealed in an airtight container in a cool, dry pantry, uruchimai keeps 6 months at peak quality and up to 1 year before flavor decline becomes obvious. Refrigerator or freezer storage extends life to 1+ year and slows the slow oxidation of rice oils that produces the "stale rice" cardboard note. Cooked rice should be eaten the day it is cooked or refrigerated within 2 hours and used within 2 days; freeze leftover cooked rice in 1-cup portions wrapped tightly in plastic ,  the texture survives microwave reheating remarkably well.'
