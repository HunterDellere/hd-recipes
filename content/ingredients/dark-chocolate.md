about: |
  The cacao percentage stamped on a bar (60%, 70%, 85%) is the combined weight of dry cocoa solids plus cocoa butter. The rest is sugar, plus a little lecithin and vanilla in most bars. So a 70% bar is 70% cacao components and 30% sugar, but the cocoa butter share inside that 70% varies by manufacturer and matters for melting behavior.

  For most baking, 60–70% is the sweet spot. There's enough cocoa butter to melt smoothly into a ganache or cookie dough, and enough cocoa solids that the chocolate flavor reads through the sugar in the rest of the recipe. Below 55% you're mostly tasting sugar; above 75% the bar can seize batter or split a ganache because the fat-to-solids balance tips and there's not enough sugar to soften the bitterness.

  Seizing is the dramatic failure. The instant ≥1% water hits melting chocolate, the sugar and cocoa solids clump around the water and the smooth fat phase breaks: chocolate goes grainy, stiff, almost like wet sand. Steam from a too-hot bain-marie or a wet spoon does it. The rescue is counterintuitive: add more water, a tablespoon at a time, stirring hard, until the mixture re-emulsifies into a smooth paste. It won't go back to tempered chocolate after that; it's now ganache territory. Use it for ganache, glaze, or fold it into brownie batter. Don't try to mold it.

  Tempering — controlled crystallization of cocoa butter for shine and snap — is its own discipline. Out of scope here; a separate tempering page will cover it.

storage: 'Cool, dry, dark; ideally 18°C (~65°F), well below room temperature in summer. Wrap tightly; chocolate absorbs odors. Bloom (gray or whitish streaks) is harmless cocoa-butter migration to the surface: the bar still bakes fine, and melting redistributes the fat. Sugar bloom (from condensation) has a grittier texture and is also fine for melting, just not for eating straight.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'dark chocolate (60–70%)'
    use: 'unsweetened chocolate + sugar'
    note: 'For 100g 70% chocolate, use 70g unsweetened (100% cacao) + 30g sugar dissolved into the recipe''s liquid. Texture is rougher and you lose the manufacturer''s lecithin emulsification.'
  - for: 'dark chocolate (60–70%)'
    use: 'dark chocolate chips'
    note: 'Chips contain stabilizers that resist melting (so they hold shape in cookies). Fine to chop and substitute, but ganache made from chips is stiffer and less glossy than ganache from a bar.'
  - for: 'dark chocolate (60–70%)'
    use: 'cocoa powder + butter + sugar'
    note: 'Rough conversion: 30g cocoa powder + 30g butter + 30g sugar ≈ 100g 70% chocolate. Works in batters and brownies; fails in anything that needs the chocolate to set firm (truffles, ganache, glaze).'
