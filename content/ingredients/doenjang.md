about: |
  Doenjang is the Korean fermented soybean paste, one of the three jangs (with ganjang, soy sauce, and gochujang, the chili paste) that form the seasoning trinity of Korean cooking. At its most fundamental it is little more than soybeans, salt, and time, but the traditional process gives it a depth that quick-fermented pastes never reach. Whole soybeans are boiled, mashed, and formed into bricks called meju, which are hung to dry and ferment with wild airborne molds (chiefly Aspergillus and Bacillus subtilis) for weeks. The dried meju are then submerged in heavy brine for months; the liquid that draws off becomes ganjang (Korean soy sauce) and the solids left behind, aged further, become doenjang. This shared-origin process is why traditional Korean soy sauce and soybean paste taste like siblings, and why doenjang carries a deep, almost cheesy funk that distinguishes it from the cleaner profile of Japanese miso.

  The fermentation does the flavor work. Bacillus subtilis on the meju produces powerful proteases that break the soy protein down into free amino acids, above all glutamate, the source of doenjang's intense savory weight. The same microbes generate the pungent, sometimes ammoniacal notes that read as funk; well-made doenjang balances that funk against a rounded, malty depth rather than letting it dominate. Because the traditional ferment is driven by wild Bacillus rather than the cultivated Aspergillus oryzae (koji) that defines miso, doenjang is funkier, saltier, and coarser, with whole and half-broken beans often still visible in the paste. The useful analogy is cheese: if young Japanese white miso is a mild fresh brie, traditional doenjang is an aged, assertive blue.

  Functional role. Doenjang is a glutamate-dense savory base, used the way a Western cook might use anchovy, parmesan rind, or a concentrated stock: a spoonful added to a pot pulls everything toward deep, fermented umami. It is rarely the loud flavor in a dish; it is the savory floor underneath the louder ones. In a gochujang-driven braise it supplies the earthy funk the chili paste lacks; in a clear vegetable soup it provides the entire savory backbone. It is both a cooking paste, dissolved into liquid, and a raw condiment, eaten uncooked in ssamjang.

  Transformation under heat. Doenjang's glutamate and salt are heat-stable and survive long simmering, which is why it can be added at the start of a jjigae or guk and cooked for the duration, unlike miso, whose aromatics are fragile and demand a late, off-boil addition. The funk does mellow and round with extended cooking as the most volatile compounds drive off; a long-simmered doenjang stew tastes deeper and less sharp than a quick one. For raw applications (ssamjang, dressings) the full pungency is on display and is the point. There is no scorch risk the way there is with chili pastes, but boiling it hard for a very long time can flatten the brighter notes, so most cooks add it early enough to integrate but keep the simmer gentle.

  Anchored in dishes by category. As a soup and stew base it is foundational: doenjang-jjigae (the everyday soybean-paste stew with tofu, vegetables, and often clams or pork) and the lighter doenjang-guk built on anchovy-kelp stock are weeknight staples in most Korean homes. In braises it works behind a chili paste, deepening dishes like dak-bokkeum-tang (spicy braised chicken) where it supplies the savory funk under the gochujang sweetness. As a raw condiment it is the base of ssamjang, the thick dipping paste of doenjang plus gochujang, sesame oil, garlic, and scallion that accompanies grilled meats (samgyeopsal, bulgogi) wrapped in lettuce. It also seasons namul (dressed vegetable banchan) and can stand in for part of the salt in marinades. Across all of these the job is the same: deep savory funk, not heat, not sweetness.

  How it differs from miso, practically. Substituting Japanese miso for doenjang works in a pinch but flattens the dish. Doenjang is funkier, saltier, and earthier; miso is cleaner and often sweeter. Red (aka) miso is the closest single substitute because it shares some of the aged depth, but it lacks the Bacillus-driven pungency. When a recipe leans on doenjang specifically for that funk (ssamjang, doenjang-jjigae), the substitution is noticeable; when it uses a small amount as a background savory note (a spoonful in a chili braise), red miso passes acceptably.

storage: |
  Refrigerate after opening, in the original tub or an airtight container, pressed flat with a layer of plastic-free wrap (parchment or a beeswax sheet pressed to the surface) to limit air contact. The very high salt content makes it extremely shelf-stable: an opened tub keeps 6 months to a year refrigerated, and traditional doenjang was historically kept for years in earthenware crocks. Unopened commercial tubs keep well past a year in a cool pantry.

  Failure modes. A thin layer of white or pale film on the surface is usually harmless yeast (kahm) common to salty ferments; scrape it off and use what is beneath. Black, green, or fuzzy colored mold means contamination, typically from a dirty wet spoon; scrape off the mold plus a centimeter beneath, and if the rest smells normal it is fine. The real spoilage signals are an aggressively sour or sharply alcoholic smell (over-fermentation, often from too-warm storage) or any slimy texture change; both mean discard. A sharp ammonia note can develop with age; mild ammonia is within the normal funk of an aged paste, but a strong, eye-watering ammonia smell means it has gone too far. Always serve with a clean dry spoon; introducing water or food bits is what starts the colored molds.
