about: |
  Brewed from glutinous rice and a wheat-based qū starter, then aged in earthenware for one to ten years. The fermentation produces ethanol around 14–18% along with a wide pool of esters, higher alcohols, and amino acids. Those amino acids (especially glutamate and proline from broken-down wheat protein) are why a splash of Shaoxing carries weight a dry sherry can't quite reach.

  In cooking, the wine does two distinct jobs. Splashed against a hot wok during a stir-fry, the ethanol flashes off and the volatile esters carry through the rim of the pan, perfuming the dish. In a marinade or braise, the alcohol denatures surface proteins on meat and dissolves fat-soluble off-aromas — the trimethylamine compounds that read as "fishy" on poultry and seafood.

  Buy "hua diao" (花雕) grade for cooking — aged longer, deeper amber, more complex. Avoid bottles labeled "Shaoxing cooking wine" sold next to soy sauce in American supermarkets; those are salted to dodge alcohol-tax rules and will throw off the seasoning. A Chinese liquor store or grocery will carry the unsalted bottles in the wine aisle.

storage: 'Pantry, capped tight. Stable for years unopened. Once opened, oxidation flattens the aromatics within 6–12 months; refrigerate after opening to slow it.'

  - for: 'shaoxing wine'
    use: 'dry sake'
    note: 'Cleaner and less complex. Works in a pinch but the dish will read flatter; bump aromatics or add a few drops of soy.'
  - for: 'shaoxing wine'
    use: 'salted "shaoxing cooking wine"'
    note: 'Reduce added salt by ~1g per tablespoon swapped. Quality is markedly worse; use only if no real bottle is reachable.'
