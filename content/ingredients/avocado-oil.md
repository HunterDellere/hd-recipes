about: |
  Pressed from the flesh of the avocado, not the pit. The fatty acid profile is mostly oleic acid — the same monounsaturated fat that dominates olive oil — with smaller amounts of palmitic and linoleic. That profile is why it behaves like olive oil nutritionally but not flavor-wise.

  Smoke point depends on processing. Refined avocado oil hits ~270°C, unrefined ~250°C. Both are high enough for any home-kitchen sear or stir-fry, which is the main reason to keep it around: you can rip a steak in a cast-iron pan without setting off every smoke alarm in the house.

  Refined is neutral; unrefined is mildly grassy and faintly green. Most bottles labeled simply "avocado oil" are refined. If a bottle says "extra virgin" or "cold pressed" it''s unrefined; save that one for finishing salads and drizzling, not for a 240°C pan.

  The case for it over olive oil is specifically when you don''t want olive''s fruity, peppery register interfering: searing fish, stir-frying with delicate aromatics, frying eggs where you want the egg to taste like egg.

storage: 'Dark cupboard, capped tight. Keeps ~12 months unrefined, longer for refined. Light and oxygen drive rancidity; a bottle on the windowsill goes off in months. Smell it before pouring; rancid oil reads like crayons or old nuts.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'avocado oil (high-heat searing, frying)'
    use: 'refined sunflower, refined canola, or peanut oil'
    note: 'All neutral, all hold heat above 220°C. Peanut adds a faint nuttiness; the others are flavor-blank. Functionally identical for searing.'
  - for: 'avocado oil (finishing, dressings)'
    use: 'extra-virgin olive oil'
    note: 'Brings fruit and pepper notes that avocado oil doesn''t. Different flavor profile; use when you want olive''s presence, not when you want neutral.'
