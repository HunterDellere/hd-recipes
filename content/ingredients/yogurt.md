about: |
  Yogurt is whole milk fermented with a bacterial culture (Lactobacillus delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus + Streptococcus thermophilus, sometimes with Lactobacillus acidophilus and Bifidobacterium added for probiotic positioning) at 40 to 45°C / 105 to 115°F for 4 to 8 hours. The lactic-acid bacteria consume the lactose and produce lactic acid, which drops the pH from ~6.7 to ~4.5; at this acidity the casein micelles aggregate and the milk goes from liquid to a thick, tangy, semi-solid curd. South Asian dahi (homemade yogurt) is set fresh daily in many households and is somewhat softer and milder than commercial Western yogurts. Greek yogurt is yogurt that has been strained through cheesecloth to remove whey, concentrating the protein and producing a much thicker, more sour product.

  Cooking behavior. Yogurt has three structural roles in South Asian cooking. First, as a marinade base for tandoori applications: the lactic acid penetrates protein surfaces, partially denaturing the surface proteins and tenderizing the meat, while the dairy fat carries the spice-and-color compounds (Kashmiri chili, garam masala, ginger-garlic) into the meat. Second, as a curry base: yogurt is whisked into hot oil-spiced bases (with the heat reduced first to prevent splitting) to provide tang and body to dishes like dahi murgh and yogurt-based kormas. Third, as a finishing condiment: raita (yogurt + chopped vegetables + spices) is a cooling counterpoint to spicy curries and biryanis.

  The splitting problem. Yogurt added to hot sauces splits if not handled correctly: the casein curds break apart, the whey separates, and the sauce reads grainy. Three protections against splitting work well: (1) whisk yogurt with 1 tsp cornstarch or rice flour per 1 cup yogurt before adding (the starch stabilizes the casein), (2) temper the yogurt by stirring in a few tablespoons of the hot sauce before adding the rest of the yogurt (gradually warming the yogurt), (3) add yogurt only to off-heat or low-heat sauces and stir thoroughly before returning to gentle simmer. Indian cooks typically use all three strategies in combination. Greek yogurt is more prone to splitting than regular yogurt because of the higher protein concentration; whisk it with a splash of water before using.

  Whole milk vs reduced-fat. Whole-milk yogurt (4% milkfat) is the structural choice for South Asian cooking; the milkfat is what carries the fat-soluble spice compounds in marinades and what gives raita its mouthfeel. Reduced-fat (2%) and non-fat yogurt have insufficient milkfat for both applications and produce noticeably worse results. Use whole milk yogurt; if using non-fat for dietary reasons, accept that the dishes will read slightly different.

  Buying notes. Indian groceries sell large containers (1 to 2 kg) of plain whole-milk yogurt that is closer to traditional dahi than American supermarket yogurt — slightly less sour, slightly looser, and excellent for cooking. American supermarket plain whole-milk yogurt (Stonyfield, Wallaby, Dannon) works well; avoid "Greek-style" unless specifically called for, as the strained product behaves differently. Avoid yogurts with added pectin, gelatin, or thickeners — these interfere with cooking applications. Cultured buttermilk is a separate product and is not equivalent to yogurt for any of these applications.

storage: |
  Refrigerated, sealed in original container: as marked, typically 2 to 3 weeks past the printed date if continuously refrigerated. The lactic acid keeps the product safe; sourness increases over time as the bacteria continue working. Once opened, use within 7 to 10 days. Off-cues are mold (white-green fuzzy patches on the surface), pink or yellow discoloration, or sour-rancid smell beyond the normal yogurt tang; discard. Frozen yogurt: thaws into a separated, watery mess; do not freeze whole yogurt.
