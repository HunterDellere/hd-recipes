It is distinguished from Cuban sofrito (which is cooked from raw, no recao, green pepper only) and from Dominican sofrito (smaller blend, less herbaceous) by the dominance of recao and cilantro and by the inclusion of ajies dulces (the sweet small Caribbean pepper that gives Puerto Rican cooking its distinct fruit-floral aroma). Made in batches and kept in the fridge or frozen as ice cubes.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Recao (culantro, Eryngium foetidum, sometimes labeled "Mexican coriander" or "long coriander" or "ngo gai") is the structural herb of Puerto Rican sofrito and is hard to substitute. It tastes like a more aggressive cilantro with a celery edge. Available at Caribbean and Southeast Asian groceries. If unavailable, double the cilantro and add 5 g celery leaves; the dish reads less Puerto Rican but is acceptable.'
  - 'Ajies dulces are the small fruit-aromatic peppers of the Caribbean (Capsicum chinense, the same species as Scotch bonnet but without the heat). The flavor is structural and not really substitutable. If unavailable, the workaround is a small amount of red bell pepper plus a tiny pinch of habanero seeds (no flesh) for the aromatic without heat.'

notes: |
  Why this matters. Sofrito (recaito) is to Puerto Rican cooking what dashi is to Japanese cooking, what mirepoix is to French cooking, what mother sauce is to Italian. Nearly every Puerto Rican savory dish starts with a tablespoon of this paste hitting hot olive oil and blooming for 30 to 60 seconds. The dish that follows (rice, beans, stew, braise) inherits the entire green-herbaceous-garlic flavor profile of the sofrito. A Puerto Rican kitchen without sofrito in the fridge is a kitchen that has stopped cooking.

  The blender texture rule. Sofrito should look like wet pesto with visible flecks of green herb. Pureeing to a smooth paste produces a bitter sofrito because the herb cell walls all rupture and release the bitter membrane compounds. Pulse in short bursts, scrape down, stop before fully smooth.

  Why olive oil in the paste. The oil does two things: it helps the blender pull the mixture into a uniform paste rather than chopping unevenly, and it preserves the paste in storage by creating an oxygen barrier on the surface. Most home cooks pour an additional thin film on top after jarring for extended fridge life.

  Storage. Refrigerated in a sealed jar with a film of oil on top, 2 weeks. Frozen as ice cubes, 3 months. Most Puerto Rican home cooks make a 1 kg batch once a month; this recipe is half that, scaled for a 2-person household.

  How to use. Spoon 1 generous tbsp (or 1 frozen cube) into hot olive oil at the start of any savory dish; bloom 30 to 60 seconds until fragrant. Then proceed with the recipe (add tomato paste, add stock, add protein). The sofrito should sizzle aggressively when it hits the oil; this is the bloom that releases the volatile aromatics.
