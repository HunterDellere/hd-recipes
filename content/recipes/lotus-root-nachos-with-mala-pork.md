  note: 'The classical nacho (Piedras Negras, 1943) sets the format: a crisp starch base of uniform pieces, a melted-cheese binder, a saucy protein, and bright finishing elements. The Sichuan substitution swaps each layer for its Chinese analog while preserving the load mechanics. Tortilla chips become deep-fried lotus root rounds (the wagon-wheel cross section is the Sichuan dry-pot signature crunch). The melted-cheese binder becomes a silken-tofu queso whose creaminess comes from the lecithin in soy and the ground sesame in Chinese sesame paste, with white miso providing the salt-funk fat-emulsification role that aged cheese plays in classical nachos. The saucy protein becomes a Sichuan mala pork mince that reads as zha jiang sauce reformatted to drape rather than to coat noodles. The bright finishers become Chinkiang-pickled red onion (the vinegar-onion of the Sichuan cold-dish register), scallion, cilantro, sesame seeds, and a final pour of Sichuan chili oil with its sediment. The dish reads structurally as nachos and structurally as Sichuan at once.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The lotus root chips must be sliced uniform thin (1.5 to 2 mm) on a mandoline, not by knife. Hand-sliced rounds vary in thickness and fry unevenly, producing some chips that scorch while others stay leathery. A cheap Japanese plastic Benriner mandoline (under $30) is the right tool. Use the safety guard; lotus root is firm and the mandoline blade is unforgiving.'
  - 'The two-stage fry is structural. First stage at 130°C / 265°F for 3 to 4 minutes dehydrates the lotus and partially gelatinizes the starch. Second stage at 175°C / 350°F for 60 to 90 seconds puffs the chip and crisps the surface through final water evaporation. A single high-heat fry produces chips that scorch on the surface before the interior fully dehydrates, leaving leathery centers. Plan for two batches of frying with a 5 minute rest between.'
  - 'Salt the chips the second they come out of the second fry, while still glossy with oil. Salt does not adhere to cooled chips; the window is 30 seconds. A Sichuan peppercorn salt (4 g flaky salt toasted with 1 g ground Sichuan peppercorn) reads structurally more on-genre than plain salt and adds aromatic citrus-numb to the chip itself.'
  - 'The assembly is fast and the chips soften fast once topped. Have everything ready before assembling: pickled onion drained, queso warm, mala pork hot, herbs picked, chili oil within reach. The platter sits on the table for at most 6 minutes before the bottom chips go from crisp to chewy.'

steps:
  - text: 'Prep the lotus chips. Combine the 1000 ml / 4 cups cold water and 10 ml / 2 tsp white vinegar in a wide bowl. Peel the 600 g lotus root with a vegetable peeler. Set up a Japanese-style plastic mandoline to 1.5 to 2 mm and slice the lotus into rounds (with the safety guard, lotus is firm and the cut is brisk). Drop each round into the acid water as you slice; cut lotus oxidizes from white to gray-pink within 2 minutes of air exposure.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Drain the lotus rounds and pat extremely dry between two clean kitchen towels (residual surface moisture on the chips will spatter aggressively in the oil and produce uneven chips). The drier the chips, the better they fry; spend 2 full minutes on the drying step.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Heat the 1000 ml neutral oil in a 22 cm / 9 in heavy pot or 5 quart Dutch oven to 130°C / 265°F on a deep-fry thermometer. Fry the lotus rounds in 3 batches of about 200 g each, 3 to 4 minutes per batch, stirring gently with a chopstick or spider; the rounds will go from translucent to opaque, lose their water, and turn a faint pale gold. They are NOT yet crisp at this stage; they are dehydrated. Lift to a wire rack to drain. Bring the oil back to 130°C between batches.'
    technique: 'batter-frying'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Rest the par-fried lotus rounds 5 minutes (this lets residual interior moisture migrate to the surface, where it can evaporate in the second fry). Meanwhile, raise the oil temperature to 175°C / 350°F.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Second fry. Drop the rested lotus rounds into the 175°C oil in 3 batches again, 60 to 90 seconds per batch, stirring constantly with the spider; the rounds will puff slightly, go deep gold, and turn audibly crisp (you will hear the lotus go from soft hiss to sharp crackle when you stir). Lift each batch to a fresh wire rack lined with paper towel and immediately scatter with about a third of the 4 g / 2 tsp flaky sea salt and 1 g / ½ tsp ground Sichuan peppercorn (whisked together) while the chips are still glossy with oil. The salt blend does not adhere to cooled chips.'
    technique: 'batter-frying'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Pickle the red onion (this overlaps with the frying). Toss the 80 g paper-thin red onion with the 1 g salt and rest 5 minutes; squeeze out the onion liquid with your hands or in a kitchen towel. In a small bowl, whisk the 20 ml Chinkiang vinegar, 4 g sugar, and 5 ml light soy. Toss the squeezed onion in the pickle liquid and rest 10 minutes (the onion will turn from purple to magenta as the anthocyanins react to the acid).'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Mala pork mince. Heat the 15 ml / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a wide skillet or carbon-steel wok over medium-high. Add the 250 g ground pork and break it up with a wooden spoon, spreading it in a thin layer; brown without disturbing 90 seconds, then stir and brown another 3 minutes, breaking the pork into a coarse rubble. The pork should be deeply browned with visible fond on the pan bottom and dry-rendered rather than wet-poached. Push the pork to the outer ring of the pan, leaving a clear central well.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Render the doubanjiang in the central well. Add the 20 g doubanjiang to the rendered pork fat in the well; mash it with the back of the spoon. Cook 60 to 90 seconds until the oil turns brilliant red and the doubanjiang has released its aromatic compounds. Add the 10 g / 3 cloves garlic and 8 g ginger to the well and bloom 30 seconds. Pull the pork in from the outer ring and stir everything together so the red oil coats every grain of pork.'
    technique: 'red-oil-rendering'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Season the mince. Add the 15 ml / 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine and let it sizzle away (30 seconds). Add the 12 ml / 2½ tsp light soy, 4 ml / ¾ tsp dark soy, 5 g / 1¼ tsp sugar, and 3 g / 1½ tsp ground Sichuan peppercorn. Toss to coat. Cook 90 seconds; the mince should be glossy, mahogany, and tight (the liquids reduced to a clinging glaze rather than a pool).'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Off heat, hit the mince with the 10 ml / 2 tsp Chinkiang vinegar and 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil. Stir, taste, and adjust; the mince should read salt-funky-numb-spicy-faintly-sweet with a clean vinegar lift at the back. Hold warm (covered, off heat) while you finish the queso.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Silken-tofu queso. In a blender or with an immersion blender in a tall jar, combine the 340 g drained silken tofu, 25 g white miso, 25 g Chinese sesame paste, 10 ml / 2 tsp light soy, 8 ml / 1½ tsp mirin, 6 g / 2 tsp cornstarch, and 60 ml / ¼ cup warm water. Blend smooth (about 45 seconds; the blend should be silky, the color of pale gold, and pourable like heavy cream).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pour the queso blend into a small saucepan and place over medium-low heat. Whisk constantly for 2 minutes; the queso will tighten to a smooth nappe consistency (coats the back of a spoon, lifts in a clean ribbon when you stop whisking). Taste; if the seasoning reads under, add a few drops more light soy. Hold warm over the lowest heat with a whisk in the pan.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Assemble. Pile the lotus chips on a wide shallow platter or quarter-sheet pan in a single overlapping layer (about three chips deep at the center, falling to one chip deep at the edges). Spoon the warm mala pork mince over the chips in irregular dollops, covering about half the chips and leaving the other half exposed for crunch contrast.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Drizzle the warm queso over everything in a back-and-forth zigzag (use a squeeze bottle if you have one; otherwise drizzle from a measuring cup or large spoon, aiming for thin ribbons rather than puddles). Scatter the drained pickled red onion across the platter, then the 30 g scallion greens, 8 g cilantro leaves, and 6 g toasted sesame seeds.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Finish with a pour of the 15 ml / 1 tbsp Sichuan chili oil (include the sediment, that is where the aromatic load lives), drizzled across the platter in 10 to 15 small pools rather than one large pour. Bring the platter to the table immediately and eat with fingers, picking chips with toppings still attached. The chips soften from the bottom within 6 minutes.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why lotus root, not tortilla chips. A tortilla chip dressed with Chinese spice is a costume-party gimmick, not a fusion. Lotus root chips are the actual Sichuan canonical crunch (dry-pot lotus, deep-fried lotus snacks at night markets, lotus chips in Cantonese cold appetizers), and their wagon-wheel structure carries a stronger visual identity than a corn chip. The fry mechanics are similar enough that they swap cleanly into the nacho-base role: crisp, salt-able, load-bearing for wet toppings for about 5 minutes before softening.

  Why the silken-tofu queso. The hardest move in this fusion is replacing the melted-cheese binder, because aged dairy has no obvious Chinese analog. The structural insight is that the queso plays three roles at once: it contributes fat, salt, and emulsified body that drapes the topping together. Silken tofu plus white miso plus Chinese sesame paste plus light soy hits each of these roles through different molecular pathways: silken tofu provides water-bound protein and lecithin (creaminess), Chinese sesame paste provides emulsified plant fat (body), white miso provides aged-protein glutamate (salt-funk-umami in the cheese register), and the cornstarch tightens the system to nappe (drape). The result reads as queso functionally without reading as cheese flavorally; this is a feature.

  Why ground roasted Sichuan peppercorn rather than whole. Whole Sichuan peppercorns toasted in oil contribute background aromatic, but their numbing-citrus (the ma half of mala) is locked inside the husk and releases only when ground. For a dish where the ma is structural to the experience (rather than background), peppercorns must be toasted whole (90 seconds in a dry skillet over medium until fragrant), then ground in a spice grinder or mortar, and added late so the volatiles do not dissipate. Pre-ground Sichuan peppercorn from a jar has lost most of its volatile load and reads as a dusty echo; grind fresh for this dish.

  Why the two-stage fry. The first low-temperature fry (130°C) dehydrates the lotus and partially gelatinizes the starch without browning; the second high-temperature fry (175°C) puffs the chip through final water evaporation and Maillard-browns the surface. A single high-heat fry produces chips that scorch on the outside while leaving the interior leathery (because the water cannot escape fast enough through the already-crisped surface). The two-stage approach is borrowed from French fry technique and Chinese twice-fried chicken; it is the same physics across all three.

  The eating mechanic. Eat with fingers, picking chips with toppings still attached. The architecture is intentionally drape-and-scatter rather than melted-and-bound, so each chip carries a slightly different load of mince, queso, onion, and herb. The chili oil sediment lands wherever it lands; this is structural variation, not sloppiness.

  Storage. The dish does not store, full stop. Assembled nachos are a 6 minute window. The components store: lotus chips keep 2 days in an airtight container at room temperature (re-crisp 3 minutes in a 150°C oven before assembly), the mala pork keeps 3 days refrigerated and reheats well over rice or noodles, the silken-tofu queso keeps 24 hours refrigerated and re-warms gently with a splash of water, the pickled onion keeps 4 days refrigerated. Build the dish only at the moment of service.
