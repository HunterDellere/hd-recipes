  note: 'Traditional Provençal ratatouille is a rustic summer vegetable stew: zucchini, eggplant, tomato, bell pepper, and onion cubed and cooked together in olive oil until soft and integrated. The confit byaldi form (developed by Michel Guérard in the 1970s, refined by Thomas Keller, and made culturally iconic by the Pixar film) separates the dish into two structural components: a cooked piperade base (the integrated vegetable depth) and a raw-then-slow-baked vegetable rosette (the visual presentation and the textural distinction). The result reads more refined and presentation-driven than the rustic stew while preserving the Provençal flavor profile. The defining elements: bell peppers and tomato in the piperade base (the Italian peperonata-adjacent component), the thinly sliced vegetable rosette (zucchini, yellow squash, eggplant, tomato in a fanned overlap pattern), the slow confit bake at 135°C / 275°F (low enough to slowly collapse the vegetables without browning, preserving the concentric color pattern), and the fresh thyme and good olive oil finish.'

Add the 200 g / 1 medium onion (finely diced); sweat 8 to 10 minutes until translucent and soft but not browned. Add the 15 g / 4 cloves minced garlic; cook 90 seconds until fragrant and pale gold (not browned).'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Add the 200 g / 1 large red bell pepper and 200 g / 1 large yellow bell pepper (both finely diced). Cook 15 to 20 minutes over medium-low, stirring occasionally, until the peppers have softened completely and reduced in volume to a soft sweet base. The peppers should be tender enough to crush with the back of a wooden spoon.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Add the 400 g / ½ can hand-crushed San Marzano tomatoes (with juice), the 4 sprigs thyme leaves (stripped), 4 g / ¾ tsp salt, and 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. Cook 12 to 15 minutes uncovered until the mixture has reduced to a thick jammy paste and the olive oil has emulsified visibly throughout (the texture should be spreadable, not soupy; reduce more if necessary). Taste; adjust salt.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Preheat oven to 135°C / 275°F. Spread the piperade base in an even layer (about 1 cm thick) across the bottom of a 25 by 30 cm / 10 by 12 in oval or rectangular oven-safe baking dish (white ceramic shows the visual best). Smooth the surface with the back of a spoon.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Arrange the rosette: starting at the outer edge of the dish, lay slices of zucchini, yellow squash, eggplant, and tomato in a repeating pattern (one of each, repeated around the perimeter), each slice overlapping the previous by about 75 percent so only a 5 mm crescent of each slice is visible. Continue inward in concentric rings, each ring about 1.5 cm closer to the center, maintaining the four-vegetable repeating pattern. The whole top of the piperade should be covered by the rosette, with the four-color spiral pattern clearly visible. Standing the dish on a turntable (a lazy susan, or a cake decorating turntable) makes the assembly much faster.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Drizzle the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil evenly over the entire rosette surface. Sprinkle the 4 sprigs of stripped fresh thyme leaves and the 3 g / ½ tsp salt evenly over the top.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Cover the dish with a piece of parchment cut to fit just inside the rim (the parchment lid prevents the vegetable tops from drying out while allowing slow steam release; do not use foil, which traps too much moisture and produces a soggy rosette). Bake on the middle rack of the preheated 135°C / 275°F oven for 90 minutes. Pull the parchment off and bake another 15 to 25 minutes uncovered until the vegetable slices have collapsed against the piperade, the tops have lightly dried and concentrated their flavor, and the edges of some slices show the first hints of brown. The total bake is 105 to 115 minutes.'
    technique: 'oil-poaching'
    time_min: 115
  - text: 'Remove from the oven. Let stand 10 minutes; the dish improves with a brief rest as the residual heat continues to soften the vegetables and the olive oil settles into the piperade. Scoop large portions with a serving spoon, taking care to capture both the piperade base and the rosette layers in each portion.'

notes: |
  The two-component structure (piperade base plus raw-vegetable rosette) is the structural innovation of the confit byaldi form, and is the reason this version reads as more refined than the traditional rustic ratatouille. The piperade base provides the cooked depth: the slow-cooked peppers, onion, garlic, and tomato have integrated into a savory jammy paste with concentrated umami. The rosette provides the textural and visual contrast: thinly sliced raw vegetables (zucchini, yellow squash, eggplant, tomato) confit-baked slowly in olive oil and the piperade''s moisture, retaining their distinct shapes and colors while collapsing to silky tenderness. The combination reads as a deliberately composed dish rather than a stewed one.

  The 2 mm slice thickness is structural and a mandoline is essential. Hand-sliced rounds vary in thickness by 1 to 3 mm; this seems trivial but in the slow bake the uneven thicknesses cook to dramatically different textures (the 1 mm slices dry out, the 3 mm slices stay underdone). A consistent 2 mm produces uniformly silky vegetables across the whole rosette. A basic Y-shaped Japanese mandoline (Benriner) costs about $30 and pays for itself within three uses.

  The low confit bake (135°C / 275°F) is structural and the temperature is deliberately below the Maillard browning threshold (which begins around 150°C / 300°F). The low temperature collapses the vegetables to tenderness through slow heat-driven moisture release into the piperade, without browning the tops. The final 15 to 25 minutes uncovered concentrates the surface flavors slightly without producing the heavy browning that would muddy the bright color pattern of the rosette. Baking at 175°C / 350°F (the home-cook''s default vegetable-roast temperature) browns the rosette unevenly and produces a structurally different dish.

  The dish improves overnight. Like most slow-cooked vegetable preparations, ratatouille rests well: the vegetables continue to absorb the piperade base, the flavors integrate further, and the olive oil settles uniformly throughout. Cooks who can plan ahead should make the dish the day before serving and rewarm in a 135°C / 275°F oven for 20 minutes; many French and Italian cooks consider day-two ratatouille to be the canonical preparation, with day-one served as a hot main and day-two served at room temperature as antipasto or part of a Mediterranean spread.

  Storage. Keeps 5 days refrigerated, covered tightly. Eats excellent cold straight from the refrigerator as a Provençal antipasto, at room temperature as a salad, or warm as a side or main. Pairs beautifully with grilled lamb, grilled chicken, poached white fish, soft polenta, or with crusty bread and a wedge of goat cheese as a light vegetarian main. Does not freeze well; the texture of the squash and eggplant collapses to mush on thaw.
