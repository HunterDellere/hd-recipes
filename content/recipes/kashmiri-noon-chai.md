steps:
  - text: 'In a wide saucepan or kadai combine the 15 g / 3 tbsp green tea, the 480 ml / 2 cups water, the 2 g / ½ tsp baking soda, the 3 cracked cardamom pods, the 1 cinnamon stick, and the 1 star anise. Bring to a vigorous boil. The baking soda is the chemical engine of this tea, it raises the pH and accelerates oxidation of the catechins and theaflavins in the green tea, which is what produces the eventual pink color. Without baking soda you get green tea with milk; with baking soda you get noon chai.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Hold at a hard rolling boil for 15 to 20 minutes, uncovered, stirring and aerating constantly. The aeration is the second engine: use a ladle to scoop the boiling tea up to head height and pour it back into the pot from a long stream. Repeat this scoop-and-pour every minute or so. The exposure to oxygen during the pour is what drives the catechins to oxidize through orange-amber and finally to brick red. The liquid will reduce by roughly half and turn a deep red-brown, that is correct.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'When the brew is a dark brick red and reduced to about 240 ml / 1 cup of liquid, pour the ice-cold 240 ml / 1 cup of water into the pot from height in a single sharp stream. The thermal shock plus the addition of unoxidized water causes a visible color flip, the brew turns from brick red to a rose-pink in seconds. This is the chemistry signature: the partially oxidized polyphenols rearrange when diluted and cooled, and the apparent color shifts dramatically. If your tea does not pink, you under-aerated; restart with an extra 5 minutes of vigorous boiling and pouring.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Strain the rose-pink decoction through a fine sieve into a clean pot, discarding the spent leaves and spices. The strained liquid is your tea base; it holds in the refrigerator 3 days and reactivates with hot milk on demand.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'In the same clean pot, add the 480 ml / 2 cups whole milk to the strained decoction. Bring to a gentle simmer over medium heat, do not hard boil; once milk is in, hard boiling breaks the emulsion and the color shifts brownward. Hold the simmer 2 minutes. The pink tea base diluted with white milk produces the characteristic pale salmon-pink of finished noon chai.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Stir in the 3 g / ½ tsp salt and, if using, the 10 g / 2 tsp sugar. Taste. Noon chai is meant to taste savory-balanced, not sweet, salt is the lead seasoning, sugar is optional and never dominant. Pour into warmed cups. Garnish each with a pinch of the 15 g crushed pistachios and the optional 10 g almonds.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Serve hot. Traditionally accompanied by Kashmiri sheermal or any rich flat bread; the bread soaks up the tea and the pistachios contribute the textural counterpoint missing from the liquid alone.'

notes: |
  The pink color is real chemistry, not food dye. Many online recipes shortcut with a drop of red food coloring; the result tastes flat because the actual flavor transformation that produces real pink chai comes from the same oxidation process that produces the color. Baking soda + sustained aeration + cold-water shock is a three-step chemical sequence that creates both the color and the characteristic mineral-bitter-sweet-savory flavor balance.

  Aeration matters more than time. Twenty minutes of hard boiling without scoop-and-pour gives a flat dark tea; ten minutes with constant aeration gives a brighter pink. The mechanical action drives the oxidation reactions that color development depends on.

  Salt is the seasoning, not sugar. Noon literally means "salt" in Kashmiri; this is a salted tea by definition. Western palates often reach for sugar to "fix" the savory note, that reflex destroys the dish. Try one cup with only salt before reaching for the sugar; the savory balance grows on the second cup.
