steps:
  - text: 'Heat a wide dry skillet (stainless or cast iron) over medium-low. Add the sesame seeds in a single layer. Stir constantly with a wooden spoon. The seeds will go through three visible stages: pale white → straw → caramel-amber. The smell shifts in parallel: grassy → bread-crust → cocoa-coffee.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Pull off heat at deep amber, just before any seeds threaten to char. The carryover from the hot pan will continue browning for 30 seconds; under-toast on the pan if anything. The visual target: caramel cookie color, not coffee bean.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Tip immediately onto a cool plate or sheet pan to stop the cooking. Let cool 5 minutes, until they''re warm but no longer steaming. Hot seeds clog the food processor; cold seeds refuse to release their oil.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Transfer the warm seeds to a food processor with the 2 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt. Run continuously for 4–5 minutes. The texture passes through three stages: crumbly powder (1 min) → wet sand (2 min) → seizing dough-ball (3 min) → loose paste (4–5 min). Scrape down the sides every minute.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'When the mixture has passed the dough-ball stage and is starting to spin smoothly as a paste, drizzle in 30 g / 2 tbsp toasted sesame oil with the motor running. The paste loosens immediately. If you want it thinner, add the optional 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil now. Process another 30 seconds until uniform and glossy.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Test the texture: it should be the consistency of natural peanut butter: thick enough to mound on a spoon, loose enough to drop slowly off it. For dan dan and dipping sauces this is the right viscosity; the recipe will thin it further with soy and vinegar.'

notes: |
  The chemistry of toast: sesame seeds at ~150°C develop pyrazines and furans through Maillard reactions on their surface proteins. Below 130°C you get nothing; above 175°C the lignans (the antioxidant compounds in the seeds) start breaking down and the paste turns bitter. The 12-minute window in the dry pan is what hits the right zone, and why a sheet pan in a 175°C oven can also work, but with less control over the moment.

  Why hulled, not unhulled: unhulled (tan-skinned) seeds carry more oxalates and a slightly bitter tannin from the seed coat. They make excellent tahini, where lightness is the goal. For the deeper-roasted Chinese paste, the hull gets in the way of the clean caramel character.

  Why this isn't tahini: Levantine tahini is hulled seeds toasted only briefly (or not at all) and ground long. The result is pale, slightly bitter, and very loose. Chinese sesame paste is dark-toasted, less sweet, more savory, and stiffer. The two are not interchangeable in their native dishes. You can use tahini for dan dan in a pinch, but it will read flat and oily. Reverse direction (sesame paste for hummus) is even worse: the toasted character clobbers the chickpea.

  The seizing stage is where most home cooks panic. The dough-ball is the seeds' lipid breakdown happening: the cell walls are rupturing under shear and the oil is mobilizing. Keep going. Adding oil before the seizing stage just gives you wet sand that never breaks down. The oil goes in *after* the seizing.

  Storage: glass jar, room temperature, dark cupboard, 6 weeks. Refrigerate and it stiffens to spreadable-cold-butter consistency; fine, but warm to room temp before using. The oil will separate over time; stir it back together. Mold appears only if water gets in (wet spoon).

  Where it earns its place: dan dan noodles (the canonical use), Beijing sesame noodles, hot pot sesame dipping sauce, jar lid scraped onto plain rice when nothing else is available. A spoonful in a stir-fry pan deglaze gives an instant savory base.
