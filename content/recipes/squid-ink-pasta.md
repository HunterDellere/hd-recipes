steps:
  - text: 'Prep the squid. Cut the tube portions into 1.5 cm rings; cut the tentacles into 4 cm pieces or leave whole if small. Pat dry with paper towels (a wet squid steams instead of searing in the initial sauté).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Begin the squid braise. Heat the 60 ml / ¼ cup olive oil in a wide heavy saucepan over medium heat. When the oil is shimmering (not smoking), add the 25 g chopped garlic and 1 g / ½ tsp red pepper flakes. Sweat 60 to 90 seconds until the garlic is pale gold and fragrant, do not brown the garlic past pale gold or it goes bitter. Add the squid rings and tentacles. Stir-fry 90 seconds; the squid will release water in the first minute, then begin to firm up. The squid is in the rubber zone right now (this is normal); the long simmer will rescue it.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Deglaze and braise. Pour in the 240 ml / 1 cup white wine. Bring to a simmer; the wine should reduce by half within 3 minutes (alcohol cooks off, the sharp wine note mellows into a savory base). Add the 400 g hand-crushed San Marzano tomatoes with their juice, 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt, and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper. Bring back to a simmer; reduce heat to low (the surface should tremble, not boil). Cover loosely with a lid and braise 35 minutes. The squid will progress: rubbery and contracted at 5 minutes, slightly more relaxed at 15 minutes, properly tender at 30 to 35 minutes. Test by cutting a ring with a knife, it should yield easily and the texture should read silky-tender, not chewy.'
    technique: 'deglazing'
    time_min: 38
  - text: 'Boil the pasta. Bring 4500 ml / 18 cups water to a rolling boil in a large pot. Add 70 g / ¼ cup kosher salt, the water must taste like the sea. Add the 400 g linguine and cook 1 minute less than the package al dente time (typically 8 to 9 minutes; check by biting a strand at minute 8). Reserve 250 ml / 1 cup of the starchy pasta water before draining. Drain the pasta but do not rinse.'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Add the squid ink. Once the squid braise has reached tenderness, stir in the 8 g squid ink. The sauce will turn dramatically jet-black within seconds; whisk gently to distribute the ink evenly. Continue simmering 2 to 3 minutes for the ink to integrate into the sauce, the ink contributes a faint marine-mineral umami in addition to the color, and a brief simmer rounds out the flavor. Taste the sauce; it should land at deeply savory, faintly briny, with the long-cooked squid having transferred gelatin into the sauce body. Adjust salt if needed.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Toss with pasta. Add the drained linguine directly to the squid braise pan. Toss vigorously with tongs to coat every strand black. Add 60 ml / 4 tbsp of the reserved pasta water; the starchy water emulsifies with the olive oil and ink to produce a glossy clinging sauce that coats every strand. If the sauce is too thick, add another 30 to 60 ml of pasta water to thin. Cook on medium heat 60 seconds while tossing, the pasta finishes its last minute of cooking in the sauce, absorbing flavor in a way that draining-and-saucing later cannot match.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Finish and plate. Pull off heat. Add the 15 g chopped parsley, 8 ml / 1½ tsp fresh lemon juice, and 15 ml / 1 tbsp finishing olive oil. Toss once. Plate into wide pasta bowls; the pasta should be deeply black with the squid pieces visible against the dark sauce. Serve immediately. Bread on the side for sopping the sauce; a glass of the same white wine used in the braise is the canonical pairing.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The 30-minute low-simmer window. Squid cookery operates on a strict rule (see ingredients/squid): tender at 90 seconds high heat, tender again at 30 to 45 minutes low simmer, rubber in between. This dish is the textbook low-simmer-window application. The squid hits the pan, sears briefly in the aromatic oil, gets immediately submerged in wine and tomato, and braises at low simmer for 35 minutes. The first 5 to 10 minutes the squid is in the rubber zone (firm, contracted, chewy); over the next 25 minutes the dense collagen sheets hydrolyze into gelatin and the squid relaxes from rubber back into silken-tender. The gelatin released from the squid also enriches the sauce body, this is why the long-simmered Italian squid sauce reads thicker and more "saucy" than the Asian short-cook squid stir-fries even though the ingredients lists are similar. The cook has to commit to the long simmer; pulling at 15 minutes (when the squid still tastes chewy) wastes the dish. Trust the timing.

  Why hand-crush the tomatoes. Hand-crushing whole peeled tomatoes (squeezing them between your fingers in a bowl) produces a sauce with both pulpy chunks and pulpy juice, varying texture across the dish. Pre-crushed or diced tomatoes have a more uniform and slightly more processed cooked-tomato flavor; the difference is small but real. Keep a colander or heavy paper towels nearby, hand-crushed tomatoes can splatter.

  The ink contributes more than color. Squid ink is melanin in a small amount of mucin and amino acids; the major contribution is visual, but the secondary contribution is a faint marine umami that compounds with the rest of the sauce in a way that pure black food coloring would not. The amount in this recipe (8 g, equivalent to two standard packets) is calibrated for the dramatic jet-black color; smaller amounts (4 g) produce a deep gray-brown sauce with the umami contribution but less visual impact. Both are valid choices.

  Ink staining. Squid ink stains everything: clothes, wooden cutting boards, lightly-glazed ceramic. Cook in an apron, work over a non-porous surface, wipe spills immediately with paper towels (not a sponge, the sponge will stain too). The pasta water will be black; rinse the pot promptly. Teeth and lips will go black during eating (this is part of the dish, embrace it; provide napkins; warn dinner-party guests). The black washes off teeth in 2 to 3 minutes of normal eating-and-drinking; do not worry about it.

  Eating mechanics. Squid ink pasta is a date-night and dinner-party dish, visually dramatic, intensively flavored, one-bowl. Pair with a crisp acidic Italian white wine (verdicchio, vermentino, soave). A small green salad before or after is the usual Italian framing; bread on the side for sopping. The dish reheats acceptably the next day, the squid stays tender (the long-simmer-window protection) and the sauce only gets richer as it sits. Add a splash of pasta water and a drizzle of olive oil when reheating to refresh the emulsion.
