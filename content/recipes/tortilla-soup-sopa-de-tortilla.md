steps:
  - text: 'Build the broth. Combine the 600 g chicken pieces, 2000 ml water, 75 g halved onion (half of the broth-base onion), 15 g smashed garlic, 12 g salt, and 2 bay leaves in a large stockpot. Bring to a simmer over medium-high; skim off any gray scum that rises in the first 10 minutes (this scum is coagulated blood proteins and is bitter). Reduce to medium-low, cover, simmer 35 to 40 minutes until the chicken is tender and falling off the bone. Lift the chicken out; let cool. Strain the broth through a fine-mesh sieve, discarding the solids. Shred the chicken meat with two forks; discard the bones and skin.'
    time_min: 45
  - text: 'Toast and rehydrate the pasilla chiles. While the broth simmers, remove the stems and seeds from the 15 g dried pasilla chiles. Toast the chiles in a dry skillet over medium heat for 30 to 45 seconds per side, until pliable, fragrant, and slightly darker (do not burn; scorched chiles read bitter). Transfer to a small heatproof bowl; cover with 200 ml of boiling water; soak 15 minutes until soft and rehydrated.'
    time_min: 17
  - text: 'Char the tomatoes. Place the 500 g tomatoes on a sheet pan; broil 6 to 8 minutes, turning once, until the skins are blistered and blackened in spots and the tomatoes have softened. The char is the structural step that adds depth to the soup base; raw tomatoes produce a thinner, less complex broth.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Blend the salsa base. Combine the charred tomatoes (skins and all), the rehydrated pasilla chiles (with about 60 ml of their soaking liquid), the 75 g remaining raw onion, the 10 g raw garlic, and the 2 g epazote in a blender. Blend on high for 60 seconds until smooth.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Fry the salsa. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide saucepan over medium-high heat until shimmering. Pour the blended salsa into the hot oil (it will spit aggressively). Cook 5 to 6 minutes, stirring, until the salsa has darkened and concentrated slightly, and the kitchen smells deeply of tomato and chile. The fry-the-salsa step is structurally what gives the soup its depth; skipping it produces a thinner, raw-tasting broth.'
    technique: 'red-oil-rendering'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Combine and simmer the soup. Pour the strained chicken broth into the saucepan with the fried salsa. Add the shredded chicken back to the soup. Bring to a simmer over medium heat; simmer 15 minutes uncovered to integrate the flavors. Taste; adjust salt. The soup should be a clear-ish reddish-brown broth with the shredded chicken suspended throughout, deeply savory with a faint smoky pasilla aromatic.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Fry the tortilla strips. While the soup simmers, heat the 300 ml / 1¼ cups neutral oil in a deep skillet to 175°C / 350°F. Fry the 8 tortillas worth of strips in 3 to 4 batches, 90 seconds per batch, until golden and crisp. Lift with a spider; drain on paper towels; immediately salt with the 2 g fine sea salt while still hot.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Set up the table garnishes. Dice the 200 g avocado just before serving (cut avocado browns within 30 minutes). Arrange the avocado, 150 g crumbled queso fresco, 150 g Mexican crema, 12 lime wedges, 20 g chopped cilantro, and the optional crumbled toasted pasilla chile in small bowls on the table. Each garnish goes on each diner''s soup at the table; this self-serve assembly is structural to the eating experience.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Serve. Ladle the hot soup into wide shallow bowls. Top each bowl with a generous handful of the fried tortilla strips (the strips must go on at the moment of serving, not pre-mixed; they crisp on top of the hot broth and slowly soften as the diner eats, providing textural contrast through the meal). Each diner then adds avocado, queso fresco, a drizzle of crema, a squeeze of lime, and a scatter of cilantro. The dish is constructed at the table; this is the canonical Mexican presentation.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  The garnish is half the dish. Sopa de tortilla without the garnish set (fried tortilla strips, avocado, queso fresco, crema, lime, cilantro) is a thin chicken-and-tomato broth. The garnishes are structurally what make the dish: the tortilla strips provide texture and the dish-name itself, the avocado provides creamy fat to balance the broth, the queso fresco provides salt-and-tang, the crema provides cooling counter to the chile, the lime provides the bright finishing acid, the cilantro provides aromatic lift. Skipping any of these flattens the dish significantly. The Mexican table-service tradition lets each diner customize the balance; this is part of the dish design.

  Stale tortillas are structurally better. Day-old (or even older) corn tortillas crisp better and faster than fresh tortillas because the lower moisture content lets the oil reach the cooking temperature on the surface faster. Fresh tortillas can be used but take longer to crisp and tend to absorb more oil. The traditional Mexican household uses tortillas that are 1 to 2 days old for tortilla soup specifically; saving leftover tortillas for the soup is the canonical practice.

  Why fry the salsa. The "freír la salsa" technique (frying blended salsa in hot oil before incorporating into the soup) is structural to Mexican cooking; the hot-oil contact develops new Maillard products in the salsa, concentrates the flavor by driving off water, and changes the color from bright red to deeper red-brown. Salsa added to the soup without the fry produces a thinner, raw-tasting broth that reads as undercooked. The fry step is non-negotiable for canonical Mexican soups, sauces, and braises.

  Epazote is structurally distinctive. Epazote (Dysphania ambrosioides) is a Mexican herb with no good substitute; the flavor is anise-medicinal-citrus, distinct and immediately identifiable. It is used canonically in Mexican tomato-and-bean dishes (sopa de tortilla, frijoles de la olla, quesadillas de huitlacoche, mole verde). Available at Mexican groceries fresh (in small bunches) or dried (in small bags); the dried version is acceptable but the fresh is dramatically better. Do not substitute oregano, basil, or any Mediterranean herb; the flavor profile is too different and the dish reads wrong.

  Storage. The broth keeps 5 days refrigerated and freezes 3 months. Reheat gently; the shredded chicken can dry out on reheat (add a splash of stock to compensate). The garnishes do not store; prepare fresh just before serving. Fried tortilla strips keep 24 hours at room temperature in a sealed container (re-crisp briefly in a 175°C / 350°F oven for 3 minutes if they have softened). The dish is structurally a make-the-broth-ahead, prepare-garnishes-fresh preparation.
