  - text: 'Peel a 6 cm knob of ginger with the edge of a spoon (scrape, do not peeler-strip; the spoon removes the thin skin without losing the flesh underneath). The microplaned paste should look wet and stringy, not chopped or coarse-grated; the wetness is the released cell juice that does the emulsifier work.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Trim and finely mince the 3 g / 1 to 2 thai chilies, seeds in for full heat (seeds out for milder). Add the chili to the bowl with the ginger.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the 60 g / 4 tbsp lime juice, the 25 g / 1½ tbsp honey, and the 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt to the ginger-and-chili bowl. Whisk vigorously for 20 to 30 seconds to dissolve the salt and the honey; the salt particles should disappear, and the honey should integrate without streaks. The mixture should look uniformly cloudy from the suspended ginger particles.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Stream in the 80 g / ⅓ cup neutral oil in a thin steady drizzle while whisking constantly. The dressing tightens as the oil emulsifies into the lime-honey-ginger base; the microplaned ginger acts as the structural emulsifier (the same role mustard plays in a classic French vinaigrette), and the dressing should ribbon off the whisk and slowly settle back into the surface. Whisk for 10 to 15 seconds after the last of the oil to integrate. Taste; the dressing should read sharp-acid first, then sweet, then heat in the throat. notes: |
  Why microplaned ginger and not grated. A microplane reduces the ginger fibers to a wet paste and releases the structural cell juice; a box-grater or food-processor grate produces coarser threads that do not emulsify and instead sit as suspended particulate. The microplane is the difference between a dressing that holds for 30 minutes and one that breaks in 5. If a microplane is unavailable, the second-best option is mortar-and-pestle pounding of the peeled ginger to a paste; the third option is a fine-grate followed by a 30-second mash with the back of a spoon against the bowl. Avoid the food processor; the volume is too small to process and the ginger oxidizes brown in the spinning blade.

  Why the dressing breaks (and how to rebuild). The emulsion in this dressing is stabilized by the microplaned ginger and not by an egg yolk or mustard, so it is less stable than a classic French vinaigrette and re-breaks faster on storage. After 20 to 30 minutes of standing, you will see a small oil layer separating on top; this is normal and the dressing is fine. Rebuild by giving the jar a vigorous 10-second shake or a 30-second whisk; the emulsion reforms. If the dressing has been refrigerated overnight, let it come to room temperature first (the cold oil is too viscous to re-emulsify) and then whisk or shake.

  Storage and batch scaling. The dressing refrigerates 4 to 5 days in a sealed jar; the ginger continues to release water and the dressing thins slightly over 48 hours. The recipe scales linearly to 2x or 3x; beyond 3x, scale the chili last and add to taste because chili heat varies plant-to-plant. Freezing is not recommended; the emulsion does not survive a thaw and the ginger texture goes mushy.

  The dressing in context. Built primarily for the savory millet salad with mango and mint, and for grain bowls with peanut-coated chicken thigh; equally good as a quick marinade for grilled fish (20 minutes contact time, no longer or the lime cooks the surface), as a dipping sauce for raw vegetables (cucumber, carrot, jicama), and as a finishing drizzle on roasted carrots or charred broccoli. The dressing is structurally a no-cook preparation; the only cooking question is whether to bloom the thai chili briefly in the oil (which produces a redder oil and a slightly milder dressing) or to keep the chili fresh-minced (which produces the sharper, more aromatic version, and is the default here).
