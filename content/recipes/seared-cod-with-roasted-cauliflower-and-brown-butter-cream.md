before_you_start:
  - 'Thaw frozen cod fully (overnight in the refrigerator) and pat it bone dry, then salt and pat again before searing. Surface moisture is the enemy of both the crust and the sauce: wet fish steams instead of browning, and water shed into the pan thins the cream.'
  - 'Get the cauliflower into a hot oven first. It needs 25 to 30 minutes and should come out as the sauce finishes; sear the cod and build the sauce while it roasts.'
  - 'Stage every sauce component (shallot minced, wine, cream, dijon, capers, lemon, dill) on the counter before the cod hits the pan. Once the fish is seared you have a 6 minute window to brown the butter and build the sauce while the cod rests.'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 230C / 450F. Toss the 600 g cauliflower florets with the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil, 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt, and 1 g / ¼ tsp black pepper directly on a rimmed sheet pan, then turn the florets cut-face-down in a single uncrowded layer. The dry, high heat caramelizes the brassica sugars and drives off the sulfurous volatiles that low, wet cooking releases.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Roast 25 to 30 minutes, undisturbed for the first 18 to 20 so the cut faces build an unbroken brown crust, then a single toss to color the second face. The florets are done when the cut faces are deep mahogany and a paring knife slides through the stem with no resistance. Hold them in the warm oven while you finish the fish.'
    time_min: 28
  - text: 'While the cauliflower roasts, prep the cod. Pat the 340 g cod fillets bone dry, season both sides with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt, and let them sit 10 minutes, then pat once more (the salt pulls a film of moisture you want gone before the sear). If using, dredge the fillets in the 20 g / 2½ tbsp flour and shake off all but a thin veil.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Heat the 14 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a wide stainless skillet over medium-high until it shimmers and the first wisp of heat rises. Lay the cod presentation-side down, press each fillet flat for 10 seconds for full contact, then sear undisturbed 3 to 4 minutes to a deep gold crust. Flip and cook 1 to 2 minutes more to 52 to 54C internal. Transfer to a plate; the fish climbs to its final doneness by carryover while you build the sauce.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Lower the heat to medium and add the 45 g / 3 tbsp butter to the same pan. Swirl past the foam until the milk solids turn amber and the butter smells of toasted hazelnut, about 2 minutes. Stir in the 5 g / 1 minced anchovy fillet and let it dissolve completely into the fat, 30 seconds; it disappears and leaves only glutamate depth.'
    technique: 'brown-butter'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 40 g / 1 large minced shallot and sweat 60 to 90 seconds to translucent, no color. Pour in the 60 g / ¼ cup dry white wine, scrape the fond off the pan, and reduce until the pan is nearly dry and syrupy, about 90 seconds. This cooks off the raw-alcohol edge and concentrates the shallot.'
    technique: 'deglazing'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour in the 120 g / ½ cup heavy cream and simmer until it coats the back of a spoon (nappe), 2 to 3 minutes. Pull the pan off the heat and whisk in the 8 g / 1 tsp dijon mustard, the 15 g / 1 tbsp drained capers, the juice of the half lemon (15 g) and ½ tsp of its grated zest. Taste: it should read bright, savory, and a touch sharp against the rich cream. Stir in the 5 g / 2 tbsp chopped dill off heat so it stays green.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Spread the 600 g roasted cauliflower across two warm plates and shower it with the 15 g / 3 tbsp grated parmigiano-reggiano while it is hot enough to melt. Set a cod fillet on each bed and spoon the brown butter cream generously over the fish so it pools into the cauliflower. Serve at once.'
    time_min: 2

    how: 'Skip the sear. After the cauliflower has roasted 18 minutes, clear two gaps, nestle the salted cod fillets in, brush with olive oil, and roast 8 to 10 minutes more to 54C. Build the brown butter cream in a small saucepan. Lower crust, far less cleanup, the right call on a true weeknight.'

notes: |
  Why brown butter under cream. Cod is the leanest white fish on the counter, and lean fish reads flat without an outside source of savory depth. Browning the butter before the cream goes in builds a layer of Maillard milk-solid flavor that a straight cream sauce never develops, and it is the single change that takes this from "fish in cream" to a sauce with a spine. The dissolved anchovy compounds that depth with glutamate; you will not taste it as anchovy, only as a sauce that tastes like it was built on stock.

  The doneness rule. Pull the cod at 52 to 54C in the thickest part and let carryover finish it. The USDA target of 63C / 145F leaves cod chalky and dry because there is almost no intramuscular fat to hold moisture. The flake should just separate under gentle pressure and still look glossy at the center when it comes off the heat. An instant-read thermometer is worth more on lean fish than on any steak.

  Frozen is fine, wet is not. Frozen-at-sea cod is often better than the "fresh" cod that has sat thawed on ice for days. The only discipline frozen fish demands is thorough thawing and thorough drying: thaw overnight in the fridge, then press the fillets between paper towels until the surface is matte, not slick. A wet fillet will not crust, and the water it sheds breaks the sauce.

  Where the sauce breaks and how to save it. If the cream is simmered too hard it can look grainy; pull it off heat and whisk in a teaspoon of cold water or an extra knob of cold butter to bring it back. If it goes too thick while you plate, loosen with a splash of the pasta-less cooking liquid or warm water. Add the lemon and dill only at the very end, off heat: acid added into a hard-boiling cream can curdle it, and dill cooked in the sauce turns drab.
