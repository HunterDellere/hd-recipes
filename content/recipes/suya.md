source:
  note: 'Suya is the Nigerian (specifically Hausa) grilled-meat street food of the north, sold in the evenings by mai suya vendors at corner stalls across Nigerian cities. The yaji spice rub is the entire technique: ground roasted peanuts (the structural element that distinguishes suya from any other grilled-meat tradition), ground dried chili (Nigerian red dried pepper or cayenne), ginger powder, onion powder, paprika, salt, and often clove and bouillon. The meat is sliced thin (4 to 5 mm) so the spice rub coats a high surface area and the cook is fast over high direct heat. Eaten with raw sliced onion and tomato as the cooling counterpoint and with extra yaji as a dipping spice.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The 30 to 60 minute rub-rest is structural; the dry yaji needs to bind to the surface moisture of the meat and the peanut oil needs to penetrate slightly. Plan for the rest. Longer (up to 4 hours) is fine and produces deeper flavor.'
  - 'Slice the beef while partially frozen (30 to 45 minutes in the freezer makes the cut dramatically easier). The slices need to be 4 to 5 mm thick for the cook to work; thicker pieces undercook or the spice burns waiting for the interior.'
  - 'Make the yaji from scratch; commercial suya spice exists in some West African groceries but the freshly ground roasted peanut is dramatically better. The peanut grind is the structural element.'
  - 'Cook over high direct heat: live coals (the canonical method), or a hot gas grill, or under a hot broiler 10 cm from the element. The yaji rub blisters and chars at high heat; medium heat produces dusty grey skewers.'
  - 'Soak wooden skewers in water for 30 minutes if using; metal skewers are sturdier and reusable.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the yaji. In a dry skillet over medium-low heat, toast the 100 g / ¾ cup raw peanuts for 8 to 10 minutes, shaking the pan every 30 to 45 seconds, until the peanuts are deep golden brown and smell distinctly nutty. Transfer to a plate to cool for 5 minutes; the residual heat keeps cooking them and they go bitter if left in the hot pan.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Grind the cooled peanuts in a spice grinder or food processor in 5 to 10 second pulses until the texture is somewhere between coarse cornmeal and fine sand. Do not over-grind or the peanuts release oil and form peanut butter; the yaji needs a dry powder consistency. If it does start to clump, that is fine, the meat sticking helps the rub adhere.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'In a small bowl, combine the ground peanuts with the 15 g / 2 tbsp sweet paprika, the 6 g / 2 tsp cayenne, the 6 g / 2 tsp ground ginger, the 8 g / 1 tbsp onion powder, the 5 g / 1½ tsp garlic powder, the 1 g / ¼ tsp ground cloves, the 10 g / 2½ tsp kosher salt, and the 4 g / 1 tsp Maggi powder if using. Mix thoroughly with a fork. The yaji should be a uniform terracotta-orange powder with a strong peanut-and-chili aroma. Reserve about one-third of the yaji (roughly 50 g) in a small bowl for the table as dipping spice; the rest is the rub.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Prepare the beef. Pull the partially frozen 700 g / 1½ lb beef from the freezer and slice against the grain into 4 to 5 mm thick strips, 8 to 10 cm long. The partially frozen meat slices cleanly; fully thawed meat tears under a knife.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'In a large bowl, toss the beef strips with the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil until every piece is lightly coated. Add the rub portion of the yaji (about 100 g) and toss again until every strip is heavily coated. Use your hands; the rub adheres better when worked in. Let the rubbed meat rest at room temperature for 30 minutes (or refrigerated for up to 4 hours). The salt in the rub draws surface moisture out, which dissolves the rub and forms a thin spice paste on the meat that grills into a deep crust.'
    time_min: 32
  - text: 'Thread the beef strips lengthwise onto skewers, 3 to 4 strips per skewer, spreading them out slightly so the air can circulate around each piece. Press any spice rub that fell off during threading back onto the skewered meat.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Heat a charcoal grill, gas grill, or broiler to high. For the broiler, set the rack 10 cm from the element; for a grill, the coals should be glowing red with no flame and the grates hot enough that water flicked on them flashes off immediately. Brush the grates with oil.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Cook the skewers over direct high heat for 2 to 3 minutes per side, flipping once or twice. The yaji rub blisters and develops a deep crusty char; the meat inside cooks to medium (slightly pink) in this short window. Pull from heat as soon as the rub has the desired char and the meat shows the first beads of moisture on the surface (overcooking past this point dries the thin strips quickly).'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Serve immediately. Arrange the skewers on a platter with the 150 g / 1 onion worth of thin red onion rings, the 150 g / 2 tomatoes worth of tomato rounds, the 30 g / 4 lime wedges, and the small bowl of reserved yaji as dipping spice. Diners squeeze lime over the meat, dip pieces into extra yaji as desired, and eat the onion and tomato alongside as cooling counterpoint to the spice heat.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why ground peanut is structural. The peanut grind is what makes yaji yaji rather than a generic dry-spice rub. The ground peanut contributes fat (which carries the chili and spice flavors), texture (the slightly sandy grind that adheres to the meat), and a recognizable nutty register that reads through every bite. Other West African and African-diaspora grilled meats use dry rubs but the peanut is the Nigerian Hausa signature.

  Why the meat is sliced thin. The thin-slice method (4 to 5 mm) maximizes the surface area for the spice rub, gives the high direct heat time to char the rub crust before the meat overcooks, and produces a chewy-tender texture that is the canonical suya bite. Thicker cuts produce a different dish; either the rub burns waiting for the interior or the interior is raw when the rub is right.

  Why high heat. The yaji rub depends on hot direct heat to blister and char into a crust; medium heat dehydrates the rub into a dusty grey coating. Live coals, a screaming-hot gas grill, or a broiler at 10 cm distance are the three working setups. Anything cooler and the dish does not work.

  Why the resting yaji on the plate. The reserved one-third of the yaji on the table as dipping spice is canonical; diners adjust their own heat and intensity by dipping pieces into extra yaji as they eat. The cooling raw onion and tomato are the counterpoint; the lime is the finishing acid.

  Make-ahead. The yaji itself keeps 2 to 3 months in an airtight jar at room temperature; the peanut goes faintly stale after that but the dry spices stay fresh. Make a double batch and keep it on hand. The meat is best cooked fresh; rubbed beef holds 24 hours refrigerated before grilling but reads better at 30 minutes to 4 hours.
