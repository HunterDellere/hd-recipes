before_you_start:
  - 'Both layers must cool completely (at least 1 hour) before any frosting touches them. A warm layer melts the ganache fat and the cake slides, so bake first and build the ganache during the cool.'
  - 'The ganache needs about 90 minutes at room temperature, or 30 to 40 minutes in the fridge, to drop from hot to a soft mayonnaise body before it can be whipped. Start it as soon as the layers come out of the oven so the two timelines converge.'
  - 'Make the raspberry reduction while the layers cool. It takes about 10 minutes to cook and strain, then needs to cool to room temperature before it goes between the layers. Reserve a few tablespoons of the looser juice as a soak before you reduce the rest to a jam.'
  - 'Keep the chocolate bar for the shavings somewhere warm. Chocolate near 85°F (29°C) curls under a peeler; cold chocolate shatters.'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 350°F (177°C) with a rack centered. Butter two 4-inch round pans with 1 tsp / 5 g softened butter, line the bottoms with parchment, and dust the walls with a little extra Dutch-process cocoa rather than flour, so the sides bake without a pale seam. Pour the hot brewed coffee (¼ cup / 60 g) over the Dutch-process cocoa (¼ cup / 22 g) and the instant espresso powder (½ tsp / 1 g); whisk smooth and rest 2 minutes. Hot water solubilizes the cocoa''s starch and disperses the fat-bound flavor compounds that stay chalky in a cold mix, while the espresso and coffee carry pyrazines and furans that overlap with roasted-chocolate aromatics, so they deepen the chocolate rather than reading as coffee.'
    technique: 'blooming-cocoa'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Whisk the all-purpose flour (⅔ cup / 80 g), baking soda (¼ tsp / 1.5 g), baking powder (¼ tsp / 1 g), and fine sea salt (¼ tsp / 1.5 g) in one bowl. In a second bowl, whisk the granulated sugar (½ cup / 100 g), dark brown sugar (2 tbsp / 25 g), neutral oil (¼ cup / 55 g), the egg (1 large / 50 g), and buttermilk (⅓ cup / 80 g) until uniform, then whisk in the warm cocoa slurry. The brown sugar''s molasses is hygroscopic, so it pulls and holds water for a wetter crumb, and its faint acidity gives the baking soda more to react against.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the dry mix to the wet in two additions, whisking only until no streaks remain. The batter is deliberately thin: that high water content sets into a soft, open crumb, while overmixing develops gluten and tightens it. Stop the moment it is smooth.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Divide the batter evenly between the pans (about 235 g each) and tap each once on the counter to release large bubbles. Bake 22 to 26 minutes, until the centers spring back and a tester pulls out with a few moist crumbs, or an instant-read shows 205 to 208°F (96 to 98°C) at the center. Pull at the low end: carryover finishes the set, and a chocolate crumb taken past 210°F (99°C) dries, because its sugar and cocoa solids hold less free water than a vanilla batter.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Cool in the pans 10 minutes, then turn out onto a rack and cool completely, at least 1 hour. Frosting a warm layer melts the ganache fat and the cake slides, so do not rush this.'
    time_min: 70
  - text: 'While the layers cool, make the raspberry reduction. Combine the raspberries (6 oz / 170 g), granulated sugar (2½ tbsp / 30 g), lemon juice (1 tsp / 5 g), and a pinch of fine sea salt in a small saucepan over medium heat. Cook 8 to 10 minutes, mashing with a fork, until the berries collapse and the liquid runs syrupy. Strain through a fine sieve, pressing hard on the solids to leave the seeds behind. Reserve 2 tbsp / 30 g of the looser strained juice in a small cup as a soak for the layers. Return the rest (about 110 g) to the pan and reduce over low heat 2 to 4 minutes to a thick, spreadable jam that mounds on a spoon, about 60 g; it firms further as it cools. The lemon juice adds acid and pectin, which set the reduction so it stays put between the layers instead of bleeding into the crumb. Cool both the reduction and the reserved soak to room temperature before assembly.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Chop the bittersweet chocolate (6 oz / 170 g, 60 to 66%) fine into a heatproof bowl with a pinch of salt. Heat the heavy cream (¾ cup / 180 g) until it steams and the edges tremble, about 185°F (85°C); do not boil it, since a hard boil concentrates the cream and throws the fat ratio off. Pour the cream over the chocolate, wait 1 minute without stirring, then stir from the center in tight circles. A glossy core forms and widens as you stir: working from the center keeps fat and water in close contact so they emulsify instead of greasing out. Stir in the soft butter (1 tbsp / 14 g) for sheen.'
    technique: 'chocolate-butter-emulsion'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Press plastic wrap onto the surface of the ganache and cool it to a soft mayonnaise body, about 90 minutes at room temperature or 30 to 40 minutes in the fridge with a stir at the halfway mark. It should hold a soft mound on a spoon rather than pour.'
    time_min: 90
  - text: 'Whip the cooled ganache just until it lightens half a shade and holds a soft peak, 30 to 60 seconds. Stop early: whipping past this point shears the emulsion, the cocoa butter coalesces, and a smooth ganache turns grainy in seconds. Level any dome on the layers. Set the first layer cut-side up and brush it with half the raspberry soak (about 1 tbsp / 15 g) until the crumb looks damp but not wet. Pipe or spoon a ring of ganache around the rim as a dam, then fill the center with the raspberry reduction (about 60 g) and spread it level inside the dam. Brush the cut face of the second layer with the rest of the soak, set it cut-side down onto the dam, and press gently to seat it. Coat the top and sides with the remaining ganache.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Scatter a pinch of flaky sea salt over the top: salt suppresses bitterness perception, so a 60% ganache reads like a higher-cacao one without going sweeter. Warm the chocolate bar (3 oz / 85 g) in your hand or a warm spot, then drag a vegetable peeler down its edge; chocolate near 85°F (29°C) curls, while cold chocolate shatters. Scatter the curls over the top and press a few onto the sides.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'The Adjustment: if the crumb bakes dry, you overshot the center temperature, so pull the next bake at 205°F (96°C) and trust carryover. If the ganache slumps off the cake, it was whipped warm or the cream ratio ran high; chill 15 minutes and rewhip. If it turns grainy and matte, you overwhipped or overheated; warm the bowl briefly over warm water, add 1 tbsp / 15 g warm cream, and stir slowly to pull the emulsion back. If the shavings snap instead of curling, the bar is too cold. If the raspberry layer squeezes out and the top slides, the ganache dam was too thin or the reduction too loose; pipe a taller ring and reduce the jam further next time. If the assembled cake reads too sweet, raise the chocolate percentage next time rather than cutting sugar from the crumb, and build the ganache at 70%.'

notes: |
  Why oil and not creamed butter. Creaming butter and sugar traps air for lift and a fine, dry-leaning crumb; this cake wants the opposite. Liquid oil coats flour proteins more completely than plastic butter does, shortening the gluten that would otherwise toughen, and it stays liquid at fridge temperature where butterfat goes firm and waxy. That is the whole reason an oil crumb eats moist straight from the cold: there is no solid fat to set. The leavening comes from the buttermilk-and-soda reaction plus the small dose of baking powder, not from creamed-in air.

  Split the sugar. Half granulated, a smaller share dark brown. Granulated sugar is clean sweetness and tender structure; the molasses in the dark brown sugar is hygroscopic, holding free water in the crumb a day longer, and its residual acidity gives the baking soda a second acid (alongside the buttermilk) to react against. All-granulated bakes a touch drier and lighter; all-brown reads heavy and damp. The split is the balance.

  The bloom is the flavor lever, not extra cocoa. Pouring hot coffee and espresso over the cocoa before it meets the flour solubilizes the cocoa''s starch and disperses the fat-bound aromatics that stay chalky in a dry mix. The coffee''s pyrazines and furans overlap with the roasted notes already in chocolate, so they amplify the cocoa rather than reading as a coffee cake. Dutch-process cocoa benefits most: alkalization has already mellowed its acidity, and the bloom rebuilds the depth that processing took out. See techniques/blooming-cocoa.

  How a ganache holds together, and how it breaks. A ganache is an emulsion: fine droplets of fat (the cocoa butter from the chocolate plus the butterfat in the cream) dispersed through a continuous water phase, the same oil-in-water structure the cream started as. The cream is mostly water carrying milk proteins and about 36% butterfat; the chocolate is cocoa solids suspended in cocoa butter. Hot cream melts the cocoa butter, and stirring from a tight central core disperses that fat into the water as fine, even droplets, each one stabilized by the lecithin and cocoa solids that sit at the oil-water boundary. Heat is the lever. Too hot and the fat thins, the droplets collide and coalesce, and the emulsion inverts to a greasy, fat-continuous split; too cold and the cocoa butter seizes before the phases bind. Cooling to a soft set lets the cocoa butter begin to crystallize, and a brief whip aerates that setting fat into a spreadable solid. Push the whip past a soft peak and mechanical shear knocks the fat droplets together, the cocoa butter crystallizes on the wrong template, and the smooth ganache breaks into grain in seconds. The window between spreadable and broken is narrow, which is why you whip in short bursts and stop at the first soft peak. See techniques/chocolate-butter-emulsion.

  Raspberry against bittersweet, and why the reduction is split. Chocolate and raspberry share fruity ester and ionone aromatics, so the pairing reads as amplification more than contrast, and the berry''s malic and citric acid cuts the ganache''s fat the way the coffee cuts the cocoa in the crumb. The reduction is split on purpose: the thick, pectin-set jam stays put between the layers, while the few tablespoons of looser juice reserved before the final reduction soak into the cut crumb and carry a bright raspberry burst into the first bite. Brush the soak on until the surface just looks damp, not wet; an oil crumb is already moist, and a waterlogged layer turns gummy and slides on the ganache. The lemon juice is doing double duty, supplying the pectin that sets the jam and the acid that keeps the fruit tasting fresh rather than jammy-flat.

  Salt as a bitterness control. The pinch in the ganache and the flaky finish on top are not garnish. Sodium ions suppress the tongue''s perception of bitterness, so a 60% ganache salted lightly reads like a higher-cacao one without adding sugar. If the assembled cake still reads too sweet, build the ganache at 70% rather than cutting sugar from the crumb, which is doing structural work the ganache is not.
