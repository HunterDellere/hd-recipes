steps:
  - text: 'Render the pancetta. Heat a heavy 6-quart Dutch oven or large saucier over medium-low heat. Add the 100 g pancetta dice and the 30 ml olive oil. Cook 8 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the pancetta is golden-crisp and has rendered roughly 60 ml of fat into the pan. The low heat and the long render are structural, high heat scorches the pancetta surface and leaves the interior fat unrendered.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Build the soffritto. Add the 30 g butter to the pancetta fat. When the butter foams, add the 200 g diced onion, 100 g diced carrot, and 100 g diced celery. Cook 12 to 15 minutes over medium-low heat, stirring every 2 to 3 minutes, until the vegetables are completely soft, the onion is translucent-to-pale-gold, and the mixture has reduced visibly in volume. The soffritto is correct when the diced shapes have softened to the point of nearly dissolving but no Maillard browning has occurred, the goal at this stage is sweetness from low-heat caramelization, not browning. Add the 12 g minced garlic in the last 90 seconds; cook until fragrant.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Brown the meat hard. Push the soffritto to the sides of the pot to clear the center. Increase heat to medium-high. Add the 500 g ground beef and 250 g ground pork in the cleared center; press flat with a wooden spoon to maximize contact with the pot bottom; sprinkle with the 8 g salt and 2 g black pepper. Do not stir for 4 to 5 minutes, let the meat develop a deep mahogany Maillard crust before breaking it up. After the first crust forms, break the meat into rough 1 cm chunks (not fine crumble, the chunks hold structure through the long cook); cook 6 to 8 more minutes, stirring occasionally, until no pink remains and the meat shows extensive browning on multiple surfaces. The Maillard layer here is the single most important flavor in the finished ragù; under-browning produces a flat, gray, sodden ragù that cannot be recovered later.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Caramelize the tomato paste. Push the meat to one side. In the cleared spot, add the 60 g tomato paste and stir into the rendered fat. Cook 2 to 3 minutes, stirring constantly, until the paste deepens from bright brick to dark mahogany and a sweet-roasted-tomato aroma replaces the sharp-fresh smell. The fry-in-fat technique transforms tomato paste from raw-and-tinny to structurally savory; skipping this step is the second-most-common Bolognese mistake after under-browning the meat.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Deglaze with white wine. Pour in the 240 ml dry white wine and scrape the pot bottom hard with a wooden spoon to lift any fond stuck during the meat browning. Bring to a vigorous simmer; cook 4 to 5 minutes until the wine has reduced by roughly two-thirds and the alcohol has cooked off (the sharp wine smell shifts to a softer, more integrated cooked-wine note).'
    technique: 'deglazing'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the milk and tomato. Pour in the 250 ml whole milk and stir to combine; the milk will look strange in the moment (the residual acid from the wine and tomato will cause some curdling), this is correct and the curdling reverses over the long simmer. Add the 400 g hand-crushed San Marzano tomatoes with their juice, the 2 bay leaves, and the 30 g parmigiano rind. Bring to a bare simmer.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'The long cook. Reduce heat to the lowest setting that maintains a tremor on the surface (about 90 °C); cover with the lid set ajar. Simmer 2 to 3 hours, stirring every 20 minutes and adding 100 ml of the chicken stock when the ragù tightens against the pot bottom. The ragù is correct when the meat has fully integrated with the soffritto, the fat has separated and pooled at the surface, the color has deepened to a brick-mahogany, and the texture coats the back of a wooden spoon thickly without running. Total simmer time: 2 hours minimum, 3 hours preferred. Discard the bay leaves and parmigiano rind at the end. Taste; salt if needed (the parmigiano rind has contributed salt; the ragù may need none or only a finishing pinch).'
    time_min: 180
  - text: 'Cook the spaghetti. Bring 5 L of water to a rolling boil with the 30 g salt. Add the 500 g spaghetti; cook 1 minute shy of package al dente (typically 8 minutes for standard spaghetti). Reserve 250 ml of starchy pasta water before draining.'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Marry pasta and ragù. Transfer the drained spaghetti directly into the simmering ragù; add 60 ml of the reserved pasta water. Toss vigorously over medium heat for 90 seconds, lifting and folding the pasta through the ragù; add more pasta water 30 ml at a time if the sauce thickens beyond a glossy coating consistency. The pasta should finish cooking in the ragù (a "risottatura" technique), this lets the spaghetti absorb the ragù flavor rather than sitting on top of it.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish and serve. Pull the pot off the heat. Add the 30 g finishing butter and 30 g of the parmigiano (half the finishing portion); toss vigorously for 30 seconds, the butter and cheese emulsify into the ragù and add a glossy, creamy finish that defines the dish. Plate into wide bowls. Top each bowl with the remaining 30 g grated parmigiano (or pass at the table with a microplane and a wedge for guest-grating, the canonical Italian service). Serve immediately.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why milk in a meat ragù. The lactic-acid component of milk (which is around 0.15 percent in fresh whole milk and 0.6 percent in cultured buttermilk) gently denatures the surface proteins of the ground meat and produces a more tender mouthfeel in the finished ragù. The technique is structural to ragù alla bolognese specifically and dates to the Accademia Italiana della Cucina's 1982 deposit, which lists milk as one of the canonical ingredients. The visible curdling on milk addition is the milk proteins reacting with the residual acid from the wine and tomato; this reverses during the long simmer and the milk integrates fully into the ragù body. Skipping the milk produces a tougher, drier ragù that reads as good but distinctly less complete than the milk-included version.

  Why white wine, not red. Bolognese specifies white wine in the canonical formulation. The reasoning is two-fold: red wine's tannin is too aggressive against the long-cooked meat and produces a bitter, drying finish; white wine's softer acid temper the meat richness without competing. Northern Italian cooking generally uses white wine for meat ragùs and reserves red wine for game ragùs (cinghiale, lepre). The home-cook substitution of red wine pulls the dish toward marinara-with-meat-sauce, which is the Italian-American interpretation of Bolognese rather than the regional Italian dish.

  Tomato is support, not lead. A defining feature of ragù alla bolognese is that the meat is the protagonist and the tomato is a supporting flavor, typically less than 200 g of tomato per 750 g of meat in the canonical proportions. The Italian-American "spaghetti and meat sauce" inverts this ratio, with 500 g or more of tomato per 500 g of meat, and the dish reads as a tomato sauce with meat folded in rather than a meat ragù with tomato as one of several flavors. Both dishes have their place; the recipe is named for the dish it intends to be, and Bolognese specifically wants the lower tomato ratio.

  The parmigiano rind. Saving the rind from every wedge of parmigiano in a labeled freezer bag pays off here. The rind dropped into the simmering ragù slowly leaches glutamate and salt into the dish over 2 hours; the contribution is meaningful and distinct, and no other ingredient quite covers the role. If no rind is on hand, add a half-teaspoon of white miso and a quarter-teaspoon of fish sauce at the same stage; the substitute approximates the umami contribution.

  The pasta-finishing technique (risottatura). Finishing the pasta in the sauce rather than tossing pre-drained pasta with sauce is structural to good Italian pasta cooking. The pasta absorbs sauce flavor through its still-active starch matrix; the starch released from the pasta thickens the sauce and helps it cling. Tossing fully-drained pasta with sauce in a separate bowl produces a pasta dish where the sauce sits on top of dry noodles, common in American home cooking and identifiably worse than the marry-in-the-pan technique used by every serious Italian kitchen.

  The freezer life. Bolognese ragù freezes essentially perfectly. Portion into 500 ml deli containers, label, freeze for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator; reheat in a saucepan with a splash of stock or pasta water to loosen. The dish is identical to fresh after the freeze-thaw cycle, which makes Bolognese the canonical batch-cook freezer staple, make a triple recipe, freeze 8 portions, eat well for 8 weeks of weeknights.
