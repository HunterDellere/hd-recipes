source:
  note: 'The Hungarian word "gulyás" means "herdsman", and the dish traces to the open-fire cooking of cattle herders on the Great Hungarian Plain (the Alföld). The canonical version is a soup with broth visible around the chunks of meat and vegetables, eaten with bread (csipetke pinched dumplings are the most traditional addition). The thick brown gravy-style "goulash" served at American and German restaurants is a different dish called pörkölt (literally "roasted") that uses the same paprika-and-onion base but reduces the liquid to a sauce. Both are legitimate; this recipe is the soup. The structural moves are constant across both: the onion base must be cooked very long (30 to 40 minutes) until soft and brown; the paprika must be bloomed off-heat in the fat to avoid scorching (paprika scorches at 130 °C, well below sauté temperature, and produces an aggressively bitter dish if it touches a hot pan); and the beef must be a slow-cook cut (chuck, shoulder, shin) given the full 90 minutes to break down.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Paprika scorches almost instantly on direct heat. The structural Hungarian move is to pull the pot off the burner before adding the paprika, stir it into the fat off-heat for 30 seconds, then return to medium heat. Throwing paprika into a hot pan produces an aggressively bitter dish that no amount of correction will fix.'
  - 'Use Hungarian sweet paprika (édes paprika). Spanish smoked pimentón shifts the flavor toward smoky-Spanish; generic American "paprika" is flat and characterless. The Hungarian sweet is the structural flavor of the dish and the most important single ingredient. See content/ingredients/hungarian-paprika.'
  - 'Use beef chuck or beef shin (osso bucco cut) for the slow-cook texture. Lean cuts like sirloin or round dry out over the 90-minute simmer; the dish depends on the gelatin from the collagen-rich cut.'
  - 'The csipetke dumplings (Step 9) are optional but canonical. They take 10 minutes to mix and 5 minutes to cook; do not skip if you want the genuine Hungarian dish.'

steps:
  - text: 'In a heavy 5 to 6 quart Dutch oven over medium-high heat, melt the 40 g / 3 tbsp lard until shimmering. Pat the 800 g / 1¾ lb beef chuck chunks dry, season all sides with the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt and the 2 g / ¾ tsp black pepper, and sear in two batches, 3 minutes per side, until deeply browned. Transfer the seared meat to a plate.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Drop the heat to medium-low. Add the 500 g / 2 large diced yellow onions to the rendered fat in the pot. Cook for 30 to 40 minutes, stirring every 4 to 5 minutes with a wooden spoon, until the onions have completely softened and turned a deep golden brown. This is the structural base of the dish; the equal-weight ratio of onion to meat plus the long cook is what gives gulyás its characteristic depth. Do not rush this phase. If the onions stick or brown too quickly, drop the heat one notch and add a splash of water.'
    technique: 'dry-sweat'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Add the 20 g / 5 cloves microplaned garlic and the 4 g / 1½ tsp lightly crushed caraway. Cook 1 minute, stirring constantly, until fragrant.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pull the pot off the burner. Sprinkle the 30 g / ¼ cup Hungarian sweet paprika and the 2 g / ½ tsp hot paprika (if using) over the onions and stir constantly for 30 seconds, off heat. The paprika should fully dissolve into the fat and turn the onion mass a deep brick-red. Working off heat is structural; paprika scorches almost instantly on direct heat and turns bitter. Once integrated, return the pot to medium-low heat for 10 seconds, just to warm.'
    technique: 'bloom-spice'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Stir in the 30 g / 2 tbsp tomato paste and cook 1 minute. Add the 200 g / 1 to 2 green peppers cut into pieces and stir to coat in the spiced onion base.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Return the seared beef and any juices on the plate to the pot. Pour in the 1800 ml / 7½ cups beef stock and add the 2 bay leaves. Bring to a vigorous simmer, then drop to low. Cover with the lid slightly ajar (1 cm gap to let steam escape) and simmer for 60 minutes, stirring every 20 minutes. The chuck will be approaching tender but not yet fully there.'
    technique: 'braise'
    time_min: 65
  - text: 'Add the 500 g / 1 lb diced potato, the 250 g / 2 large diced carrot, and the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt. Cover and simmer another 25 to 30 minutes until the potatoes are fork-tender and the meat is fully tender. The broth will have thickened slightly from the potato starch but should still read as a soup, not a stew.'
    time_min: 28
  - text: 'Make the csipetke dumplings if using (Phase 5). In a small bowl, mix the 100 g / ¾ cup flour, the 1 egg, and the 2 g / ⅓ tsp salt with a fork, adding 1 to 2 tablespoons of water as needed to form a stiff dough. Knead briefly. With floured fingers, pinch off small pieces of dough about the size of a chickpea (5 to 7 mm) and drop directly into the simmering soup. They sink at first, then rise to the surface within 60 to 90 seconds. Once all the csipetke have risen, simmer another 3 to 4 minutes to cook through.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Taste the soup. It should read deeply paprika-flavored, with rich beef broth, soft caramelized onion integrated into the liquid, and the potato and carrot tender but holding shape. Adjust salt if flat; a small splash of lemon juice (5 g / 1 tsp) at the end brightens if the broth reads heavy. Discard the bay leaves.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Serve in deep bowls with a generous ladling of broth, meat, vegetables, and csipetke. Crusty rye or sourdough bread on the side for sopping. The canonical Hungarian table also includes a small bowl of additional sour cream and a small bowl of additional hot paprika for diners to dress their own bowls to taste.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why paprika quantity matters. Hungarian gulyás uses 30 g (¼ cup) of sweet paprika for 800 g of meat, which is the structural flavor and color of the dish. American recipes routinely use 1 to 2 tablespoons (half this amount or less), producing a tan brown stew that reads recognizably wrong. The heroic paprika quantity is the point; do not scale back to American-spice-jar conventions.

  Why off-heat blooming. Paprika scorches at roughly 130 °C, which is well below normal sauté temperature. The canonical Hungarian move is to pull the pot off the burner, add the paprika to the still-hot fat, stir constantly for 30 seconds (the residual heat is enough to bloom the volatile aromatics), and then return to the burner. Throwing paprika into a hot pan produces an aggressively bitter dish that no amount of correction will fix. This is the single most common Western mistake with Hungarian cooking.

  Why equal weight of onion to meat. The 1:1 onion-to-meat ratio (500 g onion to 800 g meat, with the onion volume dramatically reducing during the long cook) is the structural base of the dish. The cooked-down onion is what gives gulyás its body and depth; it is not seasoning, it is the base.

  Csipetke dumplings. The small pinched dumplings cooked directly in the soup are the canonical Hungarian addition. They take 10 minutes of work and dramatically improve the dish; the dumplings absorb the broth and provide textural contrast. Some Hungarian cooks make them in advance and add at the table; some cook them directly in the soup (this recipe). Both work.

  Make-ahead and reheat. Gulyás improves overnight; the broth integrates and the flavors deepen. Reheat gently with a splash of water to loosen if needed, covered, over low heat for 15 minutes. Holds 4 to 5 days refrigerated, 3 months frozen. The csipetke do not freeze well (they get gluey when thawed); freeze the broth without them and make fresh csipetke when reheating.
