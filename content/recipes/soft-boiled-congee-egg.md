notes: |
  Why the table-side crack matters. Congee is one of the few preparations where the egg is functionally raw at the moment of serving and finishes cooking in the bowl. A pre-peeled egg sitting on top of hot porridge for two minutes overcooks the yolk into a normal jammy state; a freshly-cracked-just-before-eating egg has a yolk that flows the moment the spoon touches it. The whole point of the soft-boiled congee egg is that the cook gets a different texture in every spoonful for the first thirty seconds: pure runny yolk, yolk-thickened porridge, plain porridge with a piece of soft white. Pre-cracking destroys that arc.

  How this differs from a standard soft-boiled egg. The yolk target is the same, but the serving format is different. A standard soft-boiled egg is peeled, sliced, and eaten as a discrete object on toast or noodles. The congee egg is a structural component of the broth: the yolk is the secondary thickener and the white is the protein bite. Treat it as cooking that finishes in the bowl, not as a topping.

  Failure modes. Eggs cracked before the ice bath produce stringy white tails as albumen leaks and cooks. Eggs cooked from room temperature instead of cold add about 30 to 45 seconds to the timing, so a 6:30 starts to overshoot toward fully set. Eggs over-shocked (more than 2 minutes in ice) lose enough heat that the yolk firms in the shell while peeling, defeating the technique.
