source:
  name: 'The Japanese canon. Tamagoyaki (literally "grilled egg") is one of the most universal Japanese egg preparations and is structurally embedded across the Japanese table: it appears at breakfast (alongside rice, miso soup, and pickles, as part of the classical ichiju-sansai morning meal), in bento (where it is the canonical egg component, often shaped into pink or yellow blocks for color), on sushi rice (as tamagoyaki nigiri, the rectangular block of sweet egg atop a finger of rice and bound with a thin strip of nori), as an izakaya snack (with grated daikon and soy), and as a structural Japanese picnic food (slices in a furoshiki-wrapped bento on a hanami picnic during cherry blossom season).'
  note: 'Two structural schools: atsuyaki tamago (the thick sweet version, common at breakfast and in bento, more sugar-forward) and dashimaki tamago (the dashi-heavy slightly thinner version, common in Kyoto and at high-end sushi counters, more umami-forward and less sweet). The version here is a hybrid that leans toward dashimaki (more dashi, less sugar) but uses the more universally home-cook-friendly proportions of atsuyaki. The defining technique is the rectangular pan (makiyakinabe, the tamagoyaki pan) and the layer-and-roll technique: thin layers of egg are poured into the pan one at a time, partially cooked, and rolled around the previous layer to build a structurally stacked cylinder. The result is a single cohesive block with visible internal layers when sliced crosswise. Distinct from a French rolled omelet (which is a single uniform sheet rolled around itself, with no internal layering) and from a Korean gyeran-mari (a structurally adjacent Korean rolled omelet that uses vegetables and a single roll rather than the multi-layer Japanese build).'

before_you_start:
  - 'Dashi must be ready before you crack any eggs. The recipe uses 30 g of cooled dashi mixed into the egg base; if you do not have made-ahead dashi, make a quick katsuobushi-and-kombu dashi at least 30 minutes ahead so it has time to cool. Hot dashi cooks the eggs in the bowl.'
  - 'A rectangular tamagoyaki pan (makiyakinabe) is the canonical tool. A round pan works but produces an oval cylinder rather than the clean rectangular cross-section and the corner-to-corner spread of each layer is harder to control. If using a round pan, trim the cooked block on a cutting board after the rest.'
  - 'Have the sushi mat (makisu) or a clean kitchen towel laid out next to the stove before the first layer goes down. The finished cylinder transfers onto the mat immediately and rests 2 to 3 minutes; hunting for the mat at the end lets the block flatten on the cutting board and loses the rectangular shape.'

steps:
  - text: 'In a medium bowl, beat the 200 g / 4 eggs with a fork or chopsticks for 30 seconds until just uniform (no streaks of white). Do not whip air into the eggs; the goal is gentle agitation, not aeration. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp dashi, 12 g / 2 tsp mirin, 6 g / 1 tsp Japanese light soy sauce, 8 g / 2 tsp sugar, and 1 g / ¼ tsp salt; stir to combine until the sugar is fully dissolved (the mixture should be a uniform pale yellow with the seasonings fully integrated). Pass the mixture through a fine-mesh sieve into a measuring cup with a pour spout (this removes any chalazae and produces smoother layers).'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Heat a rectangular tamagoyaki pan (makiyakinabe; the canonical Japanese tool, approximately 13 by 19 cm; copper or non-stick) over medium heat for 90 seconds. Test the temperature: a drop of the egg mixture should sizzle gently and set within 5 seconds (if it sizzles aggressively, the pan is too hot and the eggs will set in dry curds rather than soft sheets; if it does not sizzle, the pan is too cool and the eggs will read greasy). Fold a paper towel into a small pad; dip into the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil and wipe the pan thoroughly to coat the bottom and sides in a thin film of oil; reserve the oiled paper towel for re-greasing between layers.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour about one-fifth of the egg mixture (about 50 g) into the pan; tilt the pan to spread the egg into a thin uniform sheet covering the entire bottom. As the egg sets (about 20 to 30 seconds), use chopsticks or a long thin spatula to pop any large air bubbles. While the egg is still partially uncooked on top (the surface should be visibly wet with a few uncooked spots), begin the first roll: starting at the side of the pan farthest from you, use the chopsticks or spatula to gently roll the egg toward you in 2 or 3 turns, building a small cylinder of egg at the near edge of the pan.'
    technique: 'layer-and-roll'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Push the rolled cylinder back to the far side of the pan. Re-grease the pan with the oiled paper towel pad, lifting the rolled cylinder briefly to oil the area underneath it. Pour another one-fifth of the egg mixture (50 g) into the pan; tilt to spread into a thin sheet and lift the existing roll so that the new egg flows under it (this is the structural detail that bonds the layers; without it the layers slide apart when sliced). When the new layer has set on the bottom but is still wet on top, roll the existing cylinder toward you, picking up the new layer as it rolls; the result after this second roll is a slightly larger cylinder.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Repeat the layer-and-roll technique 3 more times (total of 5 layers), pouring one-fifth of the remaining egg mixture each time, re-greasing the pan between layers, and rolling the cylinder forward to build each new layer onto the existing structure. By the end of the fifth roll, the cylinder should fill the pan from one short edge to the other and should be cohesive, golden-yellow, and visibly layered when viewed from the cut end. Press gently with the spatula or a sushi mat to compact and shape the cylinder into a clean rectangular block.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Slide the finished tamagoyaki onto a sushi mat (makisu) or a clean kitchen towel; roll the mat or towel tightly around the cylinder and let rest 2 to 3 minutes; the rest tightens the layers into a single cohesive block and produces the canonical rectangular cross-section. Unroll, transfer to a cutting board, and slice crosswise into 2 cm thick pieces (about 6 pieces from a single tamagoyaki). The cross-section should show 5 visible layers spiraling around the center, the structural signature of a properly executed tamagoyaki.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Plate the sliced tamagoyaki on small plates; serve warm or at room temperature with the 50 g / 2 tbsp grated daikon mounded next to the eggs and a 5 g / 1 tsp drizzle of soy sauce over the daikon. The diner takes a piece of tamagoyaki with chopsticks, lifts a small mound of daikon onto it, and eats in a single bite. Tamagoyaki travels especially well as a make-ahead component: the block keeps 2 days refrigerated and can be packed cold in bento, sliced cold for sushi, or warmed briefly in a low oven (100°C, 3 minutes) for breakfast service.'
    time_min: 1

    how: 'Increase the dashi to 60 g (a 1:1 egg-to-dashi ratio by volume) and reduce the sugar to 4 g. The increased dashi makes the egg mixture thinner and more liquid, which means each layer is more delicate and the rolling technique requires more practice; the result is structurally dashimaki tamago, the Kyoto-style high-end-sushi-counter preparation; reads more umami-forward and less sweet, with a softer more custardy interior. The canonical sushi-counter tamagoyaki.'

The cheese melts into the layers and the result is a Japanese-American hybrid that reads cheese-forward and rich; popular in modern Japanese izakaya and home cookery.'

notes: |
  Tamagoyaki is one of the most structurally precise egg preparations in any cuisine because of the layer-and-roll technique. The dish is judged on multiple axes: cross-sectional layering (5 visible spirals when sliced is the canonical reference; fewer than 4 reads as a generic omelet, more than 6 reads as overdone fussiness), texture (soft and trembling at the center, set but not dry on the edges), color (uniform pale yellow with no Maillard browning; brown spots indicate the pan was too hot), and shape (a clean rectangular block with sharp edges, achieved with the sushi-mat rest at the end). The technique requires practice; first attempts produce uneven layers and broken rolls, but the structural logic is straightforward and the technique becomes reliable after 5 to 10 attempts.

  The rectangular pan (makiyakinabe) is the canonical Japanese tool and produces meaningfully better results than a round pan, but is not strictly required. A small round nonstick skillet (15 to 18 cm diameter) produces a roundish tamagoyaki that is structurally acceptable but does not slice into the canonical rectangular blocks; the same layer-and-roll technique applies, but the rolling is less efficient because the curved sides do not provide a clean edge for the cylinder to build against. For cooks who make tamagoyaki regularly, the dedicated rectangular pan is a worthwhile investment (around $30 to $80 for a decent non-stick or copper version).

  The pan temperature is structural. A pan that is too hot produces dry, browned layers that read wrong (Japanese tamagoyaki should be pale yellow with no Maillard); a pan that is too cool produces greasy, undercooked layers that do not set into a cohesive cylinder. The right temperature is medium-low (a drop of egg should sizzle gently and set in 5 seconds, not aggressively or slowly); the pan temperature is the variable that requires the most practice to dial in. Some Japanese tamagoyaki pans have temperature indicators; for those without, the egg-drop test before pouring the first layer is the canonical home check.

  The dashi-mirin-soy seasoning is structural and what distinguishes tamagoyaki from a generic rolled omelet. The dashi provides the structural umami base, the mirin provides the sweet aromatic Japanese rice-wine character, the soy provides the structural salt-and-fermentation depth. None of the three is optional; substituting water for dashi produces a flat omelet, substituting sake for mirin produces a different (less aromatic) seasoning, substituting Chinese soy or American salt for Japanese soy produces a different (less appropriate) flavor profile.

  Versatility. Tamagoyaki is one of the most structurally adaptable Japanese egg preparations and travels brilliantly across applications. Use it as: the canonical Japanese breakfast egg component (alongside rice, miso soup, pickles, and grilled fish as part of the classical ichiju-sansai morning meal); the structural bento egg (sliced into rectangles and packed alongside rice, vegetables, and protein; the canonical Japanese lunch); a sushi component (tamagoyaki nigiri, the rectangular block of sweet egg atop a finger of rice and bound with a thin nori strip; one of the canonical sushi pieces and often the test of a sushi chef because the simplicity of the dish reveals the technique); a roll component (sliced into strips and rolled into a maki sushi with rice and other ingredients); an izakaya snack (sliced and served at room temperature with grated daikon and a soy drizzle, alongside a beer); a picnic food (slices in a furoshiki-wrapped bento on a hanami picnic during cherry blossom season; the structural Japanese cold egg); a salad component (sliced and added to a Japanese-style salad with vegetables and a sesame dressing); a structural ingredient in egg salad sandwiches (mashed tamagoyaki on milk bread, a Japanese convenience-store classic); and a versatile component for any Japanese-style meal where eggs are the structural protein.

  Pairs across Japanese (canonical), Japanese-American (where tamagoyaki has become a structural diaspora egg preparation), broader East Asian (the Korean gyeran-mari is structurally adjacent; Chinese egg rolls are different architecturally), and contemporary American Japanese-influenced cooking (especially modern sushi restaurants and Japanese bistro cookery).

  Storage. Keeps 2 days refrigerated, wrapped tightly in plastic-free beeswax wrap or a sealed glass container; the texture firms slightly in the cold and the dashi-and-mirin flavors continue to integrate. Eat cold straight from the refrigerator (the canonical bento and picnic preparation) or warm briefly in a low oven (100°C / 210°F, 3 minutes) for breakfast service. Do not microwave-reheat; the eggs turn rubbery. Freezing is not recommended; the texture degrades meaningfully on thawing.
