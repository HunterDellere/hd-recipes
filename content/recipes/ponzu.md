title: 'Ponzu'
desc: 'Yuzu juice (or a yuzu-lemon-lime blend) combined with soy sauce, mirin, rice vinegar, kombu, and katsuobushi, then refrigerated 24 hours to extract the kombu glutamate and katsuobushi inosinate into the citrus-soy base. The canonical washoku dipping and finishing sauce; dramatically more aromatic than any bottled version because the volatile citrus top notes have not had months to fade in storage.'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 24-hour make-ahead. The kombu glutamate and katsuobushi inosinate extract slowly into the cold citrus-soy liquid; under 12 hours the sauce reads thin and under-stacked. Start at least one day before you need it, two if you can.'

steps:
  - text: 'Juice the yuzu (or lemons and limes). For yuzu, halve each fruit equator-wise, pick out and discard the seeds, and squeeze through a fine-mesh strainer; you should get 12 to 18 g of juice per fruit, so plan on 4 to 6 fruits to reach the 80 g target. For the lemon-lime substitute, juice the citrus and combine in a 3:1 ratio (60 g lemon juice + 20 g lime juice). Strain the juice through a fine-mesh sieve to catch pulp and seeds; the finished ponzu must be clear, and any pulp left in the steeping liquid will cloud the sauce and degrade in storage.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Combine and steep. In a clean glass jar or plastic container with a tight-fitting lid (a 500 ml mason jar is ideal), combine the 80 g / ⅓ cup citrus juice, the 100 g / ⅓ cup + 1 tbsp light soy sauce, the 60 g / ¼ cup mirin, the 30 g / 2 tbsp rice vinegar, the 5 g / 5 cm square kombu, and the 5 g / ⅓ cup katsuobushi. Do not heat anything. Stir gently with a chopstick to wet the kombu and the katsuobushi (the katsuobushi will float at first and gradually sink as it hydrates). Cover and refrigerate 24 hours, no shorter; the kombu releases free glutamate slowly into the cold liquid (cold extraction is functional here, the same chemistry as ichiban dashi made by cold-water soak), and the katsuobushi releases inosinate over the same window. Steeping less than 12 hours produces a noticeably thinner, less umami sauce; steeping 24 to 48 hours produces the canonical depth.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Strain. After 24 hours (or up to 48), strain the ponzu through a fine-mesh sieve lined with a paper towel or muslin cloth into a clean glass bottle or jar. Let the liquid drip through; do not press the spent kombu and katsuobushi (squeezing presses tannins and bitter compounds into the clear sauce). Discard the spent kombu and katsuobushi, or save for a quick second-extraction broth (cover with 200 ml of water, simmer 5 minutes, strain; use as a light dashi for noodle soup). The finished ponzu should be clear, the color of weak tea, with the aromatic citrus rising sharply when the bottle is opened.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Bottle and store. Pour the strained ponzu into a clean glass bottle (the original brand bottles from Japanese grocery stores work well, or any tight-sealing 300 to 500 ml glass bottle). Refrigerate. The sauce is ready immediately; use over the next 4 weeks for full citrus aromatic potency, up to 3 months for cooking applications where the citrus top notes are less critical. Shake gently before each use to redistribute any settled solids.'
    time_min: 1

substitutions:
  - for: 'yuzu juice'
    use: '3 parts lemon juice to 1 part lime juice'
    note: 'The standard Western approximation. Covers the acid balance and a fraction of the floral character. The ponzu reads less floral but is still recognizably ponzu; perfectly acceptable for cooking applications. Add a 2 cm strip of grapefruit peel to the steep for a closer aromatic profile.'
  - for: 'light soy sauce'
    use: 'usukuchi shoyu (Japanese light soy) or regular Japanese shoyu'
    note: 'Usukuchi is lighter in color and saltier; works in equal volume but the sauce reads slightly saltier. Regular Japanese shoyu (Kikkoman, Yamasa) is the standard substitute and works in equal volume. Avoid Korean soy sauce (different fermentation profile, reads as a different sauce) and dark soy sauce (too sweet, too thick, wrong color).'
  - for: 'hon-mirin'
    use: 'sake + sugar (3 parts sake : 1 part sugar by weight; so 45 g sake + 15 g sugar)'
    note: 'Best emergency substitute. Lacks the koji-derived complexity but the difference is invisible under the citrus and soy. Avoid mirin-fu chōmiryō (sweetened cooking liquid sold at most US supermarkets as "mirin"); it works but lacks any depth.'
  - for: 'rice vinegar'
    use: 'apple cider vinegar or white wine vinegar'
    note: 'Both work in equal volume; rice vinegar is the cleanest option and what real ponzu uses. Avoid distilled white vinegar (too harsh) and balsamic (wrong color and flavor profile).'
  - for: 'kombu and katsuobushi'
    use: 'no good substitute; the umami extraction is core to the recipe'
    note: 'Skipping the kombu and katsuobushi produces a "quick ponzu" that is essentially soy + citrus + mirin; usable as a dipping sauce but lacks the depth that defines ponzu. The whole point of the 24-hour steep is the cold-extracted glutamate-inosinate stack.'

notes: |
  Why cold extraction. Ponzu is a textbook cold-water dashi extraction inside a citrus-soy base. The kombu releases free glutamate cleanly into cold liquid over 12 to 24 hours via slow diffusion; this is the same mechanism as the cold-water soak protocol for ichiban dashi (see the dashi technique page), and produces a cleaner glutamate extraction than any heated method. Heating the ponzu would also drive off the volatile citrus aromatics (the entire reason the sauce uses fresh juice rather than aged citrus extract) and partially break down the soy aromatics. Everything is mixed cold, refrigerated, and steeped; the technique is a study in patience over heat.

  The umami source is the soy sauce (glutamate), the kombu (additional glutamate), and the katsuobushi (inosinate); the glutamate-inosinate synergy multiplies perceived umami intensity 7 to 8 times the sum of the parts (this is the same chemistry behind dashi). The finishing acid is the citrus (yuzu juice or yuzu-lemon-lime blend) plus the rice vinegar; the two acids work in different aromatic registers, the citrus contributing the bright top notes and the rice vinegar contributing a deeper background acid that survives storage.

  Why fresh-pressed citrus matters. The volatile aromatic terpenes that define yuzu (and to a lesser extent lemon and lime) are the first compounds to fade in storage, both in juice and in the finished ponzu. A bottled commercial ponzu has typically sat in storage for 6 to 12 months between manufacture and the consumer; the floral top notes of the original citrus have largely degraded by the time the bottle is opened, and what remains is the soy-acid-mirin base with a faint citrus background. A homemade ponzu with fresh-pressed (or fresh-frozen-and-thawed) yuzu, used within the first 4 weeks, has a noticeable floral citrus presence that no bottled version reaches. This is the entire reason to make ponzu at home; the technique is trivial, the ingredients are easy, and the difference is substantial.

  Storage and longevity. Refrigerated in a sealed glass bottle, the homemade ponzu keeps the full citrus aromatic potency for about 4 weeks; after that the floral top notes fade noticeably (still a fine cooking ponzu, but no longer the dramatic version). The high acid and high salt content prevent bacterial spoilage; the sauce will keep refrigerated for 3 months without safety concern, but the flavor degrades. Plan to use the batch within a month for fresh-fish applications (grilled saba, sashimi dipping), through the second month for cooked applications (simmered fish, gyoza dipping), and into the third month only as a base for marinades or stir-fry sauces.

  Applications. Ponzu is one of the most versatile sauces in the washoku pantry. Standard uses: dipping sauce for shabu-shabu, chanko nabe, and other hot pots; finishing sauce for grilled and pan-seared fish (especially oily fish, where the citrus cuts the lipid); dipping sauce for gyoza and other dumplings (often with a few drops of chili oil added); dressing for Japanese salads (lighter ponzu vinaigrettes for greens, fuller ponzu over composed seafood salads); marinade base (with garlic and ginger added, for chicken or pork before grilling); finishing splash over agedashi tofu, grilled vegetables, sashimi, and tataki. The single bottle in the refrigerator earns its space.
