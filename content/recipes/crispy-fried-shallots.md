steps:
  - text: 'Slice 300 g / 6-8 medium peeled shallots as thinly as possible on a mandoline; the target is 1-2 mm rings. Hand-slicing works but the unevenness produces some shallots that burn before others crisp. Lay slices on a paper towel and pat off any surface moisture.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Toss the sliced shallots with 3 g / ½ tsp kosher salt and rest 5 minutes. The brief cure pulls a small amount of surface water out, which lets the shallots fry rather than steam in the oil. Spread on a fresh paper towel and pat dry again.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'In a heavy 2-quart saucepan or small Dutch oven, combine the dried 300 g shallots with 350 ml / 1½ cups neutral oil. The shallots should be fully submerged. Set over medium heat from cold, never from hot oil. The slow temperature ramp is the technique: shallots start to release moisture at about 80°C, brown at the surface around 130°C, and crisp through at 150°C. A cold start lets the moisture drive off in stages rather than all at once.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'After 8-10 minutes the oil should be at a steady gentle bubble (about 130°C / 265°F), with the shallots turning translucent and pale gold at the edges. Stir every minute or so with a slotted spoon to keep the layers from clumping at the bottom and burning.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'After about 15-18 minutes the shallots will start turning a deeper amber. The crucial visual cue: when about 70% of them are deep gold and 30% still pale, pull the pan off heat. Carry-over from the residual oil heat will continue browning them another 30-60 seconds, and pulled-too-late shallots go from amber to bitter blackened in 90 seconds.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Strain through a fine-mesh sieve set over a heatproof bowl, capturing the oil. Spread the shallots in a single layer on a paper-towel-lined tray and toss immediately with 2 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt while they are still hot and the salt can adhere. They will continue crisping as they cool over the next 5 minutes; the texture target is dry, light, and shatteringly crisp like an extruded snack.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Cool both the shallots and the strained oil completely. The oil will be a deep amber and carry the entire shallot character; this is the most underrated free ingredient in the kitchen. Once cool, transfer the shallots to an airtight glass jar and the oil to a separate clean glass jar.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  The shallot oil is arguably more valuable than the shallots themselves. Use it as a finishing oil over plain rice, congee, noodles, vegetables, soft scrambled eggs, or anywhere a recipe asks for a bland neutral oil and you want a savory backbone. A teaspoon of shallot oil over a bowl of plain rice with a soft egg is a complete meal. The oil keeps three months refrigerated.

  The cold-oil start is the technique that separates this from a flash-fry. Hot oil at the start blasts the shallot exterior into the brown stage while the interior is still wet, producing shallots that look done but taste raw and oily. Starting cold lets the moisture in the shallot drive off slowly and evenly, so the entire slice dehydrates in step. By the time the exterior browns, the interior is already at the same dryness.

  Why slow heat: the sugars in shallots (mostly fructose) caramelize starting around 110°C and burn around 165°C. The cooking window from "barely cooked" to "burned" is roughly 55°C wide, which sounds generous but compresses to about 90 seconds in a hot pan. Holding the oil between 130 and 150°C for the bulk of the fry is what keeps the cook in the safe zone.

  Why pull when 70% are gold: the volume of hot oil retains enough heat to continue cooking the shallots for another minute after they leave the heat. Pulling at fully-amber means burned by the time you strain. Pulling at 70% means perfectly amber by the time you toss with salt.

  Storage: airtight glass jar at room temperature, dark cupboard. Two weeks at peak crispness; the textural quality slowly degrades over a month as ambient moisture seeps in. Refrigeration is unnecessary and introduces condensation when removed, which softens the shallots faster. The shallot oil, separately stored, lasts three months refrigerated and a month at room temperature in a cool cupboard.

  Common applications: top a bowl of pho, congee, fried rice, soft tofu, plain rice with a poached egg, or any cooked vegetable that wants a savory crunch. Vietnamese xôi (sticky rice with mung bean) is incomplete without it. The Indonesian bawang goreng version dusts on top of nasi goreng. The Chinese yau zha sung version goes into wonton soup and noodle bowls.
