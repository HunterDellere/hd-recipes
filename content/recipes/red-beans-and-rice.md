source:
  name: 'New Orleans Creole tradition (the Monday red bean institution)'
  note: 'Red beans on Monday is a New Orleans tradition dating to the antebellum era: Sunday roast ham left a ham bone, Monday was wash day with the housekeeper hands occupied, the beans cooked themselves on the back of the stove all day, finished in time for dinner. Camellia brand dried red kidney beans (from a New Orleans family-run company) are the canonical bean; the smaller Sea Island red bean is a heritage alternative. Louis Armstrong famously signed his letters "Red beans and ricely yours."'

before_you_start:
  - 'Soak the 500 g dried red beans overnight (8 to 12 hours) in plenty of cold water with 5 g salt. The soak hydrates the beans evenly so they cook in 2 hours rather than 4; the salt seasons the bean interior and helps the skin stay intact during the long cook (counterintuitive but well-documented; salted beans are less likely to split than unsalted). If you must do the quick-soak: cover beans with boiling water, hold 1 hour, drain.'
  - 'Cook the long-grain rice as a PARALLEL TRACK in the last 30 minutes of the bean simmer. Standard 2:1 water-to-rice ratio, 18 minutes simmer, 10 minutes covered rest. The rice is the partner, not optional; the beans pool over and through the rice in the bowl.'
  - 'Source real smoked pork (ham hock, smoked turkey wing, or tasso). The smoke goes through the entire pot of beans; trying to substitute liquid smoke or smoked paprika alone produces a different (lesser) dish. If you can find tasso (heavily-spiced cured pork shoulder), use 200 g in place of the hock and serve with andouille on the side.'

steps:
  - text: 'Soak the beans. The night before, sort 500 g dried red kidney beans (pick out pebbles, broken beans, shriveled beans). Rinse under cold water. Place in a large bowl with 2 L cold water and 5 g kosher salt. Soak 8 to 12 hours uncovered at room temperature. The beans will more than double in volume; the salted soak hydrates the bean interiors evenly and seasons the skin so the cook is shorter and the skins stay intact.'
    time_min: 600
  - text: 'Drain and prep the proteins. Drain the soaked 500 g beans in a colander; rinse briefly. Rinse the 400 g smoked ham hock under cold water to wash off any surface curing residue. Slice the 300 g andouille into 1 cm coins (reserve for Phase 5).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Sweat the Trinity. Heat 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy 6 to 7 quart Dutch oven over medium heat. Add the 250 g onion, 120 g celery, and 120 g bell pepper. Sweat 8 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the onion turns translucent and the vegetables soften. Add the 25 g / 8 cloves minced garlic; stir 60 seconds.'
    technique: 'sweating-aromatics'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Add beans, hock, water, and aromatics. Add the drained 500 g soaked beans, the 400 g ham hock, 2000 ml / 8 cups water, 3 bay leaves, 3 g / 1½ tsp thyme, 3 g / 1½ tsp smoked paprika, 2 g / ½ tsp cayenne, 3 g / 1 tsp black pepper, and 8 g / 1½ tsp Worcestershire. Do NOT add salt yet; the hock will release significant salt over the simmer, and salting now risks over-seasoning.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Bring to a simmer; cook low and slow. Bring the pot to a hard boil over medium-high heat (10 to 12 minutes), then immediately reduce to low so the surface trembles, no big bubbles. Cover with the lid offset (slight gap for steam release). Cook 2 to 2.5 hours, stirring every 30 minutes to prevent the bottom from scorching. The beans are done when they crush easily between a spoon and the side of the pot, and the cooking liquid has thickened from translucent broth to creamy-opaque from released bean starch. If the liquid drops below the bean surface during the simmer, add hot water in 200 g splashes to keep them just submerged.'
    technique: 'braising'
    time_min: 145
  - text: 'Break down some of the beans. After 2 hours, scoop out 200 g / 1 cup of the cooked beans into a small bowl. Mash with a fork into a coarse paste. Stir the mash back into the pot. This is the canonical creaminess move: mashing some of the beans into the pot liquor produces the thick, gravy-like consistency that defines New Orleans red beans (as opposed to a thinner chili-bean texture). The pot should now be visibly creamy, with whole beans suspended in a thick bean-starch gravy.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add andouille and finish. Stir in the 300 g sliced andouille coins. Simmer uncovered 25 to 30 more minutes; the sausage warms through and contributes its fat to the gravy, the pot reduces slightly, and the consistency thickens further. Fish out the ham hock with tongs. Pull the meat off the bone with a fork; chop into bite-sized pieces and stir back into the pot. Discard the bone and any cartilage.'
    time_min: 28
  - text: 'Taste and adjust. Fish out the 3 bay leaves. Taste the beans. The dish should read deeply savory with an obvious smoke layer, a warm slow heat, and a creamy thick body. Salt should register as seasoning, not as salt. Add the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt by the half-teaspoon, tasting between additions; some hocks are saltier than others. If the gravy is too thick, splash in hot water 60 g at a time; if too thin, simmer uncovered 5 to 10 more minutes.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Serve over rice. Mound 120 g / ~½ cup cooked rice in the center of each wide soup bowl. Ladle 250 to 300 g of beans around (not over) the rice. Scatter the 40 g sliced green onions and 12 g chopped parsley over the surface. Each diner adds hot sauce from the table to their preference and stirs the rice into the beans at the bowl. Crusty French bread or cornbread on the side is the traditional accompaniment for mopping the last of the gravy.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why Monday. The New Orleans tradition of red beans on Monday dates to the antebellum and post-Civil-War era when Sunday was the family roast (often ham), Monday was wash day (housekeepers and home cooks had hands full of laundry), and a pot of beans could simmer untended on the back of the stove all morning. The Sunday ham bone went straight into Monday''s beans, contributing salt, smoke, and gelatin without requiring fresh meat. The dish persists in restaurants and home kitchens across New Orleans as a Monday institution; many bars and diners still feature it as a Monday-only special.

  The salted soak is correct. Long-standing kitchen folklore says salting beans before cooking makes them tough; the food-science research (Russ Parsons, Harold McGee, Modernist Cuisine) has demonstrated the opposite. Salted soak liquid penetrates the bean skin and softens the pectin in the cell walls, producing more evenly cooked beans with less skin splitting. The 5 g salt in 2 L soak water is the calibrated ratio; double the salt produces beans seasoned through to the interior, which is good, but reduces the salt available to add later.

  Camellia beans specifically. The Camellia brand red bean (a New Orleans family company, founded 1923) is a smaller, creamier variety of red kidney than the supermarket dark red kidney bean. It cooks softer, breaks down more readily, and produces the canonically creamy New Orleans red bean texture. Order online or substitute with Sea Island red beans (a heritage Carolina low-country variety from Anson Mills); supermarket dark red kidneys work but produce a chunkier, less creamy dish.

  The mash-back step is structural. Mashing 200 g of the cooked beans into a paste and stirring back into the pot is the canonical move that transforms the dish from "beans in broth" to "beans in gravy." The released bean starch thickens the cooking liquid into the spoon-coating consistency that defines New Orleans red beans. Skipping this step produces a soupy version that reads more like chili.

  Hot sauce at the table. Crystal hot sauce (Louisiana-made, vinegar-forward, mild) is the canonical New Orleans choice over a sweeter Tabasco. Each diner adds 2 to 4 dashes per portion at the bowl; the hot sauce''s vinegar lift cuts through the rich pork and bean starch in a way that no in-pot heat does.

  Day-2 is better than day-1. The smoke, salt, and bean starch continue to integrate overnight. Refrigerate up to 5 days; freezes 3 months. Reheat gently with a 60 g splash of water per portion to revive the gravy consistency.

  No tomato. Some Tex-Mex and chili-adjacent red bean recipes add tomato; New Orleans red beans do not. The dish is meant to read brown and creamy from bean starch and pork fat, not red from tomato. Adding tomato pushes the dish toward chili and away from its Louisiana identity.
