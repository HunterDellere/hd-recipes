steps:
  - text: 'Put a heavy rimmed sheet pan in the oven and preheat both to 230 °C / 450 °F. Give the pan a full 10 minutes at temperature; the cut faces have to sear on contact, not warm up slowly.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Toss the 800 g / 1 large head of cauliflower florets (and the tender inner leaves) in a bowl with the 35 g / 2½ tbsp olive oil, the 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper until every cut face is slicked with oil. Pull the hot pan out and lay the florets in a single uncrowded layer, as many cut faces flat against the metal as you can manage; they should sizzle faintly on contact. Crowding steams them, so use two pans if one will not hold a clean single layer.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Roast undisturbed for 18 minutes. Do not stir early, the cut faces need uninterrupted contact with the hot pan to build the deep brown crust, and lifting to peek resets the clock. The loose leaves and small bits will char to crisp, which is the best part of the batch.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Check and flip once. At 18 minutes the down-faces should be deep brown to nearly black at the edges. Flip the florets with a spatula and give them another 5 to 8 minutes to brown a second face and finish tender, a floret should give easily when pressed. Total roast is 23 to 26 minutes depending on your oven and floret size.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Make the finishing dress while it roasts. In a small bowl stir together the zest and juice of the 1 lemon, the 1 g / ½ tsp red pepper flakes, the 4 g / 1 clove grated garlic, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp raw olive oil. The raw garlic stays pungent this way rather than mellowing in the oven.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish off the heat. Tip the screaming-hot cauliflower into a serving bowl, pour over the lemon-chili-garlic dress, and toss so it coats every piece (the residual heat blooms the garlic and chili just enough to take off the raw edge). Scatter the 10 g / handful of chopped parsley and toss once more. Taste: it should read nutty-sweet from the char, sharp from the lemon, with a warm chili tail. Add a pinch more salt or a squeeze more lemon if it reads flat. Serve hot or warm.'

notes: |
  Why high heat and a preheated pan. Cauliflower's reputation problem is a cooking-method problem. Boiled or steamed past 6 minutes, its glucosinolates break down into dimethyl sulfide and methanethiol, the rotten-cabbage volatiles, and the texture goes to mush. Roasted dry at 230 °C, the opposite happens: the surface moisture flashes off fast, the brassica sugars caramelize, the cut faces build a deep Maillard crust, and the sulfurous compounds never get the long wet cooking window they need to dominate. The result is nutty, sweet, and savory. A preheated pan is what makes the cut face sear on contact rather than releasing its water and steaming pale; it is the difference between a side people pick at and one they fight over.

  Why the acid goes on after. Lemon juice tossed onto raw florets before the roast does two bad things: the water content steams the cut faces and blocks browning, and the acid has nothing to lift once it has cooked away to nothing in 25 minutes of dry heat. Added to the hot finished cauliflower, the same lemon reads as a sharp, aromatic brightness that cuts the richness of the char and wakes the whole dish up. The zest, stirred into raw oil off-heat, keeps its volatile citrus oils intact, which a roast would drive off entirely.

  The leaves and small bits are the best part. The tender inner leaves and the crumbs that fall off during cutting will char into crisp, dark, intensely savory chips on the hot pan. Roast them alongside the florets rather than discarding them; they are the most concentrated bite in the batch.

  Sizing and crowding. Uniform 3 to 4 cm florets finish at once; a wide size spread burns the small pieces while the large cores stay raw. And the single most common failure is crowding, piled florets trap their own steam and never brown. One clean single layer, two pans if needed, is non-negotiable for the crust that is the entire point of the dish.
