source:
  note: 'Misir wat is the lentil version of the wat family, structurally identical to doro wat (chicken) and yebeg wat (lamb) but built on red lentils instead of meat. The 30 to 45 minute dry onion sweat is the same; the berbere bloom in niter kibbeh is the same; only the protein changes. The Ethiopian Orthodox fasting calendar (roughly 180 days a year for observant practitioners) excludes animal products entirely, and the legume-and-vegetable wats of misir wat, shiro, kik alicha, and gomen are the structural fasting-day diet that produced a deeply traditional vegan cooking culture long before Western vegetarianism was a concept.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The 30 to 45 minute dry onion sweat is the foundational step; do not shortcut it. Onions are cooked dry in the pot (no oil, no water, lid off) until they break down to a thick brown paste. Without this, the dish has no body.'
  - 'Have a jar of niter kibbeh ready, see recipes/niter-kibbeh. A vegan version of niter kibbeh can be made with virgin coconut oil or vegan butter clarified the same way, but plain neutral oil will leave the dish reading flat.'
  - 'Rinse the red lentils thoroughly in cold water until the rinse water runs nearly clear; the surface starch otherwise foams aggressively during the simmer and clouds the sauce.'

steps:
  - text: 'In a heavy 4 to 5 quart Dutch oven over medium-low heat, add the 400 g / 2 medium onions worth of very finely diced onion to the dry pot. No oil, no water, no fat. Cook with the lid off, stirring every 2 to 3 minutes with a wooden spoon, for 30 to 45 minutes. The onions go through a long arc: first they release their water and steam, then translucent, then pale gold, then deep gold and broken down into a thick brown paste. If anything sticks aggressively, drop the heat one notch. Pull from heat when the onions are a thick mahogany paste that holds the shape of a spoon-drag across the bottom.'
    technique: 'dry-sweat'
    time_min: 40
  - text: 'Add the 60 g / ¼ cup niter kibbeh to the onion paste and stir over medium-low heat for 2 minutes until the fat melts and integrates. Add the 20 g / 5 cloves microplaned garlic and the 15 g / 3 cm knob microplaned ginger and cook 1 minute, stirring constantly, until fragrant.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add the 30 g / 3 tbsp berbere and the 30 g / 2 tbsp tomato paste and cook 3 minutes, stirring constantly, blooming the spices in the niter kibbeh. The mixture turns from brown-onion to brick-red as the berbere hydrates in the fat. Watch closely; berbere can scorch in under 30 seconds if the heat is too high or you stop stirring.'
    technique: 'bloom-spice'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add the 300 g / 1½ cups rinsed split red lentils and the 900 ml / 3¾ cups water or vegetable stock and the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt. Stir to combine; the mixture should be soupy at this stage. Bring to a simmer over medium heat, then drop to low. Simmer uncovered for 25 to 35 minutes, stirring every 5 minutes to prevent the lentils from sticking to the bottom, until the lentils have completely broken down and the mixture is the consistency of thick refried beans, holding the shape of a spoon-drag across the bottom of the pot.'
    technique: 'simmer'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Pull from heat. Stir in the 15 g / 1 tbsp lemon juice. Taste; the dish should read deeply spiced, slightly tangy from the lemon, fully salted. If it reads flat, more salt; if too sharp, a teaspoon of additional niter kibbeh rounds the edge.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Serve in a wide shallow bowl, ladled over or beside torn injera, as part of a shared Ethiopian platter or as a standalone bowl. Traditional accompaniments include gomen (collard greens) and a small dish of mitmita (a hotter spice blend) for diners who want more chili heat.'
    time_min: 2

Extend the simmer to 50 minutes (yellow split peas take longer to break down than red lentils). The dish is kik alicha, the mild yellow wat that sits alongside misir wat on a canonical Ethiopian platter and provides color contrast against the brick-red misir.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'liquid'
    to: 'shiro, with chickpea flour'
    how: 'Replace the lentils with 200 g chickpea flour (besan) whisked into the water before adding to the spiced base, and reduce water to 700 ml. The chickpea flour cooks into a smooth thick paste in 15 minutes, producing shiro wat, the chickpea-flour version that is a different texture entirely (smooth instead of pebbly) and a faster cook.'
    kind: 'regional'

notes: |
  Why split red lentils specifically. Split red lentils (the small orange-red lentils sold as masoor dal in Indian groceries) lose their skins in processing and break down completely in 25 to 30 minutes of simmering. The resulting texture is a thick smooth-pebbly paste that holds the shape of a spoon-drag across the pot, which is the canonical misir wat finish. Whole red lentils with skins on, brown lentils, or green lentils all hold their shape and produce a lentil soup; the dish becomes recognizable but wrong.

  The umami source is the niter kibbeh aromatic infusion plus the tomato paste plus the deep onion-sweat sugars; together they substitute for the meat collagen that drives the meat wats. The finishing acid is the lemon at the end. No animal glutamate source, fully vegan, structurally complete.

  Make-ahead and reheat. Misir wat improves overnight; the spices integrate and the texture firms further. Reheat gently with a splash of water to loosen, covered, over low heat for 10 minutes. Holds 4 to 5 days refrigerated, 3 months frozen. The dish freezes better than doro wat because there are no eggs.
