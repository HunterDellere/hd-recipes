steps:
  - text: 'Heat oven to 350°F (175°C) with a rack in the lower-middle. Butter two 4-inch round pans heavily with about 10 g / 2 tsp soft butter total, line each base with a parchment circle, butter again, and dust with cocoa, knocking out the excess. Set the pans on a sheet tray.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Whisk the 20 g / 3 tbsp Dutch-process cocoa with the 60 ml / ¼ cup hot espresso in a small bowl until it forms a glossy black paste with no dry pockets. Let it sit 2 minutes; the heat melts residual cocoa butter and hydrates the starch granules, which is what unlocks the deep red-brown color and the chocolate-amplifying bitterness from the coffee.'
    technique: 'blooming-cocoa'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Combine the 110 g / 4 oz chopped dark chocolate, 85 g / 6 tbsp butter, and 1.5 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt in a heatproof bowl over a pan of barely simmering water (or microwave in 20-second bursts, stirring between). Stir slowly with a spatula until fully smooth and glossy: the cocoa butter and dairy fat must form a stable emulsion, not separate. Off heat. Stir in the cocoa bloom from step 2; the paste loosens immediately and the color goes near-black.'
    technique: 'chocolate-butter-emulsion'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'In a medium bowl, whisk the 2 eggs, 65 g / ⅓ cup granulated sugar, 50 g / ¼ cup dark brown sugar, and 4 g / 1 tsp vanilla extract until the mixture lightens visibly and runs in a smooth ribbon off the whisk, about 60 seconds. The dark brown sugar is doing two jobs: molasses adds bitterness that bridges to the cocoa, and the hygroscopic invert sugars keep the crumb moist for days.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour the warm chocolate-butter mass (about 230 g) into the egg mixture in a slow stream while whisking constantly. The chocolate must be warm but not hot; above 50°C / 120°F it will scramble the eggs. Whisk just until uniform and glossy.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'In a small bowl, whisk the 75 g / ½ cup + 1 tbsp all-purpose flour, 2 g / ⅓ tsp baking soda, and 1 g / ¼ tsp baking powder. Sift over the chocolate-egg mixture and fold with a spatula in three passes, just until no streaks remain. Stop the moment the batter is uniform; gluten development reads as a tough crumb in a cake this rich.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Divide between the two pans (about 235 g per pan). Tap each pan firmly on the counter twice to release any large air pockets, then sprinkle 3 g / ½ tsp turbinado sugar evenly over each surface. The turbinado will not fully dissolve in 24 minutes; it lacquers into a thin sugar crackle that is the cake''s entire visual identity in place of frosting.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Bake 24 to 28 minutes. Pull when the edges are set and matte, the top is fully crackled and gently domed, and a toothpick inserted halfway between the rim and the center comes out with moist crumbs clinging (not raw batter, not fully clean). The center will read as just-set; this is correct, the cake firms as it cools and produces the dense, fudge-textured interior.'
    time_min: 26
  - text: 'Cool in the pans on a rack 10 minutes; the structure is fragile while hot. Run a thin knife around each edge, invert onto a plate, peel the parchment, and flip back onto a second plate so the crackle top is up. Sprinkle each cake with a 0.5 g pinch of flaky sea salt.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Serve warm or at room temperature. Warm: the interior is near-fondant and the cake reads as molten-adjacent. Room temp: the interior firms to a true fudge and the flavor reads more cleanly because the cocoa volatiles have settled. Refrigerated, the cake goes properly truffle-textured but the cocoa volatiles mute; pull it 30 minutes before eating to recover.'
    time_min: 0

modifications:
  - for: 'crumb texture'
    to: 'fully flourless torte'
    how: 'Drop the flour, baking soda, and baking powder entirely. Increase eggs to 3, separate them, whip the whites to soft peaks with the granulated sugar, and fold into the chocolate-butter base after the yolks are stirred in. The cake becomes a true flourless chocolate torte: denser, glossier on the cut, no crumb structure. Bake 22 to 25 minutes.'
    kind: 'texture'
  - for: 'flavor accent'
    to: 'orange-cardamom'
    how: 'Replace the 60 ml espresso bloom with 60 ml hot orange juice and add 0.5 g / ¼ tsp ground cardamom plus 2 g / 1 tsp orange zest to the cocoa paste. Coffee-style depth swaps for a brighter aromatic register.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'molten center'
    to: 'salted-caramel core'
    how: 'After dividing batter into pans, drop a 1 tbsp / 18 g spoon of thick salted caramel into the center of each pan and use a knife tip to swirl once. The caramel sinks during baking and produces a molten core that the crackle top hides until the cut.'
    kind: 'texture'

substitutions:
  - for: 'dark chocolate (70% cacao)'
    use: 'bittersweet 60% chocolate'
    note: 'Sweeter and lower in cocoa solids. Drop the granulated sugar by 10 g to compensate, or the cake will taste candy-sweet.'
  - for: 'Dutch-process cocoa powder'
    use: 'natural cocoa powder'
    note: 'Acidic, not Dutched. Color will be lighter brown and the chocolate flavor will read sharper, less round. Acceptable but not ideal here; the bloom step is calibrated for Dutched cocoa''s neutralized pH and the baking soda quantity is set for it.'
  - for: 'hot brewed espresso'
    use: 'hot water + 2 g / 1 tsp instant espresso powder'
    note: 'Same chocolate-amplification function. Avoid skipping the coffee element entirely; the bitterness it adds is what keeps the cake from reading flat-sweet at this chocolate ratio.'
  - for: 'turbinado sugar topping'
    use: 'demerara sugar'
    note: 'Same coarse-crystal lacquering behavior; demerara is a touch lighter in molasses character. Plain granulated sugar will dissolve flush and disappear, so use a coarse crystal or skip the topping.'

notes: |
  Why no frosting earns the name. At a 110 g chocolate to 85 g butter ratio against 75 g of flour, the cake carries the fat-and-cocoa payload that frosting normally provides. Layer cake gets frosting because each thin sponge is structurally lean and needs an external chocolate-fat carrier between layers; this format inverts that, with the structure carrying the fat directly. A buttercream on top would be redundant chocolate-fat on chocolate-fat with nothing to balance it.

  The crackle top replaces frosting visually and texturally. The turbinado does not fully dissolve in the bake; it fuses into the surface as a translucent sugar lacquer over the dome that forms naturally from the leavener-driven lift. It reads as a deliberate finish, not as bare cake.

  The chocolate-butter emulsion is the structural backbone. Melting the chocolate and butter together (rather than creaming butter with sugar and adding cocoa later) means the cocoa butter and dairy fat are fully integrated before the eggs and flour ever appear. That single foundation is what produces the dense, even, fudge-textured crumb instead of a conventional cake-flour matrix with chocolate suspended in it.

  Why two 4-inch pans, not one 6-inch. The surface-area-to-volume ratio is what produces the crackle top and the contrast between set edge and tender center; a single larger cake bakes through with a flatter, less developed surface and a more uniform interior. Individual format is the point of the dish, not just a portion choice.
