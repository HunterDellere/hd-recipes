    why: 'Pulled at 160°F internal, then rested for 3 minutes covered, the meat carries to roughly 165°F (USDA published-safe instantaneous target). Resting under foil drives the carryover; pulling at 165°F internal directly produces dry, chalky chicken. Vulnerable diners (pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, very old) should be served chicken cooked to 165°F internal with no carryover credit.'

source:
  name: 'The Levantine street-food canon. Shawarma is documented across the eastern Mediterranean since at least the Ottoman period and is closely related to Turkish döner and Greek gyro (all three share the vertical spit and the thin-slice technique).'
  note: 'Two structural splits: (1) the meat. Chicken shawarma (the version here) is the most common modern shawarma globally; lamb shawarma is the older traditional form; beef shawarma is more common in some regional variants. (2) The cooking method. Commercial shawarma uses a vertical spit (the meat layered onto a vertical metal stake and rotated against a heat source); the home version reformats this as either a sheet-pan roast (the most common) or an improvised vertical-skewer roast (which gets closer to the spit texture). The version here is the sheet-pan-and-broil method; the vertical-skewer method is in modifications.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the marinade: in a large bowl, whisk together the 4 g cumin, 3 g coriander, 3 g allspice, 2 g cardamom, 3 g paprika, 2 g cinnamon, 2 g turmeric, 3 g black pepper, and 14 g salt. Add the 100 g / scant ½ cup yogurt, 45 g / 3 tbsp olive oil, 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, and 16 g / 4 grated garlic cloves. Whisk to combine. The marinade should be a deep orange-amber paste, thick enough to cling to the chicken.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add the 900 g chicken thighs (butterflied or halved); turn to coat every piece thoroughly. Cover and refrigerate 4 to 24 hours (8 to 12 hours is the structural sweet spot).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'When ready to cook, preheat the oven to 220°C / 425°F. Brush a heavy sheet pan with the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil. Arrange the marinated chicken in a single layer on the sheet pan, slightly overlapping is fine but do not pile (overlapping creates the structural layered shawarma look as the edges crisp differently from the centers).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Roast 18 to 22 minutes, until the chicken is cooked through (internal temperature 71°C / 160°F at the thickest point) and the marinade has caramelized on the surface. The chicken should look deep amber-brown on top with darker char marks at the thinner edges.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Turn the broiler to high. Move the sheet pan to the top rack, about 10 cm from the heating element. Broil 3 to 5 minutes, watching closely, until the edges are crisped and slightly charred and the surface is glossy with caramelized marinade. The broil is the structural step that mimics the vertical spit; do not skip it.'
    technique: 'broiler-char'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Pull the chicken from the oven; let rest 5 minutes on the sheet pan (covered loosely with foil) to redistribute the juices.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Slice the chicken thin (3 to 4 mm slices against the grain). Pile the slices on a serving platter with all the crisp edges showing. While the chicken rests, warm the 400 g pita briefly in the oven (the chicken is already out so the oven is empty and still hot; 2 minutes is plenty).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Assemble each wrap: spread a generous 50 g of tahini-yogurt sauce across the warm pita. Pile 150 g of sliced chicken down the center. Add 2 slices tomato, 2 slices cucumber, a small handful of red onion (sprinkled with sumac), and a generous pinch of parsley leaves. Roll the pita tightly: tuck one end inward, then roll the long edge across the filling, tucking firmly as you go. Wrap the bottom half in parchment paper if eating on the go (the paper holds the wrap closed and keeps the bottom from getting drenched). Serve immediately; the wrap is built and eaten within 90 seconds. Alternatively, plate the chicken with the sauce and vegetables alongside as a shawarma platter, eaten with the warmed pita on the side.'
    time_min: 2

    to: 'as a home vertical-spit shawarma'
    how: 'Skewer the marinated chicken thighs vertically onto a single tall metal skewer (heavy 12-inch metal skewer or two stacked) standing upright in a cast-iron skillet (the skewer base wedged into a half potato or a small onion to keep it upright). Slice the chicken thin off the vertical stack with a sharp knife, working from top to bottom; the slices fall onto the skillet bottom and bathe in the juices. The result is structurally the closest home approximation to vertical-spit shawarma and is dramatically more crisp-edged than the sheet-pan method.'

notes: |
  Why seven-spice. The Lebanese baharat seven-spice blend (cumin, coriander, allspice, cardamom, paprika, cinnamon, turmeric, with variations) is what distinguishes Levantine shawarma from Turkish döner (which uses fewer spices, often just cumin and paprika) or Greek gyro (which uses oregano, thyme, and rosemary). The two structural Lebanese signatures are allspice and cardamom; without them, the blend reads as a different cuisine. Many recipes call for "shawarma seasoning" from a jar but supermarket blends vary wildly and often skimp on the structural spices in favor of cheaper paprika and salt; building from individual ground spices is worth the extra 2 minutes of measuring.

  Why the broil is structural. The defining textural feature of shawarma is the crisp slightly-charred edges produced by the vertical spit (where the meat surface is constantly exposed to the heat source and the crisped outer layer is sliced off continuously). The home sheet-pan version cannot reproduce this continuous-crisp effect, but the broil step at the end approximates it by hitting the cooked chicken with direct high heat for the final 3 to 5 minutes; the marinade surface caramelizes deeply, the edges crisp, and the dish develops the structural shawarma texture. Skip the broil and the chicken reads as marinated baked chicken rather than as shawarma.

  Versatility. The marinade transfers cleanly to: lamb cubes for grilled shawarma kebabs (skewered and grilled), shrimp shawarma (cut marinade time to 30 minutes; grill or roast 4 minutes), pork shawarma (in non-Muslim or non-Jewish contexts; cut from the pork shoulder, marinate 12 hours, roast same way). The seven-spice blend is the structural base for many Levantine preparations and can be made in larger batches and stored 6 months in an airtight jar.

  Pairs across Lebanese (canonical), Palestinian, Syrian, Jordanian, Israeli (where shawarma is one of the most ubiquitous street foods), Egyptian, and broader Levantine diaspora cookery globally. Chicken shawarma is one of the most internationally exported Middle Eastern dishes and appears in food courts, casual restaurants, and home kitchens worldwide.

  Storage. Marinated raw chicken keeps 48 hours refrigerated. Cooked sliced chicken keeps 4 days refrigerated; reheat briefly under the broiler (2 minutes) to restore the crisp edges, or eat cold in a wrap. The assembled wrap does not store well (the pita gets soggy and the vegetables release water); assemble at service from prepped components.
