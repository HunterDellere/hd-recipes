before_you_start:
  - 'This is a raw emulsion held by the gochujang''s starch and the honey, not a cooked or hydrocolloid-stabilized sauce. It will slowly separate on standing; whisk or shake it again right before dressing anything. Dress slaws and grains no more than 20 minutes before serving so the acid does not break down the vegetables into limp, weeping shreds.'
  - 'Grate the garlic on a microplane rather than mincing. Raw garlic is structural here and the fine paste integrates smoothly; mincing leaves aggressive raw-garlic shards that sit on top of the dressing instead of dissolving into it.'

steps:
  - text: 'In a medium bowl, combine the 60 g / 3 tbsp gochujang, the 25 g / 1 tbsp honey, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp warm water. Whisk hard with a fork for 30 seconds until the cold paste fully loosens and the mixture is smooth and glossy. The warm water and the honey together dissolve the dense paste; skip this step and the gochujang stays in stubborn lumps that never integrate.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Whisk in the 30 g / 2 tbsp fresh lime juice, the 1 g / ½ tsp lime zest, the 6 g / 1 grated garlic clove, and the 10 g / 2 tsp soy sauce. The acid will thin the base noticeably and lift the color brighter. Whisk until uniform.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Build the emulsion. Combine the 15 g / 1 tbsp toasted sesame oil and the 25 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a measuring cup. Whisking the gochujang base constantly and steadily, pour the combined oils in a thin stream down the side of the bowl. The gochujang starch and the honey hold the oil in a loose suspension; pour too fast and the oil pools on top instead of emulsifying, so keep the stream thin and the whisk moving. The dressing should turn opaque, glossy, and pourable, the texture of a loose vinaigrette.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Stir in the 4 g / 1½ tsp toasted sesame seeds and the 8 g / 1 sliced scallion. Taste on a spoon or a torn lettuce leaf and balance: the dressing should read lime-bright first, then sweet, then the fermented gochujang depth and slow heat as a tail, with the soy holding the savory floor. If flat, another 5 g / 1 tsp soy; if too sharp, a few more grams of honey; if too thick to pour, whisk in warm water 1 teaspoon at a time.'

  - text: 'Use within 20 minutes for dressed slaws and grain bowls (toss to coat just before serving so the acid does not wilt the vegetables to weeping mush), or transfer to a clean glass jar and refrigerate up to 1 week. The dressing separates on standing because it is a raw emulsion held only by starch and honey; shake or whisk it back together each time before pouring. notes: |
  Why lime instead of rice vinegar. The canonical Korean muchim dressing thins gochujang with rice vinegar, which sits under the fermented base as a soft, rounding acid. Lime works differently: its acid is sharper and its zest oils carry limonene and other terpenes that read as a bright aromatic lift over the dressing. Against the deep, slow, sweet-fermented gochujang, that contrast is the entire design. The zest matters as much as the juice here; the juice supplies the sour, but the grated peel oils are what make the dressing smell of lime before it tastes of chili. This is a contemporary divergence from tradition, not a claim that Koreans dress muchim with lime; the rice-vinegar version is in the substitutions for anyone who wants the canonical form.

  How the emulsion holds, and why it eventually breaks. This is a raw emulsion with no egg yolk and no hydrocolloid stabilizer. The gochujang's hydrolyzed-rice starch and the honey's viscosity are doing all the work, suspending the oil droplets in the water-and-acid phase. That is enough to hold a loose, pourable emulsion through a meal, but not enough to keep it indefinitely; on standing in the fridge, the oil slowly rises and the dressing separates into a darker base and an oil cap. This is not spoilage, it is physics. Whisk or shake it back together each time and it re-suspends fine. If you want a permanently stable, never-separating version, the creamy modification (whisking the dressing into yogurt) gives you that, because the dairy proteins and fat lock the emulsion in place.

  Pour the oil slow. The single failure mode in building this dressing is dumping the combined oils in all at once, which floods the emulsion's capacity to suspend them and leaves an oil slick floating on a thin gochujang base that no amount of whisking recovers cleanly. The discipline is the same as any vinaigrette or aioli: thin stream, constant whisk, let each addition disappear before the next. With only 40 g of total oil going into a starch-rich base, the emulsion is forgiving as long as you respect the stream.

  Dress late, eat soon. Acid and salt pull water out of shredded vegetables. A cabbage slaw dressed an hour ahead weeps into a puddle and goes limp; the same slaw dressed and tossed five minutes before serving stays crisp and bright. For make-ahead, keep the dressing and the vegetables separate and combine at the table. The exception is a deliberately quick-pickled muchim, where you want the vegetables to soften and drink the dressing; there, 20 to 30 minutes is the window, and beyond that the texture goes.

  Where this dressing belongs. It is built for cold and room-temperature applications where a thin, bright, pourable dressing does more than a thick glaze: grain bowls (rice or farro with grilled protein, kimchi, and quick-pickled cucumber), cabbage and carrot slaws, cold soba or rice-vermicelli noodle salads, shredded-chicken salads, and roasted vegetables tossed off the heat (sweet potato, Brussels sprouts, broccoli, cauliflower). For hot applications that want body and cling, a glaze or a dip, the thicker gochujang condiment-sauce is the better tool; this dressing would slide off. The two are deliberately different tools for different jobs.
