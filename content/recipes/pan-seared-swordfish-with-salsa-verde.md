source:
  name: 'The Mediterranean grilled-and-seared fish tradition, where firm oily fish (swordfish, tuna, monkfish) is cooked over high heat to a crust and finished with a raw herb-and-acid sauce. Swordfish in particular is a coastal Italian and Sicilian staple (pesce spada), eaten as steaks because the flesh is dense and meat-like rather than flaking.'
  note: 'Swordfish is a cross-section of a very large pelagic fish, so the steak is a slab of parallel muscle with sheets of collagen between the segments, structurally closer to a beef steak than to a flaky white fillet. That collagen and the near-total absence of intramuscular fat are why it dries so fast: there is no melting fat to keep the mouthfeel moist, and the muscle squeezes water out hard past 55°C. The entire technique is calibrated to pull it before that cliff and to supply, from the salsa verde, the fat, salt, and acid the lean flesh lacks on its own.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'swordfish at 52°C / 126°F internal (below the USDA published-safe 145°F for fish)'
    ref: 'safety/seafood-doneness#cooking-targets-for-fin-fish'
    why: 'The Mediterranean seared-swordfish tradition pulls the steak at 52°C / 126°F because the dense, collagen-rich, near-fatless flesh wrings itself dry and reads like cardboard past 55°C. The published 145°F is the conservative line for the immunocompromised. Swordfish from a reputable counter, brined and then hard-seared to a deep crust on both faces, follows long-standing coastal practice at the lower temperature, with the screaming-hot sear pasteurizing the surface where the bacterial load lives. Cooks who want the USDA-published-safe number should sear to 145°F / 63°C internal, accepting a drier steak; freeze-at-sea or sashimi-grade swordfish further lowers the risk.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Make the salsa verde first and let it sit at room temperature at least 15 minutes while you cook, ideally up to an hour. The garlic, anchovy, and capers need time to bleed into the olive oil; a salsa verde spooned over fish the second it is chopped reads as separate piles of herb and oil rather than one integrated sauce.'
  - 'Brine the steaks for 20 to 30 minutes while the salsa verde sits, then dry them hard. This is the whole moisture-insurance step for a fish that has none of its own, and it must finish before the pan goes on.'
  - 'Have an instant-read thermometer at the pan. Swordfish has a roughly 5°C window between perfect and ruined; pull at 52°C internal. Reading doneness by eye on a fish this dense is guesswork, and the cost of guessing wrong is a dry steak.'
  - 'The salsa verde is chopped by hand, not blended. A food processor whips air in and turns the parsley to a bruised green slurry. Use a sharp knife and a board, or a mortar for the garlic and anchovy only.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the salsa verde first so it can rest. Pound the 4 g / 1 clove grated garlic and the 8 g / 2 to 3 anchovy fillets to a paste in a mortar, or mince both together on the board until smooth. Scrape into a small bowl. Add the 15 g / 2 tbsp chopped capers, the zest of 1 lemon, the 8 g / ½ tbsp red wine vinegar, and the 5 g / 1 tsp dijon mustard. Whisk in the 90 g / scant ½ cup extra-virgin olive oil in a thin stream so it forms a loose, broken emulsion. Do not add the parsley or the lemon juice yet, acid and chlorophyll both fade if they sit too long. Let this base sit at room temperature 15 to 60 minutes; the garlic and anchovy bloom into the oil.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Brine the swordfish. Stir the 50 g / 3 tbsp kosher salt into the 1000 ml / 4 cups cold water until dissolved, a 5% brine. Submerge the two 170 g / 6 oz swordfish steaks and leave them 20 to 30 minutes, no longer. The brine does for swordfish what it does for a pork chop: salt diffuses in and the muscle proteins denature just enough to hold water against the squeeze of the sear, the only moisture insurance a near-fat-free fish gets. Past 40 minutes the texture turns rubbery-firm, so set a timer.'
    technique: 'brining'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Dry the steaks hard. Pull the swordfish from the brine and pat both faces and the edges completely dry with paper towels, pressing to wick moisture out of the surface. Set them on a wire rack and leave 5 minutes; a visibly dry surface is the precondition for a crust, because any surface water must boil off before the flesh can brown, and that delay overcooks the layer just under the surface. Season lightly with the 2 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt and the 1 g / ½ tsp coarse black pepper just before searing.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Preheat the pan. Set a heavy stainless or carbon-steel skillet over high heat for 3 to 4 minutes. Stainless is the right surface here: it builds fond and gives a more even crust than nonstick, which cannot run this hot. The pan is ready when a flicked drop of water balls up and skates across the surface (the Leidenfrost point, above 175°C). Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil and swirl to coat; it should shimmer and just begin to smoke within 10 seconds.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Sear the first side. Lay the two steaks into the smoking pan, away from you so the oil sprays off. They should sizzle loudly on contact. Press each flat with a fish spatula for 5 seconds for full contact, then leave them undisturbed for 2.5 to 3 minutes. Do not move or peek; the crust forms in one unbroken contact with the hot metal, and lifting early shears it off and restarts the clock. The edge of the flesh will turn opaque and creep about a third of the way up the side when the first side is ready.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Flip and finish to temperature. Flip the steaks to a clean spot in the pan (the just-vacated spot has cooled and pooled liquid). Sear the second side 2 to 2.5 minutes, then start checking the internal temperature in the center of the thickest steak with an instant-read thermometer. Pull both steaks the instant the center reads 52°C / 126°F. Swordfish carries over 2 to 3°C off-heat, landing at 54 to 55°C, which is the top edge of moist; that is the cliff. At 60°C and up the muscle has wrung itself dry and eats like cooked cardboard. If a steak is thicker than its partner, pull the thin one first and give the thick one another 30 seconds.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Rest briefly, then dress. Move the steaks to a warm plate and rest 3 minutes; the fibers relax and reabsorb the water the heat drove toward the center, so the cut surface stays glossy instead of weeping onto the plate. While they rest, finish the salsa verde: stir the 30 g / ¾ cup finely chopped parsley and the 15 g / 1 tbsp reserved lemon juice into the rested oil base. Taste it. It should read sharp, salty, and grassy, with the anchovy invisible as fish but present as depth; if it is flat, more lemon juice, if it is harsh, a few more drops of olive oil.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Plate. Set each swordfish steak on a warm plate. Spoon the salsa verde generously over and around, about 40 to 50 g per steak, letting it pool so each forkful can take a different ratio of sauce to fish. Drizzle the 10 g / 2 tsp finishing extra-virgin olive oil over the top and scatter the 1 g / ¼ tsp flaky salt across the cut surface. Serve with the 40 g / lemon wedges on the side; a squeeze of fresh juice at the table re-brightens the dish as it cools, because the cooked surface mutes the acid already on it. Bread to mop the sauce and a sharp green salad are the canonical companions.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why swordfish dries so fast. A swordfish steak is a cross-section of large parallel muscle bundles separated by sheets of collagen, with almost no intramuscular fat. In a marbled beef steak, melting fat lubricates the bite and masks moisture loss; swordfish has nothing to fall back on. When the muscle proteins contract past about 55°C they squeeze water out of the fibers faster than any fish with fat to spare, and the collagen sheets tighten rather than melt (they need long wet heat to gelatinize, which a quick sear never provides). The result above 60°C is the dry, chalky, cottony swordfish most people have been served and learned to dislike. Pulled at 52°C with a 2 to 3°C carryover, the same fish is moist, dense, and clean. The thermometer is not optional on a fish this unforgiving.

  Why brine, not just salt. The 5% brine for 20 to 30 minutes does two things a surface sprinkle of salt cannot. It seasons the interior of a thick steak evenly, and the partial denaturing of the surface proteins lets them hold onto water under the heat of the sear, so less of the steak's small moisture reserve is lost. It is the same logic as brining a lean pork chop or shrimp. Do not overshoot the time; past 40 minutes the surface goes rubbery and the texture suffers more than the seasoning helps.

  Why salsa verde, structurally. The sauce is not a garnish, it is the other half of the dish, supplying everything the fish lacks. Olive oil delivers the fat a lean fish has none of; anchovy and capers carry glutamate and salt for umami depth; lemon and vinegar bring the finishing acid that cuts the richness and lifts the whole plate. A perfectly cooked swordfish steak naked on a plate is competent but austere; with salsa verde it becomes a complete dish. Chop the herbs by hand and dress them at the last minute so the parsley stays bright green and grassy rather than bruising into a dull slurry, and let the oil-garlic-anchovy base sit ahead so those flavors marry before the green goes in.

  Buying and trimming. Ask for center-cut steaks of even thickness, ideally 1 to 1.25 inch, so the two cook at the same rate and the sear has time to build a crust before the thin interior overshoots. Trim the skin and the dark reddish-brown bloodline that runs along one edge; the bloodline is strong, oily, and slightly bitter, and on swordfish it can carry an off note. Fresh swordfish should smell clean and marine, not fishy; the flesh should be firm and translucent-ivory, sometimes with a faint pink or orange cast. Steaks with gaping or separating muscle segments are older or were frozen badly and will fall apart in the pan.
