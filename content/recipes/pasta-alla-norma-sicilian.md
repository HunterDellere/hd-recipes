title: 'Pasta alla Norma'
desc: 'The Catanian Sicilian pasta of fried eggplant, slow-cooked tomato sauce, torn basil, and an avalanche of grated ricotta salata. source:
  name: 'Catanian Sicilian canon'
  note: 'Named for Vincenzo Bellini''s opera Norma (Catania, 1831); the dish is one of the structural pillars of eastern Sicilian cooking. Defining elements: salt-purged fried eggplant (cubes, not slices, for surface area), a slow tomato sauce built on garlic and olive oil (no soffritto in the Roman-Bolognese sense), torn fresh basil at the finish, and grated ricotta salata (aged pressed sheep-milk ricotta, distinct from fresh ricotta or ricotta infornata). The cheese is non-negotiable: ricotta salata is dry, salty, and gratable, and the recipe reads as a different dish if substituted with fresh ricotta, parmigiano, or pecorino.'

steps:
  - text: 'Toss the 600 g / 2 medium eggplant cubes with the 12 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt in a colander set over a bowl. Let purge 45 minutes; the salt draws out water and bitter compounds, and collapses the spongy cell structure so the cubes fry without absorbing the pan of oil. After 45 minutes, rinse the cubes under cold water to remove surface salt; spread on a clean kitchen towel and blot thoroughly dry (any surface water will cause the oil to spit and the cubes will steam instead of frying).'
    technique: 'salting-eggplant'
    time_min: 50
  - text: 'Heat the 120 g / ½ cup plus 2 tbsp olive oil in a wide skillet over medium-high until shimmering (about 175°C / 350°F). Fry the eggplant cubes in two batches, single layer, 6 to 7 minutes per batch, turning every 2 minutes, until each face is deeply golden-brown and the interior has collapsed to soft custardy texture. Transfer to a paper-towel-lined plate; season immediately with a small pinch of salt. Pour off all but about 30 g / 2 tbsp of the frying oil; reserve the rest for sautéing other vegetables this week.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'In the same skillet over medium-low, add the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil to the reserved fond and the 15 g / 4 cloves sliced garlic. Sweat the garlic 90 seconds until fragrant and pale gold, never deeper; brown garlic in this dish reads bitter. Add the 1 g / ¼ tsp red pepper flakes; bloom 30 seconds.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 800 g / 28 oz hand-crushed San Marzano tomatoes (with juice). Bring to a vigorous simmer; reduce heat to medium-low. Simmer uncovered 20 to 25 minutes, breaking up the tomato chunks with a wooden spoon, until the sauce has reduced by about a third and the texture is jammy with visible olive oil emulsified through (not a thin watery tomato sauce). Season with the 4 g / ¾ tsp salt and taste; if the sauce reads too acidic for the tomato batch, add a pinch more salt rather than sugar (Sicilian tradition does not sweeten tomato sauce).'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'While the sauce reduces, bring the 4000 ml / 16 cups water to a hard boil with the 40 g / 3 tbsp salt. Add the 400 g / 14 oz rigatoni; cook 1 minute shy of package time (the pasta finishes in the sauce). When the pasta is at the 1-minute-shy mark, reserve 250 ml / 1 cup of starchy pasta water, then drain the pasta.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Add the drained pasta and the fried eggplant cubes to the skillet with the sauce. Add 60 ml / ¼ cup of the reserved pasta water. Toss vigorously over medium heat 60 to 90 seconds until the sauce coats every tube and the eggplant has integrated into the dish. The starchy water emulsifies the olive oil and tomato into a cohesive sauce that clings to the rigatoni rather than pooling at the bottom of the bowl. Taste, adjust salt.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Plate into warm bowls. Top each portion with a heavy sprinkle of the 80 g / ¾ cup grated ricotta salata (about 20 g per plate) and torn leaves of the 15 g / 1 cup fresh basil. The ricotta salata sits on top as a sharp salty seasoning, not melted in; the basil goes on whole, not chopped. Serve immediately.'
    time_min: 1

    how: 'Add 4 to 6 oil-packed anchovy fillets to the garlic in step 3; mash them into the oil with the back of a wooden spoon until they dissolve. notes: |
  The salt purge is the structural foundation of the dish. Eggplant is an open-cell sponge; dropped raw into hot oil, the cubes absorb fat aggressively and the dish reads as oil-saturated rather than fried. The 45-minute salt purge does two things: extracts about 15 percent of the eggplant''s water (which would otherwise turn the fry into a steam), and collapses the open-cell structure so the cubes brown rather than absorb. Rinsing afterward removes the surface salt; the season-from-the-inside has already happened. Cooks who skip this step end up with a greasy stew, not a Sicilian pasta.

  The ricotta salata is not interchangeable with other cheeses. Fresh ricotta is wet, creamy, and bland; it melts into the sauce as a cream layer and dilutes the tomato. Pecorino romano is dry but reads too aggressive and too sharp; it competes with the eggplant rather than seasoning it. Parmigiano reads as Northern Italian and is structurally wrong for a Catanian preparation. Ricotta salata is pressed-and-salted ricotta aged 2 to 3 months; the texture is firm enough to grate, the flavor is milky-salty-funky, and it sits on the pasta as visible flakes that finish each bite with a sharp salty seasoning. Cheese shops in any major US city stock it; smaller markets may have to be asked.

  The garlic-and-oil base (no onion, no carrot, no celery) is structurally Sicilian and distinguishes the sauce from the broader Italian soffritto tradition. Sicilian tomato cookery is typically aglio-olio (garlic and oil) based, with the sweetness of the tomato itself doing the work that onion does in Northern preparations. Skipping the onion is not a simplification; it is the cuisine.

  Storage and travel. The fried eggplant and the tomato sauce both keep separately for 3 days refrigerated; assemble fresh with newly cooked pasta. The finished dish reheats acceptably but the eggplant softens further and the basil discolors; eat the same day for the canonical texture. Does not freeze well; the eggplant texture collapses on thaw.
