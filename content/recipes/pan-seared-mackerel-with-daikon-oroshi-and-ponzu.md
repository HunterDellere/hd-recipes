  note: 'Saba (Pacific or Atlantic mackerel) is one of the most lipid-rich fish in the Japanese pantry at 12 to 18% fat, and the entire technique is a study in lipid management. Each element solves a specific problem the fish presents.'

    why: 'The Japanese saba canon pulls oily fish at 130 to 135°F for texture (the lipid fraction stays moist; past 60°C the flesh dries chalky). The 1.5% salt cure for 30 minutes firms the surface flesh through partial protein denaturation. The published 145°F is the conservative line for the immunocompromised; whole-muscle fish from a reputable counter, sourced fresh and cured before the sear, follows long-standing washoku and restaurant practice at the lower temperature. Cooks who want USDA-published-safe should pull at 145°F, accepting drier flesh.'
  - for: 'Histamine risk on mackerel and other scombroid species'

    why: 'Mackerel, tuna, mahi-mahi, sardines, and bluefish accumulate heat-stable histamine when stored above 40°F. Histamine is not destroyed by cooking, freezing, or curing. steps:
  - text: 'Examine the 350 g / 2 saba fillets. Run the back of a fingernail across the flesh from tail to head to feel for pin-bones; pull each one with sharp tweezers (a clean pull rather than a tear). The skin should be intact, unbroken, and silvery-blue-iridescent; any tear in the skin will let the fillet curl violently in the pan when it hits the heat. Pat the fillets thoroughly dry on both sides with paper towels.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Score the skin. Lay each fillet skin-up on the cutting board. With a very sharp paring knife held at a 30-degree angle to the surface, cut a tight cross-hatch pattern into the skin: parallel lines 3 mm apart in one direction, then perpendicular to make 3 mm × 3 mm diamonds. The cuts go just through the skin and 1 to 2 mm into the surface fat layer, never deep enough to slice into the muscle blocks below. The score does three things: it gives the contracting skin somewhere to release tension as it hits the pan (the fillet stays flat instead of curling into a tube), it dramatically increases the surface area where the subcutaneous fat can render and crisp, and it lets the cure (5 g / 1 tsp kosher salt applied in the next step) penetrate the skin uniformly rather than sitting on the silver hide.'
    technique: 'fish-skin-scoring'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Cure. Lay the scored fillets on a wire rack set over a small sheet pan. Brush both sides lightly with the 15 g / 1 tbsp sake; the sake rinses the surface lipids and removes the trace fishy off-notes that develop on mackerel skin within a few hours of harvest. Sprinkle the 5 g / 1 tsp kosher salt evenly over both sides of both fillets, working a small amount into the score lines on the skin side. The cure is 1.5% salt by fillet weight, the canonical saba shio ratio; less leaves the fillet under-seasoned for the rich flesh, more starts to read as aggressively salted shime-saba pickled mackerel rather than fresh-seared. Refrigerate the racked fillets uncovered for 30 minutes. Surface beading of clear liquid will appear within 10 minutes (the salt drawing water out via osmosis); by 30 minutes the surface will look slightly tacky and the flesh will have firmed visibly.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'While the fillets cure, grate the 200 g / 8 cm section daikon. Peel the section, then grate on the finest tooth of an oroshigane (Japanese ceramic-tooth grater) or the smallest holes of a microplane, working in a circular motion to keep the cell structure intact and produce the characteristic snowy fluffy texture. Stop and rotate the daikon every few strokes; grating in one direction produces stringy strands rather than the loose snow texture. Place the grated daikon in a fine-mesh sieve and let it drain over a bowl for 5 minutes; press very lightly with the back of a spoon to push out excess liquid (the grated daikon should be moist but not wet, and the bowl should hold 2 to 3 tbsp of expressed clear-pinkish liquid). The drain is necessary; ungrained oroshi sits in a puddle on the plate and dilutes the ponzu.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Set up the sear. Place a heavy carbon steel or cast iron skillet over high heat and let it preheat dry for 3 to 4 minutes; the cue is the first faint wisp of smoke from the pan surface and a drop of water sizzling and dancing into a single rolling bead within a second of contact (the Leidenfrost test). Add the 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil and tilt to coat the pan. The oil should shimmer, ripple aggressively, and just begin to smoke within 5 to 10 seconds. The pan must be at smoking temperature; cooler skin-down contact lets the fillet release moisture and steam itself rather than sear, producing soft skin and gray flesh.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Pat the cured fillets one more time with paper towels (the cure has continued drawing water; wet skin will steam) and sear. Lay the fillets in the pan skin-side-down, pulling each one toward you so the skin lays away from you (oil splash safety). The fillets will sizzle aggressively and curl slightly at the edges within the first 5 seconds; use the back of a flexible fish spatula to press each fillet flat against the pan for 15 to 20 seconds, ensuring full skin-to-pan contact across the entire surface. Do not move the fillets after that. Sear undisturbed at high heat for 3 to 4 minutes total; the visible cue is the cooked-flesh band climbing the side of the fillet from the bottom upward, reaching about two-thirds of the way up the thickness when the skin is done. The skin should release cleanly from the pan when ready; if you have to pry, give it 30 more seconds.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Flip and finish briefly. Slide the spatula under each fillet and turn it skin-side-up; the skin should be a deep mahogany-bronze with the cross-hatch score lines clearly defined and individually crisped. Cook flesh-side-down for only 30 to 60 seconds, just to set the surface and bring the internal temperature into the 55 to 58°C / 130 to 135°F window where the flesh is opaque-but-glossy and the lipid fraction is still moist. Saba past 60°C internal goes dry and chalky; under 50°C and the flesh reads gummy. Pull off heat the moment the surface flesh opaques.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Plate. On each of two warmed plates, mound half of the drained 200 g daikon oroshi as a small loose hill near the center. Lay each fillet skin-side-up so it leans against the daikon mound (the skin stays crisp because the daikon is cool and dry, not sitting in liquid). Pour 30 g / 2 tbsp of the ponzu around the base of the daikon and into the plate well, not over the fillet (the ponzu running over the skin will instantly soften the crackling; the diner pulls a piece of fish through the daikon and the ponzu pool with each bite). Scatter the 10 g / 1 stalk thin scallion rings across the daikon mound; sprinkle the 1 g / ¼ tsp shichimi tōgarashi over the daikon if using. Serve immediately with steamed Japanese short-grain rice and a clear miso soup; the rice and miso are not optional accompaniments, they are the structural counter to the rich saba.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why mackerel takes this exact treatment. Saba sits at 12 to 18% fat by weight, with the lipid concentrated in a dark muscle band along the lateral line and a dense subcutaneous fat layer just under the skin. That lipid load is the entire reason the fish is delicious (rich, oceanic, structurally creamy on the palate when fresh) and the entire reason it can taste fishy or oily when handled wrong (the lipid fraction oxidizes faster than the muscle protein degrades, and a saba past peak freshness reads as fishy oil before the flesh reads as off). The technique is a chain of decisions about lipid management. The salt cure firms the surface and pulls water that would otherwise steam the skin during the sear; the cross-hatch score gives the subcutaneous fat a path to render out under high heat instead of sitting trapped under the skin and going greasy; the smoking-pan sear renders the fat and crisps the skin in a single window so the flesh inside stays at the 55 to 58°C medium-rare doneness that the Japanese palate reads as correct for oily fish; the daikon and ponzu cut the perceived richness on the palate and reset the diner''s tongue between bites.

  The umami source is the ponzu (the soy sauce contributes glutamate, the kombu and katsuobushi in the homemade ponzu contribute additional glutamate-inosinate synergy) plus the natural inosinate in the saba flesh itself; the finishing acid is the citrus in the ponzu (yuzu juice, or yuzu-lemon-lime blend in homemade, or rice-vinegar-and-citrus in store-bought). The acid is non-negotiable on saba; the dish reads heavy and one-dimensional without it.

  The daikon oroshi is the under-discussed half of the dish. Raw daikon contains glucosinolates that, when the cell walls are ruptured by grating, are converted by the daikon''s own myrosinase enzyme into isothiocyanates (the same family of compounds responsible for the bite of horseradish, wasabi, and mustard). The isothiocyanates do two things on the palate: they cut perceived fat through a sensory mechanism that overlaps with how acid cuts fat (the perception of richness is reduced even though the actual fat on the palate is unchanged), and they activate TRPA1 receptors that produce the "sinus tingle" sensation. The grating direction matters; grating in a circular motion ruptures cells laterally and produces a fluffier, more snowy texture with more isothiocyanate release; grating straight up-and-down produces stringy oroshi with less bite. The Japanese term for the correct technique is en wo egakuyō ni, "as if drawing a circle." The 5-minute drain after grating is also functional; some isothiocyanate volatilizes during the drain, and a too-long drain (15+ minutes) produces a milder, sweeter oroshi appropriate for tempura but underpowered for saba. 5 minutes is the right window.

  The pan choice and pan management. A heavy carbon steel skillet preheated dry until smoking is the home-kitchen approximation of a Japanese izakaya teppan or charcoal grill; the high thermal mass holds temperature when the cold cured fillets hit, and the smooth seasoned surface releases the skin cleanly when the fillet is ready. A non-stick pan does not get hot enough (most non-stick coatings degrade above 260°C and the dish needs a 240°C+ pan surface), and the smooth coating produces a soft, evenly-browned skin rather than the crisp, irregularly-bronzed crackling the dish wants. Cast iron also works but takes longer to preheat and the heavier mass makes the flip awkward; carbon steel is the sweet spot.

  Sourcing the saba. Whole saba at H Mart, Mitsuwa, Nijiya, and most Japanese grocers is the best option; ask the fishmonger to fillet, debone, and skin-on (many will). Fresh whole saba should have clear shiny eyes (cloudy is past peak), bright iridescent silver-blue skin (dull silver is past peak), tight scales, no slime on the skin (a thin sheen is normal, gummy is not), and a clean ocean smell (any ammonia or strong fishy off-notes mean discard, do not cook). Frozen saba fillets at H Mart or Mitsuwa (vacuum-packed, often imported from Japan or Norway) are widely acceptable; the fillets are processed at peak freshness and frozen rapidly, often producing a fresher result than fresh fillets that have sat at the counter for a day. Atlantic mackerel from a US fishmonger (Whole Foods, local fishmongers) is interchangeable with saba for cooking purposes; the species are biologically distinct (Pacific saba is Scomber japonicus, Atlantic mackerel is Scomber scombrus) but the fat content, flesh structure, and behavior in this preparation are equivalent.

  The internal temperature window. Saba is best at 55 to 58°C / 130 to 135°F internal at the thickest part of the fillet, which corresponds to flesh that is opaque-but-glossy and just-flaking-with-pressure. Past 60°C the lipid fraction expresses water and the flesh goes dry-chalky; under 50°C the flesh reads gummy and the diner perceives the texture as raw. The 30-second flesh-side flip after the long skin-side sear is calibrated to bring the internal up into the window without overshooting. A thermometer in the thickest spot is the precise tool; an experienced cook can read doneness from the climb of the cooked-flesh band up the side of the fillet during the skin-side sear (two-thirds up by volume = correct).
