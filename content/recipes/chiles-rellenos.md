source:
  name: 'Central Mexican tradition (Puebla and Mexico City)'
  note: 'Chiles rellenos descends from Pueblan cooking, the same regional tradition that produced mole poblano and chiles en nogada. The egg-white batter technique is structurally Spanish (capeado, the rebozada technique brought by the colonial Spanish kitchens) but the stuffed-chile dish itself is indigenous Mexican. Cheese-stuffed is the everyday version; picadillo-stuffed (ground meat with raisins and almonds) is the special-occasion version; chiles en nogada (picadillo plus walnut sauce and pomegranate seeds) is the Independence Day version. This recipe is the everyday queso-stuffed version.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Roast and peel the poblanos BEFORE making the batter. The chiles need 15 minutes of steaming under a covered bowl to loosen the skin, then careful peeling. The batter has to be made and used immediately; whipped egg whites deflate within 20 minutes.'
  - 'The egg-white batter must be made and used in a single batch. Do not try to batter the chiles in two batches; the batter deflates between the first and second batch and the second batch will not puff. Fry all 8 chiles in 2 batches of 4 in the same continuous batter session.'

steps:
  - text: 'Roast the 800 g / 8 poblanos. Place directly on a gas burner over high heat (or under a hot broiler), turning with tongs every 60 seconds, until the skin is blackened and blistered all over (4 to 6 minutes total per chile on direct flame, 6 to 8 minutes under broiler). Transfer to a covered bowl or paper bag immediately; let steam 15 minutes (the structural step that loosens the skin).'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Peel and seed the chiles. Rub off the blackened skin with paper towels (do not rinse; water washes off the smoky flavor). Make a 5 cm slit lengthwise from just below the stem; carefully remove the seed cluster and the inner membranes through the slit, leaving the chile mostly intact with the stem attached. Reserve.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Build the caldillo. Combine the 400 g canned tomatoes, 100 g quartered onion, 10 g garlic, 1 g Mexican oregano, and 1 g ground cumin in a blender; puree until smooth (60 seconds). Heat the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a wide saucepan over medium-high heat; add the tomato puree (it will spit aggressively). Cook 3 to 4 minutes, stirring, until the puree has darkened and thickened slightly. Add the 600 ml / 2½ cups chicken stock and 4 g kosher salt. Simmer 15 minutes over medium-low, until the caldillo has reduced slightly and the flavors have integrated. Reserve covered, warm.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Stuff the chiles. Combine the 300 g shredded Oaxaca/mozzarella and 100 g crumbled queso fresco in a bowl. Stuff each peeled poblano with about 50 g of the cheese mix through the lengthwise slit; do not overfill (the slit should still close mostly). The cheese should bulge slightly but the chile should hold its shape.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Heat the frying oil. Pour the 500 ml / 2 cups neutral oil into a wide deep skillet to a depth of about 1.5 cm. Heat to 175°C / 350°F. While the oil heats, prepare the dredging station: dust the stuffed chiles lightly with the 60 g flour, shaking off excess (the flour coat is what allows the egg-white batter to cling).'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Whip the egg whites. Separate the 4 eggs (whites in a wide bowl, yolks in a small bowl, do not let any yolk into the whites or they will not whip). Add the 2 g salt to the whites. Whip with an electric mixer or whisk to stiff peaks (the whites should hold a peak when the whisk is lifted, with the peak just barely curling at the tip; about 3 to 4 minutes). Gently fold in the 4 egg yolks one at a time, using a silicone spatula and folding from the bottom up; do not overmix or the structure deflates. The batter should be uniform pale yellow with no streaks of white.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Batter and fry the first batch. Working quickly, dip the first 4 dredged chiles into the egg-white batter, coating completely (use 2 forks to lift and turn the chile in the batter; the batter should cling in a thick visible coat). Lay each battered chile gently into the 175°C oil, slit-side up. Fry 90 seconds per side, basting the exposed side with hot oil using a spoon, until both sides are deep golden and puffed. Transfer to a paper-towel-lined tray.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Batter and fry the second batch. Immediately batter and fry the remaining 4 chiles the same way; do not wait, the batter deflates within 5 minutes. Total fry time per chile is 3 minutes; total for 4 chiles cooked 2 at a time is about 6 minutes.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Serve. Ladle the warm caldillo into 4 shallow bowls (about 150 ml per bowl). Place 2 fried chiles in each bowl, slit-side up, partially submerged in the caldillo. The puffed batter shell will gradually soften from contact with the hot broth; this is structural to the dish, the diner eats the chiles at a stage where the shell is partially crisp and partially soaked. Serve immediately with warm corn tortillas and white rice; do not let the chiles sit in the broth for more than 5 minutes or the shell collapses entirely.'
    time_min: 3

    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  The egg-white batter is structural. Chiles rellenos are defined by the egg-white batter (capeado in Spanish), which produces a light puffed crisp shell similar to a soufflé. Beaten egg whites are folded with yolks at the end (in the inverse of the typical sequence) so the structure stays light. The result is dramatically different from a flour-and-egg tempura-style batter (which produces a dense crisp shell) or from a beer-batter (which produces a thick craggy shell); the egg-white batter is what makes chiles rellenos read as chiles rellenos and not as another fried-chile preparation. Cooks new to the technique often fail on the batter (too liquid, deflates immediately, will not cling); the key is stiff egg whites (peaks barely curling, not soft floppy peaks) and immediate use after folding in the yolks.

  The caldillo bath is structural. Chiles rellenos served on a plate dry (without the tomato caldillo) are a corrupted Tex-Mex version; the canonical Mexican presentation is the chiles partially submerged in a warm thin tomato broth. The caldillo softens the bottom of the batter while the top stays crisp, providing textural contrast in every bite. The bath is also the structural seasoning; the chile and the batter alone are mild, and the caldillo brings the tomato-acid and the savory backbone.

  Why poblano specifically. Poblano is structurally suited for stuffing because of its thick wall (holds up to the fry without collapsing) and its mild heat (does not overwhelm the cheese filling). Anaheim is the accessible substitute but is thinner-walled and slightly mishaped. Bell pepper does not work; the flavor is wrong (too sweet and grassy) and the wall is too thin. Hatch chile (when in season, August to September in the US Southwest) is an excellent substitute and is structurally similar to poblano.

  Cheese vs picadillo. The cheese-filled version (this recipe) is the everyday Mexican home preparation, simpler and quicker. The picadillo-filled version (see modifications) is the special-occasion preparation, with the additional complexity of a slow-cooked meat filling with raisins, almonds, and spices. Both are structurally chiles rellenos; the cheese version is the default unless otherwise specified.

  Storage. The fried chiles do not store well; the egg-white batter collapses within 30 minutes of frying. The caldillo can be made ahead and refrigerated 3 days; the roasted-and-peeled chiles can be made ahead and refrigerated 2 days, stuffed at the moment of cooking. Frying must be done at the moment of service; do not try to fry ahead and reheat.
