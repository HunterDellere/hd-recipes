source:
  note: 'The clarification is mechanically identical to ghee, but the aromatics are the entire point. Ginger, garlic, fenugreek, cardamom, cinnamon, clove infuse the fat as the water boils off and the milk solids brown; the spice load is lipophilic, the fat is a clean carrier, and the finished product holds the full aromatic profile of a wat base before any cooking has happened. A jar in the pantry shortens every Ethiopian dish by 20 minutes and is the difference between cooking on the right fat and approximating it.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Use unsalted butter only; salted butter concentrates the salt as the water cooks off and the finished niter kibbeh tastes harsh.'
  - 'Use a wide heavy pot (3-quart Dutch oven or saucier) for the clarification; a tall narrow pot does not let enough water steam off and the finished fat reads watery and short-keeping.'
  - 'Have a fine-mesh strainer, a coffee filter or double layer of cheesecloth, and a clean dry glass jar (500 ml or larger) ready before the cook starts; the strain happens at the end with the spices still hot.'

steps:
  - text: 'In a 3-quart heavy saucepan or saucier, melt the 450 g / 1 lb unsalted butter over medium-low heat without stirring, 4 to 5 minutes. The butter goes through three visible phases: solid yellow, melted yellow with floating white foam, and finally clear yellow with foam on top and faint milky cloud at the bottom. Pull the heat down to the lowest setting that maintains a faint shimmer of bubble at the surface.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the 60 g / ½ small onion roughly chopped, the 20 g / 4 to 5 crushed garlic cloves, the 25 g / 5 cm knob of sliced ginger, the 4 g / 1 tsp fenugreek seed, the 3 g / 6 cardamom pods crushed, the 1 g / 4 cloves, the 2 g / 1 short stick cinnamon, the 2 g / ½ tsp black peppercorns, and the 1 g / ½ tsp dried oregano (or koseret if available). Stir once with a wooden spoon to submerge the aromatics in the melted butter. The water in the aromatics will steam off over the next 30 minutes; expect mild sizzling at the start, fading as the moisture evaporates.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Maintain a bare simmer (the lowest setting where you see occasional small bubbles rising through the fat) for 30 to 40 minutes without stirring. Watch the bottom of the pan, not the top; the milk solids will gradually drop out of suspension and accumulate at the bottom, where they slowly toast from white to pale-gold to deep-amber. The fat itself should stay clear and deepen in color from yellow to gold to deep gold. The aromatics on top will brown gently and the smell will shift from buttery-spicy to deeply nutty-spicy as the solids approach amber.'
    technique: 'clarification'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Pull the pot from heat when the milk solids at the bottom are deep amber (the color of a roasted hazelnut, not yet of coffee) and the smell is fully nutty-spicy. The window is about 5 minutes wide; pulling 2 minutes early leaves the fat under-flavored, pulling 5 minutes late risks the solids burning past the brown threshold and the fat going bitter. Let the pot sit off heat for 5 minutes so the solids settle.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Place a fine-mesh strainer lined with a coffee filter (or two layers of cheesecloth) over a clean dry 500 ml glass jar. Carefully ladle the hot spiced butter through the strainer in batches; the filter catches both the aromatic solids and the toasted milk solids and yields a perfectly clear deep-gold fat. Do not press on the filter or stir the strained solids back in; the silt clouds the finished niter kibbeh and shortens its shelf life. Discard the solids (or scrape into rice or vegetables for a less-clean by-product). Let the jar cool uncovered for 1 hour, then cap tightly. The fat sets to a soft scoopable solid at room temperature.'
    time_min: 8

notes: |
  The yield is roughly 400 g (1¾ cups) of finished niter kibbeh from 450 g of butter; the loss is the water and the strained solids. Store the cooled jar at cool room temperature for 3 months, refrigerated for 6 months. The fat solidifies in the refrigerator; warm the jar at room temperature for 30 minutes before scooping if it has been chilled.

  Use it everywhere Ethiopian cooking calls for fat. The 30 to 45 minute onion sweat in doro wat or misir wat starts dry and only adds niter kibbeh after the onions have broken down; the spiced fat then carries the berbere bloom that follows. A spoonful melted into cooked greens, into the rice for a pilaf, onto steamed sweet potatoes, or into the pan for a quick sauté is also a legitimate non-Ethiopian use. The fat behaves like ghee with vastly more aromatic interest.

  Scaling: the recipe doubles or triples comfortably; use the largest heavy-bottomed pot you have so the surface area stays high relative to the volume and the water cooks off in roughly the same window. Halving works too but the 30-minute infusion timer is the same regardless of batch size.
