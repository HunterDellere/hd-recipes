steps:
  - text: 'Toast the 160 g / 1¾ cups rolled oats in a dry stainless skillet over medium heat, shaking often, until they smell nutty and the palest ones turn gold, 4 to 5 minutes. Tip onto a plate to stop the carryover and cool. Toasting drives Maillard and pyrolysis reactions in the bran and is the single largest flavor lever in the recipe; raw oats read flat and pasty by comparison.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Brown the 170 g / 1½ sticks unsalted butter in a light-colored saucepan over medium heat. It foams, quiets, then the milk solids drop and turn amber and smell of toasted hazelnut, about 5 minutes. Scrape everything, including the browned solids at the bottom, into a large heatproof bowl. Whisk in the 15 ml / 1 tbsp water off heat to replace the moisture that boiled off during browning; without it the dough bakes dry. Cool 5 minutes.'
    technique: 'brown-butter'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Whisk the 200 g / 1 cup packed light brown sugar and 55 g / ¼ cup granulated sugar into the warm butter for a full minute, then let the mixture sit 2 to 3 minutes. The residual warmth partly dissolves the sugar; dissolved sugar migrates to the surface during baking and sets into the glossy, crackled, chewy shell you want, where undissolved crystals bake in sandy and dull. The brown sugar dominates the blend on purpose: its molasses is hygroscopic and acidic, so it holds moisture for days and feeds the browning.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Whisk in the 50 g / 1 large egg, 18 g / 1 large yolk, and 8 g / 2 tsp vanilla bean paste. Beat hard for 30 to 45 seconds until the mixture turns glossy and slightly thickened. The extra yolk adds fat and emulsifiers (lecithin) without extra water, which is the lever that tips the cookie from cakey toward dense and chewy.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Fold in the 190 g / 1½ cups all-purpose flour, 5 g / 1 tsp baking soda, 4 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt, and 1.5 g / ½ tsp ground cinnamon with a spatula until the dry streaks nearly disappear. The soda is the only leavener here; brown sugar is acidic enough to fire it, and the alkalinity it leaves behind pushes extra browning and spread. Do not overwork the dough past combined or the gluten toughens.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Fold in the cooled toasted 160 g / 1¾ cups oats and the 170 g / 6 oz chopped dark chocolate until evenly distributed. Chopping a bar instead of using chips gives you a range of piece sizes, from dust that streaks the dough to shards that stay molten; chips are formulated with stabilizers to hold their shape and never pool.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Cover the bowl and rest the dough at least 30 minutes at room temperature, or up to 3 days in the refrigerator. This is not optional: the oats pull moisture from the dough and soften, the flour fully hydrates, and the firmer chilled fat spreads less in the oven. A rested dough bakes thick and chewy with a set edge; an unrested one spreads thin and greasy and the oats stay leathery.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 175 °C / 350 °F. Scoop the dough into 50 g / heaping 2-tbsp balls and space them 5 cm / 2 in apart on parchment-lined sheets. If the dough chilled hard, let the portioned balls sit 10 minutes so they spread evenly.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Bake 11 to 13 minutes, one sheet at a time on the middle rack, until the edges are set and deep gold but the centers still look soft and underbaked. Pull them early: residual heat finishes the centers on the sheet, and a fully baked-looking center means a dry cookie once cool.'
    time_min: 13
  - text: 'Finish each cookie with a few flakes of flaky salt (about 2 g / ½ tsp across the whole batch) while still hot, so it sticks. The salt lands in bright points against the sweet dough and sharpens both the chocolate and the toasted oat. Cool on the sheet 10 minutes to finish the chew before moving to a rack.'
    time_min: 10

notes: |
  Why brown the butter and toast the oats. Both are Maillard levers, and an oatmeal cookie lives or dies on toasted-grain and caramel depth rather than on any single showy ingredient. Browning the butter converts its milk-solid proteins and lactose into hundreds of roasty aroma compounds; toasting the oats does the same to the bran. Skip both and you have a pale, sweet, one-note cookie. The tradeoff on the oats is that toasting damages some starch and lowers their thickening power, but in a cookie you want flavor and chew, not the gel that matters in porridge, so the trade is all upside here.

  Why the egg-plus-yolk and the brown-sugar bias. Chew comes from moisture that stays bound and from a dense, well-emulsified protein-fat network rather than an aerated one. The extra yolk adds fat and lecithin without adding water; the brown-sugar-heavy blend brings hygroscopic molasses that holds that moisture for days; and melting the butter rather than creaming it keeps air out so the crumb packs tight. Together they push the cookie firmly toward dense and chewy and away from cakey.

  Why the rest is mandatory. Two things happen during it that nothing else can do. The oats hydrate from the surrounding dough and go from leathery to tender, and the flour fully hydrates so the raw-flour edge disappears and the gluten relaxes. Separately, the chilled fat resolidifies and spreads less in the oven, which is why a rested dough bakes thick with a set edge while the same dough baked warm spreads into a thin greasy disc. Overnight is better than 30 minutes; the flavor also deepens as enzymes break starch into simpler sugars that brown faster.
