source:
  name: 'Polish Sunday-dinner canon (19th century, descended from Austrian Wiener Schnitzel via the Austro-Hungarian Empire)'
  note: 'Kotlet schabowy is the structural Polish Sunday-dinner pork cutlet, descended from the Viennese Wiener Schnitzel tradition (which uses veal) and adapted to pork (the more accessible Polish protein). The defining elements are: boneless pork loin (schab in Polish, the structural cut) pounded thin enough that the cutlet covers the entire dinner plate; the classic three-step breading (flour, egg, breadcrumbs); shallow-frying in a butter-and-oil mix at moderate heat (the butter for flavor, the oil for raised smoke point); and the structural Polish accompaniments of mashed potato, dill pickle, and a small mound of mizeria (sour cream cucumber salad) or surówka (cabbage slaw).'

before_you_start:
  - 'The pork loin must be pounded thin (5 to 7 mm) for the cutlet to read structurally Polish. A thicker cutlet is something else (a pork chop, not a kotlet). Pound between parchment with the smooth side of a meat mallet (the spiked side tears the meat). Without a mallet, use the flat bottom of a heavy saucepan.'
  - 'The three-step breading is structural. Each cutlet goes through flour first (dries the surface and helps the egg adhere), then egg (the structural binder), then breadcrumbs (the structural crispy coating). Skipping the flour step produces a breading that slides off the cutlet during the fry.'
  - 'The frying fat must be butter plus oil at a 1:1 ratio. Pure butter burns at moderate heat; pure oil produces a less flavorful cutlet. The mix gives the structural butter aroma with a higher smoke point.'

steps:
  - text: 'Pound the pork. Lay each 600 g portion of pork loin between two sheets of parchment. Pound gently with the smooth side of a meat mallet, starting from the center and working outward, until each cutlet is 5 to 7 mm thick and covers a dinner plate. Season both sides with the 6 g / 1½ tsp salt and 2 g / 1 tsp pepper.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Set up the breading station. Place 3 wide shallow dishes in a row: the 90 g flour in the first; the 2 beaten eggs in the second; the 120 g breadcrumbs in the third. Have a clean plate at the end of the line for the breaded cutlets.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Bread each cutlet. Working with one cutlet at a time: dredge in flour, shaking off excess (a thin even coat); dip in beaten egg, letting excess drip off (the egg coats both sides fully); press into breadcrumbs, packing firmly with the heel of your hand and flipping to coat both sides (the breadcrumb coat should be uniform and cover every surface). Place on the clean plate. Repeat for all 4 cutlets.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Heat the 60 g / 4 tbsp butter and 60 ml / ¼ cup neutral oil in a wide deep skillet (30 cm / 12 inch ideal) over medium heat. The fat should be about 5 mm deep and shimmering hot but not smoking; test with a small breadcrumb, which should sizzle vigorously on contact and turn gold in 30 seconds.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add 2 breaded cutlets to the hot fat (do not crowd; the cutlets need full contact with the fat for even browning, and crowding drops the fat temperature). Fry 3 to 4 minutes per side, flipping once, until the breading is deep gold and the cutlet feels firm when pressed in the center. The pork should be cooked through to 63 °C / 145 °F internal (or visually, no pink in the center when sliced).'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Transfer to a paper-towel-lined plate to drain briefly. Repeat with the remaining 2 cutlets (add another 30 g butter and 30 ml oil to the pan if needed). Hold the finished cutlets in a 100 °C / 200 °F oven on a wire rack to keep warm and crispy while the second batch cooks.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Serve one cutlet per plate with 2 lemon wedges. Sprinkle with a small pinch of parsley if using. Squeeze lemon over the cutlet at the table; the acid cuts the rich fried butter coating and is the structural finishing element. Serve with mashed potato (or boiled new potatoes with butter and dill), a small mound of mizeria (sour cream cucumber salad) or surówka z białej kapusty (white cabbage slaw), and a dill pickle.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why pound thin. The structural Polish kotlet schabowy covers the entire dinner plate. The thinness (5 to 7 mm) lets the cutlet cook through quickly without overcooking the outside; the large surface area gives the breading-to-meat ratio that defines schabowy. A thicker cutlet (10 mm+) is structurally a pork chop, not a schabowy.

  Why three-step breading. The flour-egg-breadcrumb sequence is the structural breading method for any fried cutlet, and the steps cannot be combined or skipped. The flour dries the meat surface and provides protein binding sites; the egg adheres to the flour layer; the breadcrumb adheres to the egg layer. Single-dip or double-dip breading methods produce breadcrumbs that slide off during the fry; the three-step is structural.

  Why butter and oil at 1:1. Pure butter burns at moderate heat (above 175 °C / 350 °F the milk solids char and the butter smokes); pure oil produces a less flavorful cutlet without the dairy aroma. The butter-oil mix achieves the structural butter flavor at the higher smoke point that fried cutlets need. This is the universal European fried-cutlet rule (used in Wiener Schnitzel, French saltimbocca, German schnitzel).

  Pairings. Canonical with mashed potato (with butter and dill) or boiled new potatoes (with butter and parsley); mizeria (Polish sour cream cucumber salad) or surówka (Polish cabbage slaw); and a dill pickle on the side. Lemon wedge always. A glass of cold Polish beer (Tyskie, Żywiec) or vodka.

  Storage. Best eaten immediately; the breading softens within 20 minutes of frying. Leftovers keep 2 days refrigerated; reheat at 200 °C / 400 °F on a wire rack for 8 minutes to re-crisp (microwave produces soggy breading). Breaded uncooked cutlets can be frozen flat on a sheet pan, then bagged; fry from frozen, adding 2 minutes per side to the cook time.
