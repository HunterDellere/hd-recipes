source:
  name: 'British chip-shop canon (mid-19th century to present)'
  note: 'Fish and chips emerged in Britain in the mid-19th century as the marriage of two earlier traditions: Sephardic Jewish fried fish in batter (brought to England in the 17th century) and Belgian / French fried potato chips. The two were sold together as a complete meal by the 1860s and became the structural British working-class dinner of the industrial era. The defining elements are: a thick-cut "floury" potato (Maris Piper in the UK, Russet in the U.S.); a beer-and-flour batter that produces a lacy puffy crust through the CO2 of the beer and the rapid steam-expansion of the cold batter in hot oil; cod or haddock, the structural British white fish; malt vinegar (not white) and large-flake sea salt at the table; mushy peas as the canonical green side.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The chips require a 2-stage cook: blanch at 130 °C / 265 °F to gelatinize the starch and cook the interior, then drain and rest for at least 10 minutes (or up to 4 hours), then fry at 190 °C / 375 °F to crisp the exterior. The rest between fries is structural; the chips dry out and the starch retrogrades, producing the shattering crust. Skipping the rest produces soggy chips.'
  - 'The beer batter must be made cold (use ice-cold beer and chilled flour) and used immediately. The temperature differential between the cold batter and the hot oil (190 °C) is what produces the puffy lacy crust; a warm batter produces a dense doughy coating.'
  - 'The fish and the chips share the same oil but cook at different stages: blanch the chips first, rest them, then fry the fish to hot, hold the fish warm in a low oven, then fry the chips to crisp at the end. Reverse this order and the fish gets cold by the time the chips are ready.'

steps:
  - text: 'Cut the 1000 g peeled potatoes into 1.5 cm thick batons. Soak in cold water 15 minutes to wash off surface starch; drain and pat thoroughly dry on a kitchen towel (any surface water causes dangerous oil splatter when frying).'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Heat the 2500 ml oil in a heavy-bottomed Dutch oven or deep fryer to 130 °C / 265 °F. The oil should be about 8 cm deep. Fry the chips in 2 batches (do not crowd) for 6 to 7 minutes per batch; the chips will look pale, soft, and limp at the end of this stage. They are par-cooked, not done. Drain on paper towels in a single layer; do not stack. Rest at least 10 minutes (up to 4 hours, refrigerated).'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Crank the oil to 190 °C / 375 °F. Preheat the oven to 100 °C / 200 °F with a sheet pan inside (for holding the fried fish warm while the chips finish).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Make the beer batter. In a chilled bowl, whisk the 150 g all-purpose flour, 30 g rice flour, 3 g baking powder, and 4 g salt. Pour in the 280 ml ice-cold beer all at once; whisk gently just until the batter comes together with a few lumps still visible (over-whisking develops gluten and produces a tough batter). The consistency should be like single cream or thin pancake batter, just thick enough to coat the back of a spoon. Use immediately; do not let it sit.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pat the 4 cod portions thoroughly dry; season both sides with the 4 g salt. Dredge each portion in the 30 g dredging flour, shaking off excess; the dredge gives the batter something to grab.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Fry the fish. Working with 2 portions at a time, dip each dredged fillet into the cold batter to fully coat; lift; let the excess drip off for 2 seconds. Lower into the 190 °C oil tail-end first, swooshing back and forth gently for the first 5 seconds (this prevents the fish from sticking to the bottom of the pot and gives the batter a chance to puff into its lacy structure). Fry 5 to 6 minutes total, turning once at the 3-minute mark, until the batter is deep gold, crispy, and audibly crunchy when tapped with the tongs. Transfer to the warm oven on the sheet pan. Repeat with the remaining 2 portions.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Crank the oil back to 190 °C / 375 °F (it will have dropped during the fish fry). Fry the rested par-cooked chips in 2 batches, 3 to 4 minutes per batch, until deep gold with shattering crispy exteriors. Drain on paper towels.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Immediately season the hot chips with the 5 g flaky sea salt, sprinkled from height. Plate each portion: 1 fish fillet, a generous pile of chips, a small dish of malt vinegar for dipping or sprinkling, and (canonically) a spoonful of mushy peas or tartar sauce on the side. Serve immediately; the fish softens within 10 minutes of frying.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why double-fry chips. The first fry at 130 °C cooks the inside of the potato (gelatinizes the starch, softens the cell walls) without browning the outside. The rest period dehydrates the surface and lets the gelatinized starch retrograde (a chemistry change that makes the surface more brittle when next heated). The second fry at 190 °C dehydrates the surface further and crisps it into the shattering chip crust. Single-frying produces either crispy outside / raw inside (if hot) or fully cooked but limp (if low); only the double-fry produces both textures.

  Why cold batter on hot fish. The temperature differential between cold batter (0 to 4 °C) and hot oil (190 °C) is the structural mechanism for the puffy lacy beer-batter crust. When the cold wet batter hits the hot oil, the water in the batter flashes to steam in microseconds, and the surrounding gluten structure (developed only minimally during the gentle whisk) puffs into a lacy crispy network. A warm batter produces a dense doughy crust without lace. Keep the beer in the freezer until the moment of mixing; chill the bowl in advance.

  The rice-flour move. Modern British chip shops add 15 to 20 percent rice flour to the all-purpose flour in the batter; rice flour does not develop gluten (it has no glutenin or gliadin proteins), so the resulting crust is lighter and crispier than an all-wheat-flour batter. This is a 20-year-old chip-shop secret that has become standard.

  Pairings. Canonical with mushy peas (marrowfat peas soaked overnight, simmered with a pinch of bicarb, crushed to a coarse puree, seasoned with salt and a knob of butter). Tartar sauce is the modern restaurant addition; ketchup is the American addition. Curry sauce is the chippy-shop favorite in the north of England. A pint of bitter or a strong English breakfast tea on the side.

  Storage. Fish and chips do not store. The batter softens, the chips go limp, the fish dries out. Plan to eat within 15 minutes of frying. If reheating leftovers is unavoidable, a hot oven (220 °C / 425 °F) for 8 minutes on a wire rack helps somewhat but never restores the original texture.
