steps:
  - text: 'Red rubies. Toss the diced 200 g / 8 mm cubes of water chestnut with the 30 ml / 2 tbsp dragon fruit or beet juice in a bowl. Let stand 5 minutes, the surface dyes pink but the white center remains for visual contrast. Drain off any excess liquid. Dredge the pink-stained pieces in the 40 g / ⅓ cup tapioca starch, tossing until each piece is fully coated and the starch sticks to the damp surface. Shake off loose excess in a sieve. The starch coating is what gives the ruby effect: clear-pink jelly skin over an opaque white core when cooked.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Bring the 1000 ml / 4 cups water to a rolling boil. Drop the coated water chestnuts in. They sink, then rise as the tapioca coating gelatinizes, about 3 to 4 minutes after the water returns to a boil. Boil 1 minute past surfacing. Drain immediately into a sieve and rinse under cold running water for 15 seconds to halt cooking and wash off surface starch. Set aside in cold water until use; they hold 4 hours this way.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Basil seeds. In a small glass, stir the 15 g / 1 tbsp basil seeds into the 120 ml / ½ cup cold water. Let stand 15 minutes; the seeds hydrate into translucent gel beads with a black dot inside (the seed). Do not heat, basil seeds bloom in cold water and turn mucilaginous in hot. If using chia, identical method.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Coconut sauce. In a measuring jug whisk together the 480 ml coconut milk, the 80 g / ¼ cup condensed coconut milk, the 1 g pinch salt, and the 60 ml / ¼ cup reserved fruit syrup. Taste, should read clearly sweet but not cloying. Adjust with more condensed milk if flat, more salt if too sweet. Refrigerate.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Assembly. In each of 4 tall glasses or shallow bowls, layer 3 tbsp of the water chestnut rubies, then 2 tbsp each of the jackfruit, longan, and lychee. Spoon 2 tbsp of the hydrated basil seeds over the fruit. Add a generous handful of crushed ice (60 to 80 g per glass).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Pour 150 ml / ⅔ cup of the chilled coconut sauce over each glass. Serve immediately with a long spoon. The eater stirs the dessert together; the layering is presentation, not preservation.'

notes: |
  The dish is built on textural contrast, not flavor complexity. The coconut sauce is the unifier; the water chestnut rubies are the chewy-crunchy backbone; the basil seeds are the popping bead; the fruits are the soft sweet centers. Skip any one and the balance flattens.

  Canned tropical fruits in this dish are the correct ingredient, not a compromise. Fresh jackfruit and lychee in the West are inconsistent and often underripe; the canned syrups (which are then re-used in the sauce) carry a tropical-fruit-cocktail aroma that is structurally part of chè Thái. This is the Southeast Asian dessert that pairs well with shelf-stable ingredients; lean into the convenience.

  Adding the ice last and pouring the cold sauce over is non-negotiable. Add sauce before ice and the ice dilutes the sauce as it melts; add ice after pouring and the temperature drops gradually as it should, with the sauce thickening to a slush on the spoon by the bottom of the glass.
