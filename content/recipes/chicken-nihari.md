source:
  name: 'Pakistani Mughlai tradition (Karachi-Lahore-Delhi axis)'
  note: 'Nihari (نہاری, from Arabic nahār meaning "morning") is the canonical breakfast stew of Pakistani Muslim cooking, traditionally cooked overnight with beef shank and bone marrow and served at dawn after Fajr prayer with naan. The chicken adaptation became standard in the late 20th century as cooks looked for a same-day version that preserved the flavor architecture without the 12-hour simmer.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Decide your partner starch up front: naan, basmati rice, or roti. If basmati, start it as a PARALLEL TRACK in the last 30 minutes of the braise (20 minute soak, 12 minute simmer, 10 minute covered rest). If naan or roti, warm them on a dry skillet in the last 5 minutes; nihari with no starch to mop the gravy is a sauce in a bowl.'
  - 'This is a 130 minute cook with about 50 minutes of active time, the rest is a covered 90 minute braise. Plan the start so dinner lands when you want it; this is a Sunday-afternoon dish, not a weeknight.'
  - 'Bloom the whole spices in hot ghee before the masala paste hits the pan. Adding ground spices to cold ghee or skipping the whole-spice tadka collapses the dish to one-dimensional curry powder flavor; the spice-in-fat infusion is the structural step that distinguishes nihari from a generic chicken curry.'
  - 'Crisp the fried onions in advance, up to 24 hours ahead. They are a structural finish, not garnish; the textural contrast and the caramelized depth carry the dish. Hot-from-the-pan crisped onions added at the end overcook in residual heat; cold crisped onions stay crunchy.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the masala paste. In a small blender or mortar, blend the 30 g rough-chopped ginger, 30 g garlic, and 60 ml water into a smooth paste. Set aside. The ginger-garlic paste is structural across South Asian cooking; pre-blending is cleaner than working from whole pieces during the cook.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Toast and bloom the whole spices. Heat the 75 g ghee in a heavy 4 to 5 quart Dutch oven or wide pot over medium heat until shimmering. Add the 4 cracked cardamom pods, 4 cloves, 2 bay leaves, 5 g cinnamon stick piece, 5 g / 2 tsp cumin seeds, and 3 g cracked black peppercorns. Bloom 30 to 45 seconds; the cumin will sizzle and darken slightly, the cinnamon and cardamom will release a pronounced warm-aromatic smell, and the kitchen will fill with the toasted-spice aroma that defines South Asian cooking. Pull immediately when the cumin is fragrant; do not let the spices blacken.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Brown the onion. Add the 250 g sliced onion to the spiced ghee. Stir-fry over medium heat for 12 to 15 minutes, stirring every 30 to 60 seconds, until the onion has gone from white to gold to deep mahogany-brown. The onion will progress through stages: translucent in the first 4 minutes, pale gold at 8 minutes, deep gold at 11 minutes, mahogany at 14 minutes. Pull when the slices are uniformly deep brown but not burned. Brown onions are the structural base of nihari color and depth; pale onions produce a pale-and-flat dish.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Bhuna the masala. Reduce heat to medium-low. Add the ginger-garlic paste from step 1; the moisture will hit the hot ghee and steam dramatically. Stir-fry 60 seconds; the rawness of the paste cooks off and the smell goes from sharp to warm. Add the 12 g ground coriander, 8 g Kashmiri chili powder, 2 g cayenne, 2 g turmeric, and 4 g ground fennel. Stir-fry 60 to 90 seconds, splashing with 1 to 2 tbsp water if the spices threaten to scorch (the spices need brief contact with hot ghee to bloom but cannot sit in dry ghee for more than a minute without burning). The kitchen smells deeply of spice; the ghee should pool slightly red-orange at the edges of the pan, the visual marker that the bhuna has worked.'
    technique: 'red-oil-rendering'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Sear the chicken. Add the 1200 g chicken pieces to the masala. Toss for 3 to 4 minutes; the chicken does not need to brown deeply (it will simmer for over an hour), but the surface should turn from raw-pink to opaque-tan and pick up the masala color. Sprinkle with 12 g / 1 tbsp salt and toss.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add liquid and braise. Pour in the 1200 ml / 5 cups water (or chicken stock for richer gravy). Stir to combine. Bring to a simmer over medium-high heat, then reduce to medium-low (the surface should tremble, not boil aggressively). Cover with a tight lid. Simmer 75 to 90 minutes; the chicken will cook through within the first 25 minutes but the longer simmer extracts gelatin from the bones and integrates the spices into the gravy. Stir every 20 minutes to prevent the bottom from scorching.'
    time_min: 80
  - text: 'Toast the atta flour. While the nihari simmers, heat a small dry skillet over medium heat. Add the 30 g atta flour. Toast 4 to 5 minutes, stirring constantly, until the flour goes from pale-cream to medium-tan and smells distinctly of toasted wheat. The toasting deactivates the raw-flour starchiness and adds a nutty depth that defines authentic nihari (skipping this step produces a gummy, raw-tasting gravy). Pull from heat; let cool 2 minutes.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Slurry and thicken. After the chicken has braised 75 minutes, whisk the toasted atta with the 120 ml / ½ cup cold water in a small bowl until smooth (no lumps). Stream the slurry into the simmering nihari while stirring continuously. Continue simmering 10 to 15 minutes; the gravy will thicken to a glossy coat-the-spoon consistency that clings to the chicken pieces. Taste; adjust salt. The dish is done when the chicken is tender to a fork-poke and the gravy is thick enough that a spoon dragged through the bottom of the pot leaves a brief trail.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Plate and garnish. Transfer the nihari to a wide serving bowl. Scatter the 20 g julienned ginger, 60 g fried onions, 20 g chopped cilantro, and 10 g sliced green chilies over the surface. Sprinkle with 2 g / 1 tsp fresh garam masala. Serve immediately with naan, roti, or basmati rice and lemon wedges on the side. Each diner squeezes lemon over their portion at the table; the acid is structural to the eating experience and lifts the deep braise into balance.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  The seven-flavor target. Pakistani nihari is built on a precise balance of seven flavors: deep umami from the slow-braised meat and bones, layered warmth from the toasted whole spices, structural body from the ground coriander, color and gentle heat from the Kashmiri chili, the distinctive licorice-anise note from the ground fennel (without which the dish reads as a generic curry rather than as nihari), the spritely lift from the ginger garnish and lemon at the table, and the textural contrast of the crisp fried onions against the soft braised meat. All seven are non-negotiable; reducing the fennel or skipping the lemon turns the dish into a different (lesser) curry.

  Why bone-in chicken is structural. The bones release gelatin into the gravy over the 90-minute simmer, transforming the consistency from "watery soup" to "spoon-coating gravy." Boneless chicken cooked the same way produces a thinner gravy that has to be over-thickened with atta, throwing off the spice-to-thickener balance. If only boneless chicken is available, supplement with 200 g chicken bones (carcass, wings) tied in cheesecloth and simmered with the dish.

  The atta thickening. The toasted-flour slurry is what distinguishes nihari from other braises. Cooks who skip the atta toasting end up with raw-flour-tasting gravy (the starches need heat to cook out their raw character); cooks who skip the slurry-into-cold-water step (whisking the toasted atta with cold water before adding to hot pot) end up with lumps. The technique is conservative and safe: toast the dry atta until it smells nutty, slurry with cold water until smooth, stream into the simmering pot while stirring. Done correctly, the gravy thickens to a glossy clinging consistency; done wrong, it goes lumpy or pasty.

  Beef vs chicken vs mutton, the historical context. The original Pakistani nihari was beef nihari, cooked in tandoor-oven pots overnight at the dawn-prayer hour, served with naan as a Mughlai breakfast tradition. Chicken nihari emerged in the late 20th century as a same-day adaptation; mutton nihari is the special-occasion version. All three are valid; the chicken version is the entry point for cooks trying nihari at home for the first time, the beef version is the destination once the technique is comfortable, and the mutton version is the celebration-level dish. The recipe above is calibrated for chicken; the modifications give the path to beef and mutton.

  Eating mechanics. Nihari is a meal-on-its-own when served with naan or roti, the bread scoops up the gravy and chicken, lemon is squeezed at every bite, and the green chili and ginger garnish are eaten as crunchy aromatic counterpoint. With basmati rice it becomes a different dish (more curry-and-rice, less stew-and-bread), still excellent. The dish reheats beautifully and many cooks consider day-two nihari as good as day-one or better; the spices integrate further overnight. Refrigerate up to 4 days; freezes 2 to 3 months.
