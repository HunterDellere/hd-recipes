  name: 'Spanish coastal tradition (Andalusian and Levantine; the seafood adaptation of the Valencian rice canon)'
  note: 'Paella de mariscos is the canonical seafood version of paella, cooked along the Spanish coast from Catalonia south to Andalusia. Structurally distinct from paella Valenciana (which uses chicken and rabbit in chicken stock with green beans and butter beans): the seafood paella uses fish fumet, mixed shell-on seafood, and skips the rosemary. The 2012 Valencian D.O. paella protection initiative explicitly distinguishes paella Valenciana from paella de mariscos as separate canonical dishes, not as a corruption of one or the other. Both are correct; this is the seafood expression.'

before_you_start:
  - 'A proper paella pan (paellera) is structural: the wide flat pan distributes heat evenly across the rice in a 1.5 cm shallow layer. A 38 cm / 15 in pan serves 6. A Dutch oven or deep skillet does not work; the rice stews instead of cooking to socarrat.'
  - 'Make the fish stock ahead. The dish uses 1500 ml of stock; commercial fish stock is acceptable but homemade fumet (shrimp shells, fish bones, white wine, water; 30 minute simmer) is dramatically better and is what defines a real paella de mariscos. The stock must be at a hard simmer when it hits the pan.'
  - 'The clams and mussels need to be purged. Submerge them in cold salted water (35 g salt per liter) for 30 minutes before cooking; they expel sand from the shells. Discard any that stay open after a tap on the counter or any that are visibly cracked.'

steps:
  - text: 'Bring the 1500 ml / 6¼ cups fish stock to a hard simmer in a saucepan; add the 0.5 g / ½ tsp saffron threads. Hold at a hard simmer for the entire cook. The saffron blooms continuously in the hot stock and turns it deep orange.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Sear the squid. Heat 20 g / 1½ tbsp of the olive oil in the 38 cm paella pan over medium-high heat. Add the 300 g / 10 oz squid rings and tentacles; sear 60 to 90 seconds total, tossing once, until the surfaces are pearly-opaque but the squid is still tender. Remove to a plate; reserve. Do not overcook here, the squid will finish cooking later. Overcooked squid is rubbery and ruins the dish.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Build the sofrito. Add the remaining 40 g / 3 tbsp olive oil to the pan. Reduce heat to medium; add the 150 g / 1 small diced onion. Sweat 5 to 6 minutes until translucent and just-golden. Add the 15 g / 4 minced garlic cloves; cook 60 seconds until fragrant but not browned.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Reduce the tomato. Push the onion and garlic to the edges; add the 250 g grated tomato pulp directly into the cleared center. Cook 4 to 5 minutes, stirring the tomato actively, until the water has evaporated and the tomato has reduced to a thick paste with visible olive oil pooling around it. The tomato should be dark red, not bright red, and quite dry; this is the canonical sofrito reduction point.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Bloom the pimentón. Pull the pan off heat briefly. Add the 5 g / 2 tsp sweet pimentón directly into the reduced sofrito; stir quickly into the oil for about 10 seconds. The paprika blooms in residual heat without burning. Burnt paprika is bitter and stains the rice an off-red color; if the pan is too hot, the only fix is to start the sofrito over.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Deglaze with sherry. Return to medium heat; pour in the 60 ml / ¼ cup Fino sherry. It will steam aggressively. Stir; cook 60 to 90 seconds until the alcohol has evaporated and the wine has reduced into the sofrito (the structural Andalusian step that distinguishes coastal paella from Valenciana).'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour in 1200 ml / 5 cups of the hot saffron stock (reserve 300 ml for adjusting). Bring to a hard boil over medium-high heat. Add the 6 g / 1 tsp fine sea salt.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add the rice. When the liquid is at a hard boil, sprinkle the 450 g / 2¼ cups bomba rice evenly across the entire surface of the pan in a single shallow layer. Use a wooden spoon to gently distribute the grains so the rice surface is uniform but DO NOT STIR after this point. The rice must stay in a single shallow layer (about 1.5 cm deep) without being moved; the no-stir rule is what allows the socarrat to form on the bottom of the pan.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Cook 10 minutes over medium-high heat at a steady vigorous boil; the liquid should bubble actively through the surface of the rice. Watch the heat distribution; if any area is bubbling more aggressively than others, rotate the pan a quarter turn every 2 minutes. Do not stir.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Arrange the seafood. After 10 minutes of rice cook, reduce heat to medium-low. Arrange the 400 g clams and 400 g mussels around the perimeter of the pan, hinge-side down so they open facing upward as they steam. Arrange the 500 g shrimp in a sunburst pattern across the surface of the rice. Pour any reserved squid juices back into the pan. Cook another 6 to 8 minutes; the clams and mussels will steam open from the rising vapor, the shrimp will turn pink and curl, the rice will absorb the remaining liquid.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Add the seared squid back to the surface in the last 2 minutes of cooking. By the 18-minute total rice cook mark, the surface should be mostly dry with the rice just visible above a thin film of bubbling liquid. The clams and mussels should be fully open (discard any that stay closed; they were dead before cooking).'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Develop the socarrat. Increase heat to medium-high for the last 90 seconds. Listen for a faint crackling sound (the rice on the bottom of the pan toasting); smell for a faint toasted-rice aroma. Test by inserting a thin spoon at one edge and lifting a tiny portion of rice; the bottom should show a thin golden-brown crust. Pull off heat the moment the socarrat is developed.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Cover the pan loosely with a kitchen towel or foil; rest off heat 8 minutes. Scatter the 15 g chopped parsley across the surface. Serve directly from the pan to the table; the canonical Spanish service is the pan placed in the center on a trivet, the 6 lemon wedges passed separately, diners eat from their assigned section of the pan.'
    time_min: 8

notes: |
  Paella Valenciana vs paella de mariscos, the structural distinction. Valenciana is the canonical Valencian rice with chicken, rabbit, garrofó butter bean, ferraura green beans, rosemary, and chicken stock; it is land-based and rural. Mariscos is the coastal expression with shrimp, mussels, clams, squid, fish stock, sometimes a small amount of onion in the sofrito (forbidden in strict Valenciana), no rosemary, and a splash of sherry for depth. Both are correct paella; they are different dishes within the same rice tradition. Spanish purists insist on the distinction; international cooks frequently conflate the two. This recipe is the seafood expression; the recipe for paella Valenciana is the land expression.

  Why head-on shrimp. The shrimp heads contain the hepatopancreas (the digestive gland), which releases a deeply savory orange-amber oil into the rice during the cook. Head-on shrimp produce paella that reads dramatically more marine and complex than headless shrimp; the heads are also delicious to eat (suck the head, peel the body, eat both). Headless shrimp are acceptable as a convenience substitute; the dish reads slightly flatter.

  The no-stir rule is the same as Valenciana. Stirring breaks the rice grains, releases excess starch into the broth (producing a sticky risotto-like texture instead of grain-separated paella), and disrupts the bottom layer that needs to remain in contact with the pan to develop the socarrat. The rice goes in, gets distributed once with a wooden spoon for even thickness, and is not touched again for 18 to 20 minutes.

  The seafood timing window. The clams and mussels need 6 to 8 minutes of steam to open; the shrimp need 4 to 5 minutes to cook through; the squid needs only the brief initial sear and a 90-second reheat at the end. Adding all the seafood at the start overcooks the shrimp and squid to rubber; adding it too late means the rice is already done and the seafood is undercooked. The arrangement at the 10-minute rice mark hits the timing for all four cleanly.

  Storage. Paella is a fresh-served dish and degrades within 90 minutes of cooking (the rice continues to absorb residual moisture). It can be eaten cold the next day as a room-temperature seafood-rice salad; the Spanish leftover preparation. Do not microwave-reheat, the rice turns gluey and the seafood toughens. Keeps 2 days refrigerated. Does not freeze.
