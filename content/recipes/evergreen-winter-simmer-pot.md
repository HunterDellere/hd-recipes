steps:
  - text: 'Verify your evergreen source. Pine (especially white pine, Pinus strobus), fir (Abies), and spruce (Picea) needles are non-toxic and aromatic; yew (Taxus) is toxic and must never go in a kitchen pot. If the tree came from a commercial Christmas tree lot, the boughs were likely sprayed with anti-desiccant chemicals, these are not food-grade but are acceptable for non-edible simmer pots since you will not consume the liquid. If you are uncertain about the species, do not use it. Cedar (Thuja, eastern arborvitae) is also non-toxic and gives a distinctive sharp profile.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'In a 1.5 to 2 L stainless or enameled saucepan, combine the 30 g pine sprigs, 15 g rosemary, 4 crushed bay leaves, 10 g fresh thyme, the grapefruit peel, the lemon peel, the 15 crushed juniper berries, and the 10 black peppercorns.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 1500 ml / 6 cups water, everything should be submerged with 5 cm / 2 inches headspace. Bring to a bare simmer over medium heat, then drop to the lowest setting. Hold at the lazy simmer (occasional bubbles, never rolling) for 8 to 12 hours.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Check the water level every 2 hours; top up with hot water from a kettle when below the halfway point. The pot smells strongest in the first 3 hours as the pine and rosemary terpenes hit the air at high volatility; after that it settles into a softer cedar-citrus register that lasts through the evening.'
    time_min: 480
  - text: 'Compost the solids and rinse the pot when done. The pine resin can leave a sticky film on the pot interior, wash with hot soapy water and a stiff brush while the pot is still warm. Cold pine resin is much harder to remove.'

notes: |
  Pine terpenes (alpha-pinene, beta-pinene, limonene) are the heart of this pot. They are highly volatile and dissipate quickly when heated; that is why fresh evergreen is dramatically more aromatic than dried boughs from last year. Cut the pine sprigs within an hour of putting them in the pot for the strongest hit.

  Juniper berries are not edible in quantity (small amounts in gin are fine; concentrated they can stress the kidneys) but they are entirely safe for a simmer pot, the volatile oils released during simmering are negligible in dose and contribute a distinctive resinous-piney note that bridges the pine and the citrus.

  The aromatherapy literature credits pine and fir essential oils with bronchodilator and decongestant effects. Whether or not those effects translate to a simmer pot, the perceived freshness of pine-citrus is well documented, people consistently rate rooms that smell like evergreen as feeling cleaner and cooler. Useful in stuffy winter houses with closed windows.
