before_you_start:
  - 'Get the grill genuinely hot before anything else. Sardines want fierce direct heat (a full chimney of coals burned to white ash, or a gas grill on high with the lid down for 12 to 15 minutes) so the skin chars and the fat renders before the thin flesh overcooks. A medium grill steams the fish to mush and the skin sticks. Start the fire, then prep.'
  - 'Buy the sardines as fresh as the counter allows and have them scaled and gutted, either by the fishmonger or by you (it takes two minutes). Sardines are an oily fish that goes off fast; bright clear eyes, red gills, and firm flesh with no sour ammonia smell are the freshness cues. Cook them the day you buy them.'
  - 'The salmoriglio is whisked warm and used within minutes; it slackens as it sits. Build it while the sardines rest off the grill, not in advance. Have the lemon juiced, the garlic grated, and the oregano crumbled before the fish goes on, because once it does you have eight minutes of constant attention.'
  - 'Oil the grill grate hard right before the fish goes on. Sardine skin sticks to a cold or dirty grate and tears, which loses the crisp skin that is the point. Scrub the hot grate, then wipe it with an oil-soaked wad of paper towel held in tongs until it glistens.'

steps:
  - text: 'Light the fire and get it ripping. Burn a full chimney of charcoal to white ash and spread it for a hot direct zone, or set a gas grill to high and close the lid for 12 to 15 minutes. The grate must be hot enough that a drop of oil flicked on it smokes instantly; sardines need that intensity to char the skin before the thin flesh overcooks. While it heats, do all the salmoriglio prep so the sauce is one whisk away.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Dry and salt the sardines. Pat the 600 g / 6 to 8 sardines bone-dry inside and out with paper towels; surface water is the enemy of crisp skin and the cause of sticking. Salt them all over with the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt, working a little into the body cavity, then rub the 14 g / 1 tbsp olive oil over the skin in a thin film. The oil conducts heat into the skin and promotes Maillard browning; it is not a sauce, so keep it sparse. Let the salted fish sit 5 minutes while the grill finishes heating; the brief cure firms the flesh and draws a tacky surface that browns better.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Oil the grate, then lay the fish on. Scrub the hot grate clean, then wipe it with an oil-soaked wad of paper towel gripped in tongs until it glistens. Lay the sardines directly over the hot zone, perpendicular to the bars so they are easy to lift, all facing the same way. Close the lid if using gas. Do not touch them for 2½ to 3 minutes; the skin needs an uninterrupted contact to set and release. Lifting early tears the skin and the fish sticks.'
    technique: 'fish-skin-scoring'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Flip once. After 2½ to 3 minutes the first side should be blistered, charred in spots, and the fish should lift cleanly with a thin spatula; if it resists, give it 20 to 30 seconds more, it will release when the skin has crisped. Flip each sardine once and grill the second side 2 to 2½ minutes. The fish is done when the flesh at the thickest point behind the head is opaque to the bone and the dorsal fin pulls out easily, roughly 58 to 60 °C internal. A 75 to 100 g sardine cooks in 5 minutes total; oily fish forgives a little more than lean fish, but past well-done the flesh dries and the omega-rich fat oxidizes toward bitter.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Rest the fish and whisk the salmoriglio warm. Move the sardines to a warm platter to rest while you build the sauce. In a small bowl set over the residual warmth of the grill-side or a bowl of warm water, combine the 45 g / 1 lemon of juice, the 20 g / 4 tsp warm water, the 6 g / 2 cloves grated garlic, the 2 g / 2 tsp crumbled dried oregano, the optional 1 g / ¼ tsp red pepper flakes, and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. Whisk constantly while drizzling in the 75 g / ⅓ cup olive oil in a thin stream until it thickens to a loose, cloudy emulsion that coats a spoon. Whisk in the lemon zest and the 6 g / 1½ tbsp chopped parsley last. It should taste sharp and garlicky and herbal; salmoriglio is meant to be assertive, because it has rich oily fish to stand up to.'
    technique: 'vinaigrette-emulsion'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Dress and serve hot. Spoon the warm salmoriglio generously over and around the sardines, letting it pool on the platter. Scatter the 2 g / ½ tsp flaky sea salt over the charred skin so it crackles on the tongue, and set out the 60 g / 1 lemon of wedges for squeezing. Serve at once, while the skin is still crisp and the sauce is still warm and emulsified. Eat with fingers: lift the top fillet off the spine, then peel the spine away from the bottom fillet in one piece. Grilled bread to mop the lemon-oil on the platter is the traditional and correct accompaniment.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why oily fish wants fierce heat and acid. Sardines carry a high load of subcutaneous and intramuscular fat, much of it omega-3 polyunsaturated, which is exactly what makes them forgiving over a hot fire: that fat renders and bastes the thin fillet from within while the skin chars, where a lean fish would dry to cotton in the same conditions. The flip side is that the same unsaturated fats oxidize quickly once the fish ages or is overcooked, and oxidized fish-fat is the source of the off, fishy taste people who dislike sardines are usually reacting to. Two moves defeat it: cook the fish dead-fresh and stop the moment the flesh sets to the bone, and finish with acid. The lemon in the salmoriglio is not garnish; the protons cut through the fat and the citric acid suppresses the perception of the trimethylamine notes that read as fishiness. This is the same logic behind mackerel with vinegar, bluefish with lemon, and anchovies with their brine.

  Crisp skin is the whole technique. Everything before the fire serves the skin. Scaling matters (unscaled skin is gritty and will not crisp evenly); drying matters (surface water steams and the skin sticks and tears); the thin oil film matters (it conducts heat and drives Maillard browning on the skin proteins); and the hot, hard-oiled grate matters (a cool or dirty grate is where sardine skin goes to die). Lay the fish down and leave it: the skin proteins bond to the grate at first, then release on their own once they have crisped, usually right at the 2½ to 3 minute mark. Forcing an early flip is the single most common way to lose the skin.

  Salmoriglio, mechanically. Salmoriglio is an emulsion, not a vinaigrette splashed over fish. Lemon juice, olive oil, and a splash of warm water are whisked together until they thicken into a loose, cloudy, clinging sauce. The warm water is the part most recipes drop; it lowers the oil's viscosity and gives the emulsion a continuous phase to hold, so the sauce drapes the fish rather than separating into oil slick and lemon puddle on the platter. Whisk it warm, over the residual heat of the grill-side or a bowl of warm water, and it comes together in seconds; whisk cold oil into cold lemon and it fights you. Use it within a few minutes, because it slackens as it stands.

  Buying and freshness, the load-bearing variable. Sardines are an oily fish, which means they spoil faster than lean fish and that a tired sardine tastes dramatically worse than a tired cod. Buy them the day you cook them, from a counter that turns over fast. The cues: clear convex eyes (sunken and cloudy means old), bright red gills (brown or gray means old), firm flesh that springs back, and a clean briny smell with no sour ammonia edge. Ask the fishmonger to scale and gut them, or do it yourself under running water in two minutes per fish. No amount of technique rescues a stale oily fish, so this is the step that actually decides whether the dish is good.

  Eating them whole. Sardines are small enough that you eat them off the bone with your fingers. Lift the top fillet from the spine, peel the spine away from the bottom fillet in one piece (in a fresh, properly cooked sardine it lifts clean), and eat the second fillet. The smaller pin bones are soft and edible; the spine is not. Grilled bread on the platter is not optional in the coastal tradition, it is the plate, catching the lemon-oil and the rendered fat that pools under the fish.
