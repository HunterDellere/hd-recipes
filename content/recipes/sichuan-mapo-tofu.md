before_you_start:
  - 'Steamed jasmine rice is the partner and must be on FIRST. Rinse 360 g / 1¾ cups rice three times, start in the cooker (or stovetop covered at a bare simmer 18 minutes, then 10 minute rest off heat). The rice 15 minute rest overlaps the entire mapo tofu cook so both arrive hot together.'
  - 'Toast the Sichuan peppercorns whole and grind them within 30 minutes of plating. Pre-ground peppercorn from the jar has lost most of its volatile oils to oxidation, the má (numbing) effect that defines the dish requires the fresh-toasted-and-cracked powder as a finishing scatter.'
  - 'The tofu blanch (step 2) runs in parallel with the beef browning (step 1). Have a wide saucepan of salted water at 80 degrees C ready before the beef hits the wok, otherwise the beef sits and overcooks while the blanch water heats.'
  - 'Cube the silken tofu only after the blanch water is at temperature. Silken tofu cubes lose structural integrity if they sit cut on the board for more than a few minutes; cut, blanch, drain in one continuous move.'

steps:
  - text: 'Brown the beef. Heat a wok over high heat until lightly smoking. Add 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil and the 150 g ground beef. Stir-fry continuously, breaking the meat up with a spatula into fine granules. Cook 5 to 7 minutes; the beef will release water in the first 2 minutes (do not drain, let it cook off), then begin to brown. Pull when the granules are deep mahogany, the surface is dry, and the kitchen smells of toasted beef. This is "ma chao" technique and it is non-negotiable; a pale or grey beef base produces a pale or grey final dish. Add 10 ml / 2 tsp Shaoxing wine and 5 ml / 1 tsp light soy. Toss 30 seconds. Transfer to a plate.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Blanch the tofu. While the beef browns, bring the 1500 ml / 6 cups water to a bare simmer (80°C / 175°F, small bubbles at the edges, no rolling boil) in a wide saucepan with the 8 g / 2 tsp salt dissolved. Cut the 500 g silken tofu into 2 cm cubes very carefully (a paring knife with a wet blade works best; do not press hard). Slide the cubes into the salted water with a wide spatula. Hold at 80°C for 2 minutes (do not boil, boiling will fracture the silken curd). Drain through a wide colander. Set aside. The blanch firms the surface proteins so the cubes hold shape under the sauce, and pre-seasons the tofu from the inside.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Render the aromatic base. Wipe the wok and return to medium heat. Add the 45 ml / 3 tbsp neutral oil. When the oil is shimmering (140 to 160°C / 285 to 320°F, before smoking), add the 35 g doubanjiang. Break it up immediately with the spatula and stir-fry 90 seconds, until the oil turns brilliant brick-red and smells of toasted chili-bean paste. Add the 6 g crushed douchi, 12 g minced ginger, and 16 g minced garlic. Stir-fry 45 seconds; do not exceed medium heat or the aromatics scorch.'
    technique: 'red-oil-rendering'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Build the sauce. Pour in the 240 ml / 1 cup chicken stock, 15 ml / 1 tbsp light soy sauce, 5 ml / 1 tsp dark soy sauce, and 3 g / ½ tsp sugar. Return the browned beef to the wok. Bring to a simmer; the liquid should be aromatic, deep red-brown, and assertive. Slide in the blanched tofu cubes (do not stir aggressively, push gently with the back of the ladle to submerge them in the sauce). Simmer 3 to 4 minutes at medium-low to let the tofu absorb seasoning.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Three-pour cornstarch thickening. Whisk the 9 g cornstarch with 45 ml / 3 tbsp cold water into a slurry. Increase wok heat to medium-high. Pour one-third of the slurry around the edge of the wok in a circle. Push the tofu gently with the back of the ladle to distribute (do not stir hard). Wait 20 seconds for the sauce to thicken. Pour another third the same way; wait 20 seconds. Pour the final third; the sauce should be glossy and coat the tofu in a clinging layer rather than puddle around it. The three-pour technique is the textbook Sichuan move; a single pour produces a gummy texture, while three sequential pours build a layered viscosity that clings to the tofu without weighing it down.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish with the má. Pull the wok off the heat. Add the 25 g sliced scallion greens and 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil (if using). Toss gently. Transfer to a wide serving bowl. Sprinkle the 3 g / 1½ tsp freshly toasted-and-ground Sichuan peppercorn over the top, do not stir it in; the peppercorn powder is a finishing scatter that hits the tongue first as the cook lifts a bite. The freshness of the peppercorn powder is the whole point: pre-ground supermarket Sichuan peppercorn has lost half its volatile oils to oxidation, and the má (numbing) effect that defines the dish requires fresh-toasted whole peppercorns. Serve immediately with steamed jasmine rice.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The seven-flavor target (七味). Sichuan cuisine codifies mapo tofu's flavor profile as a precise combination of seven elements: 麻 (má, the numbing tongue tingle from Sichuan peppercorn), 辣 (là, the chili spiciness), 烫 (tàng, the temperature heat, the dish must arrive at the table near-boiling), 香 (xiāng, the aromatic complexity from the doubanjiang-douchi-aromatics layering), 鲜 (xiān, the savory-fresh umami from the beef and the stock), 嫩 (nèn, the tender silken-tofu texture), and 酥 (sū, the crumbly-meat texture from the well-browned beef granules). Cooking troubleshooting maps to which of the seven is missing: a flat dish is missing xiāng (more aromatics, more rendering); a one-dimensional spicy dish is missing má (fresher peppercorn) or xiān (more umami, more stock); a dish that read fine in the kitchen but disappointing at the table is missing tàng (let it cool too long, or the bowl was cold).

  Three-pour cornstarch is the textbook Sichuan thickening move and is the marker between home-style and restaurant-style mapo. Single-pour cornstarch produces a uniformly thick, slightly gummy sauce because all the starch hits the heat at once and gelatinizes simultaneously. Three-pour separates the starch loading into layers: the first pour starts to thicken, the next pour adds body, the final pour produces the glossy clinging finish. The technique scales to other Sichuan and Cantonese sauces and is worth learning generally.

  Why beef and not pork. The 1862 Chen-family original was beef, the Sichuan classic codifications are beef, and the Chengdu version most people order in Chengdu today is still beef. Pork mapo tofu is a Cantonese and overseas-Chinese adaptation that became the dominant Western version because most Chinese-American restaurants are run by Cantonese families and pork is the Cantonese default protein. Either is delicious; one is older and more authentic to the Sichuan source. Worth knowing both versions exist.

  The fresh-toasted Sichuan peppercorn rule. The Sichuan peppercorn (huajiao) contains hydroxy-α-sanshool, the unusual neurotransmitter-mimicking compound responsible for the má numbing sensation. Hydroxy-α-sanshool is volatile and oxidizes within months once the peppercorn is ground and exposed to air; pre-ground supermarket Sichuan peppercorn loses 50 to 70% of its má potency within a year of grinding. The home cook must buy whole peppercorns (look for plump, fragrant red Da Hong Pao or green Yan jiao varieties), toast them in a dry pan over medium heat for 60 to 90 seconds until fragrant, and grind them in a mortar or spice grinder right before serving. The freshly ground powder should make your tongue tingle when you taste a pinch raw; if it does not, the peppercorn is too old. Pre-toasted Sichuan peppercorn powder kept sealed in a jar at room temperature is acceptable for 2 to 3 months but loses ground after that.

  Eating mechanics. Mapo tofu is part of a multi-dish Chinese meal: serve with steamed jasmine rice (the rice tempers the heat and absorbs the sauce), a clear simple soup (a winter-melon or daikon clear soup), and at least one non-spicy stir-fried green vegetable. Each diner takes spoonfuls of mapo onto their rice; the saucy rice becomes its own dish. The má-là combination produces an unusual eating sensation: the chili heat hits first, the numbing tingle of the peppercorn arrives a few seconds later and runs underneath, and the combination produces an oscillating, almost vibratory sensation that defines Sichuan cooking. First-time mapo eaters often need a moment to recalibrate; experienced eaters chase the sensation. Reheats acceptably the next day; the silken tofu loses some structural integrity but the flavor deepens.
