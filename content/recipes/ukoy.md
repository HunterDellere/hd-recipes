steps:
  - text: 'For the sawsawan: combine 120 ml / ½ cup cane vinegar, 10 ml / 2 tsp fish sauce, 6 g / 2 cloves minced garlic, and 1 sliced bird''s eye chili in a small bowl. Stir and set aside, the garlic and chili need a 20-minute steep in the acid to mellow into the vinegar before serving.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'In a separate bowl, toss 115 g / 8 large peeled shrimp with 10 ml / 2 tsp fish sauce and 10 ml / 2 tsp light soy sauce. Marinate 10–30 minutes, the salt diffuses into the shrimp''s muscle and seasons it from the inside; this is the only seasoning the shrimp gets, so do not skip it.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'In a large bowl, toss 170 g / 6 oz mung bean sprouts with 225 g / 8 oz carrot matchsticks and 225 g / 8 oz sweet potato matchsticks until evenly distributed. Set aside.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'In a medium bowl, whisk 265 ml / 9 fl oz ice-cold water with 170 g / 1 cup white rice flour and 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt until smooth. The batter should pour like heavy cream, thin enough to coat each strand of vegetable, thick enough to cling. Cold water keeps the starch granules from pre-gelatinizing; warm batter goes gummy and the fritters fry stodgy.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour the batter over the vegetables. Fold with a spatula until every matchstick and sprout is evenly coated. The mixture looks underbatered, that is correct; ukoy is held together by the fried shell, not by the batter volume.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Heat oven to 200°F (95°C) with a wire rack set in a rimmed sheet pan to hold finished fritters. In a wide Dutch oven or wok, heat 1200 ml / 5 cups neutral oil over medium-high to 375°F / 190°C. Use an instant-read thermometer; the temperature is the recipe.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Form each ukoy: scoop 85 g / ½ cup of the vegetable mixture into a large heatproof spoon (a serving spoon or a small ladle), pressing gently to mound. Lay 1 marinated shrimp flat across the top, then top with another 85 g / ½ cup of vegetable mixture and press the whole assembly into a 8 cm / 3-inch disc roughly 1.5 cm / ½-inch thick. The shrimp should be fully sandwiched.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Slide the formed ukoy off the spoon directly into the 190 °C oil, use a second spoon or a small offset spatula to coax it off in one piece. Form a second ukoy and slide it in. Do not crowd the oil; two at a time keeps the temperature recovery fast.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Fry 2 minutes on the first side until the underside is golden and rigid. Flip with a slotted metal spatula. Fry the second side 2 minutes until uniformly deep gold and the shrimp shows pink-orange through the lacy shell. Total fry time is 4 minutes; longer and the 225 g sweet potato turns mealy, the shell goes too dark.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Lift to the wire rack with the slotted spatula. Season immediately with 1 pinch kosher salt while hot, surface salt adheres to the oil-wet shell. Transfer to the warm oven to hold while frying the rest.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Between batches: skim any browned batter shrapnel out of the oil with a spider, left in, it burns and contaminates the next round. Return oil to 190 °C before forming and frying the next two ukoy. Repeat until all 8 fritters are done.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Serve immediately with the sawsawan. The shell stays glassy for 5–10 minutes; longer than that and steam from the inside softens it from underneath. There is no save, eat them hot.'

notes: |
  Why rice flour, not wheat: rice flour has no gluten. When the cold batter hits 190 °C oil, the surface starch gelatinizes, dehydrates, and locks into a glassy matrix within 15 seconds, there is no stretchy gluten network to absorb steam back into chewiness. This is the texture of ukoy. A wheat-flour batter at the same ratio fries up to a dense, chewy fritter; the dish is an ingredient choice, not a recipe.

  Why ice-cold water: cold water keeps the rice-flour starch granules from pre-gelatinizing in the batter. A warm batter starts forming the gel before frying, gives off steam too slowly, and the result is gummy through the middle. The cold-water rule is the same as for tempura, and for the same molecular reason.

  Why 190 °C, not lower: at 175 °C the batter sets too slowly; the vegetables release water faster than the shell hardens, and the fritter is greasy. At 190 °C the shell sets fast enough that the vegetables steam-cook inside the shell rather than oiling it. Above 200 °C the shell goes too dark before the inside cooks through. The 5-degree window is the recipe.

  Why immediately serve: fried rice-flour shells go from glassy-shatter to brittle-and-soft within ~15 minutes as steam from the inside rehydrates the shell from underneath. There is no oven-hold or microwave that recovers it. Form, fry, eat — this is a hot-station dish, not a buffet one.

  The sawsawan is the recipe''s acid lever. Without it, the fritter is greasy and one-note. With it, every bite resets — the sharp cane vinegar cuts the oil, the garlic adds top-note pungency, the chili heats, the fish sauce brings glutamate from underneath. Serve a generous puddle per person, not a tablespoon.
