Galchi jorim is the hairtail version, structurally a radish dish as much as a fish dish, since the daikon coins on the pot bottom braise longest and carry the sauce. before_you_start:
  - 'Start short-grain rice FIRST as a parallel track. This is a rice dish, eaten with steamed Korean or Japanese short-grain rice and the sauce spooned over it between bites of fish, not a standalone stew. A 12-minute simmer plus a 10-minute rest means the rice should go on before you build the yangnyeom so it finishes as the braise reduces.'
  - 'The radish goes on the bottom and braises longest. Thick daikon coins line the pot floor, simmer in the liquid the entire time, and only turn translucent and sweet near the end; the delicate hairtail segments rest on top and cook in the steam and rising sauce for the last stretch. Do not stir once the fish is in, the flesh shreds. Spoon sauce over the segments instead.'
  - 'Make the anchovy-kelp stock first if building it from scratch (a 15-minute steep), or have 500 ml water plus the dried anchovy and kombu ready to steep while you cut the radish and fish.'

steps:
  - text: 'Build the anchovy-kelp stock. Put the 500 ml / 2 cups water, the 5 g / one 5 cm square kombu, and the 10 g / about 8 dried anchovies into a small pot. Bring to a bare simmer over medium, then pull the 5 g kombu the moment fine bubbles climb the sides (a hard boil on kombu turns the broth slick and slightly bitter). Simmer the anchovies 8 minutes more, then strain. You want about 450 ml of clean, savory stock; it carries inosinate from the anchovies and glutamate from the kombu, the umami floor the braise is built on.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Cure the fish. Lay the 500 g hairtail segments on a board, salt both sides with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt, and let them sit 10 minutes while you cut the radish. The brief cure firms the delicate flesh through surface protein denaturation and draws off a little weeping water, so the segments hold together in the braise instead of shredding. Pat them dry again right before they go in the pot.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Lay the radish bed. Peel the 400 g daikon and cut it into 1.5 cm thick coins or half-moons. Arrange them in a single tight layer across the bottom of a wide heavy pot or deep skillet; this is the base, and it braises longest, so it has to sit in the liquid the whole time. The radish is not a garnish here, it is half the dish, drinking the sauce and turning translucent and sweet as it cooks.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Mix the yangnyeom. In a bowl whisk the 16 g / 2 tbsp gochugaru, 20 g / 1 tbsp gochujang, 30 g / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, 5 g / 1 tsp dark soy sauce, 20 g minced garlic, 10 g grated ginger, 15 g / 1 tbsp mirin, and 6 g / 1½ tsp sugar into a loose red paste. This is the seasoning core; the gochugaru leads on color and clean heat, the gochujang supplies fermented body underneath.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Start the braise on the radish alone. Pour the 450 ml anchovy-kelp stock over the radish bed and spoon about half the yangnyeom over it, stirring it into the liquid. Bring to a simmer over medium-high, then cover and reduce to medium-low. Braise the radish 12 minutes before the fish goes in; the daikon needs the head start to soften and turn translucent, while the hairtail needs only the final stretch.'
    technique: 'braising'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Lay the fish on top and spoon over the rest of the sauce. Pat the cured 500 g hairtail segments dry once more and arrange them in a single layer over the radish, skin-side up. Spoon the remaining yangnyeom over each segment so the tops are coated. Do not stir from this point; the delicate flesh shreds if disturbed. Cover and braise 10 minutes, tilting the pot once or twice to spoon the bubbling sauce up and over the fish.'
    technique: 'braising'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Reduce uncovered to a glaze. Uncover, raise the heat to medium-high, and simmer hard 6 to 8 minutes to cook the sauce down by roughly half, until it turns glossy and clings to the radish and the fish. Keep spooning the reducing sauce over the segments rather than stirring. The radish should now be fully translucent and the hairtail flesh opaque, flaking cleanly off the spine when nudged with a spoon.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the fresh aromatics. Scatter the 40 g scallion batons and the 15 g sliced green chili over the top and braise 2 minutes more, just until the scallion wilts but keeps its bite. These go in late so they read fresh against the long-cooked sauce.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish off heat. Pull the pot from the heat. Drizzle the 5 g / 1 tsp rice vinegar and the 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil over the top and tilt the pot to let them run into the sauce; the vinegar is the finishing acid that lifts the fermented gochujang depth and cuts the richness of the fish, and the sesame oil goes in off heat so its volatile aroma is not cooked off. Taste the sauce: it should read sweet-hot and savory with a clean sour lift at the back. If flat, a few drops more soy; if too sharp, a pinch of sugar.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Scatter the 3 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds over the top. Serve straight from the pot with steamed short-grain rice, giving each diner radish, a hairtail segment, and a spoonful of sauce over the rice. Eat the fish off the central spine with chopsticks; the flesh lifts away in soft flakes and the sauce-soaked radish is as much the point as the fish.'

notes: |
  Why the radish goes on the bottom. Daikon (or Korean mu) is the structural base of galchi jorim, not a side vegetable. The coins line the pot floor so they sit in the braising liquid the entire cook, where they slowly turn from opaque and sharp to translucent, sweet, and saturated with sauce. They also lift the delicate fish off the direct heat of the pot bottom, letting the segments cook gently in the steam and the rising liquid rather than searing and sticking. By the end the sauce-soaked radish is as prized as the fish; many Korean cooks reach for the radish first.

  Why hairtail demands a no-stir braise. Hairtail flesh has short muscle blocks and very little connective tissue, so it flakes off the central spine with almost no force. That tenderness is the appeal and the hazard: any stirring once the fish is in the pot shreds the segments into the sauce. The technique is to lay the segments on the radish bed, spoon sauce over them, and braise undisturbed, tilting the pot to baste rather than stir. The brief salt cure before braising firms the surface enough to help the segments survive the simmer intact.

  The umami floor and the finishing acid. The glutamate-and-inosinate base comes from three sources stacked together: the light soy sauce (glutamate plus trace nucleotides), the gochujang (fermented soy depth), and the anchovy-kelp stock (kombu glutamate plus dried-anchovy inosinate, a classic umami synergy where the two compounds multiply rather than add). Against all that savory weight the dish needs a bright counter, supplied by the splash of rice vinegar stirred in off the heat plus the fresh scallion and green chili. The vinegar both lifts the fermented gochujang depth and cuts the richness of the fish; without it the braise reads heavy and one-note.

  Why gochugaru leads and gochujang supports. Unlike a gochujang-forward braise, galchi jorim leans on gochugaru for the bulk of its color and a clean, fruity, direct heat, with only a supporting spoon of gochujang for fermented body. Too much gochujang makes the sauce sweet and pasty and muddies the clean fish; the flake-to-paste ratio here keeps the sauce bright red and loose enough to glaze rather than coat. The ratio is the heat-and-depth dial: more gochugaru for a hotter, brighter pot, more gochujang for a sweeter, deeper one.

  Why this is a rice dish. Galchi jorim is built to be eaten with steamed short-grain rice, with the reduced sauce and the soft radish spooned over the rice between bites of fish. The sauce is intentionally seasoned to read strong on its own, because it is meant to season a bland, sticky starch alongside, not to be eaten by the spoonful. Start the rice before the braise so the two finish together; a galchi jorim that has to wait for rice goes cold, and the dish is at its best straight off the heat with the sauce still glossy.

  Sourcing the fish. Hairtail (galchi) is sold whole on ice at H Mart, Hannam, 99 Ranch, and most Korean and Chinese grocers, very often previously frozen since the fish oxidizes fast. Look for a bright, even, reflective silver coat with no yellowing or chalky dullness, firm springy flesh, and a clean marine smell. Have the counter gut it, trim the fin, and cross-cut it into 7 cm segments; doing it at home with a meter-long slippery ribbon is a chore. Pat the segments very dry before salting so they do not weep into and thin out the sauce.
