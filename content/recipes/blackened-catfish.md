source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Paul Prudhomme blackening technique (K-Paul''s Louisiana Kitchen, New Orleans, 1980), applied to channel catfish rather than the original redfish.'
  note: 'Catfish is the structural fish of the Mississippi delta and the obvious Louisiana candidate for blackening once redfish populations crashed under the dish''s 1980s popularity and the species was federally protected. The technique transfers directly: a screaming dry cast iron at 230 to 320 °C / 450 to 600 °F surface, a butter-dipped fillet, a dry spice dredge, a 90-second-per-side sear. What changes is the fish. Catfish runs 6 to 12% fat distributed through the flesh (not concentrated in a subcutaneous layer the way salmon or mackerel carry it), and that intramuscular fat is largely saturated and monounsaturated, so the flesh holds up to the extreme dry heat without the interior going chalky before the crust sets. The two catfish-specific moves are the salt-and-vinegar rinse to manage the freshwater geosmin back-note, and a Worcestershire spike in the butter dip to give the mild flesh a glutamate anchor under the spice.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Catfish carries a freshwater earthy back-note (the compound geosmin, accumulated through bottom-feeding biology). Well-handled farmed channel catfish in the 2020s rarely reads strongly muddy, but a salt-and-vinegar rinse cheaply insures against it: rub the fillets with 10 g / 2 tsp kosher salt and 15 g / 1 tbsp white vinegar, let sit 5 minutes, then rinse under cold water and pat bone-dry. Do this first, the fish must be fully dry before it hits the butter or the crust steams instead of chars.'
  - 'The cast iron skillet must be heated empty over the highest heat your stove provides for 8 to 10 minutes until it is smoking aggressively and a flicked drop of water flashes to steam in under a second. A cooler skillet (the single most common home error) steams the fish in melted butter rather than blackening it. Cook outdoors on a grill side-burner or under a powerful range hood at full power; the kitchen will fill with smoke.'
  - 'Pre-position everything in a line near the stove before the spice goes on any fish: the bowl of butter, the plate of spice, the dry fillets, the spatula. Once a fillet is dredged it must hit the skillet in under 30 seconds, there is no time to fetch a tool mid-sear.'

steps:
  - text: 'Rinse the catfish. Lay the 4 fillets in a colander and rub them all over with the 10 g / 2 tsp kosher salt and 15 g / 1 tbsp white vinegar. Let sit 5 minutes; the acid and salt mobilize the freshwater geosmin into solution. Rinse thoroughly under cold running water, then pat the fillets bone-dry on both sides with paper towels and lay them on a dry plate. The fish must read dry to the touch, any surface water steams under the spice and prevents the crust from charring.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Make the spice blend. Combine all of Phase 2 in a small bowl: 15 g / 1½ tbsp Hungarian paprika, 6 g / 1 tbsp cayenne, 6 g / 2 tsp garlic powder, 6 g / 2 tsp onion powder, 3 g / 1½ tsp dried oregano, 3 g / 1½ tsp dried thyme, 3 g / 1½ tsp black pepper, 2 g / 1 tsp white pepper, and 6 g / 1 tsp fine sea salt. Whisk to fully blend. This yields about 50 g; you will dredge with roughly 30 g and the rest keeps 3 months in a sealed jar.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Heat the skillet. Place a 30 cm / 12-inch cast iron skillet empty over the highest heat your stove provides and leave it 8 to 10 minutes. It is ready when it smokes aggressively and a flicked drop of water flashes to steam in under a second. Open the windows, run the range hood at full power, and if you have an outdoor side burner or high-output grill this is the moment to use it. The kitchen will fill with smoke and the alarms may sound.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Build the butter dip and stage the line. While the skillet heats, melt the 120 g / 1 stick butter in a wide shallow bowl and whisk in the 10 g / 2 tsp Worcestershire sauce. Spread 30 g of the spice blend on a wide plate. Set the bowl of butter, the plate of spice, the dry fillets, and a thin fish spatula in a single line beside the stove. Once a fillet is spiced it goes to the iron in under 30 seconds, so everything is positioned before the first dredge.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Dip and dredge the first fillet. Submerge one fillet fully in the Worcestershire-butter, coating both sides, lift and let the excess drip for 1 second. Lay it in the spice and press down so the blend coats every surface, then flip and dredge the second side. The fillet should be heavily black-red on every face. Carry it straight to the smoking skillet and lay it down flat, away from you to avoid the splatter.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Sear the first side. Sear undisturbed for 90 seconds to 2 minutes. Do not move the fish, the bottom is forming the structural blackened crust and any nudge tears it off and leaves a pale steamed patch. The smoke will be dramatic; this is correct, it is the spice volatiles reaching their pyrolysis point and is what flavors the crust. Lift a corner with the spatula: the bottom should be deep black with audibly crisp edges. If it is still red, the iron was not hot enough, give it another 30 to 60 seconds.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Flip and finish. Slide the wide thin spatula fully under the fillet (the crust is fragile) and flip in one motion. Sear the second side 2 to 3 minutes; catfish fillets run thicker than redfish, so they need slightly longer to cook through. The fish is done when a knife in the thickest part shows opaque white flake all the way through with no translucent raw band; catfish flesh breaks into wide flakes rather than the fine flake of cod, so look for the whole sheet to separate cleanly. Transfer to a warm plate.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Repeat for the remaining fillets. Give the skillet 60 seconds between fish to recover surface temperature. The spice residue from the previous fillet will smoke hard and feeds the next crust rather than burning. If the residue starts throwing a sharp acrid smoke (distinct from the cleaner spiced smoke), scrape it out with a wooden spatula before the next fillet goes in.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Plate and finish with acid. Set each fillet on a warm plate, scatter the 10 g chopped parsley over the four, and serve with 2 of the 8 lemon wedges per portion. The lemon is squeezed over the fish at the table; the bright acid cuts the spice heat and the butter richness and is the structural finishing element, not a garnish. Serve immediately with dirty rice and a sharp vinegar slaw.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why catfish behaves differently from redfish under blackening. Redfish (and snapper, grouper, the leaner Gulf fillets the technique was built on) carries very little intramuscular fat, so its window between cooked-through and dried-out is narrow, and the crust has to set fast before the flesh chalks. Catfish runs 6 to 12% fat distributed through the flesh, largely saturated and monounsaturated, which buffers the interior against the brutal dry heat and gives a slightly wider doneness window. The trade-off is that catfish fillets are thicker and denser, so the second-side sear runs a minute or two longer to bring the center to opaque flake. The result reads moister and a touch richer than blackened redfish, with the same black crust.

  Why the geosmin rinse. Catfish has a freshwater earthy reputation, technically the compound geosmin accumulated in the skin and dark muscle through bottom-feeding biology. Modern US channel-catfish farms flush the fish in clean-water tanks before harvest and well-handled farmed fish in the 2020s rarely reads strongly muddy, but the salt-and-vinegar rinse is cheap insurance: the acid and salt pull any residual geosmin into solution and the cold rinse carries it off. It is the standard Vietnamese and South Asian catfish-prep step for the same reason. Skip it only if the raw fish smells clean.

  Why Worcestershire in the butter. The redfish version relies on the spice blend alone for savory depth, which is plenty on a fish with more inherent character. Catfish is milder, so a glutamate anchor earns its place: the tamarind, anchovy, and aged molasses in Worcestershire read as a darker, more savory crust without announcing themselves as a separate flavor. It also deepens the butter''s Maillard browning in the skillet.

  Why screaming hot, and why not burned. The blackening reaction happens between 230 and 320 °C / 450 to 600 °F surface: hot enough to instantly drive off the butter''s surface moisture, char the spices to a black crust, and Maillard-sear the protein under it, all inside 90 seconds. A skillet at 200 °C steams the fish in butter instead of charring; a skillet hotter than 320 °C burns the spice to acrid ash before the fish cooks. Blackening is controlled charring, not burning; the tell is the smoke, blackening smoke smells aggressively spiced, burning smoke smells acrid and chemical. Pull at the moment of black crust, not 30 seconds later.

  Pairings. Canonical with dirty rice (rice cooked down with chicken livers, the Louisiana trinity, and Cajun spice) and a sharp vinegar slaw to throw acid against the heat. A lemon-butter sauce under the fish is the modern restaurant move. Lemon wedges at the table are non-negotiable. A cold Abita Amber or a Mexican lager on the side.

  Storage. Blackened fish does not hold; the crust softens within 30 minutes and the smoky aroma fades. Eat immediately. The leftover spice blend keeps 3 months sealed, so double or triple it for repeat cooks.
