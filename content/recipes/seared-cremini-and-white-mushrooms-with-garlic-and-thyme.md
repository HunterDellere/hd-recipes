before_you_start:
  - 'Do not crowd the pan. Mushrooms shed a lot of water, and a crowded pan traps it so they steam the whole time and never brown. Use your widest skillet and work in two batches if 450 g will not fit in a single layer; the brown crust is the entire point and crowding kills it.'
  - 'Clean the mushrooms dry, not wet. They are sponges; soaking them adds water you then have to cook off. Brush them or wipe with a barely-damp towel just before cooking.'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the pan dry and hot. Place your widest heavy skillet over high heat for 2 to 3 minutes until genuinely hot. Add the 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil, just a film, and swirl.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Dry-sear, water first. Add the 450 g / 1 lb halved mushrooms cut-face down in a single uncrowded layer. They will squeak and then, within a minute, flood the pan with their own water. Do not season, do not add fat, do not stir much yet, let the water boil off hard over high heat, 3 to 5 minutes. The pan goes from sizzling to a wet simmer back to sizzling as the liquid evaporates; that second sizzle is your cue the water is gone and browning can start.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Brown deep. Once the pan is dry again, leave the mushrooms cut-face down 2 to 3 minutes to build a deep brown crust, then toss and brown a second face for another 2 minutes. They should shrink to about half their raw volume, firm to a meaty bite, and color to a rich brown. This is the payoff of the dry sear; rush it and they stay pale and rubbery.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add butter and aromatics. Drop the 30 g / 2 tbsp cold cubed butter, the 12 g / 3 smashed garlic cloves, and the 3 g / 3 sprigs thyme into the pan. The butter will foam and brown within 20 seconds; toss the mushrooms in it and let the garlic and thyme perfume the foaming butter for 60 to 90 seconds. Tilt the pan and spoon the herb butter over the mushrooms.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Season late and deglaze with soy. Add the 10 g / 2 tsp light soy sauce, the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper, and toss; the soy will sizzle and glaze the mushrooms with a savory sheen. The salt goes in now, not earlier, because salt added during the dry sear draws water out and floods the pan. Cook 30 seconds until the soy reduces to a glaze.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Finish with acid and serve. Pull off the heat, squeeze over the 1 tsp lemon juice, and toss. Pull the thyme sprigs if you left them whole. Taste: it should read deeply savory and meaty, with the lemon lifting the butter and the soy reading as depth rather than as soy. Scatter the optional 8 g parsley and serve hot, next to a steak or a piece of fish, or piled over toast or soft polenta as a plate.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The water-then-brown sequence is the whole recipe. A mushroom is roughly 90% water, and in a hot pan that water comes out fast, floods the surface, and drops the temperature below boiling so the mushrooms simmer in their own liquid, pale, soft, and squeaky. Only once that water has fully evaporated does the pan climb back hot enough to brown the now-dry surface and firm the texture to a meaty chew. So the order is non-negotiable: dry sear first over high heat in an uncrowded pan, wait out the flood-and-evaporate, and add fat and salt only after the second sizzle tells you the water is gone. Butter or salt added early just prolongs the steaming and you never get the crust.

  Why salt goes in late. Salt draws water out of the mushrooms osmotically. Added at the start, it pulls even more liquid into the pan and lengthens the steaming phase; added after the mushrooms have browned, it seasons without flooding. This is the opposite of how you salt most vegetables, and getting it wrong is the second most common reason home-cooked mushrooms come out gray and limp (crowding is the first).

  Why soy in a French dish. Mushrooms carry free glutamate plus the ribonucleotides that multiply perceived umami; soy sauce is almost pure glutamate. A small splash compounds the mushrooms own savoriness so the dish tastes deeper and meatier than butter and garlic alone would deliver, and at 2 teaspoons across a pound it reads as savory depth, not as soy. It is the same trick that a spoon of fish sauce or a Parmesan rind does in a tomato sauce, an umami amplifier hiding in the background.

  Why the lemon at the end. Browned mushrooms in butter are rich and round and can read flat and one-note. A squeeze of lemon at the very end, off the heat, sharpens everything: it cuts the butter, brightens the earthy mushroom flavor, and gives the dish a top-note that keeps it from sitting heavy. Add it last so the acid stays bright rather than cooking dull.
