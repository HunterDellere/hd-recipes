steps:
  - text: 'Preheat oven and pan together. Set the oven to 230 °C / 450 °F with a heavy-gauge rimmed half sheet pan (33 × 46 cm / 13 × 18 inch) on the middle rack. The pan preheats with the oven, this is the structural move on which the hard-roast technique depends. Vegetables hitting a cold sheet pan steam through their first 5 minutes (the pan absorbs the surface heat the vegetables need to start browning), produce limp moisture-weeping results, and only start to brown in the back half of the roast. Vegetables hitting a screaming pan sear on contact, lock down a Maillard crust on the bottom face within the first 2 minutes, and produce dramatically deeper browning over the same total time. Give the pan a full 15 minutes in the oven after the oven reaches 230 °C.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Prep the vegetables. Peel and halve the 200 g shallots lengthwise through the root end (root holds halves together); break the 500 g cauliflower into 3 to 4 cm florets from the underside; peel and cube the 400 g sweet potato into 2 cm pieces. Uniform cube size on the sweet potato is the move; mixed sizes guarantee that the small pieces overcook to black while the large pieces stay raw at the center. Place all three vegetables in a large bowl. Add the 20 g / 5 cloves smashed garlic (skins on), the 6 g / 6 thyme sprigs, the 50 g / 3½ tbsp olive oil, the 13 g / 1 tbsp + ½ tsp kosher salt, and the 2 g / 1 tsp cracked black pepper. Toss thoroughly with your hands until every piece is glossy with oil and the salt has distributed across the surfaces. The salt-on-oiled-vegetable order matters: salt sticks to oil and doesn''t flake off into the pan during the toss-onto-hot-pan transfer.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Make the yogurt sauce while the pan preheats and the vegetables roast. Whisk the 200 g Greek yogurt, the 10 g / 2 tsp dijon mustard, the 20 g / 4 tsp lemon juice, the 2 g / 1 tsp lemon zest, the 5 g / 1 tsp honey, the 4 g / 1 clove microplaned raw garlic, the 15 g / 1 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil, and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt together in a small bowl until smooth and uniform. Taste; the sauce should read sharp-tangy-bright on first contact, with the honey reading as background sweetness rather than a sweet note in the foreground. Adjust: more lemon if it reads too rich, more honey if it reads aggressively sharp, more salt if it reads muted. Cover and refrigerate until plating; the sauce wants to be cold when it hits the hot vegetables, the temperature contrast is part of the eating experience. The sauce can be made up to 3 days ahead and improves overnight in the refrigerator as the raw garlic mellows.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Roast, first pass. Pull the preheated sheet pan from the oven (use both hands and good mitts, it is heavy and screaming hot). Dump the seasoned vegetables onto the pan in one continuous motion, the pan should hiss aggressively the second they hit. Use a metal spatula or a heat-proof spoon to spread the vegetables into a single layer across the entire pan, with at least 1 cm of space between most pieces. Crowding is the most common sheet-pan-roast failure, the vegetables steam each other in their own released moisture rather than searing. If the pan looks crowded, use a second sheet pan rather than overload one. Return to the oven, cut-faces of the 200 g shallot halves and the 500 g cauliflower florets ideally facing down for the first contact with the hot pan. Roast undisturbed for 20 minutes.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Toss and roast, second pass. After 20 minutes, the bottom-facing surfaces should be deeply browned (mahogany on the 500 g cauliflower cut faces, golden-caramel on the shallot cut faces, dark amber on the sweet potato faces in contact with the pan). Pull the pan out, toss the vegetables with a flexible metal spatula (lift from the bottom and flip rather than stir, which crumbles the cauliflower). The pan releases a fragrant rush of caramelized-sugar and Maillard aromatics; that smell is the calibration cue that the heat has been right. Spread back into a single layer. Return to the oven and roast another 15 to 20 minutes, until: the shallot halves have collapsed into jammy translucent layers with deeply browned edges, the cauliflower florets are mahogany on all sides and crisp at the tips, the sweet potato cubes are caramel-edged and yield to a paring-knife with the fudge-soft texture of fully-cooked-but-not-blown-out sweet potato. Total roast time should land at 35 to 40 minutes. If one vegetable is finished and another isn''t, pull the finished one to a separate plate and return the pan, this is normal pan-traffic and not a failure.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Plate. Pull the pan and remove the thyme sprigs (the leaves have crisped and fallen off, or are spent and bitter at this point; pull the woody stems out and discard). Squeeze 1 or 2 of the roasted garlic cloves out of their skins onto the vegetables and smash gently with a fork to spread the sweet caramelized garlic paste across the pan; leave the remaining cloves whole for the diner to squeeze out at the table. Transfer the vegetables to a wide warmed serving platter or two plates. Drizzle 60 to 80 g of the cold lemon-dijon yogurt sauce in wide ribbons across the vegetables; do not coat fully, leave bare and saucy patches so the diner can mix proportions on the fork. Serve the remaining sauce in a small bowl alongside. Scatter the 10 g / ¼ cup chopped parsley across the top in a single pass. Place lemon wedges at the edge of the platter for the diner to add additional acid as they prefer. Serve immediately while the vegetables are hot, the sauce is cold, and the temperature contrast is at peak.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why the preheated pan is the dish. A cold sheet pan absorbs heat from the vegetables for the first 3 to 5 minutes of roasting; the vegetables steam in their own released moisture for that window, producing limp surfaces and weeping juice that puddles in the pan. The browning starts only after the pan has caught up to oven temperature and the vegetable surfaces have dried back out. A preheated pan flips the dynamic: the vegetables sear on contact, lock down a Maillard crust on the bottom face in the first 2 minutes, and continue browning steadily through the full 35 to 40 minute roast. The total cook time is the same; the cooking quality is dramatically different. Every hard-roast-vegetable recipe in the modern American canon (Samin Nosrat, Yotam Ottolenghi, Andy Baraghani in the Bon Appétit / NYT Cooking school) preheats the pan as the structural first move. Once a home cook adopts the habit, the entire category of roasted vegetables reads better.

  Vegetable-specific timing and why these three work. Shallot halves: the layered structure means they cook from the outside in over 35 to 40 minutes at 230 °C, with the outer layer caramelizing first and the dense root end softening last; the root-end attachment holds the half together so the layers don''t scatter. Cauliflower florets: the dense protein structure of brassica heads Maillards aggressively on the cut faces at 230 °C, with the surface reaching deep mahogany in 25 to 30 minutes and the tips going crisp at 35 to 40; cauliflower past 45 minutes at this temperature starts to scorch on the tips and dry out at the core. Sweet potato cubes at 2 cm: the orange-flesh starch converts to sugar at the 70 to 75 °C internal threshold (the natural ripening enzyme activity speeds up under heat), and the surface caramelizes at the same temperature the brassica is Maillarding; 35 minutes is the sweet spot for a fudge-soft interior with caramel-glass edges, 45 minutes pushes into raisin-leathery territory. The three converge on the 35 to 40 minute window when cut to the right sizes; any one of them cut too small or too large breaks the dish.

  The salt-by-percentage rule. 1.2% salt to total vegetable weight is the calibrated ratio for hard-roasted vegetables. Less and the dish reads under-seasoned even after the yogurt sauce; more and the salt overwhelms the sweet caramelization the roast is supposed to produce. The math for this recipe: 200 + 500 + 400 + 20 = 1120 g of vegetable, and 1120 × 0.012 = 13.4 g of salt, rounded to 13 g for simplicity. Scaling up or down: multiply total vegetable weight by 0.012 and use that number of grams of Diamond Crystal kosher salt. The technique is robust to vegetable substitutions as long as the salt ratio is kept.

  Yogurt sauce structure. The sauce is built on six elements pulling in distinct directions: yogurt (rich, creamy, mildly tangy), dijon (sharp, tangy-bitter, slightly acidic), lemon juice (bright acid), lemon zest (citrus volatile oils, top notes the juice cannot supply), honey (sweet, balances the acid), microplaned raw garlic (sharp allium top note, the only raw allium in the dish), olive oil (richness, mouth-coating gloss). The structural payoff is contrast: against the sweet-caramelized roasted vegetables, every element of the sauce pulls in the opposite direction (acid against caramel, raw against roasted, tang against sweet, cold against hot). Drop any element and the sauce loses some of that contrast; drop two and the sauce starts reading flat.

  Why this is the companion to the chuck eye recipe on this site. The cast-iron chuck eye comes with a hot red-wine shallot pan sauce that is rich, deeply beefy, and mounted with butter; the sheet-pan vegetables come with a cold lemon-dijon yogurt sauce that is sharp, bright, and acid-forward. The two sauces are complementary by direction: the steak''s sauce is umami-and-glutamate, the vegetable''s sauce is acid-and-tang. Plate the steak slices in their pan sauce and the vegetables in their yogurt sauce side by side on the plate, and the diner alternates bites between the two registers across the meal. The vegetables also work as a standalone side or vegetarian main with no protein at all; the yogurt sauce supplies enough body to read as substantial on its own.

  Make-ahead notes. The yogurt sauce holds 3 days in the refrigerator and improves overnight as the raw garlic mellows from sharp into integrated. The vegetable prep (shallots peeled, cauliflower cut into florets, sweet potato cubed) can be done up to 24 hours ahead and held covered in the refrigerator; the cauliflower is the most fragile and starts to oxidize at the cut surfaces (small brown spots) within 24 hours, which is cosmetic rather than flavor-impacting. The full roast is best eaten within 30 minutes of coming out of the oven; reheated next day in a 220 °C oven for 8 minutes brings back most of the crisp but never quite matches first-out-of-the-oven. The vegetables are also excellent cold the next day, tossed with extra olive oil and lemon and served as a salad over leaves.

  Sheet-pan size and oven calibration. A standard half sheet pan (33 × 46 cm / 13 × 18 inch) holds this quantity (~1100 g of vegetables) at the right single-layer density. A quarter sheet pan is too small; use two pans rather than overload one. Convection ovens run 10 to 15 °C hotter than the dial reads at this temperature; drop the setting to 215 °C / 425 °F on convection. Older home ovens often run 10 to 20 °C cooler than the dial reads; an oven thermometer is the right $10 investment if the cook hasn''t calibrated their oven recently. The dish is forgiving on temperature within a 20 °C window; outside that, the timings drift enough to throw the three vegetables out of sync.
