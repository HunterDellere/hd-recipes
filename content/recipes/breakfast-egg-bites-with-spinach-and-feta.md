title: 'Breakfast egg bites with spinach and feta'
desc: 'Whole eggs blended briefly with cottage cheese, baked in a buttered standard metal muffin tin with wilted spinach, crumbled feta, scallion, and dill. before_you_start:
  - 'Pull the eggs and cottage cheese out of the refrigerator 30 minutes before baking. Room-temperature eggs blend more smoothly and bake more evenly than cold eggs straight from the refrigerator. Cold eggs in a cold blender produce a less integrated custard.'
  - 'Butter the muffin tin generously, every well, including the rim, with cold butter rubbed on with your fingers. This is not optional; egg bites stick aggressively to bare metal and ruin on transfer. Use cold butter rather than melted butter; the cold butter clings to the pan walls and stays in place during baking.'
  - 'The water bath is the technique. Set a roasting pan or oven-safe baking pan one size up on the rack, place the buttered muffin tin inside, and pour boiling water into the roasting pan to come halfway up the muffin tin (about 2 cm of water). The steam and the moderated temperature keep the eggs at custard-set rather than rubber-set.'
  - 'Wilt and drain the spinach before adding to the batter. Wet spinach releases water into the egg custard and ruins the texture. Squeeze it dry in a clean kitchen towel after wilting.'

steps:
  - text: 'Pull the 400 g / 8 eggs and 200 g / ¾ cup cottage cheese from the refrigerator and let them sit at room temperature 30 minutes. While they warm, heat the oven to 165 °C / 325 °F with a roasting pan one size up from the muffin tin on the middle rack.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Wilt the spinach. Heat the 8 g / 2 tsp olive oil in a wide skillet (stainless or cast iron, not nonstick) over medium heat. Add the 150 g spinach in two or three additions, tossing with tongs until wilted (about 60 seconds per addition). Tip into a sieve set over a bowl and press gently to drain. When cool enough to handle, transfer to a clean kitchen towel and squeeze hard over the sink to expel all remaining water. Chop the wilted spinach roughly; expect about 40 g of compressed greens.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Rub the muffin tin with butter. Take the 20 g / 1½ tbsp cold butter and, using your fingers, rub it generously into every well of a standard 12-cup metal muffin tin, including the top rim. Every surface that the egg might touch needs to be buttered. Set aside.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Blend the egg base. In a high-speed blender, combine the 400 g / 8 eggs, the 200 g cottage cheese, the 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt, the 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper, and the 0.3 g / ¼ tsp grated nutmeg. Blend on high for 30 seconds, until completely smooth and slightly foamy on top. The cottage cheese curds should fully integrate; if any curd flecks remain visible, blend another 15 seconds.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Assemble. Divide the wilted chopped spinach, the 100 g crumbled feta, the 30 g sliced scallions, and the 5 g chopped dill across the 12 buttered muffin wells (about 7 g of spinach, 8 g of feta, 2.5 g of scallion per well). Pour the blended egg base evenly over the fillings, filling each well to about 3 mm below the rim (the eggs puff slightly during baking, the small headspace prevents overflow). Each well takes about 50 g of egg base.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Set up the water bath. Pull the preheated roasting pan from the oven. Place the filled muffin tin inside the roasting pan. Carefully pour the 500 ml / 2 cups boiling water into the roasting pan around the muffin tin (not into it), until the water comes about 2 cm up the side of the muffin tin. Return the assembly to the oven.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Bake 28 to 32 minutes at 165 °C / 325 °F. The bites are done when the centers are just set with a slight wobble when the pan is gently shaken; a paring knife inserted at the edge of one bite comes out clean. The tops should be barely browned; deep brown means overcooked and the texture will be rubbery.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Cool 5 minutes in the muffin tin (the bites firm up as they cool). Run a thin paring knife or a small offset spatula around the edge of each bite to release. Gently lift each bite straight up out of its well. Serve warm.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'For storage, cool fully on a wire rack, then refrigerate in a single layer in a covered glass container for up to 5 days. Reheat individual bites in a 160 °C / 325 °F oven for 6 minutes, or microwave at 50% power for 60 seconds (lower power prevents the eggs from tightening into rubber).'

notes: |
  Why blend the cottage cheese. Raw cottage cheese in egg bites reads as cottage cheese: lumpy, discrete curds suspended in egg custard. Blended, the casein proteins fragment into tiny suspended particles that disappear visually and contribute structural body invisibly. The bites read as smooth egg custard with hidden richness, not as egg-with-cottage-cheese. This is the entire reason the bites are tender rather than rubbery; the casein particles act as thermal buffers and prevent the egg proteins from over-tightening.

  Why the water bath. A muffin tin in a dry 165 °C oven cooks the bites from all sides at the same rate: top-down from radiant heat, bottom-up from pan conduction. The bottoms hit egg-toughening temperature (~75 °C internal) before the tops, and the bites end up overcooked at the base and undercooked at the top, or evenly overcooked if held long enough for the tops to set. The water bath buffers the bottom heat (the boiling water never exceeds 100 °C, and most of that energy goes into evaporation rather than conduction), so the bites cook from the top-down only at a gentle rate. Result: even, tender custard throughout. The same technique works for crème brûlée, panna cotta baked, and crustless quiche.

  Why no nonstick muffin tin. Nonstick coatings degrade at high temperature and shed particles into food; they also cannot be heated above ~230 °C without significant degradation. The egg bites do not need nonstick to release; well-buttered raw metal releases cleanly. Use a standard aluminum or carbon steel muffin tin; cast iron muffin tins also work and are the longest-lasting option. The cold-butter rub is what does the work; melted butter pools at the bottom and leaves the sides exposed.

  Why room-temperature ingredients. Cold eggs at 4 °C plus cold cottage cheese at 4 °C plus a cold blender produce a coarse, somewhat separated batter. Room-temperature eggs (~20 °C) plus room-temperature cottage cheese blend into a smooth, fully integrated custard in 30 seconds; the casein proteins denature and integrate more readily at the warmer temperature, and the eggs whip in some air which gives the bites a slightly lighter texture. The 30-minute counter rest is not a passive instruction; it is structural.

  The salt math. Eggs need about 1% salt by weight to taste seasoned; 400 g eggs would call for 4 g salt. The feta contributes roughly 1.5 g of salt, the cottage cheese another 0.5 g, the spinach is unsalted. The 5 g total in the batter, plus the feta-and-cottage-cheese contributions, brings the integrated salt to about 1.75% by total batter weight, which reads seasoned without being aggressive. Adjust down if your feta is exceptionally salty; taste a small bite of feta before salting the eggs.

  Make-ahead and storage. The bites hold 5 days refrigerated in a covered glass container. They freeze well for 2 months; wrap individual bites in parchment squares before freezing to prevent sticking. Reheat from frozen in a 160 °C / 325 °F oven for 12 minutes, or from refrigerated for 6 minutes. The microwave reheat works for grab-and-go but tightens the texture; oven reheat preserves the custard quality.
