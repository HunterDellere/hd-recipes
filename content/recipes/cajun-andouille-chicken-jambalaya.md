  name: 'Acadiana (Cajun country) canon, distinct from New Orleans Creole'
  note: 'There are two jambalayas. The Creole jambalaya of New Orleans (already covered on this site as "creole-jambalaya") is the red version: tomato-based, often with shrimp, descended from Spanish paella. This recipe is the second, structurally different version: the Cajun jambalaya of Acadiana (the prairie country west of New Orleans), brown (no tomato), built on a deep Maillard fond from hard-browning chicken and andouille, cooked in chicken stock and Cajun seasoning rather than tomato sauce. The brown Cajun jambalaya is the structural Sunday-dinner one-pot of southwestern Louisiana; the red Creole jambalaya is the structural party-and-restaurant New Orleans dish. Both are correct, both are jambalaya, both are claimed as definitive by their respective traditions.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The structural depth of Cajun (brown) jambalaya comes from the hard-browning of the chicken and andouille to build a deep mahogany fond on the pot bottom. Crowding the pot or moving the meat too soon prevents the fond from forming; the dish becomes a pale rice-and-meat stew rather than a structurally dark Cajun jambalaya. Plan to brown the proteins in 2 batches in a single layer with at least 30 seconds of undisturbed contact per side.'
  - 'Once the rice goes in (step 6), do not stir the pot. Stirring breaks the steam seal, releases the starch, and produces gummy rice. The discipline is to trust the absorption: liquid in, lid on, do not lift the lid for 25 minutes.'
  - 'Andouille is the structural Cajun sausage: smoked, coarse-ground pork, garlic-heavy, spicy. Cajun andouille from a Louisiana butcher is best; Aidells or Wellshire andouille at most U.S. supermarkets is the substitute. Avoid Mexican chorizo, Italian sausage, or fresh bratwurst; the smoky-spicy profile is structural.'

steps:
  - text: 'Season the 700 g chicken thighs with 6 g of the kosher salt and 2 g of the black pepper. Rest 15 minutes at room temperature.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Heat a heavy-bottomed Dutch oven or wide deep skillet over medium-high heat. Add the 15 ml / 1 tbsp neutral oil only if your skillet is not already hot enough to render the chicken fat. Once shimmering, add the chicken thighs skin-side down in a single layer (work in 2 batches if needed; do not crowd). Brown 5 to 6 minutes without moving until the skin is deep mahogany; flip and brown 3 more minutes on the second side. Transfer to a plate; reserve.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'In the rendered chicken fat in the same pot, add the 300 g andouille slices. Brown 3 to 4 minutes, stirring once or twice, until the andouille has gained deep brown edges and rendered additional smoky fat. Transfer to the same plate as the chicken.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'There should be about 60 ml of rendered fat in the pot now; if more, pour off the excess (leave only 60 ml / 4 tbsp for the trinity sauté). Add the 200 g diced onion, 150 g diced celery, and 150 g diced green pepper (the holy trinity). Cook 6 to 8 minutes over medium-high, stirring occasionally, scraping the brown fond from the pot bottom into the vegetables; the trinity will turn deep amber and the fond will release into the vegetable mass. This is the structural Cajun "deglaze the fond with the trinity" move.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the 20 g / 5 cloves garlic, 15 g / 1½ tbsp Cajun seasoning, 4 g / 2 tsp thyme, 2 bay leaves, remaining 2 g kosher salt, 1 g black pepper, and 2 g / 1 tsp cayenne. Cook 90 seconds until aromatic and the Cajun seasoning has darkened.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 500 g rinsed rice; toast in the trinity-and-spice base 2 minutes, stirring constantly, until the rice grains are coated with the orange-brown trinity mixture and have turned slightly translucent at the edges.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 900 ml / 3¾ cups chicken stock and 15 ml Worcestershire sauce. Return the chicken (skin-side up) and andouille to the pot, nestling into the rice; any accumulated juices on the plate go in too. Bring to a hard boil over high heat.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Once boiling, reduce heat to the lowest setting. Cover tightly with the lid (no peeking). Cook 25 minutes undisturbed. Do not lift the lid; the rice is steaming under there and any disturbance breaks the absorption cook.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Off heat, keep covered and rest 10 minutes. Uncover; fish out the bay leaves. Fluff the rice gently with a fork, lifting from the bottom (the bottom should have a slight pegao / "grattin" of darkened rice; this is structural and prized in Cajun cooking). Lift the chicken and andouille from the rice and serve them on top of the jambalaya in shallow bowls.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Sprinkle the 50 g sliced scallions and 10 g chopped parsley over the jambalaya. Serve with the bottle of Louisiana hot sauce at the table; each diner adjusts heat to taste. A side of slaw or buttered green beans rounds out the plate.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why brown vs red. The structural distinction between Cajun (brown) and Creole (red) jambalaya is whether tomato is in the pot. Cajun jambalaya from Acadiana is brown because the prairie Cajuns historically had limited access to tomatoes and built their depth from the Maillard fond of hard-browned chicken and pork instead. Creole jambalaya from New Orleans is red because the city had access to Mediterranean ingredients (tomato, olive oil, Spanish chorizo) through the port and developed the dish under Spanish paella influence. Both are jambalaya; the regional distinction is structural and Louisiana home cooks defend their tradition.

  Why no stirring after the rice goes in. Once the lid goes on in step 8, the rice is cooking by absorption: the liquid converts to steam, the steam cooks the rice from the top down while the bottom gently toasts into the structural pegao or "grattin" (the Cajun version of the Spanish socarrat or Puerto Rican pegao). Lifting the lid releases the steam and breaks the absorption cycle. Stirring breaks the bottom toasting and releases the starch into a gummy mess. The discipline is to trust the 25 minute closed cook.

  Why the trinity matters. The "holy trinity" of Cajun and Creole cooking (onion, celery, bell pepper) is the structural aromatic base, analogous to French mirepoix (onion, carrot, celery) but with bell pepper substituting for carrot. The pepper contributes a different sweetness and a faint bitterness that defines the Louisiana flavor profile. Skipping the bell pepper produces a Cajun dish that reads off; substituting carrot produces a French-leaning dish that does not read Cajun.

  Pairings. Canonical with a side of buttered cornbread, slaw, or simple green beans cooked with bacon. Louisiana hot sauce (Crystal, Tabasco, Louisiana Brand) at the table. A cold beer (Abita Amber is the local choice) or sweet tea.

  Storage. Keeps 4 days refrigerated; the pegao softens but the dish remains good. Reheat in a covered pan over low heat with a splash of stock. Freezes well; portion into 500 g containers. Does not microwave well; the rice goes gummy.
