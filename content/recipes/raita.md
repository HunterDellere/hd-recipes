steps:
  - text: 'Prep the cucumber. Peel the 200 g cucumber and grate on the large holes of a box grater. Place the grated cucumber in a fine-mesh sieve, sprinkle with ½ tsp salt, toss, and let stand 5 minutes. Squeeze gently with your hands to expel the water (about 60 to 80 ml of cucumber water will release). The squeezing is structural, un-squeezed cucumber waters down the raita as it sits and produces a thin, broken consistency. The squeezed-out cucumber water is fine to drink or to use in a vinaigrette; do not add to the raita.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Toast and grind the cumin (if using whole seeds, the upgrade). Heat a small dry skillet over medium heat. Add 1 tsp / 2.5 g whole cumin seeds. Toast 60 to 90 seconds, swirling, until fragrant and slightly darker. Transfer to a mortar; grind to a coarse powder. The freshly toasted-and-ground cumin is dramatically more aromatic than supermarket pre-ground cumin powder, and the difference matters in a dish where cumin is the dominant aromatic. Pre-ground is acceptable but use 75% of the volume (it is more concentrated than fresh-toasted).'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Whisk the yogurt base. In a medium serving bowl, whisk the 500 g yogurt with the 60 ml water until smooth and glossy. The whisking is structural, un-whisked yogurt has lumps and uneven texture; the whisked-and-thinned yogurt has the smooth pourable consistency that defines raita.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Combine. Add the squeezed grated cucumber, 8 g chopped mint, 8 g chopped cilantro, and 4 g minced green chili (if using) to the yogurt. Add the 3 g toasted cumin, 4 g salt or kala namak, 1 g Kashmiri chili powder, 1 g black pepper, and 2 g sugar. Stir gently to combine. Taste; adjust salt and chili. The raita should taste cooling, faintly herbal, lightly spiced, with the cumin as the dominant aromatic and the cucumber as the structural texture.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Rest and serve. Cover and refrigerate 15 to 30 minutes if possible (the flavors integrate and the raita becomes more cohesive); skip if eating immediately. Sprinkle the surface with an additional small pinch (about 0.5 g) of toasted ground cumin and Kashmiri chili powder for visual elegance just before serving. Serve cold alongside biryani, pulao, kebabs, parathas, or any spicy curry.'
    time_min: 0

modifications:
  - for: 'variation'
    to: 'boondi raita (with crispy chickpea pearls)'
    how: 'Add 60 g (½ cup) boondi (small crisp deep-fried chickpea-flour pearls, sold at Indian sweet shops) to the raita just before serving. The boondi can be soaked in warm water 5 minutes first to soften slightly (the textbook method) or added crisp (the textural-contrast method). Boondi raita is the festive North Indian version, served at Holi, Diwali, and weddings.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'variation'
    to: 'vegetable raita (the Pakistani everyday)'
    how: 'Add 100 g finely diced tomato, 50 g finely diced red onion, and a handful of pomegranate seeds (when in season) to the raita along with the cucumber. The dish becomes more substantial and salad-like; common as the everyday raita in Pakistani households alongside dal and rice. Reduce mint slightly (to 5 g) to let the vegetables read.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'variation'
    to: 'beet raita (chukandar raita)'
    how: 'Replace the cucumber with 200 g grated cooked beet (boiled or roasted). The dish becomes brilliant magenta-pink with an earthy-sweet note from the beet; particularly good with grilled meats and lamb biryani.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'variation'
    to: 'mint-only raita (pudina raita)'
    how: 'Skip the cucumber entirely. Increase mint to 30 g (large handful) blended with the yogurt and 60 ml water in a blender until brilliant green and smooth. Add seasonings as written. The dish becomes a smooth pourable mint-yogurt sauce, closer to an Indian green dipping sauce than to a chunky raita; excellent with kebabs and tandoori chicken.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'temper finish'
    to: 'with tarka (the Pakistani upgrade)'
    how: 'After plating the raita, in a small skillet heat 1 tbsp ghee and bloom ½ tsp cumin seeds + ¼ tsp mustard seeds + 1 dried red chili (broken) for 20 seconds until fragrant. Pour the sizzling tarka over the surface of the raita just before serving. The hot-fat-on-cold-yogurt contrast is visually and texturally dramatic; common in some Pakistani households as the premium-version raita.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Why squeezing the cucumber matters. Un-squeezed grated cucumber holds 60 to 80 ml of water that releases over 30 to 60 minutes after mixing into the yogurt. This produces watery raita with broken texture by the time it reaches the table, especially at warm-weather meals. Squeezing the cucumber before mixing produces a stable raita that holds its texture for hours. The squeezed-out cucumber water can be used in a vinaigrette or simply discarded; do not add to the raita.

  Why toasted cumin is structural. Toasted ground cumin is the dominant aromatic of raita and the spice that distinguishes it from a generic yogurt sauce. Pre-ground supermarket cumin has lost much of its aromatic compounds; the fresh-toasted-and-ground version is dramatically more present in the dish. Ten extra seconds in a dry pan to toast whole seeds is the upgrade that takes raita from acceptable to genuinely good.

  The temperature contrast principle. Raita is structurally a cold dish served alongside hot food; the temperature contrast is part of its function. The yogurt-and-cucumber combination also lowers the perceived heat of spicy biryani, pulao, and curry by binding capsaicin in the dairy fat, this is real biochemistry, not just custom. Casein and milkfat in yogurt sequester capsaicin molecules from the tongue receptors more effectively than water (which spreads capsaicin around but does not bind it).

  Eating mechanics. Raita is part of a complete South Asian meal: a bowl of rice, a curry or dal, a flatbread (roti, naan), and a small bowl or dollop of raita on the side. The diner alternates spoonfuls of curry-and-rice with small spoonfuls of raita; the raita resets the palate between bites of intensely-spiced food. Serving format is a small individual bowl per diner (about 100 ml each) or a larger central bowl that diners spoon from. Refrigerate up to 2 days; the texture becomes slightly thinner and the herbs darken on day 2 but the raita is still perfectly good. Do not freeze (yogurt structure breaks irreversibly).
