steps:
  - text: 'Combine 240 ml / 1 cup whole milk with 15 ml / 1 tbsp white vinegar in a measuring cup or bowl. Stir once and let stand 5 minutes at room temperature. The acid denatures casein micelles, causing visible curdling: that is correct. You have made soured milk that functions identically to buttermilk. Acid content, viscosity, and leavening behavior are equivalent.'
    technique: 'acid-milk-sour'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Whisk together in a large bowl: 150 g / 1¼ cups all-purpose flour, 14 g / 1 tbsp granulated sugar, 5 g / 1¼ tsp baking powder, 3 g / ½ tsp baking soda, and 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt. The baking soda must meet the acid from the soured milk to release CO2; the baking powder provides a second thermal lift in the pan. Both are required.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Whisk the 50 g / 1 large egg into the soured milk. Add 28 g / 2 tbsp melted cooled butter and 4 ml / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste. The butter must not be hot; above ~70 °C it begins to cook the egg proteins and the batter will be lumpy from the outset.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour the wet into the dry. Fold with a spatula, 8 to 10 strokes maximum. Lumps are correct and intentional; overmixing develops gluten and produces tough, flat pancakes. The batter should be barely combined. Let it rest 3 minutes while you heat the pan. The starch granules hydrate and the initial CO2 reaction begins, building the first wave of structure.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Set a wide skillet or griddle over medium heat. Add a ~2 g / small pat of butter and let it melt completely; the foam tells you the water is cooking off. When the foam subsides and the surface just begins to shimmer, the pan is at roughly 175 °C / 350 °F: hot enough for Maillard browning but below the smoke point of butter. Reduce heat slightly if the butter browns before you add batter.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour 60 to 70 ml / ¼ cup batter per pancake. Cook undisturbed until bubbles break the surface and the edges set dry, 2 to 2½ minutes. The bubbles are CO2 migrating to the surface as the interior sets; they signal structure, not just doneness. Flip once with a wide spatula. Cook the second side 1 to 1½ minutes until the center springs back when pressed lightly. Do not press down: you are compressing the gas cells you built.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Wipe the pan between batches and add a fresh small pat of butter (~2 g) each time. Burnt milk solids from the previous batch lower browning temperature and will char the bottom of the next round before the interior sets. Transfer finished pancakes to a 95 °C / 200 °F oven on a wire rack set over a sheet pan; stacking them traps steam and makes them soggy.'
    time_min: 8

notes: |
  The acid-milk technique is not a compromise. Buttermilk is milk acidified by bacteria; you replicate the functional chemistry in 5 minutes by adding a food acid. The two things a recipe needs from buttermilk: (1) acid to react with baking soda and (2) a slightly viscous, protein-disrupted liquid that coats the flour differently than plain milk. Vinegar-soured milk delivers both.

  Baking soda is the acid-dependent leavener; baking powder is the thermally-dependent one. Using both means lift happens in two phases: the first immediately when wet and dry combine (never skip the rest), the second as the pan heats. Skip the baking soda and you lose the first burst and the browning acceleration from raising the batter pH. Skip the baking powder and you lose the in-pan structural set. Both stay in.

  The rest period after mixing is not optional. Three minutes of hydration lets starch granules swell enough to bind water. The batter goes from thin and pourable to slightly gelatinous. That viscosity traps gas bubbles and keeps them from migrating to the surface too early.
