before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 2 to 5 day project from radish to ready-to-eat kkakdugi. Active hands-on time is about 40 minutes; the rest is the salt-draw (30 minutes), the ambient ferment (2 to 4 days), and optional cold-aging.'
  - 'Equipment: a large non-reactive bowl for the salt draw, a 2 L glass jar (or two 1 L jars) with loose-fitting lid or airlock, nitrile gloves for handling the chili paste. Avoid aluminum and untreated cast iron; the chili and salt corrode reactive metal and pick up off-flavors.'
  - 'Water non-chlorinated, salt non-iodized. Same rule as all lacto-fermentation: chlorine kills the lactobacillus, iodine inhibits it. Filtered water and kosher salt (or Korean coarse sea salt) are the safe defaults.'
  - 'Hold the ferment at 18 to 22 °C for 2 to 4 days. Kkakdugi ferments noticeably faster than baechu kimchi because the cubed radish exposes more surface area to the brine and has less cellular structure to break down. Start tasting on day 2.'
  - 'Submersion under brine is non-negotiable. The cubed radish releases enough liquid during fermentation to form its own brine; press down daily to keep all pieces under the surface.'

steps:
  - text: 'Salt-draw the radish. In a large non-reactive bowl, toss the 1500 g radish cubes with the 30 g / 2 tbsp coarse salt and the 15 g / 1 tbsp sugar. Massage gently for 30 seconds so the salt coats every surface. Let stand at room temperature for 30 to 45 minutes; the radish will release a meaningful pool of liquid (about 150 to 250 g) and the cubes will turn translucent at the edges and lose their stiff raw crispness for a more pliable bite. This step is structural; unsalted radish in the kimchi paste will ferment but the cubes will stay starchy-hard and the kimchi will read raw and harsh.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Drain the radish. Pour the radish-and-released-liquid into a colander. Do not rinse; the residual surface salt is part of the kimchi salinity calculation. Press the cubes gently against the colander to expel another 50 to 100 g of liquid (you should hear the cubes squeak against each other when properly drained). Return the drained radish to the bowl. The liquid that drained off can be discarded, or saved for cooking rice (it carries radish flavor and a small amount of salt).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Build the chili paste. In a medium bowl, combine the 50 g / ½ cup + 1 tbsp gochugaru, the 40 g / 2½ tbsp fish sauce, the 15 g / 1 tbsp saeujeot if using, the 25 g microplaned garlic, and the 10 g microplaned ginger. Stir with a wooden spoon or gloved hands; the paste should look like a loose, vivid red sauce. Let stand 10 minutes for the gochugaru to fully hydrate and the paste to thicken slightly into the consistency of loose ketchup.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Toss the radish with the paste. Wearing nitrile gloves, add the chili paste and the 60 g scallion pieces to the bowl with the drained radish. Toss with hands until every cube is uniformly coated, getting the paste into all the corners. The radish should turn a vivid red and the bowl bottom should pool with a small amount of bright-red brine within 1 to 2 minutes of tossing.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Pack the kkakdugi. Transfer the radish into a clean 2 L glass jar (or two 1 L jars), pressing down firmly with a wooden spoon after every cup or so to eliminate air pockets and bring the brine to the surface. Fill the jar to about 80% capacity; the headspace allows for fermentation expansion. Scrape any remaining paste from the bowl over the top. The brine should cover the top layer of cubes within 30 minutes; if it does not, press the cubes down more aggressively or add a splash (30 g / 2 tbsp) of filtered water.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Cap with a loose-fitting lid (a quarter-turn from sealed) or an airlock lid; a fully sealed jar will build CO2 pressure during fermentation and can crack or pop. Place the jar on a small tray to catch any brine that bubbles up during active fermentation. Hold at 18 to 22 °C (room temperature, away from direct sunlight) for 2 to 4 days. Press the radish back down under the brine once daily with a clean spoon.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Taste starting on day 2. Early kkakdugi reads salty and chili-forward with no sour character; properly fermented kkakdugi reads sour, fizzy, and complex with a clear back-of-throat tang and an audible crunch when bitten. Most home cooks like day 3 for the balance. Transfer to the refrigerator (2 to 4 °C) once the kkakdugi has reached the desired sourness; fermentation slows dramatically but continues to develop complexity over the next 2 to 6 weeks.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The salt-draw difference from baechu kimchi. Whole napa cabbage gets a 7% brine for 6 to 8 hours; cubed radish gets a 2% dry-salt draw for 30 to 45 minutes. The mechanism is the same (osmotic water draw plus selective bacterial pressure) but the geometry is different. The high surface-area cubed form draws water and absorbs salt many times faster than the intact leaf-and-rib structure of cabbage, so the salt percentage and the time both come down proportionally. Over-drawing the radish (more than 90 minutes or more than 4% salt) produces flabby cubes with no crunch.

  Why kkakdugi ferments faster. The cubed form exposes radish flesh to the lactobacillus on all six surfaces, where whole cabbage only ferments at the leaf-to-leaf interfaces. Combined with the lower starting salt (the lactobacillus is less inhibited at 2 to 2.5% than at 7%) and the higher sugar content of radish (compared to cabbage), kkakdugi typically hits the sour stage in 2 to 3 days versus 4 to 6 days for baechu kimchi.

  Why the radish stays crunchy. The salt-draw collapses the radish cells' turgor pressure (releasing the bound water in the vacuoles) without breaking the cell walls, which produces a pliable-but-firm texture that holds its crunch through weeks of fermentation. Unsalted radish in the chili paste would ferment but the cells would over-soften, producing mushy kkakdugi by day 3. The 30-minute salt draw is the structural step that distinguishes proper kkakdugi from a quick radish pickle.

  Service. Kkakdugi is the canonical banchan for seollongtang (Korean ox-bone soup) and other rich beef broths; the crunch and acid cut through the fat. Also standard alongside grilled meats (galbi, samgyupsal) and in mixed banchan spreads. The brine that accumulates in the jar is drinkable and reads cooling-and-slightly-fizzy; some Korean cooks drink it directly from a shot glass as a digestive.

  Storage. Refrigerated kkakdugi keeps 4 to 6 months and remains safe indefinitely, though the texture degrades after about 8 weeks. Use over-fermented kkakdugi in kimchi fried rice or kimchi jjigae; the breakdown in texture is fine for cooked applications.

  Salt math. The 2% dry-salt draw plus the 40 g fish sauce (about 12 g salt contribution) plus the 15 g saeujeot (about 4 g salt) puts the finished kkakdugi at about 2 to 2.5% salinity, which is the canonical Korean range. Skipping the saeujeot drops it slightly below; the recipe substitution (add 10 g fish sauce) recovers the gap.

  Failure modes. Mushy texture: the salt-draw was too long or the radish was old and starchy; eat sooner or use for cooked applications. White film on the brine: kahm yeast (harmless but off-flavored); skim, press the cubes back down, and add a splash of fresh brine. No fizz after 4 days: the room is too cold or the salt was iodized; move to a warmer spot or restart.
