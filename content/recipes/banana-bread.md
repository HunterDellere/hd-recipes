notes: |
  Why brown butter, not melted butter: melting butter gives you fat and water; browning it adds the Maillard-toasted milk solids that read as caramel and roasted nut. In a quick bread that has no other source of deep browned flavor, this is the difference between "fine" and "memorable."

  Why bananas must be overripe: green starch tastes of nothing, and even fully yellow bananas have not produced the aromatic ester suite. Fully spotted to black-skinned fruit has converted starch to sugar, raised free amino acids, and built the aromatic profile that survives a 55-minute bake. This is not aesthetic preference; it is which compounds are in the fruit. If yours are not ripe, the cheat is the 150 °C / 20-minute pre-bake of the whole unpeeled bananas; the heat finishes the conversion enzymatically.

  Why baking soda alone, not powder: the bananas are acidic enough (pH 4.5–5.2) to fully activate baking soda without any additional acid. Soda is also alkaline enough to push more browning on the loaf surface during the bake: you get a darker, more caramelized crust with soda than with powder.

  Why don''t over-stir: rice-flour batters get away with whisking because they have no gluten. Wheat-flour quick breads do, and 30 seconds of overmixing develops it. The lumpy, semi-mixed look of a properly folded quick-bread batter is not laziness; it is the texture insurance for the next 55 minutes in the oven.
