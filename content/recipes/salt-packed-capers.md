before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 30 to 60-day dry-salt cure, not a recipe you finish today. Active hands-on time is about 60 minutes spread across the initial pack, a re-pack at week 1, and a final transfer at the end.'
  - 'Source fresh tight caper buds, picked at first light, all closed. Open flowers and overlarge buds cure into soft floral fragments rather than the crisp glucocapparin-mellowed buds the technique is built for. Italian or Greek specialty importers, or your own caper plant in summer, are the realistic channels.'

  - 'Find a stable cool dry spot at 15 to 20 °C for the cure window. A pantry or cupboard works; humidity above 70 percent invites mold on the salt crust.'

steps:
  - text: 'Sort the 300 g caper buds by size. Discard any open buds (yellowed, splitting, or visibly flowering; these are too far along the bud-to-flower curve and read soft after curing). The most prized grade is non-pareille (under 7 mm); the next is surfines (7 to 8 mm); larger sizes (capucines, capotes, communes) are progressively less prized but still cure successfully. Sort into size groups if you intend to grade the finished product.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Spread the sorted 300 g buds in a single layer on a clean tray and let them wilt at room temperature for 24 hours. This initial wilting is non-negotiable: raw caper buds contain glucocapparin (a glucosinolate that hydrolyzes to methyl-isothiocyanate when crushed), and the wilting begins partial degradation of the glucocapparin, mellowing the harshness that makes raw capers nearly inedible. The wilted bud should feel slightly leathery rather than turgid; the color shifts from grey-green to dusty olive.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Layer the wilted buds with the 200 g coarse sea salt in a non-reactive jar (glass with a wide-mouth lid is ideal). Alternate thin layers: 5 mm of salt at the bottom, then a single layer of buds, then salt covering the layer fully, then more buds, and so on, ending with salt on top. Press lightly between layers so no air pockets remain. Cover loosely (a paper-towel-and-rubber-band cover works; a tight-sealed lid traps off-gases from the early-stage fermentation and produces off-flavors).'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Hold at room temperature (18 to 24 °C) for 10 days. Brine forms naturally as the salt draws moisture from the buds; by day 5 the bud layer is partially submerged in its own expressed liquid. On day 10, drain off the accumulated brine completely. The buds will have shrunk by roughly 30 percent and feel firmer and saltier when tasted (do not eat raw at this stage; the cure is still in mid-progression and the flavor will read flat and over-salty).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Re-pack with the additional 100 g coarse sea salt, replacing what dissolved into the drained brine. Layer the same way (salt-bud-salt) and seal the jar. Hold at room temperature for an additional 30 days, or refrigerated for 60 days, for the cure to complete. The buds shrink further, firm, and develop the layered floral-mustard-savory flavor of properly cured capers; the color stabilizes at dusty olive-grey.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Pack for storage: transfer the cured buds to a clean glass jar with a fresh thin coat of dry salt (just enough to cover and exclude air), seal, and store at room temperature in a cool dark cabinet. The cure is shelf-stable for 1 to 2 years; some IGP-grade Italian preparations age for 6 months in a cool cellar before sale to develop further depth. To use, scoop out the desired quantity and soak in cold water for 10 minutes with one or two water changes to leach the surface salt; taste-test before adding to dishes and adjust the dish-level salt downward to compensate.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  The glucosinolate chemistry. Caper buds contain glucocapparin, a glucosinolate compound stored in the bud as a defense against herbivory. When the bud is crushed (or chewed raw), the enzyme myrosinase hydrolyzes glucocapparin into methyl-isothiocyanate, the same compound family that gives wasabi, mustard, and horseradish their pungent burn. Raw capers are too harsh to eat in any quantity because of this. The salt cure works in two ways: (1) the wilting and salt-dehydration phase deactivates myrosinase by removing free water and denaturing the enzyme, slowing or stopping further isothiocyanate production; (2) the long-cure phase allows partial spontaneous degradation of glucocapparin into milder breakdown products (methyl glucosinolate, sinigrin-like compounds) that retain the mustard pungency at a tolerable level. The result is a bud that retains the bright peppery-mustard finish without the eye-watering harshness of the raw product.

  The size grading. Caper bud sizing is the same grading system used for capers commercially: non-pareille (under 7 mm; the most prized), surfines (7 to 8 mm), capucines (9 to 11 mm), capotes (11 to 13 mm), and communes (over 13 mm). Smaller buds have a more concentrated flavor density and a more pleasing bite size for finishing dishes; larger buds are workhorses for chopped sauces (puttanesca, tartare, salsa verde). Sort during the wilt stage and cure each size group separately if you want to use them at their highest grade.

  The pre-wilting rationale. Skipping the 24-hour wilt produces a cure that takes much longer to reach drinkable harshness — the myrosinase remains active for the first several days of the salt cure, and the methyl-isothiocyanate it produces creates a harsh, almost burning quality that takes months to subside. The wilt is the cheap shortcut that knocks the harsh enzyme out of action before the long cure begins.

  Failure modes. Too little salt (under 50 percent of bud weight): allows microbial spoilage, particularly lactobacilli that produce a sour-yeast off-character, and pseudomonads that produce a slimy surface. Too high humidity during cure: the buds soften and weep, producing a mushy product that loses the crisp bite. Sun exposure during cure: bleaches color from olive-grey to dusty white and degrades the volatile aromatic compounds; cure in a cool dark cabinet only. Insufficient pre-wilt: produces a cure that retains harsh isothiocyanate burn for 6 to 12 months before mellowing; not a failure, but a slow batch.

  Sourcing fresh caper buds. Caper plants (Capparis spinosa) are perennial Mediterranean shrubs that grow as woody vines on rocky walls and dry slopes. In the US they grow in California and parts of the Southwest where the climate is dry and sunny enough; commercial production exists in California (Sonoma and Mendocino counties) but is limited and rarely retail-distributed. Fresh caper buds are essentially impossible to source at US supermarkets. This recipe is for foragers, home gardeners who grow the plant as an ornamental and harvest the buds, or those with access to a Mediterranean direct supplier. The shelf-stable salt-packed product is widely available commercially; the homemade version is a niche project for those with the access.

  Use. Salt-packed capers are the most prized form of caper for finishing dishes — vitello tonnato, tartare sauce, salsa verde, putanesca, lemon-and-caper chicken, fish piccata. Always rinse and soak briefly before use (10 minutes in cold water with one or two water changes; longer soak for larger size grades). The bud holds its shape better than brined-jar capers and contributes a more concentrated mustard-floral pop. Chopped fine for sauces; whole for visual presentation on plated dishes.
