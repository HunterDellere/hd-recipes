before_you_start:
  - 'Plan a full afternoon. The active work is 90 minutes spread across chhena making, syrup poach, and milk reduction; the dish then needs at least 2 to 3 hours chilled in the malai to absorb the saffron-cardamom flavor before serving. Total clock time to first bite is closer to 5 hours.'
  - 'Source HTST-pasteurized whole milk, not ultra-pasteurized (UHT). UHT milk coagulates into soft, broken curds that cannot be kneaded into the smooth dough rasmalai requires. Most American organic and shelf-stable whole milks are UHT; check the carton.'
  - 'Bloom the saffron 10 minutes ahead in 2 tbsp warm milk pulled from the malai. The carotenoids need time to dissolve, dumped straight in dry the malai reads weakly perfumed and pale.'
  - 'Have two pots running in parallel during the second half: the wide sugar-syrup pot for poaching the dumplings, and a separate heavy-bottomed pot reducing the malai. Both need attention at the same time.'

source:
  name: 'Bengali / North Indian sweet shop tradition'
  note: 'Ras malai (রসমালাই, রস = juice/syrup, মালাই = clotted cream) was developed in Bengal in the late 19th century at K.C. Das, the Calcutta sweet shop credited with the invention of rasgulla and many of its derivatives. The dish migrated across India and Pakistan during the 20th century and is now a staple at Hindu and Muslim weddings, festival celebrations, and sweet shops across the subcontinent.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the chhena. Bring the 2000 ml / 8 cups whole milk to a strong simmer (just below boiling, around 90 to 95°C / 195 to 200°F) in a heavy 4-quart pot, stirring occasionally to prevent scorching. As soon as the milk reaches near-boil, pull off heat. Stir in the 30 ml lemon juice slowly, in 2 to 3 additions, stirring gently between each. The milk will visibly curdle within 30 to 60 seconds: the casein will coagulate into white curds, the whey will separate into a faintly yellow liquid. If the curdle is incomplete after 60 seconds, add 1 more tbsp lemon juice; if the whey is murky and white (incomplete separation), add another tbsp.'
    technique: 'acid-milk-sour'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Strain and rinse the chhena. Line a large fine-mesh sieve with cheesecloth set over a deep bowl. Pour the curdled milk through. The whey drains through into the bowl below; the chhena curds collect in the cheesecloth. Pour 1500 ml / 6 cups cold water through the chhena to rinse off the lemon flavor and stop the coagulation; the cold water also firms the curds. Lift the cheesecloth, gather into a bundle, and squeeze gently to remove excess water. The chhena should be just-damp, holding shape, neither dripping wet nor dry-crumbly. Hang the bundle (tied to a faucet handle or wooden spoon over a bowl) for 30 minutes to drain further. The discarded whey is excellent in chapati dough or for cooking rice; do not pour down the drain unless you have no use for it.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Knead the chhena. Transfer the drained chhena to a clean countertop or large plate. Knead with the heel of your palm for 5 to 7 minutes; the chhena will go from crumbly-grainy to smooth-elastic-shiny. The kneading is structural — under-kneaded chhena produces ras malai dumplings that fall apart in the syrup; over-kneaded chhena produces dense, tough dumplings. The texture target is "smooth, slightly tacky, holding together when pressed but yielding under fingertip pressure." A small amount of palm-warmth releases the milkfat in the chhena and binds it; this is why hand-kneading is required, not food processor.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Shape the dumplings. Divide the kneaded chhena into 16 equal pieces (about 18 to 20 g each). Roll each piece between your palms into a smooth ball, then flatten gently into a disc roughly 4 cm in diameter and 1.5 cm thick. The flattening is the ras malai signature, round dumplings would be rasgulla, the precursor dish. Place the discs on a plate; do not stack.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Make the sugar syrup. In a wide saucepan or shallow Dutch oven (the syrup must accommodate all 16 dumplings in a single layer with room to puff), combine the 200 g sugar and 1200 ml / 5 cups water. Bring to a strong simmer over medium-high heat. The syrup should be active-bubbling but not violent.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Cook the dumplings. Gently lower the chhena discs into the simmering syrup using a slotted spoon. Cover with a tight lid (the cover is essential, the steam helps the dumplings puff). Cook 15 minutes at a strong simmer; the dumplings will puff dramatically (doubling in size), float to the surface, and turn slightly translucent at the edges. Do not lift the lid in the first 10 minutes (releasing steam can cause the dumplings to deflate and toughen). At minute 12, briefly check; if the dumplings are not yet doubled in size, recover and continue 3 minutes.'
    time_min: 16
  - text: 'Make the malai (the milk soak). While the dumplings cook, in a separate heavy saucepan, bring the 1000 ml / 4 cups whole milk for the malai to a low simmer over medium heat. Reduce, stirring every minute, for 12 to 15 minutes; the milk will reduce by approximately one-third, thickening slightly to a half-cream consistency. Pull off heat. Add the 100 g sugar, the crushed cardamom seeds, and the steeped saffron-milk. Stir until the sugar dissolves. Let cool 10 minutes (the reduced milk should be warm-not-hot when the dumplings join it).'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Transfer dumplings. Lift the cooked chhena dumplings out of the sugar syrup with a slotted spoon. Gently squeeze each dumpling between two spoons to expel the absorbed sugar syrup (the dumplings will deflate slightly; this is correct, the deflation is what creates the porosity that lets the dumplings absorb the malai). Drop each squeezed dumpling into the warm malai milk. The dumplings will reabsorb the malai over the next several hours.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Refrigerate and rest. Cover the bowl of dumplings-in-malai. Refrigerate at least 3 hours, ideally 6 to 8 hours or overnight. The dumplings absorb the malai progressively; the longer the soak, the more saturated they become with the cardamom-saffron flavor. The dish is genuinely better after an overnight rest.'
    time_min: 180
  - text: 'Plate and serve chilled. Spoon 2 dumplings per serving into small individual bowls. Pour over enough malai to barely submerge the dumplings (about 60 to 80 ml per portion). Garnish with slivered pistachios, almonds, and a small scatter of dried rose petals if using. Serve cold; ras malai is one of the few subcontinental sweets that is structurally a chilled dessert. The dumplings should be soft and slightly sponge-like, releasing malai when pressed by a spoon.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why HTST milk, not UHT, for chhena. Ultra-pasteurized (UHT) milk has been heated to 138°C / 280°F during processing, which partially denatures the casein proteins. UHT milk does not coagulate cleanly with acid; the curds are smaller, softer, and the whey is murky-white instead of clear yellow. Use standard high-temperature short-time (HTST) pasteurized whole milk for chhena; UHT will produce wrong-textured dumplings. Read the carton.

  The flatten-not-sphere structure. Round chhena dumplings (rasgulla) and flat chhena dumplings (ras malai) are the same dough cooked the same way, the only difference is the shape. Flattening the dough into discs creates more surface area for the malai to soak into, and produces the characteristic puff-and-deflate-and-absorb behavior that defines ras malai. Cooks who skip the flattening step end up with rasgullas in milk, which is a different and less-good dish.

  The squeeze-and-soak transfer. After the dumplings cook in syrup and puff, they are squeezed gently between two spoons to expel the absorbed syrup, then dropped into the warm malai. The squeezing is structural, under-squeezed dumplings stay sugar-syrup-saturated and never absorb the malai; over-squeezed dumplings collapse into dense pucks. The right pressure: gentle, just enough to see clear syrup pearling out, the dumpling deflating about 30%. Practice on the first one or two; the technique becomes obvious.

  The overnight soak is the dish. Ras malai eaten immediately after assembly is a fine but underwhelming dessert; the dumplings have not yet absorbed the malai meaningfully. After 6 to 8 hours, the dumplings are saturated with the cardamom-saffron-pistachio milk and the dish reaches its full character. Plan ahead, make the dumplings and malai the day before, refrigerate overnight, serve the next day.

  Eating mechanics. Ras malai is a wedding-and-festival sweet: served at Hindu weddings, Muslim Eid celebrations, Pakistani-Indian birthdays and anniversaries, and most Sunday lunches in households where Sunday is the cooking day. Served in small individual bowls, eaten cold with a spoon, often as the closing sweet of an elaborate meal. Keeps refrigerated 4 to 5 days; the dumplings continue to absorb malai and may need additional milk added on day 3 or 4 to maintain the soup-like serving consistency. Freezes acceptably for 1 month; thaws to slightly altered texture but recognizably ras malai.
