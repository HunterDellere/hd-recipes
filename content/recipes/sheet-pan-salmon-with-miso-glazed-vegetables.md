desc: 'Skin-on salmon fillets and a mix of broccoli florets and bok choy halves on a single sheet pan, brushed with a miso-honey glaze that caramelizes under the broiler in the final 3 minutes. before_you_start:
  - 'Preheat the oven to 220 °C with a heavy sheet pan inside for the full 12 minutes. The screaming-hot pan starts Maillard on the broccoli within seconds of contact; a cold pan steams the florets and the glaze never caramelizes.'
  - 'Pull the salmon from the refrigerator 15 minutes before cooking and pat the skin and flesh side dry with paper towels. Cold wet salmon releases water onto the pan and prevents the skin from crisping; room-temperature dry salmon cooks evenly to the medium-rare interior the dish is calibrated for.'
  - 'Vegetables go on first and roast 10 minutes before the salmon joins them. The 8-minute salmon window converges with the broccoli reaching tender-crisp; if you load everything at once, the salmon overcooks or the vegetables undercook.'
  - 'Mix the miso-honey glaze before anything hits the oven. Once the salmon is on the pan, the glaze brush-on happens in 30 seconds while the broiler preheats.'

  - for: 'Pulling salmon at 49 to 52°C / 120 to 125°F internal (translucent ruby, just-set) rather than the USDA-published 145°F target'
    ref: 'safety/seafood-doneness#cooking-targets-for-fin-fish'
    why: 'Fatty fish like salmon turn dry, chalky, and fall-apart past 130°F internal; the cooking tradition in Japan, France, and modern chef-driven American kitchens targets 120-125°F for medium-rare salmon, which is below USDA conservative published-safe. Source from a fishmonger handling sashimi-grade or properly parasite-cleared aquacultured Atlantic salmon. Vulnerable diners (pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, very old) should be served salmon cooked to 145°F (an additional 3 to 4 minutes under the broiler), accepting the texture loss.'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 220 °C / 425 °F with a heavy half-sheet pan inside on the middle rack. The pan needs 12 minutes of preheat. While the oven heats, mix the glaze: in a small bowl, whisk together the 60 g / ¼ cup white miso, the 30 g / 1½ tbsp honey, the 15 g / 1 tbsp rice vinegar, the 8 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame oil, the 8 g / 2 tsp microplaned ginger, and the 4 g / 1 clove microplaned garlic until smooth.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Cut the 500 g broccoli into 3 cm florets with stem attached; peel and slice any tough lower stem into 1 cm coins. Halve the 400 g baby bok choy lengthwise through the root. In a wide bowl, toss the broccoli and bok choy with the 25 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil and the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt until every surface glistens.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Pat the 600 g salmon fillets thoroughly dry on both sides with paper towels, including the skin. Season the flesh side (not the skin) with the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt. Let rest at room temperature while the oven preheats; cold salmon shocks the pan and steams.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pull the preheated sheet pan from the oven. Tip the dressed 900 g of vegetables onto it in a single layer with bok choy cut-side down on the pan; the broccoli florets fill the gaps. The pan should sizzle audibly. Roast 10 minutes without disturbing.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'After 10 minutes, push the vegetables to the edges to make four bare spots in the center of the pan. Lay the 600 g salmon fillets skin-side down on the bare pan in those spots, hot pan to skin direct contact. Brush the tops and sides of each fillet generously with the miso-honey glaze (use about half the glaze for this; reserve the rest). Drizzle a teaspoon of the glaze over the vegetables and toss them in place with a spatula.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Return the pan to the oven for 6 minutes. Then switch the oven to broil on high (with the pan on the same rack), brush a second coat of glaze over the salmon tops, and broil 2 to 3 minutes until the glaze caramelizes into a deep amber lacquer and the salmon flakes when prodded gently at the thickest point. Internal temperature should be 49 to 52 °C / 120 to 125 °F for medium-rare; the salmon carries to 54 °C / 130 °F off the heat.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Pull the pan, let rest 2 minutes. Transfer to plates: one salmon fillet, a portion of broccoli and bok choy, with any caramelized glaze scraped from the pan. Drizzle a teaspoon of the reserved miso-honey glaze over each portion. Scatter the 25 g sliced scallions and the 5 g toasted sesame seeds across the plates. Serve with the 60 g lime wedges (one per plate) for squeezing at the table; the acid cuts the rich miso and is the finishing seasoning.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why miso-honey caramelizes under the broiler. White miso is roughly 30% sugar by dry weight (a mix of rice maltose from the koji ferment and residual carbohydrates from the soybeans), and honey adds another concentrated sugar load. Under direct broiler heat at ~260 °C / 500 °F, both sugars hit caramelization temperature within 90 seconds and the surface goes from glossy painted-on glaze to deep-amber Maillard-and-caramel lacquer. The reaction is non-linear: nothing visible happens for the first minute, then the surface darkens noticeably over 30 seconds. Watch the pan during the broil; salmon goes from perfectly glazed to scorched in 60 seconds.

  Why the salmon goes on late. Salmon cooks fast at 220 °C, about 8 minutes total for a 2.5 cm fillet to medium-rare. Broccoli and bok choy need 18 minutes at the same temperature to hit tender-crisp with caramelization. Loading everything at once means either undercooked vegetables or overcooked fish. The 10-minute vegetable head start brings both elements to doneness at the same moment, with the broiler stage adding the final caramelization on the glaze.

  The skin-on salmon question. Skin-on fillets are structurally easier on this dish: the skin protects the bottom of the salmon from direct pan contact, prevents the flesh from sticking and tearing on transfer, and crisps into a textural element that some eaters prize. Skinless works but cooks one minute faster and is more vulnerable to overcooking. The skin is also where most of the omega-3 fatty acids concentrate; eat it.

  Why rice vinegar in the glaze, lime at the end. Two acid sources at two different timing points. The rice vinegar in the glaze cooks down and provides a structural acid that balances the miso''s salt-and-sweet during the bake. The lime squeezed at the table brings volatile aromatic acid (limonene, citral) that flashes on the warm fish and is the bright top note. Using only one would leave the dish either flat (vinegar only, cooked-off aromatics) or harsh (lime only, no integrated acid balance).

  Make-ahead and storage. The glaze keeps 2 weeks refrigerated in a glass jar. The cooked dish holds 2 days refrigerated; reheat in a 160 °C / 325 °F oven for 8 minutes, the salmon will dry out somewhat but stays edible. The bowl-friendly version: dice cooked leftovers, serve cold over rice with extra glaze drizzled and herbs, eats like a salmon poke bowl rather than reheated dinner.
