steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 180 °C / 350 °F. While it preheats, warm the 60 g / ¼ cup extra-virgin olive oil with the 12 g / 3 smashed garlic cloves in a small saucepan over the lowest possible heat for 8 minutes. The oil should never come to a sizzle; the goal is to extract garlic aromatics into the oil at sub-Maillard temperature so the cloves do not turn bitter. Pull off heat, fish out the garlic and discard.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Toss the 200 g / 7 oz torn sourdough chunks with the strained garlic oil and the 2 g / ⅓ tsp fine sea salt in a wide bowl until the bread has absorbed every drop. Spread on a sheet tray in a single layer with no chunks touching. Bake 12 to 14 minutes, tossing once at 7 minutes, until the chunks are uniformly golden and dry through to the centre. Pull and let cool on the tray; they crisp further as they lose residual steam.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Bring a small saucepan of water to a bare simmer (around 88 °C / 190 °F, surface trembling, no rolling bubbles). Lower the 2 fridge-cold eggs in with a slotted spoon and hold for 90 seconds. The 90-second coddle pasteurizes the surface of the egg and partially denatures the outer yolk proteins, which thickens the dressing without setting the yolk fully; the yolk should still pour. Lift the eggs straight into a bowl of cold tap water for 60 seconds to stop carryover cooking.'
    technique: 'jammy-egg'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Crack each egg over a fine-mesh sieve set over a small mixing bowl; the white drains away and the warm yolk drops in. Discard the whites (or save for another use). You should have about 35 g of warm coddled yolk in the bowl, the colour deepening from raw yellow to opaque cream.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Build the water phase first. Whisk the 18 g / 6 minced anchovy fillets, 6 g grated garlic, 8 g / 1½ tsp dijon mustard, 20 g / 4 tsp lemon juice, and 5 g / 1 tsp worcestershire sauce into the warm yolks until the anchovy fully dissolves into a uniform pale-bronze paste with no visible fillet shreds. Dissolved anchovy is non-negotiable: visible chunks read as fishy hot spots; fully dissolved, it reads only as deep glutamate savor.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Stream in the 90 g / 6 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil in a thin thread while whisking vigorously. The yolk lecithin and the dijon mucilage coat each oil droplet as it enters; the dressing thickens visibly from a thin slick to a glossy, body-having sauce that coats the back of a spoon. The egg-yolk emulsion is more powerful and holds longer than a pure mustard vinaigrette; this is the structural reason Caesar dressing has its characteristic clinging body. If the emulsion stays thin and oily-streaked, you streamed the oil too fast; see the rebuild note in the technique reference.'
    technique: 'egg-yolk-emulsion'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Whisk in the first 30 g / ⅓ cup grated parmigiano and the 1 g / ½ tsp cracked black pepper. The cheese tightens the dressing further; if it now reads too thick (it should pour from a spoon, not stand on it), thin with a teaspoon of water at a time. Taste: it should land salty from anchovy and parmigiano, sharply acidic from lemon, with a low rumbling sweetness from worcestershire and the long heat-cured note of dissolved anchovy. If a single axis falls short, add to that axis specifically: more lemon for brightness, more anchovy for depth, more parmigiano for body.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'In a wide bowl that holds the romaine without crowding, dress the 500 g / 1 lb 2 oz cold dried romaine with about three quarters of the dressing. Toss with two large spoons or clean hands for a full 60 seconds; you want the dressing inside every fold of every leaf, not pooled at the base of the bowl. Add the rest of the dressing only if leaves are still under-coated. Excess dressing wilts the romaine within 10 minutes; calibrate generously but not heavily.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the cooled croutons and toss 15 seconds more, long enough to integrate, short enough that the croutons stay crisp rather than soaking through. Transfer to a wide platter, scatter the 30 g / ¼ cup parmigiano shavings across the top, and crack a final pass of black pepper over the surface. Serve immediately; a Caesar held more than 5 minutes between dressing and eating is a different, lesser dish.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The structural argument: Caesar dressing is an egg-yolk emulsion, not a vinaigrette. The yolk's lecithin is a far more powerful emulsifier than dijon mucilage alone, and that is what gives the dressing its characteristic body. A Caesar built on dijon-and-oil without yolk reads thin and oily-streaked; with yolk it clings to every crease of every romaine leaf.

  The coddle is the safety phase, not a flavor phase. 90 seconds at 88 °C / 190 °F holds the egg long enough to denature surface salmonella proteins (the FDA-recognized pasteurization curve is 60 °C / 140 °F for 3.5 minutes, or 70 °C / 158 °F for 15 seconds; the coddle method is somewhere in between and is the kitchen-practical approach). It also lightly thickens the yolk, which gives the cook a firmer base for the emulsion than a fully raw yolk does. If you are sourcing genuinely fresh, low-bacterial-load eggs from a known producer, raw yolk works and is the original Cardini method; coddling is the Western-restaurant safety adaptation.

  Anchovy must dissolve. The single most common Caesar failure is leaving visible anchovy fillet shreds in the finished dressing; cooks who hate the dish almost always hate this version. Mince to a paste against the cutting board with the side of a knife, smear it back and forth until it is uniform, then whisk into the warm yolks where the heat helps the proteins disperse. A fully dissolved fillet reads as deep glutamate savor with no fishy character; a chunk reads as fishy hot spot.

  The umami stack is layered: anchovy (free glutamate plus IMP nucleotides from cured fish protein), parmigiano (glutamate plus IMP from aged casein), worcestershire (anchovy plus tamarind plus aged molasses, glutamate plus 5'-nucleotides), dijon (sulfur compounds, secondary). The synergy between glutamate and IMP nucleotides multiplies perceived savory depth by 7 to 8 times the sum of the individual ingredients (the Yamaguchi-Ninomiya umami synergy curve); this is why Caesar dressing punches harder than the ingredient list suggests.

  The romaine must be cold and dry. Warm romaine wilts as soon as the room-temperature dressing meets it; wet romaine produces a watery dressing pool at the bottom of the bowl. Spin dry, hold on a towel-lined tray in the fridge until you are ready to dress, then dress and eat within 5 minutes.

  No mayonnaise as a shortcut. Restaurant Caesars that build the dressing from a base of mayonnaise (the inexcusable but common shortcut) lose the lemon-and-anchovy brightness and read flat. The whole point of building from coddled yolk is that the emulsion is live — the dressing is its own thing, not flavored mayonnaise.
