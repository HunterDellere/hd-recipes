title: 'Pide'
desc: 'The Turkish boat flatbread: a soft enriched yeasted dough rolled into long ovals, edges turned up and pinched at the ends into the canonical canoe shape, generously topped with seasoned ground lamb-onion-pepper mixture (kıymalı pide, the classic), then baked at the highest oven heat on a preheated stone or steel until the dough puffs at the rim and the topping browns into the dough surface. The structural distinction from Italian pizza: the dough is enriched (egg, yogurt, butter), the shape is a boat with raised edges containing the topping, the topping is meat-or-cheese-based with no tomato sauce layer underneath, and the bake is fast and very hot.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The dough needs a 90 minute bulk rise. Make the dough first, set it to rise, then prep the topping while it works.'
  - 'Preheat the oven and a baking stone or steel for at least 45 minutes at the maximum oven temperature (260°C / 500°F or higher if your oven allows). The bake is structural; cold ovens produce flat, pale pide that miss the puffed-rim, blistered-bottom signature.'
  - 'Pide is best fresh from the oven. Stagger the bakes so each one hits the table 2 minutes after coming out; the rim softens within 10 minutes.'

source:
  name: 'The Turkish bakery canon. Pide is documented across Turkey from the Ottoman period; the kıymalı (ground meat) and peynirli (cheese) versions are the two most common, with regional toppings (pastırma, sucuk, kuşbaşı / cubed meat) widely available.'
  note: 'Pide is structurally distinct from lahmacun (thinner, no raised edge, no fold) and from Italian pizza (no tomato sauce, the dough is enriched, the shape is a boat). The closest cousin is the Georgian khachapuri (also a boat-shaped enriched flatbread, but with an egg-and-cheese filling rather than meat). The version here is kıymalı pide, the canonical ground-lamb topping; the cheese version (peynirli pide) is in modifications.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the dough: in a large bowl, whisk together the 400 g / 3⅓ cups bread flour, 4 g / 1¼ tsp instant yeast, 6 g / 1½ tsp sugar, and 7 g / 1¼ tsp fine sea salt. In a separate bowl, whisk together the 180 g / ¾ cup warm milk, 50 g / ¼ cup yogurt, 50 g / 1 egg, and 30 g / 2 tbsp melted butter. Add the wet to the dry; mix with a wooden spoon until shaggy, then turn onto a clean surface and knead 8 to 10 minutes until smooth, elastic, and tacky but not sticky. (Or knead 6 minutes in a stand mixer with the dough hook.) The dough should pass the windowpane test: a small piece stretched between fingers should thin to translucent without immediately tearing.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Place the dough in a bowl lightly oiled with the 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil; turn to coat. Cover with a damp towel and rise at warm room temperature 90 minutes, until doubled and visibly puffed.'
    technique: 'bulk-rise'
    time_min: 90
  - text: 'While the dough rises, make the topping: in a large bowl, combine the 400 g ground lamb, 200 g / 1 onion (diced), 100 g / ½ bell pepper (diced), 100 g / 1 small tomato (diced), 20 g chopped parsley, 15 g / 1 tbsp tomato paste, 3 g / 1½ tsp pul biber, 2 g / 1 tsp cumin, 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt, and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper. Mix with your hands until uniform; the texture should be wet, loose, and pourable (not a tight meatball mixture; the topping needs to spread thin on the dough). Refrigerate until ready to top.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Preheat the oven to its maximum temperature (260°C / 500°F or higher) with a baking stone or steel on the middle rack for at least 45 minutes. The stone must be fully heat-soaked or the bottom of the pide will not crisp.'
    time_min: 45
  - text: 'Divide the risen dough into 4 equal portions (each about 175 g). Shape each portion into a tight ball; rest 10 minutes covered (this relaxes the gluten so the dough rolls thin without snapping back). On a lightly floured surface, roll one ball into a long oval about 30 cm long and 12 cm wide and 4 mm thick. The pide shape is twice as long as it is wide; aim for a long canoe outline.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Spread one-quarter of the meat topping (about 240 g) over the dough, leaving a 1.5 cm border bare around the edges. Press the topping flat with the back of a spoon so it is in even contact with the dough; thick mounds bake unevenly and the dough underneath stays raw. Fold the long edges of the dough up over the topping border by about 1 cm to form the boat walls; pinch the two short ends together firmly to form the pointed boat tips. Brush the visible dough rim with the beaten 18 g / 1 yolk-and-milk wash.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Transfer the shaped pide to a sheet of parchment, slide onto the preheated stone, and bake 10 to 12 minutes until the rim is deep golden-mahogany and the topping has browned and bubbled (the meat surface should look dry and crisped at the edges, not wet and gray). Repeat with the remaining 3 portions; keep finished pide warm under foil while you bake the rest.'
    technique: 'high-heat-bake'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Brush each hot pide rim with the 30 g / 2 tbsp melted butter (split across the 4 pide). Scatter the 8 g / 2 tbsp chopped parsley over the hot tops. Cut into 4 to 5 cross-section pieces, serve immediately with lemon wedges for squeezing over the meat layer; the acid is structural for cutting the lamb richness.'
    time_min: 2

modifications:
  - for: 'peynirli pide (cheese version)'
    to: 'with cheese-and-egg filling instead of meat'
    how: 'Skip the kıymalı topping entirely. Mix 300 g grated kaşar (or low-moisture mozzarella) with 200 g crumbled feta (or beyaz peynir), 100 g chopped parsley, and 1 beaten egg. Divide among the 4 pide. Bake the same way. In the last 2 minutes of the bake, crack a whole egg into a small well in the center of each pide; close the oven and bake until the white is just set (about 2 minutes). The result is peynirli pide, the canonical cheese version, often served at breakfast.'
    kind: 'protein'
  - for: 'kuşbaşı pide (cubed meat version)'
    to: 'with cubed lamb rather than ground'
    how: 'Substitute 400 g cubed lamb shoulder (1 cm cubes) for the ground lamb; cook with the onion, pepper, tomato paste, and spices in a skillet for 8 minutes before mounding on the dough (the cubed meat needs a head start to cook through in the short bake). The dish reads more textural and more substantial; common in Eastern Turkey and the Black Sea region.'
    kind: 'regional'

    to: 'with sliced sucuk and cheese'
    how: 'Skip the kıymalı topping. Top each rolled-out dough with a thin layer of grated kaşar (about 80 g per pide), then arrange 4 to 5 thin slices of sucuk (Turkish spiced beef sausage) on top. Bake the same way. The sucuk renders into the cheese; the result is a quick brunch pide common in Turkish bakeries.'

notes: |
  Why the dough is enriched. The structural difference between pide dough and pizza dough lives in the milk, yogurt, egg, and butter additions. The fat and the dairy proteins produce a softer crumb that absorbs the meat juices without going soggy and that holds the boat shape through the bake without collapsing. A pizza dough (water, flour, yeast, salt) baked in the same boat configuration would either dry out at the rim or pool the meat juice into a wet center; the enriched dough is built for the architecture.

  Why the high heat. Pide is baked at 260°C / 500°F or higher because the topping is generous and the dough needs to set quickly underneath without the meat overcooking on top. Lower-temperature bakes produce pale, doughy pide with gray, dried-out meat; the canonical Turkish bakery pide comes out of a wood-fired oven at 350°C and the home version compromises only the temperature, not the technique. Preheat the stone or steel for the full 45 minutes; the bottom crust depends on it.

  The lemon at the table. The squeeze of fresh lemon over the hot meat layer is structural, not a garnish. The lamb fat is rich and the citrus cuts it; without the lemon, the dish reads heavy and one-note. Bring extra lemon wedges to the table.

  Versatility. The pide dough is excellent as: smaller individual pide for an appetizer course, plain flatbread shapes (no boat) for dipping in mezze, a base for any savory topping that wants an enriched crumb (mushroom, leek, spinach-feta). The kıymalı meat mixture works also as a stuffing for stuffed peppers (biber dolma), as a topping for lahmacun (with adjusted thinning), and as a filling for börek.

  Pairs across Turkish (canonical), Balkan, Cypriot, Armenian (where pasties exist), and broader Eastern Mediterranean bakery cookery. The dish is also widely served as part of Turkish brunch (kahvaltı), alongside menemen, olives, cheese, jam, and tea.

  Storage. Pide is best fresh and degrades quickly. Day-one leftovers reheat acceptably in a 220°C oven for 4 minutes (restores some of the crisp). Day-two pide is dry; cube and use as a base for fattoush-style salad. Shaped uncooked pide (topped, not baked) can be frozen on a sheet pan, then transferred to bags; bake from frozen at the same temperature with 4 minutes added.
