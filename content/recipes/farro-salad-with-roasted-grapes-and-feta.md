  - 'Two tracks run in parallel and finish together: the farro (25 to 30 min simmer) and the grapes (25 to 30 min roast at 220 °C). steps:
  - text: 'Start both tracks at once. Set the oven to 220 °C / 425 °F. Bring the 1500 ml / 6 cups water to a boil in a medium pot with the 12 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt and the 1 bay leaf. While the oven preheats, spread the 60 g / ½ cup walnuts on a small dry tray and toast in the heating oven for 6 to 8 minutes until fragrant and one shade darker, then pull and cool; toasting hydrolyzes the surface tannins and develops nutty pyrazines, raw walnuts read astringent and flat here. Set the cooled nuts aside to chop later.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Cook the farro. When the water boils, add the rinsed 200 g / 1 cup farro and cook uncovered at a steady simmer, pasta-style in the abundant water, for 25 to 30 minutes until the grains are tender with a distinct chew at the center (al dente, not blown out). Drain in a sieve, discard the bay leaf, and let the farro steam-dry in the sieve for 2 minutes so the surface is not waterlogged. Tip into a wide bowl; keep it warm.'
    time_min: 28
  - text: 'Roast the grapes. While the farro simmers, toss the 500 g / 3½ cups dried red grapes with the 15 g / 1 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil and the 2 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt on a rimmed sheet pan in a single layer, tucking the 3 g / 3 thyme sprigs among them. Roast at 220 °C / 425 °F for 25 to 30 minutes until the skins blister and split, the grapes slump, and dark syrup pools and caramelizes at the pan edges. Drying the grapes first is the difference between roasting and steaming; surface water has to flash off before the skins can blister. Scrape the grapes and every bit of the rendered pan syrup into a small bowl; that syrup is concentrated grape acid and sugar, the flavor core of the dish. Strip the thyme leaves from the spent sprigs back over the grapes and discard the stems.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 28
  - text: 'Build the vinaigrette. In a small bowl, combine the 30 g / 2 tbsp sherry vinegar with the 25 g / 1 shallot fine-minced and a pinch of the 2 g fine sea salt; let the shallot steep in the acid for 10 minutes to mellow its raw alliin bite and season the vinegar. Whisk in the 6 g / 1 tsp dijon mustard and the 7 g / 1 tsp honey until smooth, then stream in the 60 g / ¼ cup extra-virgin olive oil while whisking hard to emulsify. Whisk in the 1 g / ½ tsp cracked black pepper. The ratio is a deliberate 2:1 oil-to-vinegar, sharper than a standard 3:1, because the roasted grapes bring real sweetness and a soft vinaigrette would let the salad slide flat. Taste; it should read aggressively bright on its own.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Dress the warm farro. While the farro is still warm, pour about two-thirds of the sherry vinaigrette over it and toss; warm grain drinks the dressing into the starch, which cold grain cannot do. Add the roasted grapes along with all the rendered grape syrup from the bowl and fold gently so the grapes stay mostly intact, streaking the farro with dark syrup. The warm grain and warm syrup marry here; taste and add kosher salt only if the feta to come will not cover it.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Finish and plate. Let the dressed farro cool 5 minutes off the heat so the residual warmth does not melt the cheese to paste. Fold in the toasted 60 g / ½ cup chopped walnuts, the 15 g / ½ cup chopped parsley, and the torn 8 g / ¼ cup mint. Scatter the 120 g / 1 cup hand-crumbled feta over the top and fold once, gently, leaving visible white crumbles rather than smearing the cheese through. Drizzle the reserved one-third of the vinaigrette over the assembled salad. Taste a forkful that includes a grape, a feta crumble, and a nut; it should land sweet, salty, sharp, and crunchy in one bite. Adjust with a final crack of black pepper or a few drops more sherry vinegar if the sweetness is winning. Serve warm or at room temperature.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  Why roast the grapes hard. A grape warmed gently in the oven is just a hot grape: still mostly raw sugar and water, with the skin intact. The transformation that makes this dish happen above 200 °C, where surface water flashes off, the skin loses turgor and blisters, and the interior sugars concentrate and begin to caramelize at the pan contact points. A fully roasted grape reads almost savory: jammy, faintly bittered at the skin, with its acidity sharpened by water loss. That is the ingredient the salad is built around, not raw fruit. The single most common failure is crowding the pan or skipping the pat-dry step, both of which trap moisture and steam the grapes pale instead of roasting them dark. One layer, dried surfaces, full heat.

  Why dress the grain warm. Cooked grain starch is far more absorbent while warm; the gelatinized starch surface takes up dressing and the rendered grape syrup into the grain rather than letting it pool at the bottom of the bowl. Farro dressed cold from the refrigerator sheds the vinaigrette and eats gummy and flat. This is also why the salad is genuinely make-ahead in a useful way: dress the warm farro with the grapes and two-thirds of the vinaigrette, refrigerate, and the grain will have drunk the flavors overnight; bring it back to room temperature and add the cheese, nuts, herbs, and reserved dressing just before serving so those stay crisp and bright.

  Why the vinaigrette runs sharp. The default vinaigrette ratio is 3:1 oil to acid, tuned for raw bitter greens that need the oil to soften their bite. This salad is the opposite problem: the roasted grapes and the touch of honey bring real sweetness, so a standard soft vinaigrette would let the whole bowl slide toward flat-sweet. The 2:1 ratio here is deliberate, the acid has to push back against the sugar for the salad to read balanced rather than dessert-adjacent. Taste the dressing on its own and trust that it should seem too sharp in isolation; against the grapes it lands correct.

  Umami and acid, accounted for. The glutamate load comes from the brined sheep''s-milk feta, which is salt-and-protein-dense and reads as savory depth against the fruit. The finishing acid is the sherry vinegar in the dressing, reinforced by the concentrated acid of the roasted grape syrup itself. Both are structural, not optional; a version with under-salted cheese and a soft vinaigrette tastes like sweet wet grain.

  The Mediterranean register. This sits in the composed-grain-salad tradition that runs across Greece, southern Italy, and the Levant: a chewy whole grain, a sweet roasted or dried fruit, a salty brined cheese, toasted nuts, fresh herbs, olive oil and a bright vinegar. The grape-and-feta pairing is squarely Greek and southern-Italian in spirit. Keep the olive oil generous and good, use real PDO feta rather than a cow''s-milk imitation, and let the herbs go in raw at the end rather than cooking out their volatiles.
