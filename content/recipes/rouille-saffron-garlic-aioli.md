before_you_start:
  - 'Rouille is a partner sauce. The canonical recipe uses warm broth pulled from a bouillabaisse pot; if making standalone, bloom a generous pinch of saffron in 60 ml warm seafood stock at least 15 minutes before starting so the carotenoids dissolve. Cold or dry saffron lands the sauce dull yellow rather than rust-red.'
  - 'Bring the egg yolk and olive oil to room temperature before mounting. Cold ingredients refuse to emulsify cleanly into the bread-and-garlic base and the rouille breaks within minutes.'
  - 'Use a mortar and pestle if you have one. The food-processor version is functionally equivalent but loses the textural slight-graininess of pounded garlic that defines the classical Marseille sauce.'

source:
  name: 'The classical Provençal canon (Marseille, Toulon, the Mediterranean coast of southern France); the sauce is documented in the 19th-century French cookbooks (Reboul''s La Cuisinière Provençale, 1897) and stabilized in the modern form (with saffron, raw garlic, olive oil) over the 20th century.'
  note: 'Rouille is structurally a bread-thickened (or potato-thickened) aioli, with saffron and ground cayenne pepper or piment d''Espelette providing the rust-red color and the characteristic Provençal heat. The two main schools of rouille use either soaked stale bread (Marseille) or a small boiled potato (Toulon and the variants closer to the Spanish border) as the structural thickener; both work, with the bread version reading slightly softer and the potato version reading firmer and more aioli-adjacent. The classical preparation pounds raw garlic to a paste in a mortar before incorporating the bread, saffron broth, and yolk; the modern food-processor version is functionally equivalent but loses the textural slight-graininess that the mortar produces. Rouille is canonically dropped onto toasted baguette croutons (the rouille-on-toast preparation called rouille tartines) which float on the broth at service; it can also be stirred directly into the broth as a thickener-and-flavor finish, though this is the less canonical service. The cayenne or piment d''Espelette is essential to the dish; rouille without heat is a Provençal aioli, not a rouille. The version here is the Marseille bread-thickened canonical preparation.'

steps:
  - text: 'Soak the 30 g / ¾ cup torn bread in the 60 ml / ¼ cup warm saffron broth for 2 to 3 minutes, until the bread has fully absorbed the liquid and turned a deep yellow-orange from the saffron. Squeeze the bread gently with the back of a spoon to fully saturate. If making rouille standalone (no bouillabaisse broth available), warm the fish stock with the additional 0.1 g pinch of saffron crumbled in for 5 minutes off the heat, then proceed.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'In a heavy mortar, pound the 15 g / 3 to 4 garlic cloves with the 3 g / ½ tsp salt to a smooth paste; the salt is abrasive and breaks the garlic down faster than pounding alone. Work the paste 1 to 2 minutes until completely smooth, with no visible garlic chunks. Alternative: process the garlic and salt in a small food processor 30 seconds; the result is functionally the same but with less texture.'
    technique: 'mortar-pounding'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the saffron-soaked bread to the mortar (or food processor); pound or pulse until the bread is fully incorporated into the garlic paste, 1 to 2 minutes. The mixture should be a uniform pale-orange paste with no visible bread pieces. Add the 18 g / 1 egg yolk and the 1 g / ¼ tsp red pepper flakes; pound or pulse to incorporate, 30 seconds.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Mount with the 200 g / 1 cup olive oil in a slow steady stream, pounding constantly with the pestle (or processing on low) to keep the emulsion stable. Start with a near-drip rate; as the emulsion sets and visibly thickens (after the first 50 ml or so), the oil can be added in a thin steady stream. The finished rouille should be the texture of mayonnaise (thick enough to hold a spoon stand-up shape) and a deep red-orange color from the saffron and red pepper flakes.'
    technique: 'egg-yolk-emulsion'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Whisk in the 5 g / 1 tsp lemon juice; taste and adjust salt and red pepper flakes (the rouille should read garlic-forward, with a noticeable but not painful heat, and the saffron should come through as a faint mineral-honey note in the finish). Transfer to a small serving bowl. Serve at room temperature, alongside the bouillabaisse with toasted baguette croutons; each diner spreads rouille on the croutons and floats them in the broth, or stirs the rouille directly into the soup.'
    time_min: 2

modifications:
  - for: 'no-egg version'
    to: 'as a bread-only emulsion (the older Provençal canonical version)'
    how: 'Skip the egg yolk; double the bread to 60 g and the saffron broth to 120 ml. The bread starch acts as the emulsifier in place of egg lecithin; the result is slightly less stable than the yolk version and benefits from being made just before service. This is the older Marseille canonical preparation, predating widespread egg use in the sauce.'
    kind: 'dietary'
  - for: 'potato base instead of bread'
    to: 'as the Toulon-style potato-thickened version'
    how: 'Replace the 30 g bread with 75 g of waxy potato (Yukon Gold), peeled, boiled until soft, drained, and mashed; pound the potato into the garlic paste and proceed. The result is firmer, denser, and reads more aioli-adjacent; common in the western Provençal coast and into the Spanish border regions.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'food-processor speed'
    to: 'in a small food processor instead of a mortar'
    how: 'Combine the garlic, salt, soaked bread, yolk, and red pepper flakes in a small food processor (1 to 2 cup capacity); pulse to a uniform paste; with the motor running on low, drizzle in the olive oil through the feed tube in a slow steady stream until fully incorporated. The result is functionally identical to the mortar version but with a slightly smoother texture (no slight-grain from the mortar pounding). The mortar is canonical and produces a better mouthfeel; the food processor is faster.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Rouille and aioli are cousins, not the same sauce. Aioli (the Provençal and Catalan canon) is garlic, salt, and olive oil pounded into an emulsion with no other thickener; the sauce is a structural challenge because the only emulsifier is the small amount of natural mucilage in raw garlic, and the emulsion breaks easily. Rouille adds bread (or potato) as a starch thickener, egg yolk as a lecithin emulsifier, and saffron and chili as the defining flavor-and-color signatures; the result is more stable, more flavorful, and structurally a different sauce. Both are mounted with olive oil in the same technique, but rouille is the bouillabaisse-specific finish and aioli is the broader Provençal table sauce.

  The bread-vs-potato decision is regional. Marseille and the eastern Provençal coast use bread (the classical canonical version, documented in Reboul); Toulon and the western coast toward the Spanish border use potato (more aioli-adjacent, firmer texture). Both are correct; the bread version reads softer and more saffron-forward, the potato version reads firmer and more garlic-forward. For bouillabaisse specifically the bread version is the more traditional pairing.

  The egg-or-no-egg decision is historical. The original 19th-century rouille was bread-and-oil only; egg yolk was added to the canonical preparation in the early 20th century as the technique stabilized in restaurant kitchens. The egg version is more stable and easier to mount; the no-egg version is the older Marseille canonical preparation and reads slightly looser and more rustic. Either is correct.

  Service. Rouille is canonically spread on toasted baguette croutons (rounds of bread, toasted, rubbed with raw garlic) which float on the broth at service; each diner spoons rouille onto a crouton, drops the crouton into their bowl of broth, and watches the rouille slowly dissolve into the soup. The alternative is to stir a spoonful of rouille directly into the broth at the table, which thickens and flavors the broth in one move; this is less canonical but common in modern restaurant service. The leftover rouille is excellent on toast, with grilled fish, or spooned onto a baked potato; it keeps 3 days refrigerated in a sealed jar.

  Stability. The rouille emulsion is stable for 3 days refrigerated; bring back to room temperature 30 minutes before service for the best texture. If the emulsion breaks (separates into oil and paste), reconstitute by whisking 1 tsp of warm water into the broken sauce in a clean bowl and slowly streaming the broken rouille back in; the water rebuilds the emulsion structure.
