title: 'Chicken adobo'
desc: 'The Filipino national dish. Chicken thighs braised in vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, bay, and black peppercorns, then reduced to a dark, glossy glaze that lacquers the skin and concentrates the acetic-soy-fermented depth into a single dominant flavor. before_you_start:
  - 'Cook the rice first. Adobo holds and improves on standing, but the rice needs to be hot and freshly steamed on the plate. Time the rice so it finishes 5 minutes before the adobo reduction reaches its glossy endpoint.'
  - 'Do not stir during the first half of the braise. Stirring disrupts the chicken-skin browning that sets up the lacquered finish; let the chicken sit in the liquid undisturbed and lift the pieces only when flipping at the halfway mark.'
  - 'Filipino cooks rest finished adobo overnight before reheating; the flavor improves dramatically as the vinegar mellows and integrates with the soy and rendered chicken fat. If serving same-day, build in a 30-minute off-heat rest after the reduction; same principle, shorter window.'

source:
  name: 'Adapted from Filipino home-cook canon'
  note: 'Adobo predates Spanish colonization. The Spanish word "adobo" (marinade) was applied by the colonizers to a vinegar-based braising method that already existed in pre-Hispanic Philippines as a meat preservation technique. The soy sauce is a later (likely Chinese-trader) addition; older regional adobo (adobong puti, "white adobo") uses no soy at all. The version this recipe teaches (with soy) is the dominant modern Filipino version and the one most non-Filipinos recognize. Regional variants are legion: adobo sa gata (with coconut milk, Bicolano), adobong dilaw (with turmeric, no soy, Batangas), adobong puti (no soy, original style).'

steps:
  - text: 'Place the 1000 g salted chicken thighs skin-side-down in a wide heavy pan (cast iron or enameled dutch oven, 30 cm / 12 inch). Pour in the 180 g / ¾ cup cane vinegar, 240 ml / 1 cup water, 90 g / 6 tbsp light soy sauce, 50 g whole smashed garlic, 4 bay leaves, 6 g / 2 tsp cracked black peppercorns, and 8 g / 2 tsp sugar (if using). Do not stir. The chicken should be three-quarters submerged with the skin slightly above the liquid line.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Bring to a bare simmer over medium heat (uncovered). Once bubbling, reduce heat to maintain a gentle simmer for 25 minutes. Do not stir. The vinegar smell will be aggressive for the first 10 minutes; this is normal and is the alcohol-component of the acetic acid boiling off (do not breathe directly over the pan during this phase, the vapor is sharp). After 10 minutes the smell shifts from sharp-acetic to a deeper, more rounded vinegar-soy aromatic.'
    technique: 'vinegar-braise'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Flip each chicken thigh skin-side-up with tongs (working gently, do not tear the skin). The chicken should be cooked through (75°C / 165°F at the thickest point) but still tender; the goal of the next phase is reduction and glaze, not further cooking. Simmer uncovered another 15 to 20 minutes; the liquid reduces from about 500 ml to 150 ml and the color shifts from translucent brown to a deeper, glossier brown-black with visible reduced soy at the bottom of the pan.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Test the reduction. Tilt the pan; the remaining liquid should coat the bottom in a syrupy, almost-lacquered layer that takes 2 to 3 seconds to flow back together. If still thin, simmer another 5 minutes uncovered. The garlic cloves should be soft enough to mash with a fork; the chicken skin should be glossy and dark.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Optional: for a crispy-skin finish (often called adobong krispy in modern Filipino cooking), lift the chicken thighs from the reduced sauce. Heat the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a separate skillet over medium-high heat. Sear the chicken skin-side-down for 2 to 3 minutes until the skin crisps to a deep mahogany. Return to the sauce pan. Skip this step for the classical preparation; the soft-glazed skin is also canonical.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Pull off heat and rest 30 minutes uncovered. The reduction tightens further and the vinegar mellows; this rest is structural to the flavor profile of canonical adobo. Many Filipino cooks rest the dish overnight and reheat the next day, the flavor improves dramatically over 12 to 24 hours.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Plate. Mound a portion of hot jasmine rice on each plate. Top with 1 to 2 chicken thighs, a few of the softened garlic cloves, and a generous spoon of the dark sauce poured over the rice. Scatter sliced scallion over (if using). Serve hot. The canonical bite is a forkful of vinegar-soy-saturated rice with a piece of the salt-acid-front chicken, the mashed garlic clove smashed onto the rice between bites.'

    how: 'Substitute 1000 g pork belly, cut into 4 cm cubes, for the chicken thighs. Sear the pork cubes first in a dry hot pan to render some fat and brown the surfaces (5 minutes); pour off excess fat, then proceed with the braise. Pork adobo simmers longer (40 to 45 minutes covered, 20 minutes uncovered for reduction) because pork belly takes more time to break down to tender. Many Filipinos prefer pork adobo to chicken; chicken is the gateway version.'
    kind: 'protein'
  - for: 'cuisine'
    to: 'adobo sa gata (Bicolano coconut adobo)'
    how: 'After the chicken is fully cooked (end of step 3), add 240 ml / 1 cup full-fat coconut milk and 2 to 3 sliced bird-eye chilies. Simmer 10 more minutes; the coconut milk thickens slightly and the sauce shifts from dark-brown to creamy-tan. This is the Bicolano (southern Luzon) variant; the coconut and chili are the regional signature. Pairs especially well with pork belly.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'cuisine'
    to: 'adobong puti (white adobo, vinegar-only)'
    how: 'Skip the soy sauce entirely. Increase the salt to 10 g / 2 tsp. Use the full 270 g of vinegar (skip the water dilution or reduce water to 120 ml). The original pre-Hispanic adobo, just meat braised in vinegar, garlic, peppercorns, and bay. The finished sauce is much lighter in color and the flavor is purer vinegar-front; this is what the dish was before the Chinese soy trade reached the Philippines.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'cuisine'
    to: 'adobong dilaw (yellow adobo, Batangas)'
    how: 'Skip the soy sauce. Add 8 g / 1 tbsp ground turmeric to the braise. Use the white-adobo proportions otherwise. The Batangas variant; the turmeric tints the sauce yellow-orange and adds earthy depth. Pre-dates the soy-driven Manila adobo style.'
    kind: 'regional'

notes: |
  Why the vinegar before the soy. Filipino adobo is structurally a vinegar braise with soy as a secondary salt-and-color agent, not a soy braise with vinegar as a souring agent. The vinegar is added cold at the start, not heated and reduced as a finishing acid; this is the structural identity of adobo and the reason the dish tastes nothing like Chinese soy-braised meats. The 25-minute uncovered initial simmer drives off the acetic acid sharpness (most of the volatile acid vapor evaporates in the first 10 minutes) and leaves the rounded, fermented backbone of the vinegar that defines the dish.

  Why no stirring during the first phase. The chicken skin needs to sit in contact with the bottom of the pan to develop the Maillard browning that contributes to the glossy lacquered finish. Stirring disrupts this contact and the skin stays pale and softer. The single flip at the 25-minute mark is enough; the reduction phase that follows develops the surface character on the now-up-facing skin.

  Why uncovered simmer. The dish depends on liquid reduction; covering traps moisture and the sauce never reaches the glossy lacquered consistency that defines canonical adobo. The uncovered simmer also drives off more of the volatile vinegar compounds, leaving the deeper, rounded flavor. A covered braise produces a different dish (closer to a wet braise like Cantonese soy chicken), which is fine but is not adobo.

  Why the rest. Adobo improves dramatically with a rest of 30 minutes to 24 hours. The acetic acid continues to integrate with the fat and the rendered chicken juices; the harsh top notes fade and the deep umami-acid-salt unity emerges. Filipino home cooks routinely cook adobo a day before serving, the difference between fresh-finished and day-after is noticeable. Even a 30-minute rest off-heat at room temperature meaningfully improves the dish.

  Why bone-in skin-on. The collagen from the bones contributes structure to the reduction (the sauce gels slightly on cooling, sign of properly-extracted gelatin), and the rendered fat from the skin enriches the finished glaze. Boneless skinless thighs work but produce a leaner, less-glossy dish; if using boneless, add a tablespoon of neutral oil to the braise to compensate for the missing fat.

  Why no thickener. Real adobo is reduced to a glaze through evaporation, not thickened with starch. The sauce thickens because the soy and vinegar concentrate as the water boils off; the glossy texture comes from the dissolved chicken collagen and the reduced fat. Adding cornstarch or flour produces a gravy-like texture that misses the structural identity of the dish.

  Why served over rice (not as a stew). Adobo is structurally a salt-acid-front meat-and-glaze that needs the bland, slightly-starchy foil of jasmine rice to balance. Serving adobo as a stew with vegetables (Western convention) misses the point; the dish is intentionally narrow and is meant to be eaten in bites that combine the assertive sauce with the neutral rice. The garlic cloves softened during the braise are also part of the eating architecture; mashed onto rice between bites, they carry the braise flavor in a softer form.
