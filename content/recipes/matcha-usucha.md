steps:
  - text: 'Bring water to a full boil, then pull off the heat and let it sit for 90 seconds. This drops the temperature to roughly 80°C / 175°F, which is the working range for matcha. Boiling water (100°C) extracts the bitter catechins aggressively and the resulting tea reads grassy-harsh; below 70°C the L-theanine and chlorophyll do not extract fully and the tea reads pale and weak.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Warm the matcha bowl (chawan, or any wide low ceramic bowl about 12 cm / 5 inches across) by rinsing with the just-boiled water you set aside, then drying with a clean cloth. Warming the bowl is functional: it stabilizes the brew temperature during whisking so the matcha does not flash-cool against cold ceramic.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Sift the 2 g / 1 heaping ½ tsp matcha into the warm bowl through a small fine-mesh strainer. Sifting is non-optional, matcha powder agglomerates into tiny clumps that no amount of whisking will fully break apart. A sifted matcha whisks smooth in 15 seconds; an unsifted matcha leaves small green specks regardless of technique.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour the 60 ml / ¼ cup of 80°C water onto the sifted matcha in a single steady stream. Pick up the chasen (bamboo whisk), or a small wire whisk as a substitute, and whisk vigorously in a tight Z-pattern, keeping the whisk tines just at the surface of the liquid. Whisk for 15 to 20 seconds. The matcha will go from a flat dark slurry to a bright jade liquid with a fine, even foam covering the surface. The foam should be small-bubbled and persistent, not large-bubbled and quickly collapsing.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'When the foam is even, lift the whisk straight up out of the center with a small twist, this leaves a peaked surface rather than a depressed pool. Drink immediately. Usucha is served at the temperature of brewing; reheating destroys the foam and dulls the color.'

substitutions:

    note: 'Culinary-grade matcha in this preparation produces a bitter, dull tea that obscures the technique. If only culinary is available, make a matcha latte (recipe matcha-latte) instead, the milk and sugar mask the bitterness.'
  - for: 'chasen (bamboo whisk)'
    use: 'small balloon wire whisk or milk frother'
    note: 'A wire whisk works but compacts the powder rather than aerating it, the foam will be less even. A handheld milk frother (the small battery-powered kind) gives the closest texture to a chasen.'

modifications:

    how: 'Use 4 g / 1 tbsp matcha to only 30 ml / 2 tbsp water. Whisk slowly and gently, the goal is a smooth, paint-like thick liquid, not a frothed foam. Koicha is the tea of formal ceremony; it is sipped, not drunk, and tastes intensely umami-sweet rather than refreshing. Use only the highest grade matcha available.'

    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Temperature is the single most consequential variable. Most home matcha tastes bad because the water was at a full rolling boil, the bitter catechin extraction outpaces the sweet L-theanine extraction at high temperatures, and the resulting tea reads as one-note grassy-harsh. 80°C is the temperature that lets both compound classes extract roughly together, producing the characteristic sweet-umami balance.

  The chasen is purpose-built. The 80 to 120 fine bamboo tines aerate matcha in a way no metal whisk can match, they create a high-surface-area network that traps tiny stable bubbles instead of forcing larger ones. A good chasen lasts 6 months of daily use; replace when the tines start fraying or curling outward.

  Quality is binary in matcha. A 2-week-old bag of ceremonial matcha tastes profoundly better than a 6-month-old bag of the same brand. Store in the refrigerator in the original tin, use within a month of opening, and accept that good matcha is a perishable. The price difference between ceremonial and culinary is real flavor difference, not marketing.
