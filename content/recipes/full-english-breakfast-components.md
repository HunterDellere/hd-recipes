  note: 'The full English (also full fry-up, full Monty, full breakfast) is the structural British breakfast at home, at the greasy-spoon cafe, and at the pub. Regional variants: the full Scottish adds a tattie scone and a slice of haggis; the full Welsh adds laverbread (seaweed); the full Irish adds white pudding alongside the black; the full English proper has the canonical lineup below. The dish is less a recipe than an assembly logic; the difficulty is coordinating 7 separate components to all finish hot at the same moment.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The full English is a parallel-track cook across 4 pans (or 1 large skillet + sheet pan + saucepan + toaster). The structural difficulty is the timing: everything must arrive on the plate hot. Read the cook sequence in step 1 carefully before starting and lay out all components mise-en-place; once the cook begins, you will not have time to chop a tomato or open a can.'
  - 'British "back bacon" (a thicker, leaner cut from the loin) is the canonical choice; American streaky bacon (belly) is the substitute and works but produces a fattier plate. Look for "British rashers" at British import shops or Trader Joe''s; or use a meaty thick-cut bacon.'
  - 'British bangers (pork sausages) are looser-textured and seasoned more gently than American breakfast sausages. The canonical brands in the U.S. are the Banger Shop, Borough Market, or a local British butcher. Use bratwurst-style fresh pork sausage as the substitute.'
  - 'British baked beans (Heinz baked beans in tomato sauce, the blue can) are structurally different from American baked beans (sweeter, with molasses). Use Heinz UK or a British grocery import. American B&M or Bush''s baked beans pull the plate into Boston-baked-beans territory, structurally wrong.'

steps:
  - text: 'Read the full timing plan before starting. The cook is 25 minutes total across 4 stations: (A) a 30 cm cast iron or non-stick skillet over medium heat (sausages and bacon, plus eggs at the end); (B) a separate 20 cm skillet over medium-high (mushrooms and tomatoes); (C) a small saucepan over low heat (beans); (D) a toaster or sheet pan in a 200 °C / 400 °F oven (toast or fried bread). Plate when everything is hot. Preheat 2 dinner plates in the warm oven from minute 0.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Station A, start (minute 0). Add the 4 sausages to the unheated 30 cm cast iron skillet. Set heat to medium-low. Sausages need 12 to 15 minutes total, turning every 3 minutes; the slow cook prevents the casings from bursting and produces an even golden-brown render on all surfaces. Do not crank the heat or the casings split and the interior stays raw.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Station C, start (minute 3). Open the can of baked beans; transfer to a small saucepan over low heat. Stir occasionally, warm to a low simmer over 8 to 10 minutes. Do not boil hard; the beans will break down. Cover to keep warm once heated through.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Station B, start (minute 5). Heat the 20 cm skillet over medium-high. Add 15 g / 1 tbsp of the butter. Once melted and just starting to foam, add the 200 g mushrooms cut-side down. Do not stir for 3 minutes; let the cut faces Maillard-brown. After 3 minutes, toss and continue to cook 3 to 4 minutes more, until the mushrooms are deeply browned and the released liquid has cooked off. Season with a pinch of salt. Transfer to a small bowl; cover with a plate to keep warm.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Station B continued (minute 12). In the same skillet, add the remaining 15 g / 1 tbsp butter. Lay the tomato halves cut-side down. Cook 3 minutes without moving until the cut face is deeply browned and the tomato has begun to collapse. Flip; cook 1 to 2 minutes on the skin side to warm through. Transfer to a plate; cover.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Station A continued (minute 8 to 14). Once the sausages have been turning for 8 to 10 minutes and look 80 percent done, push them to one side of the skillet. Add the 4 rashers of bacon to the cleared space. Cook the bacon 3 to 4 minutes per side until crisp at the edges and rendered. Move the bacon to a paper-towel-lined plate; the sausages should finish in another 3 minutes.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Station D (minute 12 to 17). Start the toast 4 minutes before serving (toaster) or 6 minutes (oven). For fried bread (the canonical greasy-spoon move), after the bacon comes off the skillet, fry the bread slices directly in the rendered bacon and sausage fat over medium heat, 2 minutes per side, until deep gold and saturated with the fat. Fried bread is non-negotiable in a "proper" full English; toast is the polite home substitute.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Station A finish, the eggs (minute 18 to 22). Pull the finished sausages onto the warm plates with the bacon. Reduce heat to medium-low under the skillet. The skillet should have 1 to 2 tbsp of rendered fat remaining; if dry, add 10 g butter. Crack the 4 eggs gently into the skillet, spacing apart. Cover with a lid for 90 seconds for the whites to set (this is the structural greasy-spoon method; covering steam-cooks the tops without flipping, leaving the yolks runny). The whites should be opaque and the yolks visibly liquid. Season with a pinch of salt and pepper.'
    technique: 'egg-doneness'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Plate (minute 22 to 25). On each warm dinner plate, arrange in this canonical order from the 12 o''clock position clockwise: 2 sausages, 2 bacon rashers, 2 fried eggs (avoiding the beans, which would soak in), a small pile of mushrooms, 1 tomato half, a 60 g pool of beans (use the side of the spoon to make a clean wall against the eggs), 2 slices of fried bread or toast. Crack pepper over the eggs and tomato. Serve immediately with HP brown sauce and English mustard on the table, plus a pot of strong English breakfast tea.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why the parallel-track logic. The challenge of the full English is not any individual component but the parallel-track timing across 4 stations. Each component takes 3 to 12 minutes; the goal is for everything to finish hot at the same moment. The cook sequence above (sausages first, beans second, mushrooms and tomatoes in parallel, bacon after sausages are 80 percent done, fried bread in the rendered fat, eggs last) is the canonical greasy-spoon order and produces all-hot plates in about 25 minutes.

  The fried egg, lid technique. The British greasy-spoon fried egg is cooked over low heat with a lid for 90 seconds; the steam from the trapped moisture sets the top of the white without flipping the egg. The yolk stays liquid (which is essential for dipping toast and beans). An over-easy flip is the American substitute; a sunny-side-up no-lid is the slower French method. The lid method is the structural British way.

  Why the plate arrangement matters. The canonical arrangement keeps the wet components (eggs, beans) from soaking the dry components (fried bread, bacon) before the eater has assembled each bite. Plating the beans into a pool with a clean wall (made by the side of the spoon against the egg) is a small move that preserves the textural distinction of each component for the full 5 minutes of eating.

  Pairings. Strong English breakfast tea (Yorkshire, PG Tips, Twinings English Breakfast) is non-negotiable; coffee is American and reads wrong with this plate. HP brown sauce and English mustard at the table. Some households add a pot of Marmite for the toast (acquired taste).
