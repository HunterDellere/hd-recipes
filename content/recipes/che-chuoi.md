before_you_start:
  - 'Use bananas that are ripe but still firm, fully yellow with the first brown specks, not soft or heavily speckled. Underripe bananas read starchy; overripe bananas dissolve into the coconut base within the 4 minute poach and the dish reads as banana porridge instead of poached banana in coconut milk.'
  - 'Tapioca cooks in a separate pot in parallel with the coconut base, the two come together at the end. Set the tapioca water boiling before you open the coconut milk cans so the 12 to 15 minute pearl boil overlaps with the 8 minute coconut-and-banana phase.'

steps:
  - text: 'Bring the 1000 ml / 4 cups water to a vigorous boil. Add the 60 g / ⅓ cup small tapioca pearls and boil 12 to 15 minutes, stirring every 2 minutes so they do not stick. The pearls are done when most of each pearl is translucent and only a tiny white dot remains in the center, they will finish in residual heat. Drain into a sieve and rinse briefly under cool water to wash off surface starch. Set aside.'
    time_min: 16
  - text: 'In a wide, heavy saucepan combine the 800 ml of full-fat coconut milk, the 80 g / ½ cup chopped palm sugar, the 2 g pinch fine sea salt, and the 2 knotted pandan leaves. Bring just to a gentle simmer over medium-low heat, stirring constantly until the palm sugar dissolves. Do not let the coconut milk reach a rolling boil; the fat will break out into oily droplets and the sauce will look curdled.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Slide in the banana pieces in a single layer. Hold the temperature at a bare tremor (around 90°C / 195°F, small bubbles at the pan edge, no rolling motion) for 4 minutes. Cooked harder, the bananas dissolve into mush; cooked gentler than this they stay raw-starchy in the middle. The flesh should turn translucent at the edges and yield slightly when nudged with a spoon.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add the drained tapioca pearls. Stir once to suspend them through the coconut base. Hold off heat for 2 minutes, the residual warmth finishes the pearl centers without further cooking the bananas. Fish out the pandan leaves.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Taste the coconut liquid for sweetness. Vietnamese chè runs less sweet than Western dessert pudding; you should taste banana and coconut first, sweetness as the trailing note. Adjust with a pinch more sugar or salt as needed.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Serve warm in bowls. Scatter the 30 g / ¼ cup chopped roasted peanuts and the 5 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame seeds over each portion. The fat and crunch of the peanuts is structural, not garnish; without them the dish reads one-note sweet.'

notes: |
  Coconut milk emulsion failure is the most common kitchen mistake. Cans of coconut milk are a delicate fat-in-water emulsion stabilized by proteins; a hard boil breaks it. Hold the heat below a true simmer and the dish stays glossy and creamy throughout.

  Banana ripeness matters. Fully spotty bananas dissolve into the coconut base and lose all structure; underripe green-yellow bananas stay starchy and refuse to soften. The target is firm-yellow, beginning to spot but not soft to the squeeze. Burro bananas (also sold as "chunky" or "horse" bananas at Latin and Asian markets) are the traditional choice and hold shape best; Cavendish work but require pulling slightly earlier.

  Salt and peanuts are the dish. Without the salt the sweetness reads flat; without the peanuts there is no textural contrast and the dish slumps into one-note creamy. Skip neither.
