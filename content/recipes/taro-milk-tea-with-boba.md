steps:
  - text: 'Wear gloves while peeling raw taro. The corm contains calcium oxalate crystals that irritate skin and throat, oil your hands as a backup if gloves are unavailable, since the oxalate is fat-soluble. Peel the taro and cut into 2 cm / ¾ inch chunks. Rinse briefly to wash off any released oxalate from the cut surface.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Steam the taro over rising water for 20 to 22 minutes until completely tender, a fork should slip through with no resistance. Steaming, not boiling, preserves the gentle nutty flavor; boiled taro absorbs water and the flavor goes flat. The flesh will be ivory with faint purple veining; do not expect vivid lavender.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'While still warm, transfer the steamed taro to a bowl. Add the 50 g / ¼ cup sugar, the 1 g pinch salt, and the 30 g / 1½ tbsp sweetened condensed milk. Mash with a fork into a smooth thick paste, leave some small chunks for textural interest, or process completely smooth depending on preference. The taro paste should taste like a sweet steamed potato with a subtle perfume; if it tastes bland the taro was old or under-steamed.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Tea concentrate. Pour the 200 ml / ¾ cup plus 2 tbsp water at 85°C over the 6 g / 1 tbsp jasmine green tea. Steep 3 minutes, short steep at sub-boiling temperature pulls jasmine aroma without the catechin bitterness that a longer hot steep would produce. Strain. Cool to room temperature; hot tea breaks the dairy emulsion when poured into milk.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Build the drink. In a tall glass, place 80 g / ½ cup cooked warm tapioca pearls in the bottom. Spoon 90 g / ⅓ cup of the warm taro paste over the pearls. Pour 150 ml / ⅔ cup cold whole milk over the taro. Add a generous handful of ice (60 to 80 g). Top with 100 ml / ½ cup of the cooled tea concentrate. The layers will visually stratify briefly, then begin to merge.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Serve with a wide boba straw and a long spoon. The eater stirs everything together, the taro paste dissolves into the milk-tea, the pearls come up the straw in clumps, the color settles into a pale beige-lavender. This is the honest taro milk tea; the violet bubble-tea-shop version is artificial dye applied to taro powder.'

notes: |
  The color is the giveaway. Real taro milk tea is beige-lavender, never crayon-violet. Bubble-tea chains use a combination of cheap taro powder (mostly starch and dextrose, flavored with synthetic 2-acetyl-pyrazine compounds that mimic real taro), purple sweet potato or ube (which is genuinely purple but a different plant), and food coloring. The result tastes like fake taro candy. The real ingredient produces a different and arguably better drink, nuttier, more nuanced, less dessert-sweet, but it does not look the same and it takes 30 minutes to prepare. The trade-off is honest taste vs. instant violet.

  Sweetened condensed milk in the taro paste does work that nothing else does: it gives the round, milk-fudge creaminess that defines good taro milk tea. Skipping it produces a drink that reads as steamed-vegetable-and-tea rather than as taro milk tea. This is the one piece of bubble-tea pantry chemistry that is non-negotiable.

  Boba straws are wide because they have to be. The narrow ones standard at fast food do not accommodate pearls; pearls bunch and clog. If you do not have a wide-mouth straw, eat the pearls with a spoon and drink the tea straight.
