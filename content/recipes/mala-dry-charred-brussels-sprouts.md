steps:
  - text: 'Trim the stem ends off the 450 g / 1 lb Brussels sprouts, pull loose outer leaves, and halve each through the root so the layers stay anchored and present a flat cut face. Leave the smallest whole so everything chars at one rate.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Toast the 3 g / 1.5 tsp Sichuan peppercorns in a dry pan over medium, 120 to 150°C / 250 to 300°F, shaking until they smell citrusy and faintly piney, about 90 seconds, then grind to a coarse powder and set aside. Raw peppercorn is harsh and only releases its tingling hydroxy-alpha-sanshool aroma after a dry toast. Set the 5 g / 4 to 6 snipped dried red chilies, the 15 g / 3 cloves minced garlic, and the 4 g / 1 tsp minced ginger in one dish.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Heat a wok or heavy skillet over medium-high with the 28 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil until it shimmers, around 205°C / 400°F. Lay the sprouts cut-face down in a single layer and leave them undisturbed 3 to 4 minutes: the dense cut surface needs sustained contact to drive the Maillard browning and sugar caramelization that turns raw cabbage harshness nutty, and moving them early forfeits the crust. Work in two batches before you crowd the pan, since crowding traps steam and the faces gray. Pull when the cut faces are deep brown to nearly black at the edges.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'The cores are still raw under the crust, so add the 45 g / 3 tbsp water to the hot pan and cover for 2 minutes; the trapped steam conducts heat into the dense centers without burning the seared faces. Uncover and let the last water flash off.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Push the sprouts aside, add the snipped dried chilies and ½ tsp / 1 g of the toasted whole Sichuan peppercorn (held back from the ground portion) to the bare oil and fry 20 to 30 seconds until the chilies darken a shade and turn fragrant, not black; scorched chili goes acrid. Add the garlic and ginger and bloom 15 seconds more.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Toss everything together with the 10 g / 2 tsp light soy sauce (the glutamate hit that keeps a dry mala dish from reading thin), the 2 g / ½ tsp granulated sugar, and the 1 g / fine sea salt to taste, 30 seconds, until the sprouts are coated in fragrant oil and chili with no liquid pooling. Pull from the heat.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Off the flame, dust over 1 tsp / 2 g of the reserved ground roasted Sichuan peppercorn and add the 5 g / 1 tsp Chinkiang black vinegar: kept small and late, the vinegar lifts the heat without weighing the dish into a glaze, and off-heat it holds its bright top note. Finish with the 14 g / 2 tbsp sliced scallion greens and the 3 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds.'
    technique: 'hot-oil-finish'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'The Adjustment. If the dish reads hot but flat, more light soy by the ½ tsp before more chili; the glutamate is what the dry mala needs first. If your mouth doesn''t tingle, the peppercorn was stale or under-toasted; add another ¼ tsp of the fresh ground over the top, since the numbing fades fast once ground. If it tastes scorched and bitter, the chilies or garlic went too dark in the oil; pull them a shade sooner next time. If the centers read raw, give the cores an extra minute under the lid.'

notes: |
  Mala is two separate sensations stacked, and the toast is what unlocks one of them. The la (heat) comes from capsaicin in the dried chili, fat-soluble and bloomed into the oil so it coats every surface. The ma (numbing) comes from hydroxy-alpha-sanshool in Sichuan peppercorn, a molecule that triggers the same touch-sensing nerve fibers that detect light vibration, which the brain reads as a buzzing tingle rather than as taste.

  Raw peppercorn keeps much of that compound locked in tough husks alongside bitter, gritty notes; a dry toast just below smoking volatilizes the bitter fractions and frees the sanshool, so a quick toast-and-grind gives a clean, bright tingle where untoasted peppercorn gives a dull, numbing-but-harsh bite. Grinding it fresh and adding it off the heat matters because sanshool is volatile and fades within the hour once the husk is broken.

  The char on the cut face is doing the second half of the work. Brussels sprouts are dense with glucosinolates that read sulfurous and harsh when underdone; sustained dry-heat contact runs the Maillard reaction and caramelizes the cut-surface sugars, converting that bite into nutty sweetness. The steam-through is the only wet step, and it exists solely to cook the dense core without softening the crust.
