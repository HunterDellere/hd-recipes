The defining elements are: bone-in skin-on pork shoulder; the stab-hole "hueco" injection technique for marinade penetration; the garlic-oregano-adobo paste with white vinegar; the long slow roast at low heat; the high-heat finish to crackle the skin into cuerito. steps:
  - text: 'The day before: pat the 3 kg pork dry. Score the skin in a 2 cm cross-hatch about 5 mm deep, cutting through the skin and into the fat layer below but stopping before the meat. The scoring lets the rendered fat escape and lets the skin shrink uniformly to form cuerito.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Cut 10 to 12 stab holes into the meat, each about 3 cm deep and 2 cm wide, distributed across the top and sides. These holes will receive the marinade paste and are how the seasoning reaches the center of the shoulder. This is the canonical Puerto Rican "hueco" technique; without it, only the surface tastes seasoned.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Make the paste. In a mortar (or food processor), pound the 60 g garlic with the 30 g salt into a smooth paste. Add the 8 g oregano, 6 g pepper, 60 ml white vinegar, 60 ml olive oil, and the 5 g sazon if using. Pound (or pulse) until you have a thick wet paste.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Rub the paste all over the pork, including into every stab hole (push the paste into the holes with your finger or a small spoon, getting it to the bottom). Reserve about 2 tbsp of the paste for re-application. Place the pork skin-side up in a roasting pan or sheet pan with a rack. Cover loosely with parchment and refrigerate 12 to 24 hours.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Day of: 6 hours before serving, remove the pork from the fridge. Rest at room temperature 45 minutes (a cold roast goes into the oven and stays cold in the center for an extra hour). Preheat oven to 150 °C / 300 °F with the rack in the lower third.'
    time_min: 45
  - text: 'Roast the pork skin-side up, uncovered, on a rack over a sheet pan or in a roasting pan, for 4.5 hours. Check at the 90 minute mark; the surface should be uniformly golden and dripping fat into the pan. Baste with the pan juices every hour after the first 2 hours.'
    time_min: 270
  - text: 'Check the internal temperature in the thickest part of the meat (avoid the bone). It should read 88 to 92 °C / 190 to 197 °F. If lower, give another 20 to 30 minutes. The meat should pull back from the bone slightly and a fork inserted into the meat should meet no resistance.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Crank the oven to 230 °C / 450 °F (or 240 °C / 475 °F if your oven allows). Roast 25 to 35 minutes more, watching closely, until the scored skin has fully crackled, blistered, and turned deep mahogany. The cuerito is done when it lifts cleanly from the meat below in shattering shards. Watch for burn spots in the last 5 minutes.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Rest 30 minutes, loosely tented with foil. The juices redistribute and the meat firms up enough to pull cleanly. Lift off the cuerito (the entire crackled skin lifts as a single sheet); break it into 6 cm shards on a cutting board.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Shred the meat from the bone with two forks. Discard the bone (or save for stock). Pile the shredded meat on a platter, tuck the cuerito shards on top, and pour the pan juices (skimmed of fat) over the meat at the table. Serve with arroz con gandules and a green salad.'
    time_min: 10

notes: |
  Why the stab holes. A 3 kg / 7 lb pork shoulder is about 12 cm thick at the deepest part. Surface seasoning, even overnight, penetrates only 1 to 2 cm. The stab-hole (hueco) injection technique places marinade pockets throughout the meat, so when the long slow roast happens, the seasoning permeates outward from each hole and meets in the middle. A pernil seasoned only on the surface tastes bland in the center; with hueco, every shred is garlic-and-oregano-seasoned to the core.

  Why low and slow then hot. The 4.5 hour roast at 150 °C / 300 °F is the collagen-conversion phase; this is where the connective tissue in the shoulder melts to gelatin and the meat goes from chewy to shred-tender. The 30-minute roast at 230 °C / 450 °F at the end is the cuerito phase; this is where the skin dehydrates rapidly and the structural collagen-and-protein matrix of the pig skin puffs up into shattering crackling. Trying to do both at the same temperature fails one way or the other: low-only leaves the skin leathery; high-only burns the surface before the inside cooks through.

  The cuerito test. The cuerito is done when it lifts cleanly off the meat as a single rigid sheet and shatters when you break it on the cutting board. A bendy skin that does not crackle is undercooked; another 10 minutes at high heat will finish it. A black skin is burned; trim the burned spots, keep the good. A pernil without proper cuerito is a missed pernil; it is worth watching the oven for the final 30 minutes.

  Pairings. Canonical with arroz con gandules and a green salad (lettuce, tomato, avocado, with olive oil and vinegar). Coquito on the side for the holiday version. Leftover pernil makes Cuban sandwiches (use the leftover pork as the structural filling), tacos, breakfast hash, or pernil-and-yellow-rice bowls.

  Storage. Keeps 5 days refrigerated, separated into meat and crackling (the crackling softens in the fridge but can be re-crisped in a 200 °C / 400 °F oven for 5 minutes). Freezes well; portion shredded meat into 400 g containers. Reheat over low heat with a splash of water.
