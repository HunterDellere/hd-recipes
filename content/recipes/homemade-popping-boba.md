before_you_start:
  - 'Build the alginate bath the night before, or at minimum 4 hours ahead. Freshly blended alginate is full of trapped air bubbles that ruin pearl formation, the rest lets them rise out. Overnight in the refrigerator is best; this is the single most common skip-and-fail step.'
  - 'Once made, popping pearls hold 24 to 36 hours in syrup before the membranes thicken and lose the pop. Plan to use within a day of producing them.'
  - 'Test one droplet before producing a full batch. Pearl quality depends on the alginate bath viscosity, the calcium-juice temperature, and the drop height, a 30-second test pearl reveals problems before you have used up the whole calcium-flavored core.'

steps:
  - text: 'Build the alginate bath at least 4 hours before service. Sodium alginate clumps aggressively when sprinkled into water, two methods to avoid this: (1) blend it in with an immersion blender, or (2) pre-mix the alginate with a tablespoon of sugar before adding to water so the particles disperse. Whisk the 2.5 g / ¾ tsp sodium alginate into the 500 ml / 2 cups water using one of these methods until fully dissolved with no lumps. The solution will be visibly viscous, slightly cloudy. Cover and refrigerate at least 4 hours (overnight is better) to let trapped air bubbles rise out, bubbles in the bath cause pearls to float and deform instead of sinking and rounding.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Prepare the calcium-flavored core. Whisk the 5 g / 1 tsp calcium lactate into the 240 ml / 1 cup fruit juice until fully dissolved. If using citrus or pomegranate juice (anything below pH 3.5), also whisk in the 1 g / ¼ tsp sodium citrate to buffer the acidity, too-acidic liquids interfere with alginate gelation and produce broken or weeping pearls. Taste; add the 15 g / 1 tbsp sugar if the juice tastes too tart.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Test droplet. Transfer the calcium-flavored juice to a squeeze bottle or large dropper. Drop a single droplet from about 5 cm above into the alginate bath. After 30 seconds, lift it out with a slotted spoon. A correctly formed pearl is sphere-shaped, with a thin flexible membrane and a still-liquid center that you can feel sloshing when you press gently. If the pearl is flat or burst, the alginate bath has the wrong viscosity (add more alginate, blend, rest); if the membrane is too thick and the inside is fully set, you left it too long.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Produce the pearls. Drop the calcium-flavored juice into the alginate bath from the squeeze bottle, working in batches of 15 to 20 pearls so they do not touch each other. Touching pearls fuse together. After each batch sits for 60 to 90 seconds in the bath (longer for larger pearls, shorter for smaller), lift them out with a slotted spoon.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Rinse. Transfer the lifted pearls immediately into a bowl of cold water to halt further gelation. Swirl gently for 10 seconds, then transfer to a second bowl of clean cold water for another rinse. Rinsing removes the surface alginate; unrinsed pearls feel slimy on the tongue and continue to thicken their membranes during holding.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Hold. Drain the rinsed pearls and transfer to the holding syrup (60 ml juice + 120 ml water + 60 g sugar, dissolved). The syrup keeps the pearls from drying out and adds a flavored bath. Refrigerate; pearls hold 24 to 36 hours in syrup. After that the membrane slowly thickens and the texture goes from pop-and-burst to chewy. Use within 24 hours for the proper texture.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Reverse spherification (calcium in the food, alginate in the bath) is the right method for popping boba because the gelation stops when you remove the pearl from the bath. Basic spherification (alginate in the food, calcium in the bath) continues to gel after removal and the pearls fully set in an hour, fine for immediate service, useless for held drinks. Reverse spherification gives 24 to 36 hours of stable liquid centers.

  The 4-hour alginate bath rest is non-negotiable. Freshly blended alginate is full of tiny air bubbles; if you drop pearls into a bubbly bath they ride the bubbles to the surface and never sink to round properly. The rest lets the bubbles rise and clear. Overnight in the refrigerator is best.

  The calcium-lactate-to-juice ratio is fixed at about 2 percent by weight. More calcium and the pearls develop overly thick membranes that lose the pop. Less calcium and they never form cleanly. Weigh; don't volume-measure.

  Acidic juices need sodium citrate buffer or they refuse to form pearls. Citrus, pomegranate, cranberry, and any juice below pH 3.5 will pull alginate out of solution as alginic acid (the protonated form), which doesn't gel cleanly. The pinch of sodium citrate raises the pH back into the working range without affecting flavor.
