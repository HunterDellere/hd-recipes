before_you_start:
  - 'Use day-old rice, refrigerated overnight uncovered. Freshly cooked rice is too wet; the grains stick together and the dish becomes a sticky mass. The cold-rest tightens the starch granules (retrogradation) and the grains stay separate in the wok. If you must use fresh rice, spread it on a sheet pan and cool 30 minutes in the fridge before frying.'
  - 'Toast the terasi (3 g / ½ tsp wrapped in foil over a burner for 60 seconds per side) before pounding the bumbu. Raw terasi tastes harshly fishy in the finished dish.'
  - 'Have everything chopped and within arm''s reach of the wok before turning on the heat. Once the bumbu hits the pan, the dish is 6 minutes from done; stopping to dice an aromatic will burn the paste.'
  - 'Cook the eggs in a separate pan in parallel, sunny-side-up or over-easy with crisp lacy edges. The egg is structural to the dish (the runny yolk dresses the rice on the plate); do not skip it.'

steps:
  - text: 'Pound or blend the 80 g shallot, 25 g garlic, 30 g chili, and 3 g toasted terasi to a coarse wet paste, about 2 minutes in a food processor or 4 minutes in a mortar. The texture should be slightly chunky, not smooth puree; you want some shallot bite remaining in the finished dish.'
    technique: 'spice-paste-pounding'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide wok or 30 cm / 12 inch skillet over medium-high heat until shimmering. Add the bumbu paste; it will sputter. Fry, stirring constantly, until the raw shallot-chili smell shifts to a deeper aromatic and the paste darkens from bright orange-pink to deep brick-orange, about 3 minutes. Oil will visibly separate around the edges of the paste.'
    technique: 'spice-paste-frying'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'If using shrimp: push the bumbu to one side of the pan, add the 200 g shrimp to the bare side, sear 60 seconds per side until just turning opaque. Toss with the bumbu and remove to a plate; the shrimp finishes cooking in the residual heat. If using chicken instead, dice fine and cook 3 minutes until opaque, then remove.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Crack the 2 beaten eggs directly into the same pan with the remaining bumbu. Stir vigorously for 30 seconds to scramble; the eggs cook into soft curds coated in spice paste. Do not separate from the bumbu.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the 700 g cold day-old rice, breaking up any remaining clumps with your hands or a wide spatula as you add it. Toss aggressively with two spatulas or wok tools for 90 seconds to coat every grain in the bumbu-egg mixture and to drive off surface moisture. The rice should look pale-orange and individually grained at this point.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Drizzle the 45 g / 3 tbsp kecap manis and the 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy sauce directly over the rice. Toss for 2 to 3 minutes, scraping the bottom of the pan continuously; the kecap manis caramelizes onto the grains and the rice darkens to a deep mahogany. This is the structural color and flavor moment of the dish, the high sugar content of the kecap manis browns aggressively on direct heat. Add the 80 g shredded cabbage (if using) in the last 60 seconds to wilt just slightly.'
    technique: 'wok-stir-fry'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Return the cooked shrimp (or chicken) to the pan. Add the 40 g sliced scallion and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. Toss 30 seconds to combine and warm through. Taste, the dish should read sweet-savory-front (kecap manis), with the chili heat building after, scallion-and-shallot in the background. If flat, add 5 g / 1 tsp more kecap manis; if too sweet, 5 g / 1 tsp more light soy or fish sauce.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'While the rice finishes, fry the 4 topper eggs in a separate skillet. Heat 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil over medium-high until shimmering. Crack the eggs in one at a time, leaving space; cook 90 seconds without moving until the whites are set with crisp lacy edges and the yolk is still bright and runny. Salt the whites with a pinch as they cook.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Plate. Mound a portion of nasi goreng on each plate, top with one fried egg (yolk facing up, ready to be broken into the rice), scatter a heavy pinch of fried shallots over the egg, arrange a few cucumber and tomato slices alongside, and place a lime wedge and a small mound of sambal on the side. Serve immediately. The diner breaks the egg yolk into the rice, squeezes the lime over, and adds sambal to taste.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why day-old rice. Freshly cooked rice carries roughly 65 to 70 percent water; the grains are soft, sticky, and clump when stirred in a hot pan. Refrigerating cooked rice for 8 to 24 hours triggers starch retrogradation, the amylose molecules recrystallize into a tighter structure that holds less water and resists clumping when reheated. The result is grains that stay separate in the wok, fry crisp on their exteriors, and absorb the kecap manis caramelization without going to a sticky paste. If you must use freshly cooked rice, spread it in a single layer on a sheet pan and cool 30 minutes in the fridge before frying; the partial retrogradation is enough.

  Why the kecap manis matters more than the soy. The defining flavor and color of nasi goreng comes from kecap manis, the thick palm-sugar-sweetened Indonesian soy sauce. A nasi goreng made with regular soy sauce (light or dark) and no kecap manis is functionally a different dish, closer to Chinese fried rice; the high-sugar caramelization that produces the mahogany color and the sweet-savory-front flavor signature requires the palm sugar content. If you only have one Indonesian condiment in the cupboard, kecap manis is the one.

  Why toast the terasi. Raw terasi (fermented shrimp paste) smells aggressively fishy and ammonia-tinged; the toast (60 seconds per side in foil over an open flame) transforms it into a deep, savory-funky umami that integrates seamlessly into the bumbu. Skipping the toast is the single most common reason home-cooked nasi goreng tastes raw and one-dimensional; the terasi never fully cooks out during the brief stir-fry phase.

  Why the bumbu fry takes 3 minutes. The wet aromatic paste has to lose its raw edge before the rice goes in. Underdone bumbu produces a dish where the chili reads harsh, the shallot tastes oniony, and the terasi stays funky-raw. The 3-minute fry develops Maillard products on the shallot-garlic-chili matrix, blooms the spice-paste oils, and reduces the water content of the paste so the rice does not steam when it hits the pan.

  The egg architecture. There are two distinct egg moments in this dish. The scrambled-into-the-bumbu egg (step 4) is structural; it coats the grains with a thin egg-and-bumbu film that gives the rice its glossy, slightly-rich mouthfeel. The fried topper egg (step 8) is finishing; the runny yolk breaks over the rice at the table and dresses each bite with a yolk-kecap-manis emulsion. Both eggs are non-negotiable for the canonical presentation; the scrambled egg cannot replace the topper, and the topper cannot replace the scrambled.

  Why the cucumber and tomato. The raw vegetable garnish is the cooling foil to the spice-and-caramel weight of the rice. It is not decoration; eating a slice of cool cucumber between bites of nasi goreng is the canonical Indonesian eating rhythm, the way Vietnamese diners alternate pho bites with bites of herbs from the plate. Skipping the cucumber and tomato makes the dish feel heavier and one-noted.
