steps:
  - text: 'Soak the 400 g / 2 cups dried black beans in 2000 ml cold water for 8 to 12 hours. Drain.'
    time_min: 720
  - text: 'In a heavy-bottomed pot combine the drained beans with 2000 ml / 8 cups fresh cold water, the 2 bay leaves, and the 60 g whole green bell pepper half. Bring to a hard boil, then reduce to a steady simmer and cook 75 to 90 minutes, partially covered, until the beans are fully tender (a bean crushed between two fingers should mash with no resistance). Do not salt yet. Top up with hot water if the level drops below the beans.'
    time_min: 85
  - text: 'When the beans are tender, fish out and discard the bay leaves and the whole pepper half. Reserve 250 ml / 1 cup of the bean cooking liquid in a measuring cup. Drain the beans into a colander set over a second bowl to catch the remaining liquid (also reserve).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Wipe out the pot. Heat the 45 ml / 3 tbsp olive oil over medium heat. Add the 180 g diced onion and 150 g diced green bell pepper; sweat 6 to 8 minutes until the onion is translucent and the pepper has softened with light golden edges. Do not brown aggressively; the sofrito should be soft and aromatic, not Maillard-toasted.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the 25 g / 6 cloves minced garlic; cook 60 seconds until fragrant. Add the 5 g / 2 tsp cumin and 3 g / 1 tsp oregano; stir 30 seconds to bloom the spices in the hot oil. The oil should turn deep brick-red and the kitchen should smell aggressively of cumin.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 60 ml / ¼ cup dry white wine; scrape the sofrito loose from the pot bottom. Cook 90 seconds until the wine has reduced and the alcohol has burned off (sniff; raw alcohol smell should be gone).'
    technique: 'deglazing'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Return the drained beans to the pot, along with the 250 ml reserved bean cooking liquid. Add the 10 g / 2½ tsp salt, 5 g / 1 tsp sugar, and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper. Stir to combine. Bring to a steady simmer, partially covered, and cook 20 to 25 minutes for the flavors to meld and the liquid to thicken. The beans should look slightly soupy with a glossy black gravy, not dry. Add more reserved bean broth if the pot looks dry.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Off heat, stir in the 15 ml / 1 tbsp red wine vinegar. The acid brightens the dish and is the structural finish; without it the beans taste flat and one-note. Taste; adjust salt and vinegar if needed. Rest covered 5 minutes before serving.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Serve over white rice, with diced raw onion, lime wedges, and a splash of additional olive oil at the table. The beans are also the structural base for "moros y cristianos" (cooked with the rice together rather than served alongside).'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why no salt during the bean cook. Salt in the simmer liquid bonds with the magnesium and calcium ions in the bean skin pectin and toughens the skins. Beans cooked in salted water can take twice as long to soften, and often refuse to ever fully soften. Add salt only once the beans are already tender; the salt then penetrates the cooked beans without affecting the skin.

  The sugar-and-vinegar finish. Black bean skins carry tannins and a slightly bitter compound that reads as a faint sourness in the back of the palate. A teaspoon of sugar lifts the bitter compounds out of perception by adding a bass-note sweetness; a tablespoon of vinegar at the end brightens the dish and balances the sugar. The pair are the Cuban signature finish; many home cooks skip them and produce flat-tasting beans without understanding why.

  Why reuse the bean broth. The cooking liquid carries dissolved bean proteins, starch, and bean-pigment anthocyanins that give the finished dish its glossy black color, its slightly thickened body, and an umami bass note. Pouring off the broth and refilling with water produces a paler, thinner, blander dish.

  The Cuban green pepper rule. Cuban sofrito uses green bell pepper (slightly bitter, vegetal); Puerto Rican sofrito uses red bell pepper and ají dulce (sweet, fruity). Using red pepper in Cuban beans makes the dish read Puerto Rican; technically fine, structurally wrong.

  Storage. Improves dramatically overnight; the flavors integrate and the sofrito mellows into the beans. Keeps 5 days refrigerated. Reheat over low heat with a splash of water or broth. Freezes well; portion into 500 g containers.
