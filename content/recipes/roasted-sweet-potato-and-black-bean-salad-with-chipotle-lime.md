steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 230 °C / 450 °F with a rimmed sheet pan inside. The hot pan is structural; the 700 g / 2 sweet potato cubes laid on a cold pan steam under their own moisture for the first 5 minutes and never develop the deep caramelized edges that the dish depends on. A preheated pan starts the Maillard reaction at the cube-metal interface from the moment of contact.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'In a wide bowl, toss the 700 g / 2 sweet potato cubes with the 22 g / 1½ tbsp olive oil, the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt, the 2 g / 1 tsp ground cumin, and the 2 g / 1 tsp smoked paprika. Use clean hands; rub the spices and oil into every face of every cube. The oil and spice film should be uniform; bare patches of cube will read pale and unseasoned next to the well-coated faces.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Pull the hot sheet pan from the oven; spread the seasoned cubes in a single layer with at least 1 cm between cubes. Crowding traps steam and prevents the char; if the cubes do not fit on one pan with breathing room, use two pans on staggered racks and rotate at the halfway point. Roast 22 to 25 minutes total: do not stir or flip for the first 14 minutes (the underside develops the deep mahogany char in the un-disturbed initial contact); flip with a thin spatula at minute 14 and roast another 8 to 11 minutes until the second side is also deeply browned and the cube is tender at the center when pierced with a paring knife. Pull to a wide plate to cool; do not stack hot cubes on each other, the steam softens the char.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'While the sweet potato roasts, char the corn. Heat a heavy cast-iron or carbon-steel skillet over medium-high heat for 3 minutes until a flick of water snaps and evaporates within 2 seconds. Add the 6 g / 1½ tsp avocado oil; swirl to film the pan. Add the 200 g / ~1¼ cup corn kernels in a single layer; do not stir for the first 90 seconds (the kernels in contact with the metal need uninterrupted time to char). After 90 seconds, toss with a wooden spoon and let the second face char another 60 to 90 seconds. The kernels should now be mottled black and gold across most of the surface, smell deeply sweet-and-charred, and squeak slightly between the teeth when bitten. Tip onto a plate; salt with the 1 g / ¼ tsp kosher salt while still hot.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Macerate the red onion. In a small bowl, combine the 80 g / ½ medium diced red onion with 30 g / 2 tbsp lime juice (additional, separate from the dressing). Press the onion gently with the back of a spoon so the lime juice fully wets every dice. Let stand 5 to 10 minutes while the rest of the components come together. The lime acid denatures the onion sulfur compounds (allyl propyl disulfide and related volatiles) and shifts the onion from sharp-and-aggressive to tart-and-mild; the dice also turns a brighter pink-magenta from the anthocyanins reacting with the acid.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Build the dressing. In a small bowl or wide-mouth jar, mince together 1 to 1½ chipotle chiles and 1 tbsp of the adobo sauce on a cutting board with a chef knife until uniform paste, ~24 g total. Scrape into the bowl. Add the 45 g / 3 tbsp lime juice, the 14 g / 2 tsp honey, the 6 g / 2 cloves microplaned garlic, the 1 g / ½ tsp ground cumin, and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. Whisk vigorously for 15 to 20 seconds; the salt dissolves into the lime acid, the garlic mucilage starts to thicken the mixture, and the chipotle-adobo paste distributes evenly. Salt always goes into the acid phase first; salt does not dissolve in oil and reads as gritty hot spots if added at the end.'
    technique: 'vinaigrette-emulsion'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'While whisking constantly with one hand, stream in the 75 g / ⅓ cup olive oil with the other in a thin steady drizzle (5 to 8 seconds for the full pour, not faster). The chipotle-adobo paste is the emulsifier here, replacing the mustard role; the adobo carries enough tomato-vinegar mucilage and pepper-pulp pectin to coat the oil droplets. The dressing tightens visibly into an opaque rust-orange emulsion; by the time the last of the oil is in, it should ribbon off the whisk. Whisk an additional 15 to 20 seconds. Taste; it should read smoky-acidic-sweet in that order, fully salted, with the cumin and garlic as background. If it reads flat, more salt; if it tastes only acidic, more honey; if it reads only sweet, more lime; if it lacks the smoke, more chipotle.'
    technique: 'vinaigrette-emulsion'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Toast the 50 g / ⅓ cup pepitas. In a small dry skillet over medium heat, toast the seeds for 3 to 4 minutes, agitating the pan every 30 seconds. The seeds will pop audibly (like miniature popcorn), turn from olive-green to mottled tan-brown, and release a pronounced toasted-pumpkin-seed aroma. Pull immediately when the popping subsides and the color is set; the window from perfect to scorched is about 30 seconds. Tip onto a plate to cool; the seeds crisp further as they cool.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Season the beans. In a wide bowl, toss the 250 g drained-weight black beans (from 1 × 425 g / 15 oz can drained and rinsed in hot water) with the 1 g / ½ tsp ground cumin and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. The hot-water rinse warms the beans enough to bloom the cumin without active heat; cold beans straight from the colander leave the cumin reading raw and dusty.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Compose the salad. In a wide shallow serving bowl or platter (not a deep mixing bowl, the salad reads better spread thin), layer in this order: the 250 g seasoned black beans across the bottom, the warm 620 g roasted sweet potato cubes on top, the 200 g charred corn scattered across, the 80 g macerated red onion drained from its lime soak (reserve the soak liquid for another dressing or discard), the 20 g chopped cilantro. Drizzle two-thirds of the dressing across the top; toss gently with a large spoon, 4 to 6 turns, just enough to coat without crushing the cubes or breaking the beans. The salad should glisten with the dressing without pooling at the bottom of the bowl.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Finish. Crumble the 60 g / ½ cup cotija across the top in 5 to 8 mm chunks; resist mixing the cheese in, the contrast between unadorned crumbles and dressed vegetables is structural. Scatter the 50 g / ⅓ cup toasted pepitas across the top. Dice and add the 150 g / 1 medium avocado last, in 1.5 cm pieces, gently nudged into place rather than tossed. Drizzle the remaining third of the dressing across the cheese and pepitas; this last drizzle hits the dry components and pulls the salad together visually. Taste; the dish should read smoky-sweet-acidic with crisp-and-creamy textural counterpoints; if flat, more lime; if undersalted, a small additional sprinkle of cotija (cotija carries salt as well as flavor); if heat is missing, a few additional dabs of straight adobo sauce from the chipotle can. Serve within 20 minutes; the salad holds for 1 hour at room temperature but the sweet potato softens further past that point.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  The thesis is that aggressive char and aggressive smoke are the only way to make sweet potato salad read as more than a baby-food puree with garnishes. Sweet potato has powerful sweetness (sucrose plus enzymatically generated maltose; see the ingredient page for the beta-amylase chemistry) and a soft mealy texture once cooked; without a counterweight, the dish reads as a one-note sweet mash. The chipotle-and-adobo dressing supplies the smoke and the acid; the charred corn supplies the Maillard counterpoint at a different scale; the cotija supplies the salt and the aged-cheese umami; the toasted pepitas supply the textural break. Every component is pulling weight against the inherent softness of the base.

  Why a hot sheet pan. Sweet potato is roughly 77% water; cubes laid on a cold pan release moisture into the gap between cube and metal during the first 5 minutes of preheating, which boils as steam and prevents the Maillard browning at the contact face. A pan preheated to 230 °C reaches the cube-metal interface at the same temperature as the oven; the surface starches and free amino acids brown immediately rather than steaming. The difference between hot-pan and cold-pan roasted sweet potato cubes is visible at minute 22: the hot-pan cubes have a deep mahogany char on the contact face; the cold-pan cubes are uniformly tan with no individual char patches. The flavor difference follows.

  Why the chipotle is the emulsifier. Vinaigrettes typically rely on mustard as the emulsifier; the mustard mucilage (a glucosinolate-derived polysaccharide) coats oil droplets and stabilizes the suspension. Chipotle in adobo carries a similar mucilage load through different chemistry: the rehydrated chipotle pepper pulp contributes pectin and hemicellulose, the adobo sauce contributes tomato pectin and vinegar acid, and together they provide the same droplet-coating function with substantially more flavor than mustard. The dressing emulsifies tighter than a comparable lime-and-honey vinaigrette without an adobo base; the emulsion holds for 4 to 6 hours at room temperature without separation.

  Why three contacts of cumin. Cumin is layered across the salad in three doses: 2 g on the sweet potato (toasts in the oven heat), 1 g on the beans (blooms in the residual rinse warmth), and 1 g in the dressing (background note in the lime acid). Each dose is sub-detection on its own; together they create a cumin signature that reads as the salad's defining warm-spice note without ever feeling cumin-heavy. This layering pattern (low-dose spice across multiple components) is the standard Mexican approach and is why authentic Mexican food rarely tastes "cumin-forward" the way Tex-Mex chili powder applications do; the spice is everywhere at low doses rather than concentrated in one dose.

  Why pre-soak the red onion in additional lime. Raw red onion in this salad reads as the dominant flavor without the soak; the sulfur volatiles (allyl propyl disulfide, propyl mercaptan) overwhelm the chipotle and the cumin. The 5-to-10-minute lime soak denatures these compounds (the acid reacts with the disulfides and shifts them to less volatile forms) and shifts the onion from sharp-and-aggressive to tart-and-mild. The same trick works on white onion, shallot, or scallion in any raw application; standard practice in Mexican raw salads and salsas.

  Why warm-not-hot for assembly. The roasted sweet potato should be warm (10 to 15 minutes off the pan) when it hits the dressing; warm cubes extract the chipotle aromatic and the lime acid more efficiently than cold cubes, while staying cool enough not to melt the cotija or wilt the cilantro. Hot cubes (straight from the pan) cook the corn slightly, melt the cheese into the dressing, and produce a salad that reads as a wilted side rather than a composed warm-and-cool dish. The 10-minute rest is the inflection.

  How this salad is served. The dish reads as a main course for 4 with no other accompaniment; as a side for 6 alongside grilled or roasted meat (carne asada, pork al pastor, grilled chicken thighs); as a taco filling spooned into warm corn tortillas with extra cotija and a squeeze of lime; or as a tostada topping mounded on a crisp tortilla shell. Leftovers hold reasonably well in the refrigerator for 24 hours (the avocado browns, fold in fresh dice if serving the next day; the pepitas soften, scatter fresh toasted pepitas at serving); past 48 hours the sweet potato softens further and the salad reads more like a warm-leftover-bowl than a composed plate, still good but in a different register.

  About cumin layering, smoked paprika, and Spanish-vs-Mexican smoke. The smoked paprika here is pimentón de la Vera (Spanish), not Hungarian; pimentón is oak-smoked and contributes a wood-smoke aromatic that aligns with the chipotle (also smoke-cured, over pecan or oak in Mexican production). Hungarian paprika is typically not smoked and contributes a sweet pepper note without the smoke; using Hungarian paprika produces a salad that reads thinner on the smoke axis and where the chipotle is doing all the smoke-load work alone. Use Spanish smoked paprika; widely available at supermarkets and worth keeping in the pantry as a workhorse spice.
