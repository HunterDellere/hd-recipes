before_you_start:
  - 'The rice needs a 30-minute cold-water soak after rinsing and before cooking. Skipping the soak produces uneven hydration and the rice will not clump correctly for shaping.'
  - 'Shape onigiri in a 5 to 15 minute window after the rice rest, when the rice has cooled to 60 C / 140 F (warm but tolerable on the hands for 10 seconds). Hotter steam-cooks your palms and dries the rice, cooler will not bind.'
  - 'Prep the fillings before the rice finishes cooking. Once the rice hits the shaping window you cannot stop to flake salmon or pit umeboshi.'
  - 'If you are wrapping nori for later eating, store the nori sheets separately and wrap only at the moment of service. Nori touching rice for more than 20 minutes goes leathery.'

source:
  name: 'Adapted from Japanese home-kitchen tradition'
  note: 'Onigiri are documented as far back as the Heian period (8th to 12th century) as portable rice for travelers, soldiers, and field workers. The triangular form is the most common modern shape but balls (maru), cylinders (tawara), and flat discs all have regional precedents. The convenience-store onigiri, with its plastic-wrapped nori-sheet-separated-from-rice design, is a 1978 innovation by 7-Eleven Japan that solved the crisp-nori-vs-moist-rice problem.'

steps:
  - text: 'Cook the rice. Place the 360 g rice in a fine-mesh sieve and rinse under cold running water, swishing with your fingers, for 30 seconds. Transfer to a bowl, cover with cold water, swirl with a hand, and pour off the cloudy water. Repeat 4 to 5 times until the water runs nearly clear. Drain. Combine with the 430 ml fresh cold water in a rice cooker (or a covered heavy saucepan) and let soak undisturbed 30 minutes. Cook on the standard white-rice setting (or, in a saucepan: bring to a boil over high heat, drop to lowest heat, cover tightly, cook 12 minutes; pull from heat and let stand covered 10 minutes). The rest is non-optional ,  the post-cook 10-minute sit is what redistributes moisture and gives the rice the characteristic gentle-clump texture that holds an onigiri shape.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Set up the shaping station. Pour the 60 ml cold water into a small bowl and place beside the salt dish. Set the cooked rice in a wide bowl uncovered and let it cool to 60°C / 140°F (warm but not steaming-hot ,  your hands should tolerate it for 10 seconds without flinching). Cooler rice will not bind; hotter rice will burn your hands and steam-out the texture. The shaping window is roughly 5 to 15 minutes after the cooker timer ends, depending on ambient temperature.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Form an onigiri. Wet both hands in the tezu water (this is what keeps the rice from sticking to your skin and is non-negotiable). Pinch a small amount of fine sea salt ,  about ¼ g, a 2-fingertip pinch ,  and rub between your palms. Scoop ~90 g cooked rice (a generous ½ cup) into your wet, salted left palm. Press a shallow well in the center with your right thumb. Place ~10 g of filling in the well (one umeboshi plum; 1 tbsp flaked salmon; 1 tbsp okaka; etc.). Cup your left palm slightly to wrap rice around the filling and seal the top with a few grains of additional rice if needed. Now form the triangle: place the rice ball in the cup of your left palm with the seam down. Gently press with your right palm cupped at a 60-degree angle to form one face of the triangle; rotate 120 degrees and repeat for the second face; rotate again for the third. The motion is firm enough to compact (the onigiri must hold together when picked up) but gentle enough to keep individual grains visible (a crushed onigiri is mush). Total shaping time: 30 to 45 seconds per onigiri.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Repeat for remaining onigiri. Re-wet hands and re-salt between each. Yield: 8 onigiri at ~90 g rice each, with one of the chosen fillings in each. If forming all 8 with the same filling, you have a uniform batch; for a mixed bento, alternate fillings and mark which is which (a small dot of black sesame on top denotes umeboshi; salmon-flake garnish for salmon; etc.).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Wrap with nori at the moment of eating. Cut each yaki-nori sheet into 4 long strips (5 cm × 19 cm each). Wrap one strip around the wide bottom edge of an onigiri so the rice triangle sits with two-thirds of its surface exposed and the bottom third wrapped ,  the convenience-store-style wrap that keeps your hand clean and adds a strip of nori to each bite. The nori softens within 2 to 3 minutes of contact with rice, so for a packed lunch wrap each onigiri in plastic film with the nori strip folded separately and assemble at eating time. For immediate eating, wrap and serve.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Salt finish (optional). Some onigiri traditions sprinkle a few flakes of finishing salt on the outside of the rice triangle just before wrapping. This produces a stronger first-bite salt sensation and a clearer flavor contrast with the filling. Use a 5-finger pinch (~0.3 g) of fine sea salt or shio-koji for this. Skip if the onigiri is filled with already-salty contents (umeboshi, okaka).'
    time_min: 1

modifications:
  - for: 'shape'
    to: 'maru (round) or tawara (cylinder)'
    how: 'Form the warm rice into a sphere by rolling between two cupped wet-and-salted palms (maru) or into a 3 cm × 6 cm cylinder by shaping against a flat surface (tawara). Round onigiri are common at picnics and in school lunches; tawara onigiri are easier to grill (yaki-onigiri) because they have flat surfaces. The shaping rules are the same; only the geometry changes.'
    kind: 'texture'
  - for: 'cooking method'
    to: 'yaki-onigiri (grilled)'
    how: 'After shaping, brush the formed onigiri with a thin coat of soy sauce mixed with 1 tsp mirin per 1 tbsp soy. Place on a hot grill, dry skillet, or under a broiler for 2 to 3 minutes per face until the surface is crisped and lightly charred (the soy sauce caramelizes; the rice surface turns crackly). The interior stays soft. Yaki-onigiri can be eaten plain (the toasted exterior carries enough flavor) or with a strip of nori; the filling, if any, should be assertive (okaka, miso) because the toasting adds substantial flavor of its own.'
    kind: 'texture'
  - for: 'filling'
    to: 'mentaiko (spicy pollock roe)'
    how: 'Add 80 g mentaiko (or tarako for milder), with the membrane removed and the eggs scraped into a paste. Use 10 g per onigiri as the filling. The roe contributes salt, umami, and a faint chili heat; pairs especially well with a strip of nori because the marine notes layer.'
    kind: 'protein'
  - for: 'occasion'
    to: 'onigirazu (rice sandwich)'
    how: 'Lay a full nori sheet (cut to a square if needed) on plastic wrap. Spread half the rice (~180 g) in the center as a square layer. Add a substantial filling: tonkatsu, fried egg, lettuce, ham, etc. Top with the other half of the rice as a second square layer. Fold the nori up around the rice from all four sides like wrapping a present. Wait 2 minutes for the nori to soften and grip, then slice in half with a wet knife. The 2014-onward "rice sandwich" format is photogenic, more substantial than triangle onigiri, and accommodates fillings that would not fit in a traditional onigiri.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Salt and bacterial protection. Onigiri historically traveled with samurai, farmers, and travelers because they keep at room temperature for 6 to 8 hours without refrigeration ,  a remarkable property for cooked rice. The mechanism is the salt: the salt-rubbed surface and the salted/acidic fillings (umeboshi, salted salmon, soy-glazed okaka) suppress bacterial growth on the rice surface. A bare unsalted rice ball at room temperature is not safe for a multi-hour bento; the salt-and-filling protocol is what makes the dish portable. This is also why convenience-store onigiri use surface salt liberally and lean toward salty-acidic fillings.

  The shaping window. Rice that is too hot will steam your hands and lose moisture too fast, producing dry onigiri. Rice that is too cold will not bind ,  the amylopectin in the cooked grains needs to be still slightly thermally active to clump under pressure. The 60°C / 140°F window is roughly 5 to 15 minutes after the rice cooker beeps. If you miss the window, you can revive cooled rice by microwaving briefly (30 seconds for a portion) or by warming over low heat in a saucepan with a few drops of water; the texture will be slightly worse than fresh-from-the-cooker but workable.

  Tezu (water-and-salt for hands). The tezu mixture is sometimes prepared as a lightly-salted water (1 tsp salt per cup water) so the hand-wetting step also carries salt. This is a regional/personal preference; the alternative ,  wet hands with plain water, then pinch dry salt onto palms before each onigiri ,  gives more control over salt level. Both are correct.

  Why a triangle. The triangle shape is functional, not aesthetic: three flat faces are easier to compact uniformly than a sphere, the form factor packs flat in a bento box, and the apex makes a natural starting point for biting. Some traditions form a triangle that mimics a mountain peak (yama) for symbolic-religious reasons; the Heian-period origin of onigiri is associated with offerings to mountain deities. The convenience-store standard form is a tall, narrow triangle (5 cm × 7 cm × 5 cm) ,  taller than a casual home-shaped onigiri.

  Nori timing. The convenience-store plastic-wrap design ,  two layers of plastic with the nori sandwiched between, joined to the rice only when the eater pulls a tab ,  is the engineering solution to the crisp-nori-vs-moist-rice tradeoff. For home onigiri eaten immediately, wrap and serve. For onigiri packed for lunch, wrap each in plastic with the nori strip folded into a separate compartment of the same wrap or stored in a small dry bag, and assemble at the eating point.

  Reheating and reuse. Day-old onigiri are best converted into yaki-onigiri (toasted; see modifications) rather than eaten cold from the fridge, because refrigerated rice retrogrades ,  the amylopectin recrystallizes ,  and the texture goes hard and chalky. The toasting step rehydrates the surface and produces a different but legitimate texture. Refrigerated onigiri keep 2 days; freeze for longer storage in plastic wrap, microwave briefly to thaw, then toast.
