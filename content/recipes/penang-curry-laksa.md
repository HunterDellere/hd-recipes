before_you_start:
  - 'Poach the chicken first. The poaching liquid becomes the base stock for the laksa broth, and the cooked chicken needs to cool slightly before shredding. Start the chicken 45 minutes before the rempah goes into the pan.'
  - 'Toast the belacan (5 g / 1 tsp wrapped in foil over a burner for 60 seconds per side) before pounding the rempah. Raw belacan stays harshly fishy in the finished broth.'
  - 'Cook the noodles last and serve immediately. Thick rice noodles overcook fast and the textural contrast between the firm noodle and the rich broth is structural to laksa.'
  - 'Have every garnish (tofu puffs, beansprouts, sliced cucumber, lime wedges, sambal, fried shallots) ready and plated before assembling each bowl. Laksa is a composed dish, not a stew; assembly is the final act and must happen fast while the broth and noodles are both hot.'

source:
  name: 'Adapted from Penang Peranakan (Nyonya) tradition'
  note: 'Two main laksa families exist in Malaysia. Curry laksa (this dish, common in Penang and across Peninsular Malaysia) is coconut-rich, spice-paste-driven, and assertively spicy. Asam laksa (Penang-specific) is tamarind-and-fish-based, no coconut, sour-forward, structurally a different dish. Singaporean katong laksa is a curry-laksa variant with cut-up short noodles eaten with a spoon only. The Peranakan (Straits Chinese, Nyonya) lineage applied Chinese noodle and pork techniques to Malay spice pastes; curry laksa is the canonical Peranakan dish.'

steps:
  - text: 'Poach the chicken. Place the 800 g bone-in skin-on chicken thighs, 1800 ml / 7½ cups water, 20 g bruised ginger, 30 g bruised scallion whites, and 8 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt in a pot. Bring to a bare simmer (not boil); reduce heat and hold at a tremor for 30 to 35 minutes until the chicken reads 75°C / 165°F at the bone. Skim any scum from the surface during the first 5 minutes; clear stock is the goal.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Pull the chicken from the broth with tongs; cool 10 minutes until safe to handle. Strain the broth through a fine sieve and reserve, you should have about 1400 ml / 6 cups; if less, top up with water. Shred the chicken meat off the bone in 1 cm strips with two forks (skin discarded or reserved for the cook). Set aside.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Build the rempah. Drain the soaked 25 g chilies, reserving 60 ml / ¼ cup of the soaking liquid. Pound or blend the chilies, 150 g shallot, 40 g garlic, 40 g sliced lemongrass heart, 30 g galangal, 4 g turmeric, 30 g candlenut, 6 g coriander, and 5 g toasted belacan to a smooth paste, adding chili soaking liquid as needed for blender flow. Target is a thick, smooth, brick-orange paste; about 5 to 6 minutes of pulse-and-scrape work.'
    technique: 'spice-paste-pounding'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Fry the rempah. Heat the 60 g / 4 tbsp neutral oil in a wide heavy pan or wok over medium heat. Add the rempah paste; it will sputter. Fry, stirring constantly, until the raw chili-shallot smell shifts to a deep, jammy, almost-caramelized aroma and the paste darkens from brick-orange to deep brick-red, 12 to 15 minutes. Oil will visibly separate at the edges and pool around the paste. This is the structural flavor-development step, undercooked rempah produces a thin, raw-tasting laksa; well-fried rempah is the foundation of canonical laksa depth.'
    technique: 'spice-paste-frying'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Pour in the reserved 1400 ml chicken broth and bring to a simmer. Stir to dissolve the rempah into the broth; the liquid will turn a brilliant orange-red. Add the 800 ml / 2 cans coconut milk, 15 g / 1 tbsp palm sugar, and 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce. Bring back to a bare simmer (do not boil hard, the coconut emulsion breaks above 95°C / 200°F). Simmer gently 20 minutes to integrate; the broth should thicken to a velvety, slightly-glossy consistency that coats a spoon. Taste, add the 5 g / 1 tsp additional salt if needed; the broth should read deep-spicy-front, with coconut richness and a sweet undertone, salt-balanced. Adjust acid at the table with lime.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Add the 80 g tofu puffs to the simmering broth; they absorb broth and soften over 5 minutes. After 5 minutes, add the 300 g shrimp; they cook in 90 seconds to 2 minutes, pull the pot off heat as soon as the shrimp are pink and opaque, residual heat finishes them. The shredded chicken goes in to warm through (it is already cooked) for the last 30 seconds.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Cook the 400 g rice noodles in a separate pot of boiling water according to package directions (usually 4 to 6 minutes for thick rice noodles, less for thin). Drain and rinse briefly in cold water to stop the cook and remove surface starch; drain again. Divide among 6 bowls.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Assemble each bowl. Mound noodles in the bottom. Top with a portion of beansprouts. Ladle hot broth over (about 1½ cups per bowl), making sure each diner gets shrimp, tofu puffs, and shredded chicken. Top with cucumber matchsticks, a heavy pinch of fried shallots, a sprinkle of scallion green. Serve with a lime wedge and a small mound of sambal on the side. Diners squeeze the lime over the broth, mix the sambal in to escalate heat, and eat with chopsticks (noodles) and spoon (broth).'

notes: |
  Why the rempah fry takes 12 to 15 minutes. The wet aromatic paste must fully lose its raw edge before broth goes in. Underdone rempah produces a laksa that reads thin and harsh; the chili stays raw, the shallot tastes oniony, the candlenut never integrates. The 12-to-15-minute fry develops the Maillard products on the shallot-garlic-chili matrix, renders the candlenut oil out into the paste, blooms the turmeric and coriander, and concentrates the spice oils. Stop when the paste darkens visibly and oil pools at the edges; this is the textural and visual signal that the rempah is done.

  Why coconut milk goes in after the rempah-broth integration. The full-fat coconut milk has to bloom at low temperatures; boiling above 95°C / 200°F breaks the natural emulsion and the coconut fat separates out as a layer of greasy orange oil on top of a thinner watery broth. The 20-minute bare simmer at low heat integrates the coconut milk into the broth without breaking; the result is a velvety, slightly-glossy texture that coats a spoon. If the broth ever boils hard, the fix is to whisk in 1 tbsp cornstarch slurried in cold water; it will restabilize the emulsion but at the cost of slight thickening.

  Why the chicken poach first (and not chicken stock from a carton). The 35-minute poach of bone-in skin-on chicken in salted aromatic water produces a clean, gelatin-rich stock with chicken-fat character that is the structural broth base for laksa. Cartoned chicken stock lacks the fat and gelatin; the finished laksa reads thinner and less rich. The chicken meat that comes out of the poach is also the canonical shredded protein in the bowl; using leftover roast chicken is not the same texturally. Skipping the poach and starting from a carton works if time-pressed; the result is acceptable but not canonical.

  Why thick rice noodles. The traditional laksa noodle (5 mm flat or round rice noodle) carries the broth in the way a thinner noodle does not; each strand drags broth into the eating bite and the textural contrast between the firm noodle and the rich broth is structural. Thin vermicelli works (and is common in home kitchens) but the bowl reads less hearty.

  Why tofu puffs. The fried-and-puffed tofu (tau pok) is a structural laksa garnish, not a substitute or addition. The puff acts as a sponge for the broth; biting into a soaked tofu puff releases a small flood of curry broth in the mouth, a defining sensory moment of laksa eating. Skipping the puffs makes the dish feel incomplete to anyone who grew up eating laksa.

  Storage. The broth holds 4 days in the fridge; the coconut fat will resolidify on top and needs to be whisked back in over gentle heat. Reheat broth and protein separately from noodles; cooked noodles soften too much when refrigerated in broth and the dish goes mushy. Freeze the rempah (after the fry, before adding broth) in tablespoon portions; the made rempah keeps 3 months frozen and produces a quick laksa from frozen in 30 minutes total.
