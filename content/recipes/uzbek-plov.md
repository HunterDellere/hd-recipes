before_you_start:
  - 'Rinse the rice until the water runs clear, then soak it in warm salted water 30 to 60 minutes while you build the zirvak. Soaking and rinsing wash off surface starch and pre-hydrate the grains so they cook up separate, not gluey; this is the single most important prep step for plov.'
  - 'Plov is built in stages in one pot: sear meat and fat, build the zirvak (onion, carrot, spice, broth), then steam the rice on top by absorption. Once the rice goes on, do not stir it into the zirvak; the layers stay separate until the final mound-and-serve.'
  - 'Carrots are cut into matchsticks by hand, never grated. Grated carrot dissolves into the rice; matchsticks hold their shape and give the dish its structure and sweetness. Budget time for the knife work, it is a large volume of carrots.'
  - 'Do not skimp on oil or fat. Plov is a rich dish by design; the fat is the carrier that coats every grain and gives osh its gloss. Lamb tail fat is traditional and worth seeking out.'

source:
  name: 'Uzbek (Central Asian) tradition'
  note: 'Plov (palov, osh) is the national dish of Uzbekistan and the shared centerpiece of Central Asian cooking (Tajik, Kyrgyz, Turkmen, Uyghur, and beyond), inscribed on the UNESCO intangible heritage list. It is cooked in a kazan (a wide cast-iron wok-like pot) over fire, traditionally by an oshpaz (a plov master) for weddings and gatherings. The defining method is the zirvak: a base of seared meat, browned onion, and matchstick carrot simmered into a broth, over which raw soaked rice is steamed by absorption. Whole garlic heads, chickpeas, and barberries are classic additions; cumin (zira) is the signature spice.'

steps:
  - text: 'Render the fat and sear the meat. Heat the 90 g / 6 tbsp oil in a heavy wide pot or kazan over high heat until it shimmers and just begins to smoke. If using it, add the 100 g lamb tail fat and render 4 to 5 minutes until the cubes are golden and crisp; scoop them out and reserve. Add the 700 g lamb cubes to the hot fat and sear hard, 6 to 8 minutes, until deeply browned on all sides. Scoop the meat out and set aside.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Brown the onions. Add the 300 g sliced onion to the hot fat. Cook over medium-high, stirring, 8 to 10 minutes until deep golden-brown at the edges; this browning sets the color and sweetness of the whole dish. Return the seared lamb to the pot.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Build the zirvak. Add the 600 g matchstick carrots in an even layer over the meat and onions. Do not stir for 2 minutes, let them soften, then fold gently. Add the 6 g / 1 tbsp crushed cumin, 3 g / 1½ tsp ground coriander, 20 g / 2 tbsp barberries, the whole dried chili, and the 14 g / 1½ tbsp salt. Pour in the 700 ml / 3 cups boiling water to just cover. Bring to a simmer, then lower the heat and simmer uncovered 30 to 40 minutes, until the carrots are soft, the meat is tender, and the broth is deeply colored and rich. Taste the broth and correct the salt; it should taste slightly too salty.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 40
  - text: 'Add the garlic, chickpeas, and rice. Nestle the 2 whole garlic heads and scatter the 120 g chickpeas into the zirvak. Spread the drained soaked rice in an even layer over the top with a slotted spoon, without stirring it into the zirvak below. Level the surface.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Cook the rice by absorption. Gently pour the 600 ml / 2½ cups boiling water over the rice (pour over the back of a spoon so you do not dig craters); the water should sit about 1.5 cm above the rice. Raise the heat to medium-high and cook uncovered, without stirring, until the water level drops to the surface of the rice and the top looks dry with steam holes forming, 12 to 18 minutes. Taste a top grain: it should be nearly tender.'
    time_min: 16
  - text: 'Mound and steam. With a slotted spoon, gather the rice into a mound in the center of the pot, mounding it away from the edges. Poke 5 or 6 holes down through the rice to the bottom with a chopstick to vent steam and let the last liquid rise through. Cover with a tight lid (wrap it in a towel to trap steam), drop the heat to the lowest setting, and steam 20 to 25 minutes undisturbed until the rice is fully tender and separate.'
    technique: 'vapor-steaming'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Serve. Lift out the 2 garlic heads and set aside. Fold the whole pot together now, bringing the carrot-and-meat zirvak up from the bottom through the rice so every grain is stained orange and glossed with fat. Mound the plov on a large platter, arrange the lamb and the reserved crisp tail fat on top, and set the 2 whole garlic heads on the mound for diners to squeeze out the sweet cloves. Serve hot with achichuk (a salad of thinly sliced tomato and onion) and green tea.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  What the zirvak is and why it matters. Zirvak is the flavored base of plov: seared meat, browned onion, matchstick carrot, and spices simmered in water into a rich, deeply colored broth. Everything the finished dish tastes of is built here, before a single grain of rice goes in. The rice then cooks by absorption directly on top of the zirvak, drinking up its fat, color, and salt. This is the structural difference between Uzbek plov and the Afghan or Persian parboil-and-steam method: plov rice is never boiled in plain water and drained, so it keeps every bit of the meat-and-carrot flavor. Season the zirvak assertively; it is the only seasoning the rice gets.

  Why carrots by the pound, cut by hand. Carrots are not a garnish in plov, they are half the dish by volume and the main source of its sweetness and color. Grating them is the classic error: grated carrot melts into the rice and turns it to mush. Hand-cut 5 cm matchsticks hold their shape through the long zirvak simmer and the steam, giving the plov its texture and its orange-streaked look. Traditional cooks use both orange and yellow carrots when they can.

  The absorption method, step by step. Unlike a biryani, plov rice is cooked in place: spread raw soaked rice over the zirvak, add just enough boiling water to cover, cook uncovered on higher heat until the water drops to the rice line, then mound it, vent it with holes, and steam it covered on low. Stirring the rice into the zirvak at any point before the final serve collapses the layers and clumps the grains. Patience with the layers is what separates good plov from a carrot-and-rice hash.

  Umami and acid. The seared lamb, rendered fat, and long-simmered zirvak are a deep glutamate-rich umami base, and the barberries thread a tart note through the rice. The structural acid, though, is the accompaniment: achichuk, the sliced tomato-and-onion salad, is served with plov precisely because its raw acidity cuts the richness of the fatty rice. Do not skip it; a bowl of osh without the sharp salad alongside eats heavy.

  Pairs across Uzbek and broader Central Asian tradition. Serve as a single communal platter with achichuk salad, non (Uzbek tandoor bread), and green tea, which is drunk throughout the meal to cut the fat. Plov reheats well, covered with a splash of water to re-steam. It is traditionally cooked in large batches; the recipe doubles cleanly in a wide enough pot, and many say it tastes even better the next day.
