title: 'Goan fish curry (xacuti)'
desc: 'Xacuti (sha-KU-ti): the dark, deeply aromatic Goan fish curry built on a roasted spice paste of dry-toasted desiccated coconut, white poppy seeds, Kashmiri chilies, coriander seeds, black pepper, cloves, cinnamon, and star anise, wet-ground to a coarse paste and cooked down with a mustard-seed-and-curry-leaf tadka, caramelized onion, garlic, ginger, and kokum for the sour finish. Kingfish steaks or skin-on pomfret (or whole white fish steaks) are marinated in turmeric and salt, seared briefly, then simmered in the coconut-spice gravy until just cooked through. The defining Goan-Konkan fish preparation: a curry that reads roasted, nutty, aromatic, and sour rather than the cream-thickened or blended-tomato register of North Indian fish curries.'

source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Goan Catholic and Hindu fish curry tradition of the Konkan coast, specifically the xacuti (also spelled xacutti or chacuti, from the Konkani word meaning "spicy") preparation associated with the Old Conquests region of Goa (Tiswadi, Bardez, Salcette taluka), where the Portuguese colonial presence introduced vinegar (cane vinegar, still canonical in Goan cooking) and techniques that merged with the coconut-and-spice traditions of the Konkan fishing communities. The roasted-coconut masala paste distinguishes xacuti from the simpler caldine (mild coconut milk fish curry without roasted coconut) or Goan fish curry with raw paste.'
  note: 'Xacuti is often described as the most complex Goan curry; the complexity comes from the roasted-coconut spice paste, which requires dry-toasting each element (the coconut and each whole spice separately) before wet-grinding, and from the kokum souring that provides a distinctly fruity-astringent acid character absent from North Indian fish curries. The canonical fish is kingfish (surmai, Scomberomorus commerson) or pomfret (paplet), both common on the Konkan coast and at Indian fishmongers worldwide; in North American markets, firm-fleshed fish steaks from halibut, mahi-mahi, or whole-fish steaks from snapper or sea bass are the closest available alternatives. Mahi-mahi is a scombroid-family fish and requires a histamine safety note; see safety_notes below. The dish is associated with Goan Catholic feast days and is served with poi (Goan bread) or steamed rice, never with naan or roti (wheat bread is not structurally part of Goan food culture).'

before_you_start:
  - 'Start the basmati or jasmine rice on a PARALLEL track so it lands at the same time as the curry. The xacuti needs roughly 60 to 70 minutes of mostly attentive cooking; the rice (a 10-minute soak, 12-minute simmer, 10-minute rest for basmati) is timed to finish in the same window as the fish simmer. Without rice ready, the curry sits and the fish overcooks.'
  - 'The xacuti spice paste can be made up to 3 days ahead and refrigerated, or frozen for a month. If you are making the full dish in one session, budget the first 25 minutes for the dry-toast, blend, and rest before you touch the curry base; the paste sequence is sequential and cannot be parallelized with the onion caramelization.'
  - 'Soak the white poppy seeds in cold water for the full 10 minutes before blending. Undersoaked seeds blend gritty and the paste reads chalky in the finished curry. Start the soak as soon as the seeds come off the dry-toast pan.'
  - 'Marinate the fish steaks with turmeric and salt 15 to 20 minutes before they hit the pan. This is a brief firm-up, not a long marinade; longer than 20 minutes and the salt draws too much water and the surface will not sear cleanly. Time the marinade so the fish goes into the pan right after the spice paste is built.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'Mahi-mahi (dorado) used as the kingfish substitute'
    ref: 'safety/seafood-doneness#scombroid-histamine-the-toxin-no-temperature-fixes'
    why: 'Mahi-mahi (Coryphaena hippurus) is a scombroid-family fish that is prone to scombroid poisoning (histamine toxicity) if mishandled after catch. Histamine forms when the fish is held at warm temperatures after death; it cannot be destroyed by cooking. The risk is in the sourcing, not the recipe: buy mahi-mahi from a reputable fishmonger with rapid cold-chain, reject fish with a peppery or off smell, and cook within 1 to 2 days of purchase. Kingfish (surmai) and pomfret are not scombroid-family fish and do not carry this risk.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the xacuti spice paste. In a heavy dry skillet over medium heat, dry-toast the 60 g / ½ cup desiccated coconut, stirring constantly, until uniformly golden-brown and deeply aromatic (8 to 10 minutes). The coconut goes from white to pale to golden-brown quickly in the final 2 minutes; do not leave unattended. Transfer to a plate to cool. In the same pan, separately dry-toast the 20 g / 2 tbsp white poppy seeds (2 to 3 minutes, until slightly golden) and the whole dry spices: 15 g coriander seeds, 6 g black peppercorns, 3 g cloves, 5 g cinnamon stick, and 2 star anise, together for 90 seconds to 2 minutes until fragrant. Add the 8 whole Kashmiri dried chilies (stems removed) for the final 30 seconds. Transfer all to the plate to cool.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Blend the paste. Soak the dry-toasted poppy seeds in 30 ml cold water for 10 minutes (they absorb the water and blend more smoothly). In a blender, combine the toasted coconut, soaked poppy seeds and their water, all the toasted whole spices and Kashmiri chilies, the 3 g / 1 tsp ground turmeric (raw), the 20 g garlic cloves, and the 15 g ginger. Add 80 ml / ⅓ cup cold water and blend to a coarse paste, 60 to 90 seconds, scraping down the sides twice. The paste should be thick and slightly grainy (not a smooth puree). If the blender stalls, add water 1 tbsp at a time. This is the xacuti masala; it keeps in a sealed container in the refrigerator for 3 days or frozen for 1 month.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Marinate the fish. Rub the 700 g / about 4 to 6 fish steaks (patted dry) with the 2 g / ½ tsp turmeric and 5 g / 1 tsp salt, coating all surfaces. Set aside at room temperature for 15 to 20 minutes. The turmeric acts as a mild antimicrobial and stains the fish surface a deep yellow; the brief salt draw firms the surface for the sear.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Sear the fish. In a wide, heavy skillet or Dutch oven over high heat, warm 15 g / 1 tbsp of the oil until shimmering. Sear the marinated fish steaks in one layer (working in batches if needed), 90 seconds per side, until the surface is golden-yellow and caramelized. The fish will not be cooked through; the sear is for the Maillard crust and the flavor compounds it deposits on the pan. Transfer to a plate; reserve the pan.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Build the tadka and onion base. In the same pan (with the sear fond) over medium-high heat, add the remaining 30 g / 2 tbsp oil. When the oil is hot and shimmering (175 to 200 °C, at which point a mustard seed dropped in should sputter immediately), add the 5 g / 1 tsp mustard seeds; cover the pan loosely with a lid (the seeds pop and spatter) and cook 15 to 20 seconds until the popping slows. Remove the lid, add the 12 curry leaves (stand back — they spatter violently) and cook 5 seconds. Immediately add the 250 g / 1 large onion (fine dice) and a pinch of salt; sweat over medium heat 15 to 18 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the onion is deeply golden-brown and jammy (this extended caramelization is structural; rushing it produces a raw-tasting curry base). The onion should reduce to roughly half its original volume and turn the color of aged honey.'
    technique: 'spice-tempering'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Add the tomato and paste. Add the 200 g / 1 large chopped tomato to the caramelized onion; cook 4 to 5 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the tomato breaks down and the oil begins to separate at the edges of the pan (this is the bhuna step; the "oil separating" cue signals that the raw-spice notes have been cooked out). Add the full batch of xacuti spice paste; stir to combine, then fry the paste in the oil 3 to 4 minutes over medium heat, stirring frequently, until the paste darkens slightly and the oil separates around it again (the same bhuna principle applied to the spice paste; underdone paste reads raw and bitter). The pan should smell of deep-roasted coconut, toasted spices, and onion.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Add liquid and simmer the gravy. Pour in the 400 g / 1 can coconut milk and the 150 ml / ⅔ cup water; stir to dissolve the paste into the liquid. Add the 10 g / 3 to 4 kokum strips (add them whole; they will soften and release acid during the simmer). Add the 6 g / 1¼ tsp salt. Bring to a gentle simmer; cook uncovered 8 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the gravy has thickened slightly and the kokum has softened and released its fruity-sour character. Taste: the gravy should read deeply aromatic (roasted coconut and warm spices), mildly hot (Kashmiri chili), and lightly sour (kokum). Adjust salt and add more kokum if the gravy reads flat.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Simmer the fish in the gravy. Slide the seared fish steaks into the simmering gravy; tuck any steak edges below the surface. Simmer gently (the gravy should tremble, not boil) for 6 to 8 minutes by 3 to 4 cm steak thickness, until the fish just flakes at the thickest point when prodded with a fork. Do not stir once the fish is in the pot; the steaks are fragile and will break up. Pull off heat immediately when the fish is just-done; the residual heat in the gravy will carry over another 2 to 3 degrees. Taste the gravy once more and adjust with fine sea salt in 1 g increments if needed.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Finish and serve. Scatter the 10 g / ¼ cup torn cilantro over the curry. Serve directly from the pot or a wide shallow bowl, with steamed basmati or jasmine rice alongside. Each diner takes a fish steak (bone-in, with the crisp seared skin), spoons the coconut-spice gravy over the rice, and uses the lime wedge to taste: a small squeeze brightens the kokum sourness and lifts the rich coconut fat. The dish reads best the day it is made; the gravy can be refrigerated and reheated, but the fish should be removed first (it becomes dry when reheated in the gravy).'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  What makes xacuti different from every other South Asian fish curry. Xacuti is built in a sequence that almost no other Indian fish curry follows: dry-toast every paste ingredient separately before blending (the roasted coconut and roasted spice base), then bhuna the paste in the caramelized onion base (cook the paste in fat until the oil separates), then simmer the coconut milk into the roasted-spice base, then add the pre-seared fish at the very end. The roasted-coconut step cannot be rushed and cannot be skipped; it is what gives xacuti its dark, nutty, deeply aromatic character that reads categorically different from a raw-paste coconut curry (like a simple Goan fish caldine or a Kerala meen curry). The toasting time is also the point where most home cooks fail the recipe — the coconut must reach genuinely golden-brown (not just warm), and it must be stirred constantly in the final 2 minutes to prevent burning.

  The bhuna step (cooking the paste in fat until the oil separates) is the same technique used in North Indian curry masala bases but is applied here to the xacuti paste rather than a tomato-onion masala. The visual cue (the oil separating around the paste, leaving a dry-ish center ring) is the same across both applications: it signals that the water has cooked out of the paste, the starch has hydrated and begun to fry rather than steam, and the raw spice notes have been driven off by the fat-fry. A paste that has not bhunafied reads raw and bitter; a paste that has been correctly bhunafied reads deep, rounded, and aromatic.

  The kokum sourness is the finish that distinguishes the dish from a generic coconut fish curry. Kokum provides a fruity-astringent sour note (hydroxycitric acid) that rounds and brightens the deep roasted-coconut base in a way that tamarind or lemon cannot quite replicate. The souring is subtle in a well-made xacuti: it should read as "the curry has a lift and brightness underneath the richness," not as "this is a sour curry." If the finished dish tastes heavy and flat despite the other elements, add another strip of kokum to the simmering gravy and cook 5 more minutes.

  The umami architecture. Xacuti does not lean on the glutamate-rich ingredients that North Indian curries use (no tomato-heavy base, no cream, no yogurt). The umami comes from: (1) the roasted coconut Maillard products in the paste, which produce a range of pyrazines and furanones that read savory-nutty; (2) the deeply caramelized onion (the bhunafied onion base), which converts sucrose to caramel compounds and releases glutamate from the Maillard of the protein fraction; (3) the fish itself, which releases inosinate (5'-IMP) as it simmers in the gravy, synergizing with the glutamate from the onion and tomato (the same 5'-nucleotide synergy as dashi). The finishing acid (kokum, or a squeeze of lime at the table) is structural: without it the richness of the coconut milk reads heavy and the dish lacks the brightness that makes the aromatic complexity legible.

  Serving. Xacuti is always served with rice in Goan cuisine; steamed basmati or short-grain rice is the primary vehicle. In Goa itself, poi (a crusty, slightly fermented Goan bread made with whole wheat flour and often local palm wine or toddy) is served alongside; it is not widely available in North America. The dish is complete without bread. Leftovers: the gravy reheats well (3 days refrigerated), but remove the fish first; reheating fish in the gravy produces a dry, mushy result. The gravy can also be used as a sauce base for a second-day preparation with different protein (paneer, firm tofu, shrimp) added fresh to the reheated gravy.
