before_you_start:
  - 'Whisk-and-rest, not whisk-and-serve. The dressing needs at least 60 minutes refrigerated before service so the dried herbs hydrate in the vinegar phase and the raw garlic mellows from sharp to round. Fresh-emulsified Italian dressing tastes harshly garlic-and-vinegar-forward and the herbs read as crunchy specks rather than as integrated flavor. After an hour the components fuse into the unified herby-creamy dressing the chain is calibrated for.'
  - 'For best result, make it the day before. Day-old dressing reads markedly more cohesive than 1-hour dressing; the dried oregano and basil continue to bleed flavor into the vinegar for the first 12 hours.'

steps:
  - text: 'In a tall narrow container (deli quart or wide-mouth pint jar) combine the 90 ml / 6 tbsp white wine vinegar, 30 ml / 2 tbsp water, 20 g / 1 tbsp honey, 10 ml / 2 tsp lemon juice, the 1 grated garlic clove paste, 5 g / 1 tsp Dijon mustard, 3 g / ½ tsp salt, 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper, 1 g / ½ tsp onion powder, 2 g / 1 tsp dried oregano, 1 g / ½ tsp dried basil, 1 g / ½ tsp dried parsley, and 0.5 g / ¼ tsp red pepper flakes. Whisk briefly to dissolve the honey and salt.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 25 g / ¼ cup grated parmigiano reggiano. Whisk into the vinegar phase, the cheese should disperse rather than clump; if it clumps, the parmesan was grated too coarsely and the dressing will benefit from a 10-second pulse with an immersion blender.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Combine the 120 ml / ½ cup extra-virgin olive oil and 60 ml / ¼ cup neutral oil in a measuring cup. Drizzle the combined oils into the vinegar phase in a slow steady stream while whisking continuously, or pulse with an immersion blender. The dressing should emulsify into a uniform creamy pale-yellow liquid; if it breaks, an extra ½ tsp of Dijon mustard plus 30 seconds of vigorous re-whisking will rebuild it.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Taste. Adjust by quarter-teaspoon increments: more honey if it reads sharp, more vinegar if it reads heavy, more salt if it reads flat. The chain calibration is herby-tangy-slightly-sweet with the parmesan as a faint creamy background; aim there.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Cover and refrigerate at least 1 hour before serving, the dried herbs need time to hydrate in the vinegar phase and the garlic needs to mellow. Holds 1 week refrigerated in a sealed jar. Shake or re-whisk before each use, the dressing slowly separates as the parmesan settles.'
    time_min: 60

notes: |
  The Olive Garden Italian dressing is the chain''s most-copied product, the bottle is on grocery shelves and the recipe has been reverse-engineered countless times. The architecture is a creamy emulsified vinaigrette: vinegar and oil at roughly 1 : 2 by volume, with mustard and grated parmesan as the two emulsifiers, dried Italian herb blend, fresh garlic, lemon, and a small dose of sweetener. The cream comes from the cheese plus the mustard-emulsified oil, not from any added dairy.

  The chain uses high-fructose corn syrup; this version uses honey because it is the cleaner home pantry choice and adds a small floral note that improves the dressing. If you want the literal chain calibration, use 18 g of light corn syrup. The sweetness dose is non-trivial; it is what distinguishes this dressing from a proper Italian vinaigrette, which would be vinegar-forward with no added sugar.

  Real grated parmigiano reggiano is critical. The shaker-can stuff has wood-pulp anti-caking agents and stale cheese flour; it will not emulsify the same way and will leave a chalky deposit at the bottom of the jar. A microplane and a wedge of real parmesan or pecorino is the difference between a dressing that reads as serious and one that reads as cafeteria.
