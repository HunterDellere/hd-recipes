before_you_start:
  - 'Keep the puff pastry cold and work quickly. Warm puff leaks butter and its layers fuse, which kills the rise; if the dough softens or turns greasy at any point, slide it onto a tray and chill it before continuing.'
  - 'The shaped log needs to chill 20 to 30 minutes (or 15 in the freezer) before slicing, or the spiral squashes and the palmiers lose their shape. Build the log around the gap while the oven heats.'
  - 'Sugar caramelizes fast and then burns. Once the palmiers go in, stay at the oven; the flip partway through is the whole technique and the window between deep gold and scorched is about a minute.'

steps:
  - text: 'Toss the granulated sugar (½ cup / 120 g) with the fine sea salt (¼ tsp / 1 g). Scatter about a third of it across a cold work surface in place of flour. Lay the cold puff pastry (1 sheet / 250 g) on top and roll it to an even rectangle about 24 by 30 cm (10 by 12 in) and 3 mm thick, pressing firmly so the sugar embeds in the underside. Scatter another third of the sugar over the top and roll once more to press it in. The sugar is structural, not a coating: rolled into both faces, it melts and caramelizes against the pan instead of staying as loose grit.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Shape the heart. With a long side facing you, mark the center lightly. Fold each long edge in to meet a finger-width short of the center line, then fold each of those folded edges in again until the two rolls almost touch at the center. Scatter the last of the sugar as you go so every new surface is sugared. Close the two halves together like a book; the cross-section now shows the palm-leaf scrolls that bake into a heart. Press gently to seal.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Chill the log on a tray, uncovered, 20 to 30 minutes in the fridge or about 15 in the freezer, until firm enough to slice without squashing the spiral. Meanwhile heat the oven to 400°F (200°C) and line two sheets with parchment.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Slice the cold log into pieces about 1 cm (⅜ in) thick with a sharp knife, wiping the blade between cuts. Lay them cut-side up on the parchment with at least 5 cm (2 in) of space on every side, since they double in width as the scrolls unfurl. Pinch the base of each into a soft V to open the two lobes into a heart, and dab any bare cut faces in the leftover sugar.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Bake 12 to 15 minutes, until the undersides are deep amber and the sugar there has caramelized (lift one with a spatula to check; pale gold is not done). Pull the sheet, flip each palmier with tongs or a thin spatula, and return for 5 to 8 minutes more to caramelize the second side to the same deep amber. The sugar must color to caramel, not just melt; a palmier pulled at melt-and-pale tastes only sweet, while one taken to amber tastes of caramel and toasted butter.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Cool on the sheet 2 minutes (the caramel is molten and will hold its set as it cools), then lift to a rack. They crisp as they cool to a shatter-snap. Once fully cool they keep their crunch a day or two in an airtight tin; humidity softens them, so seal them well.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'The Adjustment: if the palmiers are pale and only sweet, the sugar never caramelized; the oven ran low or you pulled them early, so push the next batch to deep amber on both sides. If they scorch, the rack sat too high or the oven too hot; drop to 375°F (190°C) and watch the flip. If they unfurl flat into a single curl instead of a heart, the fold was too loose or the slices too thick; fold the rolls until they nearly touch and slice at 1 cm. If butter pools in the pan, the dough went warm before baking; chill the sliced palmiers 10 minutes before they go in.'

notes: |
  Why sugar instead of flour on the bench. Rolling puff pastry out on flour leaves a raw, dusty film that bakes pale and chalky. Palmiers turn that step into the recipe: you roll on sugar, press it into both faces, and it becomes the caramel shell. Sucrose melts around 160°C and browns into caramel a little above that, exactly the range the pastry surface reaches against a hot sheet pan, so the sugar that is touching metal caramelizes hardest. That is why the flip matters; without it, only one side ever touches the pan long enough to color.

  Why cold, and why the rest. Puff pastry is laminated butter and dough in alternating layers, and its rise is steam: water in the butter and dough flashes to vapor in the oven and forces the layers apart. If the butter warms and merges into the dough before baking, there are no discrete layers to lift and the pastry bakes dense. Keeping the dough cold, and chilling the shaped log before slicing, keeps the lamination intact and keeps the scroll from squashing under the knife, so each palmier opens into clean, separated leaves.

  The heart is the fold. A palmier is shaped by folding both long edges of the sheet inward to the center in two stages, then closing the two halves like a book. Sliced crosswise, that fold reads as two outward-curling scrolls joined at a point: the palm leaf the French name describes, and the heart most people see. Folding the rolls until they nearly meet, and slicing thin, is what gives a defined heart rather than a loose single spiral.

  Two ingredients, so quality shows. With nothing but pastry, sugar, and a pinch of salt, there is nowhere to hide. All-butter puff tastes of butter and caramel; oil-based puff tastes of sweetness and not much else. The salt is not optional seasoning so much as contrast: a few grains through the sugar keep the caramel from reading as flat sweetness and let the toasted-butter note come forward.
