before_you_start:
  - 'Start the basmati rinse-and-soak first; 20 to 30 minutes of cold-water soak before cooking produces the long separate grains the pulao is built on. Unsoaked rice cooks gummy and stubbier. This runs in parallel with the shrimp marinade.'
  - 'Marinate the shrimp 15 to 20 minutes before searing, no longer. The yogurt and acid begin denaturing the proteins on contact; past 30 minutes the surface goes mushy and the shrimp lose snap.'
  - 'Raita is the canonical partner. Either make it 30 minutes ahead so it rests cold (see recipes/raita) or buy plain whole-milk yogurt and stir in cumin and grated cucumber while the rice rests covered at the end.'
  - 'Use a heavy Dutch oven or thick saucepan with a tight lid. Thin-walled pots cause uneven heat at the bottom and scorch the rice during the steam phase before the grains finish hydrating.'

source:
  name: 'Coastal Indian / Mughlai-influenced tradition'
  note: 'Pulao (पुलाव, also pilaf, polo, pilav) is a rice-cooked-in-flavored-liquid technique that traces back to ancient Persian polo and migrated through Central Asia and Mughal India into the modern subcontinental kitchen. Shrimp pulao is a coastal-Indian variant (Goa, Mangalore, Kerala) that uses local seafood in place of the meat that defines northern pulaos and biryanis.'

steps:
  - text: 'Marinate the shrimp. In a bowl, combine the 500 g shrimp with the 60 g yogurt, 30 g ginger-garlic paste, 4 g Kashmiri chili powder, 1 g turmeric, 4 g salt, and 8 ml lemon juice. Toss to coat. Set aside 20 minutes at room temperature (no longer; shrimp toughens in acidic marinade past 30 minutes).'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Rinse and soak the rice. Place the 400 g basmati in a bowl and rinse under cold water 3 to 4 times until the water runs almost clear (this removes surface starch that would make the pulao gummy). Cover with fresh cold water and soak 20 to 30 minutes; soaking lets the grains pre-hydrate and produces longer, more separate grains in the finished pulao. Drain just before cooking.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Bloom the whole spices. Heat the 45 g ghee in a heavy 4-quart Dutch oven or wide pot with a tight lid over medium heat until shimmering. Add the 4 cracked cardamom pods, 4 cloves, 5 g cinnamon stick, 2 bay leaves, 4 g / 1½ tsp cumin seeds, and 1 star anise pod. Bloom 30 to 45 seconds; the cumin will sizzle and darken slightly, the kitchen will fill with the warm-spice aroma.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Brown the onion. Add the 200 g sliced onion and 8 g slit green chilies to the spiced ghee. Stir-fry over medium heat for 8 to 10 minutes until the onion is deep golden-brown at the edges and softened throughout. Pulao onion does not need to go fully mahogany like nihari onion; deep gold with brown edges is correct.'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Sear the marinated shrimp. Add the marinated shrimp (with all the marinade) to the pot. Stir-fry 2 to 3 minutes; the shrimp will turn from grey-translucent to pink-opaque and the yogurt-marinade will reduce to a thick coat. Pull the shrimp out with a slotted spoon (do not over-cook them now; they will warm through again in the steaming step). Set aside on a plate.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add the rice and toast. Add the drained rice to the pot. Stir-fry 2 minutes over medium heat; the grains will absorb the spice-flavored ghee and turn from translucent to faintly opaque. This brief toast in flavored fat is the structural pulao step (distinguishing pulao from boiled rice with sauce).'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add liquid and bring to a boil. Pour in the 200 ml coconut milk, 500 ml water, and 6 g salt. Stir gently once to distribute. Bring to a strong simmer over medium-high heat (the surface should bubble actively but not violently boil). Taste the liquid; it should be aggressively seasoned (the rice will absorb the seasoning).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Steam-cook the pulao. Once the liquid is at a strong simmer, return the cooked shrimp to the pot and gently nestle them on top of the rice (do not stir them in). Reduce heat to low, cover with a tight lid (if your lid is loose, place a clean kitchen towel between pot and lid to seal). Cook 12 minutes undisturbed. Pull off heat. Let rest, still covered, 10 minutes (the residual heat finishes the rice and lets the grains relax to their final texture).'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Fluff and serve. Remove the lid. Add the 15 g cilantro and 8 g mint leaves to the surface. Using a fork (not a spoon — a spoon mashes the grains), gently fluff the rice from the bottom of the pot upward, lifting the rice and shrimp together; this brings the spices distributed through the bottom up through the dish without breaking the grains. Transfer to a wide serving dish. Top with the 30 g fried onions. Serve immediately with the lemon wedges and a side of raita.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Aged basmati matters. The best pulao uses aged basmati rice (1 to 2 years aged from harvest). Aged basmati cooks up with longer, more separate grains and a more pronounced aromatic profile than freshly-harvested basmati. Indian groceries label "aged" or "premium aged" on premium-tier bags; the cost premium is worth it for special occasions. Standard supermarket basmati works well; just rinse and soak as written.

  Why absorption method, not boil-and-drain. Pulao is structurally absorption-cooked: a precise liquid-to-rice ratio (1.75:1 for soaked basmati, 2:1 for unsoaked) is added once and the rice absorbs all the liquid during steaming. The all-the-flavor-in-one-pot principle of pulao depends on absorption method; boiling and draining (the biryani parcook step) lets too much flavor drain off with the cooking water. The technique is the difference.

  Liquid-to-rice ratio. The exact ratio depends on the rice age, the soak length, the pot type (Dutch oven vs thin saucepan), and the heat behavior. The 1.75:1 ratio (700 ml liquid for 400 g rice when the rice is soaked 20 to 30 minutes) is the textbook Indian pulao standard and works in most home kitchens. If your pulao comes out slightly soupy, reduce next time by 50 ml; if it comes out slightly dry, add 50 ml. Cooks calibrate to their specific equipment.

  Eating mechanics. Pulao is a complete meal-on-a-plate when served with raita (yogurt + chopped cucumber or boondi + spices) and a small side of pickle (lime or mango achar) or a salad of sliced tomatoes-onions-cucumbers with lemon. The temperature contrast of cool raita and hot pulao is part of the eating experience. Reheats well, the day-after pulao often tastes better as the spices integrate further. Refrigerate up to 3 days.
