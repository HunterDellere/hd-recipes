before_you_start:
  - 'Soak the chana dal at least 1 hour (or up to 4) before cooking. Unsoaked chana dal parboils unevenly and either stays chalky at the center or blows out at the edges; the soak is what lets it cook to a clean, shape-holding tenderness.'
  - 'The entire dish depends on parboiling both the dal and the rice to about 70 percent, no further. They finish cooking together in the sealed dum steam. Dal cooked soft before layering turns to mush; rice cooked through before layering turns to paste.'
  - 'Soak the basmati 30 minutes separately from the dal. It goes into a spiced boiling bath, not the dal water.'
  - 'Have the saffron milk, the ghee, and the reserved fried onions and herbs staged before you build the layers. Dum assembly moves fast once the components are hot, and the pot needs to be sealed and on the heat quickly to trap the steam.'

source:
  name: 'Hyderabadi (Deccan) tradition'
  note: 'Qubooli (also qubani-adjacent in spelling but a distinct dish) is the vegetarian dum biryani of Hyderabad, built on chana dal (split Bengal gram) in place of the lamb of the famous Hyderabadi kachay gosht ki biryani. It shares a linguistic root with Afghan qabili palaw (both trace to qabil, worthy or acceptable), and both are festive rice dishes, but qubooli is a Deccan biryani in method: parcooked components layered and sealed for a dum steam, perfumed with saffron, mint, and fried onions. It is a signature of Hyderabadi Ramadan and vegetarian tables.'

steps:
  - text: 'Soak, then parboil the chana dal. After soaking the 250 g chana dal 1 to 4 hours, drain it, then boil in fresh water with the 1 g / ¼ tsp turmeric and 6 g / 1½ tsp salt for 10 to 12 minutes until about 70 percent cooked: a grain should be tender at the edge but still firm and whole at the center, never mushy. Drain and spread on a tray to stop the cooking. This shape-holding dal is the entire point of qubooli.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Bloom the saffron. Grind the 0.2 g saffron and steep it in the 60 ml / ¼ cup warm milk; leave it to turn gold while you work.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Make the yogurt masala. Heat the 45 g / 3 tbsp ghee in a heavy pot over medium. Add the 30 g ginger-garlic paste and 15 g slit green chilies; fry 1 to 2 minutes until raw-garlic smell is gone. Pull the pot off the heat and stir in the 250 g whisked yogurt (adding it off heat keeps it from splitting), then return to low heat. Add the 4 g / ½ tbsp Kashmiri chili powder, 1 g / ¼ tsp turmeric, and 4 g / 1½ tsp garam masala; cook 3 to 4 minutes until the masala thickens and the fat rises. Fold in the parboiled dal, half the 80 g fried onions, half the 20 g mint, half the 20 g cilantro, the 20 ml / from 1 lemon lemon juice, and the 6 g / 1½ tsp salt. Simmer gently 3 minutes into a thick, clingy masala. Level it into an even layer in the pot and pull off the heat.'
    technique: 'cooked-yogurt-sauce'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Parboil the rice. Bring the 2500 ml / 10 cups water to a rolling boil with the 24 g / 2½ tbsp salt, the 4 cardamom pods, 4 cloves, 5 g cinnamon stick, 2 bay leaves, and 3 g / 1 tsp cumin seeds. Add the drained soaked rice and boil 4 to 5 minutes until the grains are bendy but firm at the center (70 percent, like the dal). Drain immediately.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Build the dum. Spread the drained parboiled rice in an even layer directly over the dal masala in the pot. Drizzle the saffron milk over the rice in streaks, then the 30 g / 2 tbsp ghee, and scatter the reserved fried onions, mint, and cilantro over the top.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Seal and steam. Cover the pot with a tight lid; if the lid is loose, lay a clean damp towel or a band of stiff dough around the rim to seal in the steam. Cook on the lowest heat (use a heat diffuser or a tawa under the pot if you have one) for 20 to 25 minutes. The trapped steam finishes the rice and dal together and marries the layers. Do not lift the lid before 20 minutes.'
    technique: 'vapor-steaming'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Rest and serve. Pull the pot off the heat and rest, still sealed, 10 minutes; the residual steam relaxes the grains and firms the layers so they lift cleanly. Open the pot, and with a flat spoon gently dig down through the layers and turn the rice and dal over together so the pale, saffron-gold, and masala-stained rice stream through in patches without breaking the grains. Serve hot with a cooling raita and a squeeze of extra lemon.'
    time_min: 12

notes: |
  Why 70 percent, twice. Qubooli is a layered dum biryani, and its texture depends on parcooking both the dal and the rice to roughly 70 percent, then letting the sealed steam finish them together. If the chana dal is boiled soft before layering, it turns to a paste and the dish reads as khichdi, not biryani; if the rice is cooked through before layering, the dum steam overcooks it to mush. Bite-testing is the only reliable gauge: the dal should be tender at the edge and firm at the core, the rice bendy but firm at the center. Both finish in the pot.

  Why the yogurt goes in off the heat. Yogurt proteins curdle and split when they hit a hot fat directly. Pulling the pot off the heat, stirring the whisked yogurt into the aromatics, and only then returning it to low heat tempers it gradually so the masala stays smooth and creamy rather than grainy and broken. This is the same principle behind Turkish and Persian yogurt cookery and it is non-negotiable for a clean Hyderabadi masala.

  Umami and acid, built in. The yogurt, the slow-fried onions, and the chana dal itself are the layered umami of a vegetarian biryani that never touches meat stock; the fried onions in particular carry the deep-savory backbone. The acid is doubly Deccan: the lactic tang of the yogurt plus the lemon (or tamarind) folded into the masala. Hyderabadi cooking leans sour on purpose, and that sourness is what keeps a rich, ghee-laced rice dish from going heavy.

  Pairs across Hyderabadi and broader Deccan vegetarian cooking. Serve with a cooling raita (whisked yogurt with cucumber, onion, and toasted cumin), a fresh mirchi ka salan or a simple green-chili salan if you want the classic pairing, and a squeeze of lemon. Qubooli keeps 3 days refrigerated and reheats well, covered with a splash of water to re-steam; like most biryani, it is arguably better the next day once the layers have fully married.
