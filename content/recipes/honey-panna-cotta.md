  note: 'Panna cotta is traditionally sweetened with cane sugar. This version uses honey as the only sweetener, which changes two things: honey is sweeter than sugar by weight (so less is needed) and its aromatics are heat-sensitive (so it goes in off the heat), and it adds a faint acidity that the lemon zest is chosen to echo.'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a make-ahead dessert: it needs at least 4 hours in the fridge to set, and it is better after 6 to 24 hours. Plan to make it the day before, or first thing in the morning for that night.'
  - 'Bloom the gelatin in cold liquid before you heat anything. Powder sprinkled onto warm cream clumps into fish-eyes that never dissolve; it must swell in cold liquid first.'
  - 'Gelatin must never boil. Sustained heat above about 80 C hydrolyzes it and permanently weakens the set, so the cream is warmed only until steaming, never simmered.'
  - 'Decide how you will serve it. In a glass, no unmolding needed. To unmold onto a plate, lightly oil the ramekins first with a neutral oil so the set slips out cleanly.'

steps:
  - text: 'Bloom the gelatin. Pour the 60 ml / ¼ cup cold whole milk into a small bowl and sprinkle the 8 g / 2½ tsp powdered gelatin evenly over the surface (sprinkle, do not dump, so it hydrates without clumping). Leave it undisturbed 5 to 10 minutes; it will swell into a saturated, wrinkled sponge. This cold bloom is what lets the gelatin dissolve smoothly later.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Steep the cream. In a small saucepan, combine the 360 ml / 1½ cups heavy cream, the 120 ml / ½ cup whole milk, the 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste, the 2 strips of lemon zest, and the 1 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt. Warm over medium-low, stirring, until it steams and small bubbles form at the edge but before it simmers, about 4 to 5 minutes. Do not boil. Pull off the heat, cover, and let steep 10 minutes so the vanilla and lemon infuse.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Dissolve the gelatin. Return the cream to low heat until just warm again (not simmering). Add the bloomed gelatin sponge and whisk gently until it fully dissolves and the mixture is completely smooth, about 1 minute; rub a little between two fingers to confirm no granules remain. Keep the heat low, the gelatin must not boil or the set weakens.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Sweeten off the heat. Take the pan off the heat and whisk in the 85 g / ¼ cup honey until fully dissolved. Adding the honey off the heat is deliberate: honey''s floral aromatics are volatile and boiling drives them off, so the flavor stays brightest when it goes in warm rather than hot. Taste, it should read clearly of honey and vanilla, gently sweet, with the salt in the background. Fish out the two lemon strips and discard.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Strain and fill. Pour the mixture through a fine-mesh sieve into a measuring jug with a spout (the sieve catches any undissolved gelatin or vanilla fiber and gives a flawless texture). Divide evenly among five 120 ml ramekins or serving glasses. If you plan to unmold, lightly oil the ramekins with a neutral oil first.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Chill to set. Cool the filled molds on the counter 15 minutes (so the fridge does not have to fight residual heat), then refrigerate uncovered until skin-firm, about 1 hour, then cover and chill at least 4 hours total, ideally 6 to 24. The set is correct when a gentle tap makes the surface wobble like the classic panna cotta quiver but the custard holds its shape.'
    time_min: 240
  - text: 'Finish and serve. Serve in the glass, or unmold: dip each ramekin to the rim in hot water for 5 to 8 seconds, run a thin knife around the edge, invert onto a plate, and give a firm downward shake to release. Thread the 20 g / 1 tbsp finishing honey over each portion and scatter a few flakes of sea salt. The raw honey on top carries aromatics the cooked honey inside has softened, and the salt flakes give a crunch and a savory counterpoint that keeps it from cloying.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why honey goes in off the heat. Honey carries volatile floral and fruity aroma compounds inherited from the flowers the bees foraged, and those are exactly what a good honey is prized for. Heat drives them off: boil honey and you are left with a generically sweet syrup that has lost most of what made that particular honey worth using. So the honey is whisked in after the pan comes off the heat, warm enough to dissolve but not hot enough to strip the aroma. It also means the specific honey you choose is the whole flavor of the dessert, pick one you actually like the taste of.

  Why this gelatin dose. Panna cotta lives on the edge of set. Here the 8 g of gelatin is about 1.2% of the total liquid weight (the dairy plus the honey, a bit over 650 g), which is enough to unmold onto a plate with a firm shake while still giving the trembling wobble that dissolves the instant it hits the tongue. If you are only ever serving it in the glass and want it even softer, drop to 6 g; do not push much past 8 g or you trade the melt for a rubbery, sliceable set that reads like a supermarket dessert.

  Why lemon zest with honey. Honey carries a faint natural acidity (mostly gluconic acid), and a strip of lemon zest steeped in the cream echoes and lifts it without turning the custard sour. The zest oil is aromatic, not sharp; it frames the honey the way a squeeze of lemon frames a drizzle of honey on toast. Do not substitute juice, which would water the cream and risk curdling.

  Make-ahead and holding. This is a fridge dessert that only improves overnight as the flavors marry and the set firms to its final texture. It holds covered for up to 3 days; after that the surface starts to dry and the custard picks up fridge odors. Do not freeze, gelatin sets weep and turn grainy on thawing.
