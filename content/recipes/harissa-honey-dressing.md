  name: 'Built on the Tunisian harissa tradition, where the chili paste is an aromatic base (caraway, coriander, garlic) rather than a pure heat source, paired with the pan-regional Maghreb and Levantine habit of balancing chili against honey or date sweetness and lemon acid.'
  note: 'Harissa is the load-bearing ingredient; the quality of the paste sets the ceiling for the dressing. A caraway-and-coriander-forward harissa (homemade or a good jarred brand like Belazu or Mina) reads aromatic and rounded; a commodity chili paste reads as flat heat and forces the honey to overcompensate. The emulsion is a loose mustard-stabilized vinaigrette, not a tight mayonnaise; it is meant to pool and coat, not cling.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The raw garlic mellows on contact with the lemon acid. Mince or grate the garlic into the lemon juice first and let it sit 5 minutes before building the rest of the dressing; this blunts the raw-allicin harshness (the acid begins denaturing the alliinase-produced sharpness) so the garlic reads as savory backbone rather than hot pungency.'
  - 'This is an emulsion that will slowly separate at rest. Build it within 30 minutes of serving for the glossiest texture, or store it and re-shake before each use; the dijon and honey hold it together longer than a bare oil-and-lemon vinaigrette, but it is not permanently stable.'

steps:
  - text: 'Build the garlic-acid base. Grate the 6 g / 2 cloves garlic on a microplane directly into a medium bowl, then add the 45 g / 3 tbsp lemon juice and the 2 g / 1 tsp lemon zest. Stir once and let it stand 5 minutes. The lemon acid denatures the alliinase-driven sharpness of the raw garlic, so what would otherwise be a hot pungent bite mellows into a rounded savory note. Skipping this rest leaves a raw-garlic burn that dominates the finished dressing.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Whisk in the flavor base. To the garlic-lemon bowl, add the 45 g / 3 tbsp harissa paste, the 30 g / 1½ tbsp honey, the 8 g / 1 tsp dijon mustard, the 1 g / ½ tsp ground cumin, and the 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt. Whisk until completely uniform and the harissa has fully dissolved into the liquid (no streaks of undispersed paste). Salt goes into this water-phase base, before the oil, so it dissolves cleanly rather than sitting gritty in an oily emulsion. Taste the base now: it should read aggressively, sharp, salty, hot, and sweet all at once, because the oil to come will mute every one of those by roughly half.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Emulsify the oil in. Whisking constantly, add the 90 g / 100 ml extra-virgin olive oil in a thin, steady stream. The dijon mucilage and the honey both act as emulsifiers, suspending the oil as fine droplets in the lemon-and-harissa water phase; the result is a loose, glossy, opaque dressing rather than a separated oil slick. If you prefer, combine everything in a jar with a tight lid and shake hard for 20 seconds, the emulsion is looser but reforms with a re-shake. The finished texture should be pourable and coat the back of a spoon in a thin film, not cling like mayonnaise.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Adjust and finish. Taste the emulsified dressing. It should land balanced: smoky heat first, honey sweetness rounding the second half, lemon acid lifting the finish. If it reads too rich or flat, whisk in the 5 g / 1 tsp red wine vinegar for a rounder second acid that sharpens without adding more citrus. If it reads too hot, add another 8 g / ½ tbsp honey; if too sweet, another squeeze of lemon. Use immediately over warm roasted vegetables (the residual heat loosens the dressing and blooms the harissa aromatics) or grain bowls, or store and re-shake before each use.'
    time_min: 1

Either changes the aromatic top note while keeping the honey-lemon balance intact.'

    use: 'maple syrup, date molasses, or agave'
    note: 'Maple is the closest 1:1 in viscosity and sweetness and makes the dressing vegan. Date molasses leans Maghreb and adds caramel depth. Agave is the most neutral and the thinnest, so the dressing pours slightly looser.'

    note: 'Lime is sharper and more tropical; use the same quantity. A splash of orange juice softens the acid and pushes the dressing toward the Moroccan sweet-savory register, but reduce it to 15 g / 1 tbsp orange against 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon so the dressing does not go flat.'

notes: |
  Why this works as a structure. The dressing is a three-axis balance, heat (harissa), sweet (honey), and acid (lemon), held in suspension by oil over a garlic base. Each axis is doing a specific job: the harissa supplies both capsaicin heat and the caraway-coriander-garlic aromatic signature that makes it read Tunisian rather than generically spicy; the honey does not cancel the heat but rounds its leading edge and gives the chili somewhere to land sweet; the lemon keeps the whole thing from going heavy or cloying as it sits on a plate. Pull any one axis and the dressing collapses into one-note, hot-and-oily, or sweet-and-flat, or sour-and-thin.

  The garlic-in-acid rest is not optional. Raw garlic releases allicin when cut, and allicin is what produces the hot, lingering, almost metallic pungency that ruins an otherwise balanced dressing. Letting the grated garlic sit in the lemon juice for 5 minutes before building the rest lets the acid blunt that reaction, so the garlic contributes savory depth instead of raw burn. This is the same logic behind soaking sliced raw onion in acid before it goes into a salad.

  Salt the water phase, not the emulsion. Salt dissolves in water, not in oil. Adding the fine sea salt to the lemon-harissa-honey base before the oil goes in means it dissolves completely; salt added to the finished emulsified dressing sits as undissolved grit on the tongue. The same principle is why every stable vinaigrette seasons the acid before mounting the oil.

  Where it goes. The dressing is built to break the monotony of anything roasted and earthy: charred broccoli or cauliflower, roasted carrots or sweet potato, blistered green beans, a tray of any caramelized root vegetable. On a grain bowl, spoon it over warm farro, freekeh, or pearl couscous with roasted vegetables and a handful of herbs; the warmth of the grain loosens the dressing and re-blooms the harissa's volatile aromatics. It is too assertive for a delicate lettuce salad but excellent over sturdy bitter greens (radicchio, frisée) that can stand up to the heat and sweetness.

  Make-ahead and storage. The dressing keeps refrigerated in a sealed jar for up to 1 week. It will separate at rest, which is normal for a honey-and-mustard-stabilized emulsion rather than a fully locked one; re-shake or re-whisk before each use. The garlic flavor intensifies over the first day or two, then plateaus, so a dressing made the night before reads slightly more garlic-forward than one made fresh, factor that in if garlic sensitivity is a concern.
