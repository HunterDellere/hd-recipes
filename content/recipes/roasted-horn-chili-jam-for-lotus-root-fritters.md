before_you_start:
  - 'Soak the tamarind first. Break 30 g block pulp into a bowl, cover with 120 ml hot water, and leave it to soften for 15 to 20 minutes while you char and peel the chilies. You will mash and strain it right before it goes into the pan, so start the soak before lighting the burner.'
  - 'Open a window and run the vent on high before charring. Blackening 250 g of chilies over open flame throws capsaicin-laden smoke into the air; an unventilated kitchen will have everyone coughing within a minute. If you have a gas burner, char directly over the flame; an electric stove cook should char under a broiler set to high instead.'
  - 'This jam keeps. It is built to be made ahead and improves after a day in the refrigerator as the flavors marry; treat it as a 35-minute active project that pays off across a week of fritter dinners, not a last-minute sauce.'

steps:
  - text: 'Char the chilies. Lay the 200 g red horn chilies and the 10 g Thai bird''s eye chilies directly over a high gas flame (or on a sheet under a broiler set to high). Turn them with tongs every 30 to 45 seconds until the skins blister and blacken in patches across the whole surface, 4 to 6 minutes total. You want the skin charred and the flesh underneath collapsed and soft, not the flesh burned through. The char is the entire reason this jam reads smoky; a raw blend would taste green and harsh by comparison.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Steam and peel. Transfer the charred chilies to a bowl and cover with a fitted plate or a damp towel; the trapped steam loosens the blistered skin over 10 minutes. While they steam, mash and strain the tamarind: push the soaked 30 g tamarind pulp through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean bowl, scraping the underside to capture the purée, and discard the seeds and fiber. You should have about 100 ml of medium-thick tamarind liquid. Once the chilies are cool enough to handle, rub off the blackened skins with your fingers (a little clinging char is desirable, it adds smoke), pull the stems, and scrape out most of the seeds. Roughly chop the peeled chili flesh; do not purée it, the jam wants visible texture.'
    technique: 'tamarind-souring'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Fry the aromatics. Heat the 45 g / 3 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy saucepan or small wok over medium. When it shimmers, add the 30 g minced garlic and 80 g minced shallots and fry, stirring constantly with a wooden spoon, until the alliums turn soft and pale gold and smell sweet rather than raw, 4 to 6 minutes. Hold them at gold; pushed to brown they go bitter and the bitterness carries through the whole jam. This fried-allium base is what separates a cooked chili jam from a blended raw sambal.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Add the chilies and bloom. Stir the chopped charred chili flesh into the fried aromatics and cook 2 to 3 minutes, pressing it against the pan so the chili flesh fries lightly in the oil and its roasted aroma blooms into the fat. The mixture should darken slightly and smell deeply of roasted pepper. This is the structural step where the smoke moves from the chilies into the oil that binds the jam.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Build the sweet-salt-sour reduction. Add the 50 g shaved palm sugar and stir until it melts and coats everything, about 1 minute. Pour in the 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce, the strained 100 ml tamarind liquid, and the 60 ml / ¼ cup water. Bring to a simmer and reduce over medium-low, stirring often so the sugar does not catch, until the jam thickens to a sticky, spoon-coating consistency and the oil begins to pool and separate at the edges, 12 to 16 minutes. The separation is the doneness cue: it means the water has cooked off and the jam has reached jam, not sauce. Taste here and read the three axes, it should hit sweet, salty, and sour in sequence with the heat behind them.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Finish off heat and adjust. Pull the pan from the heat and stir in the 10 g / 2 tsp fresh lime juice; adding it off heat preserves its bright top-note volatiles, which boil off if cooked. Taste a cooled spoonful (hot jam reads sweeter than cooled jam). Adjust with up to 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt if it needs grounding, a few more drops of lime if it reads flat, or a pinch more palm sugar if the heat or salt is dominant. The target is a jam where no single flavor wins; sweet, salt, sour, and smoke arrive in balance with the chili heat threaded through. Cool to room temperature before serving or jarring.'
    time_min: 3

    note: 'Coconut sugar is the closest flavor match and is widely stocked in natural-foods markets; use it 1:1. notes: |
  Why char before reducing. A fresh-blended chili sauce (sambal oelek is the reference) is raw chili, salt, and acid: bright, sharp, vegetal, and built to be hot. This jam is the opposite philosophy. Blackening the chilies over open flame before they touch the pan runs the skins and surface flesh through pyrolysis and Maillard browning, which generates the smoky, roasted-pepper compounds that define the finished jam. Peeling off the charred skin then removes the bitter, papery exterior while keeping the smoke that has penetrated the flesh. Skip the char and you have a fried chili relish; it is fine, but it is not this. The char is the load-bearing technique, and it is what makes the jam read as cooked and deep rather than raw and aggressive.

  The sweet-salt-sour triangle. Vietnamese seasoning balances on a few pillars, and this jam puts three of them in tension on purpose. Palm sugar brings butterscotch-caramel sweetness (not the molasses bite of brown sugar, which is why the substitution math matters). Fish sauce brings both the salt and the glutamate umami that gives the jam its savory length. Tamarind brings a deep, rounded tartaric sourness that lime alone cannot replace, with a finishing squeeze of lime layered on top for brightness off the heat. When the jam is right, you taste those three in sequence, sweet first, then salt, then sour, with the chili heat threaded underneath the whole arc. If it tastes one-dimensional, one of the three is short: add palm sugar for a hot-and-sour jam, lime or tamarind for a sweet-and-salty one, fish sauce for a flat one.

  Doneness is a visual cue, not a clock. The jam is done when the oil pools and separates at the edges of the pan. That separation means the free water has cooked off and the remaining mixture is sugar, fat, and reduced aromatics: jam, not sauce. Pull it earlier and it slumps and weeps on the plate; push it much past separation and the sugar starts to scorch and the jam turns tacky and bitter as it cools. Because hot jam reads sweeter than cooled jam, always taste a cooled spoonful before the final adjustment.

  Storage and make-ahead. Cooked, sugar-heavy, fish-sauce-salted, and oil-sealed, this jam keeps for 2 to 3 weeks in a clean jar in the refrigerator, and it improves after a day as the flavors marry. Press a piece of parchment onto the surface or keep a thin film of the cooking oil on top to seal it from air. Bring it back to room temperature before serving; cold from the fridge the fat firms and mutes the aromatics. Beyond the fritters it works as a dipping jam for grilled pork and chicken, a spread for banh mi, and a spoon-into anything that wants a sweet-hot-savory lift.
