source:
  name: 'The Lebanese and broader Levantine canon. Documented as the canonical accompaniment to shawarma, charcoal-grilled chicken (farrouj meshwi), and the mezze table since at least the early 20th century; the modern food-processor stabilized form is a late-20th-century development that made the sauce reliably reproducible at home.'
  note: 'Toum is structurally distinct from aioli (egg-yolk-emulsified Provençal garlic sauce) and from skordalia (Greek bread- or potato-thickened garlic sauce). The defining feature is that the garlic itself, with its natural mucilage, polysaccharides, and a small amount of protein, is the sole emulsifier; no egg, no bread, no potato. The sauce can hold up to a 1:5 garlic-to-oil ratio when built correctly. The classical mortar version exists but is laborious and breaks easily; the modern food-processor version (incremental cold-stream oil, two acids, slow tempering) is the reliable production technique. The sauce reads aggressively raw-garlic-forward, with a long burn that mellows as the toum sits in the refrigerator over the first 3 to 5 days.'

steps:
  - text: 'Refrigerate the 500 g / 2¼ cups neutral oil for at least 2 hours before starting (oil at room temperature breaks the emulsion). Have the lemon juice (60 g total, divided into two 30 g additions) and ice water at hand; toum tolerates no pause once you start streaming the oil.'
    time_min: 0
  - text: 'In the bowl of a small to medium food processor (4 to 7 cup capacity; the bowl should be about half full when the toum is finished), combine the 100 g / 25 to 30 garlic cloves and the 12 g / 2 tsp salt. Process 30 seconds, scraping the sides every 10 seconds, until the garlic is reduced to a fine even paste with no visible chunks. Add the first 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice and process 15 seconds; the mixture will turn from pale yellow paste to a pale slurry as the lemon hydrates the garlic.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'With the processor running on continuous low (not pulse), drizzle in the cold neutral oil through the feed tube in a thin steady stream, starting slow (a near-drip, about 1 tbsp per 10 seconds) for the first 100 g of oil; this initial slow phase is where the emulsion takes shape. As the toum visibly thickens and turns a brilliant white (you will see and hear the change in the processor: the slosh becomes a thicker churn), the oil can be added in a slightly faster steady stream.'
    technique: 'cold-oil-emulsion'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'After about half the oil is in (250 g), pause the processor and add the second 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice; pulse 5 times to incorporate. The acid addition mid-stream is a structural stabilizer; it loosens the partial emulsion and prevents it from over-tightening and breaking. Resume streaming the remaining 250 g cold oil at a moderate pace (about 1 tbsp per 5 seconds) until fully incorporated. The finished toum should be brilliant white, light, fluffy, and the texture of soft mayonnaise; it should hold a stand-up shape on a spoon.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'If the emulsion breaks at any point (the sauce turns oily and the white fluffy texture collapses into a yellowish slick), stop immediately. Transfer the broken sauce to a separate container; clean the processor bowl. Process 2 fresh garlic cloves with 1 tsp salt and 1 tsp ice water to a paste; with the processor running, slowly drizzle the broken toum back in (treat it like the oil), and the emulsion will rebuild. The ice water rescue works because cold water plus garlic mucilage rebuilds the emulsion from a fresh structural seed.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Taste and adjust: toum should read aggressively garlicky with bright lemon and clean salt; if it reads flat at this stage, whisk in an extra 1 g / pinch of fine sea salt by hand. It will mellow significantly over the first 3 to 5 days as the raw garlic compounds (allicin and its oxidation products) break down. If too sharp at this stage, accept it; toum is meant to age. Transfer to a clean glass jar and refrigerate at least 24 hours before the first use. Serve as a dip for raw vegetables and grilled bread, a sauce for shawarma and roast chicken, a spread for sandwiches and wraps, a stir-in for grain bowls, or thinned with water as a dressing for salads and vegetables.'
    time_min: 1

modifications:
  - for: 'cuisine pivot'
    to: 'as a Provençal aioli (the egg-yolk version of the same logic)'
    how: 'Add 1 large egg yolk to the garlic-salt-first-lemon paste before streaming oil; replace the neutral oil with extra-virgin olive oil; reduce the total oil to 250 g; reduce the second lemon to 15 g. The egg yolk provides a powerful lecithin emulsifier that stabilizes the much more aggressive olive-oil emulsion. The result is structurally aioli, denser and more savory than toum, and the canonical Provençal table sauce.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'as a thinned dressing'
    to: 'as a salad and grain bowl dressing'
    how: 'Whisk 60 g / ¼ cup toum with 30 g / 2 tbsp water and 15 g / 1 tbsp additional lemon juice in a small bowl until pourable; taste and add salt if needed. The thinned toum is a dressing for romaine, leafy salads, grain bowls (farro, freekeh, bulgur), roasted vegetable salads, and grilled vegetable platters. Pairs especially well with grilled or roasted chicken, lamb, and fish.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'flavor pivot'
    to: 'as a herb toum (toum bil khodra)'
    how: 'After the toum is fully built, fold in 20 g / ½ packed cup finely chopped flat-leaf parsley, 10 g / 2 tbsp finely chopped mint, and 5 g / 1 tbsp finely chopped dill. The herb toum reads softer and more vegetal; pairs especially well with grilled fish and roasted vegetables. Less canonical than plain toum but a common Beirut variant.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'neutral oil (sunflower, grapeseed, or canola)'
    use: 'a 50/50 blend of neutral oil and a mild olive oil'
    note: 'Pure neutral oil is the canonical choice for toum because the polyphenols in olive oil destabilize the emulsion and the strong olive flavor overwhelms the garlic. Cooks who prefer a slight olive oil character can use a 50/50 blend with a mild (not robust, not extra-virgin-aggressive) olive oil; the emulsion is slightly less stable. Pure extra-virgin olive oil reliably breaks the toum emulsion; do not use it.'

    note: 'Lemon is the canonical acid in toum and the flavor is structural, but a 50/50 lemon-and-white-wine-vinegar blend reads less floral and more sharp; some cooks prefer this profile. Pure vinegar is too aggressive and reads as a fundamentally different sauce.'

notes: |
  Toum is one of the structurally most demanding sauces in the home kitchen because the only emulsifier is the garlic itself; there is no yolk, no bread, no mustard to safety-net the emulsion. The technique fails for predictable reasons: warm oil (the polar molecules align less consistently in the emulsion at higher temperature, and the sauce breaks within minutes), too-fast oil addition at the start (the early 100 g of oil sets the emulsion; if you flood the processor before the seed emulsion forms, the whole batch breaks), undersized garlic (the recipe needs the structural mass of 100 g / 25 to 30 cloves; halving the garlic for milder flavor breaks the emulsion ratio), and using olive oil (the polyphenols disrupt the emulsion structure; pure olive-oil toum reliably fails). The two-acid, cold-oil, continuous-stream technique above is the reliable production method.

  The aging curve. Fresh toum, just made, reads aggressively raw-garlic-sharp with a noticeable burn at the back of the throat from the alliinase-produced allicin. Over the first 24 hours in the refrigerator, the allicin oxidizes to milder sulfur compounds and the burn fades. Over the first week, the toum mellows progressively; by day 5 to 7 it reads garlicky but pleasant, fully integrated, the canonical balance. Toum that tastes too aggressive on day 1 will taste right by day 4. Plan toum as a make-ahead condiment, not a same-day sauce.

  Versatility. Toum is the broadest single condiment in the Levantine pantry. Use it as: a dip for raw vegetables (cucumber, carrot, radish, fennel, bell pepper, cherry tomato), a dip for grilled flatbread, the canonical sauce for shawarma (chicken, beef, lamb), the canonical sauce for charcoal-grilled chicken (farrouj meshwi), a sauce for kafta and shish kebab, a spread for sandwiches and wraps (replaces mayonnaise in any application), a sauce for fries and roasted potatoes (the Beirut snack canon), a stir-in for grain bowls (rice, freekeh, farro, bulgur), a finish for grilled or roasted vegetables (cauliflower, eggplant, zucchini, broccoli), thinned with water and lemon as a dressing for salads and grain bowls (see modifications), a stir-in for soups (cannellini bean soup, lentil soup, vegetable soup; a tablespoon stirred in at service brings a structural garlic-and-fat finish), and as a base for compound sauces (mix with harissa for a hot sauce, with za'atar oil for an herbal sauce, with pomegranate molasses for a sweet-tart variation).

  Pairs across Lebanese, broader Levantine, Mediterranean, French (in place of aioli), Turkish, Egyptian, North African, and contemporary Western contexts. The neutral oil base means toum carries flavor without competing with it; this is what makes it more cuisine-flexible than aioli.

  Storage. Keeps 3 to 4 weeks refrigerated in a sealed glass jar; the emulsion is stable cold and does not separate. Always use a clean spoon (food contamination is the only failure mode in storage; toum at refrigerator temperature is too acidic and concentrated for bacterial growth). Do not freeze; the emulsion breaks irreversibly on thawing.
