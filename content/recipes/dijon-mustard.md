steps:
  - text: 'Combine 60 g brown mustard seeds, 30 g yellow mustard seeds, 180 ml / ¾ cup dry white wine, and 60 ml / ¼ cup cold water in a glass or ceramic bowl (not metal; the heat reaction with raw mustard reads bitter against aluminum or carbon steel).   - text: 'After the soak the seeds should be plump and squashable between two fingers. If they still feel hard at the core, give it another 12 hours; undersoaked seeds blend gritty.'

notes: |
  The chemistry: mustard seeds contain glucosinolates and the enzyme myrosinase in separate compartments. Crushing the seed in water mixes them, and the enzyme cleaves the glucosinolates into isothiocyanates — the volatile compounds that punch the back of the nose. Brown seeds carry sinigrin (sharper, hotter), yellow seeds carry sinalbin (gentler, more sulfurous). The Dijon character comes from the brown:yellow ratio of about 2:1 plus the wine acidity.

  Why no heat: cooking the mustard kills the myrosinase before it can do its job. All the bite comes from the cold soak and the cold blend.

  Why metal-free: mustard reacts with reactive metals (aluminum, untreated cast iron, copper) and picks up an off-bitterness within hours. Glass, ceramic, stainless steel are fine.

  Why the mellow: freshly blended mustard is dominated by the shortest-lived isothiocyanates, which evaporate and react with water over the first 1–2 days. What's left is the stable, rounded heat that makes a real Dijon recognizable.

  Storage: airtight in the fridge, 6 months easily. The flavor actually improves through month 2. Discard if it grows pink or fuzzy on top, but properly salted mustard is hostile to most spoilage organisms.
