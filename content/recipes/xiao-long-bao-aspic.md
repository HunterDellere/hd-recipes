steps:
  - text: 'Stub: blanch the 500 g pork skin and 500 g pork bones in the 2000 ml cold water. Bring to a hard boil, boil 5 minutes, drain, rinse. Scrape the blanched skin to remove any remaining hair, fat, or impurities; this is the structural cleanup step that makes the aspic clear rather than cloudy.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Stub: combine the blanched skin and bones in a clean pot with the 1500 ml fresh cold water, 20 g ginger, 3 scallion whites, and 30 ml Shaoxing wine. Bring to a bare simmer, skim any foam, and hold at a tremor (below 90 C) for 3 to 4 hours. The skin will dissolve almost entirely into the liquid; the bones will contribute flavor without dissolving. Do not boil; a hard boil emulsifies fat and produces a cloudy aspic.'
    time_min: 200
  - text: 'Stub: strain through a fine-mesh sieve, then again through cheesecloth for a clear set. Season the strained liquid with the 6 g kosher salt while still warm; the salt dissolves cleanly and the aspic seasons evenly. Pour into a shallow square or rectangular container (the cube shape is structurally important for the dumpling application) and cool to room temperature, then refrigerate at least 8 hours and ideally overnight.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Stub: the chilled aspic should be firm enough to cut into 5 mm cubes with a knife; it should hold a clean edge without weeping. If the aspic is too soft to cube (the skin-to-bone ratio was off, or the simmer was too short), the rescue is to re-warm, add 5 g powdered gelatin per 500 g liquid, dissolve, and re-chill. The aspic keeps refrigerated 5 days; freeze in 100 g portions for up to 3 months.'
    time_min: 10
