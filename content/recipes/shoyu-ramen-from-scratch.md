steps:
  - text: 'Night before: marinate the ajitama eggs. Bring a saucepan of water to a hard boil. Lower the 4 refrigerator-cold eggs into the water on a spoon (cold-to-hot for the membrane to release cleanly from the shell). Boil exactly 6 minutes 30 seconds for a slightly runny yolk and fully set white. Transfer immediately to an ice bath; chill 5 minutes. Peel under running water. Whisk together the 80 g soy sauce, 40 g mirin, 80 g water, and 10 g sugar in a small container. Add the peeled eggs; submerge with a piece of parchment cut to fit (eggs float; the parchment keeps them under). Refrigerate 8 to 12 hours. Past 12 hours the white over-salts and goes leathery; pull at 12 hours max.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Morning: start the broth. Place the 1000 g pork bones in a large pot, cover with cold water, bring to a hard boil, boil 5 minutes (the bones release a gray scum that rises to the surface). Drain in a colander; rinse the bones under cold water to wash off any residual scum. In the same pot (rinsed), combine the blanched pork bones, 1500 g chicken backs and feet, 4000 ml cold water, halved 200 g yellow onion (skin on), 40 g sliced ginger, 30 g scallion greens, and 10 g dried shiitake. Bring to a hard boil, then immediately reduce to the lowest possible simmer where the surface barely trembles (a hard simmer or boil emulsifies fat and clouds the broth; this is critical). Skim the gray scum that rises in the first 30 minutes with a fine-mesh skimmer. Simmer covered with the lid offset 6 to 8 hours. The broth should reduce slightly and turn pale gold; it should NOT turn milky-white (that is tonkotsu, a different style requiring hard-boiling).'
    technique: 'simmering'
    time_min: 480
  - text: 'Make the shoyu tare. While the broth simmers, combine the 200 g soy sauce, 60 g mirin, 60 g sake, and 10 g sugar in a small saucepan over medium heat. Bring to a simmer; cook 2 minutes to burn off the alcohol. Pull off heat. Add the 10 g kombu and 10 g katsuobushi to the hot liquid. Let steep 30 minutes; the kombu releases glutamate, the katsuobushi releases inosinate, and the umami synergy multiplies in the soy base. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a glass jar. Refrigerate up to 3 weeks; this is more tare than 4 bowls of ramen need, so the surplus is for the next session.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Braise the chashu in the broth (at the 4-hour mark of the broth simmer). Tie the 700 g pork belly into a tight cylinder with kitchen twine at 2 cm intervals. In a separate small heavy pot or saucepan, combine 120 g soy sauce, 60 g mirin, 60 g sake, 20 g sugar, and 600 g of the simmering broth (ladle from the main pot). Submerge the rolled pork belly. Simmer covered very gently (90°C, just under a simmer) for 2.5 to 3 hours. The pork should be fork-tender but still hold its shape; over-braising into shred-tender produces chashu that falls apart when sliced. Pull the chashu from the braise liquid; let cool to room temperature, then refrigerate 4 hours or overnight (firm chashu slices into clean thin coins; warm chashu shreds). Strain and reserve the braise liquid; it can fortify the broth or season subsequent bowls.'
    technique: 'braising'
    time_min: 180
  - text: 'Finish the broth. After 6 to 8 hours total simmering, strain the broth through a fine-mesh sieve lined with a single layer of cheesecloth into a clean pot. Discard the spent bones, vegetables, and aromatics. You should have about 2500 to 2800 ml of pale gold broth; if reduced too far, top up with hot water to 2500 ml. Taste; the broth should read deeply meaty and slightly gelatinous (it will gel when cold). Do not salt the broth; the tare does all the seasoning in the bowl. Bring back to 90°C just before assembly.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Pre-assembly: stage everything. Slice the cold chashu into 4 mm thick coins (cleaner slices when fully cold). Cut the marinated eggs in half lengthwise with a damp knife; the runny yolk releases cleanly with a wet blade. Slice the 30 g scallion whites and pale greens into 2 mm bias slices. Cut the nori into 4 rectangles. Warm 4 large ramen bowls (deep, wide bowls 18 to 20 cm diameter) with a kettle of hot water; pour out the water just before assembly. Set out the 60 g menma if using.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Cook the noodles. Bring a large pot of unsalted water to a hard rolling boil (ramen noodle water is NOT salted; the kansui in the noodles provides the seasoning, and salt in the water can over-season). Drop the 500 g fresh ramen noodles into the boiling water; cook 90 seconds for medium-thin Sun Noodle (check the package; some noodles want 75 to 120 seconds). Stir continuously to prevent clumping. Drain immediately in a colander; do NOT rinse (the surface starch is the seasoning carrier).'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Assemble the bowls. Working fast (the next 30 seconds): pour out the bowl-warming water. Spoon 30 to 40 g of shoyu tare into each warm bowl (start at 30 g and dial up by household preference; the tare is the only seasoning). Ladle 500 to 600 ml of 90°C broth into each bowl, swirling to dissolve the tare. Divide the cooked noodles evenly across the 4 bowls, using chopsticks to fold them into a neat mound that breaks the broth surface. Lay 2 to 3 chashu coins overlapping on one side of each bowl. Lay one ajitama egg half on the opposite side. Mound the sliced scallion in the center. Stand one nori rectangle upright against the side of each bowl. Add the menma if using. Serve immediately; ramen waits for no one and the noodles soften within 3 minutes in the broth.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Ramen is four dishes. The structural unit of ramen is (broth) + (tare) + (noodles) + (toppings). Each component is made independently and then assembled at the moment of service. The broth provides body and depth; the tare provides salt and the flavor profile that names the bowl (shoyu, shio, miso, tonkotsu); the noodles provide texture; the toppings provide variation and visual structure. Mastering ramen at home is mastering the orchestration of four parallel cooks, not mastering one recipe.

  The broth-versus-tare distinction is critical. The broth itself is essentially unseasoned (no salt added); all the seasoning comes from the tare. This is opposite to most Western soup traditions where the stock is salted as it builds. The reason is calibration: a single broth can serve shoyu, shio, miso, and tonkotsu bowls by changing the tare, and the cook can dial the salt level by adjusting the tare quantity per bowl rather than re-salting after the broth is done. Many home cooks make the mistake of salting the broth and then adding tare on top, producing an over-salted bowl.

  The hybrid chicken-pork broth is the Tokyo approach. Pure chicken broth makes a clean but thin bowl (Tokyo shio-style); pure pork bone broth in hard boil makes tonkotsu; the 60/40 chicken-pork hybrid simmered (not boiled) is the canonical Tokyo shoyu base. The chicken provides gelatin and a clean meaty backbone; the pork adds depth and sweetness. Chicken feet are the structural addition; they provide more gelatin per gram than any other chicken part and give the broth its body.

  Do not boil; simmer. The single most important technique distinction is the simmer-versus-boil call. Tonkotsu ramen is made by hard-boiling pork bones for 12 to 18 hours to emulsify the fat into the water (the milky-white appearance). Shoyu, shio, and miso ramen are made by gentle simmering at 85 to 90°C so the broth stays clear. Boiling a shoyu broth produces a cloudy, greasy broth that no amount of tare can rescue. The lid offset on the pot, the burner on its lowest possible setting, and patience are the entire technique.

  The umami synergy: kombu (glutamate) and katsuobushi (inosinate) together produce 7 to 8 times the umami punch of either alone. This is the structural reason the tare contains both, and the structural reason Japanese stock-making puts both in the kombu-katsuobushi dashi. The synergy is the most thoroughly documented umami interaction in food science; Kikunae Ikeda discovered glutamate via kombu in 1908, and the inosinate-katsuobushi multiplier was characterized in the 1950s.

  Why the broth is not salted. The tare is the entire salt vehicle. A bowl of ramen uses 30 to 50 g of tare in 500 to 600 ml of broth, which is 5 to 8% tare by volume. The tare itself is approximately 15% salt (soy sauce is ~16% NaCl by weight), so the finished bowl is about 0.75 to 1.2% salt, which lands in the canonical seasoning range. Salting the broth on top of this overshoots into inedibly salty territory.

  The assembly is the entire dish. The 30-second window from drained noodles to served bowl is the moment where ramen is made. The noodles soften in the broth from the instant they hit; serving more than 3 minutes after the noodles drain produces noodles in lukewarm broth. This is why every component must be staged before the noodles go in the water, every bowl must be pre-warmed, and the broth must be at 90°C (not boiling, which would cook the egg yolks gray on top).

  Weekend project, ahead-prep friendly. The broth (8 hours), tare (10 minutes), chashu (3 hours, fits inside the broth window), and ajitama (10 minutes plus overnight marinade) can all be done a day or two ahead. Day-of: pull the chashu and ajitama from the fridge an hour before, slice everything, reheat the broth to 90°C, cook the noodles, assemble. With the components prepped, the day-of work is 30 minutes. This is the only practical way to make ramen at home regularly.

  Ramen is best fresh and impossible to reheat. The noodles do not survive refrigeration in broth (they bloat and turn mush). Leftover broth, tare, chashu, and eggs can all be saved separately and reassembled the next day with fresh-cooked noodles for a second-day bowl that is as good as the first. Never refrigerate assembled ramen.
