before_you_start:
  - 'Jambalaya is a one-pot pilaf, not a stew. The rice cooks IN the pot with the protein and stock; the absorption is calibrated to a 1:1.5 to 1:1.75 rice-to-liquid ratio. Once the rice goes in (Phase 5), DO NOT LIFT THE LID for 25 minutes. Lifting releases steam, breaks the heat envelope, and gives unevenly cooked rice. The 10-minute off-heat rest at the end is the canonical second phase that finishes the rice cleanly.'
  - 'Brown the chicken and the andouille well; the rendered fat carries the dish. Skipping the protein browning produces a pale, flat jambalaya regardless of how good the rest of the seasoning is. The fond on the pot bottom dissolves into the rice during the simmer and is the structural flavor base.'
  - 'Add shrimp in the LAST 5 MINUTES. Shrimp added with the chicken go rubber by the time the rice is done. The shrimp curl and turn opaque in 3 to 4 minutes at the simmer temperature; that is the cue they are done.'

steps:
  - text: 'Season the 600 g chicken chunks with 6 g kosher salt 30 minutes ahead. Slice the 300 g andouille into 1 cm coins. Dice the Trinity (250 g onion, 120 g celery, 120 g bell pepper) and mince the 18 g / 5 cloves garlic. Rinse the 360 g long-grain rice in a fine-mesh sieve until the water runs clear; drain. The mise must be complete before browning begins; the cook from the brown through the simmer is one continuous push with no chopping pauses.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Brown the proteins. Heat 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy 4 to 5 quart Dutch oven over medium-high heat until shimmering. Add the 600 g chicken chunks in a single layer; brown undisturbed 3 to 4 minutes per side until deeply mahogany on at least two sides. Transfer to a plate. Add the 300 g andouille coins; brown 2 minutes per side until the edges crisp and an orange-tinted fond accumulates on the pot bottom. Transfer to the plate. Pour off all but 2 tbsp / 28 g rendered fat (save the excess for a future roux or for sweating vegetables on another night).'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Sweat the Trinity. Reduce heat to medium. Add the 250 g onion, 120 g celery, and 120 g bell pepper to the rendered fat and fond. Stir constantly with a wooden spoon, scraping up the brown fond from the pot bottom into the vegetables. The vegetables will deglaze the pot as they release water. Sweat 6 to 8 minutes until the onion turns translucent and the vegetables soften. Add the 18 g / 5 cloves minced garlic; stir 60 seconds until fragrant.'
    technique: 'fond-deglaze'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Toast the tomato paste. Push the vegetables to the edges of the pot. Drop the 30 g / 2 tbsp tomato paste into the cleared center. Toast 90 seconds, stirring the paste alone in the hot fat until it darkens from bright red-orange to brick-brown and smells deeply caramelized. The toast is structural; raw tomato paste reads tinny and flat in the finished dish. Stir the toasted paste into the Trinity.'
    technique: 'fond-deglaze'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add spices and tomato. Add the 4 g smoked paprika, 4 g sweet paprika, 2 g oregano, 2 g thyme, 2 g cayenne, 2 bay leaves, and 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt. Stir 30 seconds to bloom the spices in the hot fat. Add the 200 g drained diced tomatoes; stir to combine.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add rice and liquid; return the protein. Add the 360 g rinsed long-grain rice to the pot; stir 60 seconds so each grain coats with the seasoned fat. Pour in the 600 ml / 2½ cups warm chicken stock, the 5 g / 1 tsp Worcestershire, and 8 g / 1½ tsp hot sauce. Stir once to evenly distribute the rice and submerge it under the liquid. Return the browned 600 g chicken and 300 g andouille (with any accumulated juices) to the pot; nestle them into the rice. Bring to a simmer over medium-high.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Cover and simmer. Once the pot is at a clear simmer, immediately reduce heat to low (the surface should tremble slightly, not roll). Cover with a tight lid. Do not lift the lid for 22 minutes. The rice is steaming in a closed envelope; lifting drops the temperature and releases the moisture the rice needs to cook evenly. Set a timer.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Add shrimp; finish. Lift the lid. The rice should look 90% cooked (still slightly al dente in the center of each grain). Scatter the 400 g seasoned shrimp evenly over the top of the rice; do not stir them in. Re-cover. Cook 4 to 5 more minutes; the shrimp will curl from translucent gray to opaque pink as they steam in the rice cloud.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Rest off heat. Pull the pot off the burner. Keep covered, do not stir. Let stand 10 minutes. This is the canonical pilaf rest; the rice finishes cooking in residual heat and the grains separate cleanly rather than clumping. Fish out and discard the 2 bay leaves.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Fluff, finish, and serve. Open the lid. Fluff the jambalaya gently with a fork, working the shrimp down through the rice and lifting the rice from the pot bottom (the bottom layer should be slightly caramelized into a soft Cajun socarrat, not burnt). Scatter the 40 g sliced green onions and 12 g chopped parsley over the surface. Taste; adjust salt with kosher salt by the half-teaspoon. Serve directly from the pot into wide bowls with the 1 lemon cut into 6 wedges and additional hot sauce on the table.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Creole versus Cajun, the actual distinction. Creole jambalaya is the New Orleans, urban, French-Quarter version with tomato; Cajun jambalaya is the rural Acadiana, brown, no-tomato version. Both are correct, both are old, and the partisan arguments are entertaining but unsettled. The Creole version reads closer to paella and West African jollof; the Cajun version reads closer to a brown rice pilaf with extreme protein browning. This recipe is Creole; the modifications give the Cajun direction.

  The 1:1.7 rice-to-liquid ratio is the structural call. Plain long-grain rice cooks at 1:2 (rice to water by volume). Jambalaya cooks closer to 1:1.7 because the protein renders fat and moisture into the pot, the tomato adds liquid, and the simmer is calibrated to leave a slight caramelized bottom layer (the soft Cajun socarrat). Adding the full 1:2 ratio produces a soupy, soft jambalaya; going below 1:1.5 burns the bottom layer. The recipe number above is calibrated; resist the urge to add more stock mid-cook.

  Do not lift the lid. The single most common failure in home jambalaya is opening the pot to peek. The rice is steam-cooking in a closed envelope; lifting releases steam, drops the temperature, and produces unevenly cooked grains (crunchy top, mushy bottom). Set a 22-minute timer when you put the lid on after Phase 6. Trust the timer.

  The rest is the second cook. Once the rice has simmered 22 minutes covered, then the shrimp have steamed 4 minutes covered, the 10-minute off-heat rest is the canonical pilaf finish. The starch on the grains finishes hydrating in residual heat, the grains separate cleanly when fluffed, and the protein finishes carrying over to safe temperature. Skipping the rest produces a wetter jambalaya with grains that still feel slightly al dente at the center.

  Why long-grain only. Long-grain rice (Louisiana long-grain, basmati, Carolina Gold) has a low amylopectin-to-amylose ratio; the grains stay separate even after a long simmer. Short-grain rice (Japanese, arborio, bomba) is high in amylopectin, which releases starch and produces a creamy, clumped texture; that is the right behavior for risotto and paella socarrat but the wrong behavior for jambalaya, where the grains should be distinct.

  Day-2 jambalaya is excellent. Refrigerate up to 4 days; the shrimp may go slightly rubbery on reheat (this is acceptable for leftovers, not for fresh service). Reheat gently in a covered pot over medium-low with a splash of water (60 to 80 g per portion) to revive the rice moisture. Freeze 2 to 3 months but expect the shrimp to read softer after thaw.

  Heat calibration. The 2 g / ½ tsp cayenne plus 8 g / 1½ tsp hot sauce in the pot is a moderate Creole heat level; New Orleans home cooks regularly run hotter (3 to 4 g cayenne plus 15 to 20 g hot sauce). The table-side hot sauce lets each diner dial up. The dish is meant to register heat as a warm slow burn, not as a finishing bite.
