  name: 'Adapted from the Italian antipasto tradition of raw celery (sedano) with Parmigiano, walnut, and lemon, a Central and Northern Italian winter-table standard.'
  note: 'The dish belongs to the family of Italian raw-vegetable salads (insalata di sedano, the celery cousin of the more famous insalata di finocchio e arance) that lean on a single excellent vegetable, a hard aged cheese, a nut, and lemon. The entire technique is in the knife and the timing: slice thin enough that raw celery reads as crisp rather than fibrous, salt and dress at the last moment so the celery stays crunchy, and shave the cheese rather than grate so it reads as a textural layer instead of dissolving into the dressing.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Toast the walnuts first and let them cool completely before building the salad. Warm walnuts wilt the celery and turn the dressing greasy as their oils thin in the heat; they need to be cool and crisp when they go in. This is the only step with a lead time, 8 minutes of toasting plus 10 minutes of cooling.'
  - 'Use the pale inner celery hearts, not the dark tough outer ribs. The outer ribs are stringy and aggressively bitter raw; the hearts are sweeter, more tender, and the whole point of serving celery uncooked. Reserve the outer ribs for stock or a soffritto.'
  - 'Dress the celery no more than 5 minutes before serving. Salt and acid draw water out of the cut cells over time; dressed early, the celery goes limp and weeps a watery puddle. This salad is assembled to order.'

steps:
  - text: 'Toast the walnuts. Spread the 60 g / ½ cup walnut halves in a single layer in a dry stainless or cast-iron skillet over medium heat. Toast, tossing every 30 seconds, for 6 to 8 minutes until the nuts smell distinctly nutty and the cut faces have gone one shade darker. Walnuts hold bitter tannins in their papery skin and a raw-green astringency in the flesh; dry-toasting volatilizes both and develops the fat into a rounder, more savory flavor. Tip them onto a cool plate immediately, the residual pan heat will push them to scorched and acrid within a minute. Let them cool completely, then break into rough pieces with your fingers. Cooling is not optional: warm walnuts wilt the celery on contact.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Slice the celery. Pull 6 to 8 pale inner ribs from the head, the tender yellow-green hearts, and reserve the soft inner leaves whole. Lay each rib on the board and slice 1 to 2 mm thick on a sharp bias (the knife angled about 45 degrees down the rib). The bias cut does two things: it lengthens each slice into an elegant ellipse and, more importantly, it cuts across more of the longitudinal fibers, so raw celery reads as a clean crisp snap rather than a stringy chew. You want about 300 g / 6 to 8 ribs of sliced celery. Keep the slices in a bowl and the leaves separate.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Build the dressing. In a small bowl, whisk the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice with the 2 g / scant ½ tsp fine sea salt until the salt dissolves fully, 20 to 30 seconds; dissolving the salt into the acid first means it seasons evenly and you avoid gritty undissolved crystals on the celery. Then whisk in the 45 g / 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil in a thin stream to form a loose, just-emulsified vinaigrette. This is a raw, uncooked dressing, so the olive oil''s green, peppery polyphenols come through undiluted, the reason a finishing-grade oil earns its place here over a neutral cooking oil. Taste: it should read sharp and bright on its own, since the cheese and celery will both temper it.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Dress the celery, at the last moment. No more than 5 minutes before serving, pour the dressing over the sliced 300 g celery and the reserved leaves, then toss gently with your hands until every slice is coated and glossy. Add the 1 g / ½ tsp reserved lemon zest now for an aromatic citrus-oil top-note that the juice alone cannot supply. Do not dress ahead: salt and acid osmotically pull water out of the cut celery cells over time, so an early-dressed salad collapses into a limp, watery puddle within 15 minutes. The celery should still snap when you bite it as it goes to the plate.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Assemble and finish. Pile the dressed celery loosely onto a wide platter or into shallow bowls, lifting and mounding rather than pressing flat so the salad keeps height and air. Tuck the cooled 60 g / ½ cup toasted walnut pieces throughout. Drape the 60 g / 2 oz shaved Parmigiano-Reggiano sheets over and through the salad in loose folds, layering them so the cheese reads as ribbons of texture and salt-umami rather than a uniform blanket; the shave (not a grate) is the whole textural argument of the dish. Crack the 2 g / 1 tsp coarse black pepper over the top, where the large piperine flakes hit the tongue first. Taste a forkful: the Parmigiano carries the salt and the deep glutamate umami, the lemon and zest carry the finishing acid, and the two should land in balance. If it reads flat, finish with a pinch of the optional 1 g / ¼ tsp flaky salt for a sharp top-note; if it reads too sharp, a final thread of olive oil rounds it. Serve immediately, while the celery is still crisp.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why raw celery rewards the knife. Celery is mostly water held in long parallel vascular bundles, the strings, that run the length of each rib. Cooked, those bundles soften and the cell water concentrates into sweetness. Raw, they read as the texture problem: a thick slice is fibrous and stringy, and the longer the fiber, the more it catches between the teeth. The bias slice at 1 to 2 mm is the entire fix. Cutting at an angle severs more of the longitudinal fibers per slice and keeps each piece short, so the celery reads as a clean wet snap rather than a chew. A mandoline is the most consistent way to get there; a sharp knife and patience get you the same result.

  Why the inner hearts only. The pale yellow-green inner ribs of a celery head are protected by the outer canopy and never fully photosynthesize, so they develop less of the bitter apigenin and fewer tough fibers than the dark outer ribs. They are sweeter, more tender, and meant to be eaten raw. The dark outer ribs are not a lesser version of the same thing, they are a different ingredient: stringy, aggressively bitter, and built for long cooking in a soffritto or stock where their bitterness reads as savory depth. Do not serve them raw in this salad.

  The acid-salt-umami triangle. This is a five-ingredient salad with no heat and no place to hide, so the seasoning logic has to be exact. The Parmigiano is the glutamate source and carries most of the salt; aged 24 months, it brings free-amino-acid umami and the crunchy tyrosine crystals that read as savory depth. The lemon is the finishing acid; its job is to cut the fat of both the cheese and the toasted walnuts so the salad stays bright rather than going heavy and cloying. The fine sea salt in the dressing is the bridge, tuned down because the cheese is already salty. Taste the finished salad before it leaves the kitchen: if it reads flat, it almost always needs acid (more lemon or a pinch of flaky salt), not more oil.

  Why toast the walnuts. Raw walnuts carry bitter tannins concentrated in the papery skin and a green, astringent note in the flesh. A dry toast of 6 to 8 minutes volatilizes the astringent compounds, drives off surface moisture, and pushes the nut fat into a deeper, rounder, more savory range through light Maillard browning on the cut faces. The cost of skipping it is a salad that reads green and slightly bitter, with the raw nut fighting the celery''s own bitterness rather than balancing it. Cool the walnuts completely before they touch the salad, since their warmth wilts the celery and thins their oils into a greasy slick on the dressing.

  Timing is the whole discipline. There is no cooking to get wrong here, only sequencing. Toast and cool the nuts first. Slice the celery and hold it dry. Build the dressing and hold it separate. Then dress, shave, and serve in one continuous motion no more than 5 minutes before it hits the table. Dressed celery is a clock: salt and acid osmotically pull water from the cut cells, and a salad built 20 minutes ahead arrives limp and sitting in its own weeping liquid. Assemble to order and the same five ingredients arrive crisp, glossy, and sharp.
