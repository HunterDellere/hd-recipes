before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 4 to 12-month protein-hydrolysis cure, not a recipe you finish today. Active work is 90 minutes upfront for cleaning and layering, plus a 30-minute repack at the 6-month mark; the rest is patience.'
  - 'Source sashimi-grade fresh anchovies caught within 24 hours. Previously frozen, supermarket-display fish carry too much enzymatic decay and cure into mushy off-flavored fillets. A reliable Mediterranean or Asian fish market is the right channel; call ahead.'
  - 'Find a stable cool spot at 12 to 16 °C for the full cure window. A pantry, root cellar, or wine fridge works; a refrigerator (4 °C) stalls the cathepsin proteases and the cure never matures. A warm kitchen (above 20 °C) accelerates spoilage past the safe window.'
  - 'Use non-iodized coarse sea salt only. Iodized table salt or fine salts pack too dense and produce a salty, under-hydrolyzed cure with a metallic finish.'

steps:
  - text: 'Clean the 1000 g / 2.2 lb anchovies: pinch off the head with a clean break that pulls the gut out attached, run a thumbnail along the belly cavity to remove any residual viscera, scale lightly with the back of a knife. Rinse briefly in cold sea-water-salinity brine (35 g salt per litre water) for 60 seconds to firm the flesh, then drain. The fish must be very fresh; sashimi-grade or caught within 24 hours is the working standard. Older fish develops trimethylamine before the cure can stabilize the flesh, and the resulting cure tastes fishy rather than savory.'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'Layer in a non-reactive container (glass jar, terracotta crock, or food-grade plastic; avoid metal, the salt-acid environment leaches metallic flavor). Begin with a 1 cm bed of the 500 g coarse sea salt at the bottom. Lay anchovies belly-up tail-to-head in a single layer, then cover the layer fully with another 5 to 8 mm of salt. Repeat until the anchovies are used and the top layer is salt. The pattern is salt-fish-salt-fish-salt; never fish-on-fish without a salt barrier between layers, or the contact points cure unevenly and develop soft spots.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Top with a heavy weight: a plate that fits inside the container plus a clean rock, a water-filled jar, or a purpose-made fermentation weight. The weight should press the anchovies down hard enough that the brine that forms over the first week (the salt drawing water out of the fish) submerges them completely. If after 7 days the brine line is below the salt, top off with additional saturated brine (35 percent salt by weight in cold water) to bring the level up.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Hold at 12 to 16 °C: a cool cellar, a wine fridge, or a basement room are ideal. A standard refrigerator (2 to 4 °C) is too cold and slows the cure to 9 to 12 months for an equivalent depth; above 18 °C the rate of microbial spoilage outruns the rate of cathepsin protein hydrolysis and the cure fails. The temperature window is the single most important variable in this recipe. Hold for a minimum of 4 months and up to 12 months for fuller flavor development. The longer cures develop more pronounced glutamate-and-IMP umami; shorter cures retain more of the original fresh-fish character.'
    time_min: 0
  - text: 'Check monthly. Skim any white surface mold (this is normal benign penicillium-style growth and is harmless if discarded; black mold or pink discoloration are spoilage signs and the entire batch must be discarded). Top off the brine if it has dropped below the salt line. The flesh should darken from pink-red to grey-pink to russet over the cure window; no smell should escape the container until you crack the lid, at which point a clean savory ocean-and-cheese-rind aroma is the right signal. Sour or ammonia smells indicate spoilage.'
    time_min: 0
  - text: 'At month 4, taste-test: pull a single fillet from the top, rinse off the surface salt under cold water, taste. The fillet should pull cleanly off the spine with the spine sliding out as a clean ribbon. The flavor should read salty-savory-fishy with no rawness, no metallic copper note, and no ammonia. If the fillet still reads underdeveloped (residual rawness, soft texture that does not hold its shape), continue curing in 1-month increments and re-test.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Once cured to taste, fillet the entire batch: split each anchovy along the belly with a thumbnail, lift the spine and pull it out from head to tail in one motion (the bones come with it), separate the two fillets. Rinse each fillet briefly in dry red wine (the traditional Spanish-Cantabrian rinse) or in extra-virgin olive oil. Pack the fillets standing on edge in clean glass jars, layered tightly with no air pockets, submerged completely in 250 g extra-virgin olive oil. Add 1 to 2 dried bay leaves and 5 to 6 black peppercorns per jar for aromatic complexity (optional; not all traditions include this).'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'The Adjustment: if the cure tastes flat or under-salty after 4 months, the temperature was too low (cure stalled) or the salt-to-fish ratio was inadequate (less than 50 percent salt). The fix is to extend the cure another 2 to 3 months at the proper temperature. If the cure tastes harsh or one-note salty after the same period, the temperature was too high (cure overran) and the cure cannot be saved; treat the harshness with a brief pre-use rinse in olive oil. The oil-pack jar holds 6 to 12 months refrigerated and develops further flavor depth on the shelf.'

notes: |
  The protein hydrolysis chemistry. Salt-curing anchovies for months is a bacterially-quiet protein-hydrolysis ferment, not a microbial fermentation. The fish's own enzymes (particularly the cathepsins: cathepsin B, D, and L, which are intracellular proteases active in muscle tissue) slowly break down the myofibrillar proteins (myosin, actin, troponin) into oligopeptides and finally into free amino acids. The resulting free glutamate, plus inosine monophosphate (IMP) generated from the breakdown of nucleotides, is the source of the dramatic umami punch that cured anchovy fillets deliver in pasta, vinaigrettes, and Caesar dressing. The 50 percent salt-to-fish ratio is the canonical Mediterranean ratio because it is high enough to suppress most bacterial spoilage organisms (which need free water) while allowing the cathepsins to remain active in the residual saline matrix. Less salt and the fish spoils microbially before the proteases finish their work; more salt and the fish dehydrates so completely that even the cathepsins shut down, producing a hard, intensely salty product that never softens.

  The temperature sensitivity. Below 10 °C the cathepsins slow to a near-stall and the cure runs to 12 months or longer for an equivalent depth; above 18 °C the rate of microbial spoilage organisms (halophilic bacteria, certain yeasts) outruns the rate of enzymatic hydrolysis and the batch fails. The 12 to 16 °C window is where the cathepsins are active enough to drive the cure on a 4-to-12 month timeline and the spoilage organisms are still suppressed. A cool cellar (the traditional Mediterranean storage) sits naturally in this window year-round.

  The regional variants. Cantabrian Spain (anchoas del cantábrico) cures 6 to 9 months at 12 to 14 °C and rinses in sherry; Sicily (alici sotto sale) cures 6 to 12 months with bay aromatics; Liguria (acciughe sotto sale) cures 8 to 14 months at the longest end of the timeline; Provence (anchoïade fillets) cures 4 to 6 months at the shorter end and is rinsed in olive oil. The product differs subtly across regions but the underlying chemistry is identical.

  Troubleshooting. White surface mold throughout the cure is harmless penicillium-family growth (the same family as the molds on aged cheese rinds); skim and discard. Black mold, pink discoloration, or any sour or ammonia smell is spoilage — discard the entire batch. A dry-feeling top layer that does not feel submerged in brine indicates the cure has dehydrated unevenly; top off with saturated brine. A cured fillet that pulls apart in shreds rather than separating cleanly from the spine indicates over-cure (proteolysis went too far); the fillets are still edible but read mushy and are best chopped fine for vinaigrettes and Caesar dressing rather than displayed whole.

  Sourcing. Whole fresh anchovies are essentially impossible to source at standard US supermarkets. The supply chain runs through specialty fishmongers willing to special-order Mediterranean or Pacific anchovy on a multi-day notice; ask 2 to 3 weeks ahead. May/June and October/November are the typical fresh-anchovy seasons depending on coast. Frozen anchovies (flash-frozen at sea) are an acceptable substitute and are more available than fresh; the cure works identically. The supermarket "anchovy" in the cooler case is almost always already cured and is the wrong starting product.

  Use. The cured fillets go into anchoiade, bagna cauda, Caesar dressing, vitello tonnato, putanesca, and any Italian or Mediterranean preparation calling for "anchovy fillets." A 12 g serving (3 fillets) brings about 850 mg of free glutamate, comparable to 30 g of parmigiano reggiano on a per-portion basis. The fillet character is glutamate-rich, salt-forward, and aromatically complex; commercial canned anchovies are a faster industrial cure and are noticeably more uniform and less complex than the home-cured product.
