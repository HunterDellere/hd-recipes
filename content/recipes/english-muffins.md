source:
  name: 'Adapted from the British griddle-bread tradition, the same stovetop lineage that produces crumpets and muffins on a bakestone rather than in an oven.'
  note: 'The English muffin is defined by what it is not: it is not oven-baked. The dough cooks on a flat dry griddle between two faces, so it never develops the all-around crust of a baked roll. The open, ragged crumb comes from a deliberately wet dough (roughly 80 percent hydration) cooked slowly enough that the gas cells expand and tear the loose crumb open before the starch sets. Semolina or coarse cornmeal on the griddle is both a non-stick layer and the signature gritty dusting on the finished muffin.'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a two-rise recipe with a roughly 75 minute bulk ferment and a 35 to 45 minute proof in the rings. Start it 3 hours before you want to eat, or build the dough the night before: after mixing, refrigerate the covered bowl overnight and let it come to room temperature for an hour before the second rise. The cold overnight ferment deepens flavor and makes the slack dough easier to handle.'
  - 'You need 8 metal rings (English-muffin rings, or clean tuna cans with both ends removed, or 9 to 10 cm flan rings). Without rings the wet dough spreads into flat discs instead of tall muffins. Butter the inside walls of each ring before the dough goes in, or the muffin will weld to the metal.'
  - 'The dough is wetter than a kneadable bread dough, closer to a thick batter that barely holds shape. Do not be tempted to add flour to firm it up. The high hydration is what produces the open crumb; a stiffer dough bakes tight and bready.'
  - 'Griddle thermal control is the whole game. The skillet must be genuinely low, around 135 to 150 °C / 275 to 300 °F surface, so the 12 to 15 minutes of cook time sets the interior before the faces burn. A drop of water should sizzle gently and skitter, not crack and flash to steam. Test on a scrap before committing the batch.'

steps:
  - text: 'Mix the dry. In a large bowl, whisk the 360 g / 3 cups bread flour, 7 g / 1¼ tsp fine sea salt, 8 g / 2 tsp granulated sugar, and 5 g / 1½ tsp instant yeast until evenly combined. Keep the salt and the yeast on opposite sides of the bowl as you add them, then the whisk integrates them, direct prolonged contact between concentrated salt and yeast can blunt the yeast.'
    technique: 'enriched-dough'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Build the wet. Warm the 290 g / 1¼ cups whole milk to 38 °C / 100 °F (warm to the wrist, not hot). Stir in the 25 g / 1¾ tbsp melted-and-cooled butter and the 10 g / 1½ tsp honey until the honey dissolves. Pour the wet into the dry.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Mix to a slack dough, do not knead stiff. With a wooden spoon or a stand mixer fitted with the paddle, beat the dough hard for 3 to 4 minutes. It will be wet, sticky, and stretchy, closer to a thick batter than a ball, and it should pull off the spoon in long elastic sheets. This beating develops the gluten in place of kneading; the dough is too wet to knead by hand on a counter. Resist adding flour. The target is a dough that holds a slow ripple when you stop beating but will not hold a peak.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Bulk ferment. Scrape the dough into a lightly buttered bowl, cover with a damp towel or a fitted lid, and let it rise at warm room temperature (around 24 °C / 75 °F) until roughly doubled and visibly domed with bubbles breaking the surface, 60 to 90 minutes. For a deeper flavor and an easier-to-handle dough, refrigerate the covered bowl overnight instead, then let it sit at room temperature for an hour before the next step.'
    time_min: 75
  - text: 'Set up the griddle and rings. Butter the inside walls of 8 rings with the 10 g / 2 tsp softened butter. Dust a sheet of parchment heavily with about half the 40 g / ⅓ cup semolina, and set the buttered rings on it. Dust the inside floor of each ring (the parchment under it) with a little more semolina so the muffin bottoms pick up the grit. Place the rings on a cold dry skillet or griddle, do not preheat yet.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Portion into the rings. The dough is too slack to shape by hand. Either pipe it or spoon it: scoop roughly 95 g of dough per ring (a heaped ⅓ cup), filling each ring just under halfway. The dough will look like a thick batter pooling to the ring walls. Dust the tops with the remaining semolina. Cover loosely with a damp towel.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Proof in the rings. Let the filled rings proof at warm room temperature until the dough has risen to fill the rings nearly to the top and looks puffed and domed, 35 to 45 minutes. This second rise is what gives the muffin its height; the dough should jiggle like set custard when you nudge the skillet. Underproofed muffins bake dense and squat.'
    time_min: 40
  - text: 'Cook the first face, low and slow. Set the skillet over the lowest steady heat your burner holds, targeting a surface of 135 to 150 °C / 275 to 300 °F. This is the central technique: the heat must be low enough that the 6 to 8 minutes per side sets the interior crumb without scorching the face. Cook the first face undisturbed for 6 to 8 minutes, until the bottom is a deep even golden brown and the sides look set and matte rather than wet. Lift one with a thin spatula to check color before the flip; a face that browns in under 4 minutes means the pan is too hot, pull it off the heat for a minute to drop the temperature.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Flip and cook the second face. Slide a thin spatula under each muffin (the ring comes with it), invert onto the second face, and lift the ring off with tongs or a folded damp towel (it is hot). If a muffin sticks in its ring, run a small knife around the inside wall to release it. Cook the second face 6 to 8 minutes more at the same low heat, until equally golden and the muffin feels light and sounds hollow when tapped. Internal temperature at the center should read 96 to 98 °C / 205 to 208 °F; a thermometer is the only sure test that the wet interior has fully set.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Cool fully before splitting. Move the muffins to a wire rack and let them cool at least 30 minutes, ideally to room temperature. The crumb is still gelatinized and gummy when hot; it finishes setting as it cools. Splitting a hot muffin compresses the open crumb into a dense streak and you lose the nooks. To split, push the tines of a fork all the way around the equator of the muffin, working it in at the seam, then pull the two halves apart by hand. The fork tears along the natural cell walls and leaves the ragged cratered surface; a knife slices flat across the crumb and shears the nooks closed. Toast the torn faces; the craters crisp and catch melted butter.'
    time_min: 30

notes: |
  Why a wet dough and not a kneadable one. The open, cavernous crumb that defines an English muffin (the nooks and crannies) is a direct consequence of hydration. At roughly 80 percent hydration the dough is too loose to hold a tight gluten network, so the gas the yeast produces inflates large, irregular cells instead of the fine even crumb of a sandwich loaf. The slow griddle heat then sets the starch gradually, giving those large cells time to expand and tear the crumb open before the structure locks. A stiffer, hand-kneadable dough at 60 percent hydration bakes into a tight, bready muffin with no craters. If your muffins come out dense and closed, the most likely cause is too much flour added during mixing; weigh the flour and trust the slack dough.

  Why the griddle, not the oven. An oven surrounds the dough with hot air and forms a crust on all sides, which is a dinner roll, not an English muffin. The griddle cooks only the two flat faces by conduction, leaving the sides soft and the interior set but pale. That two-faces-only geometry is the entire reason the muffin splits cleanly into a top and bottom with an open, toastable interior. The flat dry pan also means no added fat in the cook; the semolina dusting is the only thing between dough and metal.

  Thermal control is the failure point. The single most common mistake is too high a heat. A pan at frying temperature browns the faces in 2 to 3 minutes while the center is still raw, gummy batter, and you cannot fix it by flipping more because the interior never reaches the 96 to 98 °C it needs to gelatinize. The fix is patience and a low burner: 135 to 150 °C surface, 6 to 8 minutes a side, and a thermometer in the center of the first muffin off the pan to confirm. If you have an electric griddle, set it to 300 °F and leave it; the steady temperature is more forgiving than a gas flame.

  The fork-split rule is not folklore. Slicing a muffin with a knife shears flat across the crumb and presses the open cell walls closed, giving you a smooth surface that toasts evenly and dully. Tearing it apart along a fork-pierced seam follows the natural lines of the open crumb, leaving the ragged cratered faces standing proud. Those craters are what crisp under the toaster and pool melted butter; the technique only pays off on the torn surface.

  Make-ahead and storage. Cooked muffins keep 2 days at room temperature in a paper bag inside a fitted-lid container, or freeze split (so they go straight into the toaster) for up to 2 months. The dough itself holds an overnight cold ferment beautifully; building it the night before is the path of least resistance for fresh muffins at breakfast, since the morning work is only the second rise and the griddle.
