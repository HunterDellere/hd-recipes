Flesh out: the 1:3 ratio reasoning (the classical French ratio that produces a salad reading bright but not sharp; 1:2 for sharper, 1:4 for milder), the role of dijon mucilage as a Pickering emulsifier (the mustard seed coat polysaccharides that surround oil droplets), the salt-dissolution-first sequence (so it does not pocket as gritty hot spots), the slow-stream oil technique (smaller droplets stay suspended longer, so the cook is shearing the oil into a fine colloid as it enters), the variants (sherry vinegar, white wine vinegar, lemon-juice substitution at 1:3), the additions (chopped shallot, anchovy, honey, herbs), and the storage failure mode (the emulsion breaks within hours, the dressing rebuilds with a fresh teaspoon of mustard whisked into 1 tbsp warm water if needed).

  The recipe is the foundation for Tuscan panzanella, Provencal Niçoise, French green salad, and almost any composed Mediterranean salad. Cooks should build a small batch fresh per meal rather than holding a jar in the fridge; the difference between a 5-minute-old vinaigrette and a 2-day-old jar is dramatic.
