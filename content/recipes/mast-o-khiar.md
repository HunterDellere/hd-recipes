steps:
  - text: 'Grate the 400 g cucumbers on the large holes of a box grater into a fine-mesh sieve set over a bowl. Toss with the 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt. Let drain 15 to 20 minutes; the salt pulls the cucumber water out via osmosis. After draining, press the cucumber gently against the sieve with the back of a spoon to release additional water. Discard the cucumber water (or save for cocktails). The drained cucumber should look fluffy and weigh roughly 250 g.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'In a serving bowl, whisk the 500 g / 2 cups Greek yogurt until smooth. Add the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt and 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper; whisk again. The yogurt should be thick enough to hold a spoon-mound briefly.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Fold in the drained cucumber, the 8 g / ¼ cup chopped mint, and the 6 g / 2 tbsp chopped dill. Stir gently with a wooden spoon; do not overmix or the dip turns runny from the cucumber bruising.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Fold in the 40 g / ⅓ cup toasted crushed walnuts, the 30 g / ¼ cup drained raisins, and most of the 2 g / 2 tsp crushed rose petals (reserve a pinch for garnish). Taste: the dip should read cool, herbal, faintly sweet from the raisins, faintly floral from the rose, with a satisfying crunch from the walnut. Adjust salt in 1 g increments.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Cover and chill at least 1 hour (up to 24). The herbs hydrate, the rose blooms, and the walnut softens slightly. The dip thickens further as the yogurt absorbs the residual cucumber moisture.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'To serve, transfer to a wide shallow bowl. Drizzle the 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil across the top in a slow stream. Scatter the reserved rose petals. Serve cold alongside hot rice and kebabs, as a starter with grilled flatbread (lavash or pita), or as part of a sabzi khordan (Persian herb-and-relish plate).'
    time_min: 1

    how: 'Skip the walnuts, raisins, and rose petals. Use only yogurt, drained cucumber, mint, salt, and pepper (the dill is optional in the simplest version). The dish reads cleaner and is the everyday Persian-table standard; the elaborated version here is the party variant. Either is correct.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'as a soup (abdoogh khiar)'
    to: 'with cold water and ice to thin into a chilled summer soup'
    how: 'Thin the finished mast-o-khiar with 300 g cold water (or cold sparkling water for a livelier version) and 100 g ice; whisk to combine. Add 100 g additional yogurt to compensate for the dilution. Float small cubes of crustless day-old bread (60 g) on top and serve in chilled bowls. The result is abdoogh khiar, the canonical Persian summer cold soup; structurally the same flavor profile, served as a meal.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'as borani esfenaj (spinach-yogurt)'
    to: 'with wilted spinach and caramelized garlic instead of cucumber'
    how: 'Skip the cucumber. Wilt 300 g spinach in a dry skillet with 15 g olive oil and 12 g minced garlic (cooked golden, not burnt) until just collapsed; cool, squeeze out excess water, chop. Fold into the yogurt with the herbs. The result is borani esfenaj, a Persian sister dip, served the same way.'
    kind: 'regional'

notes: |
  The cucumber-drain is structural. Cucumbers are roughly 96% water. Folded raw into yogurt without salting and draining, they release water continuously over the rest period, thinning the dip to a sauce by the time it hits the table. The 15 to 20 minute salt drain pulls roughly 30 to 40% of the cucumber water out before the cucumber meets the yogurt; the remaining moisture is bound enough not to leach significantly. This same osmotic drain technique applies to Greek tzatziki and Turkish cacık and is the universal rule for any yogurt-cucumber dish.

  Why dill, why rose, why raisin. The three elaborations that distinguish mast-o-khiar from tzatziki or cacık are not arbitrary. Dill is the structural Persian fresh herb (used in baghali polo, in mahi sefid, in countless preparations); mint and dill together read distinctly Persian rather than Mediterranean. Rose petals carry the Persian floral signature (rosewater appears across the cuisine in both sweet and savory contexts). Raisins introduce the sweet-savory pull that is one of the deep flavor moves of Persian cooking (raisins also appear in rice pilafs, in stuffings, in some khoresh). All three are optional in the strictest sense but together they make the dish recognizably Persian.

  Versatility. Mast-o-khiar serves as: the canonical side to grilled kebabs (joojeh kabab, koobideh, shishlik), a cool counterpoint to spiced rice dishes (especially heavy or saffron-rich rice), a dip with crackers, lavash, or pita as a starter, a topping for grain bowls, a sauce for grilled vegetables, and the base for abdoogh khiar (thinned into a chilled soup).

  Pairs across Persian (canonical), broader Iranian diaspora, Afghan (where similar yogurt-cucumber dips exist), and modern Mediterranean tables where the dish is increasingly visible as the distinctive Persian counterpoint to tzatziki and cacık.

  Storage. Keeps 3 to 4 days refrigerated. The cucumber may release additional water over time; pour off excess water and stir before serving on day 2 or 3. The flavor is best on day 1 (after the 1-hour chill) and day 2; by day 4 the herbs have wilted and the rose has lost its bloom.
