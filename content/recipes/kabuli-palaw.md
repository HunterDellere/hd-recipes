before_you_start:
  - 'This is a multi-track dish with a long braise. Read it through once first. The lamb gharma (braise) runs 60 to 90 minutes and produces the broth the rice is steamed over, so start it first and build the other components (carrots, caramel, nuts) while it simmers.'
  - 'Soak the basmati 30 to 60 minutes before you plan to parboil it. Soaked grains elongate and stay separate; unsoaked rice cooks shorter and stubbier. Start the soak when the braise goes on.'
  - 'The rice is cooked twice: parboiled in heavily salted water until al dente and drained, then steamed (dum) over the flavored broth to finish. Do not try to cook it once by absorption; the two-stage method is what makes palaw grains long and distinct rather than sticky.'
  - 'The amber-brown color of authentic Kabuli palaw comes from burnt sugar (caramel taken dark), not from turmeric or saffron. Have a little water ready to stop the caramel; taken too far it turns acrid, and the window between deep-brown and burnt is short.'

source:
  name: 'Afghan (Kabuli) tradition'
  note: 'Qabili palaw or Kabuli palaw (قابلی پلو) is the national dish of Afghanistan, served at weddings, holidays, and guest meals. The name traces to Kabul and to qabili (meaning capable or worthy, a dish worthy of honored guests). The defining marks are the burnt-sugar-colored long-grain rice steamed over a lamb broth (gharma), and the sweet topping of caramelized carrots (zardak) and raisins (kishmish) that plays against the savory meat. Bone-in lamb or goat is traditional; the bones enrich the gharma the rice is built on.'

steps:
  - text: 'Start the soak and the braise. Rinse and soak the 600 g basmati (30 to 60 minutes, then drain). Meanwhile, pat the 1000 g lamb dry. Heat the 75 g / 5 tbsp oil in a heavy pot over medium-high and brown the lamb hard on all sides, 8 to 10 minutes, working in batches so it sears rather than steams. Remove the lamb.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Build the gharma. Lower the heat to medium and add the 350 g chopped onion to the same pot. Cook 12 to 15 minutes, stirring, until deep golden-brown; this browning is the flavor and color foundation of the broth. Add the 15 g minced garlic, the 5 cracked cardamom pods, 5 cloves, 5 g cinnamon stick, and 4 g / 1½ tsp cumin seeds; bloom 45 seconds until fragrant. Return the lamb, add the 12 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt and the 1000 ml / 4 cups water, bring to a simmer, cover, and braise on low 60 to 90 minutes until the lamb is fork-tender.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 80
  - text: 'Make the burnt-sugar color. In a small dry saucepan over medium heat, melt the 24 g / 2 tbsp sugar without stirring until it turns a deep mahogany brown and just begins to smoke, 3 to 5 minutes; the goal is dark and slightly bitter, one shade before burnt. Stand back and add the 60 ml / ¼ cup hot water (it will sputter violently), swirl to dissolve into a dark syrup, and pull off the heat. This caramel is what gives palaw its amber-brown grains.'
    technique: 'dry-caramel'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Make the char masala. Combine the 3 g / 1½ tsp ground cumin, 2 g / 1 tsp ground cardamom, 1.5 g / ¾ tsp ground cinnamon, and 0.5 g / ¼ tsp ground cloves in a small bowl. This is the finishing spice blend that perfumes the rice during the steam.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Candy the carrots and raisins. Heat the 20 g / 1½ tbsp oil in a skillet over medium. Add the 300 g julienned carrots and the 20 g / 1½ tbsp sugar; cook 5 to 7 minutes, tossing, until the carrots are glossy, tender-crisp, and lightly caramelized at the edges. Add the 90 g raisins and cook 1 minute more until they plump and swell. Scrape onto a plate. In the same pan, toast the 30 g slivered almonds and 30 g pistachios 1 to 2 minutes until fragrant; reserve with the carrots.'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Strain the gharma. Lift the tender lamb pieces out and set aside. Strain the broth, discarding the whole spices; you want about 500 ml of gharma broth for the rice. Reserve it. Skim excess fat if there is a heavy cap, leaving a little for richness.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Parboil the rice. Bring the 3000 ml / 12 cups water to a rolling boil with the 30 g / 3 tbsp kosher salt (it should taste aggressively salty). Add the drained soaked rice and boil 5 to 7 minutes until the grains are elongated and bendy but still firm at the very center (bite one: al dente, not soft). Drain immediately in a colander.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Layer for the dum. Wipe the braise pot and add a thin film of the reserved gharma. Spread the parboiled rice into the pot in a mound. Pour 300 ml of the reserved gharma broth and all of the dark caramel syrup evenly over the rice, then scatter the char masala over the top. Fold gently once or twice to streak the color through (do not overmix; uneven amber streaking is correct and beautiful). Bury the reserved lamb pieces in the center of the rice. Fold in half the candied carrots and raisins, reserving the rest for the top.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Steam (dum). With the handle of a spoon, poke 4 or 5 holes down through the rice to the bottom to vent steam. Cover the pot with a tight lid (wrap the lid in a clean towel to trap steam), and cook on the lowest heat 25 to 35 minutes, until the rice is fully tender and fluffy and a golden crust has formed at the bottom. Do not lift the lid before 25 minutes; the trapped steam is doing the cooking.'
    technique: 'vapor-steaming'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Serve. Gently fluff and mound the rice on a large platter with a fork, lifting from the bottom so the amber and pale grains stream together and the bottom crust comes up in pieces. Nestle the lamb through the rice or set it on top. Crown with the reserved candied carrots and raisins (about 175 g, the half you set aside), the 30 g slivered almonds, and the 30 g pistachios. Serve hot with a cilantro-chili chutney and a chopped salad of tomato, cucumber, and onion alongside for the fresh acid the rich platter wants.'
    time_min: 3

    how: 'Use 1 kg bone-in beef short rib or chuck. Braise 2 to 2.5 hours until fork-tender, as beef takes longer than lamb to break down. The gharma is deeper and beefier; add an extra cup of water to the braise to account for the longer cook.'
    kind: 'protein'

notes: |
  Why the color is burnt sugar, not turmeric. The amber-brown of authentic Kabuli palaw is caramel color, made by taking sugar to a dark, faintly bitter stage and dissolving it into syrup. Turmeric gives a flat yellow and an earthy flavor that is wrong for this dish; saffron gives gold but not the deep brown, and would be a Persian move, not an Afghan one. The caramel does double duty: color plus a whisper of bitter-sweet depth that grounds the sweetness of the carrots and raisins. Take it one shade past golden, to mahogany just as it smokes, then stop it with water; too pale and the rice stays blond, too far and it turns acrid.

  Why cook the rice twice. Parboiling in abundant salty water lets each grain hydrate and elongate freely while washing off surface starch, then draining stops it at al dente. The second stage, the covered dum steam over the flavored broth, finishes the grains gently in trapped steam so they stay long and separate and build the prized bottom crust. Trying to cook palaw in one absorption step (all the liquid in at once, lid on) gives sticky, clumped rice, the single most common way Kabuli palaw drifts wrong in a Western kitchen.

  Umami and acid, and the savory-sweet balance. The gharma broth, built on hard-browned lamb bones and deeply caramelized onion, is the glutamate-rich umami spine that the rice absorbs during the steam, this is why steaming over the broth (not over water) matters. The dish itself is savory-sweet by design and carries no acid, so the traditional accompaniments supply it: an Afghan cilantro-and-green-chili chutney sharpened with vinegar or lemon, and a chopped tomato-cucumber-onion salad. Serve at least one of them; the fresh acid is what keeps a rich platter from cloying.

  The carrot-and-raisin topping is not a garnish. The candied carrots (zardak) and plumped raisins (kishmish) are a defining, structural element, the sweet counterweight that makes the savory lamb and caramel-scented rice sing. Fold half through the rice so the sweetness threads the dish and reserve half for the top so the platter reads as celebration food. Skipping them leaves you with a competent pilaf but not Kabuli palaw.

  Pairs across Afghan and broader Central Asian tradition. Serve as a one-platter centerpiece with cilantro chutney (chutney-e gashniz), a shepherd salad, and Afghan naan on the side. Leftovers reheat well, covered, with a splash of water to re-steam; the flavors deepen overnight. The braised lamb and gharma can be made a day ahead and refrigerated, which also makes skimming the fat easier and spreads the labor across two days.
