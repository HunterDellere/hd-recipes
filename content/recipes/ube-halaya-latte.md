before_you_start:
  - 'Chill the cream bowl in the freezer for 10 minutes BEFORE you begin anything else. Cold heavy cream whipped in a cold bowl reaches soft peaks in under 60 seconds; warm bowl produces broken or under-whipped cream that cannot fold halaya in cleanly.'
  - 'The ube halaya whip, the coconut-sweetened milk, and the espresso shot all need to land on the bar at the same moment. Build the whip first (it holds in the fridge for the 5 minute shot-and-milk window), heat the milk second, pull the shot last.'
  - 'Toast the desiccated coconut at the very start and tip it onto a cold plate. Pan carryover takes toasted coconut from gold to bitter in under 60 seconds, and the toasted coconut is the finishing aroma at the cream-meets-coffee boundary; do not toast at the end with a hot pan and hurried plating.'

steps:
  - text: 'Toast 3 g / 1½ tsp desiccated coconut in a small dry stainless skillet over medium-low heat, shaking constantly, until the shreds turn uniform pale golden and smell intensely roasted-nutty, about 90 seconds. Tip immediately onto a cold plate; the pan''s carry-over heat will take it from gold to bitter in under 60 seconds.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Combine 60 g / ¼ cup very cold heavy cream and a 0.2 g pinch fine sea salt in a small bowl that has been chilled in the freezer for 10 minutes. Whisk 45–60 seconds to soft peaks: the cream should hold a peak that slumps over at the tip, not stand up straight. Stiff cream loses the ability to fold halaya in without breaking.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'In a separate small bowl, stir 22 g / 1½ tbsp ube halaya for 10 seconds with a spoon to loosen (the jam thickens cold and resists folding straight from the jar). Add 2 tablespoons of the soft-whipped cream into the halaya and stir to lighten; this brings the densities closer so the next fold doesn''t deflate the rest of the cream. Then scrape the lightened halaya back into the cream bowl and fold gently with a rubber spatula in 5 to 7 strokes, stopping at streaks rather than full incorporation. The cream should be lavender with darker violet veins. Refrigerate uncovered while you pull the shot.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Combine 180 g / ¾ cup whole milk, 25 g / scant 2 tbsp coconut cream, 12 g / 1 tbsp coconut sugar, and a 0.3 g pinch fine sea salt in a small saucepan. Heat over medium-low, whisking constantly, until the sugar fully dissolves and the milk reads 60 to 65 °C / 140 to 150 °F on an instant-read thermometer; steam visibly rises but no bubbles break the surface. Do not boil: above 75 °C the whey proteins denature and form a skin, and the casein begins to scorch on the pan bottom. Pull off the heat.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Grind 18 g / 1 modest tablespoon whole espresso beans to a fine espresso grind (just-finer than table salt, slightly tacky between fingertips). Dose the basket, distribute, and tamp level with ~13 kg / 30 lb force. Pull the shot into a pre-warmed 60 ml / 2 oz espresso glass, targeting 36 g / about 1.2 oz of yield in 25 to 30 seconds at 9 bar pressure. The pour should start at second 6 to 8 as honey-thick rope, lighten to hazelnut, and finish before it goes pale and watery. Crema should sit ~3 mm thick.'
    time_min: 1
    technique: 'espresso-extraction'
  - text: 'Pour the warm coconut-sweetened milk into a 300 ml / 10 oz mug, leaving 3 cm of headroom. Slip the spoon under the surface of the milk and pour the 36 g espresso shot through it in one steady stream. The casein in the milk binds the espresso''s bitter polyphenols on contact, which is what you taste as "balanced" rather than "bitter coffee with sweetener over top."'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Scoop the lavender ube halaya cream onto the surface in a single ~80 g mound; it will sit, then slowly settle into a dome. Shower 3 g / 1½ tsp of the toasted desiccated coconut over the top in an arc, concentrated at the cream-meets-coffee boundary so the toasted-coconut Maillard notes hit at the first sip. Serve immediately; the cream is at its best in the first 90 seconds before it fully melts into the drink.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why coconut sugar specifically: the butterscotch-floral aromatic profile of coconut sugar (driven by Maillard-derived furanones from the slow palm-sap reduction) bridges the espresso''s roasted-bitter notes and the coconut fat''s lactone-rich aroma in a way that refined sugar cannot. White sugar would leave the espresso and the coconut tasting like two unrelated ingredients in the same cup; coconut sugar smears them into one continuous flavor.

  Why fold halaya into already-whipped cream rather than whip them together: the halaya is too dense and too sweet to whip directly. Adding it to cream before whipping inhibits the fat-globule rupture that builds the foam; the cream goes from heavy liquid straight to broken without ever passing through the airy intermediate. Whipping the cream first, then folding the loosened halaya in, preserves the foam while distributing the violet pigment.

  Why the cream is folded to streaks, not full incorporation: the visible violet veins are doing aesthetic work, yes, but they are also serving a functional purpose. A fully homogenized cream tastes like one note; a streaked cream gives the drinker a bite of pure halaya next to a bite of plain cream, which keeps the palate from saturating on sweetness. The same principle applies to a Vietnamese cà phê sữa đá where the layered condensed milk is meant to be stirred in over the course of the drink, not pre-mixed.

  Why the toasted coconut concentrates at the cream-coffee boundary, not scattered evenly: Maillard products on the toasted coconut are aromatic and the volatiles flash off above ~50 °C. Sitting on cool cream they last; sitting on the hot espresso surface they would be gone in 30 seconds. The arc concentrates the toasted notes where the cream insulates them.

  Steam-pitcher version: if you have an espresso machine with a steam wand, skip the saucepan. Combine all four Phase 1 ingredients in a cold steam pitcher, purge the wand, and steam to 60 to 65 °C while introducing a brief stretch of microfoam in the first 5 seconds (just enough to lift the surface ~1 cm; you do not want dry foam here, the ube halaya cream is the textural top). Pour over the espresso instead of pouring espresso through milk; the latte-art window is open for this drink and the contrast of white milk against the brown-violet base reads beautifully.
