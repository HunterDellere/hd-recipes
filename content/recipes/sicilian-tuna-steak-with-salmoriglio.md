before_you_start:
  - 'The salmoriglio is best whisked just before serving and used warm, not made far ahead. It is a quick emulsion that holds for a few minutes off heat but slackens as it sits, so build it while the tuna rests, not before you start cooking.'
  - 'The fish is cooked through, so any fresh tuna steak from a regular counter works, no sashimi grade. Sear it to an internal 55 to 58 °C (just-opaque center) and pull it; it climbs to a moist medium as it rests under the warm sauce. Past 65 °C tuna dries to chalk, and the window is narrow, so use a thermometer the first time.'

steps:
  - text: 'Fry the capers. Heat the 14 g / 1 tbsp olive oil in a small skillet over medium-high. Add the 20 g / 2 tbsp rinsed, very dry capers and fry 1 to 2 minutes, swirling, until they burst open and crisp. Scoop onto paper towel; they crisp further as they cool. Keep the caper-flavored oil for the sauce if you like. Make sure the capers are dry before they hit the oil or they will spit violently.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Salt and sear the tuna. Pat the two 340 g / 12 oz tuna steaks bone-dry and season both faces with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt. Heat the 14 g / 1 tbsp olive oil in a wide cast-iron or stainless skillet over medium-high until just smoking. Sear the steaks undisturbed about 2 minutes per face for a 2.5 cm steak, until well-browned outside and the center reads 55 to 58 °C on an instant-read thermometer. Pull them to a board to rest; they finish to a moist medium off the heat.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Whisk the salmoriglio warm. While the tuna rests, combine the 40 g / 1 lemon of juice with the 20 g / 4 tsp warm water, the 6 g / 2 cloves grated garlic, the 2 g / 2 tsp crumbled dried oregano, and the 2 g / ½ tsp salt in a small bowl set over the warm (off-heat) pan or a bowl of warm water. Whisk constantly while drizzling in the 60 g / ¼ cup olive oil in a thin stream, until it thickens to a loose, cloudy emulsion that coats a spoon. Whisk in the lemon zest and the 6 g / 1½ tbsp chopped parsley last. It should taste sharp, garlicky, and herbal; salmoriglio is meant to be assertive.'
    technique: 'vinaigrette-emulsion'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Dress and serve. Set each tuna steak on a warm plate and spoon the warm salmoriglio generously over and around it, letting it pool. Scatter the 20 g / 2 tbsp fried capers over the top. Serve at once while the sauce is still warm and emulsified and the capers are crisp; the warm oil-and-lemon dressing on the just-cooked fish is the entire point, so do not let it sit and slacken.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  What salmoriglio is, mechanically. Salmoriglio is an emulsion, not a vinaigrette poured over fish: lemon juice and olive oil whisked together with a splash of warm water until they thicken into a loose, cloudy, clinging sauce. The warm water is the trick most recipes leave out; it lowers the oil's viscosity and gives the emulsion something to hold onto, so the sauce drapes the fish instead of separating into oil-slick and lemon-puddle. Whisk it warm (over the residual heat of the pan or a bowl of warm water) and it comes together easily; whisk cold oil and cold lemon and it fights you.

  Why this is the cooked-tuna recipe. In Sicily, tonno is overwhelmingly cooked, seared firm or grilled, then dressed, and only rarely served raw outside modern crudo cooking. That makes salmoriglio the natural sauce for the fish you buy at a regular counter and cook all the way through. The lean cooked tuna brings firmness and a clean flavor; the salmoriglio brings the fat, acid, garlic, and herb that the fish on its own lacks. Take the steak to a just-opaque medium, no further, and let the warm sauce do the rest.

  Raw garlic, dried oregano, on purpose. Both are deliberately raw and assertive here. The garlic is microplaned in raw so it reads sharp and pungent against the rich oil; cooking it would round it into something milder and less Sicilian. The oregano is dried rather than fresh because Sicilian oregano dried on the branch is more concentrated and resinous than the fresh herb, which is the flavor the sauce is built around. Crumble it between your fingers as it goes in to wake up its oils.

  Fried capers earn their place. Beyond seasoning, the fried capers are texture: they burst open into crisp little salt-bombs that punctuate the soft fish and the slick sauce. Fry them genuinely crisp and dry them well, a soggy caper just adds brine. They are optional but they turn a good plate into a complete one, and they take ninety seconds.
