steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 180 °C / 350 °F. While it preheats, warm the 60 g / ¼ cup extra-virgin olive oil with the 12 g / 3 smashed garlic cloves in a small saucepan over the lowest possible heat for 8 minutes. The oil should never come to a sizzle; keep it sub-Maillard so the garlic infuses without browning. A scorched garlic clove turns the oil bitter and contaminates the entire batch.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Pull the oil off heat at 8 minutes. Fish out the garlic cloves and discard them; their job was the infusion, not the bake; cloves left in the oil at 180 °C for 14 minutes turn black and bitter. The strained oil should smell deeply garlicky and read pale gold.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'In a wide bowl, toss the 250 g / 9 oz torn sourdough chunks with the strained garlic oil, the 2 g / ⅓ tsp fine sea salt, and (if using) the 1 g cracked black pepper. Toss for a full 60 seconds; you want every surface of every chunk wet with oil. Day-old bread is dry enough that the oil absorbs into the crumb rather than pooling on the surface; if your bread is fresher than day-old, the oil will sit on the outside and the croutons will fry unevenly.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Spread the chunks on a sheet tray in a single layer with no chunks touching. Bake for 12 to 14 minutes, tossing once at 7 minutes, until the chunks are uniformly golden and dry through to the centre when you bite into one. The exterior should shatter and the interior should be dry but slightly tender, not rock-hard like a melba toast. Pull and cool on the tray; they crisp further as they lose residual steam during the first 3 minutes off heat.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Use immediately for best texture or hold in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 5 days. Recrisp in a 150 °C / 300 °F oven for 4 minutes if they soften; do not microwave (steams them into a leathery state).'

notes: |
  Why day-old bread. Fresh sourdough has 35 to 38 percent moisture in the crumb; day-old has dropped to 22 to 25 percent. The difference shows up in the bake: fresh bread releases steam during oven time and the chunks come out leathery in the centre with hard exterior; day-old bread releases minimal steam and crisps uniformly. Bakers who plan ahead set aside the heel of yesterday's loaf for crouton duty rather than fighting fresh bread's water content.

  Why garlic-oil infusion at sub-Maillard temperature. Garlic added directly to the bread before the bake at 180 °C burns within 6 minutes (the surface temperature exceeds the 150 °C / 300 °F threshold where allicin and the related sulfur compounds start to oxidize past pleasantness). Pre-infusing the oil at 80 to 90 °C for 8 minutes extracts the aromatics into the fat without burning, then the garlic is removed before the bake. The flavor lands on the croutons via the oil; the garlic itself never enters the oven.

  Why oven, not pan. A pan fry produces unevenly crisped croutons (the side touching the pan crisps fast, the exposed sides take longer, the cook fights for uniformity). The oven produces uniform results because all surfaces of the chunk meet hot dry air at the same temperature. Pan-frying is right when the cook wants the deeper flavor of butter or fat-rendered croutons (see modifications); the oven is right for everyday salad croutons.

  Salt timing. Salt with the oil during the toss, before the bake — this gives the salt time to dissolve into the surface oil and adhere uniformly. Salting after the bake produces croutons with visible salt crystals in some places and bland in others.
