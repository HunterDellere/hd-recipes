source:
  name: 'German home and Sunday-dinner canon'
  note: 'Rouladen (singular: Roulade) is the structural German Sunday-dinner beef dish: thin slices of beef top round wrapped around a salty-tangy filling of bacon, dill pickle, and onion, secured with kitchen twine, browned hard, and braised in red wine to fork-tender. The defining elements are: thin, lean, tough beef cuts (top round is canonical, flank steak is the alternative) that benefit from the long braise; the structural salt-fat-acid filling triad (bacon, mustard, pickle); the red wine and beef stock braise that produces the glossy red-brown gravy; and the long braise time that transforms the lean beef from chewy to spoon-tender. Served canonically with red cabbage (Rotkohl), potato dumplings (Knödel), or boiled potatoes.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The beef slices need to be very thin and very large (12 cm wide, 20 cm long, 5 mm thick maximum). If buying pre-sliced, look for "rouladen meat" at German butchers; otherwise have your butcher slice top round across the grain into 5 mm scaloppini. The pounding step in the recipe brings the slices to the right thinness if your butcher cuts are a bit thicker.'
  - 'The whole-cook is 2 hours: 30 min prep, 15 min sear, 90 min braise. Plan a Sunday-afternoon cook.'

steps:
  - text: 'Pound the beef. Lay each 600 g portion of beef between two sheets of parchment. Pound gently with the smooth side of a meat mallet (not the spiked side) until each slice is uniformly 5 mm thick and about 12 × 20 cm. Season both sides with the 6 g / 1½ tsp salt and 3 g / 1½ tsp pepper.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Assemble. Lay each beef slice flat on the cutting board. Spread 15 g / 1 tbsp Dijon mustard evenly over the surface. Lay 1 bacon slice across the short dimension. Lay 4 pickle sticks parallel to the bacon. Sprinkle about 35 g of diced onion across the surface. Starting from one short end, roll the beef tightly around the filling like a jelly roll, tucking in the sides as you go to seal the filling inside.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Secure each roulade with kitchen twine: tie one loop around the middle, then one loop near each end. Or use 3 toothpicks to pin the seam closed (remove before serving). The roulade should hold its shape firmly without leaking filling.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Heat the 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Brown the 4 rouladen in 2 batches if needed, 2 minutes per side (4 sides per roulade, rotating to brown evenly; about 8 minutes per batch total). The exteriors should be deep mahogany and the kitchen should smell of seared beef.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 16
  - text: 'Transfer the seared rouladen to a plate. Reduce heat to medium. Add the 150 g diced onion, 100 g diced carrot, and 80 g diced celery to the same pot; sauté 5 minutes in the beef-fond fat, scraping the fond off the bottom.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp tomato paste; cook 2 minutes until darkened. Add the 15 g / 2 tbsp flour; stir 60 seconds.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pour in the 300 ml / 1¼ cups red wine; scrape the fond. Cook 90 seconds to reduce slightly and burn off alcohol. Add the 500 ml / 2 cups beef stock, 2 bay leaves, and 2 g whole peppercorns. Bring to a hard simmer.'
    technique: 'deglazing'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Return the seared rouladen to the pot, seam-side down, with any accumulated juices. The liquid should come about two-thirds up the rouladen (not fully submerged). Cover with the lid slightly ajar. Transfer to a 160 °C / 325 °F oven (or maintain a steady stovetop simmer). Braise 90 minutes; check at the 60-minute mark, the rouladen should be just yielding to a fork.'
    technique: 'braising'
    time_min: 90
  - text: 'Lift the rouladen out of the pot with tongs to a warm plate; cover loosely with foil. Strain the braising liquid through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean saucepan, pressing the solids to extract liquid; discard the spent vegetables and bay leaves. Reduce the strained gravy over medium-high heat for 5 to 8 minutes until it has thickened to a glossy consistency that coats the back of a spoon. Taste; adjust salt.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Remove the twine (or toothpicks) from the rouladen. Place one roulade on each plate; spoon the reduced gravy generously over the top. Sprinkle with the 10 g parsley. Serve with red cabbage (Rotkohl) and either potato dumplings (Knödel), boiled potatoes, or spaetzle.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  Why thin lean beef. The structural German cut for rouladen is thin-pounded top round, a lean tough cut that becomes spoon-tender after 90 minutes of braise. The thinness (5 mm) lets the roll wrap tightly around the filling; the leanness ensures the meat absorbs the braising liquid rather than rendering its own fat into the gravy. A fattier or thicker cut (chuck, ribeye) does not work in the same way; the dish becomes structurally different.

  Why mustard, bacon, pickle, onion. The classic German filling is the structural German salt-fat-acid triad: bacon (fat and smoke), pickle (acid and salt), mustard (sharp and tangy), onion (sweet and aromatic). These four together inside the beef braise produce the characteristic German rouladen flavor that distinguishes it from a generic stuffed beef roll. Substituting any one weakens the structural signature.

  Why braise 90 minutes. Beef top round is a tough cut (high collagen, low intramuscular fat); the 90-minute braise at 160 °C / 325 °F is the structural conversion phase where collagen melts to gelatin and the meat goes from chewy to spoon-tender. Shortening the braise to 45 or 60 minutes produces tough, chewy rouladen; the cook time is the cook time.

  Pairings. Canonical with red cabbage (Rotkohl, slow-braised with apple, onion, vinegar, sugar, cloves), potato dumplings (Knödel), boiled potatoes, or spaetzle. A glass of German Spätburgunder (German pinot noir) on the side.

  Storage. Improves dramatically overnight; the flavors integrate and the gravy thickens. Keeps 4 days refrigerated. Reheat gently in the gravy in a covered pan over low heat or in a 160 °C / 325 °F oven for 25 minutes. Freezes well; portion each roulade in its own gravy.
