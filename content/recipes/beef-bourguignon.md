title: 'Beef bourguignon'
desc: 'The Burgundian red-wine beef braise: chuck cubes browned hard, lardon-rendered fat as the structural base, deglazed with a full bottle of dry red Burgundy (or pinot noir), aromatic bouquet garni, slow oven braise 3 hours until the collagen converts to gelatin, finished with sautéed pearl onions and pan-glazed cremini mushrooms tucked in just before service.   note: 'The dish is a peasant Burgundian preparation elevated by Escoffier into the haute cuisine canon; Julia Child''s 1961 Boeuf Bourguignon recipe is the most influential English-language reference and the structural template that most American home cooks follow. The defining elements: chuck (the high-collagen cut that converts to gelatin during the long braise), lardons (cubed slab bacon or pancetta rendered as the structural fat and aromatic base), red Burgundy or pinot noir (the canonical wine; the dish reads like the wine, so a thin or imbalanced wine produces a thin or imbalanced stew), bouquet garni (thyme, bay, parsley stems tied with kitchen twine), pearl onions sautéed separately and glazed (added at the end, not braised through), cremini or button mushrooms sautéed separately (added at the end, not braised through), and the resting-overnight integration (the dish reads dramatically better on day two as the gelatin and wine reduce and integrate).'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 3 hour active project plus a 3 hour oven braise, and is structurally better when made the day before and rested overnight in the refrigerator. The braise extracts collagen and the cooled-and-reheated dish has more cohesive sauce body. Plan for day 1 = cook to the end of the braise + cool overnight; day 2 = warm the braise to a tremor, finish with the onions and mushrooms, serve. Same-day service is acceptable but reads less integrated.'
  - 'Open the bottle of red Burgundy or pinot noir 1 hour before the cook begins; the wine benefits from aeration and the dish reads better when the wine is not freshly-poured-from-cork-tight bottle. Drink a glass while cooking; that is also Burgundian tradition.'
  - 'Brown the beef in small batches (no more than a single uncrowded layer). Crowding the pan turns the hard sear into a steam; the Maillard crust is the structural source of the deep brown sauce color and savory depth. Plan for 4 to 5 separate batches over 25 to 30 minutes; this is the slow patient phase of the dish.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'Beef chuck braised to fully fork-tender (internal 90°C / 195°F) rather than a medium-rare doneness'
    ref: 'safety/meat-doneness#beef-and-lamb-whole-muscle'
    why: 'Beef bourguignon is a braise of high-collagen chuck cooked well past the USDA published-safe target of 145°F internal; the dish is fully cooked through to fork-tender (around 90°C / 195°F internal) for the canonical texture, which is when the collagen has fully converted to gelatin. This is structurally a different cooking regime than steakhouse beef and the USDA whole-muscle target does not apply in the same way; the long simmer above 75°C provides a generous pasteurization equivalency for any pathogens present. The food-safety attention in a braise is on (a) chilling leftovers rapidly within 2 hours of cooking and (b) reheating to 75°C / 165°F throughout before serving, not on the internal temperature at first service.'

steps:
  - text: 'Salt the 1500 g / 3.3 lb beef chuck cubes with the 12 g / 2½ tsp salt and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper 30 minutes ahead. Pat the surface dry with paper towels (any surface water prevents the sear). Toss in the 25 g / 3 tbsp flour to coat lightly. Preheat the oven to 160°C / 325°F. Set a 6 to 7 L heavy-bottomed Dutch oven over medium heat.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Render the lardons: add the 200 g / 7 oz cubed bacon to the cold Dutch oven; set over medium-low heat. Render slowly 8 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the lardons are deeply golden and crispy and the fat has rendered out to about 60 g of rendered fat in the pan. Remove the crispy lardons with a slotted spoon to a paper-towel-lined plate; reserve. Pour off all but about 30 g of the rendered fat; reserve the rest in a small heatproof bowl for the garnish sauté.'
    technique: 'lardon-rendering'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Increase heat to medium-high. Brown the floured beef cubes in 4 to 5 batches, never crowding the pan (a single uncrowded layer, ~7 to 8 cubes per batch), 3 to 4 minutes per side until each face has a deep brown crust. Transfer the browned cubes to a plate after each batch. Adjust the heat as the fond darkens; the bottom of the pot should be coated with a dark brown but not black fond. If the fond starts to scorch, add 30 ml / 2 tbsp of the wine and scrape it loose with a wooden spoon between batches.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Reduce heat to medium. Add the 250 g / 1 large onion (large dice), 150 g / 2 carrots (large dice), and 25 g / 6 to 8 cloves smashed garlic to the Dutch oven; sauté 6 to 7 minutes until the onion is soft and translucent and the surface of the pot has begun to brown again. Add the 45 g / 3 tbsp tomato paste; cook 2 to 3 minutes, stirring constantly, until the paste has darkened from bright red to brick red and is sizzling visibly in the fat (the bloom releases its glutamate concentration into the fat).'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Pour in the 750 ml / 1 bottle of red Burgundy or pinot noir all at once. Scrape the bottom of the pot with a wooden spoon to deglaze the fond into the wine (this is the deep brown color of the finished sauce; do not skip). Bring to a hard boil; reduce by about half (about 8 to 10 minutes; the smell shifts from raw wine to a clean fruity tang). The reduction concentrates the wine and cooks off the harsh alcohol notes.'
    technique: 'deglazing'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Add the 500 ml / 2 cups beef stock, the browned beef cubes (and any accumulated juices), and the bouquet garni (1 bundle). Stir to settle the beef into the liquid; the meat should be just barely covered by the liquid. If short, add hot water to just-cover. Bring to a bare simmer (tremor only, never a vigorous boil; visible bubbles breaking the surface but no rolling boil). Cover with the lid slightly ajar (to allow 10 percent evaporation).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Transfer the Dutch oven to the preheated 160°C / 325°F oven. Braise 2½ to 3 hours, checking every 45 minutes to confirm the temperature is a tremor (not a boil) and to stir gently. The beef is done when a fork inserted into a cube slides in with no resistance and the cube can be pulled apart with the slightest pressure; if there is still resistance at 2½ hours, give it another 30 to 45 minutes. The collagen has fully converted to gelatin; the sauce has thickened to a glossy spoon-coating consistency.'
    time_min: 165
  - text: 'While the braise is in the final 30 minutes, sauté the garnish vegetables. In a separate wide skillet over medium-high heat, add 15 g / 1 tbsp of the reserved bacon fat plus 15 g / 1 tbsp of the butter. Add the 250 g / 8 oz pearl onions; sauté 5 to 6 minutes until lightly browned on multiple sides. Add the 5 g / 1 tsp sugar and 30 ml / 2 tbsp of water; reduce heat to medium-low. Cook 3 to 4 minutes more, shaking the pan, until the sugar has caramelized and the onions are glossy with a light brown glaze.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'In the same skillet, add the remaining 15 g / 1 tbsp butter. Increase heat to medium-high. Add the 350 g / 12 oz quartered cremini mushrooms; cook 6 to 8 minutes without stirring for the first 3 minutes (let them release their water and start to brown). Then stir occasionally until the mushrooms have shrunk by half, browned deeply on multiple sides, and the pan is dry of liquid. Season with a pinch of salt.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'When the braise is done, remove from the oven. Fish out the bouquet garni and discard. Add the sautéed pearl onions, sautéed mushrooms, and reserved crispy lardons to the braise. Stir gently to integrate. Let stand 10 minutes uncovered to allow the garnish to warm through and the sauce to settle. Taste; adjust salt (the long braise concentrates flavors and the stew often needs a final ½ tsp salt at this point). Top with the 10 g chopped parsley just before serving. Serve in shallow bowls over buttered egg noodles or mashed potatoes; the sauce is structural to the dish and should pool generously around the meat.'
    time_min: 10

notes: |
  Beef bourguignon is a wine-driven dish, and the wine selection is structural. The dish reads like the wine it was cooked in, amplified by the reduction. A thin imbalanced wine produces a thin imbalanced stew; a deep fruit-forward acidic wine produces a deep fruit-forward acidic stew. The classical pairing is real red Burgundy (pinot noir from the Côte d''Or), but a good California or Oregon pinot noir produces an excellent and more affordable version. Do not use heavy oaky cabernet sauvignon or syrah; the tannin profile is wrong for the slow braise and the dish reads jammy and astringent. The wine you cook with should also be a wine you would happily drink with the finished dish; "cooking wine" with added salt is a different product and does not belong in serious cookery.

  The Maillard sear on the beef is structurally where the dish gets its dark brown color and savory depth. Crowding the pan turns the sear into a steam, the beef expels its water rather than browning, and the resulting stew is pale and watery. Four to five separate uncrowded batches over 25 to 30 minutes is the patient way; this is the most important phase of the dish. The browned fond on the bottom of the Dutch oven after the sears is the structural concentrate that the wine deglaze pulls into the sauce.

  The garnish is sautéed separately and added at the end, structurally for two reasons: (1) braised pearl onions and braised mushrooms turn mushy and lose their texture during the long oven cook, and (2) sautéing them separately develops a Maillard browning that adds a second layer of savory depth to the finished stew. The Julia Child template that has dominated American home cooking is right about this; some shortcuts that braise everything together produce a one-note stew where every component has the same texture.

  The make-ahead is genuinely better, not just convenient. The overnight rest in the refrigerator lets the gelatin and wine integrate, the fat solidifies and lifts off the top (allowing portion-control of richness), and the flavors integrate from a disparate collection of brown elements into a single cohesive deep-brown sauce that reads like a single thing. Plan for the day-ahead cook if at all possible; same-day service is acceptable but not canonical.

  Storage. Keeps 4 to 5 days refrigerated; reheats well on the stovetop over medium-low heat to a tremor, adding 30 ml of water or stock if the sauce has tightened too much. Freezes well; portion into 500 g containers, freeze flat, keeps 3 months at -18°C / 0°F. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator, warm to a tremor on the stovetop. Do not microwave; the wine sauce separates and the gelatin breaks.
