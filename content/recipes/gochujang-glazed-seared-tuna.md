source:
  name: 'The Korean gochujang-glaze idiom (the same sweet-spicy-fermented lacquer used on grilled fish and dakgalbi) applied to a seared-rare tuna block rather than a fully-cooked protein.'
  note: 'The restraint is the point. Gochujang on a braised or fully-cooked protein goes on thick and becomes the dish; here it is a thin brushed coat whose sugars caramelize in the sear while the fermented backbone stays in the background, so the tuna itself, not the paste, is what you taste.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The tuna is served rare, so it must be sashimi-grade or commercially flash-frozen for parasite kill (a home freezer cannot do this). Ask the counter "was this commercially flash-frozen," not "is this sushi-grade." If you cannot source it, sear the same glazed block through to a cooked tuna-steak doneness instead, see the modification.'
  - 'The glaze carries sugar from both the honey and the gochujang rice base, and sugar burns fast in a smoking pan. Keep the coat thin, keep the sear short, and have the plate and garnishes ready, this is a sub-90-second sear with no time to fix a scorching glaze mid-cook.'
  - 'Sear from cold. A fridge-cold (or briefly freezer-chilled) block lets the glaze lacquer and the surface set while the center stays under 50 °C; a room-temperature block overshoots the center in the same time. Do not temper the fish.'

steps:
  - text: 'Mix the glaze. In a small bowl whisk the 30 g / 1½ tbsp gochujang, the 15 g / 1 tbsp rice vinegar, the 12 g / 2 tsp honey, the 10 g / 2 tsp light soy sauce, the 3 g / ½ tsp toasted sesame oil, and the 4 g / 1 clove grated garlic into a smooth, pourable glaze. It should be the consistency of thin ketchup; if it is paste-thick, loosen with another few drops of rice vinegar. Reserve about a third of the glaze in a separate clean bowl to brush on after searing (never reuse the brush that touched raw fish for the finishing glaze).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Toast the sesame and prep the finish. Put the 8 g / 1 tbsp white sesame seeds in a dry pan over medium heat, agitating, until pale caramel-bronze and fragrant, 3 to 4 minutes; tip onto a cool plate. Slice the 15 g / 1 to 2 scallions thin on the bias. Set both aside.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Salt and glaze the tuna. Pat both 350 g / 12 oz tuna blocks bone-dry, surface water steams and makes the glaze slide. Season all faces with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt. Using the brush you will discard, paint a thin even coat of glaze (from the non-reserved portion) over all four long faces and the two ends. Thin is the operative word: a thick coat scorches before the surface sets.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Heat the pan. Place a heavy stainless or cast-iron skillet over high heat for 3 to 4 minutes until smoking. Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil and swirl; it should shimmer and just smoke within 5 seconds.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Sear fast, on the clock. Lay the first block in the pan and sear 30 to 40 seconds per face, rolling around all four long sides plus a 15-second touch on each end. The glaze sugars will catch and lacquer to a glossy, slightly charred red-brown, watch for the color, and pull the block before the glaze goes past deep brown into black-bitter. Total time in the pan is about 3 minutes. The cooked-opaque band should creep in only 2 to 3 mm, leaving a wide cool ruby center. Sear the second block the same way. The glaze chars faster than a bare crust, so lean toward the shorter end of each window.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Slice across the grain and finish. Rest the blocks 60 seconds to halt carryover, then slice each across the grain into 1 cm slabs. The cross-section: a thin lacquered-red rim, a 2 to 3 mm pale cooked band, a wide ruby center. Fan the slices on a plate, cut-face up. Brush or drizzle a little of the reserved (clean) glaze over the slices, scatter the 8 g / 1 tbsp toasted sesame, the 15 g / scallion, and the optional 1 g / ½ tsp gochugaru. Serve immediately while the lacquer is still glossy and the center cool.'
    time_min: 2

modifications:
  - for: 'no sashimi-grade fish available'
    to: 'fully cooked glazed tuna steak'
    how: 'Do not serve it rare from a regular fish counter. Use the same glaze, but sear each face about 90 seconds and cook the center through, pulling at an internal 55 to 60 °C. Because the longer sear risks scorching the glaze, brush most of it on in the last 30 seconds and again off-heat, so the sugars caramelize without burning. The fish reads firmer; the glaze carries it.'
    kind: 'protein'
  - for: 'heat and depth'
    to: 'doenjang-gochujang blend'
    how: 'Replace 10 g of the gochujang with 10 g doenjang (Korean fermented soybean paste). The doenjang brings a funkier, earthier, less sweet backbone, so the glaze reads more savory-fermented and less sweet-spicy. Add 5 g more honey if you want to keep the lacquering sugar level constant. This is closer to a ssamjang register.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'service as a rice bowl'
    to: 'gochujang tuna deopbap'
    how: 'Slice the seared glazed tuna over a bowl of hot short-grain rice, add quick-pickled cucumber and shredded perilla or lettuce, top with the sesame, scallion, and a soft egg yolk. notes: |
  Why thin glaze, not braising sauce. Gochujang's default home in Korean cooking is in quantity, 40 to 60 g braising a pan of dakgalbi, or stirred through a stew. Here it works in the opposite direction: a thin brushed coat whose job is to lacquer and lightly char in the sear, contributing color and a sweet-spicy-fermented edge while staying out of the way of the fish. Keep the coat thin and the sear short and the gochujang reads as a seasoning that frames the tuna; go thick and long and it reads as a sauce that smothers it. The user-facing rule: you should taste tuna first, glaze second.

  Sugar and the scorch line. The glaze carries sugar from the honey and from gochujang's rice base, and sugar caramelizes then carbonizes fast in a smoking pan. That is a feature, the light char on the lacquer is most of the flavor, and a liability, a beat too long and the glaze goes acrid black. The mitigations stack: keep the coat thin, sear toward the short end of each window, and lean on the cold center to buy time (a cold block tolerates a slightly longer surface sear before the heat reaches the middle). Reserve a clean portion of glaze to brush on after searing so the fresh raw-garlic-and-vinegar brightness is on the plate alongside the caramelized version.

  Why source still gates the dish. As with all seared-rare tuna, the center is functionally raw and the fish must be sashimi-grade or commercially flash-frozen for parasite kill. A home freezer does not reach the threshold. If the counter cannot confirm flash-freezing, cook the glazed block through (the modification above) rather than serving it rare; the glaze is forgiving enough that a fully-cooked version still eats well.

  The finish does real work. The raw scallion, the toasted sesame, and the optional gochugaru are not garnish, they are the dish's fresh-and-textural counterpoint to the cooked lacquer. The scallion adds a sharp allium bite, the sesame a nutty crunch, the gochugaru a fruity-warm chili note that reinforces the gochujang without adding wet sweetness. Add them after slicing, off the heat, so they stay crisp and bright.
