steps:
  - text: 'In a small bowl whisk together the 150 g / ½ cup plus 2 tbsp mayonnaise, 30 g / 2 tbsp sriracha, 8 ml / 1½ tsp rice vinegar, 5 g / 1 tsp sugar, 3 g / ½ tsp toasted sesame oil, the 1 grated garlic clove, and the 0.5 g pinch salt.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Whisk vigorously for 30 seconds until the sauce is a uniform coral-pink. Streaks of red mean undermixed; the sauce should look like a single color, not a swirl.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Taste. Heat-tolerant cooks will want more sriracha (up to 50 g for a vivid red, near-spicy version). Heat-averse will pull back to 20 g for a gentler pink-toned sauce. The vinegar-sugar balance is fixed; adjust only the sriracha and salt to your palate.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Transfer to a sealed jar. Refrigerate at least 15 minutes, the garlic mellows from sharp-raw to integrated-pungent, and the heat softens slightly. Holds 2 weeks refrigerated; the color may darken slightly as the sriracha oxidizes but flavor stays stable.'
    time_min: 15

notes: |
  The sriracha-mayo ratio of 1:5 by weight is the sweet spot. The Huy Fong rooster sriracha brings real garlic, vinegar, and a moderate-burn pepper profile; in this dilution it reads as warm-spicy rather than aggressive. Pushing the sriracha higher (1:4 or 1:3) is correct for sushi spread and spicy tuna rolls; pulling back (1:7) is right for sandwich spread and tame palates.

  Toasted sesame oil at half a teaspoon is the secret ingredient. The compound 2-furfurylthiol in toasted sesame oil has an aroma threshold in parts per billion, even a tiny dose registers as warm-nutty depth that lifts the sauce out of generic spicy-mayo territory. Do not exceed 5 g / 1 tsp; sesame oil is overpowering at higher doses and pulls the sauce toward bitter.

  The 15-minute rest meaningfully changes the sauce. Raw grated garlic carries a sharp allicin heat that fades into rounded garlic flavor after a short rest in acidic medium (the rice vinegar). Fresh-mixed sauce tastes harshly garlic; rested sauce tastes integrated.
