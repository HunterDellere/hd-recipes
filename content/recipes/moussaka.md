title: 'Moussaka'
desc: 'The Greek layered casserole: salt-purged and pan-seared eggplant slices alternated with a deeply spiced lamb-and-tomato ragù (cinnamon, allspice, oregano, red wine, tomato), topped with a thick blanket of béchamel enriched with egg yolks and grated kefalotyri (the canonical sharp Greek hard cheese, with parmigiano as the structural substitute), baked until the béchamel has set into a custardy golden brown cap. Structurally a Greek-Ottoman dish that combines three independent preparations (the seared eggplant, the cinnamon-warm meat sauce, the egg-enriched béchamel) into a single layered casserole; the result is one of the great Mediterranean comfort dishes.'

source:
  name: 'Greek canon (modern form codified by Nikolaos Tselementes, 1920s Athens)'
  note: 'Moussaka in its modern Greek form (eggplant + lamb + béchamel + cheese, layered and baked) is a 20th-century codification by Nikolaos Tselementes, who introduced the French-influenced béchamel topping to a traditional Ottoman lamb-eggplant casserole. The dish has Ottoman roots (the word moussaka is from Arabic musaqqaʿa, "chilled"), but the structural form most internationally recognized today is the Tselementes Greek version with the béchamel cap. Defining elements: salt-purged eggplant (the salt extracts bitter alkaloids and collapses cell walls so the slices pan-sear without absorbing oil), a deeply spiced lamb ragù (cinnamon and allspice are structural Greek-Ottoman warm spices, distinct from Italian ragù), tomato and red wine, and the egg-enriched béchamel topping (the eggs convert the béchamel from a sauce into a custardy set cap during the bake). The béchamel is structurally non-negotiable for canonical moussaka; without it the dish is simply a lamb-and-eggplant casserole.'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 3 to 4 hour project with three independent components (eggplant prep + pan-sear, lamb ragù simmer, béchamel) that come together in the final layered bake. Plan the order: start the eggplant salt-purge first (45 minutes passive), start the lamb ragù while the eggplant purges (30 minute simmer), pan-sear the eggplant while the ragù simmers, make the béchamel last just before assembly. The whole dish can be assembled and held in the refrigerator for up to 8 hours before baking, then baked from cold (add 15 minutes to the bake time).'
  - 'The eggplant salt-purge is structural and not optional. Skip it and the slices absorb oil aggressively in the pan-sear, the dish reads as oil-saturated, and the finished casserole has a greasy mouthfeel. The 45-minute purge cannot be shortcut.'
  - 'Have the eggs at room temperature for the béchamel; cold eggs added to hot béchamel cause the proteins to seize into scrambled-egg lumps.'

steps:
  - text: 'Lay the 1200 g / 3 to 4 eggplants (sliced 1 cm thick) on two large rimmed sheet pans in single layers. Sprinkle both sides of each slice with the 18 g / 1½ tbsp salt total (about 4 to 5 g per pan). Let purge 45 minutes; brown beads of bitter alkaloid water will form on the surface. After 45 minutes, blot every slice thoroughly dry with paper towels (any surface water causes oil-spitting during the sear).'
    technique: 'salting-eggplant'
    time_min: 50
  - text: 'While the eggplant purges, make the lamb ragù. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil in a wide Dutch oven over medium heat. Add the 250 g / 1 large onion (finely diced); sweat 6 to 7 minutes until translucent and soft. Add the 20 g / 5 to 6 cloves minced garlic; cook 90 seconds until fragrant and pale gold.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Increase heat to medium-high. Add the 700 g / 1½ lb ground lamb; break up with a wooden spoon. Cook 8 to 10 minutes until the lamb is no longer pink, the fat has rendered, and the meat has begun to brown on multiple surfaces. Pour off any excess fat above 30 g (lamb releases substantial fat; the rendered fat should coat the meat but not pool).'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Add the 45 g / 3 tbsp tomato paste; cook 2 minutes, stirring constantly, until the paste has darkened from bright red to brick red. Add the 180 g / ¾ cup red wine; reduce 4 to 5 minutes until almost dry. Add the 400 g / ½ can hand-crushed San Marzano tomatoes, 2 g / 1 tsp ground cinnamon, 1 g / ½ tsp ground allspice, 2 g / 1½ tsp dried oregano, 2 bay leaves, and 6 g / 1 tsp salt. Stir to combine; reduce heat to medium-low.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Simmer the ragù uncovered 30 to 40 minutes, stirring occasionally, until reduced to a thick spreadable consistency (not soupy; the casserole structure depends on a dry ragù that does not flood the bottom of the dish during baking). Taste; adjust salt. Fish out the bay leaves. Reserve at room temperature.'
    time_min: 38
  - text: 'Meanwhile, pan-sear the eggplant. Heat the 120 g / ½ cup olive oil in a wide skillet over medium-high until shimmering. Sear the eggplant slices in batches (single layer, no overlap), 3 to 4 minutes per side, until deeply golden brown on both faces and softened through. Transfer to a paper-towel-lined plate. Continue until all eggplant is seared, adding olive oil to the pan as needed if it runs low (the eggplant absorbs a small amount during the sear).'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Make the béchamel. Warm the 750 g / 3 cups milk in a saucepan to 60°C / 140°F (just barely steaming, not simmering). In a separate medium saucepan over medium heat, melt the 80 g / 5½ tbsp butter. Add the 60 g / ½ cup flour all at once; whisk vigorously to a smooth paste (the roux). Cook the roux 2 to 3 minutes, whisking constantly, until it smells slightly nutty but has not browned (blonde roux, not brown roux).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the warm milk to the roux in three additions, whisking vigorously between each addition to fully incorporate before adding more. The mixture should be lump-free and gradually thicken. After all milk is in, increase heat to medium; bring to a bare simmer, whisking constantly, and cook 4 to 5 minutes until the béchamel coats the back of a spoon and the surface has thickened to a heavy spoonable consistency. Pull from heat. Add the 1 g / ¼ tsp grated nutmeg and 2 g / ⅓ tsp salt.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Temper the egg yolks: in a small bowl, beat the 54 g / 3 yolks lightly. Whisking constantly, drizzle about 120 g / ½ cup of the hot béchamel into the yolks in a thin stream (this warms the yolks gradually without scrambling them). Then pour the tempered yolk mixture back into the main pot of béchamel; whisk to combine. Add 60 g of the grated kefalotyri (or parmigiano); whisk until melted in. Reserve the remaining 40 g of cheese for the top of the casserole.'
    technique: 'tempering-eggs'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Assemble. Preheat oven to 180°C / 350°F. Lightly oil a 22 by 33 cm / 9 by 13 in baking dish. Lay half the seared eggplant slices in an even layer on the bottom (overlap slightly to cover the entire surface). Spread the lamb ragù evenly over the eggplant in a single layer (about 2 cm thick). Lay the remaining seared eggplant slices over the ragù in an even layer. Pour the béchamel over the top eggplant layer in an even thick blanket (about 1.5 cm deep); smooth with the back of a spatula so it reaches into all corners. Sprinkle the reserved 40 g of grated kefalotyri evenly over the béchamel.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Bake on the middle rack of the preheated 180°C / 350°F oven 45 to 55 minutes, until the béchamel has puffed slightly, the top is deeply golden brown with visible darker brown spots, and the entire casserole is bubbling at the edges. The béchamel should have set to a custardy texture (insert a knife at the edge; it should come out clean of liquid béchamel but coated with set custard).'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 50
  - text: 'Rest 20 minutes before serving; this rest is structural and not optional. The béchamel continues to set during the rest, and cutting immediately produces a sloppy collapse of layers. After 20 minutes the casserole can be cut into clean 9 by 13 cm portions that hold their layered structure on the plate. Serve with a small green salad (horiatiki is the canonical pairing), crusty bread, and the remaining red wine.'
    time_min: 20

    how: 'Execute steps 1 through 9; assemble the casserole in the baking dish but do not bake. Cover tightly with plastic wrap and refrigerate up to 24 hours. notes: |
  The cinnamon and allspice are structural and define the dish as Greek-Ottoman rather than Italian. The warm spices (the "moussaka spice profile") distinguish a Greek lamb ragù from an Italian lamb ragù; without them the meat sauce reads as Bolognese-adjacent rather than Greek. Cinnamon in particular is the signature; it surprises Western cooks who associate cinnamon exclusively with sweet preparations, but its use in savory Greek and Ottoman lamb cookery is canonical and structural.

  The béchamel must be egg-enriched for the canonical custardy set. A plain béchamel (milk, butter, flour) without yolks softens during the bake but does not set into a sliceable custard; the moussaka comes out looking like saucy lamb-and-eggplant with melted-cheese topping. The 3 egg yolks tempered into the béchamel before baking convert the topping from a sauce into a set custard during the oven cook; the finished moussaka can be cut into clean rectangular portions that hold their layered structure on the plate. This is the structural Tselementes innovation that defines modern Greek moussaka.

  The eggplant must be pan-seared, not roasted. Some modern shortcuts recommend roasting the eggplant slices on a sheet pan instead of pan-searing; the result is acceptable but reads different (the roasted eggplant is drier and less infused with olive oil). The canonical Greek technique is the shallow olive-oil pan-sear: the slices are partially fried in olive oil to develop both a Maillard browning on the surface and an oil-saturated tenderness through the slice. The salt purge step is what prevents the eggplant from absorbing the entire pan of oil; salt-purged eggplant absorbs about 20 percent of the oil that raw eggplant absorbs.

  The 20-minute rest after baking is structural and not optional. The casserole comes out of the oven with the béchamel just barely set and the lamb ragù still bubbling underneath; cutting immediately produces a sloppy plate where the layers collapse together. The 20-minute rest lets the béchamel finish setting, the ragù thicken slightly as it cools, and the entire casserole stabilize into a sliceable structure. Cut before the rest and the moussaka reads as a mess; cut after the rest and it presents as the layered casserole it is.

  Storage. Keeps 5 days refrigerated, covered tightly. Reheats beautifully in a 160°C / 325°F oven for 25 minutes from refrigerator-cold (cover loosely with foil for the first 15 minutes to prevent the top from over-browning). Many Greek cooks consider day-two moussaka to be the canonical preparation, with the layered flavors more integrated than on day one. Freezes well; portion into single-serving containers, freeze 3 months at -18°C / 0°F. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator, reheat in a 160°C / 325°F oven for 30 minutes covered.
