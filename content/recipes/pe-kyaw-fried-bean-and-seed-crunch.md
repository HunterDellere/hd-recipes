steps:
  - text: 'Soak and dry the legumes. Cover the 80 g / ½ cup yellow split peas and the 60 g / ⅓ cup dried beans (if using) with plenty of cool water and soak 25 minutes, no longer. This is a partial hydration, not the full soak of a dal: just enough that the interior will cook through to a clean crunch rather than staying chalky-raw, but not so much that the legume softens. Drain thoroughly and spread on a towel; pat the tops dry. They must be surface-dry before they hit the oil.'
    time_min: 27
  - text: 'Toast the peanuts and sesame first. In a dry skillet over medium, toast the 70 g / ½ cup raw peanuts, shaking often, for 8 to 10 minutes until the skins darken and the nuts smell roasted; tip them out to cool. In the same dry pan, toast the 20 g / 2 tbsp sesame seeds over medium-low, stirring constantly, for 2 to 3 minutes until golden and popping; tip out. Doing these dry-toasts first keeps the nuts and seeds out of the frying oil, where they would scorch.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Fry the split peas slow. Heat the 240 ml / 1 cup oil in a small saucepan to about 150°C / 300°F (a split pea dropped in should bubble steadily, not violently). Add the dried split peas and fry over medium-low, stirring, for 5 to 8 minutes. They will bubble hard as their water boils off, then the bubbling slows and they turn a nutty gold. Pull them with a slotted spoon the moment the bubbling subsides and they are golden, before they go dark, and drain on paper.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Fry the beans in their own batch. If using the 60 g / ⅓ cup beans, fry them the same way in the same oil; being larger they take a minute or two longer to stop bubbling. Pull at nutty-gold and drain. Pour the now bean-and-pea-flavored frying oil into a jar and save it, it is excellent garlic-free frying or finishing oil.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Combine while warm and salt. In a bowl, toss the fried split peas, fried beans, toasted peanuts, and toasted sesame together with the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt while everything is still warm so the salt clings. Cool completely; the legumes finish hardening to a full crunch as they cool.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Store airtight. Once fully cool and crisp, store the mix in an airtight jar at room temperature. It holds its crunch for a week or two; if it softens with humidity, re-crisp briefly in a dry pan and re-cool. Scatter generously over lahpet thoke, noodle bowls, or rice.'

notes: |
  Why the short soak. A bone-dry split pea dropped into frying oil browns and hardens on the outside while the very center stays raw and chalky; a fully soaked, soft split pea fries greasy and never crisps. The 25-minute partial soak threads the needle, hydrating the interior just enough to cook through to a clean, even crunch, then drying it so it crisps rather than steams. The same logic applies to the larger beans, which simply need a couple more minutes in the oil to cook all the way through.

  Why fry low and pull at the bubble-stop. The legumes are full of water, and frying is really a dehydration: the violent bubbling you see at the start is that water boiling off through the oil. As long as it is bubbling hard, water is still leaving and the inside is still cooking. When the bubbling slows to a lazy simmer and the color turns nutty-gold, the water is gone and the legume is crisp through, that is the moment to pull, before residual heat tips it to burnt and bitter. Frying low (around 150°C) gives you a wide, forgiving window to catch that point.

  Why dry-toast the nuts and seeds separately. Peanuts and sesame scorch fast and unevenly in deep oil and would muddy the frying batch. Dry-toasting them in a separate pan gives cleaner, more controllable color and keeps the frying oil clean enough to save and reuse. This crunch, fried legumes plus toasted nuts and seeds, is a core, non-negotiable element of lahpet thoke, not a garnish; it provides the textural contrast against the soft fermented tea and the crisp raw vegetables.
