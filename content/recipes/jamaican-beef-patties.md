  note: 'The Jamaican beef patty is the country''s most ubiquitous handheld food, sold from bakery chains, gas stations, and street carts. The defining elements are the turmeric-and-curry-yellow pastry (color is structural to recognizability), the shortcrust that crackles into flaky layers, and the spice-forward beef filling with scotch bonnet and allspice. The dish is descended from Cornish pasties brought by British colonial settlers and reinvented through Jamaican spice tradition.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The pastry needs at least 90 minutes of cold rest after mixing; this hydrates the flour evenly and relaxes the gluten so the dough rolls thin without snapping back. Plan the dough first, make the filling while the dough rests, then assemble.'
  - 'Both the butter and the lard or shortening must be cold (4 °C / 40 °F or colder) when cut into the flour. Warm fat smears into the flour instead of staying in discrete pieces, and the pastry comes out dense rather than flaky. Cube the fats and chill them in the freezer 15 minutes before starting.'
  - 'The filling must be fully cooled before assembly. A warm filling steams the pastry, melts the butter pockets, and produces leaky soggy patties.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the pastry. In a large bowl whisk the 400 g all-purpose flour, 5 g / 2 tsp turmeric, 5 g / 2 tsp curry powder, and 6 g / 1 tsp fine sea salt until uniformly yellow with no streaks of white flour. Add the 120 g cubed cold butter and the 90 g cubed cold lard. Using a pastry cutter or fingertips (work fast, do not warm the fat with palm heat), cut the fats into the flour until the mixture looks like coarse cornmeal with pea-sized butter chunks still visible. Visible butter chunks are the structural source of flakiness; do not over-work to a uniform sand.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Drizzle in the 130 ml / ½ cup + 1 tbsp ice water while tossing with a fork. Stop adding water as soon as the dough holds together when pressed between fingers; it should look shaggy, not smooth. Gather into a flat disc, wrap tightly in beeswax wrap or parchment, and refrigerate at least 90 minutes (overnight is better).'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'While the dough rests, cook the filling. Heat the 15 ml / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a wide skillet over medium-high heat. Add the 150 g diced onion; sweat 4 minutes until translucent. Add the 50 g scallions, 15 g / 4 cloves garlic, 4 g / 2 tsp thyme, and 8 g minced scotch bonnet; cook 1 minute until fragrant.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the 450 g ground beef. Break up with a wooden spoon and cook 6 to 8 minutes over medium-high, stirring occasionally, until the beef has lost all pink and the bottom of the pan has developed a brown fond. Do not stir constantly; let the beef sit and Maillard-brown on the pan bottom in patches. The fond is the structural source of the filling''s deep savor.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the 3 g / 1½ tsp allspice, 5 g / 2 tsp curry powder, 6 g / 1½ tsp salt, and 2 g / 1 tsp pepper. Stir 30 seconds to bloom the spices in the rendered beef fat. Add the 15 ml / 1 tbsp soy sauce and the 180 ml / ¾ cup water; scrape the fond off the pan bottom. Bring to a hard simmer.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Sprinkle in the 60 g / ½ cup breadcrumbs while stirring; the crumbs absorb the liquid and turn the filling from loose-and-wet into a tight, scoopable paste in about 90 seconds. Cook 2 more minutes until the mixture pulls away from the sides of the pan when stirred. Transfer to a wide plate or sheet pan to cool fully (about 30 minutes; speed up with the freezer). A warm filling melts the pastry butter and produces soggy patties.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Preheat oven to 200 °C / 400 °F with the rack in the center. Line two sheet pans with parchment. Divide the chilled dough into 8 equal pieces (about 90 g each). Work with one piece at a time; keep the rest in the fridge. On a lightly floured surface, roll each piece into a 16 cm / 6.5 inch round about 2 mm thick. The pastry rolls more easily when very cold; if it cracks, rest 5 more minutes in the fridge.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Place about 75 g of cooled filling on one half of the round, leaving a 2 cm border. Brush the border with the beaten egg wash. Fold the pastry over to make a half-moon; press the edges firmly with the tines of a fork to seal (the fork crimp is the canonical Jamaican look). Transfer to the parchment-lined sheet pan. Repeat with the remaining 7 dough pieces.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Brush the tops of all patties with egg wash (gives them the bakery sheen and deepens the yellow color). Cut a small steam vent (5 mm) in the top of each patty with a sharp knife.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Bake 30 to 35 minutes, rotating the pans front-to-back at the 15-minute mark, until the pastry is deep golden yellow and the bottom is firm and crackly when lifted with a spatula. Cool on the pan 5 minutes before serving; the filling is napalm-hot directly from the oven.'
    time_min: 35

substitutions:
  - for: 'cold lard or vegetable shortening'
    use: 'all butter (240 g total instead of 120 g butter + 90 g lard)'
    note: 'All-butter pastry produces a more flavorful but slightly less flaky shortcrust. Lard produces the canonical Jamaican texture; vegetable shortening produces a similar texture with less flavor.'
  - for: 'curry powder (Jamaican-style)'
    use: 'Madras curry powder'
    note: 'Jamaican curry powder is heavier on turmeric and allspice than Madras; the difference reads but is acceptable. Avoid Thai or Japanese curry powders, the spice profile is wrong.'
  - for: 'scotch bonnet'
    use: 'habanero, or a pinch of cayenne plus a pinch of dried mango powder'
    note: 'Habanero is the closest 1:1 substitute. Cayenne misses the fruit-floral notes that are structural to jerk and patty flavor.'

notes: |
  Why the breadcrumbs. The filling has to be wet enough to taste juicy but tight enough not to leak out of the pastry. Cooking the beef in 180 ml of liquid and then binding with 60 g of dry breadcrumbs creates a paste that holds its shape when scooped but tastes like a wet braise rather than a dry crumble. Skip the breadcrumbs and the filling either leaks (too wet) or tastes dry (cooked off completely).

  Why turmeric in the pastry. The yellow is structural to Jamaican patty identity. A pale pastry reads as wrong, even if the flavor is identical. The turmeric also contributes a faint earthy bitterness that balances the rich beef filling. Curry powder in the pastry doubles the color depth and reinforces the spice signal of the filling.

  The cold-fat rule. Cold butter and lard stay as discrete pockets in the flour during mixing; when the pastry hits the oven, the water in the butter vaporizes and pushes the surrounding dough apart, creating the laminated flaky layers. Warm fat smears into the flour and waterproofs it, producing a dense crumbly pastry without flake. The freezer chill is not optional.

  Storage. Baked patties keep 3 days refrigerated; reheat in a 180 °C / 350 °F oven 8 to 10 minutes to re-crisp the pastry (microwave produces soggy pastry). Unbaked patties freeze beautifully: assemble through step 8, freeze flat on the sheet pan until solid, then bag. Bake from frozen at 200 °C / 400 °F for 38 to 42 minutes.
