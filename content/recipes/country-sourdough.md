  - 'A mature, well-fed sourdough starter is the structural prerequisite. This recipe assumes you have a starter that has been fed regularly (at least once a week) for at least a month, doubles reliably in 6 to 8 hours after feeding, and smells pleasantly tangy. A sheet pan with water in the oven below does not work as well; the Dutch oven is non-negotiable for this recipe.'

steps:
  - text: 'Build the levain (8 to 10 hours before mixing the dough; Friday 9 PM for a Saturday 7 AM mix, or Friday 7 AM for a Saturday 5 PM mix). Combine the 30 g active starter, 100 g bread flour, and 100 g room-temperature water in a clean jar. Stir to a smooth paste. Cover loosely (with the lid resting on top, not screwed down; the levain needs gas exchange to ferment properly). Let stand at room temperature, 22 to 24°C / 72 to 75°F, until tripled in volume with visible bubbles, a domed top, and a pleasantly tangy smell. The float test: drop a small spoonful in cold water; it should float, indicating the levain is fully matured and ready to use.'
    time_min: 540
  - text: 'Autolyse (the structural passive gluten-development rest). Combine the 850 g bread flour, 150 g whole wheat flour, and 720 g warm water in a large mixing bowl. Mix by hand or with a Danish dough whisk until no dry flour remains; the dough will be shaggy and rough. Cover with a damp kitchen towel; rest 45 minutes at room temperature. During the autolyse, the flour fully hydrates, the gluten begins to develop passively without kneading, and the enzymes in the flour start breaking down starches into sugars that the yeast will use.'
    time_min: 50
  - text: 'Mix the salt and levain into the dough. After the autolyse, the dough should look noticeably smoother and more cohesive. Add the 22 g fine sea salt and the 200 g matured levain. Pinch and fold the dough vigorously with one hand for 3 to 4 minutes (working the salt and levain through the dough; use a slap-and-fold motion: lift the dough up, slap it down, fold it over). The dough will go from shaggy to smoother and slightly stretchy by the end. Cover with the damp towel.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Bulk ferment with stretch-and-folds. Ferment at 22 to 24°C / 72 to 75°F for 4 hours total. Perform stretch-and-folds at minutes 30, 60, 90, and 120 (4 sets total): wet one hand with water, reach under one side of the dough, lift it up to its full stretch, and fold it over to the opposite side; rotate the bowl 90 degrees and repeat 3 more times (4 stretches per set). After the 4th set at the 120-minute mark, do not disturb the dough for the remaining 2 hours. The dough should rise about 50% by volume by the 4-hour mark, with visible bubbles on the surface and a slightly domed top.'
    time_min: 240
  - text: 'Pre-shape and bench rest. Lightly flour a clean work surface. Turn the bulk-fermented dough out onto the surface (use a wet bench scraper to release if it sticks). Pre-shape into a loose round: pull the edges of the dough into the center, flip over, and use the bench scraper to drag the dough in tight circles toward you, creating surface tension on the bottom (the now-bottom). Cover with the damp kitchen towel; rest 25 to 30 minutes (the bench rest relaxes the gluten, making the final shape easier).'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Final shape. Lightly flour the work surface. Flip the pre-shaped round upside down (so the smooth top is now the bottom). Stretch the dough into a rough rectangle. Fold the bottom third up, the top third down (like folding a letter), and then roll up from the side (creating a tight cylinder of dough). Pinch the seam closed; flip seam-side-down; gently drag in tight circles toward you, building surface tension on the now-bottom. Transfer the shaped dough seam-side-UP into a flour-dusted banneton (a 10-inch round banneton, or a bowl lined with a flour-dusted kitchen towel). Use the rice flour for the dusting; it prevents sticking better than wheat flour.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Cold proof overnight. Cover the banneton with a plastic shower cap or wrap; refrigerate at 4°C / 40°F for 12 to 16 hours. The cold proof slows the yeast activity dramatically while letting the bacterial fermentation continue at a slower pace; this is the structural step that develops the deep complex flavor of overnight-cold-proofed sourdough vs same-day-room-temperature sourdough.'
    time_min: 720
  - text: 'Preheat the oven and Dutch oven. The next morning (or 12+ hours after the cold proof started), preheat the oven with the Dutch oven inside at 245°C / 475°F for 45 minutes. The Dutch oven needs to be fully hot before the dough goes in; rushing this step produces a pale crust and inadequate oven-spring.'
    time_min: 45
  - text: 'Score and load. Cut a 30 cm square of parchment paper. Pull the banneton from the refrigerator (the dough goes straight from cold to hot; do not let it warm up). Turn the dough out of the banneton onto the parchment (the smooth top is now up; the seam is on the bottom). Use a lame, sharp razor blade, or sharp paring knife to score the top with one decisive slash 1 cm deep across the dough (a single curved slash, or an X if you want a more dramatic pattern). The score is structurally important; it controls where the bread expands during oven-spring and prevents random bursting of the crust.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Bake covered for the oven-spring. Carefully pull the hot Dutch oven from the oven; remove the lid. Lift the parchment-and-dough using the parchment edges as handles; lower into the Dutch oven (the parchment lines the bottom, the dough sits on the parchment). Cover with the lid; return to the oven; bake 22 minutes covered. The trapped steam from the dough creates the structural oven-spring (the dramatic expansion of the loaf during the first 10 minutes of baking).'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Uncover and finish. Carefully pull the lid off (use heavy oven mitts; steam will release aggressively). Reduce the oven to 230°C / 450°F. Bake another 22 to 28 minutes uncovered, until the crust is deep mahogany-brown (not just golden, structurally), the loaf sounds hollow when tapped on the bottom, and the internal temperature reads 96 to 99°C / 205 to 210°F on an instant-read thermometer. The deep crust is the structural visual marker of a properly baked sourdough; pale crust indicates underbake.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Cool fully before slicing. Transfer the bread to a wire rack; let cool at least 1 hour before slicing (2 hours is better). The cooling is structural; the crumb continues to cook from residual heat for the first 30 minutes out of the oven, and slicing while warm produces gummy doughy slices instead of clean fully-set crumb. The cooling time is non-negotiable for the canonical sourdough texture.'
    time_min: 60

notes: |
  The autolyse mechanism. The 45-minute rest of flour-and-water without yeast or salt allows three structural things to happen: the flour fully hydrates (a process that takes about 30 minutes for bread flour), the gluten begins to form passively as the wheat proteins (glutenin and gliadin) hydrate and start crosslinking, and the enzymes in the flour (amylase, protease) begin breaking down starches into simple sugars and proteins into amino acids. The dough that emerges from the autolyse is dramatically easier to work with than a no-autolyse dough; the technique is structural to high-hydration sourdough.

  The cold-proof flavor chemistry. The overnight refrigeration at 4°C / 40°F slows the yeast (which prefers 22 to 28°C) to about 10 percent of its normal activity, while the lactobacilli (the bacteria responsible for sourdough acidity) continue to function at about 40 percent of their normal activity. This selective slowdown allows the bacterial flavor compounds (lactic acid, acetic acid, complex aromatic byproducts) to accumulate without the yeast over-fermenting the dough. The result is a loaf with significantly deeper, more complex flavor than a same-day-baked sourdough; the technique is the structural difference between professional bakery sourdough and beginner sourdough.

  The Dutch-oven steam trap. The dough releases a significant amount of water vapor during the first 10 minutes of baking; in a covered Dutch oven, this steam stays in contact with the dough surface, keeping the crust soft and pliable while the interior continues to expand (the oven-spring). After the 22-minute covered phase, the crust has set enough that removing the lid allows the surface to crisp and brown without compromising the structure. The Dutch oven is the home-bakery substitute for the steam-injection professional bakery ovens; the alternative (sheet pan with water in the oven below) produces visibly less oven-spring and a duller crust.

  Bulk ferment cues. The 4-hour bulk at 22 to 24°C is calibrated for a healthy mature levain; the actual bulk time can range from 3 to 6 hours depending on starter strength, room temperature, and flour. The visual and tactile cues are more reliable than the timer: the dough should rise about 50% by volume (mark the level on the bowl with a rubber band at the start), the surface should show visible bubbles, the dough should feel airy and slightly jiggly when the bowl is shaken, and a finger poked into the dough should spring back about halfway (full spring-back means undefermented; no spring-back means overfermented). With practice, the feel of fully-bulked dough becomes immediately recognizable.

  Hydration adjustment. The 72% hydration is moderate for a country sourdough; some flours absorb less and produce a slack dough at this percentage. Reduce to 70% if the dough feels unmanageable; increase to 75% once you are comfortable with the basic technique. Whole wheat flour absorbs more water than bread flour, so the 15% whole wheat in this recipe pulls the effective hydration down slightly compared to a pure white-flour loaf at the same percentage.

  Storage. Cooled completely, the bread keeps at room temperature in a paper bag or wrapped in a kitchen towel for 3 to 4 days (the crust will soften progressively; the crumb stays fresh). Freezing: slice the cooled loaf, wrap individual slices in parchment, store in a zip-top freezer bag, frozen 3 months; thaw slices at room temperature for 10 minutes or toast directly from frozen. Do not store in plastic at room temperature; the trapped moisture softens the crust and accelerates mold growth. The dish does not reheat well as a whole loaf; toasting individual slices is the better approach for refreshing day-old bread.
