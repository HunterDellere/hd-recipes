source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Marseille fisherman canon (the dish was born as a fishermen''s pot-on-the-quay preparation in the late 18th and early 19th century, using the unsold rockfish and bony Mediterranean species that the market would not buy: rascasse, vive, galinette, congre, baudroie). The dish stabilized in the late 19th century around the Charte de la Bouillabaisse Marseillaise (1980), an industry-defined standard for the canonical Marseille preparation: at least four named local fish, saffron, fennel, orange peel, no shellfish (the older fishermen''s preparation excluded crustaceans for cost), broth-strained-and-served-separately, rouille-and-croutons accompaniment.'
  note: 'Bouillabaisse is structurally distinct from its Italian-American descendant cioppino in three ways. First, the broth is built from many fish heads and bones simmered hard for 30 to 45 minutes to extract gelatin and emulsify the olive oil into a milky orange-pink suspension (the bouillon emulsifié), then strained completely; the fish chunks that go into the broth at the end are added to the strained, clarified, lighter-bodied liquid for a 6 to 10 minute poach. Cioppino simmers everything together in a tomato-broth base; bouillabaisse uses the strain step to make a more refined and concentrated broth. Second, bouillabaisse is canonically served in two courses: the broth first (over saffron-rubbed croutons spread with rouille, the soup course), the fish second (on a platter with steamed potatoes and more rouille, the main course); cioppino is a single bowl. Third, the aromatic signature is different: bouillabaisse uses orange peel and fennel and saffron as the structural defining trio (the Provençal-Mediterranean-North-African crosspollination); cioppino uses fennel and saffron but no orange, with red pepper flakes substituting for the bitter-citrus depth. The dish is the structural ancestor of cioppino but reads as a more refined, more aromatic, more technique-driven Provençal preparation. The fish mix in this version (monkfish, cod, halibut, snapper, sea bass) is the US-market substitution for the canonical Provençal mix (rascasse, conger eel, monkfish, john dory); the Charte requires four named local Mediterranean fish but the structural rule is "multiple firm white fish, with bony heads and bones for the broth, ideally including at least one species with prominent fin-and-cheek structure for the gelatin extract."'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'Pulling the firm white fish chunks at 60°C / 140°F internal (just-flaky, juicy) rather than the USDA-published 145°F target'
    ref: 'safety/seafood-doneness#cooking-targets-for-fin-fish'
    why: 'Halibut, cod, monkfish, snapper, and sea bass all have low-fat flesh that turns chalky and dry past 60°C. The traditional French and Provençal cooking target for white fish is the just-flaky moment around 130 to 140°F internal, where the protein has set but not expelled water. This is below the USDA published-safe instantaneous target of 145°F; cooks should source fresh, properly-handled fish from a reputable counter and accept the tradeoff. Vulnerable diners (pregnant, immunocompromised) should be served fish cooked to 145°F internal (additional 1 to 2 minutes of poach time) accepting the texture loss.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Order the right fish a day ahead. The dish needs at least three firm white fish species (monkfish, cod, halibut, snapper, sea bass) AND their heads and bones for the broth; ask the fishmonger to fillet whole fish and bag the carcasses. Without the head-and-bone fraction the broth cannot emulsify to the canonical milky orange-pink suspension.'
  - 'Make the broth first, up to 24 hours ahead. The fish broth simmers hard for 30 to 45 minutes (a vigorous boil, not a tremor: emulsification depends on mechanical agitation), then strains and rests. Doing this the day before pays back in clearer flavor and a much shorter window between final fish poach and service.'
  - 'Steam the potatoes and toast the croutons in parallel with the final fish poach. The fish chunks need only 6 to 10 minutes in the broth at the end; the potatoes (15 to 20 minutes) and croutons (8 minutes at 180°C / 350°F) must be ready when the fish comes out, or the two-course service collapses.'
  - 'The rouille can be made up to 4 hours ahead and held at room temperature; bloom the saffron in warm broth for the rouille AND for the broth at the same time. Cold rouille from the fridge breaks when it hits the hot crouton.'

steps:
  - text: 'Build the fish broth. In a wide 8 to 10 quart stockpot over medium heat, warm the 60 g / ¼ cup olive oil. Add the 200 g onion, 150 g leek, 250 g fennel bulb dice, 30 g / 6 to 8 smashed garlic cloves, 3 g / 1½ tsp fennel seed, 1 g / ¼ tsp red pepper flakes, and 12 g / 2½ tsp salt. Sweat 8 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the vegetables turn translucent and start to take on a faint golden-edge color (the goal is softened, not browned).'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Push the aromatics to the edges of the pot, drop the 60 g / ¼ cup tomato paste into the cleared center, and let it sizzle in the oil for 60 to 90 seconds without stirring, until the paste turns from bright red to a deeper rust-red. Stir in the 400 g / ½ can crushed San Marzano tomatoes; cook 3 to 4 minutes, breaking up the tomato chunks, until the mixture has thickened slightly and smells of roasted-tomato.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the 1500 g / 3 to 4 lbs of fish bones, heads, and trimmings (gills removed, rinsed); pour in the 250 ml / 1 cup white wine; scrape up any fond. Increase heat to high and bring to a boil; cook 2 minutes to burn off the alcohol. Add the 2500 ml / 10½ cups water (or enough to cover the bones by 5 cm), the 0.5 g saffron, the wide-strip orange peel from 1 orange, the 2 bay leaves, the 6 thyme sprigs, and the bundled parsley stems. Bring back to a boil, then reduce to a hard simmer (the canonical bouillabaisse broth simmers harder than a standard fumet, the goal is emulsification of the olive oil into the gelatinous broth, which produces the milky orange-pink color).'
    technique: 'deglazing'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Hard-simmer the broth uncovered 35 to 45 minutes, breaking up the bones with a wooden spoon every 10 minutes to release more gelatin. The broth should reduce by roughly a quarter and turn a milky orange-pink color from the emulsified oil. Taste at 30 minutes; the broth should read intensely saffron-tomato-fish, with no raw bone notes. The hardest-simmer technique here is canonical and structural; a gentle simmer produces a clear thin broth (a fumet), which is wrong for bouillabaisse.'
    time_min: 40
  - text: 'Strain the broth. Set a fine-mesh strainer (or a colander lined with damp cheesecloth) over a clean large bowl or stockpot. Pour the broth and solids through the strainer; press hard on the solids with the back of a ladle to extract every drop (the strained broth should yield 1.5 to 2 L). Discard the bones and aromatic solids. Return the strained broth to a clean wide pot or Dutch oven. Taste and adjust salt (typically another 2 to 4 g / ½ to 1 tsp). The broth can be made up to 24 hours ahead and refrigerated; bring back to a bare simmer before adding the fish chunks.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'During the broth strain, prepare the accompaniments. Steam or simmer the 600 g sliced waxy potatoes in salted water until tender (10 to 12 minutes); drain and keep warm. Toast the 300 g / 12 to 18 baguette rounds: brush both sides with the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil; toast in a heavy skillet or a 200°C / 400°F oven 4 to 6 minutes per side until deeply golden and crisp. Immediately rub one side of each hot crouton with the cut face of the 8 g / 2 halved garlic cloves; pile on a board next to the soup bowls.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Bring the strained broth to a bare simmer (about 90°C / 195°F, just-trembling, no rolling boil; harder simmer breaks up the fish chunks). Add the 400 g monkfish chunks (the densest fish, longest cook); simmer 4 minutes. Add the 600 g whole-fish steaks (bone-in cross-sections); simmer 2 more minutes (6 minutes total for monkfish). Add the 400 g cod chunks and the 400 g halibut chunks; simmer 4 to 6 more minutes, until all the fish is just-flaky at the center (60°C / 140°F internal for halibut and cod; 60 to 65°C / 140 to 150°F for monkfish; the dense fish-and-bone steaks read done when the flesh just-pulls from the bone). Pull off the heat immediately; the residual heat finishes the last bit of cook.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Plate the fish. With a slotted spoon or spider, lift the fish chunks gently from the broth (the cooked flesh is fragile); arrange on a warm serving platter, alternating the species. Pour 100 ml of the broth over the fish to keep it moist; surround with the steamed potato slices. Sprinkle with the 15 g chopped parsley and the 5 g chopped fennel fronds. Cover loosely with foil to keep warm.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Serve in two courses. First course (the soup): ladle the saffron-orange broth into wide shallow bowls. Place 2 to 3 garlic-rubbed croutons on the side of each bowl with a generous spoonful of rouille on each crouton; diners spread the rouille onto the toasts and float them on the broth, watching the rouille slowly dissolve and color the broth a deeper red-orange. Second course (the fish): present the platter of fish-and-potatoes at the table; let diners help themselves, with more rouille on the side. The juxtaposition is the dish: the broth is the saffron-fennel-orange-tomato concentrate, the fish-and-potato is the textural mild counterpoint, and the rouille is the garlic-saffron-chili bridge that ties the two courses together.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 5

modifications:
  - for: 'shellfish addition (modern restaurant preparation, non-canonical)'
    to: 'with mussels and clams added in the final poach'
    how: 'The Charte de la Bouillabaisse Marseillaise specifically excludes shellfish (the dish is fin-fish-only by tradition; the original fishermen''s preparation skipped crustaceans for cost). Modern restaurant versions outside Marseille often add shellfish; if doing so, add 500 g of cleaned mussels and 500 g of purged littleneck clams to the strained broth at the start of the fish poach (Step 7), tucked into the broth before adding the fish; cover and cook 4 minutes until shells open, then proceed with the fish chunks. The dish moves toward cioppino territory; the canonical Marseille preparation does not include shellfish.'
    kind: 'protein'

Common in modern restaurant menus; loses the textural and visual drama of the two-course canonical service but is faster and uses less fish.'

  Origin and the Charte de la Bouillabaisse. Bouillabaisse was born as a fishermen''s pot-on-the-quay preparation in Marseille and the surrounding Provençal coast in the late 18th and early 19th century, using the unsold rockfish and bony Mediterranean species the market would not buy: rascasse (red scorpionfish), vive (weever), galinette (gurnard), congre (conger eel), and baudroie (monkfish). The fishermen would build a fire on the quay, throw the unsold catch into a pot of seawater with whatever aromatics were available (onion, fennel, garlic, tomato when in season, the saffron that traveled the Mediterranean trade routes from Iran via Spain and the eastern Mediterranean), simmer hard until the broth turned milky from emulsified olive oil and gelatin, then ladle out a working lunch. The dish stabilized in the late 19th century as Marseille developed a restaurant culture; the saffron-fennel-orange-tomato signature crystallized in the early 20th century as the canonical preparation. In 1980 a group of Marseille restaurateurs published the Charte de la Bouillabaisse Marseillaise, a defining standard for the dish: at least four named local Mediterranean fish species (the canonical four are rascasse, vive, galinette, and one other), saffron, fennel, garlic, tomato, orange peel, no shellfish, no potato cooked in the broth (the potatoes are steamed separately and served alongside), the broth strained and served as a separate first course over rouille croutons, and the fish served as the second course on a platter. The Charte exists because Marseille restaurants in the 1970s were watering down bouillabaisse with stock cubes and underselling it to tourists; the document defended the canonical preparation and the price point ($60 to $100 per person at canonical Marseille restaurants today). The version here is the home-cook adaptation of the Charte, with US-market fish substitutions for the rare Mediterranean species that the Charte specifies.

  The broth-strain technique is the dish. The canonical bouillabaisse broth is built from many bony fish heads and frames simmered hard for 30 to 45 minutes; the hard simmer (rolling, not gentle) does two structural things. First, it extracts gelatin from the fish bones and connective tissue, building body and viscosity in the broth. Second, the agitation of the rolling boil emulsifies the olive oil into the gelatinous broth, producing the canonical milky orange-pink color and the silky mouthfeel that distinguishes a real bouillabaisse broth from a clear fumet. After the strain, the broth comes back to a bare simmer for the fish poach; the harsh-simmer-then-strain-then-gentle-poach sequence is the structural technique that defines the dish. Cooks who treat the broth like a French fumet (gentle simmer, clear strain, light body) end up with a thin underwhelming soup; the bouillabaisse broth must be hard-simmered to emulsify.

  The fennel-saffron-orange aromatic signature. Bouillabaisse is structurally distinct from cioppino (its Italian-American descendant) and cacciucco (its Tuscan cousin) in the orange peel. Cioppino uses fennel and saffron but no orange; cacciucco uses red wine and red pepper flakes but neither saffron nor orange; bouillabaisse uses all three (saffron, fennel, orange peel) as the structural defining trio. The orange peel is the Provençal-Mediterranean-North-African crosspollination signature: the citrus oils traveled north from Morocco and Spain along the same Mediterranean trade routes as the saffron, and the combined effect is a broth that reads distinctly Provençal. Cooks who skip the orange peel reproduce a generic fish-saffron soup, not a bouillabaisse.

  The umami architecture. The broth carries three structural umami sources: the bloomed tomato paste in Phase 1 (60 g, the umami-and-color amplifier), the cooked-out San Marzano tomatoes through the 35 to 45 minute simmer (free glutamate from ripe peeled tomato), and the fish bones and heads that release inosinate (the 5''-nucleotide that combines with glutamate to produce the umami synergy effect, the same mechanism behind dashi and Italian Parmesan-and-tomato sauces). Layered on top is the saffron (safranal, picrocrocin), which provides the floral-mineral aromatic, and the fennel and orange peel which contribute structural sweet-bitter aromatics. The finishing acid comes from the wine reduction (Phase 1) and the rouille at service (which carries lemon juice in the emulsion); the salt comes from the broth and the rouille both. Salt-acid-fat-umami are all present in deliberate layers.

  Sourcing the fish. The dish is forgiving of substitutions across the firm-white-fish lineup but the structural rules are: multiple species (the textural variety on the platter is part of the dish), at least one species with prominent bony head-and-frame (snapper, sea bass, branzino, halibut all work well), no oily fish (salmon, mackerel, bluefish, sardines all overwhelm the saffron-fennel signature). H Mart and Asian seafood markets are excellent sources for the bony whole-fish requirement (snapper, sea bass, branzino are routinely sold whole at $8 to $20 per fish). Whole Foods carries monkfish loin, halibut, and cod consistently. Specialty Italian and Portuguese markets carry the bony Mediterranean species (rouget, dorado, john dory) when available. Ask the fishmonger to save fish frames from any fillet you buy; most counters will give them away free of charge or at a token price ($1 to $3 per pound).

  Service notes. Bouillabaisse is a two-course dish presented at the table. First course: ladle the saffron-orange broth into wide shallow bowls; place 2 to 3 garlic-rubbed croutons on the side of each bowl with a generous spoonful of rouille on each crouton; diners spread the rouille on the toast and float the croutons on the broth, watching the rouille dissolve and tint the broth a deeper red-orange; the rouille can also be stirred directly into the broth with a spoon. Second course (10 minutes after the soup): present the platter of fish-and-potatoes at the table with more rouille on the side; let diners help themselves; squeeze a wedge of lemon if the dish reads heavy. The two-course service is the canonical Marseille preparation and is the structural difference from cioppino (single bowl). The leftover broth is excellent the next day over pasta or as a base for risotto al frutti di mare; the leftover fish flakes well into a salad or omelet.

  Pairing. Provençal rosé (Bandol, Côtes de Provence) is the canonical wine; the dry white Provençal wines (Cassis, Picpoul de Pinet, Bandol blanc) work equally well; an unoaked Mediterranean white (Sicilian Grillo, Spanish Albariño) is the next best option. Avoid heavy oaked whites and reds; the saffron-fennel-orange signature is delicate and an aggressive wine overwhelms it.
