steps:
  - text: 'Drain the soaked 200 g / 1 cup black-eyed peas. Combine with the 1200 ml / 5 cups fresh water in a heavy saucepan. Bring to a hard boil, skim any gray foam that rises, then drop to a low simmer. Add 1 g of the fine sea salt (about ¼ tsp). Simmer uncovered 50 to 60 minutes until the peas are completely tender but still hold their shape, they should crush easily against the roof of the mouth but not have burst skins. Do not add sugar yet; sugar competes with starch for water and slows softening.'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'While the peas cook, prepare the rice. Drain the soaked 150 g / ¾ cup glutinous rice. Combine with the 360 ml / 1½ cups water in a small saucepan. Bring to a boil, then drop to the lowest possible heat, cover, and cook 18 minutes. Pull off heat and let stand covered 10 more minutes, the residual steam finishes the centers. The rice should be tender and cohesive but not pasty; individual grains visible.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'When the peas are tender, stir in the 80 g / ½ cup chopped palm sugar, the remaining 2 g salt, and the 2 knotted pandan leaves. Simmer 5 minutes to dissolve the sugar and infuse the pandan. Stir in the cooked sticky rice. Hold over the lowest heat 5 minutes so the rice loosens into the bean liquid and the whole pot reads as a textured warm porridge. Add 60 to 120 ml / ¼ to ½ cup hot water if the consistency is tighter than a loose oatmeal, chè đậu trắng should pour from the ladle in a heavy stream, not flop in clumps.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Coconut sauce. In a small saucepan combine the 480 ml coconut milk, the 15 g / 1 tbsp sugar, and a small pinch salt. Warm over low heat just until the sugar dissolves; do not boil. Whisk in the 5 g tapioca slurry; stir gently 1 minute as it thickens to a pourable cream.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Taste the chè base. Black-eyed pea chè should read mostly bean-and-rice with sweetness as a backdrop, not a foreground. Adjust with a pinch more palm sugar or a small pinch of salt as needed. Fish out the pandan leaves.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Serve warm in shallow bowls. Ladle the bean-and-rice base into each bowl, then pour 3 tbsp of the coconut sauce in a ribbon over the top. Finish with the 5 g toasted sesame and the 20 g chopped peanuts. The fat and crunch of the peanuts is structural; without them the dish reads sleepy.'

notes: |
  Black-eyed peas are functionally a savory bean in most of the world; their use as the base for a sweet pudding is a Vietnamese particularity, and the small amount of sugar relative to bean is the dish's signature. A Western cook tasting this for the first time often reads it as undersweet, that perception is the cultural calibration to dial back, not the recipe. The pleasure is in the textural play of soft bean against chewy rice in a coconut-warmed liquid, not in pure sweetness.

  The baking soda in the soak (a pinch, 1 g / ¼ tsp per cup of beans) softens the bean skins and shortens cook time by about 15 minutes. Too much and the skins disintegrate and the beans turn mushy; the small pinch is the right balance. Some cooks skip it; the trade-off is longer cooking and slightly tougher skins.

  Adding the sugar after the beans are fully tender, not during cooking, is essential. Sugar binds water and slows starch gelatinization; beans cooked in sweetened water never reach the proper soft texture. This is true for all bean-based desserts, adzuki, mung, black-eyed pea, and is the most common home mistake.
