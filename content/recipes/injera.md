source:
  note: 'Real injera is teff flour, water, and time. The wild yeasts and lactic acid bacteria on the teff kernel surface drive a vigorous spontaneous fermentation that produces the sour tang and the CO2 bubbles that become the characteristic eyes on the cooked surface. Restaurant injera in the West often uses a teff-and-wheat blend for cost (wheat is cheaper and produces a paler more even crumb); pure teff injera is the traditional version and is naturally gluten-free. Some recipes call for adding a small amount of yeast or sourdough starter to accelerate or insure the ferment; the canonical method is wild ferment with no additions, and that is what this recipe documents.'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 2 to 3 day project. The teff ferment needs 48 to 72 hours at warm room temperature (22 to 26 °C) to develop the sour tang and the bubble structure that produces real injera. Plan the timeline backward from the meal: start the ferment Friday morning to cook injera Sunday lunch, or Wednesday evening to cook Saturday lunch. There is no honest shortcut.'
  - 'The ferment vessel needs to be loosely covered, not sealed. A glass jar with a clean kitchen towel rubber-banded over the top is the standard setup; a sealed container traps too much CO2 and the ferment goes sluggish.'
  - 'The cook surface matters. A traditional mitad is a large clay or non-stick electric plate; at home, a 25 to 28 cm non-stick crepe pan or seasoned cast iron pan works, kept at a moderate-medium heat. Never oil the surface; injera cooks dry, and any oil makes it slide instead of forming the bubbly eyes.'
  - 'The first one or two injera off the pan are often duds (the pan is finding its temperature, the batter is finding its consistency). Plan to discard or eat the first 1 to 2 as cook''s tribute and the rest will be uniform.'

steps:
  - text: 'Day 1, build the ferment. In a 2-liter glass jar or wide ceramic bowl, whisk the 400 g / 3¼ cups teff flour with the 600 ml / 2½ cups cool filtered water until completely smooth, no dry lumps. The batter should be the consistency of heavy cream. Cover the jar loosely with a clean kitchen towel held in place with a rubber band (not a lid, the ferment needs to release CO2). Place in a warm spot in the kitchen, ideally 22 to 26 °C. Leave undisturbed.'
    technique: 'wild-ferment'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Day 2, check the ferment. After 24 hours, lift the towel. The batter should show visible bubbles at the surface, a faintly sour aroma, and a thin layer of clear-to-yellowish liquid floating on top (this is the "absit", the natural separated whey of the ferment). Do not stir or pour it off; it is part of the structure. The surface should look slightly puffy and the smell should be tangy-malty, not unpleasant. If there is no visible activity at all after 24 hours, the kitchen is too cold; move the jar to a warmer spot (on top of the refrigerator, near a gas-pilot oven, in a turned-off oven with the light on).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Day 3, finish the ferment. After 48 to 72 hours total (the longer ferment produces a more pronounced sour tang and is the traditional Ethiopian preference), the batter should be visibly bubbly, distinctly sour-smelling, with a thicker clear liquid layer on top. The texture under the liquid will have thickened to a paste. Whisk in 200 ml / ¾ cup additional water (or enough to bring the batter back to a heavy cream consistency, like buttermilk pancake batter), then whisk in the 4 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt. Let the salted batter rest 15 minutes; the salt slows the ferment to a near-halt and the batter develops a slightly thicker more cohesive texture that is easier to cook.'
    time_min: 17
  - text: 'Heat a 25 to 28 cm non-stick crepe pan or well-seasoned cast iron pan over medium heat for 4 to 5 minutes. Test the temperature with a few drops of water; they should skitter and evaporate within 2 to 3 seconds. Too hot and the bottom burns before the top sets; too cold and no bubbles form. Adjust to medium-low if the water flashes instantly, or medium-high if it sits and slowly steams off.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Pour 90 to 110 g / about ⅓ cup of the salted batter into the center of the hot pan in a slow steady stream, then immediately tilt and rotate the pan in a quick spiral motion to spread the batter into a thin even circle reaching the edges of the pan. The spread happens fast (5 to 8 seconds) before the batter sets; if you wait, you cannot move it. Set the pan flat on the heat and cover with a fitted lid.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Cook covered for 90 to 120 seconds. The injera cooks only on the bottom; the top sets from steam. As it cooks, watch for the characteristic eyes (small bubbles) forming across the surface from the CO2 in the fermented batter. When the top surface has fully set from glossy-wet to matte-dry and the edges begin to lift slightly from the pan, lift the lid. Do not flip; injera cooks only on one side. The bottom should be smooth and pale-tan, the top should be bubbly-eyed and dry to the touch.'
    technique: 'one-side-cook'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Slide the injera onto a clean dry kitchen towel or onto a wire rack. Do not stack them while hot; they will steam-stick together. Let each one cool for 2 to 3 minutes before stacking on a serving platter or transferring to a beeswax wrap for storage. Continue cooking the rest of the batter, adjusting heat as needed; injera 3 through 8 will cook more uniformly than the first 2 as the pan settles into rhythm.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Serve as the platter base for any combination of wats: doro wat, misir wat, gomen (greens), kik alicha (yellow split pea), shiro (chickpea flour). Tear pieces from the platter injera to scoop up the stews. Additional folded injera goes alongside as extra scooping bread. Eaten with the right hand, no utensils.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why the ferment matters. Two to three days of wild fermentation at warm room temperature develop both the sour tang (from lactic acid bacteria producing lactic and acetic acid) and the CO2 bubble structure (from wild yeasts) that together define injera. Skipping or shortening the ferment produces a flat teff pancake without the eyes or the sour depth; using a different grain produces a different bread entirely. The 48 to 72 hour ferment is the work of the recipe.

  Why one-side cooking. Injera is cooked only on one side, covered, so the bottom develops the smooth pale crust while the top sets from steam and shows the characteristic bubbles (eyes) that came from the ferment. Flipping it would close the eyes and crisp the bubbled side, which is not the texture of injera. The covered cook is what lets the top set without browning.

  Why no oil on the pan. Oil prevents the injera from forming the bubbled-eye structure; the batter slides instead of setting in place, and the bubbles cannot rise. The pan must be dry. A well-seasoned cast iron or non-stick crepe pan is what makes this work.

  Sourdough starter for ongoing batches. Reserve 50 to 100 g of the fermented day-3 batter before salting and adding water. This becomes the sourdough starter for the next batch; using it accelerates the next ferment to 18 to 24 hours instead of 48 to 72, and the lactic culture builds character over multiple batches. Refrigerate the reserved starter between batches; feed weekly with 50 g teff flour and 75 g water if not used.

  Storage. Cooked injera holds 1 day at room temperature wrapped in a beeswax wrap or clean towel, 3 to 4 days refrigerated in a sealed container, 1 month frozen. Reheat briefly in a warm pan or in a 150 °C oven wrapped in foil; do not microwave or it goes rubbery.

  Troubleshooting. Flat injera with no eyes: ferment was too short, or pan was too cold. Burned bottom: pan was too hot. Sticky top that will not dry: pan was too cold or lid was off. Sour smell overpowering: ferment went past 72 hours; add more salt next time, ferment less. Mild dough-y smell with no sour: ferment was too cold; warmer spot needed.
