source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Cantonese (Yue) steamed-fish canon, central to the southern Chinese seafood tradition and a fixture of every Cantonese banquet menu.'
  note: 'The dish reads as restraint but executes on three precise transitions: cure the fish briefly to firm the surface and pull surface water, steam at full vapor to set the protein without collapsing the texture, then deploy aromatics in three thermal registers (raw chiffonade, warm seasoning sauce, smoking oil bloom). Each register extracts a different fraction of the ginger-scallion aromatic compounds. The wrong register at any stage flattens the dish.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Start jasmine rice FIRST as a parallel track. The fish steams 8 to 12 minutes and plates within 60 seconds of coming out of the steamer; the rice (12 minute simmer plus 10 minute rest) must be finished by the moment the smoking oil hits the aromatics or the dish goes cold waiting.'
  - 'Set up a steamer that fits a 700 g whole fish flat on a heatproof plate. A wok with a bamboo steamer or a deep skillet with a wire rack both work; what does not work is a small saucepan that forces you to bend or break the fish. Verify clearance with the empty plate before the fish goes anywhere near it.'
  - 'Stage the three aromatic registers BEFORE the fish hits the steam: raw ginger-scallion julienne in a bowl by the plate, soy-sesame seasoning warmed in a small pot, neutral oil ready in a small saucepan to be pushed to smoking the moment the steamer comes off heat. The oil-pour finish is a 5 second move; fumbling with the julienne mid-pour means cold oil and no aromatic bloom.'

steps:
  - text: 'Examine the 700 g / 1 whole fish. The fishmonger should have gutted, scaled, and removed the gills; verify all three. Rinse the cavity under cold running water; scrape any dark blood line along the inside of the spine cavity with the back of a small spoon (the blood line carries the strongest fishy flavor and is the single biggest source of "fishy fish" complaints). Pat the fish completely dry inside and out with paper towels. Wet fish steams unevenly and the seasoning sauce gets diluted on the plate; dry is non-negotiable.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'With a sharp paring knife, score three diagonal slashes 1 cm deep into the flesh on each side of the fish, from the gill plate to the tail. The slashes do three things: increase surface area for the brief 4 g / 1 tsp kosher-salt cure that follows, guarantee the heat penetrates evenly to the spine in the steaming window, and provide a visual cue for doneness (the flesh in the slashes opaques first). Without slashes, the thickest part of the fillet stays translucent at the spine while the thinner belly overcooks.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Rub the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt over the skin on both sides and inside the cavity, working the salt into the slashes. Splash the 15 g / 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine over the same surfaces. The brief cure (about 10 minutes resting while the steamer comes up) firms the surface flesh through partial protein denaturation, draws out a small amount of surface water, and lets the wine''s aromatic esters perfume the fish. Stuff the cavity loosely with the 15 g / sliced ginger coins (from the 25 g total knob) and the 20 g / 2 bruised scallion stalks; the aromatics deodorize the cavity from the inside during the steam.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Set up the steamer. The traditional setup is a 30 cm bamboo steamer over a wok with 3 cm of water, but a deep skillet or wok with a fitted rack and lid works (the water needs to be 2 to 3 cm below the fish plate). Bring the steaming water to a hard rolling boil over high heat; this is critical, the steaming temperature is the hard rolling boil of water (100 °C with full vapor flux, not gentle simmer). A bamboo steamer is preferred because the wood absorbs surface condensation and prevents drips of cool water onto the fish; a metal lid wrapped in a clean kitchen towel achieves the same effect.'
    technique: 'vapor-steaming'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Choose a heatproof rimmed plate or shallow dish that fits inside the steamer with at least 2 cm of clearance on every side for vapor circulation. Lay 2 to 3 chopsticks or two short scallion-top batons across the plate as a low rack; the gap underneath the fish lets vapor circulate around the bottom of the fish and keeps the bottom skin from going gummy in pooled liquid. Lay the cured fish across the chopsticks. Reserve any liquid that has pooled around the fish during the cure; discard it (this is fishy and not part of the dish).'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'When the steaming water is at a hard rolling boil, place the plate of fish in the steamer, cover, and steam at full heat. Time by thickness at the thickest part: 8 minutes for a fish 2.5 cm thick at the spine, 10 minutes for a fish 3 cm thick, 12 minutes for a 3.5 cm fish, 14 minutes for a 4 cm fish. The 700 g whole branzino in this recipe is typically 3 cm at the thickest and needs 10 minutes; check at 9 by lifting the lid (carefully, vapor escapes upward) and inserting a chopstick into the deepest slash near the spine. The flesh should be opaque and lift cleanly from the bone; if it still has a translucent core, replace the lid and continue 90 seconds at a time. The single most common failure mode is understeaming because the cook fears overcooking; the steam window is wider than it feels and a fish at 11 minutes is still good, while one at 7 minutes is raw at the spine.'
    technique: 'vapor-steaming'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'While the fish steams, build the soy seasoning sauce. In a small saucepan, combine the 30 g / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, the 30 g / 2 tbsp hot water, the 4 g / ¾ tsp crushed rock sugar, and the 0.5 g / ¼ tsp white pepper. Warm over low heat until the sugar dissolves (1 to 2 minutes); pull off heat. Stir in the 4 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil; the sesame oil goes in off-heat to preserve its volatile aromatic compounds (heating sesame oil destroys the toast notes). The seasoning sauce should taste pronouncedly salty-sweet with a faint sesame-pepper depth; this seasons the fish from the outside.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Prepare the raw aromatics for finishing. Cut the reserved 10 g / from the original 25 g knob ginger into the finest julienne possible (3 cm long matchsticks; the finer the julienne, the better the hot-oil bloom). Cut the 30 g / 4 stalks scallion whites and pale greens into 5 cm batons and julienne lengthwise into very thin slivers. Drop the scallion julienne into a small bowl of ice water; within 5 to 10 minutes the slivers will curl into elegant white-and-pale-green ribbons (the cell walls on the cut surface contract differently from the intact surface in cold water, producing the curl). Pick the 8 g / ¼ cup cilantro leaves; tear roughly. The aromatic stage is loaded and ready before the fish comes out.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'When the fish is done, pull the steamer off the heat and uncover. Carefully transfer the plate to a heatproof surface; a spatula in each hand under opposite ends of the plate works. Tilt the plate slightly and pour off all of the liquid that has pooled on the plate; this liquid is full of fishy proteins released during steaming and is the single biggest contributor to a finished dish that tastes "off." Discard the liquid. Discard the bruised aromatics from the cavity (their job is done); the cavity stays empty for the finished plating.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour the warm soy seasoning sauce around (not directly over) the fish; the sauce should pool around the fish and gently flow under the slashes, seasoning the flesh from the bottom and the cut surfaces. Lift the curled 30 g / 4 stalks scallion slivers out of the ice water with a strainer, shake dry briefly, and scatter them across the top of the fish along the spine line. Scatter the ginger julienne over the scallions in a tight strip down the center of the fish (the visual structure is a strip, not a scattering). Scatter the cilantro leaves loosely over the platter as a final green note.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a small saucepan over medium-high until just smoking, about 200 °C / 390 °F. The visual cue: a wooden chopstick dipped in produces vigorous bubbles around it within a second, the surface shimmers aggressively, and the first wisp of pale smoke rises. The oil must be at smoking temperature; cooler oil produces a soft sad pour that does not bloom the aromatics, and the dish reads as having "raw scallions on top of cooked fish" rather than as a unified plate. Pull off heat the moment smoke first appears.'
    technique: 'hot-oil-finish'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'In a single steady pour from a height of 5 to 10 cm, pour the smoking-hot oil directly over the strip of 10 g ginger and 30 g scallion julienne on top of the fish. The aromatics will sizzle violently for 5 to 10 seconds, releasing a cloud of fragrant aromatic vapor (this is the volatile sulfur and allium compounds from the raw aromatics flashing into the air; the kitchen will smell intensely of ginger and scallion within 3 seconds of the pour). The aromatics on top of the fish will brown slightly at the edges where the oil pooled, stay green-and-pale at the centers, and visibly cook in place. The hot oil also crisps the cilantro leaves at the edges and pulls the flavors of all the aromatics down into the fish flesh through the slashes. Serve immediately, with steamed jasmine rice; each diner takes a piece of fish with chopsticks, spooning a little of the seasoned-oil-and-soy from the plate over the rice.'
    technique: 'hot-oil-finish'
    time_min: 1

The dish becomes "steamed fish with black bean sauce" (chi zhi zheng yu); the black bean adds funky umami depth that pairs particularly well with oilier fish (mackerel, butterfish). The hot-oil finish step is preserved.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'cut'
    to: 'with thick fish fillets instead of whole fish'
    how: 'Replace the whole fish with 2 fillets of black cod, halibut, or sea bass (each 200 to 250 g, skin on, ~3 cm thick). Skip the cure step (the fillets cure in 5 minutes with the same salt and Shaoxing rub). Steam 7 to 9 minutes by thickness. The dish reads more weeknight-friendly and works for households without access to whole fish, though the visual drama and the cavity-aromatic step are gone.'

notes: |
  Why whole fish and not fillets. The whole-fish presentation is structurally the dish; the bone, skin, and head contribute to the flavor of the steaming juices and the visual register. The collagen along the spine releases gelatin into the steaming liquid, which is why the seasoning sauce that pools on the plate has body. The cheek meat (a small lobe of dense flesh just behind the eye) and the belly meat (the most fatty part) read differently from the back fillet, and a Cantonese diner appreciates all three textures from a single fish. A fillet version skips this; the dish becomes a different and slightly less interesting preparation.

  The umami source is the light soy sauce in the seasoning sauce (the soy contributes glutamate and trace nucleotides) plus the natural glutamate in the fish itself (released during the steam); the finishing acid is technically absent in the canonical preparation but the rock sugar plus the white pepper provide the balancing sensations. Cantonese steamed fish is one of the few dishes that does not have an explicit acid finish; the soy provides the seasoning anchor and the dish is plated immediately so the fresh-aromatic top notes carry. A squeeze of lime at the table is acceptable in a non-canonical home version.

  Why the hot-oil finish matters. The pour of smoking oil over the raw ginger-scallion julienne is the technique that distinguishes this preparation from "steamed fish with raw aromatics on top." The 200 °C oil flash-cooks the surface of the aromatics in 3 to 5 seconds, releasing the volatile aromatic compounds (allyl sulfides from the scallion, gingerols and shogaols from the ginger) into the air around the dish and depositing the cooked-but-still-textured aromatics back onto the fish. The hot-oil bloom also crisps the cilantro at the edges and pulls all the aromatic flavors down into the fish through the slashes. Cool oil produces a sad raw-scallion-on-fish dish; smoking-hot oil produces the canonical aromatic bloom that defines Cantonese steamed fish. The 30 seconds of saucepan heating are the most important 30 seconds in the recipe.

  The water management is the subtle technique. The fish releases 30 to 50 g of liquid during the steam (a combination of cell water from the muscle, fat that renders out, and condensation from the bamboo lid). This liquid is fishy, full of denatured proteins, and dilutes any seasoning that follows. Pouring it off completely before the seasoning sauce goes on is the difference between a dish that tastes clean and one that tastes "fishy." Some restaurants pour the steaming liquid into a separate bowl and serve it as a small consommé alongside the rice; a home cook can do the same if the fish was very fresh, or discard it if there is any doubt.

  The fish-doneness chemistry. White fish flesh is structured around very short collagen fibers separating muscle blocks; the fibers gelatinize at 55 to 60 °C and the flesh transitions from translucent-firm to opaque-flaky. The 100 °C steam temperature pushes the surface flesh past 90 °C within 2 minutes and gradually heats the spine to 60 °C over 8 to 10 minutes for a 3 cm fish. Past 12 minutes the spine flesh dries out, the muscle blocks separate too aggressively, and the fish reads as overcooked. The visual cue for doneness is opaque flesh that lifts cleanly from the bone with gentle pressure; the texture cue is flaky-but-moist (not chalky-dry). A thermometer at the spine reads 60 to 63 °C at the moment of doneness; serving temperature is irrelevant because the dish is served at full steaming heat.

  Sourcing the whole fish. H Mart, 99 Ranch, Whole Foods, and most Asian seafood markets carry whole branzino, black sea bass, snapper, and porgy in the 600 to 900 g size range that fits a single steaming plate. Ask the fishmonger to gut, scale, and remove the gills; verify all three when you get home. Fresh whole fish should have clear (not cloudy) eyes, bright red gills (not gray-brown), tight scales (not flaking), no slime on the skin (a thin sheen is normal, gummy slime is not), and a clean ocean smell (no ammonia, no funk). The fish should be served the day of purchase or, at the latest, the day after; whole fish on ice keeps less well than fillets because the gut cavity is exposed to bacteria.
