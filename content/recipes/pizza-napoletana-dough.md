source:
  name: 'Neapolitan canon (Associazione Verace Pizza Napoletana standard, AVPN 2008)'
  note: 'The Associazione Verace Pizza Napoletana publishes a written standard for canonical Neapolitan pizza: 00 flour (Caputo Pizzeria or equivalent W250 to W320 strength), water, sea salt, fresh or instant yeast (1 percent or less), hydration 55 to 62 percent (this recipe uses 62 to 65 percent for a home oven that does not reach 450°C, the slightly higher hydration compensates for the slower bake), bulk fermentation followed by individual ball ferment, hand-shaping only (no rolling pin, the pin compresses the airy cornicione the dough develops), and a 60 to 90 second bake at 450 to 485°C in a wood-fired oven. The home-oven adaptation here pushes the cold ferment longer (24 to 48 hours instead of the AVPN-standard 8 hours) to develop more flavor and lighter texture, since the home bake takes 5 to 8 minutes at 280 to 300°C instead of the 60-second wood-fired bake.'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 2 to 3 day project. The cold ferment is 24 to 48 hours at 4°C / 39°F (refrigerator); do not shortcut. The long cold ferment is the entire technique; a same-day pizza dough is a different product (closer to a focaccia or a sourdough).'
  - 'Get a pizza steel or stone heated to maximum oven temperature (260 to 290°C / 500 to 550°F) for at least 45 minutes before baking; the bake depends on a screaming hot surface to set the crust before the moisture evaporates. A cold or under-heated surface produces a soggy bottom and pale crust.'
  - 'Mix the dough by hand (not stand mixer) for the canonical texture. Stand mixers over-develop gluten in this recipe; the high-hydration dough builds structure through fermentation, not kneading.'
  - 'Plan toppings ahead. The bake is 5 to 8 minutes; toppings must be ready when the oven hits temperature. Classical margherita: 60 g San Marzano sauce (hand-crushed, salted only), 80 g fior di latte (cow-milk fresh mozzarella, drained and torn), 5 to 6 fresh basil leaves, 5 ml extra-virgin olive oil drizzle.'

steps:
  - text: 'In a large mixing bowl, combine the 390 g / 1¾ cups cool water and the 1 g / ¼ tsp instant yeast. Stir gently with a wooden spoon for 10 seconds to dissolve the yeast. Add the 18 g / 3 tsp salt; stir until dissolved. The order matters: yeast first into the cool water (so the cells hydrate without salt contact, which slows yeast activity), then salt; reversed order delays yeast activation noticeably.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 600 g / 4½ cups 00 flour all at once. Mix with a wooden spoon for 60 seconds until a shaggy ragged dough forms, then switch to one hand: scoop the dough from the bottom of the bowl, fold over the top, rotate the bowl a quarter turn, and repeat for 3 to 4 minutes. The dough at this stage is sticky and rough; do not add more flour. The hydration is structural, and the surface will smooth out during fermentation.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Cover the bowl with plastic wrap pressed directly against the dough surface (no air gap; surface drying creates a skin). Bulk-ferment at room temperature (20 to 22°C / 68 to 72°F) for 60 minutes. Every 20 minutes during this hour, perform a stretch-and-fold: with a wet hand, grab the dough at one side, stretch it upward, and fold it over the center; rotate the bowl 90 degrees and repeat 4 times total. Three rounds of stretch-and-fold over the hour. The dough firms up visibly between rounds; this is the gluten developing through extension rather than kneading.'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'After the 60-minute bulk ferment, scrape the dough onto a lightly floured (use some of the 50 g reserved flour) work surface. Divide into 4 equal portions (250 g each, a digital scale is essential). Pre-shape each portion into a tight ball: tuck the edges underneath, then roll the ball on the bench with cupped hands to tighten the surface tension into a smooth taut sphere. The skin should be smooth and the ball should hold its shape.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Transfer each dough ball to a small lightly-oiled glass or plastic container with a tight lid (about 500 ml / 16 oz each; the dough doubles, so the container must have headspace). Cover tightly. Refrigerate at 4°C / 39°F for 24 to 48 hours. The cold ferment is the entire technique: enzymatic action breaks starches into sugars, slow yeast activity builds complex aromatic byproducts, and the gluten relaxes without mechanical work. 24 hours produces a clean light dough; 48 hours produces deeper flavor with more visible bubble structure. Do not exceed 72 hours, the dough begins to over-ferment and acidic alcohols accumulate.'
    time_min: 2880
  - text: 'On baking day, remove the dough balls from the refrigerator 2 hours before you want to bake. Let them warm at room temperature (20 to 22°C) covered loosely with damp kitchen towels (not plastic, the dough needs to breathe); the dough should approximately double from its cold-pulled volume. Place a pizza steel (preferred) or pizza stone on the middle rack of the oven; preheat to maximum (260 to 290°C / 500 to 550°F) for at least 45 minutes. Switch to broiler-on for the last 10 minutes of preheat to push the surface above the standard max.'
    time_min: 120
  - text: 'To shape: flour the bench with the reserved 00 flour. Tip one dough ball onto the flour. With fingertips (no rolling pin, ever; the pin destroys the airy cornicione), gently press the center flat in concentric outward circles, leaving a 1.5 cm thick raised rim around the edge. Pick up the disc and gently stretch it over the backs of your knuckles, rotating the disc to thin the center to about 3 mm while keeping the rim intact. Target diameter: 28 cm / 11 in. The dough should be thin enough to see light through the center when held up; the rim stays puffy.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Transfer the shaped dough to a lightly floured pizza peel (semolina is the canonical bench dust, it acts as ball bearings to slide the pizza off the peel). Top quickly: 60 g hand-crushed San Marzano sauce (salted with a pinch of salt, no garlic or herbs), 80 g torn fior di latte fresh mozzarella drained on paper towels (wet mozzarella floods the pizza), 5 ml olive oil drizzled in a spiral. The whole topping operation should take 30 seconds; the dough begins to absorb water from the sauce immediately.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Slide the pizza onto the hot steel with a sharp forward-then-back motion of the peel (the semolina lets it release cleanly). Switch the oven to broil-on. Bake 4 to 7 minutes, watching closely (the steel-and-broiler combination produces an effective 320 to 350°C / 600 to 660°F top heat). The pizza is done when the rim has puffed dramatically with visible large air pockets, the cornicione has charred dark brown to nearly-black leopard spots, the cheese has melted and bubbled with a few golden spots, and the bottom is firm and crisp (lift an edge with tongs to check). Pull immediately; the leopard-char window is narrow.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Slide the finished pizza onto a wooden cutting board. Tear 5 to 6 fresh basil leaves over the top while the pizza is still hot (the residual heat releases the basil oils without cooking them to black). Cut into 6 wedges with a wheel cutter or scissors. Serve immediately, in two-minute waves to all diners; Neapolitan pizza is structurally a hot-from-the-oven food and degrades within 5 minutes of plating.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The hydration is the single most variable parameter in pizza dough, and 65 percent is on the high end for a beginner home cook. Beginners should start at 60 percent (360 g water to 600 g flour) and work up over multiple bakes as the handling becomes intuitive; the 65 percent dough is sticky and demands confident wet-hand technique. The reward is a lighter airier crumb and a more dramatic cornicione puff in the bake.

  The cold ferment is irreplaceable. The 24 to 48 hour refrigerated rest is doing structural work: amylase enzymes in the flour convert starch to maltose (the sugar that browns the crust), proteolytic enzymes break long gluten chains into shorter more extensible strands (the dough becomes easier to stretch), and yeast metabolism slows to a rate that produces complex organic acids and esters (flavor compounds) without exhausting the available sugars. A same-day dough has none of these benefits; it is a different bread.

  The hand-shape rule is non-negotiable. The rolling pin compresses the gas pockets that the cold ferment has built into the dough; a pin-rolled pizza will not puff into a cornicione at the bake. Finger-press the center, then knuckle-stretch the perimeter; the rim must remain undisturbed. Cooks new to the technique often default to the pin out of insecurity; commit to the hand-shape from the first attempt.

  The steel-and-broiler combination is the home-oven workaround for the 450°C wood-fired oven the dish was designed for. The pizza steel (Baking Steel brand or a 1/4 inch thick A36 mild steel plate cut to 14 by 16 inches) has 7 to 10 times the thermal conductivity of a pizza stone; preheated at maximum oven temperature with the broiler running for the last 10 minutes, the steel surface reaches 300 to 320°C and delivers the canonical leopard char in 5 to 7 minutes. A pizza stone produces a similar but slower bake (8 to 10 minutes) and the leopard char is less dramatic. Neither matches a wood-fired oven, but the steel comes the closest.

  Storage. The cold-ferment dough balls keep 24 to 72 hours in the refrigerator; pull and use within this window. Frozen dough balls (sealed tightly, wrapped in plastic) keep 30 days at -18°C / 0°F; thaw overnight in the refrigerator and bring to room temperature 2 hours before baking. The texture of frozen-then-thawed dough is acceptable but reads denser than fresh-fermented; the wild yeast and enzymatic balance shifts during the freeze.
