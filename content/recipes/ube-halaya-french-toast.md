before_you_start:
  - 'Use 2 to 3 day-old brioche, not fresh. Fresh bread is too soft and saturates to mush before the custard fully sets the interior. Brioche left uncovered for 48 hr on the counter (or sliced and dried in a 95 °C / 200 °F oven for 15 minutes) has lost enough surface moisture to take on custard without falling apart.'
  - 'Pull the ube halaya from the fridge 15 minutes before starting. Cold jam will not whisk smoothly into the custard and you will get streaks of unincorporated paste that block even absorption.'
  - 'Toast the desiccated coconut at the very start and tip it onto a cold plate. Pan carryover takes toasted coconut from gold to bitter in under 60 seconds, and it is the finishing aromatic; do not toast at the end with a hot pan.'

steps:
  - text: 'Toast 6 g / 1 tbsp desiccated coconut in a small dry stainless skillet over medium-low heat, shaking constantly, until the shreds turn uniform pale golden and smell intensely roasted-nutty, about 90 seconds. Tip immediately onto a cold plate; the pan carry-over takes it from gold to bitter in under 60 seconds.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Whisk 3 large eggs (about 150 g) in a wide shallow bowl until the white and yolk are fully homogenized and the mixture is uniform yellow with no streaks. Add 150 ml / ⅔ cup whole milk, 60 g / ¼ cup heavy cream, 100 g / scant ½ cup room-temperature ube halaya, 4 ml / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste, and 1.5 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt. Whisk vigorously for 60 seconds until the halaya is fully dispersed and the custard is a uniform muted violet with no visible jam lumps. A wide shallow bowl matters here; you need to dip a 2.5 cm thick slice and have the custard cover the surface, not just sit at the bottom.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Lay the 4 brioche slices (about 240 g total) into the custard in a single layer. Press gently with your fingertips to encourage absorption on the down face. After 30 seconds, flip each slice. After another 30 seconds, lift one corner of a slice and tilt; the custard should have penetrated about halfway through the thickness. If the slice feels saturated all the way through and droops heavily, the bread was too fresh or sat too long; pull immediately. Transfer the dipped slices to a plate while you heat the pan; do not let them sit in the custard for more than 90 seconds total.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Set a wide nonstick or well-seasoned cast-iron skillet over medium-low heat (one notch lower than classic French toast). Add 14 g / 1 tbsp unsalted butter and let it melt completely. When the foam subsides but the butter has not yet darkened toward amber, the pan is at roughly 160 °C / 320 °F. This is below the threshold for visible Maillard browning; you want a gentle gold on the surface, not the deep mahogany of classic French toast, because aggressive browning will read brown over the violet custard interior and visually muddy the dish.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Lay 2 dipped slices into the pan with the more saturated face down. Cook undisturbed for 2½ to 3 minutes; lift one corner periodically with a thin spatula and check the underside. The target is a uniform pale gold with darker spots where halaya pooled, not deep brown. Flip and cook the second side for 2 minutes, pressing the center gently with the spatula to confirm the custard has set (it should spring back firmly, not jiggle). Transfer to a wire rack set over a sheet pan in a 95 °C / 200 °F oven. Wipe the pan, add 14 g / 1 tbsp fresh butter, and cook the remaining 2 slices the same way.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Plate 2 slices per serving, fanned. Dollop 30 g / 2 tbsp of the chilled coconut cream over the slices, then drizzle the warmed 30 g / 2 tbsp ube halaya in thin ribbons across the cream. Shower the toasted desiccated coconut over the whole plate. Skip the maple syrup; the halaya in the custard plus the halaya drizzle on top already deliver 50+ g of sugar across the plate, and the coconut cream is the canonical Filipino sweet-breakfast finish that reads cleaner than maple against the violet.'
    time_min: 1

modifications:
  - for: 'occasion'
    to: 'ube halaya French toast bake (oven, serves 6)'
    how: 'Triple the custard (9 eggs, 450 ml whole milk, 180 g heavy cream, 300 g ube halaya, 12 ml vanilla, 4.5 g salt). Cube 720 g brioche into 2.5 cm pieces, toss in a 23 x 33 cm / 9 x 13 in buttered baking dish, pour the custard over, and press the cubes down to submerge. Cover and refrigerate 4 hr or overnight. Bake at 175 °C / 350 °F for 40 to 45 minutes until the surface is set and pale gold and the interior reads 80 °C / 175 °F on an instant-read thermometer. Same plating finish.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'texture'
    to: 'crispier, more caramelized exterior'
    how: 'After the basic cook, dust the cooked slices very lightly with granulated sugar (about 2 g per slice) and run a kitchen torch over the surface for 5 to 10 seconds until the sugar bubbles and sets to a crackle. This brings the surface contrast back without forcing the pan temperature higher (which would muddy the violet). The torch is the right tool here because it caramelizes only the surface sugar without re-cooking the custard interior.'
    kind: 'texture'

notes: |
  Why halaya goes IN the custard and as a drizzle on top, not just as a drizzle. A drizzle alone gives you classic French toast with a violet sauce on top; the interior is still plain custard. Whisking 100 g of halaya into the custard itself turns the entire interior violet, sweetened, and yam-flavored, so every bite carries the headline. The drizzle on top is reinforcement and a visual cue for what to expect inside, not the primary delivery mechanism.

  Why the custard ratio is 3 eggs to 210 g dairy plus 100 g halaya. The classical French toast ratio is roughly 1 egg per 60 g dairy, which sets a firm but not eggy custard. Adding halaya shifts the math: halaya is about 35 to 45% sugar and 8 to 14% fat by weight, so the 100 g of halaya contributes roughly 38 g of sugar and 11 g of fat to the custard. That sugar slightly destabilizes the egg gel (sugar interferes with protein coagulation), so the recipe uses 3 eggs instead of the proportional 2½ to recover the set firmness. The result tastes set, not raw or soggy, with the halaya structurally bound into the egg gel rather than just dispersed.

  Why pan temperature one notch below classic. Anthocyanin pigment degrades above 80 °C in dry heat and the violet starts to fade. The custard interior is fine because the surface insulates it (the interior reads about 80 °C when the surface reads 160 °C), but if you crank the pan to 180 °C for classic mahogany browning, you also push the surface anthocyanin past its stable zone and the cooked slices read brown-grey instead of pale-violet-with-gold. The lower pan temperature keeps the violet readable through the surface gold; you sacrifice the deepest caramelized notes of classic French toast but preserve the dish identity.

  Why no maple syrup. Same reasoning as the pancakes: maple is the default North American breakfast finish but its woody-caramel aromatics fight ube, and the brown sauce visually muddies the violet. Coconut cream and toasted desiccated coconut are the canonical Filipino sweet-breakfast pairing and read cleaner across the plate. If you want a thinner sauce element, a kalamansi or lime squeeze across the slices before plating works well; the acid brightens the halaya the way it does in a *halo-halo* finish.
