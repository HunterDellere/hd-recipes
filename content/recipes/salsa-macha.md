source:
  name: 'The Veracruzan canon (the eastern Mexican Gulf coast state, with roots in the Orizaba and Córdoba highlands and in the Mexican-Lebanese diaspora of the late 19th century, which is the structural reason salsa macha shares architectural DNA with the chili oils of the broader Levant). Documented in the modern stabilized form (dried chilies, garlic, nuts, seeds, fried in neutral oil, ground to a coarse paste, balanced with vinegar) across late 20th-century Mexican cookbooks; the dish has become an international phenomenon over the past decade through restaurants like Pujol (Mexico City) and through commercial brands.'
  note: 'Salsa macha is structurally distinct from the broader Mexican salsa tradition (raw or roasted-tomato-based pourable sauces) because it is oil-based rather than water-based; this gives it the cross-cuisine flexibility that the water-based salsas lack (an oil-based condiment travels onto applications where a water-based one would dilute). The traditional Veracruzan preparation is mortar-ground and oily; the modern food-processor preparation is faster and produces a slightly more uniform texture. Both are correct. The dish varies meaningfully by household: chili blend (morita-and-árbol is the most common modern reference; pure ancho or pasilla produces a milder smokier version), nut blend (peanut-only, almond-only, mixed; some versions use sunflower seeds), and acid (apple cider vinegar is the most common modern reference; some use white vinegar, others use lime juice off-heat).'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the 250 g / 1 cup plus 2 tbsp neutral oil in a medium saucepan over medium-low heat until just shimmering and 130 to 150°C / 270 to 300°F (a sesame seed dropped in should sizzle gently and dance, not burst; if the oil is too hot the chilies and aromatics burn instantly and the salsa goes bitter). The oil temperature control is the single most important variable in salsa macha; warm oil produces deep clean flavor, hot oil produces burnt bitter sauce. Use a thermometer or test with a single sesame seed before adding the rest of the ingredients.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add the 20 g / 4 to 5 sliced garlic cloves to the warm oil; fry gently, stirring with a wooden spoon, for 90 seconds to 2 minutes until the garlic turns a light golden color and just begins to crisp at the edges. Pull the pan off the heat the moment the garlic reads pale gold; garlic continues cooking in the residual oil heat and goes from golden to burnt in 30 seconds. Burnt garlic ruins the salsa and there is no rescue; pull early.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Return the pan to medium-low heat. Add the 50 g / ⅓ cup peanuts; fry 90 seconds, stirring constantly, until the peanuts are a shade darker and aromatic. Add the 20 g / 3 tbsp pepitas and 20 g / 3 tbsp sesame seeds; fry 60 seconds, stirring, until the sesame seeds turn light golden and start to pop and the pepitas puff slightly.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add the 15 g / 4 to 5 morita chilies, 8 g / 10 to 12 chiles de árbol, and 12 g / 1 large ancho chili (torn into pieces); fry only 30 to 45 seconds, stirring constantly, just until the chilies darken slightly and release a deep aromatic smoke. Do not over-fry the chilies; the dried chilies absorb the warm oil quickly and burn from the inside, and burnt chilies make the entire batch bitter. Pull the pan off the heat the moment the chilies smell aggressively aromatic; the residual oil heat continues the cook.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Let the oil-and-ingredients mixture cool 10 minutes off the heat; the residual heat continues to extract flavor into the oil during the cool-down (a brief infusion step that is structural for depth). Pour the entire mixture into the bowl of a food processor (5 to 7 cup capacity); add the 6 g / 1 tsp salt and 5 g / 1 tsp brown sugar. Pulse 10 to 12 times in 1-second bursts to a coarse-medium grind; the texture should preserve visible bits of chili, garlic, peanut, pepita, and sesame (a chunky oil-suspended sauce, not a smooth puree). Add the 20 g / 4 tsp apple cider vinegar; pulse 3 to 4 more times to integrate.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Taste and adjust: salsa macha should read deeply chili-smoky-fried with crunchy nut-and-seed texture, balanced acid from the vinegar, gentle sweetness from the brown sugar, and a long warm heat at the back; if flat, an extra 1 g / pinch of fine sea salt and 5 g / 1 tsp apple cider vinegar; if too acidic, more sugar. Transfer to a clean glass jar (about 400 ml capacity); the salsa is meant to have visible oil pooled on top with the chunky paste below. Let rest at room temperature 2 hours before first use for the flavors to integrate fully; refrigerate after the rest period. Use as a condiment for tacos, eggs (especially fried or scrambled), grilled meat (steak, chicken, pork, lamb), grilled or roasted vegetables, beans (canonical with stewed black beans and pinto beans), soups (a spoonful stirred into a bowl of bean or tomato soup), cheese (on quesadillas, grilled cheese, queso fresco, or just spooned over a wedge of feta), and avocado.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  The oil temperature is the single most important variable in salsa macha and the most common point of failure for first-time cooks. The traditional Veracruzan technique uses warm oil (130 to 150°C / 270 to 300°F), not hot oil; the temperature is just enough to gently fry the aromatics and extract their flavor into the oil over a few minutes, without burning the chilies or the spices. Hot oil (180°C and above) burns the chilies and garlic in under 30 seconds, producing a bitter scorched flavor that no amount of vinegar or sugar can rescue; the entire batch has to be discarded. Use a thermometer for the first few times you make this; the visual cues (gentle shimmer, a sesame seed that dances rather than bursts) become reliable after experience.

  The fry order matters. Garlic first (because it needs the longest fry time and is the most heat-sensitive); then peanuts (which need a medium fry to deepen the toast); then the smaller pepitas and sesame seeds (which fry quickly); finally the chilies (which need only 30 to 45 seconds because they are the smallest and absorb the oil heat fastest). The wrong order (chilies first, then peanuts) produces burnt chilies and undertoasted nuts; the canonical sequence builds each ingredient at its appropriate temperature.

  The texture is structural. Salsa macha is not a smooth sauce and not a chunky chip-and-dip salsa; it sits between the two as a coarse oil-suspended paste with visible textural ingredients. Pulse the food processor in short bursts and stop while there is still visible structure in the chilies, peanuts, pepitas, and sesame; the texture is what distinguishes salsa macha from a generic chili paste. Some Veracruzan households use a mortar instead of a processor, which produces an even chunkier and more rustic texture; both are correct, with the processor being faster and the mortar being more traditional.

  The acid-and-sugar balance is structural. Salsa macha without vinegar reads heavy and one-dimensional; salsa macha without sugar reads sharp and unrounded. The small amounts of both (20 g vinegar, 5 g sugar) are not optional; they balance the deep fried chili and nut flavors and give the salsa its characteristic depth and length. The piloncillo (raw cane sugar in cone form) is the canonical Mexican sugar choice and reads more caramel-molasses than light brown sugar; if you can find it, use it.

  Versatility. Salsa macha is one of the most cuisine-flexible sauces in the global pantry, and the only Mexican salsa that travels naturally across non-Mexican applications. Use it as: a condiment for tacos of all kinds (carnitas, al pastor, barbacoa, fish, vegetable; a small spoonful on top of each taco brings smoky chili depth without watering down the filling); a topping for eggs (fried, scrambled, poached; a teaspoon on a fried egg over rice is one of the most successful breakfast pairings in modern Mexican cooking); a finish for grilled meat (steak, chicken, pork, lamb); a finish for grilled or roasted vegetables (sweet potato, cauliflower, broccoli, eggplant, carrots, Brussels sprouts; the smoky chili oil crusts on the surface beautifully); a topping for beans (the canonical pairing with stewed black beans and pinto beans, especially as a finish on a bowl of beans with rice); a stir-in for soups (a teaspoon stirred into a bowl of bean, tomato, vegetable, or even ramen brings smoky depth); a topping for cheese (queso fresco, feta, fresh mozzarella, ricotta; the chili oil over a wedge of cheese is a brilliant snack); a finish for avocado (over avocado toast, sliced avocado, or guacamole); a flavor base for marinades (mix 30 g salsa macha with 30 g oil and 15 g lime juice as a marinade for chicken or shrimp); a topping for pizza (in place of or in addition to chili oil); a sauce for grain bowls and rice bowls (a Mexican alternative to chili crisp); a topping for ramen and noodle bowls (the chili-crisp-hybrid version, see modifications); and as a finish for sandwiches, especially grilled cheese, breakfast sandwiches, and torta-style Mexican sandwiches.

  Pairs across Mexican (canonical), Tex-Mex, broader Central American, modern American (where salsa macha has been deeply integrated into contemporary cooking through restaurants and commercial brands like Tia Lupita and El Yucateco), contemporary fusion (the salsa macha-chili crisp hybrid is the structural bridge between Mexican and Chinese pantry cookery), and modern European cooking (where chefs use salsa macha as the structural chili condiment on the modern Mediterranean and contemporary Italian table).

  Storage. Keeps 3 to 4 weeks refrigerated in a sealed glass jar; the oil layer on top should be maintained (refresh with a tablespoon of neutral oil after a few scoops have pulled the level down). The salsa thickens significantly in the cold (the fried particles settle and the oil firms); stir before serving to redistribute. Do not freeze; the texture is acceptable on thawing but the freshness fades.
