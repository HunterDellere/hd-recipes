desc: 'Desalted salt cod gently poached in garlic-and-bay milk, then beaten with warm olive oil and starchy boiled potato into a warm, ivory, emulsified spread. source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Provençal and Languedoc tradition of brandade de morue, the pounded salt-cod emulsion of Nîmes and Marseille, with the Venetian baccalà mantecato and Basque pil-pil as the adjacent salt-cod-and-oil emulsions.'
  note: 'Brandade (from Provençal brandar, to beat or stir vigorously) is mechanically an emulsion: warm olive oil driven drop by drop into warm poached salt cod, exactly as oil is driven into egg yolk for aioli, with potato added in the Nîmes style for body and to stretch a costly ingredient. The Marseille purist version omits potato entirely and relies on the cod gelatin alone to carry the oil. This recipe uses potato for reliability and a more forgiving emulsion, then finishes as a gratin, the most common modern service.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Salt cod must be desalted before any cooking. If you are using commercial dried bacalao or your own home-cured salt cod, submerge it in cold water in the refrigerator for 24 to 48 hours, changing the water 3 to 4 times. The fish is ready when a corner tastes pleasantly seasoned rather than aggressively salty. Skip this step and the dish is inedible. The active time below assumes the cod is already desalted.'
  - 'Have the olive oil and the milk warm and in separate vessels before you start beating. The emulsion holds only while both the cod and the oil are warm; cold oil added to warm cod, or warm oil added to a cooled cod paste, will slide out instead of binding.'
  - 'If serving as a gratin, position an oven rack 15 cm below the broiler element and preheat the broiler while you mount the brandade, so the spread goes under heat while still warm.'

steps:
  - text: 'Confirm the desalt. The 450 g / 1 lb soaked salt cod should have plumped and softened over its 24 to 48 hours in cold water. Tear off a corner and taste it raw: it should read like a well-seasoned fresh fish, not a salt lick. If it is still aggressively salty, soak another 6 to 12 hours with one more water change. Drain, pat dry, and cut into 6 cm pieces.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Poach the cod. Combine the cod pieces, the 500 ml / 2 cups whole milk, the 18 g / 4 to 5 whole peeled garlic cloves, the 1 bay leaf, and the 2 g / 1 tsp whole black peppercorns in a saucepan. Add no salt. Bring to a bare tremor over medium-low heat (small bubbles at the edge, never a rolling boil) and hold at 70 to 80 °C for 8 to 10 minutes, until the cod flakes apart at the touch of a fork. Boiling toughens the protein and curdles the milk; the gentle poach keeps the flesh tender and leaches the collagen and gelatin that will later help carry the oil. Lift the cod and the poached garlic out with a slotted spoon. Reserve the warm poaching milk, strain out the bay and peppercorns.'
    technique: 'low-temperature-poaching'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Boil the potato. While the cod poaches, cover the 250 g / 2 medium waxy potato chunks with cold water in a separate pot, add no salt, and bring to a simmer. Cook 12 to 15 minutes until a knife slides through with no resistance. Drain, return to the dry hot pot, and let them steam-dry off the heat for 2 minutes. Dry potato absorbs oil; wet potato weeps water and breaks the emulsion. Press them through a ricer or mash smooth while still hot.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Flake and warm the base. Pull the cod apart with two forks, checking for and discarding any pin bones or skin. Combine the warm flaked cod, the warm riced potato, and the 18 g of poached garlic in a heavy warm bowl or, for the most reliable emulsion, in the bowl of a stand mixer with the paddle. Add the 9 g / 2 to 3 raw garlic cloves grated to a paste. Beat on low for 30 seconds to break the cod into fine strands. Everything must stay warm; if the base has cooled below body temperature, set the bowl over a pot of hot water for a minute.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Mount the emulsion. With the mixer on low (or beating hard by hand with a wooden spoon), drizzle in roughly half of the 180 ml / ¾ cup warm olive oil, about 90 ml, in a thin steady stream, the way you would build aioli. The cod gelatin and the potato starch are the emulsifiers; they hold the oil in suspension as long as it goes in slowly enough to disperse. The paste will lighten in color and turn glossy and cohesive. If it looks oily or starts to slide apart, stop the oil and beat in a tablespoon of the warm reserved poaching milk to re-tighten the emulsion before continuing.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Loosen and season. Beat in the 60 ml / ¼ cup warm heavy cream and enough of the reserved warm poaching milk, a tablespoon at a time, to bring the brandade to a soft, spreadable, ricotta-like consistency. It firms as it cools, so keep it slightly looser than you want it on the toast. Add the finely grated zest of the lemon (reserve the juice), the few rasps of nutmeg, and the 1 g / ¼ tsp white pepper. Taste. The cod usually carries enough salt; add the 1 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt only if the spread reads flat. Then add lemon juice a teaspoon at a time: the acid is the structural counterweight to the oil and cream, and the brandade should taste bright and savory rather than heavy. This is the umami-and-acid axis of the dish, the cured cod is the glutamate, the lemon is the lift.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Gratin (optional but classic). Scrape the brandade into a shallow buttered gratin dish, swirling the surface into ridges with the back of a spoon so they catch color. Drizzle the surface with 30 g / 2 tbsp of the remaining warm olive oil. Slide under the preheated broiler 15 cm from the element for 4 to 6 minutes, until the ridges are spotted deep golden brown. Watch it; the surface goes from pale to scorched fast. The browning is Maillard on the surface oil and the cod proteins, and it adds the toasted contrast that the soft interior lacks.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Toast and serve. Toast or grill the 240 g / 8 slices of sourdough until crisp at the edges, and rub each hot slice with a cut raw garlic clove. Finish the brandade with the chopped 6 g / 2 tbsp parsley, a last thread of the remaining olive oil, and, if using, the 40 g / ¼ cup niçoise olives scattered alongside. Serve warm, with the reserved lemon at the table to squeeze over. Spread it thick on the warm toast; the contrast of crisp bread, warm emulsified cod, and the bright squeeze of lemon is the entire point of the dish.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why brandade is an emulsion, not a mash. The defining move is mounting warm olive oil into warm cod the way you mount oil into egg yolk for aioli or mayonnaise. The emulsifiers here are the gelatin and soluble collagen the cod releases during the gentle poach, plus the amylose and amylopectin from the riced potato. Both wrap around the oil droplets and hold them suspended in the water phase, so the finished spread is glossy and cohesive rather than greasy and separated. This is the same physics as the Basque bacalao al pil-pil, where the cod gelatin alone emulsifies olive oil into a sauce with nothing but a swirling pan. Get the temperature and the slow oil addition right and the brandade lightens and pulls together visibly under the spoon; rush the oil or let the base cool and it slides apart into an oil slick.

  Temperature is the whole game. The emulsion forms and holds only in a warm window. Everything, the cod, the potato, the oil, the milk, the cream, must be warm when it goes in, and the finished brandade is best mounted and served warm. A brandade that breaks has almost always cooled too far or taken oil too fast. The fix for a broken emulsion is the same as for broken mayonnaise: beat a tablespoon of warm poaching milk into a clean warm bowl and slowly beat the broken mass back into it.

  Desalting is non-negotiable and not optional. Commercial salt cod is preserved at roughly 20 percent salt by weight, far past palatability. The 24-to-48-hour cold soak with several water changes pulls the salt back down to a seasoned, edible level through simple diffusion across the concentration gradient. Cold water and the refrigerator matter; warm-water soaking is faster but starts to spoil the fish and gives off-flavors. Taste a corner before you commit; cod from different producers desalts at different rates.

  Salt cod versus fresh cod. The cure is not just preservation, it concentrates the flavor and firms the texture in a way fresh cod cannot replicate. The osmotic water loss during salting tightens the protein structure and intensifies the savory, slightly funky depth that defines bacalao. A fresh-cod version is pleasant but reads as a mild fish-and-potato puree; the cured version reads as brandade. If you cannot get dried bacalao, home-curing fresh cod is the right path, not skipping the cure.

  Regional map. Three closely related salt-cod-and-oil emulsions share this physics. Provençal brandade de morue (Nîmes and Marseille) is the version here, often with potato and frequently gratinéed. Venetian baccalà mantecato is whipped salt cod and olive oil with no potato and no garlic, served cool on grilled polenta or bread as cicchetti. Basque bacalao al pil-pil emulsifies the cod gelatin with olive oil and garlic into a loose pourable sauce in the pan, no whipping and no potato. They are dialects of one technique: warm cod gelatin carrying warm olive oil.

  Make-ahead. The brandade holds 3 days covered in the refrigerator. It firms solid when cold; rewarm gently in a low oven or over a water bath, beating in a splash of warm milk to bring the emulsion back to a spreadable softness before gratinéing or serving. Do not microwave on high, it overheats unevenly and breaks the emulsion.
