title: 'Couscous with seven vegetables'
desc: 'The canonical Moroccan Friday meal: medium-grain semolina couscous hand-steamed three times over a deep saffron-and-ginger broth of lamb, chickpeas, and seven layered vegetables (carrot, turnip, zucchini, cabbage, pumpkin or butternut squash, sweet potato, and tomato), the grains fluffed and oiled between steamings until they emerge separate, light, and perfumed with the broth steam. The structural defining technique is the triple steam in the couscoussier; box-instant couscous boiled in stock is a different (and inferior) dish. The dish that gives Maghreb cuisine its name to the world.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Soak the chickpeas overnight (or quick-soak). Canned chickpeas work as a fallback (add late).'
  - 'A couscoussier (a tall two-piece pot with a perforated steaming insert) is the canonical vessel; the bottom holds the broth and stew, the top holds the couscous over the steam. The closest home substitute is a tall stockpot with a fine-mesh sieve or steamer basket fitted snugly over the rim and sealed with a damp kitchen towel wrapped around the join (the seal forces the steam through the couscous rather than escaping at the side). Cheesecloth-lined sieves work too. Aluminum foil over the top of the steamer, crimped tight, completes the seal.'
  - 'Bloom the saffron 15 minutes before the broth starts. Grind 0.4 g threads with a pinch of sugar, steep in 50 g hot water.'
  - 'Plan 3 hours total. The dish is a project; budget for it. The flavor and the grain texture are worth the time and structurally cannot be shortcut.'

  name: 'The Moroccan Friday meal canon. Couscous with seven vegetables (couscous tfaya in some regions, couscous seffa in others, with regional name variations) is documented across Moroccan home cookery as the traditional Friday lunch following weekly prayers; the seven-vegetable formula is symbolic and traditional but not strictly limited to seven (six or eight are acceptable; the principle is the layered vegetable variety).'
  note: 'The triple-steam technique is structural and is the difference between a great couscous and a generic boiled-grain dish. The hand-rolling-and-oiling between steamings ensures each grain stays separate, absorbs steam without becoming gummy, and develops the characteristic light, perfumed texture. Instant couscous (the pre-steamed boxed product) is acceptable for weeknight cooking but produces a structurally different and meaningfully inferior dish; the triple-steam version takes 90 minutes of total work but the result is irreplaceable.'

steps:
  - text: 'Cook the soaked 200 g chickpeas in 1 L fresh water 45 minutes until tender; drain and reserve. Bloom the 0.4 g saffron with 1 g sugar and 50 g hot water; steep 15 minutes.'
    time_min: 50
  - text: 'In the bottom of a couscoussier (or a tall stockpot), heat the 45 g / 3 tbsp olive oil over medium-high heat. Brown the 800 g lamb pieces on 2 sides, 8 minutes total. Reduce heat to medium; add the 300 g / 2 onions and sweat 10 minutes until golden. Add the 16 g / 4 garlic and 25 g grated ginger; cook 60 seconds. Add the 5 g / 2½ tsp turmeric, 3 g / 1½ tsp cumin, 3 g / 1½ tsp paprika, and 3 g / 1½ tsp black pepper; stir 30 seconds.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Add the 200 g crushed tomatoes, the bloomed saffron liquid, the 2000 g / 8 cups water, the cilantro-and-parsley bundle, and 10 g / 2½ tsp of the kosher salt. Bring to a simmer; cover and braise gently 45 minutes while you work on the first couscous steam.'
    time_min: 45
  - text: 'First couscous rinse-and-fluff: in a wide shallow bowl, spread the 500 g dry couscous. Sprinkle with 200 g of the cold water (mixed with 4 g of the fine sea salt) and 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil; rub the grains between your palms for 2 minutes, breaking up any clumps until the grains are evenly moistened and separated. Let stand 5 minutes to absorb.'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Transfer the moistened couscous to the steamer insert (lined with cheesecloth if your insert has large holes). Set the steamer over the simmering lamb broth; if there is a gap at the join, seal with a damp cloth or aluminum foil so the steam is forced up through the couscous, not around it. Steam uncovered 20 minutes from when you see the first steam rising through the grains. Do not cover; the couscous needs to vent. The grains will plump and become al dente.'
    technique: 'couscous-steam'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Turn the steamed couscous out into the wide bowl. Sprinkle with 200 g cold water plus 4 g salt, and 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil. Working with a spatula at first (the grains are very hot), break up any clumps and re-roll between palms once cooled enough to touch. The grains should look swollen, separate, and glossy with oil. Rest 5 minutes.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Second steam: return the couscous to the steamer over the broth (which has been simmering all this time). Steam 20 minutes. After 45 minutes of lamb braise, the meat should be fork-tender; lift it out to a plate, cover, and reserve.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'After the second steam, turn out and rub again with the remaining 200 g water and 15 g olive oil; rest 5 minutes.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the vegetables to the broth in order of cooking time: first the 300 g carrots, 250 g turnips, 400 g squash, and 300 g sweet potato (these need 30 minutes); cover and simmer 15 minutes. Then add the 300 g cabbage wedges, 200 g tomato wedges, and the cooked chickpeas (these need 15 minutes); add the remaining 8 g / 2 tsp salt. Simmer 15 minutes. In the last 8 minutes, add the 300 g zucchini batons (which cook fastest).'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Third and final couscous steam: return the couscous to the steamer over the now vegetable-rich broth. Steam 15 to 20 minutes; the steam from the vegetables perfumes the grains beautifully in this final pass. The couscous should be tender, fluffy, and slightly toasted-fragrant.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Turn the steamed couscous into a large wide serving platter. Add the 60 g / 4 tbsp butter, cut into small pieces, and fold gently with a fork until the butter melts into the hot grains. Taste a few grains; the seasoning should read of warm spice, lamb broth, and faint saffron. The grains should be perfectly separate, fluffy, and glossy.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Mound the couscous on the platter; arrange the lamb pieces and the vegetables on top in a colorful array. Ladle 200 g of the hot broth over the top of the platter; serve the remaining broth in a small bowl on the side so each diner can wet their portion to taste. Discard the herb bundle. Serve immediately; the canonical service is family-style from the platter, with each diner eating from the section closest to them.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why the triple steam is structural. The hand-rolling-and-oiling-between-steamings technique is what produces the structural texture that defines proper couscous: grains that are tender but distinct, that have absorbed broth steam without becoming gummy, and that carry a fluffy, light, and slightly toasted character. Each steam cycle does specific work: the first hydrates the dry grains, the second deepens the absorption and re-separates them after the slight compression of the first steam, the third perfumes the grains with the vegetable-rich final broth and brings them to their finished texture. Boxed instant couscous boiled in stock skips all of this and produces a uniformly hydrated, slightly gummy grain that is recognizably couscous-adjacent but lacks the structural lightness of the triple-steam version.

  The seven-vegetable formula is symbolic, not strict. The number seven is traditional and culturally meaningful (often associated with completeness and abundance in North African folk numerology); the actual vegetables vary by region, season, and family. Some traditional formulas include fava beans, eggplant, leek, or red bell pepper in place of one of the canonical seven. The principle is the layered variety, not the specific count; six or eight vegetables in the same proportions work fine. The cooking order matters more than the count: dense root vegetables go in first, leafy and quick-cooking vegetables go in last.

  Versatility. The broth-and-vegetable-stew portion of this dish is essentially a Moroccan winter pot-au-feu and stands alone with bread instead of couscous. The triple-steam technique transfers to other grain steamings (Cape Malay yellow rice, Egyptian roz bil shaareyah). The lamb-and-spice base is the structural template for many Moroccan slow braises beyond couscous.

  Pairs across Moroccan (canonical, especially the Friday meal tradition), broader Maghreb (similar steamed-couscous dishes exist in Algeria and Tunisia under regional names), and contemporary North African diaspora cookery. Especially central to family meals in Morocco where Friday couscous is a weekly institution.

  Storage. The stew portion keeps 4 days refrigerated and freezes 2 months; the couscous keeps 3 days and reheats best with a brief re-steam (place in a sieve over simmering water 5 minutes). Leftover couscous can also be turned into cold couscous salad with vinaigrette, herbs, dried fruit, and toasted nuts. The freshly-steamed-and-buttered grain is the structural ideal; reheated couscous loses some of the lightness.
