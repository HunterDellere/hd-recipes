steps:
  - text: 'Hydrate the wakame. Place the 4 g dried wakame in a small bowl and cover with cold water. Let stand 5 minutes; the flakes will swell to roughly 7 to 10 times their dried volume and turn vivid emerald. Drain through a small sieve and squeeze gently; do not press hard. Set aside.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Heat the dashi. In a 2-quart saucepan, bring the 800 ml dashi to 80°C / 175°F (small bubbles forming at the edges, no rolling boil). If you are using freshly-strained ichiban dashi, it should already be near this temperature; if niban or refrigerated dashi, warm gradually over medium-low heat. Do not boil the dashi vigorously; the kombu and katsuobushi flavors are heat-sensitive even after extraction.'
    technique: 'dashi'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add the tofu. Slide the 200 g cubed silken tofu gently into the dashi. The cubes should be just submerged; bring the pot back up to 80°C and hold for 1 minute to warm the tofu through. Do not boil ,  boiling fractures the silken curd into ragged shreds. The cubes will look slightly translucent at the edges when warmed.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the wakame. Scatter the 4 g rehydrated wakame across the surface of the dashi. Hold for 30 seconds to warm through; do not stir aggressively, the wakame is delicate and excess agitation tears the leaves.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Whisk in the miso off-heat. Pull the saucepan off the burner. Place a small fine-mesh sieve into the dashi and add the 60 g miso into the sieve. Press the miso through the sieve with the back of a small spoon or whisk while moving the sieve in slow circles through the dashi; the sieve breaks up clumps and ensures the miso dissolves evenly without grainy chunks. Alternatively, ladle 60 ml of hot dashi into a small bowl, whisk the miso into the bowl until smooth, then pour back into the pot. The miso should fully dissolve in 30 seconds; the dashi will turn from clear to cloudy-tan-brown. Do not return to a boil under any circumstances. Boiling drives off the volatile esters that miso adds and dulls the umami; this is the single rule that separates a working miso soup from a flat one.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Serve immediately. Ladle into 4 small bowls (200 ml each). Sprinkle the sliced scallion greens on top of each bowl as a finishing aromatic. Serve as part of a washoku meal ,  the standard accompaniment is a bowl of plain Japanese rice and one or two banchan or okazu (a piece of grilled fish, a small salad, a quick-pickled cucumber). The miso soup goes from the kitchen to the table within 2 minutes; reheating it to a boil ruins the flavor, and a tepid bowl of miso soup is one of the saddest things in a Japanese kitchen.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The order of operations matters. Tofu before wakame before miso, with miso always last and always off-heat. The sequence is not arbitrary: tofu needs 1 minute at 80°C to warm through and absorb dashi seasoning; wakame needs 30 seconds, no more, to warm without going slimy; miso needs zero seconds at heat ,  it goes in off-burner and gets dissolved with no further cooking. Front-loading the miso (whisking it in at the start, then adding the solids) is a common mistake that produces a flat, slightly bitter, dulled-aroma soup because the miso has been cooked through and the volatile esters and amino acid aromatics that define its character have been driven off.

  Why a sieve. Miso is a paste that does not dissolve readily in water unless broken up. The fine-mesh sieve serves two functions: (1) it traps any insoluble husk fragments or unpasteurized koji clumps that some artisanal misos contain, producing a cleaner-looking soup; (2) it provides a mechanical break-up surface that disperses the paste evenly through the dashi without aggressive whisking. Whisking miso into hot dashi in a free pot tends to leave grainy chunks suspended; the sieve protocol is the textbook home solution.

  Salt and miso brand variation. Miso varies substantially in salt by brand and style: light-fermented shiro misos sit at roughly 5 to 6% salt by weight, while aged akamiso goes to 10 to 14%. A 60 g dose of a 5% white miso contributes 3 g salt to 800 ml of soup; the same dose of 12% red miso contributes 7.2 g ,  more than double. The 60 g recommendation in this recipe is calibrated for awase miso (~8 to 10% salt). Adjust by 10 to 20 g either direction based on your specific brand and your salt tolerance; taste the soup before serving and add small increments of miso through the sieve if it tastes flat.

  The 200 ml serving size. The Japanese home miso-soup bowl is small ,  150 to 220 ml ,  and the soup is one of several small dishes in a washoku meal, not a main course. Western recipes often serve miso soup in 350 to 400 ml bowls, which dilutes the seasoning and shifts the dish away from its design. If you want a larger bowl, scale up the recipe; do not stretch a 4-bowl recipe across 2 oversized bowls.

  Storage and reheating. Made-and-served-fresh is the only acceptable mode for serious miso soup. The miso should not be reheated after the first off-heat dissolve; reheating drives off the aromatics that justify the off-heat protocol in the first place. For a meal-prep pattern, store the dashi (with cooked solids) in the refrigerator for up to 2 days and add miso fresh each time you reheat ,  warm the dashi to 80°C, pull off heat, whisk the miso in, serve. Never store miso-soup-with-miso-already-added overnight; the next day's reheated bowl tastes like a miso soup that has had the soul cooked out of it, because it has.

  The miso soup test. A common Japanese home-cook quality test: ladle a spoonful of finished miso soup, taste it. The first sensation should be the dashi ,  clean, faintly fishy-marine, gently sweet. The second sensation should be the miso ,  savory, slightly funky, salt-tinged. The third sensation is the aromatics from the scallion and wakame. If the miso hits first and dominates, the soup has too much miso or too thin a dashi base; reduce miso by 10 g and rebuild. If the dashi tastes weak and the miso supplies all the flavor, the dashi was underextracted; remake the dashi with more kombu and katsuobushi.
