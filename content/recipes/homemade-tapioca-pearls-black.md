steps:
  - text: 'Combine the 60 ml / ¼ cup water and the 30 g / 2½ tbsp muscovado sugar in a small saucepan. Bring to a rolling boil, swirling to dissolve the sugar; hold at the hard boil 30 seconds. This boiling sweetened water is the dough binder. The high temperature is non-negotiable; it pre-gelatinizes a fraction of the tapioca starch on contact in the next step, which is what gives the dough plasticity rather than crumbly dryness.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Place 120 g / ¾ cup plus 1 tbsp of the tapioca starch in a heatproof bowl (reserve the remaining 30 g for adjustment and dusting). Pour the boiling sugar-water onto the starch all at once. Stir aggressively with a wooden spoon for 15 seconds, the starch will look lumpy and uneven at first, then come together as a tacky, putty-colored mass. Let stand 30 seconds.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Turn out onto a clean work surface lightly dusted with the reserved tapioca starch. Knead 2 minutes by hand. The dough should feel like soft modeling clay, firm enough to hold a ball, just slightly sticky on the surface, not crumbly. If it cracks when folded, your sugar-water cooled too much before contact; add 1 tsp boiling water and re-knead. If it is sticky and refuses to hold shape, dust in 1 tbsp more starch and re-knead.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Roll the dough into a log about 30 cm / 12 inches long, dust with tapioca starch, then cut crosswise into 4 equal pieces. Working with one piece at a time (keep the others under a damp towel), roll into a thin rope about 1 cm / ⅜ inch thick. Cut the rope crosswise into small pieces of about 7 mm, each piece should weigh about 1 g.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Roll each small piece between your palms (or against the work surface) into a ball about 8 mm / ⅓ inch across. Toss each finished ball in extra tapioca starch in a sieve and shake off excess; the dusting prevents the pearls from sticking to each other in the pile. Continue until all dough is shaped. You will have about 250 to 300 pearls. This step is the time sink; budget 45 minutes if you have not done it before.'
    time_min: 40
  - text: 'Bring the 2000 ml / 8 cups water to a furious rolling boil in a wide pot, the water must never stop boiling hard while the pearls cook. Sift the pearls through a sieve once more to knock off loose starch, then drop them into the boiling water in a single addition. Stir gently for 30 seconds so they do not stick to the bottom; the boil will drop temporarily.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Boil the pearls hard for 25 minutes. They will float within 2 minutes, expand to roughly twice their dry size, and turn from opaque putty-colored to dark translucent brown over the course of cooking. Stir gently every 5 minutes. Do not lower the heat; gentle simmering produces pearls with raw centers.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Pull off the heat. Cover the pot and let stand 30 minutes, this residual-heat rest finishes the pearl centers without overcooking the perimeter to mush. This rest is the difference between supermarket-quality boba and good boba.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'While the pearls rest, build the syrup. Combine the 240 ml / 1 cup water, 200 g / 1 cup muscovado sugar, and 1 g pinch salt in a saucepan. Bring to a hard boil and reduce 5 minutes until the syrup is glossy, slightly thickened, and the bubbles have a tight, dense texture. Pull off heat.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Drain the rested pearls and rinse very briefly under cool water to wash off surface starch, 5 seconds, no more, or the pearls will start to harden. Transfer the warm pearls directly into the warm brown sugar syrup. Stir to coat; let stand 15 minutes for the syrup to penetrate and the pearls to take on the deep mahogany finish.'
    time_min: 16
  - text: 'The pearls are now ready to use. They hold at room temperature in their syrup for 4 to 6 hours, this is the entire service window. Do not refrigerate cooked pearls; starch retrogrades on cooling and the pearls turn hard and chalky. Plan to make pearls the same day you will drink them.'

notes: |
  The boiling-water hydration is the single most important step. Cold-water doughs do not work for boba, the starch never gelatinizes enough to hold the pearl together through the cooking boil, and the pearls dissolve into starchy mush. The boiling sugar-water at the moment of contact partially gelatinizes the surface starch granules into a tacky binder that holds the rest of the dough during shaping.

  The 30-minute off-heat rest after boiling is non-negotiable. During active boiling the pearl perimeter cooks much faster than the center; pulling them straight out of the boil leaves a raw, chalky core. The rest lets residual heat slowly penetrate to the center while the perimeter stops cooking. Skipping this step produces the dense, chalky pearls that give bad boba its bad reputation.

  Cooked pearls have a 4 to 6 hour service window. Refrigeration is fatal, starch retrogrades, water leaves the gel network, and the pearls turn hard within 2 hours of cooling. There is no recovery; recook fresh. This is also why bubble-tea shops cook pearls every 4 hours rather than holding them. If you want to plan ahead, freeze the raw uncooked pearls (after step 5) for up to 3 months; cook from frozen with an extra 5 minutes added to the boil.
