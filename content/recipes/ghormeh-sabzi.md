before_you_start:
  - 'Soak the red kidney beans overnight (or quick-soak: cover with water, bring to a boil, off heat 1 hour). Unsoaked beans never fully soften in the 2 to 3 hour braise and the stew reads chalky.'
  - 'Wash the fresh herbs (1.5 kg total raw) in two changes of water, spin or pat very dry, and chop fine. Wet herbs steam instead of sautéeing in step 1 and the herb-darkening phase fails; the dish reads green and grassy instead of deep and complex.'
  - 'Serve over chelow or tahdig (the canonical Persian pairing). Start the rice soak when you start the bean soak and the rice parboil 90 minutes before the stew finishes.'
  - 'Dried Persian limes (limoo amani) are structural. They are sold whole, dried hard like wooden balls, in Middle Eastern groceries; pierce each one twice with a paring knife before adding so the interior fermentation can release. Fresh lime juice substitutes the citric acid but not the fermented depth.'

source:
  name: 'The Iranian khoresh canon. Ghormeh sabzi (literally "stewed greens") is documented across Persian cookery for at least 500 years and is widely considered the national dish of Iran.'
  note: 'Two structural schools split on the herb mix: the canonical mix is parsley, cilantro, fenugreek leaves (shanbalileh), and scallion; some regional variants add dill, mint, leek tops, or chives. The dried-lime sourness is non-negotiable; the kidney bean is non-negotiable; the deep-brown onion fond is non-negotiable. Beyond those three structural anchors, the herb proportions vary by family. Fresh fenugreek leaves are the hardest to source outside Iran; dried fenugreek leaves (kasoori methi from Indian groceries) substitute well at one-quarter the volume.'

steps:
  - text: 'Drain the soaked 200 g kidney beans; place in a pot with 1 L fresh water, bring to a boil, reduce to a simmer, and cook 30 to 45 minutes until just tender (still holding shape, slight resistance at the center). Drain and reserve. This pre-cook is structural; raw beans added to the stew do not soften enough in the braise time.'
    time_min: 45
  - text: 'Heat 30 g / 2 tbsp of the neutral oil in a wide skillet over medium heat. Add all the chopped herbs (parsley, cilantro, scallion, fenugreek). The pan will look overstuffed; the herbs collapse dramatically as they cook. Sauté 25 to 35 minutes, stirring frequently and reducing heat slightly as the herbs darken, until the green color deepens from bright spring-green to deep forest-green to near-black, the volume reduces by about 70%, and the herbs smell deeply roasted with no raw grassy note. This long sauté is the structural defining technique of ghormeh sabzi; rushed herbs taste of raw chlorophyll and the stew never integrates.'
    technique: 'herb-darkening'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Meanwhile, in a heavy 5-quart Dutch oven, heat the remaining 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil over medium heat. Add the 300 g / 2 onions (diced) and cook 15 to 20 minutes, stirring frequently, until deep golden brown with crispy edges (much deeper than translucent; the structural fond color is dark amber-mahogany, not light blonde). Reduce heat if the onions threaten to burn.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Add the 800 g lamb cubes to the browned onions; raise heat to medium-high. Brown the lamb on all sides, 6 to 8 minutes total. Add the 6 g / 1 tbsp turmeric, 8 g of the kosher salt (about 2 tsp), and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper; stir 30 seconds to bloom the turmeric in the lamb fat. The pot bottom should be deeply browned with a layer of fond.'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Add the darkened herb mixture to the lamb pot; stir to combine. The herbs will smell intensely fragrant, almost meaty-vegetal at this point. Add the 1200 ml / 5 cups water; scrape the bottom to lift the fond. Add the pierced 12 g / 4 to 6 dried limes. Bring to a steady simmer, cover, and reduce heat to low.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Simmer covered 90 minutes, stirring every 30 minutes. The stew should hold a tremor, not a rolling boil; aggressive boil emulsifies the lamb fat into a greasy slurry instead of letting it float on top.'
    time_min: 90
  - text: 'Add the cooked beans and the remaining 6 g salt (about 1½ tsp). Continue simmering covered 30 to 45 minutes more until the lamb is fork-tender (collapses under gentle pressure) and the stew has reduced and darkened, the surface oils separating slightly. The total braise time is structurally 2 to 2.5 hours; longer is fine and improves the dish.'
    time_min: 45
  - text: 'Taste. The stew should read deeply herbal, sour from the dried lime, savory from the long-browned onion and lamb, and faintly bitter from the fenugreek. If the sour reads soft, fish out one dried lime, crush against the side of the pot with a spoon to release more interior, and stir back in; or add the 15 g / 1 tbsp fresh lemon juice. If the salt reads low, add in 2 g increments. Pull the dried limes before serving (they soften to mush and read bitter if bitten into).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Serve over chelow (plain steamed Persian rice) or alongside tahdig in a shallow bowl. The canonical service is the rice and the stew side-by-side, not mixed, so each bite can be composed by the diner.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The herb-darkening phase explained. The 25 to 35 minute sauté of fresh herbs in oil is not optional and not a step you can shorten. Three chemical changes occur during the darkening: (1) the chlorophyll in the herbs degrades from bright green to olive to near-black as the magnesium ion at its center is displaced by hydrogen under prolonged heat; (2) the green volatiles (cis-3-hexenal, the grassy compound that makes raw cilantro smell soapy to some palates) cook off and deeper roasted volatiles develop; (3) the cell walls break down and the cellular water evaporates, concentrating the herb flavor. The result is a herb base that tastes deep, savory, and almost meaty rather than fresh, grassy, and vegetal. Skip or shorten this step and the stew tastes of raw herbs; do it correctly and the herbs carry the dish.

  Why the dried lime is structural. Limoo amani (Persian dried limes) are limes that are boiled briefly, then sun-dried for weeks until they are hard, hollow, and dark. The drying ferments the lime interior; the resulting flavor is sour, citrusy, but also fermented, slightly bitter, and umami-savory in a way that fresh lime juice cannot replicate. Pierced limes added whole to a stew slowly release this complex sourness over the long braise; the lime acts as both an acid and a fermented depth element. The dried lime is the second structural defining ingredient of ghormeh sabzi (after the darkened herbs); without it the dish is not the dish.

  Versatility. The herb-darkening technique transfers to other Persian khoresh: khoresh-e karafs (celery and herb stew with mint), khoresh-e bademjan (eggplant and herb stew), khoresh-e baghali (fava and dill stew). The deep-brown onion technique is universal across the cuisine and the home-cook foundation of any khoresh.

  Pairs across Persian (canonical), broader Iranian diaspora, Afghan (similar herb stews exist as qorma sabzi), and contemporary Mediterranean-leaning herb-forward cookery.

  Storage. Ghormeh sabzi improves with a day in the refrigerator; the flavors integrate and the herbs deepen further. Keeps 5 days refrigerated and freezes 3 months. Reheat gently on the stovetop; do not boil aggressively (the lamb fat will emulsify and read greasy). Many Iranian cooks consider day-two ghormeh sabzi the structural ideal; make ahead when serving for guests.
