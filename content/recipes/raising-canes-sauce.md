before_you_start:
  - 'The four-hour refrigerated rest is the recipe, not an afterthought. Cane''s sauce mixed and tasted within an hour reads as a harsh peppery mayo, the pepper is sharp and the Worcestershire reads as a discrete flavor note rather than as background umami. After 4 hours in the cold the cracked pepper hydrates and bleeds piperine into the fat phase, the Worcestershire integrates into the matrix, and the garlic powder fully blooms. The sauce undergoes a meaningful transformation that no shortcut replicates.'

steps:
  - text: 'Crack the 3 g / 1½ tsp black peppercorns in a mortar or under the base of a heavy skillet until coarse-ground. The pieces should be visibly chunky, not powder; a coffee grinder pulsed twice is the next-best option. Pre-ground supermarket black pepper has lost most of its piperine volatiles and will not produce the right result here.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'In a small bowl combine the 150 g / ⅔ cup mayonnaise, 30 g / 2 tbsp ketchup, 8 g / 1½ tsp Worcestershire sauce, the 3 g / 1½ tsp cracked black pepper, 1.5 g / ¾ tsp garlic powder, 0.5 g / ¼ tsp paprika, and a 0.5 g pinch salt.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Whisk vigorously for 30 seconds until completely smooth and uniformly pale beige with visible black pepper flecks. The Worcestershire should fully integrate, not show as brown streaks; if streaks remain, push them through with the back of the spoon until the color is even.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Cover and refrigerate at least 4 hours before serving, 12 hours is better. This is the recipe; sauce served before the rest tastes harsh and disjointed. The cracked pepper needs to hydrate and bleed into the fat phase, the Worcestershire needs to integrate, and the garlic powder needs to bloom. Holds 1 week refrigerated in a sealed jar.'
    time_min: 240
  - text: 'Taste after the rest. If it reads under-peppered, add another 1 g / ½ tsp cracked pepper and rest 1 more hour; if it reads under-salted, add a small pinch and re-whisk. Most adjustments will be unnecessary if the initial ratios were measured accurately.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Raising Cane''s has never published its sauce recipe, but the franchise has confirmed in interviews that mayonnaise, ketchup, Worcestershire, garlic powder, and heavy black pepper are the components, and that a long refrigerated rest is part of the procedure. Every credible copycat lands within a few grams of these numbers. The signature is the heavy hand on coarsely cracked black pepper plus the four-hour rest; under-dose the pepper or skip the rest and you are making a generic burger spread instead.

  Worcestershire is the secret. The sauce reads as having far more complexity than its five-ingredient list suggests because Worcestershire is itself a fermented condiment with anchovy, tamarind, molasses, and aged vinegar. A teaspoon and a half in a small batch is invisible as discrete flavor but provides the umami backbone that distinguishes Cane''s sauce from a plain mayo-ketchup combination.

  The black pepper must be coarsely cracked, not finely ground. The chain version has visible black pepper flecks suspended in the sauce, and the pepper character is a slow-building heat that you feel on the back of the tongue rather than a sharp top note. Fine ground pepper dissolves uniformly and produces a flat peppery flavor without the same arc. A mortar and pestle or a coarse grinder setting is mandatory.
