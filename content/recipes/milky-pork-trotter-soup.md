before_you_start:
  - 'Budget a 3.5-hour cook window: a 15-minute blanch, then roughly 3 hours of hard rolling boil. The boil is passive but the pot stays on the stove and needs a splash of hot water every 30 to 40 minutes as it reduces, or the broth over-concentrates and turns gluey.'
  - 'The milkiness comes from a HARD rolling boil, the opposite of a clear-broth simmer. Keep it at a rolling, roiling boil the entire time; the mechanical shear is what emulsifies fat and gelatin into the water. If you drop it to a gentle simmer, the broth stays thin and clear.'
  - 'Soak the wakame in cool water for 10 minutes and build the chili dip while the soup boils; both are quick and neither should be rushed at the end.'

steps:
  - text: 'Blanch the trotters. Combine the 1400 g split pork trotters, 3000 ml / 12 cups cold water, 20 g smashed ginger, and 30 ml / 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine in a heavy 8-quart stockpot. Bring to a boil over high heat; a heavy gray scum will lift within a few minutes. Boil hard for 5 minutes to purge blood and surface impurities, then drain into a colander and rinse the trotter sections under cold running water, rubbing off any clinging scum. This blanch is what lets the finished broth read clean and sweet rather than muddy-porky.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Start the boil. Return the rinsed trotters to the cleaned pot. Add 3000 ml / 12 cups fresh cold water, the 30 g smashed ginger, 4 smashed scallion whites, 20 g / 5 smashed garlic cloves, and 3 g / 1 tsp cracked white peppercorns. Bring to a full boil over high heat, uncovered.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Hold a hard rolling boil for 3 hours. Keep the heat high enough that the surface roils continuously; this is the entire technique. The mechanical agitation shears rendered pork fat into micro-droplets and drives dissolved gelatin to coat them, building an oil-in-water emulsion that scatters light and turns the broth opaque cream-white. Top up with hot water every 30 to 40 minutes to hold the level near the starting point, or the broth over-reduces into something gluey and salty-concentrated. By hour two the liquid should be visibly milky; by hour three the trotter skin and tendon are spoonably soft.'
    time_min: 180
  - text: 'Fish out and reserve the trotters. Lift the trotter sections into a bowl with a spider or tongs. Strain the broth through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean pot, discarding the spent ginger, scallion, garlic, and peppercorns. Return the trotters to the strained broth. You should have roughly 2 L; if it reduced well past that, add hot water to bring it back.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Season the broth. Return to a gentle simmer. Add 15 ml / 1 tbsp fish sauce and 8 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt, then taste. The fish sauce is doing double duty as the glutamate backbone and part of the salt; adjust with a pinch more kosher salt until the broth tastes full and round, not flat. Season lightly on purpose, the point is to frame the sweet pork, not to bury it.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Finish. Off the heat, stir in the drained 8 g wakame, 1 g / ½ tsp ground white pepper, and 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil. Ladle into bowls, giving each a share of the soft trotter, and scatter with the 15 g / 2 tbsp sliced scallion greens.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Make the chili dip while the soup boils. Stir together the 15 g minced red chili, 6 g / 2 cloves grated garlic, 30 ml / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, 20 ml / 4 tsp Chinkiang black vinegar, 4 g / 1 tsp granulated sugar, 10 g / 3 tbsp minced cilantro, and 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil. Just before serving, spoon in 30 ml / 2 tbsp of the hot broth to loosen it and bloom the aromatics. Serve alongside; the black vinegar is the dish''s finishing acid, cutting the collagen richness bite by bite as you dip the trotter meat.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  Why a rolling boil, not a simmer. Almost every stock instinct says never boil, because boiling clouds a broth. That is exactly the point here. This soup wants to be clouded: the opacity is an emulsion of rendered fat suspended by solubilized collagen (gelatin acting as the surfactant), and only the violent agitation of a hard boil generates it. The same trotters simmered gently would give a beautiful clear soup and no milkiness at all, which is the clear-broth modification above.

  Why blanch first anyway. Even though the goal is a cloudy broth, the blanch still matters: it strips the blood and scum that would otherwise taste muddy and off, not just look cloudy. Milky and clean are compatible; milky and dirty are not.

  Seasoning restraint. The menu framing is "gently seasoned to highlight the sweetness of the pork," and that is the right instinct. Pork skin and tendon read faintly sweet once broken down, and a heavy hand with soy or salt in the pot flattens it. Keep the broth itself modest and let the black-vinegar chili dip do the loud work on the side, so every bowl has both a mild, collagen-rich sip and a sharp, punchy dip.

  Make-ahead and storage. The broth deepens overnight and sets to a firm jelly in the fridge from the gelatin, which is the mark of a good extraction; it reheats to liquid in a minute. Refrigerated: 4 days. Frozen: 3 months. Reheat gently and re-check the salt after thawing, as chilling dulls the perception of seasoning.
