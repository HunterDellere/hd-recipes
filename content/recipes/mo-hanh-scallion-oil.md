before_you_start:
  - 'Slice all the scallion before the oil goes on the heat. The bloom is a single fast pour: once the oil is at temperature you have seconds to pour, and there is no time to slice mid-pour. Have the sliced scallion seasoned and waiting in a heatproof bowl.'
  - 'Use a heatproof bowl, metal or thick ceramic. The oil reaches roughly 160 to 170 °C / 320 to 340 °F at the pour; a thin glass bowl can crack. A small heavy saucepan to heat the oil and a heatproof bowl to receive it is the whole rig.'
  - 'This is a finishing condiment, not a standalone dish. Plan it as the last thing made before plating grilled meat or com tam, or make it ahead and refrigerate; it keeps about a week and the flavor peaks at days 1 to 3.'

steps:
  - text: 'Slice 100 g / 8 to 10 stalks scallion crosswise into thin 2 to 3 mm rings, whites and greens together, and put them in a heatproof metal or thick-ceramic bowl. Slicing rather than mincing is the texture target: mo hanh reads as distinct slack rings suspended in oil, not the dense paste of the Cantonese version. The greens carry most of the color, so keep them in.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Stir 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt and 4 g / 1 tsp granulated sugar through the sliced scallion until evenly coated. The salt and sugar season the scallion from the surface inward and pull a little water to the cut edges, which the oil will flash off at the pour. The sugar is the Southern signature; it rounds the raw allium and the salt so the finished oil reads balanced rather than sharp.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Heat 120 ml / ½ cup neutral oil in a small heavy saucepan over medium-high until it shimmers and just begins to wisp, about 160 to 170 °C / 320 to 340 °F. This is cooler than the Cantonese ginger-scallion pour: the goal is to blanch the scallion and fix the green, not to deep-fry it to gold. The cue without a thermometer is a single scallion ring dropped in sizzling steadily within a second or two, not violently, and not browning. Pull off heat the moment it reaches that point.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pour the hot oil over the seasoned scallion in one steady stream. The oil will sizzle and foam for 10 to 15 seconds as the surface water flashes off; this brief contact is what quenches the allinase enzyme that drives raw onion pungency, softening the bite, and fixes the chlorophyll green so it stays emerald instead of dulling to khaki. Stir once or twice with a heatproof spoon to submerge every ring. The scallion should go slack and bright green within seconds, not brown; if any rings darken past green, the oil was too hot, which is the one common failure here.'
    technique: 'hot-oil-finish'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Let the oil cool 2 to 3 minutes to warm, then stir in the optional 5 g / 1 tsp fish sauce and the 5 g / 1 tsp fresh lime juice. Both go in off the heat: fish sauce added to smoking oil scorches and turns acrid, and lime added hot flashes off its bright top notes. The fish sauce is the umami anchor and the lime is the finishing acid; together they take mo hanh from a plain scallion fat to a seasoned condiment that needs nothing else on the plate.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Use warm, spooned generously over com tam (broken rice) and grilled meat, or transfer to a clean glass jar (scallion and oil together) and refrigerate. Stir before each use; the scallion settles and the oil clarifies on top. Pull from the fridge 10 minutes before serving so the oil regains its pourable consistency.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  How mo hanh differs from Cantonese ginger-scallion oil. Both pour hot oil over raw scallion, but they are different preparations aimed at different dishes. The Cantonese ginger-scallion oil minces ginger and scallion into a dense paste and pours oil at 175 to 190 °C to deep-fry the aromatics in place, building a thick savory paste for white-cooked chicken. Mo hanh has no ginger, slices the scallion into rings rather than mincing it, pours the oil cooler (160 to 170 °C) so the scallion blanches and stays slack and emerald rather than frying to gold, and carries a pinch of sugar for the Southern-sweet seasoning balance. The result is a thinner, brighter, pourable oil whose job is to gloss broken rice and grilled meat, not to anchor a plate as a paste.

  The allinase quench is the technique. Cut scallion releases allinase, the enzyme that converts the cell's sulfur precursors into the volatile thiosulfinates responsible for raw onion bite. At room temperature that reaction runs for minutes and the scallion stays harsh; the brief contact with hot oil denatures the enzyme within seconds, arresting the reaction and locking the flavor at a softened point. The same flash fixes the chlorophyll, setting the bright green before the heat can degrade it to dull khaki. Pour too cool and the scallion stays raw and oniony; pour too hot and it browns past green into a bitter fried note. The 160 to 170 °C window is the target.

  Why the oil goes cooler than the ginger-scallion version. The defining visual of a good mo hanh is slack, bright green scallion rings suspended in clear oil, not browned bits. Because there is no dense ginger to protect and no paste to build, the scallion is fully exposed to the oil and browns fast; pulling the oil at 160 to 170 °C rather than at smoking gives the scallion the brief blanch it needs without tipping into fried. If the rings darken at the pour, the oil was too hot, drop it 10 to 15 °C next time.

  How to use it. Spoon mo hanh over com tam (broken rice) with grilled pork chop (suon nuong) or shredded pork skin; over any grilled meat off the charcoal; over a plate of plain jasmine rice with a fried egg; stirred into a bowl of rice noodles with grilled meat (bun thit nuong); brushed inside a banh mi. It is a finishing oil, never a cooking oil; the bloom is the only heat it sees.

  Storage. Refrigerate in a sealed glass jar with the scallion and oil together. It keeps about a week refrigerated; the flavor peaks at days 1 to 3 and the green slowly dulls after that. Never introduce water (a wet spoon, a condensation-beaded lid) into the jar; water is what turns any aromatic oil rancid and cloudy. Pull from the fridge 10 minutes before serving so the oil loosens from its fridge-set thickness.
