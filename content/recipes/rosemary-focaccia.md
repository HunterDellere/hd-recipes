title: 'Rosemary focaccia'
desc: 'A high-hydration bread-flour dough (80% water) built with a 24-to-48-hour cold ferment for deep wheat and lactic flavor, no-knead with three sets of stretch-and-folds to develop gluten passively, proofed in an olive-oil-flooded sheet pan, then dimpled and flooded a second time with extra-virgin olive oil, a salamoia brine, flaky salt, and rosemary before baking hot. The result is the Ligurian ideal: a crisp olive-oil-fried bottom crust, a top crackle of caramelized brine, and an interior that is open, airy, and saturated with grassy oil. The brine-on-top technique is the structural move that separates a real focaccia from a flatbread, the surface puddles of oil and salted water steam-fry the crust and keep the crumb from drying.'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a two-day bake. Mix the dough on day one, then cold-ferment it in the refrigerator 24 to 48 hours. The flavor and the open crumb both come from that long cold rest, do not shortcut it. Budget the dough mix plus three fold sets (about 2 hours of low-attention time) on day one, and 3 to 4 hours of proof-and-bake on day two.'
  - 'The dough is 80% hydration and will look like a wet, shaggy, almost pourable batter, not a kneadable ball. This is correct. Do not add flour to firm it up, the wetness is what produces the open crumb. Wet hands, not floured hands, when you handle it.'
  - 'Oil the sheet pan generously before the dough goes in for its final proof, the bottom layer of oil is what fries the underside into a crisp golden crust rather than steaming it pale. Use a metal sheet pan, not glass, glass conducts heat poorly and the bottom will not crisp.'
  - 'Make the salamoia brine (the emulsion of olive oil, water, and salt whisked together) just before the final dimple-and-flood, and whisk it again right before you pour, it separates fast.'

steps:
  - text: 'Day one, mix the dough. In a large bowl, whisk together the 400 g / 1⅔ cups lukewarm water, 4 g / 1¼ tsp instant yeast, and 7 g / 1 tsp honey until the yeast disperses. Add the 500 g / 4 cups bread flour, the 12 g / 2 tsp fine sea salt, and the 25 g / 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil. Stir with a wooden spoon or a wet hand until no dry flour remains and you have a wet, sticky, shaggy mass. Do not knead, the dough develops on its own over the long ferment. It should look like a thick batter, not a smooth ball. Cover the bowl with a damp towel.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Day one, three sets of stretch-and-folds. Over the next 90 minutes, perform three sets of folds spaced about 30 minutes apart. For each set, wet one hand, reach under one side of the dough, stretch it up and over to the center, then rotate the bowl a quarter turn and repeat four times around. The dough tightens noticeably from slack batter to a cohesive, jiggly, gluten-networked mass across the three sets. This passive development replaces kneading and aligns the gluten so the dough can trap the gas the long ferment produces. Re-cover with the damp towel between sets.'
    time_min: 90
  - text: 'Day one, cold ferment. After the third fold set, cover the bowl tightly with a fitted lid or a plate and refrigerate 24 to 48 hours. This is the flavor step. Slow cold fermentation lets amylase enzymes break starch into sugars and the yeast produce organic acids, building a complex wheaty, faintly sour, deeply savory flavor that a fast proof never reaches. The dough will roughly double and develop a bubbled surface. Longer (toward 48 hours) reads more flavorful and faintly more sour, shorter (24 hours) reads cleaner and sweeter, both are correct.'
    time_min: 1440
  - text: 'Day two, pan the dough. Pour the 40 g / 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil into a 33 × 23 cm / 9 × 13 inch metal sheet pan and spread it across the bottom and up the sides with your fingers. Scrape the cold fermented dough out of the bowl directly into the oiled pan with a wet bowl scraper, letting it fall as a single slab. Turn the dough once to coat both faces in the pan oil, then leave it. Do not press it out yet, it is too tight straight from the fridge and will fight back.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Day two, room-temperature proof. Cover the pan with a damp towel and let the dough proof at warm room temperature (around 24 to 26 °C / 75 to 78 °F) for 2 to 4 hours, until it has relaxed, spread toward the corners on its own, and looks puffy and bubbled. As it warms and proofs it will become extensible. Halfway through, with oiled fingertips, gently coax it toward the corners of the pan without deflating it, it will resist at first, rest 20 minutes, then coax again. The dough is ready when it fills most of the pan, jiggles like a set custard, and has a visibly bubbled surface.'
    time_min: 180
  - text: 'Preheat. Set an oven rack in the lower third and preheat to 230 °C / 450 °F at least 30 minutes before baking, with a baking stone or steel on the rack if you have one (it drives the bottom crust). A fully preheated hot oven is what fries the oil-flooded bottom into a crisp crust rather than steaming a pale soft one.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Make the salamoia and dimple. Whisk the 45 g / 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil, the 30 g / 2 tbsp water, and 2 g / ½ tsp of the flaky salt into a cloudy emulsion, the salamoia brine. Whisk again right before pouring, it separates fast. Pour the brine evenly over the proofed dough. Now dimple: with all ten fingertips, press straight down through the dough to the bottom of the pan in rows across the whole surface, spaced about 3 cm / 1 inch apart. Press confidently, the deep dimples are structural, they hold the brine in pools so each well steam-fries and lacquers as it bakes, and they keep the crumb from doming into a loaf. The surface should look like a cratered, oil-puddled landscape.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Top and bake. Scatter the 6 g / 2 tbsp rosemary leaves and the remaining 3 g / 1 tsp flaky salt across the surface, nudging some leaves into the oil pools so they do not scorch dry. Bake at 230 °C / 450 °F for 18 to 20 minutes, until the top is turning golden and the edges are deeply bronzed. Pull the pan, scatter the 30 g / ⅓ cup grated parmigiano reggiano evenly over the top, and return for a final 5 to 7 minutes until the cheese has browned into a savory crackle and the whole top is golden-mahogany. Total bake is 23 to 27 minutes. The bottom should be crisp and golden when you lift a corner with a spatula, if it is pale, give it 3 more minutes directly on the stone or the pan floor.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Finish and rest. Slide the focaccia out of the pan immediately onto a wire rack, do not let it sit in the pan or the trapped steam softens the crisp bottom you just built. While it is still hot, grate the zest of ½ lemon (3 g) directly over the surface, the citrus oils cut the richness of the olive oil and lift the rosemary, and they only survive if added off the heat. Let it cool 10 minutes before cutting, the crumb is still setting straight out of the oven and a too-early cut compresses it gummy. Cut into 12 squares with a serrated knife. Eat warm.'
    time_min: 12

notes: |
  Why high hydration. At 80% hydration the dough holds enough free water that the gluten network stays loose and extensible, and the steam generated inside the crumb during the bake inflates the open, irregular, custard-soft holes that define a good focaccia. A drier dough (60 to 65%) bakes into a tighter, more uniform, sandwich-bread crumb, fine, but not focaccia. The cost of high hydration is that the dough is impossible to knead by hand, which is exactly why the no-knead stretch-and-fold method exists: it develops the gluten passively over time without ever asking you to handle a sticky batter on a counter.

  Why the long cold ferment is the whole recipe. The 24-to-48-hour refrigerator rest is where the flavor lives. At cold temperature the yeast works slowly while amylase enzymes keep cleaving starch into fermentable and browning sugars, and lactic and acetic acids accumulate from background bacterial and yeast activity. The result is a dough that tastes of wheat, nuts, and a faint pleasant sourness rather than just yeast and salt. Skip the cold ferment and you have bread that is structurally fine and flavorfully flat. If you only have one day, use the same-day modification, but understand you are trading away the best part.

  The salamoia brine, explained. Salamoia is simply olive oil, water, and salt whisked into a temporary emulsion and poured over the dimpled dough before baking. It does three things at once. The water pools in the dimples and flashes to steam in the hot oven, which keeps the top of the crumb moist and tender even as the surface browns. The oil fries the surface and the dimple edges into a crisp, lacquered, golden crust. The salt seasons the crust directly. A focaccia made without the brine, with oil alone, is good, but the brine is the difference between a crackling, glassy, professionally textured top and a merely oily one. Whisk it right before pouring, it separates within a minute.

  The dimples are structural, not decorative. Pressing your fingertips all the way to the pan bottom does two jobs: it holds the brine in discrete pools so each well steam-fries independently, and it interrupts the gluten so the bread spreads flat and even instead of doming into a single inflated loaf. Press confidently and deeply. Timid dimples spring back and you lose both effects.

  The umami and acid that keep it from being plain bread. Focaccia genovese is traditionally just dough, oil, and salt, and it is excellent that way. This version adds two functional finishers: parmigiano reggiano grated over the top in the last minutes of the bake, where its glutamate browns into a savory crackle (the focaccia col formaggio tradition from Recco, just down the Ligurian coast), and lemon zest grated over the warm bread off the heat, whose citrus oils cut the olive-oil richness and lift the rosemary. Both are optional in the strictest classic sense, but together they make the bread read as a complete, craveable thing rather than a vehicle for oil. If you want the austere classic, omit both and lean on excellent olive oil and flaky salt.

  Storage and reheat. Focaccia is best the day it is baked. It keeps at room temperature, cut-side down on a board under a damp towel or in a bread bag, for 2 days, the crumb stales fastest. To revive, sprinkle the cut surface with a few drops of water and warm in a 180 °C / 350 °F oven for 5 minutes, the crust crisps back and the crumb softens. It freezes well: cut into squares, freeze on a tray, then bag, and reheat from frozen at 180 °C / 350 °F for 8 to 10 minutes. Do not refrigerate, the fridge stales bread faster than the counter.
