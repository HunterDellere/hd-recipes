The structure is unchanged. Fry the floured fish in butter, set it aside warm, then push the same butter to beurre noisette while the sliced almonds toast in it, and finish off heat with lemon and parsley. The brown-butter milk solids and the Worcestershire supply the glutamate; the lemon is the finishing acid.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Have everything measured and within reach before the first fillet goes into the pan. The brown-butter window is a matter of seconds, and once the almonds are in there is no time to hunt for the lemon or pick the parsley. Juice the lemon, chop the parsley, and portion the almonds into a small bowl first.'
  - 'Keep the fried trout warm while you build the sauce. Set a plate in a low oven (90°C / 200°F) or under loose foil; the fish holds its crust for the two minutes the beurre noisette takes, and a cold plate kills the dish.'
  - 'Use a stainless or light-colored pan, not non-stick. You have to read the butter by color through the foam to catch the beurre noisette at amber, and a dark or coated pan hides the cue. The same pan carries the fond from frying the fish straight into the sauce.'

steps:
  - text: 'Pat the 280 g / 2 trout fillets bone-dry on both sides with paper towels and run a finger down each to check for stray pin bones; pull any you find. A dry surface is the precondition for a crust, because surface water has to boil off before the flour can brown. Season the fillets on both sides with about half of the 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt and all of the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Set up the dredge in two shallow dishes. Pour the 120 g / ½ cup whole milk into the first. In the second, whisk the 60 g / ½ cup all-purpose flour with the remaining kosher salt from the 5 g total. The milk dip is optional but worth it: it wets the surface evenly so the flour grabs in a thin, continuous coat rather than patchy clumps, and the milk sugars contribute to browning. Dip each fillet in the milk, let the excess run off, then press both sides into the seasoned flour. Lift and shake off all but a whisper-thin film; a heavy coat steams instead of crisping. Set the dredged fillets on a rack.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Heat the 14 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil and the 28 g / 2 tbsp frying butter in a wide stainless or light-colored skillet over medium-high until the butter foam subsides and the fat shimmers. The oil raises the effective smoke point so the butter can brown the fish without scorching. Lay the fillets in flesh-side down (skin up) and fry undisturbed 2 to 3 minutes until the underside is deep gold; resist moving them, since the crust releases itself when it has set. Flip and fry the skin side 2 minutes more, until the flesh is just opaque at the center and reads about 52 to 54°C. Trout is thin and cooks fast; pull it the moment it turns opaque rather than chasing color on the second side.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Transfer the fried trout to the warm plate and hold it in the low oven or under loose foil. Wipe the pan only if the flour fond has scorched; if it is golden, leave it, because it folds flavor into the sauce. The pan goes back on the heat for the beurre noisette and the timing tightens from here, so move with intent.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the 85 g / 6 tbsp butter to the same pan over medium heat. Let it melt, foam, and then quiet as the water boils off. When the foam thins, add the 50 g / ½ cup sliced almonds and swirl constantly. The almonds and the butter''s milk solids brown together: the butter passes through pale yellow to amber, the almonds go from white to gold, and the smell turns from buttery to toasted hazelnut. Swirl, do not whisk, so the milk solids stay in contact with the hot pan. Pull the pan off the heat the instant the butter is amber and the almonds are golden, which is roughly 2 to 3 minutes in and a window of seconds wide; carryover in the hot pan will take both a shade darker after you pull it.'
    technique: 'brown-butter'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Off the heat, and standing back because it will sputter, add the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice and the 5 g / 1 tsp Worcestershire sauce to the brown butter and swirl. The lemon is the finishing acid that cuts the richness and brightens the toasted-almond aroma; the Worcestershire is the Creole dash, layering anchovy and tamarind glutamate under the nutty brown-butter depth. Swirl in the 8 g / 2 tbsp chopped parsley. Taste the sauce and correct with a pinch of the kosher salt if the brown butter reads flat.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Plate the warm trout fillets and spoon the almond beurre noisette, the 85 g / 6 tbsp brown butter with its toasted almonds, over each so the almonds and parsley land on top. Serve immediately, while the sauce is still foaming and the crust is still crisp. The dish does not hold: the lemon-brightened brown butter is at its peak in the first minute on the plate.'
    time_min: 1

    use: 'Arctic char, branzino, or catfish fillets'
    note: 'Arctic char is the closest by flesh and fat, a salmonid like trout. Branzino fillets pan-fry cleanly at the same thickness. Catfish is the Creole-context swap, the fish New Orleans kitchens fry alongside trout. Avoid salmon: too thick and fatty for the thin crust and the delicate sauce.'

notes: |
  Why brown butter is the whole dish. Trout amandine is meunière (floured fish fried in butter) plus a beurre noisette finish, and the beurre noisette is where the flavor lives. Browning the butter runs a Maillard reaction on the milk-solid caseins, which release furfurals, pyrazines, and diacetyl: the toasted, nutty, popcorn-and-butter compounds that read as far more than fat and salt. Toasting the sliced almonds in that same butter stacks a second set of roasted aromatics on top. This is the umami anchor of the dish, reinforced by the Worcestershire glutamate; the lemon at the end is the acid that keeps all that richness from going heavy. Skip the browning and you have fried fish under melted butter, which is a different and lesser thing.

  The timing is the difficulty. There is no hard step here, but the beurre noisette window is seconds wide and it happens at the end, with the fried fish already waiting. The amber stage tips to burnt in about thirty seconds, and carryover heat in the pan keeps cooking it after you pull it off the burner, so amber-and-pulled is correct, not amber-and-held. Everything downstream (lemon, Worcestershire, parsley) goes in off the heat both to stop the browning and to protect the volatile lemon and parsley aromatics from cooking flat. This is why the mise has to be complete before the first fillet hits the pan.

  Why trout, and why not salmon. Trout sits at a moderate 6 to 8% fat, lean enough to take a thin, crisp flour crust and mild enough to carry a delicate brown-butter sauce rather than fight it. A salmon fillet is thicker and fattier; it does not crust the same way and it overwhelms the amandine sauce. If you cannot get trout, reach for another lean-to-moderate fine-fleshed fish (Arctic char, Dover sole, branzino, catfish in a Creole kitchen) before you reach for salmon. See the rainbow trout ingredient page for the full substitution logic by use case.

  The seasoning curve. Salt the fish before the dredge, salt the flour, and correct the finished sauce at the end. The fish needs its own seasoning under the crust; the flour seasons the crust itself; and the brown butter, which has no added salt until the end, needs a final pinch to bring the nutty depth forward. Taste the sauce before it goes on the plate. The Worcestershire and the lemon both carry salt and acid, so adjust the final pinch after they are in, not before.
