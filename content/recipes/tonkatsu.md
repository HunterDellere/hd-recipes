source:
  name: 'Yōshoku (Western-influenced Japanese) tradition'
  note: 'Tonkatsu (豚カツ) emerged in late Meiji-era Japan (early 1900s) as a Japanese adaptation of the European pork cutlet (Wiener schnitzel or French côtelette). The name combines "ton" (pork) with "katsu" (a Japanese pronunciation of "cutlet"). The dish became canonical yōshoku by the 1930s and is now standard Japanese home and restaurant fare; specialist tonkatsu restaurants (tonkatsu-ya) are common in Japan. The thick-cut deep-fried style with panko is the canonical contemporary form.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'pulling pork loin at internal 62°C / 144°F'
    ref: 'safety/meat-doneness#pork-whole-muscle'
    why: 'USDA published-safe target for whole-muscle pork has been 63°C / 145°F + 3-minute rest since the 2011 revision (the previous 71°C / 160°F target was for Trichinella, which is essentially eliminated in US commercial pork). Pulling at 62°C and resting 3 minutes brings the meat to 64 to 65°C internal, cleanly above the published threshold. The result is medium-pink, juicy pork; cooking past 70°C produces the dry, chalky cardboard pork the old USDA target gave America for decades.'
  - for: 'chicken katsu modification: pulling chicken at 71°C / 160°F internal (with carryover to 165°F via rest)'
    ref: 'safety/meat-doneness#poultry'
    why: 'Pulled at 160°F internal then rested 3 minutes covered carries the meat to 165°F (USDA published-safe instantaneous). Pulling at 165°F directly produces dry chicken. Vulnerable diners (pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, very old) should be served chicken cooked to 165°F internal with no carryover credit.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Pound the cutlets to even 1.5 cm thickness. Uneven cutlets cook unevenly; the thick parts read raw when the thin parts are overcooked. Use a meat mallet, the bottom of a heavy pan, or a rolling pin between two sheets of parchment.'
  - 'Score the silverskin and connective tissue along the edges of the pork loin before breading. The connective tissue contracts in the fry and curls the cutlet into a bowl shape; scoring it every 2 cm releases the contraction so the cutlet stays flat.'
  - 'Cook the rice as a PARALLEL TRACK. Japanese short-grain rice (Koshihikari, Tamanishiki) needs washing, 30-minute soak, 18-minute simmer, 10-minute covered rest. Plan to start the rice 45 minutes before serving so it lands hot when the tonkatsu finishes frying.'
  - 'Use a thermometer in the oil. Tonkatsu requires 170°C / 340°F oil at the start; the temperature drops 20°C when cold cutlets hit, and the recovery to 165°C / 330°F working temperature requires burner adjustment. Frying at 150°C produces soggy katsu with pale crust; frying at 190°C burns the panko before the pork cooks.'

steps:
  - text: 'Prep the pork. Pat the 600 g pork cutlets dry. Lay each cutlet between two sheets of parchment. Pound with a meat mallet, the bottom of a heavy pan, or a rolling pin to an even 1.5 cm / ⅝ inch thickness. With the tip of a sharp knife, score through the silverskin and connective tissue along the edges of each cutlet, making cuts about 1 cm deep every 2 cm. The scoring releases the contraction that would otherwise curl the cutlet into a bowl shape in the fry. Sprinkle both sides of all 4 cutlets evenly with the 6 g kosher salt and 2 g black pepper.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Rest the seasoned cutlets. Let the salted cutlets stand 30 to 45 minutes at room temperature. The salt draws surface moisture out, then re-absorbs as a thin brine that seasons through the cutlet and primes the surface to bond with the flour dredge.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Stage the breading station. Set three wide shallow bowls or pie plates in a row: (1) 80 g all-purpose flour, (2) the 2 beaten eggs with 15 g water, (3) 200 g panko. The order is dry-wet-dry; the flour dries the surface so the egg adheres, the egg is the adhesive for the panko, the panko is the crust. Set a wire rack at the end of the line for the breaded cutlets to rest.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Bread the cutlets. Working one at a time, dredge each cutlet in the 80 g flour, pressing the flour into both sides and shaking off excess. Dip into the egg, coating both sides fully. Lay onto the 200 g panko; press panko firmly onto both sides, including the edges, and into any crevices. Lift and shake off any panko that has not bonded. Set on the wire rack. Repeat with all 4 cutlets. Let the breaded cutlets rest 10 minutes; the panko bonds tightly to the egg-flour layer and is less likely to slough off in the fry.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Heat the oil. Pour 1500 g neutral oil into a heavy 4 to 5 quart Dutch oven to a depth of 5 cm / 2 inch. Clip a deep-fry thermometer to the side of the pot. Heat over medium-high until the oil reads 175°C / 350°F (the starting temperature; the oil drops to ~160°C / 320°F when cold breaded cutlets hit it). Preheat the oven to 90°C / 200°F with a wire rack set over a sheet pan inside (for holding fried katsu while you fry batches).'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Fry, batch 1 (2 cutlets). Carefully lower 2 breaded cutlets into the hot oil with tongs, pulling away from yourself. The oil should bubble aggressively around the panko; that is the cue the temperature is right. Adjust the burner to maintain 165 to 170°C through the fry. Fry undisturbed 3 to 4 minutes per side until the panko is a deep golden-brown. Total fry time: 7 to 8 minutes for a 1.5 cm cutlet. Check the internal temperature in the thickest part with an instant-read; pull when it reads 62°C / 144°F.'
    technique: 'shallow-frying'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Drain and rest. Lift the cutlets out of the oil with tongs; let excess oil drip back into the pot for 3 seconds. Stand the cutlets on their edges on a wire rack (not flat; standing them lets oil drain off both sides simultaneously and keeps the bottom crust crispy). Hold in the 90°C oven while you fry batch 2.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Fry, batch 2 (2 cutlets). Bring the oil back to 175°C (takes 1 to 2 minutes between batches). Fry the second 2 cutlets the same way, 3 to 4 minutes per side until deep gold and 62°C internal. Drain and rest on the rack with batch 1; carryover will bring the internal to 64 to 65°C during the rest.'
    technique: 'shallow-frying'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Slice and plate. Place each rested cutlet on a cutting board. Slice across the grain into 1.5 to 2 cm thick strips with a sharp knife (a serrated knife shatters the crust; a sharp chef knife cuts cleanly). Mound 75 g of the shredded ice-water cabbage on one side of each dinner plate. Lay the sliced cutlet next to the cabbage, reassembled to look like a whole cutlet sliced. Set a lemon wedge on the cabbage. Serve with a small bowl of tonkatsu sauce (20 g per person) and a separate bowl of cooked short-grain rice. Offer the optional karashi mustard on the side. The traditional eating pattern: cabbage to refresh the palate between bites of rich fried pork, lemon squeezed over the cabbage and the cutlet, sauce drizzled or used as a dip per bite, rice as the foundation.'
    time_min: 4

notes: |
  Panko is the dish. The single ingredient that defines Japanese tonkatsu (as opposed to European pork schnitzel) is panko: large irregular flakes of crustless white bread, dried and toasted to a pale tan, with a much higher surface-area-to-mass ratio than standard breadcrumbs. The flakes fry up shatter-crisp with deep air pockets; the resulting crust is dramatically lighter and crunchier than a schnitzel crust. Real Japanese panko (Kikkoman, Yutaka, House Foods) is the canonical ingredient; American "panko-style" breadcrumbs vary in quality, but most are acceptable.

  Why score the silverskin. Pork loin has a sheath of silverskin and connective tissue along its long edges. When that tissue hits the hot oil, it contracts faster than the meat itself, curling the cutlet into a bowl shape with raised edges and a sunken center. Scoring through the silverskin every 2 cm releases the contraction so the cutlet stays flat in the fry, producing even cook and even crust. Skipping this step is the most common failure mode for home tonkatsu and produces curled, unevenly cooked cutlets.

  The 1.5 cm thickness is calibrated. Too thin (under 1 cm) and the pork cooks through before the panko fully develops color; too thick (over 2 cm) and the panko burns before the pork reaches 62°C internal. The 1.5 cm sweet spot gives 3 to 4 minutes per side at 165 to 170°C, producing simultaneously developed crust and properly cooked interior. Pound by hand or with a mallet to an even thickness; uneven cutlets cook unevenly.

  The 2011 USDA pork revision matters here. The old American pork standard (71°C / 160°F) was a Trichinella-prevention number that produced gray, dry, cardboard pork for two generations of American cooks. The 2011 revision dropped it to 63°C / 145°F + 3-minute rest, which is what professional Japanese cooks have always done. Pull tonkatsu at 62°C internal; carryover to 64 to 65°C during the rest; the pork reads pink-blushed, juicy, and entirely safe. Cooks who pull at 71°C are following an outdated and never-actually-necessary safety guideline.

  Shredded cabbage is structural, not garnish. The pile of finely shredded raw cabbage that accompanies tonkatsu (kyabetsu no sengiri) is the canonical Japanese serving convention. The crisp, slightly sweet raw cabbage refreshes the palate between bites of rich fried pork, contributes vitamin C to balance the fat-and-protein meal, and provides textural contrast. The ice-water soak is the technique that makes home cabbage fluff up to restaurant-spec crispness. Skipping the cabbage produces a meal that reads incomplete by Japanese eating convention.

  Tonkatsu sauce versus lemon. Most diners use both. Lemon squeezed over the cutlet just before eating brightens the rich fried pork; tonkatsu sauce (sweet-savory, Worcestershire-derived) adds depth and the canonical Japanese flavor profile. Karashi mustard (hot Japanese mustard, similar to English mustard) is the optional heat accent for diners who want it. The traditional eating order: lemon first, then dip each bite in sauce, with mustard for the bites that want extra punch.

  Tonkatsu-ya restaurants in Japan are specialist establishments. Tonkatsu Maisen (Tokyo, since 1965) and Wako (national chain) are the canonical restaurant references. They use specialty cuts (rosa pork ribeye, kurobuta black hog), pressure-cook the cabbage before shredding for maximum crispness, and grind their own panko fresh. Home tonkatsu can approach restaurant quality if the technique is right; the ingredient upgrades (better pork, fresh panko, real rice bran oil) close the gap.

  Day-2 tonkatsu reheats acceptably. Reheat in a 175°C oven on a wire rack for 8 to 10 minutes; the crust re-crisps and the pork warms through without rubberizing. Microwave reheating is the wrong move (the panko steams soft). Cold sliced leftover tonkatsu in a katsu sando the next day is excellent.
