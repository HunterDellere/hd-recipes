source:
  name: 'The Tunisian and broader Maghreb canon (Tunisia, Algeria, Libya, eastern Morocco). Tunisia is the canonical producer (the country produces more harissa per capita than anywhere else, and Tunisian harissa was inscribed on the UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2022); the documented preparation dates to the 16th century, after the Columbian Exchange brought New World chilies to North Africa. The modern stabilized form (dried red chilies, roasted red pepper, garlic, caraway, coriander, cumin, salt, olive oil) is the post-19th-century commercial and home reference.'
  note: 'Two structural schools: Tunisian harissa is the canonical form, with caraway and coriander as the dominant spice signature, a smooth thick paste consistency, and a deep dark-red color; Moroccan harissa often reads as a thinner sauce closer to a chili-and-tomato condiment, and uses cumin and mint more aggressively than caraway. The version here is the Tunisian canon. Distinct from rose harissa (a modern variant with dried rose petals) and from harissa rouge (the commercial smooth paste sold in tubes); both are valid variations of the same structural sauce. Some preparations include a brief fermentation of the chilies (3 to 5 days at room temperature in salt brine); the version here uses dried chilies (which are already partially fermented during their slow sun-drying) and skips the additional fermentation step in favor of a quicker preparation.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Soak the dried chilies in hot water for 20 minutes before you can build the paste. Start the soak first, then char the bell pepper, roast the garlic, and toast the spices in parallel; everything converges at the food processor.'
  - 'The harissa is not done when you jar it. Plan to rest the finished paste at least 24 hours in the refrigerator before first use; freshly built harissa reads disjointed and aggressive, and the same paste after a day in the fridge reads cohesive and deep. Make this the day before you want to cook with it.'
  - 'Reserve 60 ml of the chili soaking liquid before draining. The liquid is sweet, chili-flavored, and structural for thinning the paste in the processor; pouring it down the drain forces you to thin with plain water and loses flavor.'
  - 'Have a clean glass jar and a thin film of extra olive oil ready for the storage seal. The paste oxidizes and grows mold within days if exposed to air; the oil layer is what gives harissa its 3 to 4 week shelf life, and it needs to be refreshed after every scoop.'

steps:
  - text: 'Soak the 60 g / 8 to 10 dried chilies in 300 ml hot water for 20 minutes until fully softened and pliable. Drain (reserving 60 ml of the soaking liquid, which is sweet, chili-flavored, and structural for thinning the paste). Scrape the soft chili flesh from the skin with the back of a spoon if the skin is tough; discard the skin and any stems. If the chilies are tender enough that the skin breaks down with grinding, skip the scrape.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Char the 150 g / 1 large red bell pepper directly over a gas flame or under a broiler, turning, until the skin is fully blackened on all sides (6 to 8 minutes). Transfer to a bowl, cover with a plate, and let steam 5 minutes; the steam loosens the skin. Peel off the blackened skin (do not rinse under water; the rinse washes away the smoky flavor), remove the seeds and stem, and roughly chop. The char is the structural smoky-sweet base of the harissa.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'In a small dry skillet over medium heat, dry-roast the 15 g / 3 to 4 garlic cloves 5 minutes, turning, until the skins are golden and the cloves are soft; cool and mash. In the same skillet, toast the 4 g / 2 tsp caraway, 4 g / 2 tsp coriander, and 3 g / 1½ tsp cumin seeds for 2 minutes, shaking, until fragrant and slightly darker; transfer to a spice grinder or mortar and grind to a fine powder.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'In the bowl of a food processor (4 to 7 cup capacity), combine the drained chili flesh, the roasted bell pepper, the mashed roasted garlic, the ground spice mix (caraway, coriander, cumin), the 4 g / 2 tsp smoked paprika, the 8 g / 1¼ tsp salt, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp lemon juice. Process 60 seconds, scraping the sides every 15 seconds, until a coarse paste forms. With the motor running, drizzle in the 60 g / ¼ cup olive oil through the feed tube; process another 30 seconds until the paste is uniform and glossy. Add 30 to 60 ml of the reserved chili soaking liquid to thin to a thick spoonable consistency, just slightly looser than tomato paste.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Taste and adjust: harissa should read deeply chili-forward with smoky paprika, structural caraway-and-coriander aromatics, gentle garlic, and a clean salt-and-acid finish; if flat, an extra 1 g / pinch of fine sea salt and 5 g / 1 tsp lemon juice; if too sharp from the chilies, more roasted pepper; if too thick, more soaking liquid. Transfer to a clean glass jar (about 250 ml capacity); smooth the surface with the back of a spoon; pour the additional 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil over the surface to form a continuous seal that prevents oxidation. Refrigerate; let rest at least 24 hours before first use so the flavors integrate. Use after each scoop a clean spoon, smooth the surface, and refresh the oil seal.'
    time_min: 2

modifications:
  - for: 'flavor pivot'
    to: 'as rose harissa (the modern variant with dried rose petals)'
    how: 'Add 5 g / 2 tbsp dried rose petals (food-grade, from a Middle Eastern grocery or specialty spice shop) to the food processor in step 4; grind with the other ingredients. The result is rose harissa, a modern variant popularized by Belazu and other commercial producers; reads more floral and slightly sweeter, with the chili heat softened by the floral aromatics. Pairs especially well with lamb, eggplant, and slow-cooked vegetable stews.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'heat level'
    to: 'as a mild harissa (for cooks who prefer less heat)'
    how: 'Use all mild chilies (guajillo or ancho only) and skip the hot chilies entirely; remove all seeds from the soaked chilies before grinding. The result reads deeply chili-flavored with structural smoky and caraway character, but without the heat backbone; a useful entry point for cooks new to harissa and a brilliant background flavor in stews and soups where heat would dominate.'
    kind: 'heat'
  - for: 'fermentation pivot'
    to: 'as a lacto-fermented harissa (the older Tunisian preparation)'
    how: 'After the paste is built, transfer to a clean glass jar and pack down firmly; cover the surface with a 2 percent salt brine (2 g salt per 100 ml water; about 50 ml brine total). Cap loosely and ferment at room temperature 3 to 5 days, burping the jar daily; the surface will develop a faint white film (kahm yeast, harmless; scrape off and discard) and the paste will turn slightly bubbly and develop a tangy fermented depth. Transfer to the refrigerator after fermentation; the lacto-fermented harissa keeps 6 months refrigerated and reads more complex and slightly funky compared to the unfermented version.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Harissa is one of the most structurally pantry-deep condiments in any global cuisine. The combination of dried-and-rehydrated chilies, roasted fresh pepper, toasted whole spices, roasted garlic, and salt produces a paste with structural complexity that survives weeks of refrigeration and keeps developing in flavor; the long shelf life is what makes harissa a household staple in Tunisia and increasingly across the contemporary cooking diaspora. A single jar of homemade harissa is a month of background flavor for stews, eggs, marinades, and sandwiches.

  The caraway is non-negotiable. The single difference between Tunisian harissa (the canonical form) and the dozens of chili pastes made in other cuisines (sambal, sambal oelek, gochujang, doubanjiang, calabrian chili paste, salsa macha, ezme, muhammara) is the caraway. The slight licorice-citrus-anise note of caraway is the structural Tunisian signature; without it, the paste is recognizable as a chili condiment but not as harissa specifically. Toast the caraway before grinding; the toast deepens the aromatic compounds and integrates the spice with the chili base.

  The oil seal during storage is structural for preservation. Harissa exposed to air oxidizes (the surface darkens visibly) and grows mold within days; the same paste kept under a thin film of olive oil keeps 3 to 4 weeks refrigerated with no visible degradation. After each use, smooth the surface of the remaining paste with the back of a spoon and refresh the oil layer; the discipline of resealing after each scoop is the difference between a 3-day shelf life and a 4-week shelf life. Always use a clean spoon (contamination is the only common failure mode in storage).

  Versatility. Harissa is one of the broadest single condiments in any pantry. Use it as: a flavor base for stews (Tunisian and North African tagines, Moroccan chickpea and lentil stews, North African fish stews; add 1 to 2 tbsp to the cooking liquid for structural depth); a stir-in for soups (lentil, chickpea, vegetable, tomato; a teaspoon stirred into each bowl at service brings smoky chili depth); a marinade for grilled meats (chicken, lamb, pork, fish; mix 30 g harissa with 30 g olive oil and 15 g lemon juice as the marinade base); a finishing condiment for grilled meat (a spoonful on grilled lamb chops, chicken thighs, steak); a sauce for eggs (stir into shakshuka, scrambled eggs, fried eggs; spread under poached eggs on toast; the eggs-and-harissa pairing is canonical across North African breakfast cookery); a spread for sandwiches and wraps (especially with grilled chicken, lamb, roasted vegetables, hummus); a stir-in for hummus and other dips (1 tbsp transforms plain hummus into harissa hummus); a flavor base for roasted vegetable preparations (toss carrots, cauliflower, sweet potato, eggplant with olive oil and 1 tbsp harissa before roasting; the chili paste crusts on the surface during roasting and builds deep flavor); a finishing drizzle (thinned with extra olive oil and a squeeze of lemon) for grain bowls, salads, and grilled vegetables; a flavor base for grain pilafs (stir 1 tbsp into the cooking liquid for rice, couscous, freekeh, farro); a stir-in for Mediterranean tomato sauces (a teaspoon transforms a tomato sauce into a smoky chili-tomato sauce); and as a base for compound butters (whip 30 g harissa into 100 g softened butter for a harissa butter for steaks and corn).

  Pairs across Tunisian (canonical), Algerian, Moroccan, Libyan, broader North African, eastern Mediterranean, modern Israeli cookery (where harissa has become deeply integrated into the contemporary table), contemporary American Mediterranean cooking, and modern French cookery (where harissa appears in stews and grilled meat preparations).

  Storage. Keeps 3 to 4 weeks refrigerated in a sealed glass jar with the oil seal maintained; the lacto-fermented version (see modifications) keeps 6 months. Do not freeze; freezing breaks the emulsion and the paste reads grainy on thawing. The first 24 hours of rest after making is structural for flavor integration; harissa eaten immediately reads disjointed and aggressive, while the same paste after a day in the refrigerator reads cohesive and deep.
