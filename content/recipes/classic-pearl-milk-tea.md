steps:
  - text: 'Build simple syrup. Combine the 120 ml / ½ cup water and 120 g / ½ cup sugar in a small saucepan. Bring to a simmer, stir until sugar fully dissolves (about 90 seconds), then pull off heat. Cool to room temperature. This is enough syrup for 6 drinks; it holds 2 weeks in the refrigerator.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Brew the tea concentrate. Pour the 360 ml / 1½ cups boiling water over the 10 g / 2 tbsp black tea in a heatproof jug. Steep 5 minutes, longer than usual because milk and ice will dilute heavily. Strain. Cool to room temperature; hot tea breaks dairy emulsions and dilutes the cold drink unevenly.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Build the drink. Spoon 80 g / ⅓ cup of the warm cooked tapioca pearls into the bottom of each of two tall glasses (about 400 ml / 14 oz). Drizzle 2 tbsp of the cooled simple syrup over the pearls in each glass, the pearls absorb sweetness directly, which is part of the textural pleasure.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour 120 ml / ½ cup cold whole milk into each glass over the pearls. Add a generous handful of ice (60 to 80 g per glass), about 5 to 6 cubes.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour 180 ml / ¾ cup of the cooled tea concentrate down the inside wall of each glass. The tea pools above the milk briefly, then they begin to merge from the bottom up as the ice melts and shifts the column. Serve immediately with a wide boba straw.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'The eater stirs everything together at the table. A well-built classic pearl milk tea should be a deep tan color (not pale beige, that is undertea), distinctly sweet but not cloying (you should taste tea bitter under the sweet), and chewy with pearls coming up the straw 3 to 4 at a time.'

notes: |
  This is the historical original. Liu Han-Chieh of Chun Shui Tang teahouse in Taichung dropped a portion of his own fen-yuan tapioca dessert into iced milk tea in 1986; the resulting drink became pearl milk tea and the global boba phenomenon followed. The original was not brown-sugar-painted or fruit-flavored or topped with cheese foam, it was tea, milk, syrup, pearls. Most of the bubble-tea innovation of the last decade has been adding things to this base; understanding the base is the foundation.

  Tea strength is the single most important variable. A drink built with under-extracted tea reads as flavored milk; built with proper tea concentrate it tastes like tea, balanced by milk and sweet. Most home boba fails on this point, the brewer reaches for one tea bag in 2 cups of water and the tea disappears under the dairy. Use the full 10 g / 2 tbsp and steep 5 minutes.

  Non-dairy creamer is not a downgrade in this drink. The original 1980s Taiwanese formulation used powdered non-dairy creamer specifically because it was cheap and shelf-stable; the slightly artificial milky note became part of the flavor profile that boba drinkers grew up on. Both versions (whole milk and non-dairy) are legitimate; they taste different.
