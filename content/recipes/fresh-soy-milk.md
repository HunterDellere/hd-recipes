  note: 'Fresh soy milk (豆漿, dou jiang) is a canonical Cantonese-Hong Kong breakfast preparation, served hot and sweet (with sugar) or hot and savory (with soy sauce, dried shrimp, scallion, and shredded zha cai pickle in the Taiwanese xian dou jiang preparation), commonly accompanied by youtiao (fried dough crullers) for dipping. The technique of grinding soaked soybeans with water and straining is the foundational starting point for all soy-based foods: silken tofu (douhua), firm tofu, fermented tofu (furu), soy milk skin (yuba). The fresh preparation tastes dramatically different from commercial supermarket soy milk; sweeter, more bean-forward, with a structural fullness that commercial cannot replicate.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The overnight soak is structural, not optional. The dry soybeans need 8+ hours of cold-water soak to soften enough to break down during the blend; skipping the soak produces gritty grainy soy milk no matter how powerful the blender.'
  - 'The cooking step (20 minutes at controlled simmer) is structurally required for food safety, not optional. Raw soy milk contains trypsin inhibitor, a protein that interferes with digestion and is mildly toxic in significant quantities. The 20-minute cook deactivates the trypsin inhibitor and is the structural difference between safe-to-drink soy milk and raw soy puree.'
  - 'Stir constantly during the cook. Soy milk scorches readily on the pan bottom; the proteins coagulate and stick. A heavy-bottomed pot and constant stirring with a silicone spatula are structural; rushing this step produces a slightly burnt-tasting soy milk that wastes the work.'

steps:
  - text: 'Soak the soybeans. The night before, combine the 250 g dry yellow soybeans and the 1000 ml cold water in a large bowl. Cover loosely (with plastic wrap or a plate). Soak 8 to 12 hours at room temperature; the beans will roughly double in size, the skins will loosen slightly, and the water will turn slightly cloudy.'
    time_min: 480
  - text: 'Drain and rinse. After the soak, drain the soybeans in a colander; rinse under cold running water for 30 seconds, gently rubbing the beans with your hands to dislodge any loose skins (some skins will float; rinse them away). The skins do not need to be fully removed; the cheesecloth strain later removes them anyway. The drained beans should be firm and plump.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Blend the beans with fresh water. Transfer the drained beans to a high-powered blender (Vitamix, Blendtec, or similar; standard blenders work but produce a slightly grittier soy milk). Add the 1500 ml / 6¼ cups cold filtered water. Blend on high for 90 seconds, until the mixture is uniformly smooth and creamy with no visible bean fragments. The resulting liquid will be off-white with a slight greenish-tan tint, and will smell strongly of raw beans (raw, slightly grassy; this is normal at this stage and will mellow during the cook).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Transfer to a heavy-bottomed pot. Pour the blended mixture into a heavy 4 to 5 quart pot (cast iron or thick stainless; thin pots scorch the bottom). The mixture will look thick and creamy.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Bring to a simmer. Place the pot over medium-high heat. Stir constantly with a silicone spatula or wooden spoon, scraping the bottom and edges every 10 to 15 seconds. As the mixture heats, foam will form on the surface and rise toward the top; this is normal. When the surface starts to bubble actively (about 8 to 10 minutes from start), reduce the heat to low.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Cook for 20 minutes. Maintain a gentle simmer at low heat for 20 minutes, stirring constantly. The mixture will reduce slightly, thicken slightly, and develop the structural soy-milk character: the raw-bean smell will mellow into a clean lightly-sweet aroma. The 20-minute cook is structural for food safety (trypsin inhibitor deactivation) and for flavor development (the raw-bean character cooks off). Watch the foam carefully; if it rises too aggressively, lower the heat further. If the mixture sticks to the bottom, pull off heat immediately and stir vigorously to incorporate the stuck portion before continuing.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Add the salt. Stir in the 1 g fine sea salt; cook another 30 seconds. The salt is added at the end so it does not interfere with the blend; it brings out the natural sweetness of the soybeans without making the milk noticeably salty.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Strain through cheesecloth. Line a fine-mesh sieve with two layers of cheesecloth (or a clean unscented kitchen towel); set over a clean pot or large bowl. Pour the cooked mixture through the cheesecloth in batches. After most of the liquid has drained, gather the corners of the cheesecloth into a bundle (be careful, it is hot; use kitchen tongs or wait 5 minutes for it to cool slightly), twist gently, and squeeze firmly to extract the last of the milk. The leftover pulp in the cheesecloth is okara (soybean pulp) and is itself an ingredient; reserve it for baking, vegetable stir-fries, or fritters.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Use or store. The fresh soy milk is now ready for use. For hot Cantonese breakfast: serve immediately in a small bowl with 10 to 15 g of sugar stirred in, plus a youtiao (fried dough cruller) for dipping. For tofu-making: use the hot milk directly while still at 70 to 80°C and add nigari or gypsum coagulant per a tofu recipe. For drinking cold: cool to room temperature (about 30 minutes uncovered), then refrigerate; serve cold or warmed gently. Refrigerated in a sealed container, fresh soy milk keeps 3 to 4 days; the flavor degrades after that point.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why the 20-minute cook is non-negotiable. Raw soybeans (and raw soy milk) contain trypsin inhibitor, a protein that blocks the digestive enzyme trypsin, reducing protein digestion and causing mild gastrointestinal distress. The trypsin inhibitor is heat-labile: 100°C / boiling water for 5 to 7 minutes deactivates about 80 percent; 15 to 20 minutes deactivates over 95 percent. Most home-cook soy milk recipes get this right by recommending a 20-minute simmer; some shortcut recipes (blender-only or microwave-only) do not adequately deactivate the inhibitor and produce mildly upsetting soy milk. The cook is structural; do not skip or shortcut. Commercial supermarket soy milk is heat-treated at higher temperatures during UHT processing and is fully safe.

  Why the cook also develops flavor. Beyond the safety consideration, the 20-minute cook develops the structural soy-milk flavor: the raw-bean grassiness mellows into a clean lightly-sweet aroma, the proteins partially denature and contribute to mouthfeel, and the natural sugars in the soybeans caramelize slightly to add depth. Soy milk cooked for only 5 to 10 minutes tastes noticeably less developed than the full 20-minute version; soy milk cooked for 30+ minutes starts to lose subtle aromatics and reads slightly cooked-over.

  Bean-to-water ratios by application. The 1:6 ratio in this recipe (250 g beans : 1500 ml water) produces a moderately rich soy milk suitable for drinking, breakfast, and most general applications. The 1:5 ratio (250 g beans : 1250 ml water) produces a thicker protein-richer milk structurally better for setting into tofu. The 1:8 ratio (250 g beans : 2000 ml water) produces a thinner more drinkable milk preferred by some cooks for everyday consumption. The 1:10 ratio is used for thin soy milk soups and porridge bases. Each ratio is correct for its application; the 1:6 is the canonical default.

  The okara is structural. The leftover soybean pulp from the strain (about 300 g of moist okara per 250 g dry beans) is a complete ingredient in its own right. Korean kongbiji jjigae (okara soup) and Japanese unohana (sautéed okara with vegetables) are canonical preparations; okara can also be added to baked goods (muffins, cookies, bread) for a fiber-and-protein boost, used as a fritter binder, or sautéed with vegetables as a stir-fry base. The canonical Asian-kitchen practice is full ingredient utilization; throwing away the okara wastes a significant byproduct. Refrigerated in a sealed container, okara keeps 5 days; frozen, 3 months.

  Asian-grocery vs supermarket beans. Asian-grocery soybeans (especially those imported from East Asia, often labeled as "yellow soybeans for tofu" or "non-GMO yellow soybeans") are typically smaller, sweeter, and produce a more aromatic soy milk than US-supermarket organic soybeans. The traditional choice for Cantonese douhua and Japanese tofu is small Asian-cultivar yellow soybeans. US-supermarket beans (Whole Foods, bulk-bin organic) work fine but read slightly less complex; acceptable for everyday use.

  Storage. The fresh soy milk keeps 3 to 4 days refrigerated in a sealed container; the flavor degrades noticeably after that point and the milk develops a slightly sour note as bacterial fermentation starts. Do not store at room temperature; soy milk grows bacteria readily. Frozen: 2 months in a freezer-safe container, though freezing-and-thawing causes some texture changes (slight curdling of the proteins) that affect mouthfeel; acceptable for cooking applications but not for drinking. The dish is structurally a fresh-make preparation; make weekly and use through the week, or make in larger batches and freeze for cooking applications.
