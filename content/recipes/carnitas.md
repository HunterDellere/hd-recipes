Carnitas (literally little meats) is the structural pork dish of the state of Michoacán in western Mexico; large copper pots (cazos de cobre) of lard simmer whole pig parts (head, shoulder, ribs, belly) at Sunday markets and the carnitas master chops the cooked pork to order. steps:
  - text: 'Phase 1, set up the confit. Season the 1800 g pork chunks generously with the 18 g kosher salt (work it into all surfaces; the salt is the structural cure). Let rest 15 minutes at room temperature. Heat the 500 g lard in a large heavy Dutch oven (5 L works) over medium heat until just melted and shimmering, not yet smoking. Add the pork chunks in a single layer; the lard should come about three-quarters of the way up the pork (add more melted lard if needed).'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the aromatics. Squeeze the juice from the 200 g orange into the pot; add the spent orange peels whole. Add the 250 g milk, 200 g quartered onion, 40 g halved garlic head, 3 bay leaves, 5 g canela stick, and 4 g Mexican oregano. Bring to a bare simmer (small bubbles at the surface; the lard should be at about 95 to 100°C / 200 to 212°F, not boiling). Reduce heat to low or medium-low; partially cover with the lid askew (a tight lid produces steam that emulsifies the lard and prevents the final crisp).'
    technique: 'low-and-slow-simmer'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Simmer 2 to 2.5 hours undisturbed. Check the temperature every 30 minutes; adjust burner to maintain the bare simmer. The lard slowly cooks the pork by fat conduction; the meat reads firm at the 90-minute mark and starts to become fork-tender at the 2-hour mark. The structural test for doneness: a piece of pork shreds when pressed with a wooden spoon but holds its overall shape when lifted with tongs. Do not let the lard boil; boiling produces tough stringy meat rather than tender carnitas.'
    time_min: 140
  - text: 'Phase 2, crisp the surface. Remove the spent aromatics from the pot (orange peels, onion quarters, garlic, bay leaves, canela stick) with tongs and discard. Increase heat to medium-high; the lard will start to bubble more aggressively as the milk lactose caramelizes. Continue cooking 15 to 20 minutes, stirring gently every 3 to 4 minutes, until the pork develops deep brown caramelized edges and the surface crisps against the now-hotter lard. The pork should still be tender inside; the goal is structural surface browning, not deep-frying.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Drain and shred. Lift the pork pieces out of the lard with a slotted spoon onto a sheet pan or large plate; let drain 2 minutes. Discard the bone (the meat falls off easily) and any large fat caps. Shred the pork with two forks into ragged irregular pieces; do not over-shred or chop into uniform cubes (the structural carnitas texture is ragged with mixed crisp edges and tender interior). Strain the lard through a fine-mesh sieve into a heatproof jar; refrigerate for reuse.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Phase 3, warm tortillas and assemble. Warm the 360 g corn tortillas on a dry comal or skillet over medium-high heat 15 to 20 seconds per side; stack and cover with a clean kitchen towel. Set out the 30 g chopped cilantro, 120 g diced raw onion, 90 g lime wedges, and 200 g salsa in small bowls. Build each taco: warm tortilla flat, mound about 50 g shredded carnitas in the center, scatter cilantro and onion over, spoon a teaspoon of salsa, finish with a squeeze of lime. Serve immediately. The diner builds each taco; do not pre-assemble.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  Carnitas is the most forgiving of the long Mexican pork projects and the easiest entry point for a home cook learning the structural techniques of Mexican slow cooking. The cook is unattended for 2 hours, the ingredient list is short, the technique tolerates moderate temperature inattention (the lard buffers the heat and prevents the meat from overcooking), and the resulting pork is dramatically better than any quick-cooked alternative. Master carnitas first; the techniques transfer to barbacoa, cochinita pibil, and most of the long-cooked Mexican braised meats.

  The confit technique is structural. Cooking the pork submerged (or nearly submerged) in lard at a gentle 95 to 100°C / 200 to 212°F is structurally different from braising in water or stock; the fat conducts heat more efficiently than water at the same temperature, the meat does not lose moisture to the cooking medium (since the lard is hydrophobic), and the surface stays in contact with the fat-and-Maillard-products rather than with the steam that comes off a wet braise. The result is meat that is tender through, retains its own moisture, and develops a structural crisp at the surface during the final high-heat phase that water-based braising cannot produce.

  The milk addition is structural and frequently misunderstood. The lactose in the milk caramelizes during the long cook, contributing to the structural surface browning that distinguishes carnitas from generic confit pork. The milk also coagulates into a soft curd that settles at the bottom of the pot and adds a structural dairy depth to the lard; this is a feature, not a problem. Some Mexican cooks use evaporated milk or sweetened condensed milk for additional caramelization; both are acceptable variations.

  The orange peels are structural. The orange juice contributes the structural bright acid; the orange peels contribute the structural bitter-aromatic note from the d-limonene in the peel oil. Both work together to balance the rich fat of the lard and the pork. Skipping the peel and using only juice produces a flat carnitas; using only the peel and no juice produces a bitter carnitas. Use both, in the canonical ratio.

  The crisp-at-the-end phase is the structural difference between carnitas and pulled pork. The 15-to-20-minute high-heat phase at the end of the cook is what produces the ragged crisp-edged texture that defines carnitas; without it, the dish reads as tender shredded pork in lard, which is good but not the same dish. The lard temperature rises from the confit window (95 to 100°C) to a frying window (170 to 180°C) as the milk evaporates; the pork surface caramelizes from this elevated temperature. Stir gently to crisp multiple surfaces without breaking the pork into uniform fragments.

  The shred-with-forks step is the third structural element. Carnitas should be ragged and irregular, with mixed crisp edges and tender interior, not uniform cubes. Pull the pork apart with two forks (or by hand once cool enough to handle) into pieces of varying sizes; the structural texture difference between bites is the point. Chop with a knife only if specifically targeting the al pastor-style uniform dice for tacos; for plate service or sandwich service, leave it ragged.

  Versatility. Carnitas travels well beyond tacos. Use it for: carnitas tortas (sandwich format), carnitas plates over rice with beans, carnitas burritos (rolled in a flour tortilla with rice and beans), carnitas tamales (mixed with masa and steamed in banana leaves), carnitas-stuffed empanadas, carnitas tacos dorados (filled and fried crisp), carnitas chilaquiles (over a bed of tortilla chips in salsa), and as the structural protein in any Mexican composed dish where shredded pork is appropriate.

  Storage. Cooked carnitas keeps 5 days refrigerated; freeze in 250 g portions for 3 months. Reheat in a hot dry skillet 3 to 4 minutes (the carnitas re-crisps with the residual lard); do not microwave, the texture turns rubbery. The strained lard keeps 2 months refrigerated and is the structurally most-flavorful lard for future cooking; use it for refried beans, tamale masa, the next carnitas batch, or any savory application where lard is structural.
