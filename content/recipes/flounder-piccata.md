source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Italian-American piccata technique applied to flatfish'
  note: 'Piccata is the Italian-American template of a thin floured-and-pan-fried cutlet finished with a lemon-caper-wine pan sauce, classically veal or chicken; here it is reset onto flounder, whose thin lean fillets cook in 2 to 3 minutes and ask for exactly this kind of high-acid, briny, glutamate-forward finish. The dish runs on two precise restraints. First, the fish is pulled the instant the flesh turns opaque, because a flatfish fillet crosses from silky to chalky in under a minute. Second, the butter that finishes the sauce is mounted off aggressive heat and never browned, so it thickens into a glossy emulsion rather than splitting into hazelnut-scented butterfat, which is the line that separates piccata from a brown-butter meunière.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The fillets are thin and fast. A flounder or sole fillet sets in 2 to 3 minutes total and overcooks in seconds past that, so have the dredge, the seasoned fish, and every sauce component measured and within reach before the pan goes on. There is no time to mince parsley once the fish is in the oil.'
  - 'Build the entire sauce in the same pan the fish fried in. The browned flour and fish fond left behind are the body of the sauce, so do not wash the pan between frying and saucing; pour off only excess fat.'
  - 'Keep the fish warm and dry while you build the sauce. Hold the fried fillets on a rack set over a warm plate, not stacked on a cold plate where they sweat and the crust softens, then nap them with sauce only at the very end.'

steps:
  - text: 'Pat the 400 g / 4 flounder fillets bone-dry on both sides; surface water steams and prevents the flour from forming a thin crust. Season both sides with the 4 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. Spread the 60 g / ½ cup all-purpose flour on a wide plate. Just before frying, press each fillet into the flour on both sides, then lift and shake off all but a thin film; a heavy dredge gums and fries pale, the goal is a barely-there coat that browns and seals the surface.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Set a wide stainless or carbon-steel skillet over medium-high heat. Add the 28 g / 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil and the 14 g / 1 tbsp butter; the butter is here for flavor and color, so swirl it into the oil and move on before it browns. When the foam subsides and the fat shimmers, lay in the floured fillets in a single layer without crowding (work in two batches if needed).'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Fry the fillets 60 to 90 seconds on the first side, until the underside is golden and the flour has set into a thin crust. Flip once with a thin slotted spatula and fry 45 to 60 seconds on the second side, just until the flesh turns opaque through the center; a 1 cm flatfish fillet is done at 2 to 3 minutes total and goes chalky seconds past that. Pull each fillet as it sets and rest it on a rack over a warm plate. Do not stack them.'
    technique: 'pan-fry'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Pour off all but a thin film of fat from the pan, leaving the browned flour and fish fond behind; that fond is the body of the sauce. Lower the heat to medium and add the 25 g / 1 minced shallot, the 6 g / 1 to 2 minced garlic cloves, and the 4 g / 1 minced anchovy fillet. Sweat 60 to 90 seconds until the shallot is translucent and the anchovy has melted into the fat, stirring so the garlic does not scorch.'
    technique: 'fond-deglaze'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour in the 120 g / ½ cup dry white wine and raise the heat to high to deglaze, scraping the fond off the pan bottom with a wooden spoon. Simmer hard until the wine reduces by about half and no longer smells sharp with alcohol, 2 to 3 minutes. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, the 60 g / ¼ cup water or fish stock, and the 20 g / 2 tbsp drained capers, and let it return to a simmer; the sauce should taste aggressively bright and briny, because it will be tempered by the butter and the mild fish.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pull the pan off the heat and let the bubbling settle for 10 seconds; mounting cold butter into liquid that is too hot splits it into greasy butterfat instead of a glossy emulsion. Add the 45 g / 3 tbsp cold cubed butter a few pieces at a time, swirling the pan constantly, so the butter softens and emulsifies rather than fully melting. The sauce will thicken and turn glossy and opaque, coating the back of a spoon. This is the mounted-butter finish, not a brown butter; the butter must never color.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Stir the 8 g / 2 tbsp chopped parsley into the finished sauce. Taste and correct: the capers and anchovy bring the salt, the lemon and wine bring the acid, so adjust only with a few drops more lemon juice if it reads flat or a pinch more salt if the capers were light. Return the fillets to the pan for 15 to 20 seconds to warm through and bathe in the sauce, or plate the fillets and spoon the sauce over. Serve immediately, while the emulsion is glossy and the fish is just set.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why mounted butter, not brown butter. This is the technical hinge of the dish and the line that separates piccata from sole meunière. In meunière the butter is cooked past the foam until the milk solids toast amber and the fat smells of hazelnut, and that browned butter is the sauce. In piccata the butter does the opposite job: it is added cold, off the heat, into an acidic wine-and-lemon reduction, and swirled so it softens and emulsifies into the water phase without ever fully melting or coloring. The result is a pale, glossy, opaque emulsion that clings to the fish, thickened by intact butterfat globules suspended in the reduction. Let the pan get too hot and the emulsion breaks into a greasy slick of separated butterfat; that broken sauce is, ironically, the beginning of a brown butter. Pull the pan off the heat, mount the butter a few cubes at a time, and keep it moving.

  Where the umami and acid live. Flounder is among the leanest of food fish, roughly 1 to 2% fat, bright white and only faintly sweet, so it cannot anchor a dish on its own and is the entire reason piccata pairs so well with it. The glutamate-rich umami comes from the capers and the optional anchovy, both of which are concentrated savory hits that dissolve invisibly into the sauce, plus the browned flour fond and the butter. The finishing acid is doubled: the dry white wine reduces to a bright backbone and the fresh lemon juice goes in late to keep its volatile top notes. Taste the sauce before it touches the fish and push it toward aggressively bright and briny, because the mild fish and the butter both pull it back toward the middle.

  Why the fish comes out first. A 1 cm flatfish fillet sets in 2 to 3 minutes and crosses from silky-translucent to chalky-dry in under a minute past that, with almost no fat to buffer the overshoot. Frying the fish first, pulling it the instant it turns opaque, holding it warm on a rack, and building the sauce in the empty pan means the fish is never subjected to the several minutes the sauce needs to reduce and mount. The fillets rejoin the sauce only for a 15 to 20 second warm-through at the very end. Trying to cook the fish in the simmering sauce guarantees an overcooked, broken-apart fillet.

  Why the dredge, and keep it thin. The thin flour coat does three things: it browns into a light crust that adds flavor and a touch of texture against the lean flesh, it seals the surface so the delicate flake holds together through the flip, and the small amount of flour that sloughs into the pan helps thicken and stabilize the later sauce. The coat must be thin, pressed on and then shaken back off, and applied at the last second; flour left sitting on a wet fillet turns to paste and fries blond and gummy instead of golden and crisp.

  Sourcing and the pan. Buy thin skinless flounder, fluke, or sole fillets about 1 cm thick and 100 g each; ask the counter for the freshest flatfish and look for firm, bright-white, odorless flesh. Use a wide stainless or carbon-steel skillet, not nonstick: the fond that the flour and fish leave on the pan bottom is the foundation of the sauce, and nonstick surfaces release too little of it to build body. A thin fish spatula is the right tool for flipping the delicate fillets without tearing them.
