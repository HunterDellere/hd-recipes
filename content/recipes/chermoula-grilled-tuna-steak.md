  name: 'Chermoula (charmoula) is the Maghrebi marinade-and-sauce for fish across Morocco, Algeria, and Tunisia, traditionally built in a mortar from fresh cilantro and parsley, garlic, cumin, sweet paprika, and lemon. Grilled or baked tuna and swordfish are among its most common partners.'

  note: 'Split the chermoula into a marinade portion and a finishing portion. The grill cooks the fish fully and chars the marinade onto the surface; the reserved raw paste, stirred loose with oil and lemon, goes on at the end so the bright raw-herb and garlic notes survive the heat. before_you_start:
  - 'Budget 30 minutes of passive marinating before you light the grill. The tuna sits in half the chermoula for 30 minutes (no longer, the lemon acid will start to mush the surface past about 45 minutes), so make the paste first, split it, marinate, and only then heat the grill.'
  - 'Make a full batch of chermoula and reserve half raw. Half seasons the fish as a marinade and chars on the grill; the other half is the finishing sauce that goes on uncooked, so do not grill all of it. The raw finishing portion is where the bright cilantro-garlic-lemon lift lives.'
  - 'The fish is cooked through, so any fresh tuna steak from a normal counter works, no sashimi grade. Pull it at an internal 55 to 58 °C, slightly underdone in the center, because it climbs to a moist medium off the heat. Tuna over 65 °C turns chalky and dry; the grill moves fast, so watch it.'

steps:
  - text: 'Toast and grind the spice. In a dry skillet over medium heat, toast the 4 g / 2 tsp cumin seeds for about 60 to 90 seconds, agitating, until fragrant and a shade darker. Grind them with the 3 g / 1 tsp ground coriander in a mortar or spice grinder. Toasting the cumin whole rather than using pre-ground is the single biggest flavor upgrade here; the aroma is sharper and nuttier.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pound the chermoula. In a mortar (or pulse in a small processor), work the 12 g / 4 cloves garlic with a pinch of the salt to a paste, then add the 30 g / 1 bunch cilantro and 15 g / ½ bunch parsley and pound to a coarse green paste. Work in the ground cumin-coriander, the 4 g / 2 tsp sweet paprika, the 1 g / ½ tsp smoked paprika, the 1 g / ½ tsp red pepper flakes, and the remaining 5 g / 1¼ tsp salt total. Stir in the 30 g / 1 lemon of juice and the 70 g / 5 tbsp olive oil until it loosens into a spoonable sauce.'
    technique: 'mortar-pounding'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Split and marinate. Spoon half the chermoula into a separate bowl and set aside, this is the raw finishing sauce, do not let it touch the fish. Coat both 340 g / 12 oz tuna steaks all over with the other half and let them sit 30 minutes at cool room temperature. The lemon lightly firms the surface and the spice paste penetrates; do not go past 45 minutes or the acid starts to mush the exterior.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Heat the grill hot. Bring a charcoal or gas grill to high, clean and oil the grate well, the marinade and lean fish stick easily. The grate must be genuinely hot so the steaks release cleanly and char fast; a cool grate tears the surface and the fish cooks before it marks.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Grill, cooking through. Lay the marinated steaks on the hot grate and grill undisturbed about 2 to 3 minutes per face, until well-marked and the paste has charred to a fragrant crust. The center should cook fully but stay just-translucent at the very middle when you pull it; an instant-read thermometer should read 55 to 58 °C at the thickest point. Pull immediately, lean grilled tuna goes from juicy to dry in well under a minute of extra heat.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Dress with the raw chermoula and rest. Move the steaks to a plate, spoon the reserved raw chermoula generously over the top, and let them rest 2 minutes. The residual heat warms the raw paste just enough to release its aroma while keeping its fresh, sharp brightness intact, and the rest lets the center finish to a moist medium. Serve with lemon wedges (15 g / ½ lemon per plate) and any pooled sauce spooned back over.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why split the chermoula. A single chermoula does two different jobs that pull in opposite directions. As a marinade it wants heat: the spices bloom, the garlic mellows, the paste chars onto the fish and builds a savory crust. As a sauce it wants to stay raw: the whole point of chermoula is the bright, sharp, almost aggressive freshness of raw cilantro, raw garlic, and lemon, all of which the grill destroys. Using one batch for both gives you a dish that is either under-charred or has lost its lift. Splitting it (half cooked onto the fish, half spooned on raw at the end) lets the same paste deliver both the depth and the brightness.

  Why toast and grind your own cumin. Pre-ground cumin sitting in a jar has lost most of its volatile aromatics; the difference between it and seeds you toast and grind to order is not subtle. Toasting drives a quick Maillard and Strecker reaction in the seed that builds nutty, deeper notes on top of the raw cumin's earthiness. In a paste this cumin-forward, that step is most of the flavor, so do not skip it for the jar.

  Why the 30-minute marinade cap. Chermoula is acidic, and acid on raw fish is a slow chemical cook (the same denaturation that drives ceviche). A short marinade seasons and lightly firms the surface, which is good. A long one, past about 45 minutes, starts to denature and mush the exterior so it grills to a soft, pasty texture rather than taking a clean char. If you need to prep ahead, hold the fish and the paste separately and combine only 30 minutes before grilling.

  The cooked-through promise. This is built for fish you cannot or do not want to eat raw. Grilled fully to a moist medium, it asks nothing of the source beyond freshness, so it is the recipe to reach for when the counter cannot confirm sashimi grade. The lean cooked flesh leans hard on the chermoula for moisture and flavor, which is exactly why the finishing sauce is non-negotiable and why you pull the fish a few degrees early.
