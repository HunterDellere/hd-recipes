title: 'Spanakopita'
desc: 'The Greek spinach pie: heavily wilted and squeezed-dry fresh spinach combined with crumbled feta, scallions, dill, parsley, beaten egg, and grated kefalotyri, layered between sheets of olive-oil-brushed phyllo dough, scored into portions, and baked until the phyllo is shatteringly crisp and the filling has just-set. Structurally a study in moisture management: the spinach must be dried aggressively after wilting (squeezed in a clean kitchen towel) or the released water turns the phyllo to soggy paste, and the phyllo must be brushed individually with olive oil between layers to develop the canonical crisp golden shattering crust. The most internationally recognized Greek savory pie and a structural pillar of Greek and Balkan filo pastry tradition.'

  name: 'Greek canon (descended from broader Greek and Balkan phyllo pie tradition)'
  note: 'Spanakopita is the spinach version of the broader Greek and Balkan phyllo pie family (which includes tiropita with cheese, kreatopita with meat, kolokithopita with squash, and many other regional variants). The structural elements: fresh spinach (not frozen; the texture is structurally different, see substitutions), real PDO Greek feta, scallions and fresh dill (both structural; dried dill does not work), fresh parsley, whole beaten eggs as the binder (3 to 4 eggs for a 9 by 13 pan), grated hard sheep cheese like kefalotyri (the second cheese, distinct from feta, that adds melt and umami depth), and the phyllo dough (frozen commercial phyllo is the practical home choice; the structurally canonical version uses homemade phyllo rolled paper-thin on a long dowel, a multi-hour technique reserved for traditional Greek bakeries and very ambitious home cooks). The defining technique is brushing each sheet of phyllo individually with olive oil before stacking; this is what produces the crisp golden shattering crust that distinguishes well-made spanakopita from a soggy disappointment. The Greek-American shortcut of brushing every-other-sheet, or brushing one heavy layer over the stack, produces structurally inferior pies.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Frozen phyllo dough must be thawed slowly in the refrigerator overnight (or 4 hours on the counter); thawing in the microwave or in hot water causes the sheets to gum together and tear. Pull from the freezer the night before. Once thawed, keep the unused phyllo covered with a slightly damp kitchen towel throughout the assembly; phyllo dries out within 60 seconds of exposure to air and becomes brittle and tears.'
  - 'The spinach-drying step is structural and not optional. Wet spinach in the filling turns the phyllo to soggy paste in the bake and the pie reads as a structural failure. Wilt the spinach thoroughly, transfer to a clean kitchen towel, gather the corners, and squeeze HARD over the sink for 60 seconds to extract every drop of moisture. The drained dry-feeling spinach is the structurally correct state.'

steps:
  - text: 'Wilt the spinach. Heat a very wide skillet or Dutch oven (the pot must be wide enough to hold the spinach in 2 or 3 batches) over medium-high. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil. Add the 900 g / 2 lb spinach in 2 or 3 batches, tossing with tongs as each batch wilts down to a fraction of its volume; add more spinach as space opens up. The whole wilt takes 4 to 6 minutes; the spinach should be uniformly wilted but still bright green (not stewed to drab olive-grey).'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Transfer the wilted spinach to a colander; press with the back of a wooden spoon to release the first round of water. When cool enough to handle (about 8 minutes), transfer the spinach to a clean lint-free kitchen towel or a piece of cheesecloth. Gather the corners into a tight bundle and SQUEEZE HARD over the sink for 60 to 90 seconds, twisting the towel tighter and tighter, until no more water comes out. The drained spinach should weigh about 250 g and feel almost dry to the touch.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Roughly chop the drained spinach with a chef''s knife into 1 cm pieces; transfer to a large mixing bowl. Add the 100 g / 5 to 6 scallions (sliced), 10 g / 2 to 3 cloves garlic (minced), 30 g / 1 cup chopped dill, 20 g / ⅔ cup chopped parsley, 350 g / 12 oz crumbled feta, 60 g / ¾ cup grated kefalotyri, 200 g / 4 lightly beaten eggs, 1 g / ¼ tsp nutmeg, 2 g / 1 tsp pepper, and 2 g / ⅓ tsp salt. Mix gently with a wooden spoon until uniformly combined (the eggs should coat all the spinach; the feta chunks should be evenly distributed). Taste; adjust salt cautiously, the feta is salty.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Preheat oven to 190°C / 375°F. Brush the bottom and sides of a 22 by 33 cm / 9 by 13 in baking dish (glass or ceramic; metal pans cause the bottom to brown too aggressively) with about 15 g / 1 tbsp of the olive oil.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Assemble the bottom phyllo: take one sheet of phyllo from the stack (keep the rest covered with a damp towel at all times). Lay it in the bottom of the baking dish so the phyllo overhangs the edges. Brush the surface lightly but evenly with olive oil using a pastry brush (about 7 g / 1½ tsp per sheet; do not pool the oil, brush in light strokes that coat the sheet without saturating it). Lay a second sheet on top of the first; brush with oil. Continue until you have used 10 sheets, all brushed individually with oil. The total oil used at this stage should be about 80 g / ½ cup, and the phyllo stack should reach the rim of the dish.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Spoon the spinach-feta filling onto the phyllo base; spread evenly to the edges in a single thick layer (about 3 cm / 1 in thick). Smooth with the back of the spoon.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Top phyllo: lay another phyllo sheet over the filling, again with overhang. Brush with oil. Continue until you have used 10 more sheets, each brushed individually. The total oil used for the top stack should be about 80 g / ½ cup. The top sheet should be brushed last with a final 20 g / 4 tsp of oil for a generously coated golden surface.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Fold the overhanging phyllo from the bottom (sides) up and over the top, tucking neatly inward against the inside of the baking dish to seal the edges (this creates a rustic crimped border). Brush the folded edges lightly with oil.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Score the top of the pie with a sharp knife into your serving portions (8 large squares or 12 appetizer-size squares); cut through the top phyllo layers only (about 1 cm deep), not all the way through the filling and bottom phyllo. The pre-scoring prevents the phyllo from shattering chaotically when you try to cut the baked pie.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Bake on the middle rack of the preheated 190°C / 375°F oven 45 to 55 minutes, until the top is uniformly deep golden brown and the phyllo has visibly puffed and crisped. The filling should bubble visibly at the cut score lines (the eggs have set the filling). If the top is browning too quickly (over 30 minutes of bake), cover loosely with foil for the remaining time.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 50
  - text: 'Cool 15 to 20 minutes before cutting and serving; this rest is structural and not optional. The filling continues to set during the cool, and cutting immediately produces a loose filling that floods out of the cut portions. After 20 minutes, cut along the scored lines with a sharp knife, lifting portions out with an offset spatula. Serve warm (best) or at room temperature with a small green salad and crusty bread; spanakopita is structurally a make-ahead dish and reads excellent at room temperature.'
    time_min: 17

notes: |
  The spinach-drying step is the single most important structural moment of the dish. Spinach is over 90 percent water by weight; when wilted, this water releases into the filling and (if not drained aggressively) will saturate the bottom phyllo layer during the bake, producing a soggy pie. The required technique: wilt the spinach in olive oil, transfer to a colander to drain, then transfer to a clean lint-free kitchen towel, gather the corners into a tight bundle, and SQUEEZE HARD over the sink for 60 to 90 seconds. Twist the towel tighter as more water comes out. The drained spinach should weigh about 250 g (from 900 g fresh) and feel almost dry to the touch. Cooks who skim this step out of impatience produce structurally failed spanakopitas; commit to the full 90-second squeeze.

  The individual phyllo-sheet oiling is the structural technique that produces the canonical shattering crust. Each sheet of phyllo is brushed individually with olive oil before the next sheet is laid on top; this allows the oil to penetrate and coat each layer separately, so during the bake the layers crisp into hundreds of separate flaky leaves rather than fusing into a single dense bread layer. The Greek-American shortcut of brushing every-other-sheet (or, worse, pouring olive oil over the assembled stack) produces a structurally inferior pie where the phyllo bakes into a dense bread-like texture rather than the canonical shatter. The 80 g of olive oil per 10-sheet stack is the canonical quantity; less oil produces dry phyllo that does not crisp.

  Olive oil is the canonical Greek fat for phyllo, not butter. Greek phyllo tradition uses olive oil; Eastern European and Austrian phyllo traditions (strudel) use butter. The olive oil produces a savory grassy finish that complements the feta and dill; butter produces a richer dairy-forward finish that reads more central European. Use olive oil for Greek phyllo work.

  Fresh dill is structural and not optional. Dried dill is a different ingredient entirely; the volatile aromatics of fresh dill are essential to the canonical spanakopita flavor, and dried dill reads as a stale dusty echo of the fresh herb. If fresh dill is unavailable, increase the fresh parsley to 50 g and add 1 g of fennel pollen or fennel seed instead of substituting dried dill; do not use dried dill in this dish.

  Storage. Keeps 3 days refrigerated, covered loosely (do not seal tightly; the phyllo softens under condensation). Reheat in a 175°C / 350°F oven 10 to 12 minutes to re-crisp the phyllo; do not microwave (the phyllo goes soggy). Freezes well unbaked (see modification); does not freeze well after baking, the phyllo loses its crisp texture on thaw. Excellent at room temperature for picnic or buffet service for up to 4 hours.
