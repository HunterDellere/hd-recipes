title: 'Lahmacun'
desc: 'The Turkish thin flatbread: paper-thin unleavened (or lightly leavened) dough rounds spread with a wet, finely-processed paste of ground lamb, tomato, red pepper, parsley, onion, garlic, and aromatic spices, baked in a screaming-hot oven for 4 to 6 minutes until the dough crisps at the edges and the meat layer just sets without drying. Eaten by rolling the warm round around a handful of parsley, sliced raw onion, and sumac, with a generous squeeze of lemon. The structural difference from pide and from pizza: the meat is processed to a near-paste so it spreads ultra-thin, the dough is rolled almost translucent, the bake is fast and very hot, and the round is rolled to eat, not cut.'

source:
  name: 'The southeastern Turkish and Armenian canon. Lahmacun (Armenian lahmajun, "meat with dough" in Arabic) is documented across the Turkish-Armenian-Syrian-Lebanese borderlands; the dish is shared and disputed across the region.'
  note: 'The Turkish (Gaziantep and Şanlıurfa) tradition and the Armenian tradition produce essentially the same dish with minor topping variations (the Armenian version sometimes includes red bell pepper paste; the Urfa version uses a higher proportion of red pepper and may add a small amount of biber salçası, Turkish red-pepper paste). The dough is structurally simple: flour, water, salt, a small amount of yeast for handling, no oil. The topping is the structural identity; processing the meat-vegetable mixture to a near-paste is what allows the topping to spread thin enough to bake in the very short oven time.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the dough: in a medium bowl, whisk together the 300 g / 2½ cups flour, 2 g / ½ tsp yeast, and 5 g / 1 tsp fine sea salt. Add the 180 g / ¾ cup warm water; stir with a fork until shaggy, then turn onto a clean surface and knead 6 to 8 minutes until smooth and slightly elastic. The dough should be soft but not sticky (slightly tacky to the touch but releases cleanly from the counter). Place in a lightly oiled bowl, cover with a damp towel, and rest 60 minutes at warm room temperature. The dough will not double; it should look slightly puffed and feel relaxed.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Make the topping paste: in a food processor, combine the 150 g onion, 100 g bell pepper, 100 g tomato, 30 g parsley, and 12 g garlic. Pulse 15 to 20 times until uniformly minced but not liquefied (the texture should be a coarse wet relish, not a smooth purée).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Transfer the processed vegetables to a fine-mesh sieve set over a bowl and press gently with the back of a spoon for 2 minutes to drain excess water. This step is structural; too-wet topping steams the dough underneath and produces a soggy lahmacun. Discard the drained liquid (or save for a soup base).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'In a medium bowl, combine the drained vegetable mixture with the 250 g ground lamb, 20 g / 4 tsp tomato paste, 4 g / 2 tsp pul biber, 3 g / 1½ tsp cumin, 2 g / 1 tsp paprika, 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt, 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper, and 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil. Mix with your hands or a wooden spoon until uniform and slightly sticky. The texture should be like a wet meatball mixture, just loose enough to spread thinly with the back of a spoon. Refrigerate while the oven preheats.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Preheat the oven to its maximum temperature (260°C / 500°F or higher) with a baking stone or steel on the upper-middle rack for at least 45 minutes.'
    time_min: 45
  - text: 'Divide the rested dough into 8 equal portions (about 60 g each). Shape each portion into a tight ball; rest 10 minutes covered. Roll each ball on a lightly floured surface into a thin round about 22 cm / 9 inches across and 2 mm thick. The dough should be nearly translucent; aim for as thin as you can roll without tearing.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Spread one-eighth of the topping (about 75 g) over each rolled round in a very thin even layer using the back of a spoon, going right to the edges. The topping should be visibly translucent over the dough (3 mm thick maximum); thicker mounds will not cook through in the short bake.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Transfer one or two lahmacun to a parchment-lined pizza peel or sheet pan and slide onto the preheated stone. Bake 4 to 6 minutes until the dough edges are crisp and slightly puffed, the topping has lost its raw red look and turned dark caramel-brown at the edges, and the meat is just-cooked through (no pink visible). Pull and stack on a clean kitchen towel; keep wrapped while you bake the rest. Repeat with the remaining rounds.'
    technique: 'high-heat-bake'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Serve immediately. Each diner spreads their hot lahmacun open, mounds a small handful of the 40 g fresh parsley leaves and a few rings of the 100 g sliced red onion down the middle, sprinkles with the 4 g sumac, squeezes a wedge of lemon generously over the topping (the lemon is structural; it transforms the dish), then rolls the lahmacun into a tight cylinder and eats with the hands.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why the topping is processed to a paste. The structural defining feature of lahmacun is the thinness of both the dough and the topping. The topping layer is no more than 3 mm thick when spread, which means the meat-vegetable mixture has to be extremely fine and uniform; chunks of onion or bits of pepper would protrude through the topping and burn before the dough cooked. Processing the vegetables to a wet relish (and then draining off the water) produces a paste that spreads paper-thin and cooks in the short bake without leaving any component undercooked. This is why a lahmacun made by hand-mincing the vegetables reads as a thinner, less interesting pide rather than as the dish itself; the processing is structural.

  Why the bake is so short. The very high oven heat and the very thin dough-and-topping mean the bake is 4 to 6 minutes. Longer bakes dry out the topping and crack the dough; shorter bakes leave the meat undercooked. The signal is visual: the dough edges should be crisp and slightly puffed (no chew on the edge), the topping should be visibly darkened from raw red to caramel-brown at the edges, and the meat surface should be just-set (no glistening pink). Pull at this exact window; the lahmacun continues to cook briefly on the towel.

  Why fresh parsley and onion are not garnish. The pile of fresh parsley and sliced raw onion inside the rolled lahmacun is structural, not decorative. The dish without these elements is a small pizza; with them it becomes a wrap that combines hot crisp dough, cooked meat, raw vegetable bite, and sumac-and-lemon acid in every bite. The eating ritual (roll, fill, squeeze lemon, eat with hands) is part of the dish; sliced lahmacun eaten with a fork misses the structural point.

  Versatility. The topping paste is structurally identical to köfte (Turkish meatball) mixture and can be rolled into oval kebabs for grilling. The dough is the same lightly-leavened flatbread that doubles as a base for any thin Mediterranean topping; toppings of cheese-and-egg, mushroom-and-leek, or grilled vegetables all work on the same base.

  Pairs across Turkish (canonical, especially southeast Turkey), Armenian, Syrian, Lebanese (where the dish is called sfiha or lahm bi ajeen in some regions, with structural variations), and broader Levantine-Anatolian borderland cookery. Often served as a quick weekday lunch or street food across the region.

  Storage. Lahmacun is best fresh and degrades quickly; the dough turns leathery within hours. Leftover baked lahmacun reheats acceptably in a 220°C oven for 3 minutes (restores some crispness). Unbaked shaped-and-topped lahmacun can be frozen on a sheet pan, then transferred to bags; bake from frozen at the same temperature with 2 minutes added. The topping paste keeps 3 days refrigerated and freezes 2 months; the dough does not freeze well after rising.
