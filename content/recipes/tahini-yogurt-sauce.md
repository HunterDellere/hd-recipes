steps:
  - text: 'In a medium bowl, combine the 120 g / ½ cup tahini, 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, 6 g / 1 to 2 grated garlic cloves, and 4 g / ¾ tsp salt. Whisk vigorously with a wooden spoon or stiff whisk; the mixture will seize and turn pasty within 10 seconds, going from a loose tahini cream to a thick clay-like paste. This seize is structural and expected; it is the tahini protein and starch reacting to the acid and water in the lemon juice. Do not panic; do not add oil; do not add more tahini.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the 100 g / ½ cup yogurt and whisk to combine; the mixture will loosen significantly and become a thick spoonable cream. The yogurt water content reverses some of the seize and the dairy fat softens the tahini bitterness; the bowl will smell of garlic and lemon and the texture will look like soft hummus.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the 60 g / ¼ cup cold water in a steady stream while whisking continuously. The sauce will thin smoothly to a pourable consistency, slightly thicker than heavy cream and just thick enough to coat the back of a spoon and pour off in a slow ribbon. The cold-water addition is what breaks any remaining tahini seize and gives the sauce its characteristic glossy appearance. If the sauce is too thick for the application (drizzling vs dipping), add water 1 tbsp at a time; if too thin, whisk in 1 tbsp tahini.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Taste and adjust: tahini-yogurt sauce should read tahini-forward with bright lemon, gentle garlic, and clean salt; the yogurt should be background body, not foreground tang. If flat, an extra 1 g / pinch of fine sea salt and 5 g / 1 tsp lemon juice; if too aggressive (too garlicky or too sharp), more yogurt or a pinch of sugar to round the finish. Serve immediately or refrigerate up to 4 days. Drizzle over roasted vegetables, falafel, shawarma, kebabs, grain bowls, fattoush and chopped salads, fresh raw vegetables, grilled bread, fried eggplant, hummus and other dip platters, soft-boiled eggs, or thinned with more water as a dressing for leafy salads.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The seize-and-rescue logic. Tahini reacts dramatically to acid and water: a small amount of lemon juice on a bowl of tahini causes immediate seizing (the tahini turns from a loose cream into a stiff paste within seconds), and inexperienced cooks panic and add oil to thin it back. Adding oil makes the sauce greasy and breaks the structural integrity; the right move is to push through the seize by adding the yogurt and the cold water in sequence. The seize is the tahini proteins and starches binding the lemon water; once you add enough additional water (via yogurt and the final water addition), the sauce passes the seize point and turns smooth and pourable. Trust the structural curve: paste, soft cream, pourable sauce.

  Pure tarator vs yogurt-extended. The pure tahini sauce (tarator: tahini, lemon, garlic, water, salt) is the older and more canonical Levantine form, structurally elegant and more concentrated. The yogurt-extended version (this recipe) is a modern home-kitchen hybrid that softens the bitterness, lightens the body, and dramatically improves cross-cuisine usability; the dairy carries flavor in a way that pure tarator does not, which makes the yogurt sauce easier to use as a finishing drizzle on non-Levantine dishes (roasted Brussels sprouts, sweet potato wedges, Mexican grain bowls, contemporary Western chicken plates). Both are correct; choose based on the application. For a traditional shawarma plate, tarator is the canon; for a roasted-vegetable bowl that travels across multiple food traditions, the yogurt version is more useful.

  Versatility. Tahini-yogurt sauce is the most cuisine-flexible drizzle in the Levantine and Mediterranean pantry. Use it as: a drizzle over roasted vegetables (cauliflower, sweet potato, Brussels sprouts, eggplant, carrot, beet, broccoli, butternut squash), a sauce for falafel and chickpea fritters (the canonical pairing), a sauce for shawarma (chicken, beef, lamb), a sauce for grilled or roasted lamb (kebabs, leg of lamb, lamb shoulder), a drizzle for grain bowls (farro, freekeh, bulgur, brown rice, quinoa) with roasted vegetables and herbs, a dressing for chopped salads (fattoush, tabbouleh-adjacent salads, cucumber-tomato-onion), a dip for raw vegetables (carrot, cucumber, radish, fennel, bell pepper, kohlrabi), a dip for grilled bread and pita, a sauce for grilled fish and shellfish (salmon, halibut, branzino, shrimp), a sauce for soft-boiled or jammy eggs (the egg-and-tahini canon), thinned with more water as a dressing for leafy green salads (romaine, little gem, arugula), a stir-in for soups (lentil, chickpea, vegetable; a tablespoon at service finishes the soup with body and acid), and as a base for compound sauces (add harissa for a hot version, fold in roasted-eggplant flesh for moutabal, fold in roasted-red-pepper paste for a sweet-smoky variant).

  Pairs across Lebanese, Palestinian, Syrian, Turkish, Greek, Egyptian, North African, broader Mediterranean, contemporary American, modern Israeli, and modern European contexts. The neutral pourable body and the gentle lemon-garlic-tahini profile means the sauce carries without dominating; this is what makes it the most-used drizzle in modern Mediterranean-influenced kitchens.

  Storage. Keeps 4 days refrigerated in a sealed jar; thickens significantly in the cold (the tahini fat firms; the yogurt protein binds water). Bring to room temperature 15 minutes before use, or thin with 1 to 2 tbsp warm water and re-whisk to restore pourable consistency. Do not freeze; the yogurt breaks on thawing and the texture turns grainy.
