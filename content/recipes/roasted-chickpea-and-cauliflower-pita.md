before_you_start:
  - 'The chickpeas must be drained and patted bone-dry before roasting. Spread on a kitchen towel, top with a second towel, and press out every drop of surface moisture. Wet chickpeas steam instead of crisp; this is the single most common failure mode and ruins the entire dish.'
  - 'Preheat the oven to 230 °C with a heavy sheet pan inside for 15 minutes. The hot pan starts caramelization on the cauliflower immediately; a cold pan steams.'
  - 'Massage the red cabbage with the salt and lemon juice 5 to 10 minutes before assembly. The salt-acid combination wilts and tenderizes the cabbage so it behaves like a slaw rather than a raw shred; skipping this step gives a pita that fights the eater''s teeth.'
  - 'Warm the pitas in a dry skillet (cast iron, stainless, not nonstick) for 30 seconds per side just before assembly. Cold dry pita cracks when folded; warm pliable pita wraps cleanly.'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 230 °C / 450 °F with a heavy half-sheet pan inside on the middle rack. The pan needs 15 minutes preheat. While the oven heats, drain the 300 g chickpeas and pat them bone-dry. Spread on a kitchen towel, top with a second, press gently to absorb every drop of surface moisture.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Prep the cauliflower. Cut the 600 g cauliflower into 2 to 3 cm florets. In a wide bowl, toss with the 25 g / 2 tbsp olive oil, the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt, and the 2 g / 1 tsp ground cumin. Every floret should glisten.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Prep the chickpeas. In a separate bowl, toss the dry chickpeas with the 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil, the 2 g / 1 tsp cumin, the 2 g / 1 tsp coriander, the 2 g / 1 tsp smoked paprika, and the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pull the preheated sheet pan from the oven. Tip the cauliflower onto one half of the pan and the chickpeas onto the other, both in single layers with space between pieces. The pan should sizzle audibly. Roast 22 to 25 minutes without disturbing for the first 15 minutes, then toss each side separately and finish for 8 to 10 minutes more. The cauliflower should show deep mahogany on the cut faces, the chickpeas should rattle when shaken on the pan.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'While the vegetables roast, massage the cabbage slaw. In a wide bowl, combine the 250 g shredded red cabbage with the 20 g / 1½ tbsp lemon juice and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. Massage with your hands for 90 seconds; the cabbage begins to release water, soften, and turn brighter pink. Let sit 5 to 10 minutes. Just before assembly, toss in the 8 g / 2 tsp olive oil and the 15 g chopped parsley.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Build the tahini sauce. In a small bowl, whisk together the 80 g / ⅓ cup tahini, the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, the 4 g / 1 clove microplaned garlic, and the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt. The tahini will seize into a tight paste; this is expected. Stream in the 45 g / 3 tbsp ice water in 1 tbsp additions, whisking between each, until the sauce loosens to a thick but pourable consistency that ribbons off the whisk.'
    technique: 'tahini-emulsion'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Warm the pitas. Heat a dry skillet (cast iron or stainless) over medium heat. Warm each of the 4 pitas for 30 seconds per side, just until pliable and slightly steamed. Stack and cover with a damp clean kitchen towel to keep warm; cold pita tears when folded.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Assemble. Cut each pita in half through the equator to open the pocket. Schmear 1 tbsp / 15 g of the tahini sauce on the inside of each half. Stuff each half with one-quarter of the cauliflower, one-quarter of the chickpeas, and a generous handful of the cabbage slaw. Drizzle another teaspoon of tahini sauce over the top of the filling before closing. Serve immediately with extra tahini sauce on the side for dipping.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'For office-lunch packing: assemble the pita whole (do not cut in half) and wrap tightly in parchment paper, then in a beeswax wrap or a fitted reusable container. The wrap holds 4 hours at room temperature; the chickpeas stay crisp and the cabbage stays crunchy because the tahini sauce is on the inside of the pita pocket rather than touching the bread directly. Pack extra tahini sauce in a small glass jar for drizzle-at-eating.'

notes: |
  Why the chickpea-dryness obsession. Crispy chickpeas are not optional crunch; they are the structural protein and the textural backbone of the wrap. A wet chickpea spends the first 10 minutes of the roast boiling off surface water, and by the time the surface is dry the interior has overcooked into a pasty mush. Dry chickpeas hit the hot pan and start dehydrating-and-browning immediately, producing the crispy outside-creamy inside texture that anchors the dish. If the chickpeas are not crispy, the wrap is mediocre. The kitchen-towel-pat is the structural step.

  Why massage the cabbage. Raw shredded cabbage is rigid: the cell walls hold their structure and the leaves stay crunchy in a way that fights the eater''s teeth and tears the pita. Salt-and-acid massage breaks down the cell wall structure (the salt draws water out through osmosis, the acid denatures the pectin), and the cabbage wilts to a tender but still crunchy texture. The slaw goes from "raw cabbage in a wrap" to "tender bright slaw that integrates with the other fillings." The 5-minute rest after the massage lets the changes complete; longer is fine, up to 24 hours refrigerated.

  Why pita instead of wraps. Pocket pita has a specific texture profile: a slightly chewy crust, an air-pocket interior that holds fillings in place, and a flexibility that lets it fold without cracking. Flour tortillas and burrito-style wraps work but read as a different category of food entirely (the wrap absorbs sauce and goes soggy; the pita pocket keeps wet and dry components separated). Lavash and naan are valid alternatives but each has its own texture register.

  The tahini-acid pairing. Tahini is fat-rich and slightly bitter on its own; the lemon juice does two things structurally. First, the acid finishes the tahini''s protein gel (the casein-like sesame proteins are hydrated and slightly denatured by the acid, which produces a smoother sauce). Second, the acid balances the fat, making the sauce read brighter and lighter rather than heavy. A tahini sauce without lemon reads as fat-only and weighs down the wrap; with lemon, it integrates as a structural acid alongside the cabbage slaw''s acid.

  Make-ahead and storage. Every component holds well separately for 3 days refrigerated. The chickpeas are best within 24 hours of roasting (they soften over time but still taste good). The cauliflower keeps texture for 3 days. The tahini sauce keeps 5 days. The cabbage slaw keeps 24 hours but stays bright pink and crunchy for that window. Assemble wraps fresh; pre-assembled wraps go soggy within 6 hours regardless of packaging. The format is calibrated for "batch-cook the components on Sunday, assemble one wrap each morning in 4 minutes."

  Why no plastic wrap for office lunch. Parchment paper plus a beeswax wrap (or a fitted reusable container) is the right packing setup. Plastic wrap traps moisture against the warm components and accelerates the softening of the chickpeas and pita. Parchment breathes slightly, beeswax seals while allowing minimal moisture exchange, and the wrap stays in good condition for 4 hours at room temperature.
