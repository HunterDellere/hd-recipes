before_you_start:
  - 'Get the oven to 230 °C with a heavy sheet pan inside before you do anything else. The pan needs 15 minutes preheat for the vegetables to sizzle on contact, and the chickpeas need a screaming-hot pan to crisp rather than steam.'
  - 'Start the farro in salted water as soon as you turn the oven on. Pearled farro takes 20 to 25 minutes, whole farro takes 35 to 45; both run in parallel with the vegetable roast and the sauce assembly.'
  - 'Drain and dry the chickpeas thoroughly. Spread on a kitchen towel and pat the tops dry with a second towel. Wet chickpeas steam instead of crisp; this is the single most common failure point.'
  - 'The tahini-yogurt sauce can be made up to 4 days ahead. The raw garlic mellows over the first 24 hours and the sauce reads more rounded by day two.'

steps:
  - text: 'Set the oven to 230 °C / 450 °F with a heavy half-sheet pan inside on the middle rack. The pan needs 15 minutes of preheat. While the oven heats, bring 1500 ml / 6 cups of water to a boil with the 12 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt for the farro.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Add the 200 g / 1 cup pearled farro to the boiling salted water. Cook at a steady simmer, uncovered, for 20 to 25 minutes until the grains are tender but still have a discernible bite at the center. Drain in a sieve and spread on a sheet pan to stop the carryover cooking; this prevents the grains from continuing to soften into mush.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Cut the 350 g / 2 medium zucchini into 2 cm half-moons, the 250 g / 2 medium red peppers into 2 cm squares, and the 200 g / 1 large red onion into 8 root-attached wedges. In a wide bowl, toss the cut vegetables with the 35 g / 2½ tbsp olive oil, the 9 g / 2¼ tsp kosher salt, and the 2 g / 1 tsp dried oregano. Every surface should glisten.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Drain and pat the chickpeas dry. Spread the 250 g / 1 cup chickpeas on a kitchen towel, top with a second towel, and press gently to absorb every drop of surface moisture. In a small bowl, toss the dried chickpeas with the 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil, the 2 g / 1 tsp ground cumin, the 2 g / 1 tsp smoked paprika, and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Pull the preheated sheet pan out of the oven. Tip the dressed 800 g vegetables onto one half of the pan in a single layer with space between pieces; tip the seasoned 250 g chickpeas onto the other half. The pan should sizzle audibly on contact. Roast 20 minutes without disturbing.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'After 20 minutes, toss the vegetables and chickpeas separately with a spatula, scraping any browned bits from the pan. Return for another 10 to 12 minutes until the vegetables show deep caramelization on the cut faces and the chickpeas are crisp on the surface (they should rattle slightly when shaken on the pan). Pull and let rest on the pan for 5 minutes.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Build the tahini-yogurt sauce while the vegetables roast. In a medium bowl, whisk the 200 g / ¾ cup Greek yogurt, the 60 g / ¼ cup tahini, the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, the 4 g / 1 clove microplaned garlic, and the 3 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt until smooth. The sauce will be thick. Stream in the 30 g / 2 tbsp ice water in 1 tbsp additions, whisking between each, until the sauce ribbons off the whisk and settles slowly back into the surface. Taste; it should read tahini-led, lemon-bright, fully salted.'
    technique: 'tahini-emulsion'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Assemble. Divide the cooked farro across four wide shallow bowls (about 100 g / ½ cup per bowl). Top each with one-quarter of the roasted vegetables, one-quarter of the crispy chickpeas, and a generous spoonful (about 70 g / ¼ cup) of the tahini-yogurt sauce. Scatter the 20 g chopped parsley, the 10 g chopped dill, the 2 g microplaned lemon zest, and the 80 g crumbled feta across the bowls. Serve immediately, warm or at room temperature.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'For batch storage, hold every component separately in covered glass containers in the refrigerator. The farro keeps 4 days, the vegetables 3 days, the chickpeas 3 days (they soften over time but still eat well), the sauce 4 days. Assemble cold from the fridge or warm individual portions in a 160 °C / 325 °F oven for 8 minutes before adding the cold sauce and herbs.'

notes: |
  Why hard-roast the vegetables instead of soft-roast. The grain-bowl format dies when every component reads neutral. A 200 °C / 390 °F oven cooks the vegetables tender but leaves them flat in flavor; the bowl reads as steamed vegetables on a bed of grain. At 230 °C / 450 °F on a preheated sheet pan, the vegetable surfaces hit Maillard temperature within 5 minutes of pan contact and develop the caramelized character that anchors the bowl. Same vegetables, same time investment, structurally different result.

  Why dry the chickpeas hard. Surface moisture is the single point of failure on crispy chickpeas. Wet chickpeas spend the first 10 minutes of the roast boiling off water, and by the time the surface is dry the interior has overcooked into a paste. Dry chickpeas hit the hot pan and start dehydrating-and-browning immediately. The kitchen-towel-pat is not optional; it is the technique.

  The sauce calibration. Tahini alone is thick and bitter without salt and acid. Yogurt alone reads as plain dairy. The combination at roughly 75% yogurt / 25% tahini by weight produces a sauce that reads creamy-and-tangy with the structural sesame depth that pulls the Mediterranean register together. The lemon juice and the microplaned raw garlic are non-negotiable; the acid finishes the protein hydration in the tahini and the garlic compounds the savory.

  Why batch-cook this format. Every component reheats or eats cold without quality loss, and the assembled bowl reads as composed-on-purpose rather than reheated-leftovers because the cold sauce and fresh herbs go on at serving time. The format is calibrated for the work-from-home or office-lunch use case where the cook wants 4 lunches from one 60-minute Sunday session. Hold all components in glass (not plastic) to avoid the off-flavors that build up in plastic containers over multiple days.
