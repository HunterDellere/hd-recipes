source:
  name: 'Adapted from the central Thai pla rad prik (fish doused in chili sauce) canon'
  note: 'Pla rad prik is the Thai whole-fried fish drenched in a three-flavor sauce, sam rot, the sauce that balances salty, sweet, and sour with a strong chili-garlic edge. Structurally the dish is two problems solved in parallel: a frying problem (a dry, well-scored fish in hot oil, so the skin shatters and the dense flesh near the spine cooks through) and a balance problem (the sauce supplies all the salt, sweet, sour, and heat the lean fish lacks on its own). Tilapia is the value play here, a cheap clean-flavored freshwater fish whose mild flake reads as a neutral canvas for the aggressive sauce; in Thailand the same treatment runs on pla tap tim (red tilapia) and pla kapong (barramundi). Gluten-free as written: the dredge is rice flour and cornstarch, and the sauce is built on fish sauce rather than wheat-based soy, so a gluten-free fish sauce keeps the whole plate gluten-free.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Soak the tamarind and build the sauce base before you touch the fish. Tamarind pulp needs 10 minutes in warm water to soften before you can press the sour liquid out, and the sauce must be ready to spoon over the fish the instant it drains. Stage the tamarind, palm sugar, fish sauce, and the minced aromatics before the frying phase so it runs uninterrupted.'
  - 'Score and dry the fish thoroughly before anything goes near the oil. A wet fish does two bad things: the surface water flashes to steam and the skin steams limp instead of crisping, and water hitting 185 C oil throws violent spatter. Cross-score to the bone on both sides, salt, then pat bone-dry inside, out, and into every cut. Air-drying uncovered on a rack in the refrigerator for 30 minutes is the single biggest upgrade to the crust.'
  - 'Manage the oil temperature with a thermometer and clear the area around the pot. Fry at 180 to 185 C; below 170 C the fish absorbs oil and goes greasy, above 195 C the dredge scorches before the spine cooks. A whole fish displaces a lot of oil and can boil over a too-full vessel, so fill no more than halfway and keep a fitted lid and a sheet pan within reach.'
  - 'Steam the jasmine rice on a parallel track so it finishes as the fish fries. The dish is eaten over rice, and a hot three-flavor sauce demands a neutral starch alongside to carry it.'

steps:
  - text: 'Soak the tamarind. Break the 30 g / 2 tbsp tamarind pulp into the 90 g / 6 tbsp warm water and let it stand 10 minutes, then mash it with a fork and press it through a fine sieve, working the pulp against the mesh. Keep the strained brown liquid (about 100 g) and discard the seeds and fiber. This tamarind liquid is the sour spine of the sauce; see the tamarind-souring technique for why a soaked-and-pressed block reads cleaner and fruitier than bottled concentrate.'
    technique: 'tamarind-souring'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Prep the fish. Rinse the 600 g tilapia inside and out and scrape any dark blood line from the cavity with the back of a spoon; the blood line carries the strongest muddy off-flavor, which tilapia is prone to. Lay the fish flat and, with a sharp knife held at a 45-degree angle, cut parallel cross-scores about 2 cm apart all the way down to the bone across the whole body, on both sides. The deep scoring lets hot oil reach the dense flesh near the spine so the thick center cooks through before the thin belly burns, and it multiplies the crisp surface and gives the sauce channels to pool in. See the fish-skin-scoring technique for the angle and depth.'
    technique: 'fish-skin-scoring'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Salt and dry. Rub the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt over the skin, into every score, and through the cavity. Let the fish sit 10 minutes, then pat it completely dry with paper towels, working into the cuts. For the best crust, set the fish on a rack and air-dry uncovered in the refrigerator for 30 minutes. A bone-dry fish is the difference between a skin that shatters and one that steams limp, and a dry surface fries far more safely.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Build the sauce base off-heat first. In a small bowl combine the strained 100 g tamarind liquid, the 50 g / ¼ cup grated palm sugar, and the 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce. Stir to start the palm sugar dissolving; it will finish melting in the pan. Taste the raw mix: it should read aggressively sour, salty, and sweet all at once, since the lean fish will dilute it. Set aside.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Dredge. Whisk the 40 g / ⅓ cup white rice flour and the 30 g / ¼ cup cornstarch together on a wide plate. Press the dry fish into the mixture on both sides and into the scores, coating it in a thin, even, dry film, then lift and shake off all excess so the coat is barely visible. A thick wet dredge gums up and fries pale; a thin dry one shatters.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Heat the 1000 g / about 4 cups neutral oil in a wide deep pan or wok to 180 to 185 C / 355 to 365 F, filling the vessel no more than halfway. Use a thermometer; below 170 C the fish soaks oil and goes greasy, above 195 C the dredge scorches before the spine cooks. The oil is ready when a pinch of the dredge dropped in sizzles vigorously and rises at once.'
    technique: 'deep-fry'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Fry the fish. Lay it into the oil away from you to limit spatter and fry without moving it for 5 to 6 minutes, until the down side is deep golden and rigid. Slide two spatulas under it and turn once, carefully, then fry the second side 5 to 6 minutes more. Total fry is 10 to 12 minutes for a 600 g tilapia, longer than a thin flatfish because of the rounder body; the skin and fins should be uniformly crisp and the flesh in the scores opaque and flaking off the bone. Spoon hot oil over any thick part that rides above the oil line.'
    technique: 'deep-fry'
    time_min: 11
  - text: 'Drain. Lift the fish out, let it drain a few seconds over the pot, then set it on a rack set over a sheet pan for 1 minute. Resting on a rack rather than a flat plate keeps the bottom skin crisp; a fried fish left on a flat plate steams its own underside soft. Transfer the drained fish to the serving platter.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Make the three-flavor sauce. Pour off the frying oil, wipe the wok, and return it to medium heat with the 20 g / 1½ tbsp fresh neutral oil. Add the 20 g / 5 to 6 cloves minced garlic and fry, stirring, 30 to 45 seconds until it just turns pale gold and smells nutty, then add the 40 g / 2 minced shallots and the 9 g / 4 to 6 Thai chilies (pounded and sliced) and fry another 60 seconds until the shallot is soft and the chili is fragrant and the kitchen makes you cough slightly, the sign the capsaicin has bloomed into the oil. See blooming-aromatics for why the raw chili and garlic must hit hot fat before liquid goes in.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Reduce the sauce. Pour the tamarind, palm sugar, and fish sauce base into the hot aromatics; it will boil hard at once. Simmer over medium heat 2 to 3 minutes, stirring, until the palm sugar has fully melted and the sauce reduces to a glossy, lightly syrupy consistency that coats the back of a spoon, about half its starting volume. Pull off the heat and whisk in the 15 g / 1 tbsp lime juice. Taste and adjust to a true four-flavor balance: more fish sauce for salt, more palm sugar for sweet, more tamarind or lime for sour, more chili for heat. The sauce should be loud; it is the entire seasoning of the dish.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Drench and serve. Spoon the hot three-flavor sauce over the whole crisp fish so it runs into every score and pools around the frame, lifting the fried garlic and chili out of the pan and over the skin. Scatter the 10 g / ½ cup cilantro sprigs and the 15 g / 1 to 2 sliced scallions over the top. Serve immediately with the cooked 300 g / 1½ cups jasmine rice. Each diner lifts crisp flesh and skin off the frame, drags it through the sauce that has pooled in the scores, and eats it over rice; the lean clean fish, the shattering skin, and the sweet-sour-salty-spicy sauce are the whole point of the dish.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why tilapia. Tilapia is a cheap, widely available freshwater fish with mild lean white flesh and very little fat, which makes it a near-neutral canvas for an aggressive sauce. That same mildness is why it can taste muddy or flat when poorly handled, so the blood line in the cavity must be scraped clean and the fish salted and dried before frying. In Thailand pla rad prik most often runs on pla tap tim (farmed red tilapia) or pla kapong (barramundi); tilapia is the direct stand-in and the cheapest path to the dish. The whole fish matters: the bone-in frame keeps the flesh moist through a long fry, the fins and tail crisp into edible crackling, and the scored body gives the sauce a hundred channels to pool in.

  Why dry, dry, dry. Three things crisp a fried fish: a dry surface, a thin starch coat, and hot enough oil. The dry-salt cure pulls surface moisture and firms the skin, the 30-minute uncovered air-dry in the refrigerator finishes the job, and the thin rice-flour-and-cornstarch dredge gives the crisp a scaffold without gumming. Skip the drying and the skin steams limp under its own water, and the wet surface throws dangerous spatter when it hits the oil. This is the single highest-leverage step in the recipe.

  Why rice flour and cornstarch, not wheat. Rice flour and cornstarch are pure or near-pure starch with little protein, so they fry to a glassy shatter rather than the bready, gluten-bound crust wheat flour gives, and they hold that crisp longer once the hot sauce hits the fish. The mix also keeps the whole plate gluten-free, which a wheat-flour dredge or a wheat-based soy sauce would break. Potato starch is the most shatter-crisp option and swaps in directly for the cornstarch.

  The three-flavor sauce is the whole dish. Sam rot means three flavors, salty and sweet and sour, with chili heat as the assertive fourth, and this sauce is where every operational directive lives. The fish sauce is the glutamate-rich umami and the salt; the tamarind is the deep cooked sour; the lime whisked in off the heat is the bright finishing acid that the cooked tamarind alone cannot supply; the palm sugar balances both against the salt; and the fried garlic, shallot, and chili are the aromatic body and heat. The order is structural: bloom the aromatics in hot oil first so the chili and garlic lose their raw edge, then add the liquid to reduce. Taste and rebalance the finished sauce to a true four-flavor proportion before it touches the fish, the way a Thai cook checks a curry. It should be loud enough to season a lean fish twice its volume.

  Oil temperature and safety. Fry at 180 to 185 C. Too cool and the fish absorbs oil and turns greasy; too hot and the starch coat scorches before the dense flesh at the spine is cooked. A rounder fish like tilapia needs the full 10 to 12 minutes, longer than a thin flatfish, so manage the temperature rather than the clock and pull the fish when the flesh in the scores flakes clean off the bone. A whole fish displaces a large volume of oil and can boil the pot over if it is more than half full, so use a wide vessel, keep a fitted lid and a sheet pan nearby, lower the fish in away from your body, and never add a wet fish to hot oil. A thermometer is not optional here.

  Sauce-on versus sauce-aside. Pla rad prik is traditionally drenched, the hot sauce poured over the whole fish at the table so it runs into the crisp scores and softens the skin slightly into something between crackling and glaze. If you want to keep the skin fully shattering, serve about half the sauce poured and the rest in a bowl alongside for dipping, so each forkful can take a different sauce-to-fish ratio. Either way, sauce and serve the instant the fish drains; a fried fish that sits goes soft regardless of the sauce.
