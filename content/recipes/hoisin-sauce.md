  note: 'Hoisin (海鮮 in Chinese, literally "seafood" though the sauce contains none) is the canonical Cantonese sweet-savory condiment, traditionally made with fermented soybean paste, sweet sugar, garlic, sesame, and Chinese five-spice. The name "seafood sauce" likely refers to its use as a dipping sauce for seafood at Cantonese banquets rather than its ingredients. Commercial hoisin (Koon Chun, Lee Kum Kee, Pearl River Bridge) is widely available but the homemade version is dramatically fresher and the cook controls the sweet-savory balance. Used canonically for: char siu lacquering, Peking duck dipping, mu shu pork wrapper sauce, Vietnamese banh mi grilled meat brushing.'

steps:
  - text: 'Combine the wet ingredients. In a small heavy-bottomed saucepan, combine the 40 g doubanjiang (or yellow miso), 30 g smooth natural peanut butter, 45 g dark soy sauce, 15 g light soy sauce, 45 g dark brown sugar, 15 g honey, 15 g rice vinegar, 8 g grated garlic, 2 g five-spice powder, optional 1 g gochugaru, and 30 ml water. Whisk vigorously to combine; the mixture will look chunky and uneven at the start as the peanut butter and doubanjiang resist incorporation.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Cook to integrate. Place the saucepan over medium-low heat. Stir constantly with a silicone spatula or wooden spoon (the sauce burns readily on the pan bottom because of the sugar). Cook 4 to 5 minutes, until the sauce comes to a gentle simmer, the doubanjiang dissolves completely, the peanut butter melts into the liquid, and the mixture transforms from chunky to glossy uniform paste. The sauce will thicken slightly as it cooks; the texture target is thick spoonable but still pourable (about the consistency of warm honey).'
    technique: 'gentle-reduction'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Adjust the consistency and balance. Pull off heat. The sauce thickens further as it cools; if it is already too thick on the heat, add another 10 to 15 ml of warm water. If it is too thin, return to heat and simmer another 2 minutes. Taste: the sauce should be sweet-savory-umami-faintly-spicy in balance, with a pronounced fermented-soybean depth. If flat, add another 5 g of dark soy. If too sweet, add another 5 g of rice vinegar. If too aggressive (the doubanjiang sometimes reads sharp), add another 10 g of brown sugar.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish with sesame oil. Stir in the 8 g toasted sesame oil. The sesame oil is added off-heat so the aromatic compounds (sesamol, methylpyrazine) are not driven off by heat; this is the structural Chinese finishing technique for any sauce that includes sesame oil.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Cool and store. Pour the hoisin into a clean glass jar; cool to room temperature (about 15 minutes uncovered) before sealing and refrigerating. The sauce will continue to thicken over the first 24 hours as the starches in the peanut butter and the soybean paste hydrate fully; the final texture is the thick lacquering consistency that hoisin is known for. Use after the 24-hour rest for peak quality; keeps 3 weeks refrigerated.'
    time_min: 4

notes: |
  The fermented-soybean base is structural. Traditional commercial hoisin uses a specific fermented soybean paste (mei dou jang or fermented yellow soybeans) that is difficult to source outside Chinese groceries. Doubanjiang (fermented broad bean paste with chili) is the accessible substitute that produces the closest structural match; yellow miso is the second-best substitute. The fermented-soybean character is what gives hoisin its depth; substituting regular soy sauce alone produces a soy-based sauce, not a hoisin. The fermentation note is non-negotiable.

  Why peanut butter. The peanut butter is not for peanut flavor; it provides the structural body and gloss that distinguishes hoisin from a thin soy-based sauce. The natural oils in the peanut butter emulsify with the water and soy to produce the thick spoonable texture that characterizes commercial hoisin. Tahini (sesame paste) is the structural alternative; both work mechanically. A hoisin without the nut-butter body reads thinner and less canonical; adding cornstarch as a substitute does not produce the same mouthfeel.

  Five-spice as the structural aromatic. Chinese five-spice (typically star anise, cloves, Chinese cinnamon, fennel seed, Sichuan peppercorn) is the canonical hoisin aromatic; the warm-spice profile is what makes hoisin read as Cantonese and not as a generic sweet-savory sauce. Pre-made five-spice powder is fine for everyday use; for premium hoisin, toast the whole spices briefly and grind fresh (the aromatic intensity is dramatically higher). Skipping the five-spice produces a hoisin-shaped sauce without the structural Chinese identity.

  Storage. Refrigerated in a sealed glass jar, 3 weeks at peak quality. The sauce thickens significantly during the first 24 hours of storage as the starches hydrate; the post-rest texture is the structural target. Does not freeze well (the emulsion separates on thaw); keep refrigerated only. The sauce develops slightly deeper fermented notes over the first week of storage as the doubanjiang continues to evolve; the second-week sauce is structurally better than the first-day sauce. Make a batch on Saturday for use over the next 3 weekends.

  When commercial is fine. For casual sandwich-spread use or for recipes where hoisin is a small component (3 tbsp in a stir-fry sauce), commercial hoisin from Koon Chun or Lee Kum Kee is structurally fine and the difference is hard to detect. For finishing applications where hoisin is the primary flavor (char siu lacquer, Peking duck dipping sauce, banh mi brushing), the homemade version is dramatically better and worth the 15 minutes of work. Knowing when to make and when to buy is part of cooking efficiently.
