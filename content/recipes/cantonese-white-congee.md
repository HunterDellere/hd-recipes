steps:
  - text: 'Rinse 200 g / 1 cup jasmine rice in cold water three times, swirling and pouring off the cloudy water until it runs from milky to faintly hazy. Drain in a sieve five minutes.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Massage the drained rice with 7 g / ½ tbsp neutral oil and 3 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. Work it through the grains for 30 seconds. The oil partially waterproofs the polished surface so water has to penetrate through any milling fractures, which forces the grain to break inside-out instead of swelling intact. Rest the rice fifteen minutes.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'In a heavy 4-quart pot or Dutch oven, combine the oiled rice, 2000 ml / 8½ cups cold water, the smacked 15 g / 1-inch knob ginger coins, and the optional 8 g / 2 tbsp dried scallop or dried shrimp if using. Bring to a hard boil over high heat, uncovered, stirring once after the surface starts to foam to keep grains off the bottom.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Drop the heat to the lowest setting that still produces a steady tremor on the surface (small bubbles in random patches, never a rolling boil). Crack the lid so the pot vents steam without losing more than 200 ml / ¾ cup of water over the simmer. Simmer 60 minutes, stirring with a wooden spoon every 10-15 minutes to scrape the bottom. The grains should visibly fracture and bloom into a cloudy, white starch suspension.'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'After 60 minutes the rice should be fully fragmented and the liquid the consistency of single cream that coats the back of the spoon. If still loose, simmer another 10-15 minutes uncovered to drive off water; if too thick, splash in 60-100 ml / ¼-½ cup hot water. Fish out the ginger coins and the scallop or shrimp (chop the rehydrated scallop fine and stir back in for body, if you used it).'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Season the pot with 4 g / ½ tsp kosher salt to start. Taste. White congee is intentionally under-seasoned at this stage because the table finish carries the rest.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Ladle into bowls. Per bowl, drizzle 5 g / 1 tsp light soy sauce, a few drops (~2 g / ½ tsp) toasted sesame oil, a scatter of the sliced 4 stalks scallion (greens for color, whites if you want oniony bite), and a dust of white pepper. Stir once and eat immediately. The pot is the technique; the table is the seasoning.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  The rice-to-water ratio is the single biggest variable. 1:10 by mass produces a medium-bodied Cantonese-style congee that pours from the spoon in a smooth ribbon. Drop to 1:8 for a thicker, more porridge-like result; push to 1:12 for a looser, more soup-like bowl better suited to delicate toppings. Adjust by eye after the first 45 minutes of simmer.

  Why no aggressive aromatics in the base: white congee is the canvas. Garlic, scallion whites, and chili belong with the toppings or on the table. Loading the pot with heavy aromatics commits the entire batch to one flavor profile, which defeats the point of cooking a versatile base on a Sunday and reheating it across a week of toppings.

  Why the lid is cracked: a sealed lid traps water and prevents the slight reduction that concentrates the starch into the silky body. A fully open pot reduces too fast and risks scorching at the bottom. Cracked is the compromise.

  Storage and reheating: cooled congee thickens dramatically in the refrigerator into something close to wallpaper paste. This is fine. Reheat with a splash of water or stock and stir over low heat until it loosens back to ribbon consistency. Five days refrigerated; freezes for two months in portion-sized containers.

  The minimum table finish is salt, soy, sesame oil, scallion, white pepper. Anything beyond that is a topping pivot, not the base. Common additions across Cantonese tradition: a soft-poached or runny center egg cracked into the hot bowl, ginger-scallion oil drizzled on top, crispy fried shallots scattered over, a sliver of cured egg yolk grated through a microplane, youtiao torn into bite-sized pieces and dropped in just before eating so they soak halfway and stay crisp on top.
