before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 4 to 8 day project from cabbage to ready-to-eat kimchi. Active hands-on time is about 75 minutes split across two sessions (brine the cabbage, paste it the next day); the rest is passive brine, ferment, and cold-store time.'
  - 'Equipment: a large non-reactive bowl or stockpot (8 L+) for the brine, two clean 1 L glass jars with loose-fitting lids or proper airlock lids (sealed jars build CO2 pressure and can crack or explode), food-safe nitrile gloves for handling the chili paste, and a clean ceramic plate or glass weight to keep the cabbage submerged in the brine. Reactive metal (aluminum, copper, untreated cast iron) contacts the chili-and-salt paste and turns bitter within hours; use stainless, glass, or food-grade plastic.'
  - 'Water must be non-chlorinated. Chlorine and chloramine kill lacto-fermentation bacteria; either use filtered water, bottled spring water, or tap water that has been boiled for 10 minutes and cooled to room temperature.'
  - 'Salt must be non-iodized. Iodine inhibits lacto-fermentation. Use kosher salt, fine sea salt, or Korean coarse sea salt (cheonilyeom); avoid Morton iodized table salt.'
  - 'Hold the ferment at 18 to 22 °C (room temperature) for 3 to 7 days. Cooler than 16 °C stalls; warmer than 25 °C runs too fast and produces soft, mushy kimchi. Transfer to refrigerator (2 to 4 °C) once the kimchi has developed the right sour-funky character (taste daily starting day 3).'
  - 'Submersion under brine is non-negotiable. Anything above the liquid line grows mold within 48 hours; use a clean weight or pack the kimchi tightly with no air pockets.'

steps:
  - text: 'Day 1: prepare the cabbage brine. Cut the 2000 g napa cabbage lengthwise into quarters through the root core (which holds the leaves together for layering). If the cabbage is very large, cut into 6 wedges instead of 4. Sprinkle the 140 g coarse non-iodized salt between the leaves of each wedge, getting salt into every layer all the way to the core, rubbing it lightly into the thicker white ribs. Place the salted wedges cut-side up in a large non-reactive bowl or stockpot.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Pour the 2000 g cold filtered water over the cabbage to mostly submerge it; the cabbage will float. Weigh down with a clean ceramic plate or a sealed bag of water so the wedges stay submerged. Let stand at room temperature for 6 to 8 hours, flipping the wedges and re-weighting once at the halfway mark. The cabbage is correctly brined when the thickest white rib at the core bends easily (90 degrees) without snapping; an under-brined cabbage will not absorb the paste and will produce kimchi that ferments unevenly. Drain in a colander for 30 minutes, rinsing twice with cold non-chlorinated water to remove excess surface salt, then pressing gently to expel water without crushing the leaves.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Day 2: make the porridge base. In a small saucepan, whisk the 30 g / 3 tbsp glutinous rice flour into the 300 g / 1¼ cups water until smooth (no lumps). Cook over medium heat, stirring constantly with a wooden spoon, for 4 to 6 minutes until the mixture thickens to the consistency of thin custard and turns glossy-translucent. Off heat, stir in the 15 g / 1 tbsp granulated sugar until dissolved. Cool to room temperature; hot porridge will denature the lactobacillus on the cabbage and cook the gochugaru proteins into a duller flavor.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Build the chili paste. In a wide bowl, combine the cooled porridge with the 90 g / 1 cup gochugaru, the 60 g / ¼ cup fish sauce, the 30 g / 2 tbsp saeujeot if using, the 40 g microplaned garlic, and the 20 g microplaned ginger. In a separate blender or mini food processor, puree the 100 g chopped onion and the 100 g chopped pear with 30 g / 2 tbsp water until smooth; add the puree to the paste bowl. Mix everything with a wooden spoon or gloved hands; the paste should look like loose, glossy chili jam. Let stand 10 minutes for the gochugaru to fully hydrate and the paste to thicken slightly.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the vegetable filling. Stir the 300 g julienned radish, the 100 g 4 cm scallion lengths, and the 50 g Korean chives if using into the chili paste. Toss until every piece of vegetable is uniformly coated; the paste should feel heavy and the radish should turn a vivid red. Let stand 5 minutes; the salt in the paste begins to draw water from the radish, loosening the paste and making it spreadable.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pack the kimchi. Wearing nitrile gloves (gochugaru stains skin yellow for days and is uncomfortable on cuts), take each brined cabbage wedge and lay it cut-side up in the paste bowl. Working from the outermost leaf inward, smear a thin layer of paste between every leaf, getting paste all the way to the core. Once paste-coated, fold the cabbage wedge in half lengthwise and wrap the outermost leaf around the bundle to hold its shape, packing the radish-and-scallion filling into the folds. Place each wrapped wedge into a clean 1 L glass jar, pressing down firmly with a wooden spoon to eliminate air pockets and bring the brine to the surface. The cabbage should fill the jar to about 80% capacity; the headspace allows for fermentation expansion.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Scrape any leftover paste over the top of the packed jars and add a splash of the rinse water (about 30 g / 2 tbsp per jar) if the kimchi looks dry. Press the cabbage down once more to ensure the brine covers the top. Cap with a loose-fitting lid (a quarter-turn from sealed) or an airlock lid; a fully sealed jar will build CO2 pressure during fermentation and can pop or crack.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Ferment at 18 to 22 °C (room temperature, away from direct sunlight) for 3 to 7 days. Place the jars on a small tray to catch any brine that bubbles up during active fermentation (this peaks at days 2 to 4 and is normal). Press the cabbage back down under the brine once daily with a clean spoon. Taste starting on day 3: early kimchi reads salty and chili-forward with no sour character; properly fermented kimchi reads sour, fizzy, and complex with a clear back-of-throat tang. The exact timing depends on temperature; warmer rooms hit the sour stage faster.'
    time_min: 0
  - text: 'Cold-store the kimchi. Once the kimchi has reached the desired sourness (most home cooks like day 4 to 5), transfer the jars to the refrigerator (2 to 4 °C); fermentation continues but slows to a crawl. The kimchi continues to develop complexity for the next 4 to 6 weeks and is at peak flavor between week 2 and month 3. Keeps 6+ months refrigerated; over-fermented kimchi (very sour, soft texture) is ideal for kimchi jjigae (stew) and kimchi fried rice rather than fresh eating.'
    time_min: 5

  - for: 'glutinous rice flour porridge'
    use: 'all-purpose flour porridge, or skip entirely'
    note: 'All-purpose flour works at the same weight but produces a slightly less glossy paste. Skipping the porridge is acceptable for a quick kimchi; the paste will be looser and the kimchi will ferment in 1 to 2 days less time but with slightly less body to the brine.'
  - for: 'asian pear'
    use: 'Honeycrisp or Fuji apple, or 30 g additional sugar'
    note: 'Pear and apple both contribute the fruit sugars and enzymatic tenderizing; apples are slightly tarter. Pure sugar substitutes the fermentation feed but loses the enzymatic effect; use 30 g additional sugar and accept slightly tougher cabbage.'
  - for: 'Korean radish (mu)'
    use: 'daikon, or watermelon radish'
    note: 'Daikon is the standard substitute and works identically. Watermelon radish has a similar crunch with mild bitterness that reads differently but acceptably. Red radishes are too small and bitter for this application.'

notes: |
  The lacto-fermentation chemistry. Kimchi fermentation is driven by a succession of lactobacillus species native to the cabbage and the saeujeot (the salted shrimp carries its own marine lactobacillus). The 7% cabbage brine in phase 1 selectively kills off most aerobic spoilage bacteria while preserving the salt-tolerant lactobacillus. During the ambient ferment, lactobacillus consumes the rice porridge sugars and the pear sugars, producing lactic acid (which drives the sour development), CO2 (the fizzy character and the bubbling-up brine), and small amounts of other organic acids and esters that build complexity over time. The salt level, temperature, and time are the three variables that determine the final character; the recipe values above produce a mid-sour, mid-funky kimchi that is the canonical baseline.

  Why the 7% brine. Korean kimchi brine targets 7% salt by weight, which is high enough to selectively kill aerobic spoilage bacteria (which die at salt levels above 5%) while preserving the salt-tolerant lactobacillus (which thrives up to 12%). A lower brine (3 to 5%) produces mushy, mold-prone kimchi; a higher brine (over 10%) stalls the fermentation entirely and produces a saltier, less complex kimchi.

  The two-day workflow. Day 1 is the brine (6 to 8 hours); day 2 is the paste-and-pack (about 75 minutes). This separation is structural; brining the cabbage and pasting it on the same day produces undersoftened leaves that cannot absorb the paste evenly. The overnight rest also allows the cabbage to fully drain.

  Salt math. The 7% cabbage brine pulls roughly 25% of the cabbage weight as water (the cabbage shrinks from 2000 g to about 1500 g of drained brined cabbage). The chili paste contributes about 60 g of salt (from the fish sauce). Combined with the residual cabbage salt, the finished kimchi reads at about 2.5 to 3% salinity, which is the canonical Korean range for ready-to-eat kimchi.

  Failure modes. Mold (white fuzzy growth on the top): the kimchi was not fully submerged in brine, or the headspace was too large; skim off the mold and the top layer if minor, discard the batch if extensive. Soft, mushy texture: the ferment ran too warm or too long; the kimchi is still edible but no longer crisp, use for kimchi jjigae or fried rice. Bitter or off flavors: the salt was iodized, the water was chlorinated, or the chili paste touched reactive metal; correct the variable and start over. No fizz, no sour development after 5 days: the room is too cold, the salt was too high, or the lactobacillus was killed by chlorinated water; move to a warmer spot and wait, or restart with fresh ingredients.

  The "kimchi smell" question. Actively fermenting kimchi produces a distinctive sulfurous, garlic-and-funk aroma that some households find unpleasant. Korean homes traditionally store kimchi in a separate dedicated kimchi refrigerator (a kimjang); the smell is part of the cuisine, not a sign of spoilage. If the smell becomes overwhelming, move the jars to a garage, basement, or sealed container during the active ferment.

  Storage and use. Refrigerated kimchi keeps 6 months easily and remains safe to eat indefinitely (the high acid and salt make it hostile to pathogens), though the texture degrades after about 3 months. Fresh kimchi (weeks 1 to 4) is best for eating raw as a side dish; medium kimchi (weeks 4 to 12) is the all-purpose stage; old kimchi (over 3 months) is too sour for fresh eating but is the canonical base for kimchi jjigae, kimchi fried rice, kimchi pancakes (kimchi-jeon), and braised pork with kimchi (kimchi-jjim).
