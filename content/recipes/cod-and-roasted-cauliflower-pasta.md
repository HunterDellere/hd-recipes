  note: 'Two southern-Italian rules drive the dish. First, no cheese on fish pasta: the pangrattato (toasted garlic breadcrumb) is the texture and the savory top note, the poor cook''s parmesan. Second, the anchovy is melted to nothing so it reads as depth, never as fish. The cod is poached in the seasoned oil rather than seared so the large flakes stay intact.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Thaw frozen cod fully and pat it very dry, then cut it into 3 cm chunks so the pieces hold their shape in the sauce instead of shredding to mush.'
  - 'Roast the cauliflower first; it needs about 25 minutes. Toast the pangrattato while it roasts so both are ready before the pasta goes into the water.'
  - 'Get the pasta water boiling before you start the sauce. The cod poaches in about 4 minutes and the orecchiette needs to land in the hot skillet the moment it is one minute shy of al dente, so the timing has to line up.'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 230C / 450F. Toss the 450 g cauliflower florets with the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil and 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt on a rimmed sheet pan and turn them cut-face-down in a single layer. Roast 22 to 25 minutes, undisturbed, until the cut faces are deep brown and the stems tender. The hard dry roast is what makes cauliflower sweet and nutty instead of sulfurous.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 24
  - text: 'Make the pangrattato while the cauliflower roasts. Warm the 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil in a small skillet over medium, add the 40 g / ⅔ cup panko and the 6 g / 1 grated garlic clove, and toast, stirring constantly, 2 to 3 minutes until deep gold and crisp. Season with a pinch of salt, scrape onto a plate, and wipe the pan. Watch it closely; breadcrumbs go from gold to burnt in seconds.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Bring about 2 L of water to a rolling boil with the 20 g / 2 tbsp kosher salt. It should taste assertively of salt, like the sea, because this is the only seasoning the pasta itself receives.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Build the cod oil. In a wide skillet, warm the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil over medium and add the 12 g / 3 sliced garlic cloves with the 2 g / 1 tsp red pepper flakes. Sizzle gently to pale gold, never brown (browned garlic turns bitter). Add the 12 g / 4 anchovy fillets and mash them with a spoon until they dissolve into the oil, about 1 minute.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pour in the 60 g / ¼ cup dry white wine and the 15 g / 1 tbsp drained capers, and simmer 1 minute to cook off the raw alcohol. Lay the 250 g cod chunks into the oil, spoon the liquid over them, cover, and poach gently 3 to 4 minutes until the fish is just opaque and reaches 52 to 54C. Do not stir, or the flakes break apart; let the chunks sit and baste.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Meanwhile, cook the 200 g orecchiette to 1 minute shy of al dente. Reserve about 200 g / ¾ cup of the starchy pasta water, then drain. Add the drained pasta and the roasted cauliflower to the skillet with 60 g / ¼ cup of the reserved water.'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Toss the pan off heat for 60 to 90 seconds, folding gently so the starchy water emulsifies the oil into a glossy sauce that coats every piece, while the cod breaks into large flakes through the pasta. If it looks tight or oily, add reserved water a tablespoon at a time until it turns silky and clings.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Off heat, add the juice of the half lemon (18 g) and ½ tsp of its zest and the 10 g / 3 tbsp chopped parsley, and toss once. Plate into warm bowls and shower each generously with the garlic pangrattato. Serve at once, while the crumbs are still crisp.'
    time_min: 1

    how: 'Drop the cod and double the anchovy work with a different umami source, since the anchovy is the only thing carrying the savory load. notes: |
  Why poach the cod instead of searing it. A seared fillet is the wrong texture for a tossed pasta; you want large, tender flakes that fold through the noodles, not a crusted slab. Poaching the 3 cm chunks gently in the seasoned oil keeps them moist and intact, and the oil they poach in becomes the backbone of the sauce. The discipline is to not stir while they cook: cod flakes are wide and fragile, and agitation turns them to shreds.

  Anchovy is seasoning, not fish. Four fillets melted into hot oil disappear completely and leave glutamate and salt behind. This is the southern-Italian use of anchovy, the same role it plays in puttanesca and in the dressing of a Caesar; cooks who think they dislike anchovy almost never notice it here. If you genuinely have none, a tablespoon of fish sauce or a teaspoon of colatura does the same job.

  No cheese, and why. On a Sicilian fish pasta the toasted breadcrumb is the cheese: it carries the savory top note and, more importantly, the textural contrast that grated cheese cannot. Adding parmigiano on top of fish is a real Italian taboo, not a snobbish one; the dairy flattens the brightness of the lemon and anchovy. The pangrattato does the job better. Toast it at the last minute and add it only at the table, or the steam from the pasta turns it soft.

  The emulsion is the dish. Like any good pasta, this lives on the toss: starchy pasta water whisked into the oil off the heat builds a sauce that clings, where oil alone just slides off and pools at the bottom of the bowl. Reserve more pasta water than you think you need and add it in stages. If the pasta tightens up as it sits (it will), a final splash of hot water loosens it back to silky.
