steps:
  - text: 'Combine the 500 g / scant 4 cups bread flour, 5 g / 1½ tsp instant yeast, and 5 g / 1 tsp granulated sugar in a stand-mixer bowl with a dough hook (or in a wide mixing bowl if working by hand). Add the 320 g / 1⅓ cups lukewarm 32 °C / 90 °F water and the 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil. Mix on low speed until the dough comes together as a shaggy mass, ~2 minutes. Add the 10 g / 2 tsp fine sea salt at this point rather than at the start; salt added too early slows the yeast bloom and salt added too late does not have enough mixing time to distribute evenly.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Knead on medium speed for 8 to 10 minutes (or 12 to 15 minutes by hand on a lightly floured surface, with a slap-and-fold technique to develop gluten without adding excess flour). The dough is fully kneaded when it passes a windowpane test: pinch off a piece, stretch it gently between thumbs and fingers; properly developed dough stretches to a thin translucent membrane without tearing. Underdeveloped dough tears immediately; over-kneaded dough at this hydration is rare in a stand mixer.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Bulk ferment 90 minutes at 24 °C / 75 °F until doubled in volume. Transfer the dough to a lightly oiled bowl, cover with a damp tea towel or a fitted lid; the warm-room ferment is faster than a cool ferment and produces a less complex but more reliable pita. The dough is fully fermented when a finger pressed into the surface leaves an indent that springs back slowly and partially.'
    time_min: 90
  - text: 'Punch down the bulk-fermented dough by folding it on itself two or three times. Divide into 8 equal pieces of ~105 g each, weighed on a kitchen scale. Shape each piece into a tight ball: pull the surface taut by tucking the edges underneath repeatedly until you have a smooth round with surface tension. Place the balls on a lightly floured surface, cover with a damp towel, and rest 30 minutes; the gluten relaxes during the rest and the balls become rollable without tearing or springing back.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Heat the oven with a pizza stone or baking steel on the middle rack to 290 °C / 550 °F (or as high as your oven goes; 260 °C / 500 °F minimum) for at least 45 minutes before the first bake. The stone must be at full temperature for the steam-puff to work; a partially heated stone produces flat, dense pita rather than puffed pocket pita. Use the broiler element if available to maintain peak temperature on the stone surface.'
    time_min: 45
  - text: 'Roll each dough ball on a lightly floured work surface into a round of 20 cm / 8 inches diameter and 3 to 4 mm thickness. Even thickness across the round is critical: a piece that is 5 mm in the center and 2 mm at the edge will puff unevenly because the surface sets at different rates. Use a tape measure or thickness gauge if your eye is unreliable. Cover the rolled rounds with a damp towel as you work to prevent the surface from drying.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Load 2 to 3 rolled rounds onto the hot stone using a peel or a flat baking sheet. Bake 90 seconds to 2½ minutes; the dough will puff into a complete balloon within 30 to 60 seconds as the interior steam pressure exceeds the surface tension. Pull the moment the surface is just gold-flecked, before deeper browning. Over-baked pita loses its softness and turns into pita chips; under-baked pita does not develop the gluten structure to hold the pocket once cooled.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Stack the baked pita immediately under a clean dry kitchen towel as you continue baking the remaining rounds. The residual steam trapped under the towel softens the pita slightly so they pack and store as soft pocket bread rather than crisp crackers. The stack should remain covered for 10 to 15 minutes after the last pita comes out of the oven; do not seal in plastic until fully cooled or condensation will make the bread soggy.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'The Adjustment: pita that puffs only partially had uneven thickness, insufficient stone preheat, or oven temperature below 260 °C; reroll, repreheat, retry. Pita that splits at the edge instead of forming a pocket was rolled too thick or stretched too much during transfer to the stone. Pita that scorches on the underside before puffing had the stone too close to the heating element; move to the middle rack. Pita that does not separate into two layers when torn open was insufficiently developed at the gluten stage; knead longer next time.'
    time_min: 0

The whole-wheat version produces a slightly denser pita with a more pronounced wheat character and a less reliable pocket; expect 50 to 70 percent of the rounds to puff fully rather than 90 percent.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'leavening'
    to: 'sourdough pita'
    how: 'Replace the 5 g instant yeast with 100 g of active 100% hydration sourdough starter (drop the water to 270 g to compensate for the starter water). Bulk ferment 4 to 6 hours rather than 90 minutes. The sourdough version produces a more complex flavor with the same pocket structure when properly puffed; the longer ferment produces more aroma but more variability in the puff.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'thickness'
    to: 'thicker Greek-style pita (no pocket)'
    how: 'Roll each ball to 5 to 6 mm thickness rather than 3 to 4 mm. The dough sets too slowly to develop interior steam pressure and produces a soft, thick, no-pocket flatbread (the canonical Greek-style pita for souvlaki and gyro wrapping). Bake at 230 °C / 450 °F for 4 to 5 minutes per side rather than the 90-second high-heat puff. The product is a different bread, not a failed pita.'
    kind: 'texture'
  - for: 'use'
    to: 'fattoush bread (intentionally staled)'
    how: 'Bake the pita per the standard recipe. Once cool, leave the loaves uncovered in a paper bag at room temperature for 24 to 48 hours; the moisture loss produces a uniformly stale bread that crisps audibly when fried or baked. Tear into 4 cm pieces and oven-toast at 200 °C / 400 °F for 8 to 10 minutes until uniformly crisp; the result is the structural cracker of a Lebanese fattoush salad. Day-fresh pita does not toast as evenly because the residual moisture varies across the loaf.'
    kind: 'texture'

notes: |
  The gluten development math. Lebanese pita lives on a 64 percent hydration ratio (320 g water to 500 g flour); higher hydration produces a more open crumb but less reliable pocket formation, lower hydration produces a denser bread that does not puff well. The 12 to 13 percent protein bread flour is structural: the gluten network needs to be strong enough to contain the steam pressure that builds during the 30 to 60 seconds before the surface sets, then to hold the resulting hollow shape after the pita comes out of the oven. Lower-protein flours produce a weaker network that either tears under steam pressure (no pocket forms) or collapses on cooling (pocket forms briefly, then the loaf falls flat).

  The roll-thickness tolerance. A difference of 1 mm matters dramatically in pita. Rounds that are uniformly 3 to 4 mm puff reliably; rounds that vary from 2 mm to 5 mm across the surface produce a flat-and-bubbled chimera that is neither pita nor flatbread. Practice with the rolling pin until you can roll an even thickness without thinking about it; serious pita bakers use a thickness gauge or a pair of dowels as a roll-pin spacer to enforce the dimension.

  The temperature requirement. The 290 °C / 550 °F oven temperature is not arbitrary. Below 260 °C / 500 °F the dough surface sets too slowly, allowing the interior steam to escape through micro-tears in the surface before pressure builds to the puff threshold; the result is a flat or partially-puffed loaf. Above 290 °C the surface sets so fast that the steam puffs before the loaf has fully spread, producing a small irregular pocket. The 290 °C window is where the surface sets in the right 10 to 15 second timeframe to trap the maximum interior steam.

  The day-after staling for fattoush. A Lebanese fattoush salad lives on day-after-stale pita that has been oven-toasted to a deep brown crisp. Day-fresh pita has variable interior moisture (trapped steam, the soft-and-crisp pocket-and-edge differential) that produces uneven toasting; day-after-stale pita has uniform moisture loss across the loaf and toasts evenly. Set the cooled pita in a paper bag at room temperature for 24 to 48 hours before tearing and toasting for fattoush. Plastic-wrapped pita does not stale evenly because the moisture redistributes within the package; paper bags allow the moisture to escape uniformly.

  Failure modes. Pita does not puff: insufficient oven preheat, oven temperature too low, dough too thick or uneven, gluten not developed enough during knead. Pita splits at the edge instead of pocketing: rolled too thin at the edge, stretched during transfer to the stone, surface dried during the rest stage. Pita scorches on the underside before puffing: stone too close to the heating element, oven temperature too high. Pita does not separate cleanly into two layers when torn: gluten was not developed enough, or the pocket did not form completely (a partial puff produces a partially-separated interior).

  Storage. Cooled pita stores in a sealed plastic bag at room temperature for 2 to 3 days, in the freezer (well-wrapped) for 2 to 3 months. To revive day-stored pita, sprinkle lightly with water and warm in a 180 °C / 350 °F oven for 60 seconds; the moisture re-softens the bread and the heat brings back some of the day-fresh character.

  Use. Lebanese pita goes into shawarma wraps, gyro wraps, falafel pockets, hummus and dip dipping, fattoush as the structural cracker, and as the bread component of any Lebanese or Levantine meze spread. The pocket version (this recipe) is the dipping-and-stuffing pita; the no-pocket version (Greek-style modification above) is the wrapping pita.
