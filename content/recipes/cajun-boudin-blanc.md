source:
  name: 'Acadiana boudin-stand canon (southwestern Louisiana)'
  note: 'Cajun boudin (specifically boudin blanc, the white pork-and-rice sausage; distinct from boudin noir, the French blood sausage) is the structural Acadiana road-trip food, sold at gas stations, grocery butcher counters, and dedicated boudin stands throughout the bayou parishes (Lafayette, Acadia, St. Landry, Vermilion). The defining elements are: pork shoulder and pork liver in roughly 4:1 ratio (the liver contributes structural depth and color); long-grain rice fully cooked and folded in (about 1:1 by weight with the pork); holy trinity and Cajun spice; coarse grind; hog casings; gentle poach to set without bursting. The traditional Cajun way to eat boudin is to squeeze the soft filling directly from the casing into your mouth at the stand, casing discarded; modern restaurant presentations slice it into rounds and grill or pan-fry.'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a weekend project that requires a meat grinder (KitchenAid stand mixer attachment, or dedicated grinder), a sausage stuffer (grinder attachment with a stuffing horn, or a dedicated stuffer), and natural hog casings (sold at butcher shops, or online; the casings need a 60 minute fresh-water soak before use to soften and remove the salt-packing brine). Without these tools, see the modification for "boudin balls" (fried portions without the casing step), the most popular Cajun substitute when the home cook does not have a stuffer.'
  - 'The pork and liver need to braise for 60 to 75 minutes before grinding. Plan a 3 hour total cook; the braise plus the cool-down to grindable temperature plus the grind and stuff plus the gentle poach.'
  - 'Hog casings are typically 32 to 35 mm in diameter (the same size as bratwurst casings). Save the casings for at least the first hour of soaking; over-soaked casings tear during stuffing.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'Pork liver in the boudin mix, cooked to fully tender (90 to 95 °C / 195 to 205 °F) during the braise rather than the USDA published-safe 70°C / 160°F target for offal'
    ref: 'safety/meat-doneness#pork-whole-muscle'
    why: 'The pork liver and pork shoulder both braise for 60 to 75 minutes at full simmer (above 95 °C). The long-cook far exceeds the USDA published-safe instantaneous target for pork offal; the meat reaches structural tenderness well past pasteurization. The food-safety attention in boudin is on (a) rapid cooling of the cooked meat before grinding (within 2 hours; the warm meat should not sit at room temperature), and (b) keeping the stuffing temperature low (use a chilled bowl and work near the fridge) so the sausage temperature stays below 5 °C during the fill stage.'

steps:
  - text: 'Cook the rice (Phase 2). 250 g rice + 500 ml water + 4 g salt; absorption method, 18 minutes simmer + 10 minute rest. Spread on a sheet pan to cool to room temperature; cold rice incorporates more cleanly than warm.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Combine all of Phase 1 (800 g pork shoulder, 200 g pork liver, 200 g onion, 100 g celery, 120 g bell pepper, 20 g garlic, 2 bay leaves, 1500 ml water, 8 g salt) in a heavy-bottomed pot. Bring to a hard boil, then reduce to a steady simmer. Cook 60 to 75 minutes, partially covered, until the pork shoulder is fully tender (a fork inserted slides in without resistance). Skim any grey foam in the first 15 minutes.'
    time_min: 75
  - text: 'Drain the pork mix in a colander set over a bowl; reserve the braising liquid. Discard the bay leaves. Spread the meat and vegetables on a sheet pan; cool to room temperature (about 20 minutes). Cooler than 5 °C is ideal for the grinder.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Soak the 6 m hog casings in cold fresh water for 60 minutes; rinse the inside of the casings under cold running water (slip the casing onto a tap with the open end and run cold water through; the casing self-cleans of any residual salt brine).'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'Grind the cooled meat-and-vegetable mixture through a coarse die (6 mm) of a meat grinder. The grind should be visibly chunky, with discrete pieces of meat and vegetable, not a fine puree.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'In a large bowl, combine the ground meat-vegetable mix with the cooled cooked rice, 20 g Cajun seasoning, 10 g salt, 3 g cayenne, 4 g black pepper, 2 g white pepper, 80 g sliced scallions, and 30 g chopped parsley. Mix thoroughly with gloved hands; the mixture should be moist, evenly seasoned, and hold together when squeezed. If too dry, add 100 to 300 ml of the reserved braising liquid (start with 200 ml; the mix should be pipeable but not sloppy). Taste a spoonful (fully cooked, safe to taste); adjust salt and cayenne.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Stuff the casings. Fit the sausage stuffer or grinder-stuffer-attachment with the stuffing horn. Slide the soaked casing onto the horn (it goes on as a 30 cm bunched-up tube; you push the mixture into one end and the casing fills and slides off the horn). Tie a knot at the free end of the casing. Crank the stuffer slowly, filling each link to a firm-but-not-tight tension (over-stuffed links burst during the poach; under-stuffed links read floppy). Twist into 15 to 18 cm links by spinning every third link in alternate directions every 15 cm or so; tie off the final end. Refrigerate 30 minutes to firm before poaching.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Poach the boudin. Bring a large wide pot of water (10 L) to 75 to 80 °C / 165 to 175 °F (a bare simmer with single bubbles rising lazily, not a hard simmer or boil). A boil bursts the casings. Gently lower the linked boudin into the water. Poach 15 to 20 minutes; the boudin is done when the casings have tightened around the filling, the internal temperature reads 75 °C / 165 °F, and a needle pierced into a link releases clear juice (not bloody or murky).'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Lift the boudin out with tongs; transfer to a sheet pan. Pat dry. Serve immediately, or cool and refrigerate up to 4 days. Eat by cutting the links into 8 cm segments and squeezing the soft filling out of the casing directly into your mouth (the canonical Cajun roadside method, casing discarded), or by slicing into 1 cm rounds and pan-searing in butter 90 seconds per side until the cut sides brown. Serve with Crystal hot sauce and a cold beer.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  Why pork shoulder plus liver. The 4:1 pork-shoulder-to-liver ratio is the structural Cajun blend that gives boudin its distinctive depth and color. The shoulder provides the structural lean-and-fat balance; the liver provides iron-rich depth, slight earthiness, and the characteristic pale-pink color of cooked boudin. An all-shoulder boudin (no liver) reads paler and less structurally Cajun.

  Why rice in approximately equal weight to the pork. Cajun boudin is structurally about 50 percent rice by weight; this is what distinguishes it from a French rice-pork sausage (less rice) or a Spanish morcilla (different rice ratio plus blood). The high rice load is structural for the Cajun signature texture (soft, scoopable, almost spreadable filling).

  Why poach, not boil. Sausage casings burst at temperatures above 85 °C; a hard boil at 100 °C splits every link. The poach at 75 to 80 °C cooks the filling through without stressing the casing. Cajun boudin is structurally fragile compared to bratwurst (the loose rice-and-pork filling does not have the binding-myosin matrix of a fresh pork sausage); the gentle poach is non-negotiable.

  Why eat from the casing. The Cajun roadside method of eating boudin (cut the link in half, squeeze the soft filling directly into the mouth, discard the casing) developed because the boudin filling is structurally soft and the natural hog casing does not have the snap of a bratwurst casing. The casing is a delivery vessel, not part of the eating experience. Modern Cajun restaurants slice the boudin into rounds and pan-sear; both are correct.

  Pairings. Crystal or Tabasco hot sauce at the table. A cold Abita Amber beer or sweet tea. Boudin is canonically a breakfast or roadside-snack food; it is rarely served as a dinner main but often as an appetizer or a side at a Cajun crawfish boil. Pickled okra and saltine crackers are common accompaniments.

  Storage. Cooked boudin keeps 4 days refrigerated. Reheat in a 175 °C / 350 °F oven 8 to 10 minutes (in casing), or pan-fry sliced rounds 90 seconds per side in butter. Freezes well as fully-cooked links; portion into 2-link bags. Thaw overnight in fridge before reheating.
