steps:
  - text: 'Stub: combine 300 g whole-wheat and 200 g all-purpose flour with 8 g fine sea salt in a bowl. Add 320 g warm water and 15 g olive oil; mix to a shaggy mass, then knead 8 minutes until smooth and slightly tacky. Cover and rest at room temperature for 30 minutes — gluten relaxation is what enables the paper-thin stretch later.'
    time_min: 40
  - text: 'Stub: divide into 6 equal balls (~140 g each), cover, rest another 15 minutes. Heat a saj (domed convex griddle) or invert a heavy carbon-steel wok over high heat until water dropped on the surface skitters and evaporates in under 2 seconds (~250 °C / 480 °F).'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Stub: stretch each ball over the back of the hands or on a lightly floured cloth into a round 30 cm wide, paper-thin. The dough should be translucent enough to read print through. Slap onto the hot saj; cook 20–30 seconds per side. The bread should bubble, blister, and just start to brown; do not let it crisp dry. Stack the cooked rounds under a clean towel to keep them pliable.'

notes: |
  This is a stub. Flesh out: the regional distinction between shrak (paper-thin, eaten the day it is made) and markook (slightly thicker, keeps a few days), the role of durum hard wheat in producing the elastic stretch, the saj surface chemistry (a domed cast-iron or carbon-steel surface concentrates heat at the apex and lets the bread cook in seconds without drying through), the at-home substitutes (an inverted wok over a gas burner, or a heavy carbon-steel skillet held empty over high heat for 5+ minutes), the hand-stretch technique with the back-of-the-knuckles flip and shimmy that produces an even paper-thin round without tearing, why no yeast (the bread is meant to be dense-pliable rather than risen and the cook time is too short for a yeast lift to matter), and the two roles in a mansaf service: the broken-up base layer that absorbs jameed sauce as it sits, and the torn whole rounds passed at the table for diners to scoop with their right hand.

  Storage: shrak is best the day it is made. Wrapped tight in a towel inside a plastic bag, the bread keeps pliable for 24 hours at room temperature; refrigeration stales it almost immediately. Frozen rounds (interleaved with parchment, sealed) thaw cleanly in 30 minutes covered.

  When the saj is unavailable, the inverted-wok method is the closest approximation. A large carbon-steel skillet preheated empty over high heat for 5 minutes also works for smaller rounds (~22 cm), though the absence of the dome shape changes the cook profile slightly: the center cooks first instead of the edges, so the bread reads more like a thick lavash than a true shrak.
