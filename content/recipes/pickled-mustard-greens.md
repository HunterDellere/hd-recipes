before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 7 to 10 day lacto-ferment. Plan for the jar to occupy counter space at 18 to 22 C for that window, fluctuating temperatures or a cold kitchen below 16 C slow the ferment past 14 days and risk yeast bloom before lactic acid drops the pH.'
  - 'Use non-chlorinated water (filtered or boiled-and-cooled). Tap-water chlorine inhibits the lactobacilli that drive the ferment, the brine will stall or develop off-aromas instead of clean sour.'
  - 'The greens must stay fully submerged under the brine for the duration. Use a fermentation weight, a small water-filled ziplock bag, or a clean stone, any leaf exposed to air will mold and contaminate the batch.'
  - 'Burp the jar daily for the first 4 days. CO2 builds aggressively in the active phase and an unvented jar can crack or pop the lid.'

steps:
  - text: 'Wash the 1000 g mustard greens carefully. Trim any browned tips. Pull the heads apart into individual leaves so the brine can reach every surface; large heads can be halved lengthwise instead. Lay the leaves on a clean tray in a single layer and air-dry 4 hours, or until they wilt visibly and lose 10 to 15% of their weight. The wilt step concentrates flavor and reduces water that would otherwise dilute the brine.'
    time_min: 250
  - text: 'Bring the 1500 ml / 6 cups water to a boil with the 45 g / 3 tbsp kosher salt and any optional aromatics (5 g / 2 tsp Sichuan peppercorns, 20 g sliced ginger, 4 dried chilies if using). Stir to fully dissolve the salt. Pull off heat and cool completely to room temperature; warm brine kills the lactic acid bacteria you want to encourage, and chlorinated tap water suppresses them, so cold-and-clean is the rule.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Pack the wilted greens into a clean 2-liter glass jar (a wide-mouth jar is easier to load and unload). Press them down hard with a wooden spoon as you pack to expel air; trapped air pockets harbor unwanted yeasts and molds. Pour the cooled brine over to fully submerge the greens.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Weigh the greens down with a brine-bag (a zip-top bag filled with extra brine, sealed, and pressed onto the surface) or a clean glass weight. Every leaf must stay below the brine surface; any leaf that floats up will go moldy within 48 hours. Cover the jar with a clean cloth secured with a rubber band, or with a fermentation airlock lid. Do not seal airtight: the lacto-fermentation produces CO2 that needs to escape, and a sealed jar will eventually pop.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Hold at 18 to 22°C / 65 to 72°F, out of direct sunlight, for 7 to 10 days. From day 3, you should see small bubbles rising through the brine and the brine itself going cloudy: this is healthy lactic fermentation. The greens will progress from bright green to olive to a uniform deep yellow-green. Taste a leaf at day 7: it should be tangy, faintly funky, crisp, and noticeably saltier than fresh. If still under-tangy, give 2 more days; if already aggressively sour, refrigerate immediately to slow the ferment.'
    time_min: 14040
  - text: 'When the greens are at desired tang, refrigerate the whole jar. They keep 6 months in the fridge, slowly developing more sourness; pull them at month 3 to 4 for the cleanest tang-to-funk balance. To use, lift out a leaf or two with clean tongs (never fingers, never a wet utensil), squeeze out the brine, slice as the recipe directs, and return the rest to the jar. Save the brine: it is a finished ingredient itself, used in suan cai yu fish soup and Northeastern dumplings.'
    time_min: 5

    how: 'Replace mustard greens with Napa cabbage. Salt-pack the cabbage instead of brine: layer wedges with 30 g salt per 1 kg cabbage in the jar, weight, and let the cabbage release its own brine. Ferment 14 to 21 days at 4 to 10°C / 40 to 50°F (refrigerator or cold cellar). The slower cold ferment produces the cleaner, less aggressive suan cai used in Northeastern dumpling fillings and pork-and-cabbage stew.'

notes: |
  How to use. Squeezed and chopped into a small dish on the table beside congee, where two or three pieces per spoonful contribute the salt-sour-crunch contrast that plain rice porridge needs. Stir-fried briefly with rendered pork belly, scallions, and chili for the classic suan cai chao rou over rice. Layered into beef noodle soup. Folded into Northeastern pork-and-cabbage dumplings. Floated in suan cai yu fish soup. Eaten on its own with rice and a fried egg.

  The 3% brine is the entire structural rule. Lower than 2.5% and unwanted yeasts and molds win the fermentation race against the lactic acid bacteria. Higher than 4% and the bacteria themselves are inhibited and the ferment stalls. The 3% mark is the safe, classical concentration that food-science laboratories converged on for vegetable lacto-pickles, and it is what every traditional Chinese suan cai recipe corresponds to once you back-calculate from the salt-to-water ratios.

  Failure modes. White surface yeast (kahm yeast) appearing as a thin film on the brine is non-toxic but indicates greens have surfaced; skim it off and re-submerge. Black, blue, or pink fuzzy mold means contamination has won; discard the entire batch and re-do with cleaner technique. The brine smelling cleanly tangy and faintly funky is healthy; smelling rotten or sulfurous is not. Trust your nose.
