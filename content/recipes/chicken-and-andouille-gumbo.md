source:
  name: 'Louisiana Cajun tradition (rural Acadiana, roux-thickened school)'
  note: 'Three thickening schools coexist in Louisiana gumbo: file (Choctaw sassafras leaf powder, often added at the table), okra (West African, used in seafood gumbos and gumbo z''herbes), and dark roux (Acadian-French, most common in chicken-andouille). This recipe sits in the dark-roux school; file is offered at the table per diner. Gumbo and jambalaya are siblings, not the same dish, gumbo is a stew served over rice, jambalaya is rice cooked in the pot with the protein.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'pulling chicken thighs at internal 75°C / 167°F after the 90-minute braise'
    ref: 'safety/meat-doneness#poultry'
    why: 'Bone-in thigh meat is forgiving past the USDA published-safe 74°C / 165°F target and reads structurally better at 75 to 80°C as the connective tissue softens. The 90-minute simmer above 85°C clears pasteurization equivalency by an enormous margin; the thermometer reading is a doneness check, not a safety check.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The dark roux is the entire dish. Plan 30 to 45 minutes of continuous stirring at the stove with no phone, no distractions, and oven mitts ready. Roux at the chocolate-dark stage is 175 to 185°C, the hottest substance in your kitchen, splash burns are severe, and aspirated fumes can trigger coughing. Ventilate the kitchen and keep small children and pets out for the duration.'
  - 'Cook the long-grain rice as a PARALLEL TRACK in the final 30 minutes of the gumbo simmer. Standard 2:1 water-to-rice ratio, covered, 18 minutes simmer, 10 minutes covered rest off heat. Gumbo without rice in the bowl is sauce in a bowl; the rice is structural to the eating experience, not garnish.'
  - 'Brown the chicken and the andouille in the same pot before starting the roux, both contribute fond to the eventual roux base. Drain the rendered fat and use it as part of the roux fat for additional depth.'
  - 'Build a real chicken stock the day before if at all possible. The gumbo gravy is reduced stock layered with roux; a thin store-bought broth produces a thin-tasting gumbo no matter how good the roux. See homemade_alternatives.'

steps:
  - text: 'Season the 1200 g chicken thighs with 8 g kosher salt and 2 g black pepper an hour before cooking; let them temper at room temperature while you slice the 400 g andouille into 1 cm coins and dice the Trinity. The dry-brine pellicle on the chicken skin produces a noticeably better sear than salting at the last second.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'Brown the proteins. Heat 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy 5 to 7 quart Dutch oven over medium-high heat until shimmering. Lay the 1200 g chicken thighs skin-side down in a single layer (in two batches if needed). Brown undisturbed 5 to 7 minutes per side until the skin is deep mahogany. Transfer to a plate. Add the 400 g andouille coins in a single layer and brown 2 to 3 minutes per side until the edges crisp and a sticky orange-brown fond accumulates on the pot bottom. Transfer to the chicken plate. Pour off the rendered fat into a heatproof measuring cup; reserve.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Build the dark roux. This is the structural moment of the dish; do not rush it. Pour 120 g / ½ cup + 2 tbsp neutral oil (using reserved chicken-and-sausage fat for up to half) back into the same pot over medium heat. Whisk in the 120 g / 1 cup all-purpose flour all at once until smooth. Reduce heat to medium-low. Stir continuously with a flat-bottomed wooden spoon or silicone spatula, scraping the bottom and corners every 5 to 10 seconds without pause. The roux passes through stages: pale blond at 5 minutes (smells of cookie dough), peanut-butter beige at 15 minutes (smells nutty), milk-chocolate brown at 25 minutes (smells of toasted nuts and caramel), and finally dark-chocolate brown at 30 to 45 minutes (smells of coffee and burnt sugar, the target). Pull the instant the color reads dark chocolate and the smell registers as toasted coffee; one minute past that is acrid burnt-roux territory and the entire batch goes in the trash.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 40
  - text: 'Drop the Trinity into the roux. Add the 300 g diced onion, 150 g diced celery, and 150 g diced bell pepper all at once to the screaming-hot roux. The vegetables will hiss violently and the roux temperature will drop sharply (this is intentional; the cool vegetables stop the roux from advancing past dark chocolate). Stir continuously for 4 to 5 minutes until the onion turns translucent and the vegetables soften and slump into the roux. Add the 20 g / 6 cloves minced garlic; stir 60 seconds until fragrant.'
    technique: 'fond-deglaze'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Add the stock and aromatics. Slowly pour in the 2000 ml / 8 cups warm chicken stock while whisking constantly to prevent the roux from clumping. The roux-stock mixture should go smooth and slightly thicken within 60 seconds. Add the 3 bay leaves, 2 g / 1 tsp dried thyme, 4 g / 2 tsp smoked paprika, 2 g / ½ tsp cayenne, 10 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt, 10 g / 2 tsp Worcestershire, and 8 g / 1½ tsp hot sauce. Stir to combine. Return the browned chicken (with any accumulated juices) and andouille to the pot.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Braise. Bring to a simmer over medium-high, then reduce to medium-low so the surface trembles rather than boils. Cover with the lid offset (slight gap for steam release). Simmer 75 to 90 minutes. The chicken is done structurally at 30 minutes but the longer simmer is what marries the roux, stock, Trinity, and andouille into the unified mahogany gravy that defines gumbo. Stir every 15 to 20 minutes, scraping the pot bottom to prevent the roux from settling and scorching.'
    technique: 'braising'
    time_min: 80
  - text: 'Pull and shred the chicken. After 75 to 90 minutes, fish out the chicken thighs with tongs onto a cutting board. Let them cool 5 minutes (they are at 80°C and will scald). Pull the meat from the bones in large bite-sized chunks; discard skin and bones. The skin has done its job (rendered fat into the braise) and turns flabby in long-cooked gumbo. Return the shredded chicken to the pot. Fish out and discard the 3 bay leaves.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Adjust and finish. Taste the gumbo. It should read deeply savory with an obvious smoke note from the andouille and paprika, a warm slow heat from the cayenne and hot sauce, the dark coffee-toast backnote from the roux, and salt that registers as seasoning rather than as salt. Adjust salt with kosher salt by the teaspoon. If the heat is too gentle, add hot sauce by the half-teaspoon. If the gravy is too thick, thin with hot water by the quarter-cup; if too thin, simmer uncovered 5 to 10 more minutes. Stir in the 50 g sliced green onions and 15 g chopped parsley off-heat 60 seconds before serving.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Serve over rice. Mound 100 g / ½ cup cooked long-grain rice in the center of each wide soup bowl. Ladle 250 to 300 g of the gumbo around (not over) the rice mound, so the rice peeks through the gravy as an island. Each diner stirs the rice into their portion at the table, dusts with file powder (4 g / 2 tsp total for the pot, offered as a small bowl with a spoon for each diner to take a pinch), and adds hot sauce to their personal heat level. The rice-to-gumbo ratio is the diner''s call; the canonical is roughly one part rice to two parts gumbo by volume.'
    time_min: 3

modifications:
  - for: 'protein'
    to: 'seafood gumbo (shrimp, crab, oyster)'
    how: 'Skip the chicken and andouille. Build a seafood stock from 500 g shrimp shells (browned then simmered 30 minutes in 2 L water). Use a medium-brown roux (peanut butter stage, 18 to 22 minutes) rather than dark chocolate; seafood is too delicate for the dark roux''s assertive bitterness. Add 500 g peeled shrimp and 200 g lump crab in the last 4 minutes of the simmer. Finish with 12 oysters and their liquor in the last 60 seconds (the oysters curl and the dish is ready). Often thickened with okra (200 g sliced, sweated separately to remove slime, then added with the seafood) rather than with extra roux.'
    kind: 'protein'
  - for: 'thickener'
    to: 'gumbo z''herbes (Holy Thursday Lenten gumbo)'
    how: 'Skip the meat entirely; use 1.5 kg total of 7 to 11 different greens (collards, mustard greens, turnip greens, beet tops, spinach, watercress, chicory, parsley, cabbage, scallion tops, kale), simmered 30 minutes in salted water then chopped fine. Build a medium-dark roux (28 minutes), Trinity, garlic, then add the chopped greens with 2 L of the green-cooking liquid. Simmer 30 minutes. Traditionally vegetarian (Lenten observance); modernly often built with ham hock or tasso for body.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'pressure cooker'
    to: 'instant pot gumbo (cuts time in half)'
    how: 'Build the roux and Trinity on saute mode as written (the roux must still be made manually; there is no pressure-cooker shortcut for browning flour). Add stock, chicken, and andouille. Pressure cook on high for 25 minutes, natural release 15 minutes. The roux flavor is 90% of stovetop; the Trinity vegetables come out softer (sometimes too soft for some palates).'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'thickener'
    to: 'okra-thickened (no roux)'
    how: 'Skip the roux entirely. Sweat 400 g sliced fresh okra in 30 g neutral oil with the Trinity for 8 to 10 minutes until the okra slime breaks down and the vegetables go glossy-thick. Add stock and proceed. The dish reads lighter, more vegetal, and is the form most common in West African-influenced gumbo (the word "gumbo" derives from the Bantu word for okra). Pairs especially well with shrimp.'
    kind: 'regional'

notes: |
  The dark roux is the recipe. Cajun cooks treat the roux as a personality test for the cook; some swear by 45 minutes of stirring over low heat, others by an 18-minute aggressive medium-high cook with constant stirring (the so-called Paul Prudhomme method, which produces nearly the same color in half the time but requires more attention and faster recovery from near-burns). Both work. The non-negotiable is the color, dark chocolate at minimum, and the smell, coffee and toasted nut. Pale roux produces pale gumbo no matter how long you simmer.

  Why no garlic in the Trinity. The Cajun Holy Trinity is onion-celery-bell-pepper, with no garlic. Garlic goes in as a separate addition after the Trinity has softened, since garlic burns faster than the larger-cut Trinity vegetables. The Trinity is sometimes referred to as the Pope (with garlic added) but the canonical three are unanimous across Acadian cooking. This is the major structural distinction from the French mirepoix (onion-carrot-celery) that built the Old World French stock tradition the Cajuns descended from.

  Roux-versus-okra-versus-file. The three Louisiana thickening schools are not interchangeable in canonical practice. Roux-thickened gumbos are the most common (chicken-andouille, dark-meat gumbos generally); okra-thickened are typical of seafood gumbos and have stronger West African lineage; file-thickened (file added at the table) is the Choctaw indigenous contribution and is usually used as a finisher rather than as the only thickener. Mixing all three in one pot is not authentic to any single tradition but produces a dish many cooks like; the canonical move is to pick one.

  Gumbo improves on day 2 and day 3. The roux, stock, and andouille smoke molecules continue to integrate overnight, and the dish reads more unified the second day than the first. Make the day before serving if possible. Refrigerate up to 5 days; freezes 3 months. Reheat gently over medium-low; high heat can break the emulsified roux.

  The rice is partner, not garnish. Long-grain (Louisiana white rice, basmati, jasmine) is the canonical bed; short-grain rice is wrong (too sticky, clumps in the gumbo). The rice goes in the bowl first and the gumbo ladles over and around it. Each diner stirs the rice into their portion at the table. Some Cajun households serve potato salad on the side as a second starch; that is regional and worth a try.

  File powder at the table, not in the pot. File (filé, ground sassafras leaf) in simmering liquid turns ropy and gluey (mucilage). The Choctaw and Cajun canonical use is to add a pinch at the table, off-heat, just before eating; each diner doses to their preference. The flavor is woodsy and earthy with a tea-like finish; it adds depth without dramatically changing the dish.

  Heat calibration. A traditional Cajun gumbo runs hotter than this recipe; the 2 g / ½ tsp cayenne plus 8 g / 1½ tsp hot sauce in the pot lands the heat at a warm-backnote level appropriate for diners who do not regularly eat Louisiana hot. Cajun cooks would push to 4 g cayenne plus 20 g hot sauce easily, with more hot sauce at the table. Calibrate to the household.
