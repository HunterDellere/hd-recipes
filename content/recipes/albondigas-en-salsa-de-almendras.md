before_you_start:
  - 'Toast the almonds first. The whole structural premise of the sauce depends on properly toasted almonds; raw almonds in the sauce read flat and chalky. Toast at 175°C / 350°F for 10 to 12 minutes until pale golden and intensely aromatic, then cool before grinding.'
  - 'Soak the bread in milk for at least 5 minutes before mixing the meatballs. The milk-soaked bread (panade) is the structural tenderizer that keeps the meatballs from going dense; skipping it produces rubbery meatballs.'

steps:
  - text: 'Toast the 100 g almonds in a 175°C / 350°F oven for 10 to 12 minutes, until pale golden and intensely aromatic. Cool fully (10 minutes on a plate). The almonds will crack and split slightly as they cool; this is correct.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Soak the panade. Tear the 60 g crustless bread into rough chunks; place in a small bowl. Pour the 120 ml warm milk over the bread; let stand 5 minutes, then mash with a fork into a smooth paste.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Mix the meatballs. In a large bowl, combine the 400 g ground pork, 400 g ground beef, 1 egg, half (7 g) of the minced garlic, half (5 g) of the chopped parsley, the 9 g fine sea salt, 2 g black pepper, 1 g ground cumin, and the milk-soaked panade. Mix gently with your hands just until the panade is evenly distributed; do not overwork or the meatballs go dense. Form into 24 meatballs of about 50 g each (slightly larger than a golf ball).'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Dredge the meatballs lightly in the 50 g flour, shaking off excess. The thin flour coating builds a more cohesive sear and helps the sauce cling to the meatballs.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Brown the meatballs. Heat 50 g / 3 tbsp of the olive oil in a wide heavy skillet or Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Brown the meatballs in two batches, 5 to 6 minutes per batch, turning to get color on all sides. The meatballs should be deeply browned on the outside but not cooked through; the braise will finish them. Remove to a plate.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Build the sauce. Add the remaining 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil to the pan. Add the 150 g diced onion; sweat 6 to 8 minutes over medium heat until translucent and just-golden. Add the remaining 8 g minced garlic; cook 60 seconds.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the 200 g canned tomatoes, crushing them with a wooden spoon as they hit the pan. Cook 5 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the tomato liquid has reduced and the mixture looks pasty.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Grind the almonds. While the tomato reduces, transfer the cooled toasted 100 g almonds to a food processor or mortar with about 60 ml of the chicken stock. Pulse or pound to a fine paste, like coarse almond butter. The texture should be uniform but not entirely smooth, a slight grain is correct and structural to the sauce body.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Deglaze with sherry. Pour the 120 ml / ½ cup Fino sherry into the tomato base. It will steam aggressively; scrape any browned bits up from the pan. Cook 2 to 3 minutes until the alcohol has evaporated and the sherry has reduced into the tomato.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add the remaining 440 ml chicken stock, the almond paste, the 0.3 g saffron threads (crumbled between fingers as they go in to release the color), and the 1 bay leaf. Stir to integrate. Bring to a simmer; taste, the sauce should be savory, slightly nutty, faintly sherry-floral, with the saffron color just beginning to develop.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Braise the meatballs. Return the browned meatballs and any accumulated juices to the sauce. The liquid should come up to about three-quarters of the meatball height. Simmer covered over medium-low heat for 25 minutes; the meatballs cook through and the sauce thickens to a glossy coating consistency as the almond paste hydrates. Stir gently every 8 minutes to prevent the bottom from scorching.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Finish and serve. Pull the bay leaf. Taste, adjust with the 3 g fine sea salt as needed. The sauce should coat the back of a spoon, with the meatballs sitting in just enough sauce to mop with bread. Scatter the remaining 5 g chopped parsley across the surface. Serve in a wide shallow dish (the canonical Spanish tapas-bar presentation) with bread for mopping or over white rice. Pass the lemon wedges; a small squeeze on each meatball is the finishing acid that lifts the dish.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  The Moorish thread. Almond-thickened sauces are the structural fingerprint of Andalusian cooking and descend directly from the 8 centuries of Moorish presence in southern Spain. The use of ground nuts as a sauce body (instead of flour, cream, or egg yolk) is shared across the Mediterranean Islamic world: Turkish tarator, Persian fesenjan, Lebanese muhammara, Catalan romesco, and Andalusian salsa de almendras all share this technique. The cumin and saffron in the meatball mix and sauce reinforce the Moorish lineage; this is not a "twist," it is the canonical dish.

  Why panade is non-negotiable. The bread-and-milk paste (panade) tenderizes ground meat by interfering with the formation of dense myosin cross-links during cooking. Meatballs made without panade go rubbery; meatballs made with panade stay tender even when fully cooked. The technique is structural across European meatball traditions: Italian polpette use bread-and-milk, Greek keftedes use bread-and-milk, Swedish köttbullar use bread-and-cream. Skip the panade and the dish is materially worse.

  Almond grind texture. A slightly coarse almond grind (with visible specks of almond) produces a sauce with textural interest and a more pronounced nutty bite. A fully smooth almond grind (like almond butter) produces a velvety sauce with no texture. Both are valid; the slightly coarse grind is the traditional Andalusian preference and is what the recipe targets. The grind is too coarse if individual almond pieces are larger than a sesame seed in the final sauce.

  Bread choice for the panade. Day-old or stale Spanish country bread (pan rústico) is the canonical choice; the crustless interior is what gets soaked. Sourdough or any rustic white bread works. Avoid soft American sandwich bread (too sweet, too soft, results in mushy meatballs); avoid whole wheat (too aggressive, overpowers the almond and saffron). Pita and naan also do not work; the textures are wrong.

  Storage. Refrigerated in a sealed container for 4 days; the dish improves significantly overnight as the almond-saffron sauce integrates further into the meatballs. Freezes well for 3 months; thaw overnight in the refrigerator and reheat gently in a covered pan with a splash of water if the sauce has thickened too much. The dish reheats better than it serves fresh; consider making it a day ahead.
