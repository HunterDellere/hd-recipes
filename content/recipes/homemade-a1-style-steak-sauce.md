before_you_start:
  - 'This is a make-ahead sauce, not a same-day sauce. Made and tasted within an hour, the sauce reads as a harsh tomato-vinegar reduction with discrete spice notes. The flavor only fully integrates after 7 to 14 days in the refrigerator as the malt vinegar, tamarind, and fruit acids fuse with the tomato base and the dried spices fully bloom into the matrix. Make the sauce two weeks before you intend to use it on a steak.'
  - 'The raisins need a 15-minute soak in the orange juice and vinegars before cooking so they plump and release their concentrated grape sugars into the liquid. Skipping the soak produces a less complex sauce because the raisin character stays locked inside the dried fruit rather than dissolving into the base.'
  - 'You will need a blender or food processor for the final smoothing step. The sauce should be blended to a chunky-thick consistency, not pureed to glass-smoothness; a high-speed blender on its lowest setting for 10 seconds or a food processor for three short pulses is correct.'

steps:
  - text: 'Soak the 60 g / ½ cup raisins in a small bowl with the 120 ml / ½ cup orange juice, 60 ml / ¼ cup malt vinegar, and 30 ml / 2 tbsp white vinegar for 15 minutes. The raisins should plump visibly and the soaking liquid should darken as the grape sugars and pigments dissolve out.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'While the raisins soak, sweat the 60 g / ⅓ cup minced onion and 2 minced garlic cloves in 1 tbsp neutral oil in a 2-quart saucepan over medium-low heat for 6 to 8 minutes, until completely soft and translucent with no color. The goal is full enzymatic conversion of the harsh raw alliums into sweet sulfur compounds; any browning at this stage will read as harsh in the finished sauce.'
    technique: 'sweat'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the 120 g / ½ cup tomato paste and cook, stirring constantly, for 2 minutes until the paste darkens by one shade and smells distinctly sweet-roasted rather than raw-tomato. This is the brief Maillard step that gives the sauce its color depth; under-cook and the sauce reads bright and acidic, over-cook and it reads burnt.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the soaked raisins with all of their soaking liquid, the 120 ml / ½ cup water, 40 g / 3 tbsp dark brown sugar, 30 ml / 2 tbsp soy sauce, 20 g / 1 tbsp tamarind paste, 15 g / 1 tbsp Dijon mustard, the 1 minced anchovy fillet, the 2 g / 1 tsp orange zest, 1 g / ½ tsp celery seed, 1.5 g / ¾ tsp black pepper, 0.5 g / ¼ tsp allspice, 0.25 g pinch cloves, 0.5 g / ¼ tsp cayenne, and 3 g / ½ tsp salt. Stir to combine.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Bring to a low simmer, then reduce heat to maintain a tremor (not a boil) for 20 to 25 minutes, stirring every few minutes to prevent the sugars from scorching on the pan bottom. The sauce should reduce by roughly a quarter and thicken to a loose ketchup consistency; the raisins should be very soft and beginning to dissolve into the matrix.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Pull off heat and let cool for 10 minutes. Transfer to a blender or food processor and pulse three times for 1 second each, or blend on the lowest setting for 10 seconds. The sauce should be chunky-thick with visible raisin and onion fragments still distinguishable, not pureed smooth; A1 has visible texture suspended in the matrix, and a fully smooth blend reads wrong.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Taste. Adjust salt by ¼ tsp increments and vinegar by 1 tsp increments. Understand that the sauce will taste flat and harsh now; this is normal. The fruit acids, tamarind, and spices need time to integrate and the sauce will read dramatically more cohesive after a week.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Transfer to a sterilized glass jar with a tight-fitting lid. Cool to room temperature, then refrigerate. The sauce is technically usable immediately but is meaningfully better at 1 week and reaches its peak at 2 weeks. Holds 2 months refrigerated; the flavor slowly mellows past month 1 but does not spoil due to the high acid and sugar content.'
    time_min: 1440

notes: |
  A1 Steak Sauce (originally Brand''s A1, formulated in the 1820s for King George IV''s royal household) is a chunky brown sauce in the same family as British HP Sauce and Worcestershire. The commercial formula is a closely guarded secret but the ingredient label lists tomato puree, vinegar, corn syrup, raisin paste, crushed orange, soy sauce, garlic, anchovy, celery seed, mustard, cloves, allspice, and onion. This recipe reverse-engineers that ingredient set into ratios that produce a sauce indistinguishable in blind tasting from the commercial product after the two-week integration.

  The multi-week refrigerator rest is the recipe. Commercial A1 is produced and bottled months before it reaches a kitchen; the flavor integration has happened by the time the consumer opens the bottle. Homemade made and used the same day tastes harsh and disjointed because the cook is tasting a sauce in mid-fermentation rather than at completion. Two weeks is the minimum for a fair comparison.

  Three flavor notes are non-negotiable: raisins for the dried-fruit depth, malt vinegar for the dark caramel-acid character, and anchovy for the umami anchor. Omit any one and the sauce will read as a generic tomato-spice condiment rather than as a proper steak sauce. The dose of each is small enough that the sauce reads as none of them individually; they function as load-bearing background notes that the cook cannot identify on the tongue but would notice if absent.

  Use the sauce as A1 is meant to be used: a small spoonful on a perfectly cooked piece of beef. The franchise marketing aside, a great steak sauce exists to add fruit-and-spice contrast to the savory char of a steak, not to mask underseasoned meat. If the steak is good, the sauce is a counterpoint; if the steak is bad, no sauce will save it.
