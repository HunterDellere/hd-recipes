before_you_start:
  - 'The kale needs a 10 minute rest after massaging, so strip and ribbon the leaves and start the massage before you build the dressing. The salt and acid keep working during the rest, and the leaf goes from leathery to salad-tender in that window; dressing kale that has only just been massaged reads tough.'
  - 'Use day-old sourdough for the crumbs. Fresh bread tears into pasty wads that bake leathery; stale bread tears into rough open-faced shards that crisp with shatter. If you only have fresh, tear it an hour ahead and leave the crumbs uncovered on the counter to dry the surfaces.'
  - 'Stage everything before you toss: crumbs cooled, dressing finished, kale massaged and rested, cheese grated, platter ready. Massaged kale holds a dressed state far longer than romaine (it will not wilt in 5 minutes), but the crumbs soften once they hit the dressing, so dress and add the crumbs last, just before serving.'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 180 °C / 350 °F. While it preheats, warm the 45 g / 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil with the 12 g / 3 smashed garlic cloves in a small saucepan over the lowest possible heat for 8 minutes. The oil should never sizzle; the goal is to extract garlic aromatics at sub-Maillard temperature so the cloves stay sweet rather than turning bitter. Pull off the heat, fish out the garlic, and discard.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Toss the 180 g / 6 oz torn sourdough shards with the strained garlic oil and the 2 g / ⅓ tsp fine sea salt in a wide bowl until the bread has absorbed every drop. Spread on a sheet tray in a single layer, craggy faces up, with no shards touching. Bake 12 to 14 minutes, tossing once at 7 minutes, until uniformly golden and dry through to the centre. Pull and cool on the tray; the crumbs crisp further as residual steam escapes. The rough hand-torn faces give more crisp surface area than a clean cube, which is the textural point of crumbs over croutons.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Massage the kale. Put the 400 g / 8 cups ribboned lacinato kale in a large bowl with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt and the 10 g / 2 tsp lemon juice. Rub the kale between your palms with firm pressure for 90 seconds, working in handfuls, until the volume in the bowl drops by roughly half, the leaves darken and gloss, and a ribbon feels pliant and silky rather than stiff. The salt pulls water from the cells osmotically, the mechanical bruising collapses the cell walls, and the acid lowers the pH to blunt the bitter isothiocyanates; the result is a kale that eats tender and only mildly bitter. Set it aside to rest while you build the dressing, at least 10 minutes; the tenderizing continues during the rest.'
    technique: 'kale-massage'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Bring a small saucepan of water to a bare simmer (around 88 °C / 190 °F, surface trembling, no rolling bubbles). Lower the 2 fridge-cold eggs in with a slotted spoon and hold for 90 seconds. The 90-second coddle pasteurizes the egg surface and partially denatures the outer yolk proteins, which thickens the dressing without setting the yolk; the yolk should still pour. Lift the eggs straight into cold tap water for 60 seconds to stop carryover cooking.'
    technique: 'jammy-egg'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Crack each egg over a fine-mesh sieve set over a small mixing bowl; the white drains away and the warm yolk drops in. Discard the whites or save them for another use. You should have about 35 g of warm coddled yolk in the bowl, its colour deepened from raw yellow to opaque cream.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Build the water phase first. Whisk the 18 g / 6 minced anchovy fillets, the 6 g grated garlic, the 8 g / 1½ tsp dijon mustard, the 18 g / 3½ tsp lemon juice, and the 5 g / 1 tsp worcestershire sauce into the warm yolks until the anchovy fully dissolves into a uniform pale-bronze paste with no visible fillet shreds. Dissolved anchovy is non-negotiable: visible chunks read as fishy hot spots, while fully dissolved it reads only as deep glutamate savor.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Stream in the 90 g / 6 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil in a thin thread while whisking vigorously. The yolk lecithin and the dijon mucilage coat each oil droplet as it enters, and the dressing thickens visibly from a thin slick to a glossy sauce that coats the back of a spoon. The egg-yolk emulsion holds longer and clings harder than a pure mustard vinaigrette; that body is what lets the dressing grip the massaged kale. If the emulsion stays thin and oil-streaked, you streamed the oil too fast; see the rebuild note in the technique reference.'
    technique: 'egg-yolk-emulsion'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Whisk in the 30 g / ⅓ cup grated parmigiano and the 1 g / ½ tsp cracked black pepper. The cheese tightens the dressing further; if it now reads too thick to pour from a spoon, thin with a teaspoon of water at a time. Taste: it should land salty from anchovy and parmigiano, sharply acidic from lemon, with a low rumbling sweetness from the worcestershire. If a single axis falls short, add to that axis specifically: more lemon for brightness, more anchovy for depth, more parmigiano for body. Kale is sturdier and slightly more bitter than romaine, so push the lemon and salt a touch harder than you would for a romaine caesar.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Dress the kale. The massaged kale will have shed a little liquid in the bowl; tip that off so it does not dilute the dressing. Add about three-quarters of the dressing to the 400 g rested kale and toss with two large spoons or clean hands for a full 60 seconds, working the dressing into every ribbon. Add the rest only if the kale still reads under-coated. Massaged kale takes more dressing than romaine because the collapsed leaf has more surface for the emulsion to grip; coat generously.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the cooled sourdough crumbs and the 30 g / ¼ cup shaved parmigiano and toss 15 seconds more, just long enough to distribute. Transfer to a wide platter, scatter a few reserved crumbs and shavings over the top, and crack a final pass of black pepper across the surface. Serve within a few minutes of adding the crumbs; the kale itself holds beautifully, but the crumbs soften as they sit in the dressing, and their shatter is the whole reason they are crumbs.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why massage the kale. Raw lacinato is leathery and reads bitter on first chew because breaking the cell walls releases isothiocyanates onto the tongue. The salt-and-acid massage does three things at once: the salt pulls water from the cells osmotically so the leaf goes from stiff to pliant, the mechanical bruising collapses the cell walls and disperses the bitter compounds into the liquid that drains off, and the lemon lowers the pH to partially denature the myrosinase enzyme so less isothiocyanate is produced going forward. Ninety seconds of firm rubbing plus a 10-minute rest takes the kale from rubbery and aggressive to tender and only mildly green-bitter, and it drops the bowl volume by about half. Skipping the massage is the single most common failure of a home kale caesar; the result is a salad you have to chew like a leaf of cabbage.

  Crumbs, not croutons. The romaine caesar uses discrete cubed croutons because the crisp leaf already supplies crunch and the crouton is a textural accent. Kale is chewy rather than crisp, so the bread carries the crunch for the whole dish; tearing it into rough open-faced shards rather than cubes maximizes crisp surface area and lets the crumbs distribute through every forkful instead of being fished out one cube at a time. The ragged torn faces also crisp harder than clean knife-cut faces because more surface is exposed to the garlic oil and the oven.

  The dressing is unchanged from the classic. This is still a coddled-yolk anchovy emulsion, not a vinaigrette and not flavored mayonnaise. The yolk''s lecithin is a far more powerful emulsifier than dijon mucilage alone, which is what gives caesar dressing its characteristic clinging body. Against kale specifically, that body matters more than it does against romaine: the collapsed massaged leaf has more surface area to grip, so a thin dressing slides off and pools while a properly emulsified one coats every ribbon.

  The coddle is the safety phase, not a flavor phase. Ninety seconds at 88 °C / 190 °F holds the egg long enough to denature surface salmonella proteins (the FDA-recognized pasteurization curve is 60 °C / 140 °F for 3.5 minutes, or 70 °C / 158 °F for 15 seconds; the coddle sits between and is the kitchen-practical approach). It also lightly thickens the yolk into a firmer base for the emulsion. With genuinely fresh, low-bacterial-load eggs from a known producer, raw yolk works and is the original Cardini method; coddling is the Western-restaurant safety adaptation.

  Anchovy must dissolve. The most common caesar failure is leaving visible anchovy shreds in the finished dressing; cooks who think they hate caesar almost always hate this. Mince to a paste against the board with the side of a knife, smear it back and forth until uniform, then whisk into the warm yolks where the heat helps the proteins disperse. A fully dissolved fillet reads as deep glutamate savor with no fishy character; a chunk reads as a fishy hot spot.

  The umami-and-acid spine. The savory depth comes from a stacked glutamate-plus-nucleotide system: anchovy (free glutamate plus IMP nucleotides from cured fish protein), parmigiano (glutamate plus IMP from aged casein), and worcestershire (anchovy, tamarind, aged molasses). The Yamaguchi-Ninomiya synergy between glutamate and IMP multiplies perceived savory depth several-fold over the sum of the parts, which is why a caesar punches harder than its ingredient list suggests. The finishing acid is the lemon, carried in both the massage and the dressing; against kale''s slight bitterness, lean on it harder than you would for romaine.

  Make-ahead reality. Unlike a romaine caesar, this one tolerates a head start. The kale can be massaged and held in the fridge for a few hours, and dressed kale holds without wilting far better than dressed romaine. The only time-sensitive element is the crumbs, which soften once they sit in the dressing; bake them ahead, store airtight at room temperature, and fold them in only at the moment of serving.
