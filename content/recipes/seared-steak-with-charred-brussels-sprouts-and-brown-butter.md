safety_notes:
  - for: 'Pulling steak at 52 to 57 °C / 126 to 135 °F internal (below the USDA published-safe 145 °F for whole-muscle beef)'
    ref: 'safety/meat-doneness#beef-and-lamb-whole-muscle'
    why: 'The USDA published-safe target for whole-muscle beef is 145 °F (63 °C) followed by a 3-minute rest. The steakhouse canon pulls medium-rare steaks at 52 to 57 °C (126 to 135 °F) after a rest, and this is where the steak eats best. Whole-muscle beef from a reputable source carries far lower pathogen risk than ground beef because the bacterial load lives on the surface, which the hard sear pasteurizes, while the interior of an intact muscle from a healthy animal is essentially sterile. Cooks who want the published-safe number should pull at 145 °F, accepting drier meat. Ground, mechanically tenderized, or blade-tenderized cuts should always reach 71 °C / 160 °F since surface bacteria are mixed throughout.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Dry-brine the steaks the night before. Salt them on both sides at roughly 0.9% of their weight, set them on a rack over a tray, and refrigerate uncovered 12 to 24 hours. The surface must be dry to the touch before searing; this is what builds the crust and what separates a home steak from a steakhouse one.'
  - 'Pull the steaks out 45 minutes before cooking to temper. A fridge-cold interior overshoots the sear window, blackening the surface before the center reaches 54 °C.'
  - 'Halve the sprouts and have the garlic, lemon, and butter cut and staged before the pan goes on. Once the cast iron is smoking the whole sequence runs in under 15 minutes with no time to stop and prep.'
  - 'Run the kitchen fan on high. A smoking cast-iron pan plus brown butter fills the room with smoke within 90 seconds.'

steps:
  - text: 'The night before, pat both 225 g steaks bone-dry and lay them on a rack over a tray. Sprinkle the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt evenly across both sides of both steaks (roughly 0.9% of their weight) and refrigerate uncovered 12 to 24 hours. The salt seasons through the cross-section while the dry fridge air forms a tacky pellicle that sears fast.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 720
  - text: 'An hour before cooking, pull the steaks out to temper for 45 minutes and crack the 2 g / 1 tsp coarse black pepper onto both sides. Halve the 450 g / 1 lb sprouts, smash the 12 g / 3 garlic cloves, separate the 4 g / 4 thyme sprigs, and zest and juice half the lemon into a small bowl.'
    time_min: 45
  - text: 'Char the sprouts first. Set a heavy 30 cm / 12-inch cast-iron skillet over medium-high heat for 4 to 5 minutes until it just smokes. Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil, then lay the sprouts cut-face down in a single layer and season with the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. Leave them undisturbed 4 to 5 minutes until the cut faces are deep brown and charred at the edges. Toss once, cook another 2 minutes to soften the domes, then scrape the sprouts (and the loose charred leaves) onto a plate. They will finish in the brown butter later; right now they should be browned and just shy of fully tender.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Sear the steaks in the same pan. Crank the heat to high and let the pan come back to smoking, 1 to 2 minutes. The film of beef-free oil and any sprout fond left behind is fine. Lay the 2 steaks down away from you so the oil sprays back, press each flat with a spatula for 5 seconds, then leave them undisturbed 90 seconds. Flip to clean pan territory and sear the second side another 90 seconds. The crust should be deep mahogany before each flip.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Brown the butter and baste. Drop the 45 g / 3 tbsp cold cubed butter, the 12 g / 3 smashed garlic cloves, and the 4 g / 4 thyme sprigs into the pan around the steaks. The butter foams, then the milk solids turn amber and smell of toasted hazelnut within 60 to 90 seconds. Tilt the pan, spoon the foaming brown butter over the steaks repeatedly, and check the internal temperature; pull the steaks to a board at 50 to 52 °C / 122 to 126 °F (they climb 3 to 5 °C as they rest). Tent loosely, do not seal, and rest 5 to 7 minutes.'
    technique: 'brown-butter'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Finish the sprouts in the brown butter. Return the charred sprouts to the pan of brown butter, garlic, and thyme off or on low heat, and toss to coat and warm them through, 1 to 2 minutes. Squeeze in the juice of the half lemon and add its zest; the acid lifts the nutty butter and cuts the beef fat. Taste and add a pinch of kosher salt (about 1 g / ¼ tsp) if the sprouts read flat. Discard the spent thyme sprigs; squeeze the soft garlic out of its skins back into the sprouts or eat it as the cook''s treat.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Plate. Slice each rested steak across the grain into 1 cm slices and lay over a warmed plate. Spoon the brown-butter sprouts alongside and drizzle any remaining pan butter over both. Scatter the 2 g / ½ tsp flaky salt over the steak, add a lemon wedge, and serve immediately while the sprout leaves are still crisp at the edges.'
    time_min: 2

The low oven gives an even interior and the final sear builds crust without overshooting.'

notes: |
  Why one pan and one fat. The dish is engineered so that nothing is wasted and nothing is washed mid-cook. The sprouts char first in a little neutral oil and leave their fond behind; the steaks sear in that same seasoned pan; the brown butter built around the resting steaks becomes the finishing sauce for the sprouts. Each component picks up the Maillard flavor of the one before it, and the brown butter is the through-line that makes a plate of steak and a plate of vegetables read as one dish.

  Why brussels sprouts are the right side for steak. A hard-seared steak is rich and fatty, and the palate wants a foil. The classic foils are acid (a red-wine or mustard pan sauce) and bitterness. Charred brussels sprouts deliver both: the Maillard char carries a pleasant bitter edge, the brassica glucosinolates read faintly mustardy, and the lemon finish supplies the acid outright. The sprouts do the job a pan sauce would, which is why this plate needs no separate sauce.

  Brown butter, not plain butter. The 45 g of butter is browned deliberately, past the foam to amber milk solids, because the Maillard products in those toasted solids add a nutty savory depth that plain melted butter cannot. That depth is what lets the same butter season both the steak baste and the sprout finish without anything else added but garlic, thyme, and lemon. Use a light-colored stainless reaction in the cast iron is fine, watch the color through the foam and pull at the first hint of nuttiness, not at black.

  Timing the temperature. Pull the steaks at 50 to 52 °C internal; carryover during the rest climbs them into the 52 to 57 °C medium-rare window. The sprouts are forgiving by comparison: charred and just-tender off the first cook, fully tender after their turn in the brown butter. If the steaks need an extra minute, the sprouts hold happily on their plate; build the timing around the steak and let the sprouts wait.
