source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Korean galchi-gui canon, the salt pan-fried and grilled beltfish preparation that anchors the everyday Korean rice table.'
  note: 'Galchi-gui reads as plain fried fish but turns on two controls. First, a salt cure plus a thorough rest and pat-dry: the cure firms the famously delicate hairtail flesh through surface protein denaturation and draws off the surface water that would otherwise steam the skin instead of crisping it. Second, a single confident flip at moderate heat, because beltfish has short muscle blocks and almost no connective tissue and will shred into the pan under any second turn. The flour dredge is optional in the tradition; here it earns its place by building an even, shatter-prone shell on a fish whose own skin is thin enough to tear.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Salt the segments and rest them 15 to 20 minutes, then pat them bone-dry just before they hit the pan. Dry surface is the whole game: a wet hairtail steams, sticks, spatters, and tears instead of crisping, and the salt rest both firms the fragile flesh and pulls the surface water that prevents a golden skin. Do not skip the second pat-dry after the rest.'
  - 'Hairtail is fragile and must be flipped exactly once. The ingredient page is blunt about this: the flesh lifts off the central spine in clean flakes with almost no force, which means a tentative or repeated flip shreds the segment. Slide a thin spatula fully under each piece and turn it in one motion, then leave it alone.'
  - 'Start Korean short-grain rice FIRST as a parallel track. Galchi-gui is a rice-table fish, best eaten the moment it leaves the pan while the skin is crispest; the rice (a roughly 15 minute simmer plus 10 minute rest) should be finished and resting before the first segment goes into the oil.'

steps:
  - text: 'Prep the fish. The counter should have gutted the ribbon, trimmed the dorsal fin, and cross-cut it into 7 to 8 cm segments; verify and rinse each of the 450 g / about 1 fish worth of segments inside and out under cold water, scraping any dark matter from the spine cavity. Score one shallow slash into the thickest face of each segment so the salt and heat reach the spine and the flesh has somewhere to release tension instead of curling. Pat every segment completely dry, inside the cut and out.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Salt-cure. Rub the 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt evenly over both faces of all the segments, working it into the slashes. Rest the salted segments on a rack, uncovered, for 15 to 20 minutes. On hairtail the cure does double duty: it firms the delicate, low-collagen flesh through surface protein denaturation so the segments hold together through the flip, and it draws cell water into beads on the surface that you will blot away for a crisp skin. Meanwhile, with the rice already started, build the dip.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Mix the cho-ganjang dip. In a small bowl whisk the 30 g / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, the 15 g / 1 tbsp rice vinegar, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp water, then stir in the 15 g / 1 stalk minced scallion, the 4 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, and the 2 g / 1 tsp crushed sesame seeds. The soy is the glutamate anchor and the rice vinegar is the finishing acid; taste and adjust so the dip reads bright and salty against the mild fish. Set aside.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Dry and dredge. After the cure, pat the segments bone-dry a second time with paper towels, blotting the beaded moisture off the silver skin and out of the slashes; residual water is what makes a segment steam, stick, and tear. Spread the 30 g / ¼ cup all-purpose flour on a plate and press each segment into it on both faces for a thin, even coat, then lift and tap off all excess. The dredge should be a sheer dusting, not a crust; a heavy coat goes gummy in the pan.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Heat the oil. Film a nonstick or well-seasoned skillet with the 45 g / 3 tbsp neutral oil over medium heat until it shimmers and a pinch of flour dropped in sizzles steadily, about 170 to 180 °C. Moderate heat is the rule: too hot and the thin skin scorches and the flour burns before the flesh sets; too cool and the segments absorb oil and the skin goes greasy-soft. The oil should sizzle on contact but not smoke.'
    technique: 'shallow-fry'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Fry the first side. Lay the segments into the oil away from you, skin-side down, giving each room; work in two batches if the pan is crowded, since a crowded pan drops the oil temperature and steams the surface. Fry undisturbed for 4 to 5 minutes. Do not move or poke the fish; the surface needs uninterrupted contact to dehydrate, brown, and release on its own. The first side is done when the lower edge has gone opaque and golden and the segment slides freely when you nudge the pan.'
    technique: 'shallow-fry'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Flip once, gently. This is the only turn the fish gets. notes: |
  The cure is the key step. Hairtail is the most delicate of the rice-table fish: short muscle blocks, almost no connective tissue, flesh that lifts off the spine in soft sheets. Left untreated it weeps water and falls apart in the pan. The salt cure fixes both problems at once. Salt draws cell water to the surface during the 15 to 20 minute rest, where you blot it away, and the same osmotic action firms the surface proteins so the segment holds its shape through frying and the single flip. The two pat-dry passes, before the cure and after, are the most important non-obvious moves in the recipe; a segment that goes into the oil wet will steam, stick, and shred.

  The one-flip rule. Because the binding collagen is sparse, hairtail flesh has no structural margin for handling. The technique is to lay each segment down skin-side first, leave it completely alone until the surface has dehydrated and browned enough to release on its own, then turn it exactly once with a thin spatula slid all the way under the piece. A second flip, or a tentative flip that catches only half the segment, tears the ribbon into the pan. This is the same fragility that forbids stirring in the braised galchi-jorim version of the fish; here it dictates a patient first side and a single confident turn.

  Pan-fried or grilled. Galchi-gui spans both a shallow-fry and a salt-grill in the Korean tradition, and the dish is the same idea executed in two heat environments. The pan-fried version (this recipe) uses a light flour dredge to build an even, shatter-prone shell and crisps the whole surface in oil; the grilled version (see the modification) skips the flour, blisters the skin under direct heat, and reads smokier and drier. Both depend on the same cure-and-dry foundation, and both flip only once.

  Umami and acid. The glutamate source is the soy sauce in the cho-ganjang dip, which seasons the mild, clean-flavored hairtail flesh from the outside and supplies the savory depth the lean fish is too delicate to carry on its own. The finishing acid arrives twice: the rice vinegar whisked into the dip, which cuts the fried richness on every bite, and the lemon wedge at the table for a brighter, sharper second hit. A fried fish without an acid counter reads heavy and flat; this one is built to stay bright against the rice.

  Temperature discipline over heat. The instinct with fried fish is to crank the burner, but the thin hairtail skin and the flour dredge both scorch well before a hot pan would set the interior. Moderate heat, around 170 to 180 °C, crisps the skin in step with the lean flesh cooking to the spine. If the skin is browning faster than the flesh is setting, the oil is too hot; pull the pan off the heat for a few seconds rather than fighting it. The thin segments cook through quickly, so the real risk at the end is pushing past moist into dry; pull the moment the flesh at the bone turns opaque.

  Sourcing the fish. Hairtail (galchi) is a counter and ice-bed fish, sold at H Mart, Hannam, 99 Ranch, and most Korean, Chinese, and Japanese grocers, very often previously frozen since the guanine-coated skin oxidizes fast. Judge it by the silver: a good specimen has a bright, even, reflective metallic coat with no yellow or rust-brown patches, firm flesh that springs back, and a clean marine smell. Yellowing of the coat, a dull chalky surface, or any sour or ammoniated note means the surface lipids have oxidized and the fish is past its window. Have the counter gut it, trim the dorsal fin, and cross-cut it into 7 to 8 cm segments; doing it at home with a meter-long slippery ribbon is a chore. Pat the segments very dry before salting.

  Gluten-free path. Swap the all-purpose flour for rice flour or potato starch and use a tamari or certified gluten-free soy in the dip; the segments fry up if anything crisper, and the dish loses nothing.
