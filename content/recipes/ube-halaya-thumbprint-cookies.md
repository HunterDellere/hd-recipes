before_you_start:
  - 'The brown butter has to be chilled back to a soft, plastic solid before you cream it, which takes about 45 minutes in the fridge. Brown it first thing, then mise everything else while it cools. Liquid brown butter will not cream with the sugar and gives you a greasy dough that spreads instead of holding a thumb well.'
  - 'Hold back a third of the ube halaya cold for after the bake. Anthocyanin fades 15 to 20% under oven heat, so the jam baked into the well dulls; a fresh cold spoonful dropped into each cookie once cooled restores the vivid violet that makes the cookie read as ube rather than generic jam.'
  - 'Use muted-violet jarred halaya (Good Shepherd) rather than a fluorescent-purple, dye-loaded jar. In a thumbprint the jam sits exposed in the center, so its honest color is on full display.'

notes: |
  Why a thumbprint is the native home for halaya. Most thumbprint fillings are fruit jams chosen for set and shine. Ube halaya is already a cooked, spreadable, glossy jam, so it drops into the well with no adaptation. The only design problem is heat fade, which is solved by the two-stage fill: a baked layer that anchors and sets into the cookie, and a cold layer added after for the vivid violet and soft, fresh texture.

  Why brown butter has to be solidified first. A thumbprint relies on creaming to whip air into the fat so the cookie holds a defined well instead of melting into a puddle. Liquid brown butter cannot trap that air. Chilling it back to a soft plastic solid restores the creamable state while keeping all the toasted milk solids, so you get both the hazelnut depth and the structural lift.

  Why egg yolk and cornstarch, no white. The white is mostly water and protein; it would toughen the crumb and add moisture this dough does not want. The yolk alone binds with fat and emulsifiers and keeps the texture short. Cornstarch lowers the effective gluten-forming protein further, which is what gives shortbread its clean sandy fracture rather than a chew.

  Why coconut on the edge. Coconut and ube are a canonical Filipino pairing (think ube-macapuno). Rolling the raw dough through desiccated coconut puts it where it toasts in the oven, adding aroma and a textural rim that frames the soft violet center.
