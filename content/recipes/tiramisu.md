source:
  name: 'Veneto / Friuli canon (Treviso, 1960s-1970s)'
  note: 'The modern tiramisu is firmly Venetian; the dish was codified at Le Beccherie restaurant in Treviso in the early 1970s (chef Roberto Linguanotto), and the structural elements have been remarkably stable since: savoiardi (dry crisp Italian ladyfingers, not American soft sponge fingers), strong espresso (not coffee, not instant), mascarpone (the Lombard double-cream cheese), egg yolks worked into a sabayon with sugar, optional egg whites whipped to a meringue for additional lightness, and a finishing dust of bitter unsweetened cocoa. Marsala or amaretto is the canonical alcohol addition for the dipping liquid; modern adaptations sometimes use rum or coffee liqueur. The classical version uses raw egg yolks folded into the mascarpone; the version here uses the safer pasteurized hot-sabayon technique (egg yolks beaten with sugar over a bain-marie to 75°C / 167°F, which inactivates Salmonella while preserving the emulsification properties of the yolk lecithin).'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 4-hour chill minimum, ideally overnight. The dessert must cure: the sponge must absorb the cream, the cream must firm up to a sliceable texture, and the flavors must integrate. A tiramisu pulled at 4 hours is good; one rested overnight (12 hours) is structurally complete.'
  - 'Brew the espresso first and let it cool to room temperature before assembly; hot espresso melts the cream the moment the soaked savoiardi hit it. About 350 ml of brewed espresso is needed; a strong moka pot, an espresso machine, or a 1:6 immersion brew at coarse grind all work.'
  - 'Have all components staged (mascarpone at room temperature, eggs separated, sugar weighed, savoiardi in a dish, cocoa in a fine-mesh sieve) before starting the sabayon. The hot-sabayon technique is time-sensitive and cannot be paused.'

steps:
  - text: 'Brew the 350 g / 1½ cups espresso in advance and cool to room temperature in a wide shallow bowl (the bowl is the dipping vessel; about 22 cm / 9 in diameter is right). Whisk in the 15 g / 1 tbsp sugar until dissolved, then the 45 g / 3 tbsp Marsala. Set aside at room temperature.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Separate the 5 egg yolks (90 g) into a large heatproof mixing bowl that fits over a saucepan (the bain-marie). Separate the 3 egg whites (90 g) into a separate clean grease-free bowl. Weigh the 100 g / ½ cup sugar into two portions: 70 g for the yolks, 30 g for the whites.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Set up a bain-marie: fill a saucepan with 5 cm / 2 in of water, bring to a bare simmer. Set the bowl of egg yolks over the saucepan (the bowl bottom should not touch the water). Add the 70 g sugar to the yolks. Whisk vigorously and continuously for 5 to 7 minutes; the mixture transforms from streaky and grainy to pale, glossy, thick, and ribbon-forming (when the whisk is lifted, the mixture falls back in a thick ribbon that holds its shape for 2 to 3 seconds before sinking). Hit an instant-read thermometer to the mixture; pull at 75°C / 167°F (this temperature, held for the time it took to whisk to thickness, pasteurizes the eggs against Salmonella while preserving the lecithin emulsification capacity).'
    technique: 'tempering-eggs'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Remove the bowl from the bain-marie; set on a kitchen towel. Continue whisking 2 minutes off heat to cool slightly (down to about 45°C / 113°F). Add the 500 g / 2 cups room-temperature mascarpone in two additions. Whisk the first half in gently for 30 seconds until uniform; add the second half, whisk for 30 more seconds. Add the 1 g / ¼ tsp salt. The cream should be smooth, glossy, and just-thick enough to hold a soft peak. Cold mascarpone added to the warm sabayon causes the dairy to seize into lumps; if the mascarpone was not at room temperature and lumps form, smooth with an immersion blender for 10 seconds.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'In the separate bowl with the 90 g egg whites, whip with an electric mixer or balloon whisk on medium speed until soft peaks form (about 90 seconds); add the 30 g sugar gradually while whipping. Continue whipping on medium-high to firm glossy peaks (the meringue should hold a stiff peak when the beater is lifted but the peak tip just slightly curls over; do not whip to dry stiff peaks). Total time: about 4 minutes from start.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Fold the meringue into the mascarpone-sabayon in three additions, using a rubber spatula and a gentle cutting motion (down the center, across the bottom, up the side, rotate the bowl a quarter turn). The first addition lightens the base; the second and third preserve volume. Fold until just-uniform with no visible streaks of white but maximum residual airy structure. The cream is now complete.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Assembly: dip each savoiardo in the espresso-Marsala mixture for ONE second per side (a literal one-second count; the dry biscuit absorbs aggressively, and a 3-second dip turns it to mush). Lay the dipped savoiardi in a single tight layer in the bottom of a 22 by 22 cm / 9 by 9 in baking dish (about 15 savoiardi per layer, broken to fit corners). Spread half the cream over the savoiardi layer in an even thick layer (about 2.5 cm / 1 in thick). Repeat: second layer of dipped savoiardi, second layer of cream. Smooth the top with the back of an offset spatula.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Cover the dish tightly with a sheet of parchment laid directly on the cream surface (the parchment prevents the cocoa from absorbing fridge moisture and clumping, and prevents a skin from forming on the cream). Refrigerate at least 4 hours, ideally 12 hours (overnight). The chill cures the dessert: the sponge fully absorbs the cream layer above and the espresso below, the cream firms to a sliceable but creamy texture, and the flavors integrate into a single cohesive whole.'
    time_min: 240
  - text: 'Just before serving: remove the parchment. Sift the 20 g / 3 tbsp cocoa powder over the top in an even thick layer through a fine-mesh sieve (cover the entire surface so no white shows through; a generous cocoa dust is structural, not a sprinkle). Optionally finish with the 20 g / 2 tbsp grated dark chocolate. Cut into 8 portions with a sharp knife wiped clean between cuts. Serve cold straight from the refrigerator; the dessert should be eaten within 30 minutes of the cocoa dusting, or the cocoa absorbs moisture and forms a dark paste.'
    time_min: 2

safety_notes:
  - for: 'Eggs cooked to 75°C / 167°F via the hot-sabayon technique (pasteurized) rather than left raw'
    ref: 'safety/meat-doneness#poultry'
    why: 'Classical tiramisu uses raw egg yolks folded into the mascarpone; the modern food-safety standard is to use the hot-sabayon technique (egg yolks whisked with sugar over a bain-marie to 75°C / 167°F), which inactivates Salmonella while preserving the emulsification properties of the lecithin. The pasteurization is structurally invisible in the finished dessert; the texture and flavor are indistinguishable from the raw-yolk version. Vulnerable diners (pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, very old) should only eat tiramisu made with the hot-sabayon technique; the raw-egg version is not appropriate for them. The whipped egg whites also carry Salmonella risk; if serving to vulnerable diners, omit the egg whites (or use pasteurized liquid egg whites from a carton) and substitute 200 g of cold heavy cream whipped to firm peaks for additional volume.'

notes: |
  The one-second dip is the single most important technique in tiramisu, and the single most common failure mode. Savoiardi are intensely dry, brittle biscuits engineered to absorb liquid aggressively; a literal one-second per-side dip wets the surface to the right damp-but-still-cohesive state. A two- or three-second dip oversaturates the biscuit, which collapses to mush in the assembled dessert and produces a tiramisu that is structurally a coffee-soaked-pudding rather than a layered cake. Cooks new to the dish over-dip out of insecurity; commit to the one-second count.

  The hot sabayon serves two functions: pasteurization (75°C / 167°F inactivates Salmonella in the yolks) and emulsification (the heated and whipped yolks form a much more stable cream than raw yolks alone). The traditional Italian tiramisu uses raw yolks; the modern technique uses the hot sabayon and the textural result is identical or slightly better. The ribbon stage (where the lifted whisk drops mixture in a slowly-sinking thick ribbon) is the visual cue for sufficient cook; the thermometer is the safety check. Pull at 75°C and not above (overcooked yolks scramble at 82°C and the mixture forms grainy lumps that cannot be re-emulsified).

  Mascarpone temperature is structural. The 18 to 20°C / 65 to 68°F room temperature lets the cheese fold smoothly into the warm sabayon without lumping; refrigerator-cold mascarpone (around 4°C) seizes on contact with the warm yolks and forms hard cheese clumps suspended in the cream. Pull the mascarpone from the refrigerator 60 to 90 minutes before assembly. If the kitchen is cold (below 18°C), bring the mascarpone to room temperature over a 30-second bain-marie before folding.

  The 4-hour minimum chill is non-negotiable, and overnight is meaningfully better. The chill cures the dessert from three separate components (soaked sponge, cream, cocoa) into a single cohesive structure where the sponge has absorbed the cream above and the espresso below, the cream has firmed to a sliceable but soft texture, and the flavors have integrated. A tiramisu pulled at 2 hours is structurally a layered mess; the same dessert at 12 hours is one of the great desserts of European cooking.

  Storage. Keeps 3 days refrigerated, covered tightly with parchment (not plastic, which traps condensation and makes the cocoa soggy). The dessert improves over the first 24 hours as the cream and espresso integrate, then begins to soften noticeably on day 3. Does not freeze; the dairy in the mascarpone develops a grainy texture on thaw.
