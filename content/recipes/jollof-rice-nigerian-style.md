source:
  note: 'The Nigerian-Ghanaian-Senegalese "jollof wars" reflect real regional differences. The dish traces to Senegalese thieboudienne (Wolof, ceebu jen), the original fish-and-rice one-pot from which all jollof descends. The Ghanaian version uses jasmine rice, runs slightly sweeter and milder, and often includes basil. The Nigerian version (this recipe) uses long-grain parboiled rice, runs bolder and hotter with more scotch bonnet, includes a curry powder and thyme aromatic, and prizes the caramelized bottom-of-pot crust (called "party jollof" when cooked over wood fire). All three are legitimate; this recipe commits to the Nigerian style because it is the version that puts the rice front-and-center as the structural element, with the obe ata pepper base cooked down until the oil splits out before the rice goes in.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The obe ata pepper base is made separately and cooked down for 25 to 30 minutes before the rice goes in. Plan the timeline: blend the peppers first, parboil the rice second, cook down the obe ata third, then combine. The base cannot be skipped or shortened; the entire dish lives on the depth of the reduced pepper-tomato sauce.'
  - 'Use long-grain parboiled rice, not jasmine, basmati, or American long-grain. The parboiled grain (sometimes labeled "easy cook" or "converted") has been steamed in the husk before milling and resists overcooking, which is what allows the long 30-minute simmer in the pepper base to develop the bottom caramelization without turning the rice to mush.'
  - 'Source real scotch bonnet (atarodo) or habanero; bell pepper alone makes a different dish. The fruity-tropical heat of C. chinense is structural.'
  - 'The bottom-of-pot caramelization (the prized brown crust) only develops if you commit to the final 10-minute medium-low phase without stirring. Lifting the lid during this phase releases steam and the caramelization stalls.'

steps:
  - text: 'Bring the 2000 ml / 8 cups water to a boil in a large pot with the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt. Add the rinsed 500 g / 2½ cups parboiled rice and boil for exactly 4 minutes (longer for non-parboiled rice), stirring once. Drain in a colander, rinse briefly under cold water to stop the cook, and set aside. The rice is now half-cooked; it will finish in the pepper base for another 25 to 30 minutes. This parboil is the Nigerian-method signature; it lets the rice absorb the pepper base flavor without going mushy.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'In a high-powered blender, combine the 400 g / 3 large red bell peppers, the 400 g / 4 to 5 roma tomatoes, the 12 g / 2 to 3 scotch bonnets, and 150 g / half of the 1 large red onion. Blend on high for 60 to 90 seconds to a completely smooth bright-red puree, scraping down sides as needed. This is the obe ata, the structural base of Nigerian cooking. The puree should be thick but pourable; if it is too thick to blend smoothly, add 2 to 3 tablespoons of water. Set aside.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'In a heavy 5 to 6 quart Dutch oven over medium heat, warm the 90 g / 6 tbsp neutral oil for 1 minute. Add the 150 g / other half of the diced red onion and cook 6 to 8 minutes, stirring every minute, until the onion is deep golden brown and soft. This is the second flavor layer below the pepper blend.'
    technique: 'dry-sweat'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Push the onions to one side of the pot and add the 60 g / 4 tbsp tomato paste to the empty side. Fry the tomato paste in the oil for 3 to 4 minutes, stirring constantly with a wooden spoon, until it darkens from bright red to brick red and the oil takes on its color. The fry concentrates the paste''s glutamates and shifts the flavor from raw-tomato to roasted-tomato. Stir the onions back through.'
    technique: 'bloom-spice'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add the 15 g / 4 cloves microplaned garlic, the 10 g / 2 cm knob microplaned ginger, the 5 g / 2 tsp curry powder, the 3 g / 1 tsp fresh thyme, and the 2 bay leaves. Cook 1 minute, stirring, until fragrant.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour the blended obe ata pepper puree into the pot and stir to combine. Bring to a vigorous simmer over medium heat, then drop to medium-low and cook uncovered for 25 to 30 minutes, stirring every 5 minutes. The puree starts watery and bright red; over the cook, the water boils off and the sauce thickens and darkens to a deep brick-red. The signal for done is when the oil visibly splits out at the edges and the sauce is the consistency of thick pizza sauce. This long reduction is non-negotiable; skipping it produces thin watery jollof with no depth.'
    technique: 'pepper-reduction'
    time_min: 28
  - text: 'Add the 4 g / 1 Maggi cube (crumbled in) and the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt. Stir to dissolve. Taste the base; it should read deeply spiced, bold, slightly hot from the scotch bonnet. Add more salt if flat.'

  - text: 'Add the parboiled 500 g / 2½ cups rice to the reduced pepper base and stir to coat every grain in the brick-red sauce. notes: |
  The parboiled rice. Parboiled long-grain rice (sometimes labeled "easy cook" or "converted") has been steamed in the husk before milling, which gelatinizes the surface starch and makes the cooked grain firmer and less prone to falling apart in a long simmer. The Nigerian jollof method depends on this property; the 4-minute parboil plus the 30-minute steam in the pepper base would turn jasmine or basmati into mush, but parboiled rice holds its grain integrity through both phases.

  Why fry the tomato paste. Frying tomato paste in oil before adding the blended base is a structural West African move that shows up across jollof, stews, and many soups. The fry concentrates the paste''s glutamates, darkens the color to brick-red, and shifts the flavor from raw-tomato to roasted-tomato. Skipping it leaves the dish with a slightly raw-tomato edge; doing it adds a deeper background that reads through the finished rice.

  Why the obe ata reduction matters. The blended pepper-tomato-onion puree starts watery and bright red. Over 25 to 30 minutes of uncovered simmer, the water boils off and the sauce thickens and darkens until the oil visibly splits out at the edges. This reduction concentrates everything; it is what produces deep flavor in the finished jollof. Cooks who skip or shorten it produce thin watery jollof; the long cook is non-negotiable.

  The party jollof crust. The bottom-of-pot caramelization is prized in Nigerian and Ghanaian cooking, called the bottom or the base. It develops in the final medium-low covered phase as the rice releases starch onto the pot bottom and that starch slowly caramelizes. The crust pieces are the most flavorful part of the dish and traditionally get scraped up and mixed through the fluffier top rice. The smell will go from rice-and-pepper to faintly burnt-caramel; pull from heat at the burnt-caramel stage, before aggressive scorching.

  Make-ahead and reheat. Jollof improves the next day. The flavors continue to integrate and the rice firms up. Reheat covered with a splash of water in a 150 °C oven for 20 minutes, or in a covered pot over low heat for 15 minutes. Holds 4 days refrigerated, 2 months frozen.
