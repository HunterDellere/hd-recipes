  note: 'Żurek (full name "żur") is the structural Polish sour rye soup, served traditionally on Easter Sunday morning (the meal that breaks the 40-day Lent fast) and as a year-round Polish home dish. The defining elements are: zakwas, the lacto-fermented rye liquid (rye flour, garlic, cold water, 4 to 5 days fermentation); smoked Polish sausage (biała kielbasa, the unsmoked white sausage, is the Easter canonical; wiejska or other smoked kielbasa is the year-round substitute); bacon and onion for the structural soup base; marjoram (the structural Polish soup herb); a halved hard-boiled egg per bowl; and dark rye bread on the side. before_you_start:
  - 'The zakwas (rye sour starter) needs 4 to 5 days of room-temperature fermentation. Plan a week ahead. The starter is straightforward (rye flour, garlic, cold water, room temperature) but cannot be rushed; the lacto-fermentation produces the structural tang that defines żurek. Bottled żurek starter is sold at Polish groceries (Krakus or Smak brand) if you cannot wait the week.'
  - 'The biała kiełbasa (white sausage) is the Easter canonical preparation; it must be poached gently (not boiled, which bursts the casings) for 25 minutes before being added to the soup. Outside Easter, smoked wiejska kielbasa (the everyday Polish smoked sausage) can be sliced and added directly without pre-poaching. Both versions are structurally correct żurek.'

steps:
  - text: '4 to 5 days ahead, make the zakwas. In a clean 1 L glass jar, combine the 100 g rye flour, 800 ml cold filtered water, 15 g smashed garlic, and 1 bay leaf. Stir vigorously until no flour lumps remain. Cover with a piece of cheesecloth or a loose lid (the ferment needs air exchange). Let stand at room temperature (20 to 22 °C / 68 to 72 °F) for 4 to 5 days. Stir once daily. The mixture will separate into a milky liquid on top and a paste at the bottom; this is normal. By day 4 or 5 the zakwas should smell aggressively sour-tangy, fizzy, like sourdough starter; taste a drop, it should pucker the mouth.'
    time_min: 7200
  - text: 'Day of soup: stir the zakwas vigorously to recombine the liquid and paste. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a measuring cup, pressing the rye paste to extract liquid; discard the rye solids and the garlic and bay. You should have about 600 ml of strained zakwas; reserve.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Pre-cook the biała kiełbasa (Easter version only). Place the 400 g fresh white sausage links in a wide saucepan; cover with cold water. Bring to 75 to 80 °C / 165 to 175 °F (a bare simmer, single bubbles rising lazily); poach 25 minutes. Lift out with tongs; cool slightly. Slice into 1.5 cm rounds. Reserve. Skip this step if using pre-smoked wiejska kielbasa.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'In a large pot or Dutch oven over medium-high heat, render the 120 g diced bacon for 4 to 5 minutes until crispy with rendered fat. Add the 200 g diced onion; sauté 4 to 5 minutes until softened. Add the 200 g sliced mushrooms; cook 6 minutes until the mushrooms have released their water and started to brown.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Add the 15 g / 4 cloves minced garlic and 5 g / 1 tbsp marjoram; cook 60 seconds until fragrant.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour in the 1200 ml / 5 cups stock, the 600 ml zakwas, the 2 bay leaves, and the 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper. Bring to a steady simmer; reduce heat to medium-low. Simmer 15 minutes for the flavors to integrate.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Add the sliced sausage (the poached biała kiełbasa, or the wiejska smoked kielbasa); simmer 5 more minutes to warm through.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Temper the sour cream. Ladle about 200 ml of the hot soup into a small bowl with the 80 g sour cream; whisk smooth to bring the cream up to soup temperature (adding cold sour cream directly to hot soup causes it to curdle into white specks). Pour the tempered cream back into the soup; stir gently to integrate. Do not let the soup boil after adding the cream; it can still curdle if cranked.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Taste; adjust salt (the bacon and sausage contribute most of the salt; rarely needs more). Fish out the bay leaves.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Ladle the soup into wide bowls. Top each bowl with ½ a hard-boiled egg (cut-side up) and a sprinkle of fresh marjoram or parsley. Serve with thick slices of dark rye bread on the side for sopping. The egg on top is the structural Easter signal; many Polish homes serve the soup in a hollowed-out round bread loaf as the bowl, with the soup ladled inside.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why the zakwas. The structural defining element of żurek is the rye sour starter; without it the soup is just a smoky bacon-and-sausage broth. The 4 to 5 day room-temperature ferment of rye flour with garlic and cold water cultivates wild lactobacillus (native to the rye grain) that produces lactic acid, slight CO2, and the complex sour-tangy flavor profile that is structurally Polish. Polish homes traditionally have a jar of zakwas in the fridge year-round, refreshed with new flour and water as it depletes.

  Why marjoram. Marjoram (Origanum majorana, sweeter and milder than oregano) is the structural Polish soup herb; it appears in żurek, in flaki (tripe soup), in mushroom soups, and in many Polish meat dishes. The flavor is gentler and more floral than oregano; substituting oregano produces a soup that reads off (more Italian than Polish).

  Why temper the sour cream. Polish soups are finished with sour cream for body and to round the acidity. Adding cold sour cream directly to hot soup curdles it into white specks; tempering (whisking some hot soup into the cream first to bring its temperature up gradually) prevents the curdle. This is the universal rule for cream-finished European soups.

  The Easter egg on top. The halved hard-boiled egg is the structural Easter symbol (egg = rebirth, resurrection) and the structural finish for żurek served on Easter Sunday morning. The egg is also genuinely delicious in the soup (richness on top of sour-smoky depth). Year-round żurek often skips the egg; Easter żurek never does.

  Pairings. Canonical with dark rye bread (the seedy Polish razowy chleb), or in a bowl made from a hollowed-out round loaf of bread (a Polish presentation flourish at Easter). A glass of cold vodka (Żubrówka or Belvedere) on the side for the holiday version.

  Storage. Keeps 4 days refrigerated; the soup tastes better on day 2 as the flavors integrate. Reheat gently over low heat (do not boil after the cream is added). Freezes well; store without the sour cream (add fresh tempered cream to each reheated portion).
