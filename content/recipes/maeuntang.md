source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Korean maeuntang canon'
  note: 'Maeuntang (매운탕, literally "spicy soup") is the Korean spicy fish stew built on a whole fish broken down to sections, head and spine included. It is the canonical fate of a fresh whole fish in Korean home cooking once the prime fillet has been claimed for hoe (raw) or gui (grilled), and it is engineered around the carcass: the collagen-dense head and frame are exactly what give the broth its gelatinous body, so the stew is also the value play on a whole fish. The seasoning is a yangnyeom of gochugaru, gochujang, doenjang, garlic, and ginger; the umami spine is anchovy-kelp stock plus doenjang plus the fish bones; the brightness comes from chili, scallion, and a final crown of crown daisy (ssukgat) or watercress. Radish simmered to sweet translucence is the structural vegetable.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Make the anchovy-kelp stock FIRST, or have a substitute ready. The stock is a 25-minute task (kelp steeped cold then warmed, anchovies simmered 10 minutes, both strained) and the entire stew is built on it. Start it before you touch the fish. If you are short on time, a quick dashi or even lightly salted water plus extra doenjang will carry, but the broth loses depth.'
  - 'Start short-grain rice in parallel. Maeuntang is eaten as a shared pot with individual bowls of rice, the broth spooned over. Time the rice (about 12-minute simmer plus 10-minute rest) so it is finished when the stew is, since the fish should be eaten the moment it is poached through.'
  - 'The radish simmers BEFORE the fish goes in. This is the one piece of timing that cannot be collapsed: the radish needs 12 to 15 minutes in the seasoned broth to turn sweet and translucent, while the delicate fish needs only 6 to 10 minutes and goes in last. Adding them together gives you crunchy radish and overcooked, disintegrating fish. Read steps 4 through 6 before you start.'

steps:
  - text: 'Build the anchovy-kelp stock first. Put the 10 g / one 10 cm piece kombu in a pot with the 1400 ml / 6 cups water and let it steep 15 minutes off heat (a cold steep extracts the kelp glutamate without the slimy alginates a hard boil would draw out). Pinch the heads and black guts off the 25 g / 12 to 15 dried anchovies and add the cleaned bodies. Bring to a gentle simmer over medium heat; the moment it simmers, lift out the kombu (boiled kelp turns the stock bitter and slimy). Simmer the anchovies 10 minutes, then strain out and discard the anchovies. You should have about 1.2 L of clean, savory stock. Set it aside.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Prep and pre-salt the fish. If the counter did not section it, scale, gut, and pull the gills, then scrape the dark blood line from the inside of the spine cavity with a spoon (the blood line is the single biggest source of fishy flavor). Rinse and cut the whole fish crosswise into 4 cm sections through the bone with a heavy knife, splitting the head in half; keep the head and spine, they are the gelatin. Pat the pieces dry and salt them with the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt. Rest 10 minutes; the brief salt firms the surface and draws out a little surface water, which keeps the flesh from clouding the broth later.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Mix the seasoning paste (yangnyeom). In a small bowl stir together the 18 g / 3 tbsp gochugaru, the 30 g / 1½ tbsp gochujang, the 20 g / 1 tbsp doenjang, the 18 g / 5 to 6 cloves minced garlic, and the 8 g / 1 tbsp minced ginger into a loose paste. Letting it sit while the radish cooks lets the gochugaru hydrate and bloom its color into the fats already in the paste.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Start the radish in the seasoned broth. Bring the 1.2 L anchovy-kelp stock to a simmer in a wide, shallow pot. Whisk in the full seasoning paste (the 18 g gochugaru, 30 g gochujang, 20 g doenjang, 18 g garlic, 8 g ginger) until the broth turns a deep, even red. Add the 300 g / 2.5 cm-cubed Korean radish. Simmer 12 to 15 minutes, uncovered, until the radish is translucent at the edges and tender to a skewer; this is also where the doenjang and gochugaru lose their raw edge and the broth deepens. Taste: it should read savory, gently spicy, and faintly sweet from the radish. Adjust salt only with a little more doenjang, not table salt.'
    technique: 'doenjang-broth'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Poach the fish. Slide the salted fish sections into the simmering broth, head pieces and spine first since they take longest, then the body sections. Tuck the 200 g / 2 cm-slab firm tofu in around the fish. Keep the pot at a steady simmer, not a hard boil (a violent boil shreds the lean flake and clouds the broth). Do not stir; nudge gently if needed. Cook 6 to 10 minutes by the thickness of the sections, until the flesh is opaque and just pulls from the bone at the thickest piece (an internal 55 to 58 °C). Skim any gray foam that rises.'
    technique: 'gentle-poach'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Add the aromatics and fresh heat. Scatter in the 30 g / 2 sliced green chili and the 40 g / 3 to 4 stalk scallion batons. Simmer 2 minutes more; the chili and scallion go in late so they keep their fresh, bright bite and lift the deep fermented base rather than stewing into it. Taste the broth one last time and correct: a little more gochugaru for color and warmth, a touch more doenjang for salt and depth.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish off heat with the herbal crown. Pull the pot off the heat. Pile the 50 g / 2 handfuls crown daisy (or watercress) over the top and, if using, drizzle the 4 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil over the surface. The residual heat wilts the greens in about 30 seconds while keeping their grassy, slightly bitter aroma intact; that raw herbal brightness against the fiery, gelatin-rich broth is the finishing register of the dish. Bring the pot to the table and serve immediately, ladling broth, fish, tofu, and radish into bowls over the 600 g / 3 cups steamed short-grain rice.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why the whole carcass, not fillets. Maeuntang is engineered around the parts most cooks throw away. The head and spine of a black sea bass are dense in collagen; under the 15-to-25-minute simmer that collagen hydrolyzes to gelatin, and the gelatin is what gives the broth its body, the faint set you see when the leftovers chill. Build the same stew on lean skinless fillet and the broth comes out thin and watery no matter how much paste you add. Sectioning a whole fish through the bone and including the head is therefore both the flavor play and the value play: you pay for one fish and the cheapest part does the most work.

  The umami architecture. The savory depth comes from three stacked glutamate sources: the anchovy-kelp stock (inosinate from the dried anchovies, glutamate from the kombu, the two synergize for an umami far greater than either alone), the doenjang (a fermented-soybean glutamate paste, and the ingredient that specifically tames the fishy notes), and the fish bones themselves (free glutamate and gelatin released into the simmer). The gochujang adds a fourth, fermented-and-sweet layer plus body. This is why the stew tastes complete without any meat stock; the umami is built from the sea and the ferment, not from chicken or beef.

  The brightness and finishing register. A stew this deep and fiery needs a counter, and maeuntang supplies it at the end rather than the start: the late-added fresh green chili and scallion bring sharp, volatile heat and allium lift that have not had time to stew flat, and the off-heat crown of crown daisy or watercress lands a raw, grassy, faintly bitter top note over the rich broth. Some households also finish with a small splash of acid, a squeeze of lemon or a few drops of rice vinegar at the table, which cuts the richness further; it is optional and not canonical but it works the same way the late chili does, resetting the palate between spoonfuls.

  Why radish first, fish last. The radish needs 12 to 15 minutes to turn sweet and translucent and to give its sweetness up to the broth, and that long simmer is also where the gochugaru and doenjang shed their raw edge. The fish needs only 6 to 10 minutes and is fragile. Cook them together and you get crunchy radish and shredded, overcooked fish. The radius of error on the fish is small: pull it the moment the flesh turns opaque and lifts from the bone, around 55 to 58 °C, and hold the pot at a simmer rather than a hard boil, since a rolling boil tears the lean flake apart and clouds the broth with expelled protein.

  Sourcing and handling the fish. Buy the fish whole and as fresh as possible, on the ice bed at H Mart or a coastal counter: clear domed eyes, bright red gills, tight scales, firm flesh, clean ocean smell. Ask them to scale and gut it but keep the head and frame on. At home, scrape the blood line from the spine cavity and rinse well; the blood line and any gut remnants are the main sources of off-fishiness, and a clean cavity plus the doenjang and ginger in the broth give a stew that reads deep and savory rather than fishy. A 700 to 900 g fish is right for 3 to 4 people; scale the radish and tofu with the pot.

  Serving and leftovers. Maeuntang is a shared-pot dish: it comes to the table in the cooking pot, often kept at a low flame, and each diner ladles broth, a piece of fish, tofu, and radish into a bowl and eats it over or alongside a personal bowl of short-grain rice. Eating around the bones is expected; the cheek and collar meat are prized. Leftovers keep 2 days refrigerated and the broth sets to a soft gel from the carcass gelatin; reheat gently to a simmer, never a hard boil, and refresh with a little fresh scallion and chili.
