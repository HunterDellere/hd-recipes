The dish is the structural Haitian pivot from the Creole tomato-sofrito tradition: where Cuban arroz con pollo cooks the chicken and the rice together, the Haitian preparation keeps them separate (chicken in a saucy braise, rice on the side), and the Scotch bonnet rides whole on top for fragrance.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the epis. Combine all of Phase 1 (30 g parsley, 80 g scallions, 25 g garlic, 80 g green pepper, 4 g thyme, 8 g Scotch bonnet, 30 ml lime juice, 30 ml oil) in a blender or food processor. Pulse until you have a coarse green paste, scraping down once or twice. The paste should look like a chunky pesto, not a smooth puree. Yields about 250 g; you will use about 120 g in the marinade and the rest keeps 2 weeks refrigerated for future use.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Rub the 1200 g chicken with the 12 g / 3 tsp salt, 3 g / 1½ tsp pepper, and 120 g of the fresh epis. Massage into the skin and under any loose skin flaps. Refrigerate 4 to 24 hours.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Day of: scrape most of the epis off the chicken back into the marinade bowl (the wet paste burns aggressively during the sear); reserve the scraped-off epis for the braise. Pat the chicken pieces dry with paper towels. Heat the 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide deep skillet or shallow Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Sear the chicken in 2 batches, skin-side down first, 5 to 6 minutes per side until deep gold. Transfer to a plate; reserve.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Reduce heat to medium. Add the 200 g sliced onion and 150 g sliced red bell pepper to the pan; cook 5 to 6 minutes in the rendered chicken fat until the onion is translucent and starting to brown at the edges and the pepper has softened.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Add the reserved epis (from the scrape-off in step 3) and the 45 g / 3 tbsp tomato paste. Cook 2 minutes, stirring, until the tomato paste has darkened from bright red to brick red and the epis has begun to caramelize into the onion. The pan should smell aggressively of garlic, thyme, and Maillard-tomato.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 400 ml / 1⅔ cups water or stock; scrape the fond off the pan bottom. Bring to a hard simmer. Return the seared chicken to the pan, skin-side up, along with any accumulated juices. The liquid should come about two-thirds up the chicken. Float the whole 12 g Scotch bonnet on top.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Reduce heat to medium-low, cover with the lid slightly ajar (10 percent evaporation), and braise 30 minutes. Check at the 20-minute mark; the sauce should be steadily simmering and reducing.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Uncover; raise heat to medium. Reduce the sauce 5 to 8 minutes more until it has thickened to a glossy consistency that coats the back of a spoon and the chicken is fully tender (a fork inserted into a thigh meets no resistance). Fish out and discard the Scotch bonnet (carefully, do not pierce now). Off heat, drizzle in the 15 ml / 1 tbsp fresh lime juice and stir gently to integrate.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Serve over diri djon djon, white rice, or rice and beans, with a side of fried sweet plantain or pikliz (Haitian fermented Scotch-bonnet cabbage condiment). Spoon the sauce generously over both the chicken and the rice.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why epis. Epis is the Haitian equivalent of Cuban sofrito, Puerto Rican recaito, and Brazilian temperos verdes: a green seasoning paste made in advance, kept in the fridge, and used as the savory base for nearly every Haitian savory dish. The blend (parsley, scallion, garlic, thyme, bell pepper, Scotch bonnet, lime) is structural to Haitian cooking and gives every dish a recognizable green-herbaceous bass note that distinguishes Haitian from Cuban or Puerto Rican cooking.

  Why scrape the epis off before searing. The wet green paste burns aggressively at sear temperatures (180 to 200 °C); leaving it on the chicken produces black bitter spots and prevents the Maillard crust from forming on the skin. Scraping most of it off and adding it back during the braise step (where the temperature is lower) gets the best of both: a crispy seared chicken plus a green-herbaceous sauce.

  The whole Scotch bonnet rule. As in jerk and rice and peas, the Scotch bonnet rides whole on top during the braise. The skin releases fruit-floral capsaicin volatiles into the steam; the intact seed-and-placenta does not rupture. A pierced or chopped chili turns the dish dramatically hotter and pushes Haitian-mild into Jamaican-jerk territory.

  Storage. Keeps 4 days refrigerated; improves overnight as the epis integrates into the sauce. Reheat over medium-low heat with a splash of water. Freezes well; portion into 500 g containers.
