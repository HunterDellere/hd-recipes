before_you_start:
  - 'Dry-salt the thighs at least 4 hours ahead, ideally overnight. Salt them, set them skin-up uncovered on a rack in the fridge, and the air-dried skin will crisp and lacquer in the oven instead of steaming flabby. Skin-on chicken with wet skin never crisps no matter how hot the oven.'
  - 'Take the thighs out 30 minutes before roasting so they are not fridge-cold; cold thighs need longer in the oven and the sprouts overcook waiting for the chicken to reach temperature.'
  - 'Cut the sprouts to match the chicken''s roast time. Halved (not quartered) golf-ball sprouts finish in roughly the same 30 to 35 minutes the thighs need, so the two components come out together.'

steps:
  - text: 'At least 4 hours ahead (overnight is better), pat the 900 g / 6 thighs bone-dry and salt them all over with the 9 g / 2¼ tsp kosher salt, working a little under the skin. Set them skin-up on a rack over a tray and refrigerate uncovered. The salt seasons the meat and the dry air dehydrates the skin so it crisps rather than steams.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 240
  - text: 'Thirty minutes before cooking, pull the thighs out to lose the fridge chill. Preheat the oven to 220 °C / 425 °F. Whisk the glaze: the 45 g / 3 tbsp dijon, the 30 g / 2 tbsp maple syrup, the 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil, the 9 g / 3 cloves grated garlic, the 2 g / 2 tsp thyme leaves, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper into a smooth paste.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Toss the 680 g / 1.5 lb halved sprouts with the 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil and the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt, and spread them cut-face down in a single layer across a rimmed sheet pan. Set a wire rack over the pan (or simply nestle the thighs among the sprouts if you have no rack). Coat the thighs all over with the mustard-maple glaze, getting it under and over the skin, and arrange them skin-up on the rack directly above the sprouts so the rendering fat drips onto them.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Roast at 220 °C / 425 °F for 30 to 35 minutes. The chicken fat renders down onto the sprouts and bastes them while the dry oven heat crisps and lacquers the glazed skin to deep amber. The sprouts caramelize in the schmaltz against the maple in the drippings. Do not open the oven before 25 minutes; the skin needs uninterrupted dry heat to crisp.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 33
  - text: 'Check at 33 minutes. The thigh skin should be deep amber and lacquered, and an instant-read thermometer in the thickest part against the bone should read 79 to 82 °C / 175 to 180 °F (thighs are best well past the 74 °C / 165 °F safe minimum; the connective tissue needs the higher temperature to read tender rather than rubbery). The sprouts underneath should be deeply browned and tender. If the skin needs more color, give it 3 to 5 minutes under the broiler, watching constantly.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Rest and finish. Move the thighs to a board to rest 5 minutes. Toss the sprouts on the pan with the 10 g / 2 tsp apple cider vinegar, scraping up the caramelized glaze and schmaltz from the pan bottom into them; the acid cuts the rendered fat and brightens the whole pan. Taste and adjust salt. Pile the sprouts onto plates, set the thighs on top, and spoon any pan juices over. Serve immediately.'

notes: |
  Why the thighs go above the sprouts. The entire design rests on letting gravity do the basting. A bone-in, skin-on thigh renders a meaningful amount of fat as it roasts, and that schmaltz is flavor. Roasted on a rack directly above the sprouts, the fat and the dripping maple-mustard glaze rain down onto the brassicas, seasoning and basting them in a way a tablespoon of oil never could. If you have no rack, nestle the thighs among the sprouts so the drippings still pool into the vegetables; just toss the sprouts halfway so the ones under the chicken do not sit soggy.

  Why dry-salting the skin matters more than the brine. Chicken skin crisps only when its surface moisture has been driven off. Salting the thighs and resting them uncovered in the fridge for several hours dehydrates the skin and seasons the meat at the same time. Skip this step and the skin steams flabby under its own moisture no matter how hot the oven runs. Four hours is the minimum that makes a visible difference; overnight is better.

  Thigh temperature, not breast temperature. Dark meat is best cooked well past the 74 °C / 165 °F that is safe for white meat. Thighs carry connective tissue and collagen that only break down and read tender and juicy in the high 70s to low 80s Celsius; pulled at 165 °F a thigh is safe but reads rubbery and tight. Trust the 79 to 82 °C reading at the bone, not the clock, and not the white-meat number.

  The vinegar at the end is not optional. The sprouts finish roasting in rendered chicken fat and sweet glaze, which without an acid reads heavy and one-note. The two teaspoons of apple cider vinegar tossed through at the finish is what cuts the schmaltz and snaps the whole pan into focus, the same role lemon plays against brown butter. Do not skip it, and add it off the heat so it stays bright.
