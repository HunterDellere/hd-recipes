source:
  name: 'Parisian bistro canon (Les Halles tradition; codified by Escoffier)'
  note: 'The dish is descended from the late-night onion soup served to porters and revelers at the old Les Halles food market in Paris, gratinée-finished with cheese and bread to provide quick calories at 4 AM. Escoffier codified the recipe in the late 19th century. The structural elements: yellow onions (not red, not sweet Vidalia; yellow onions caramelize cleanly and develop the canonical color), butter as the cooking fat (the Maillard byproducts on the dairy proteins reinforce the onion caramelization), a slow patient caramelization over 60 to 75 minutes (no shortcuts; the deep mahogany color is the entire flavor of the soup), deglazing with dry sherry and dry white wine (the sherry is structural and not optional, the oxidatively-aged nutty profile complements the caramelized onion), real beef stock (commercial bouillon produces a salty bouillon-y soup with no body), thyme and bay, and the final gratin (toasted baguette slice topped with grated Gruyère, broiled until the cheese has melted, bubbled, and browned).'

before_you_start:
  - 'The caramelization is 60 to 75 minutes of nearly-constant stirring. Block out the time; this is not a passive cook. The first 30 minutes the onions sweat down to a fraction of their starting volume; the second 30 minutes they slowly transform from pale yellow to deep mahogany. Cooks who rush the caramelization (high heat, less than 60 minutes) produce a pale broth with no depth; the time is the technique.'
  - 'Have 6 oven-safe soup crocks or wide ramekins (350 to 450 ml capacity) ready. The crocks need to be heat-safe to broiler temperatures (260°C / 500°F); generic ceramic ramekins crack under broiler heat. Onion-soup crocks (Le Creuset stoneware, Apilco, Pillivuyt) are designed for this. A 9 by 13 inch oven-safe baking dish with all soup ladled in and the bread-and-cheese topping spread across the surface is the structurally legitimate one-vessel alternative if individual crocks are unavailable.'
  - 'Toast the baguette slices in advance; they need to be fully dry and crisp before they meet the soup, or they collapse into the broth and the gratin reads as soggy bread rather than a crisp toast.'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the 60 g / 4 tbsp butter and 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil in a wide heavy-bottomed Dutch oven or 7 L pot over medium heat until the butter is melted and starting to foam. Add the 1500 g / 6 large onions (sliced) all at once; the pot will be heaped full. Sprinkle with the 8 g / 1¾ tsp salt and the 5 g / 1 tsp sugar. Stir to coat all the onions in the fat; the volume will halve within 10 minutes as the onions release water.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'The caramelization (the entire technique): reduce heat to medium-low. Cook the onions 60 to 75 minutes total, stirring with a wooden spoon every 3 to 4 minutes (more frequently in the second half), scraping the bottom of the pot to release the building fond. The phases: first 15 minutes the onions are pale and watery; minutes 15 to 30 they are wilted and beginning to color at the edges; minutes 30 to 50 they are pale gold and the bottom of the pot is darkening with caramelized sugar fond; minutes 50 to 75 they transform progressively to medium gold, dark gold, light mahogany, and finally deep mahogany brown. The onions should be soft and jammy with no remaining white or pale yellow patches. The bottom of the pot will repeatedly develop a brown fond that you scrape into the onions every 3 to 4 minutes; this fond is the entire flavor of the soup.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 70
  - text: 'When the onions are deeply mahogany and reduced to about 500 g in the pot (a third of their starting volume), increase heat to medium. Add the 60 g / ¼ cup sherry; stir vigorously to deglaze the fond and let the sherry reduce to almost dry (about 60 seconds; the alcohol burns off cleanly). Add the 240 g / 1 cup white wine; let reduce by half (about 4 to 5 minutes; the alcohol burns off and the wine concentrates).'
    technique: 'deglazing'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Add the 1800 ml / 7½ cups beef stock, 6 sprigs fresh thyme, 2 bay leaves, and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper. Bring to a hard simmer; reduce heat to medium-low. Simmer uncovered 30 to 40 minutes, until the soup has reduced slightly (by about 15 percent) and the flavors have integrated. Fish out the thyme stems and bay leaves. Taste; adjust salt (real homemade beef stock is unsalted, so the soup typically needs another 4 to 6 g of salt at this stage; commercial low-sodium stock typically needs less).'
    time_min: 40
  - text: 'While the soup simmers, toast the baguette: lay the 180 g / 6 to 12 slices of baguette (cut to fit the crock diameter) on a baking sheet. Toast in a 180°C / 350°F oven 8 to 10 minutes per side until completely dry, golden, and cracker-crisp on both sides. The dry crisp toast is structural for the gratin; soft bread soaks the soup and collapses to mush. Hold the toasted slices in the warm oven until ready to assemble.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Set 6 oven-safe soup crocks on a sheet pan (the sheet pan catches any boil-overs during the broiler step). Ladle the hot soup into each crock, filling to about 2 cm below the rim (about 350 ml per crock). Float 1 or 2 toasted baguette rounds on top of each crock (the bread will float on the broth, partly submerged). Pile the 300 g / 3 cups grated Gruyère evenly over the bread, mounding generously (50 g per crock). Sprinkle the optional 30 g / ¼ cup grated parmigiano over the Gruyère.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Switch the oven to broil on high (260°C / 500°F or maximum). Place the sheet pan with the crocks on the upper rack, 10 to 15 cm from the broiler element. Broil 3 to 5 minutes, watching constantly (the cheese goes from molten to burnt in 30 seconds at the end). The gratin is done when the cheese has fully melted across the top, has formed a continuous browned crust with deep golden-brown to dark-brown bubbled spots, and the surface is just beginning to crisp at the edges where the cheese has dripped down the crock walls. Pull immediately.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Carry the sheet pan from the oven to the table (the crocks themselves are extremely hot, do not handle without trivets or a folded towel). Let stand 2 minutes before serving; the soup is well above boiling and will scald an aggressive eater. Serve with a small green salad and the rest of the white wine. The canonical eating technique is to break through the cheese crust with the bowl of a spoon, scooping melted cheese and bread together with the broth in each bite.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  The caramelization is the entire technique, and the most common failure mode is rushing it. Onions caramelize slowly because the process is a Maillard reaction (amino acids reacting with sugars) that proceeds at a fixed rate at a given temperature; the home cook cannot speed it up by raising the heat without burning the surface before the inside has time to dehydrate and brown. The full 60 to 75 minute caramelization at medium-low heat is non-negotiable for the canonical deep mahogany color and concentrated flavor. Cooks who pull the onions at 30 minutes (still pale gold) produce a soup that is bouillon-flavored rather than onion-flavored; the depth of the soup is exactly proportional to the depth of the caramelization.

  Baking soda is sometimes recommended as a caramelization shortcut (a pinch of baking soda raises the pH of the onions and accelerates Maillard browning). Avoid this shortcut for French onion soup specifically: the baking-soda accelerated caramelization produces a slightly soapy mineral taste and an unpleasantly mushy onion texture in the finished soup. The slow patient caramelization is the only structurally correct technique.

  The sherry is structural and skipping it produces a flatter, less complex soup. Fino or amontillado sherry has an oxidatively-aged nutty profile that complements the caramelized-onion sweetness in a way that wine alone cannot. The cost of a bottle of decent dry sherry is justified by the number of dishes that benefit from it (French onion soup, Spanish gambas al ajillo, English mushroom-on-toast); a bottle keeps 3 months refrigerated after opening if it is fino, longer if amontillado or oloroso.

  The gratin is the structural finishing technique that distinguishes French onion soup (gratinée) from a generic onion broth. The bread-and-cheese cap is not a garnish; it is a structural component that creates a crispy molten browned crust over the broth, and the eating technique (break through the cheese crust with the spoon, scoop cheese and bread together with broth) is part of the dish. Onion soup served without the gratin is a different and lesser preparation.

  The crock shape matters. Traditional French onion soup crocks (Le Creuset stoneware, Apilco, Pillivuyt) are tall and narrow with vertical walls; this shape lets the cheese melt down the walls of the crock and form a thick browned crust at the soup line. Wide shallow bowls produce a thin scatter of cheese across the surface that does not gratin properly. Invest in proper crocks or use a wide oven-safe baking dish with all the soup ladled in and the bread-and-cheese topping spread across the surface; serve at the table from the central dish, ladling individual portions.

  Storage. The soup base (without the bread and cheese) keeps 5 days refrigerated and improves over the first 48 hours as the caramelized-onion flavor integrates. Freezes well; portion into 500 ml containers, freezes 3 months. To serve from leftover soup, reheat to a hard simmer on the stovetop, ladle into crocks, top fresh with bread and cheese, and broil. Do not store assembled gratinéed soup; the bread turns to mush and the cheese seizes.
