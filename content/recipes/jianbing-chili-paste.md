steps:
  - text: 'Stub: pound the garlic and doubanjiang. In a mortar with a pestle, pound the 30 g / 8 to 10 garlic cloves to a rough paste (about 30 seconds of working). Add the 50 g doubanjiang and pound another 30 seconds until the bean paste is broken up and the garlic is distributed. The texture should be a coarse paste, not smooth; the doubanjiang chunks contribute mouthfeel in the final.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Stub: bloom in hot oil. In a small saucepan, warm the 100 g chili oil over medium heat for 1 to 2 minutes until it just starts to shimmer (about 140°C / 285°F; should not smoke). Pour the warm oil over the garlic-doubanjiang pound in the mortar; it will sizzle aggressively as the garlic blooms and the doubanjiang releases aroma. Stir with the pestle to integrate, then sprinkle the 2 g ground Sichuan peppercorn over the top while still warm.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Stub: finish. Stir in the 10 g toasted sesame oil and 8 g light soy sauce while the paste is warm but no longer sizzling. Cool to room temperature, then transfer to a clean glass jar. Refrigerate; keeps 6 weeks. Bring to room temperature before brushing onto a jianbing (cold paste does not spread evenly).'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  This is a stub. To flesh out: the garlic-pounding logic (pounding fractures the cell walls and releases more allicin than mincing, producing a stronger garlic note that survives the hot-oil bloom; food-processed garlic produces a watery purée that dilutes the paste), the doubanjiang sourcing (Pixian-county doubanjiang aged 2 years is the gold standard; any reputable brand from a Chinese grocery is acceptable; American supermarket "chili bean paste" is usually too thin and corn-syrup-sweetened), the chili oil sourcing (Lao Gan Ma is workable but a fresh-made Sichuan chili oil with sediment is better; the sediment is structural to the paste), the optional add-ins (some Tianjin vendors stir in 1 tsp fermented black beans pounded into the garlic; others add a pinch of five-spice; both are legitimate but optional), and the food-safety reasoning (the soy sauce and the salt in the doubanjiang give the finished paste enough preservative load to be stable refrigerated for 6 weeks; for longer storage, freeze in 50 g portions).

  This paste is the secret of jianbing that travel-experienced cooks recognize immediately: the difference between a wrap that tastes flat-spicy (one-note bottled chili oil) and one that tastes layered-spicy (garlic, fermented bean, peppercorn, sesame, soy all reading sequentially across the bite). The investment of 12 minutes of active work produces enough paste for 4 weeks of weekly jianbing breakfasts, and the paste is also excellent on cold noodles, dumpling dip, or stirred into congee.

  Failure modes: paste too thin (chili oil with too little sediment; add 1 tsp additional doubanjiang); paste too salty (heavy hand on the soy sauce; balance with a pinch of sugar in the next batch); garlic raw-tasting (oil not hot enough when poured over; aim for the shimmer-point, not just warm).
