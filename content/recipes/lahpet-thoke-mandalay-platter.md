before_you_start:
  - 'This salad is an assembly job, but it depends on three made-ahead components: fermented tea leaves (lahpet), the fried bean-and-seed crunch (pè kyaw), and crispy fried garlic with its oil. Make or buy all three before you start; the recipes for each are linked below. With those in hand the platter takes 20 minutes.'
  - 'Keep every element in its own separate pile on the plate until serving. The whole point of the platter style is that the crunchy elements stay crunchy and the fermented tea stays distinct; you (or your guests) toss it all together only at the last second, then eat immediately before the crunch softens.'
  - 'Dress at the table, not in the kitchen. Once the fish sauce, lime, and garlic oil hit the crunch it starts to soften; the salad is at its best in the first couple of minutes after tossing.'

steps:
  - text: 'Ready the tea. Drain the 60 g / ¼ cup lahpet of its brine and oil, squeeze it gently to shed excess liquid, and pinch out any tough stems. If it tastes very strong or sour, rinse it briefly and re-squeeze. Mound it in the center of a wide, flat serving plate.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Build the rings of crunch. Around the tea, arrange the dry crunchy elements in separate, touching piles: the 80 g / ⅔ cup pè kyaw bean-and-seed crunch, the 30 g / ¼ cup extra roasted peanuts, the 10 g / 1 tbsp toasted sesame seeds, and the 15 g / 2 tbsp crispy fried garlic. Keeping them separate, not pre-mixed, is what preserves their crunch until the toss.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Build the fresh ring. Add the raw elements in their own piles around the outside: the 120 g / 1 medium diced tomato, the 100 g / 1½ cups finely shredded green cabbage, and the 10 g / 1 to 2 sliced green chili if using. The cabbage gives crisp bulk and the tomato a sweet-acid pop against the fermented tea.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Dress at the table and toss. Spoon the 30 g / 2 tbsp garlic oil, the 15 g / 1 tbsp fish sauce, and the 20 g / juice of 1 lime over the whole platter. Bring it to the table looking like a composed wheel, then toss everything together thoroughly with two spoons (or clean hands) right before eating, lifting from the bottom so the tea, crunch, vegetables, and dressing fully combine. Taste: it should land salty, sour, savory, bitter, and crunchy all at once. Adjust with more lime for brightness or fish sauce for salt, and serve at once with lime wedges.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  Why the platter stays segregated until the table. Lahpet thoke is a study in contrast: soft, sour fermented tea against hard fried beans, crisp raw cabbage, juicy tomato, shatter-crisp garlic. Every one of those textures degrades the moment dressing hits it, the beans soften, the garlic goes limp, the cabbage weeps. Arranging the elements in separate piles and tossing only at the table is not presentation for its own sake; it is the technique that keeps the textural contrast intact through the first bites, which is where the dish lives. This composed, toss-at-table form is the more traditional and ceremonial way lahpet thoke is served.

  Why fish sauce and lime are non-negotiable. The fermented tea brings sourness and a savory funk, but on its own it reads incomplete, flat and one-dimensionally sour. The fish sauce adds the salt and the deep glutamate umami that makes the whole thing taste savory rather than merely pickled, and the fresh lime layers a bright top-note acid over the deeper fermented sourness. This is the classic Southeast Asian balance, salty, sour, savory, with the tea supplying bitterness, and the dressing is what pulls the separate piles into one coherent salad.

  How to eat it. Toss thoroughly, then eat in big mixed forkfuls so every bite carries tea, crunch, vegetable, and dressing together. In Myanmar lahpet thoke is everywhere, a snack, a palate cleanser, a gesture of hospitality, and traditionally a peace offering, and the caffeine in the eaten leaf gives it a gentle lift. Pair it with plain rice or serve it as the bright, sharp counterpoint on a table of richer curries.
