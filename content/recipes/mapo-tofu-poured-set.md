steps:
  - text: 'Pre-warm 4 individual heat-tolerant serving bowls (10 to 14 cm / 4 to 6 inch diameter, ceramic or porcelain) by running hot tap water over them for 30 seconds, then drying with a clean towel. Cold bowls will cause uneven setting at the bottom of the curd. Set the bowls on a stable counter where they will not be disturbed for the next 30 minutes.'
    technique: 'hot-set-tofu'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Brown the beef and prepare the mapo sauce in advance, both will be ladled over the set tofu, so they need to be done first and held warm. Heat a wok over high heat. Add 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil and the 200 g ground beef. Stir-fry continuously, breaking the meat into fine granules, for 6 to 8 minutes until deeply browned, dry, and aromatic. Add 10 ml / 2 tsp Shaoxing and 5 ml / 1 tsp light soy. Toss 30 seconds. Transfer to a bowl.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Render the aromatic base. Wipe the wok and return to medium heat. Add 45 ml / 3 tbsp neutral oil. When shimmering (140 to 160°C / 285 to 320°F), add 40 g doubanjiang. Break up and stir-fry 90 seconds until oil turns brilliant brick-red. Add 6 g crushed douchi, 15 g minced ginger, 18 g minced garlic. Stir-fry 45 seconds.'
    technique: 'red-oil-rendering'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Build the sauce. Pour in 240 ml / 1 cup chicken stock, 18 ml / 1¼ tbsp light soy, 5 ml / 1 tsp dark soy, 3 g / ½ tsp sugar. Return the browned beef. Bring to a simmer. Whisk the 9 g cornstarch with 45 ml / 3 tbsp cold water; stream in slowly, stirring constantly, until the sauce is glossy and thick, it should mound on a spoon rather than run off. Pull off heat. Hold the sauce warm (return to lowest heat just before serving if needed; the sauce should be near-boiling when it goes onto the tofu). The sauce can sit covered up to 15 minutes.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Heat the soy milk. In a clean saucepan, warm the 1000 ml / 4 cups soy milk over medium heat to 80°C / 175°F (small bubbles forming at the edges, no rolling boil). Use a thermometer for the first attempts; the temperature window for GDL coagulation is genuinely narrow (70 to 90°C). Pull off heat as soon as 80°C is reached.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Set the tofu. Dissolve the 2.8 g GDL in the 30 ml / 2 tbsp warm water in a small bowl until clear (no lumps). Pour the GDL solution into the warm soy milk and whisk for 5 seconds, long enough to distribute, short enough not to introduce excessive air. Immediately pour the soy-milk-GDL mixture into the four pre-warmed serving bowls (250 ml / 1 cup per bowl). Cover each bowl loosely with plastic wrap or a lid (prevents skin formation). Leave undisturbed at warm room temperature for 18 to 22 minutes. Test by gently tilting one bowl; the surface should not move and a finger pressed at the edge should leave a clean indent that fills with clear whey.'
    technique: 'hot-set-tofu'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Reheat the sauce. While the tofu is finishing its set, return the sauce to medium heat and bring back to a brisk simmer. The sauce must be near-boiling when it goes onto the tofu, the temperature contrast (boiling sauce against custard-soft fresh tofu) is part of the dish. The sauce will thicken slightly as it reheats; if it goes too thick, add 15 to 30 ml / 1 to 2 tbsp hot water to thin.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Toast and grind the Sichuan peppercorns. Heat a small dry skillet over medium heat. Add 4 g / 2 tsp whole Sichuan peppercorns. Toast 60 to 90 seconds, swirling continuously, until fragrant and slightly oily-looking (do not let them blacken). Transfer to a mortar and grind to a coarse powder. The fresh-toasted-and-ground peppercorn must hit the dish within 5 minutes of grinding for the má (numbing) effect to be at its peak.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Plate at the table. Bring the four set-tofu bowls to the table on a tray, with the simmering hot sauce in a small saucepan and the peppercorn powder, scallion greens, and cilantro in small bowls alongside. For each diner, ladle 80 to 100 ml of the near-boiling mapo sauce over the surface of the set tofu, pour gently, the sauce will sit on top and slowly migrate down through the curd. Sprinkle with a generous pinch of fresh peppercorn powder, scallion greens, and a few cilantro leaves. The contrast hits at the table: the diner sees the boiling-red sauce poured over the white custard, smells the má-là aromatics rising as the sauce hits the cooler curd, and gets the dish at maximum tàng (temperature heat) and minimum holding-time degradation. Eat with a spoon (no chopsticks, the curd is too soft); the spoon takes a portion of curd and a portion of sauce together, and the texture-and-temperature contrast is the entire point.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why this format. The poured-set version is more involved than the standard cubed-silken mapo (it requires GDL, plain soy milk, a thermometer, and a 20-minute undisturbed set), but it produces a fundamentally different eating experience: the silken curd is fresher and softer than any commercial silken tofu, the dish lands at the highest possible temperature contrast (boiling sauce hitting custard-soft cooler curd), and the visual presentation is dramatic. Restaurants in Chengdu and Chongqing serve this format under the name "douhua mapo" (豆花麻婆) or "yuan zi mapo" (源汁麻婆); it is the version aficionados seek out.

  The contrast principle. The dish is structurally about contrast, temperature contrast (boiling sauce on cooler tofu), texture contrast (custard-soft curd against the sauce's clinging density and the beef's dry granularity), and flavor contrast (the mild sweet soy milk against the aggressive doubanjiang-douchi-peppercorn sauce). All three contrasts are maximized by the pour-and-set format and minimized by cooking the silken tofu in the sauce (which warms the tofu and softens the textural distinction). Once you have eaten the dish in this format, the standard cubed version reads as a compromise.

  Timing. The active cooking time is similar to standard mapo (about 30 minutes), but the total elapsed time is longer (about 60 minutes) because the tofu needs 18 to 22 minutes of undisturbed setting. The setting time is hands-off: while the tofu sets, prepare the peppercorn finish, set the table, plate any other dishes for the meal. The dish is also more time-sensitive at the end than standard mapo, the set tofu does not improve sitting (water begins to weep from the curd surface within 30 minutes of setting), and the sauce should be poured over within 25 to 30 minutes of pouring the soy milk. Cook all components in parallel or with the sauce slightly ahead so that the final assembly happens immediately after the tofu sets.

  GDL accuracy. Weigh the GDL on a 0.1 g scale; volumetric measurement (½ tsp, etc.) is not accurate enough. The GDL-to-soy-milk ratio is the variable that determines whether the curd is too soft (under 0.20%, will not hold its shape under sauce), correct (0.25 to 0.30%, soft custard wobble), or too firm (above 0.35%, the curd reads dense and slightly sour from excess gluconic acid). 0.28% (2.8 g per 1000 g soy milk) is the recommended starting point and produces the canonical douhua texture.

  The temperature contrast at serving. The dish lives or dies on the temperature contrast at serving. The set tofu, having sat for 20 minutes after the soy milk was poured at 80°C, will be around 60°C when the sauce hits it, pleasantly warm, not hot. The sauce must be near-boiling (95 to 100°C / 200 to 212°F) when ladled. The temperature gradient produced as the boiling sauce hits the cooler curd is sensory drama, the diner sees steam rising, smells the má-là volatiles released as the hot sauce contacts the cooler surface, and gets a textural contrast of hot-on-warm that defines the dish. Sauce that is only warm-not-hot when ladled produces a tepid eating experience and undersells the format. Reheat the sauce immediately before serving; do not let it rest off heat.

  Eating mechanics. Use a soup spoon (no chopsticks). The spoon takes a portion of the curd from underneath the sauce, the curd should give cleanly under the spoon and lift in a soft cradle holding sauce on top. The first bite should hit the má-là of the sauce, then the soft sweet soy milk of the curd underneath, then the spice-tingle aftertaste as the peppercorn settles in. Some diners prefer to fold sauce and curd together with the spoon before each bite; others prefer to sample sauce and curd in distinct alternation. Both work. The dish does not reheat, once sauced, eat within 10 minutes.
