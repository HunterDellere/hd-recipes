source:
  name: 'The classic American marble pound/loaf cake, with the chocolate-vs-vanilla two-tone extended to a three-tone vanilla-ube-cocoa by treating ube halaya as the third batter base.'
  note: 'Marbling is a deliberately incomplete mix. The three batters must stay visually distinct, so they are layered and dragged exactly once. Over-swirl and the cocoa and ube bleed into a uniform grey-purple; under-swirl and you get stripes, not marble. The sour cream is structural: its acidity tenderizes the crumb and, at pH around 4.5, also sits in the range that keeps the ube anthocyanin reading violet rather than dull.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Bring the butter, eggs, and sour cream to room temperature (about 30 minutes out). Cold butter will not cream to the aerated state the cake rises on, and cold eggs or sour cream can seize the creamed butter into curdled lumps that bake gummy.'
  - 'Have three bowls ready before you start splitting the batter. The base batter is divided into vanilla, ube, and cocoa portions, and you want to flavor each and start layering before the leavener loses its first lift, so stage the bowls, the sifted cocoa, and the halaya in advance.'
  - 'Line the loaf pan with a parchment sling (a strip up the long sides) and butter the short ends. The cake bakes nearly an hour and the sour-cream crumb is tender; the sling lifts it out cleanly without tearing the marbled top.'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 175 °C / 350 °F with a rack in the lower third. Line a 23 x 13 cm / 9 x 5-inch loaf pan with a parchment sling up the long sides and butter the exposed short ends. Whisk the 250 g / 2 cups all-purpose flour, 6 g / 1½ tsp baking powder, and 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt in a bowl and set aside. Stir the 120 g / ½ cup sour cream and 60 g / ¼ cup whole milk together in a measuring cup.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Cream the 170 g / ¾ cup softened butter and 230 g / 1 cup + 2 tbsp granulated sugar with the paddle on medium for 3 to 4 minutes until pale and visibly fluffy, scraping down twice. Add the 3 room-temperature eggs one at a time, beating 20 seconds after each until emulsified, then beat in the 8 g / 1½ tsp vanilla bean paste. If the mixture looks curdled, the eggs were too cold; keep beating and it will come together.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Add the flour mixture in three additions alternating with the sour-cream mixture in two, beginning and ending with flour, mixing on low just until each addition disappears. Stop the moment the last streak of flour goes; overmixing builds gluten and toughens the crumb. This is the base batter.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Split the base batter. Scoop about a third of it (roughly 320 g) into a second bowl and fold in the 100 g / scant ½ cup ube halaya and the optional 4 ml / ¾ tsp ube extract until uniform violet. Scoop another third into a third bowl and fold in the 18 g / 3 tbsp sifted dutch-process cocoa loosened with the 20 g / 4 tsp warm milk until uniform dark brown. Leave the final third plain vanilla. You now have three batters of roughly equal volume and the same consistency.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Layer for marble. Drop the batters into the pan in alternating heaping spoonfuls, scattering vanilla, ube, and cocoa dollops across each layer so no two same-color blobs stack directly, until all three are used and the pan is evenly full. Resist any urge to smooth or stir as you go; the dollops are the marble.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Swirl exactly once. Push a thin table knife or skewer down to the bottom of the pan and draw it through the batter in a single slow figure-eight running the length of the loaf, then lift out. One pass is the whole technique: it drags the three colors into folded veins while keeping them distinct. A second or third pass blends them toward a muddy uniform purple-brown, so stop after one. Tap the pan once on the counter to settle.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Bake 55 to 65 minutes, until a skewer inserted into the center comes out with a few moist crumbs but no wet batter, and the top springs back to a light press. If the top browns before the center sets (around the 40-minute mark), tent loosely with foil. Cool in the pan 15 minutes, then lift out by the sling onto a rack and cool completely before slicing; a warm cut drags the tender crumb and smears the marble.'
    time_min: 65

notes: |
  Why three batters from one base. Marbling reads best when the swirled batters differ only in color and flavor, not in density or rise, so they bake at the same rate and hold their veins crisp instead of one sinking past another. Splitting a single finished base batter into vanilla, ube, and cocoa portions guarantees that. The only adjustments are additive: halaya and extract into one, cocoa loosened with a little warm milk into another (the milk compensates for the cocoa''s drying starch so all three stay the same pourable thickness). Equal volume and equal consistency are what make the marble clean.

  Why ube wants the sour cream, chemically. Ube''s violet is anthocyanin, and anthocyanin reads most vividly violet in the slightly acidic pH 5 to 7 range, shifting dull and grey toward neutral-to-alkaline. A plain butter cake batter raised with baking powder trends slightly alkaline, which is exactly where the ube color goes muddy. The sour cream (pH around 4.5) pulls the batter back into the acidic window that keeps the pigment violet, and does double duty tenderizing the crumb. This is why the ube vein in a sour-cream cake stays brighter than in a straight buttermilk-free pound cake.

  The single-swirl rule. The instinct is to keep dragging the knife until the cake "looks marbled," but every pass blends the three colors further toward a uniform purple-brown. One slow figure-eight through the layered dollops is enough: it folds the colors into distinct veins while leaving large zones of each still pure. If you want a more dramatic marble, add more dollops and more layers, not more swirling. Think of it as folding, not stirring.

  Reading doneness on a marbled loaf. The cocoa vein bakes a touch faster and darker than the ube and vanilla, so judge by the center skewer (moist crumbs, no wet batter) and the spring-back, not by the surface color, which is unevenly dark by design. A tall loaf pan holds the heat long; if the top is setting dark before the center cooks through, tent with foil at the 40-minute mark and keep baking. Cool it completely before the first slice, the sour-cream crumb is tender and a warm knife smears the marble you worked to keep distinct.
