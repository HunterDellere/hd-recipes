source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Iberian escabeche tradition, the pre-refrigeration acid-cure used across Spain and Portugal for oily fish, game birds, and vegetables.'
  note: 'Escabeche is a preservation method first and a flavor method second. The fish is fried to set the surface protein and build a dredge crust, then drowned in a hot acidulated marinade of vinegar, olive oil, wine, and fried aromatics. The acid (pH well below 4.5) denatures surface protein and creates an environment hostile to spoilage organisms, which is why a properly made escabeche keeps a week or more refrigerated and historically kept for days at ambient temperature. Mackerel is the ideal subject: its 12 to 18% polyunsaturated fat is the fastest-spoiling lipid of any common finfish, and the same acid that preserves it also cuts the perceived oily-fishiness that undercooked mackerel can carry. The dish is always served cold or at room temperature, after the marinade has had at least one overnight to penetrate; eating it hot defeats the entire logic.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'Holding fried, acid-cured mackerel refrigerated for up to a week rather than eating it the day it is cooked'
    ref: 'safety/seafood-doneness#scombroid-histamine-the-toxin-no-temperature-fixes'
    why: 'Mackerel is a scombroid species: held above 40°F, bacteria convert its naturally high histidine into histamine, which is heat-stable and not destroyed by frying, vinegar, or refrigeration. Buy from a counter that handles ice well, keep the fish cold until it goes into the flour, and cook it within a day of purchase; the acid cure preserves fresh fish but cannot reverse temperature abuse. The fish is then fully fried through before it enters the marinade (this is cooked fish, not a raw cure), and the acidic oil-capped marinade holds it. Keep the fish fully submerged under the oil at all times, refrigerate within two hours of pouring the warm marinade, and eat within 5 to 6 days. Discard if the marinade ever smells of ammonia.'

  - 'Serve it cold or at room temperature, never hot. If serving from cold, pull the dish from the refrigerator 30 to 45 minutes ahead so the olive oil, which sets cloudy and semi-solid when cold, melts back to clear and the aromatics read.'
  - 'Buy the freshest mackerel you can find and check it cold; oily fish oxidizes fast. Have the fish filleted and pin-boned before you start, and keep it on ice until the moment it goes into the flour.'
  - 'The marinade is layered hot over the fried fish in a single non-reactive dish (glass or ceramic, never bare aluminum or cast iron; the acid reacts and reads metallic). Have that dish ready before you start frying.'

steps:
  - text: 'Salt and dredge the fish. Cut the 500 g / 1 lb mackerel fillets crosswise into 6 cm pieces and pat very dry on paper towels. Season all sides with the 7 g / 2 tsp kosher salt and let sit 10 minutes; the salt firms the surface protein so the dredge adheres and the flesh stays intact in the fry. Spread the 60 g / ½ cup all-purpose flour on a plate. Just before frying, dredge each piece in the flour and shake off all but a whisper; a thick coat turns gummy in the marinade rather than crisp.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Fry the fish to set, not to brown hard. Heat the 120 g / ½ cup frying olive oil in a wide skillet over medium-high until it shimmers and a flour speck sizzles on contact, about 180°C / 355°F. Lay the fish in skin-side down, working in two batches so the pan is not crowded, and fry 90 seconds per side until the flour crust is set and pale gold and the flesh is just cooked through. This is a shallow fry to firm and seal the fish, not a deep golden fry; the marinade does the rest of the flavor work. Lift each piece to the non-reactive serving dish (glass or ceramic) and arrange in a single snug layer. Pour off and discard the spent frying oil, but do not wipe the pan; the browned flour fond stays.'
    technique: 'batter-frying'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Build the aromatic base. Wipe the pan to remove burnt flour specks, then add the 180 g / ¾ cup marinade olive oil over medium-low heat. Add the 30 g / 8 cloves sliced garlic and fry gently until pale gold and fragrant, about 2 minutes; do not let it brown past blond or the marinade turns bitter. Add the 200 g / 1 onion sliced thin and the 120 g / 2 carrots in coins, and sweat slowly, stirring, for 8 to 10 minutes until the onion is fully translucent and limp and the carrot has softened but holds its shape. Stir in the 10 g / 4 chopped anchovy fillets and cook 1 minute until they dissolve into the oil; they are the umami spine of the marinade and will not read as fish.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Bloom the spices off direct high heat. Pull the pan to low. Add the 2 g / 3 bay leaves, the 2 g / 1 tsp crushed fennel seed, and the 3 g / 1 tsp lightly cracked black peppercorns; stir 30 seconds in the warm oil to release their volatiles. Take the pan off the heat entirely and stir in the 4 g / 2 tsp smoked paprika and the bloomed 0.1 g / pinch saffron with its soaking water; paprika scorches and turns acrid in seconds over direct heat, so the residual warmth of the oil is what blooms it.'
    technique: 'spice-tempering'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Acidulate and simmer the marinade. Return the pan to medium. Pour in the 120 g / ½ cup sherry vinegar and the 120 g / ½ cup dry white wine, add the 3 g / 1 tsp kosher salt, and bring to a gentle simmer. Let it bubble 3 to 4 minutes; this drives off the raw alcohol harshness from the wine and softens the vinegar from a sharp top-note into a rounded acidity, while the marinade emulsifies loosely as the oil and acidulated liquid bubble together. Taste a drop on a spoon: it should read aggressively acidic and savory, because the fish and the overnight rest will mellow it considerably.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Drown the fish warm. Pour the hot marinade, oil, aromatics, and all, evenly over the fried mackerel so every piece is fully submerged under the liquid and the oil rises to cap the surface; the warm marinade penetrating warm fish is what carries the aromatics into the flesh. Distribute the onion, carrot, and bay over the top. Let cool on the counter 30 minutes, until no longer steaming, then cover with a fitted lid or parchment pressed to the surface and refrigerate at least 12 hours, ideally 24. Do not skip the rest; on the first night the fish tastes fried and the marinade tastes sharp, by the next day the two have become one dish.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Serve cold or at room temperature. Pull the dish from the refrigerator 30 to 45 minutes before serving so the olive oil, which sets cloudy and waxy when cold, melts back to clear; the dish is meant to read at cool room temperature, not fridge-cold and never hot. Lift the fish and a generous spoonful of the onion, carrot, and oil onto plates. Scatter the 6 g / 2 tbsp chopped parsley over the top, set out the lemon wedges from 1 lemon for a final fresh acid at the plate, and serve with the 200 g / half loaf crusty bread to mop the vinegar-saturated oil. The oil left in the dish, steeped with smoked paprika, saffron, and sherry vinegar, is itself a prize; spoon it onto the bread.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  Why escabeche is a preservation method, not a marinade. The name comes from the Arabic sikbaj, a medieval vinegar stew, carried into Iberia and out to Latin America and the Philippines, where it became the parent of dishes like Filipino paksiw and escabeche. The original purpose was to keep cooked fish edible for days without refrigeration: the fish is fried (the heat kills surface organisms and sets the protein), then sealed under acid and oil (the low pH and the oxygen-excluding oil cap suppress regrowth). Refrigeration has made the keeping function optional, but the flavor logic is inseparable from it. The dish must rest; eating it the day it is cooked is eating an unfinished thing.

  Why mackerel specifically. Of all the fish escabeche is made from, the oily blue fish are the most rewarded by it. Mackerel carries 12 to 18% polyunsaturated fat, the fastest-oxidizing lipid of any common finfish and the source of its undeserved fishy reputation. The acid does two jobs at once here: it preserves, and it cleaves the perceived oiliness, converting the very quality that makes mackerel hard to love when mishandled into the quality that makes escabeche of mackerel one of the great cold fish dishes. A lean white fish gains nothing from the acid bath and loses moisture; this dish exists for the oily fish.

  The fry is a seal, not a sear. Resist the instinct to fry the fish to deep golden-brown crisp. The flour coat is there to set the surface and give the marinade something to cling to, not to provide crunch; any crunch you build in the pan dissolves overnight in the acid anyway. Fry just until the crust sets pale gold and the flesh turns opaque, then move on. The browning you want is in the marinade, from the garlic, the anchovy, and the bloomed pimentón.

  The two acid phases. The marinade vinegar is a long, infused, mellowed acid that has had a day to work into the fish. The lemon wedge at the table is a fresh, volatile, bright acid that sits on top. They are not redundant; the first is the structure of the dish and the second is the lift at the moment of eating. Skipping the lemon leaves the dish reading flat despite all the vinegar in it, because the marinade acid has gone soft and round by serving time and wants a sharp top-note against it.

  Serving temperature is the most common error. This dish is cold-kitchen food, eaten at cool room temperature. Served fridge-cold, the olive oil is waxy and the flavors are muted and locked; served hot, the whole acid-cure logic collapses and you have soggy fried fish in vinegar. Forty-five minutes on the counter before serving is the difference between a dull dish and a complete one. In Spain this is tapas-bar and home-larder food, made in advance and pulled out when people arrive.
