before_you_start:
  - 'Source matters more than technique here. The tuna is barely cooked, so it must be sashimi-grade or its functional equivalent (commercially flash-frozen at -35 °C for parasite kill, which a home freezer cannot do). Ask the fish counter "was this commercially flash-frozen," not "is this sushi-grade," which carries no legal weight. If you cannot source it, cook the same block fully through to a tuna-steak doneness instead, see the modification at the bottom.'
  - 'Have everything ready before the pan goes on. The sear is under 90 seconds total and there is no time to mix sauce or toast sesame mid-cook. Mix the dipping sauce, toast and cool the sesame on a plate, slice the scallion, and pat the tuna bone-dry first. A wet surface steams instead of searing and the sesame slides off.'
  - 'Chill the block, sear from cold. A cold-from-the-fridge tuna block buys you a wider margin: the surface can toast while the center stays under 50 °C. A room-temperature block overshoots the center in the same sear time. This is the opposite of steak, where you temper the meat first; for rare tuna you want the core as cold as possible going in.'

steps:
  - text: 'Mix the dipping sauce. In a small bowl whisk the 30 g / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, the 15 g / 1 tbsp yuzu juice, the 10 g / 2 tsp mirin, the 3 g / ½ tsp toasted sesame oil, and the 4 g / 1 tsp grated ginger. Stir in the 10 g / 1 scallion sliced thin. Taste: it should read salty-tart-savory with a gentle sweet backnote, sharper than you expect, because the cold fatty fish blunts it. Set aside so the flavors marry while you cook.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Toast the sesame. Put the 25 g / 3 tbsp white sesame seeds in a dry skillet over medium heat and agitate constantly. At 60 to 90 seconds they pop softly; at 3 to 4 minutes they reach a pale caramel-bronze and smell distinctly of toasted sesame. Pull them the moment the color is right (residual pan heat keeps toasting them), and tip onto a cool plate. Stir in the 10 g / 1 tbsp black sesame seeds. Spread the mix into an even layer on the plate; this is your crust bed.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Salt and coat the tuna. Pat both 350 g / 12 oz tuna blocks completely dry once more with paper towel, any surface water and the sesame will not stick and the sear will steam. Season all faces of both blocks with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt. Press each block firmly into the sesame plate, rolling it so all four long faces and both ends pick up a dense, even coat; press the seeds in with your palm so they adhere. The blocks should look fully jacketed in sesame.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Heat the pan. Place a heavy stainless or cast-iron skillet over high heat for 3 to 4 minutes until it is genuinely smoking. Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil and swirl to coat; it should shimmer and just begin to smoke within 5 seconds. The pan being hot enough is the entire game, a cool pan toasts the sesame slowly and the heat creeps into the center before the crust is set.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Sear, face by face, on the clock. Lay the first block in the smoking oil and leave it undisturbed for 30 to 45 seconds, until the sesame crust on the contact face is fragrant and set to a toasted gold. Using tongs, roll the block onto the next face and repeat, 30 to 45 seconds per face, working around all four long sides plus a quick 15-second touch on each end. The whole block should be in the pan no more than about 3 minutes total. Watch the cut line of cooked-opaque flesh creeping in from each seared face: you want it to climb only 2 to 3 mm before you turn, leaving a wide cool ruby band through the center. Sear the second block the same way. Pull both to a board the instant the last face is done.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Rest briefly, then slice across the grain. Let the blocks sit 60 seconds, no longer, just enough to stop the residual heat from migrating inward (unlike steak, there is no juice to redistribute here, the rest is purely to halt carryover). With a sharp knife, slice each block across the grain into 1 cm slabs. The cross-section is the payoff: a thin toasted-sesame rim, a 2 to 3 mm band of pale cooked flesh, and a wide cool ruby-translucent center. If the center reads warm or opaque, your pan was not hot enough or you seared too long; note it for next time, the fix is more heat and less time, not the reverse.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Plate and serve immediately. Fan the slices on a plate, cut-face up so the ruby center shows. Serve the dipping sauce alongside in a small bowl for dipping, or spoon a thin line of it across the slices, do not drown them, the sesame crust should stay crisp. Eat right away while the crust is still toasty and the center is cool; seared tuna does not hold, the contrast collapses as the block sits and the center warms through.'
    time_min: 1

modifications:
  - for: 'no sashimi-grade fish available'
    to: 'fully cooked tuna steak'
    how: 'If you cannot source sashimi-grade tuna, do not serve it rare. Use the same sesame coat and pan, but sear each face longer (about 90 seconds) and let the center cook through to opaque, pulling at an internal 55 to 60 °C for a moist medium rather than chasing rare. The fish reads firmer and less luxurious but is safe from a regular fish counter; squeeze more yuzu over the top to keep it from drying.'
    kind: 'protein'
  - for: 'crust direction'
    to: 'furikake or togarashi crust'
    how: 'Swap the plain sesame bed for a furikake blend (sesame, nori flakes, salt) or stir 2 g / 1 tsp shichimi togarashi into the white sesame for a seven-spice heat. The nori adds an oceanic glutamate layer; the togarashi brings citrus-peel and chili. Coat and sear identically.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'service style'
    to: 'tataki with ponzu and daikon'
    how: 'Skip the sesame crust entirely. Sear the salted bare blocks the same 30 to 45 seconds per face, then shock them in an ice bath for 10 seconds to stop the cook cold, pat dry, and slice. Serve over grated daikon (oroshi) with ponzu poured around rather than a soy-yuzu dip. This is closer to classic katsuo no tataki and reads cleaner and more austere.'
    kind: 'regional'

notes: |
  Why source dictates the whole dish. This is the rare recipe where the ingredient, not the technique, is the gate. The center stays under 50 °C, which is functionally raw, so the fish must be safe to eat raw: sashimi or sushi grade, which in practice means commercially flash-frozen at -35 °C for 15 hours to kill anisakis larvae. Your home freezer runs -18 °C and does not reach the parasite-kill threshold, so freezing a fresh-counter loin overnight does not make it safe. The label "sushi-grade" carries no FDA legal weight; the real question for the counter is whether the fish was commercially flash-frozen. If the answer is no or unclear, cook the block through (the modification at the top) rather than serving it rare.

  Why cold fish, hot pan. The dish is a contrast you build through a temperature gradient: a toasted crust over a cool raw center. The wider the gap between the surface and the core when the block hits the pan, the easier that gradient is to hold. So you do the opposite of steak prep, no tempering. A fridge-cold block (or even one chilled 15 minutes in the freezer) gives the surface time to toast and set while the center barely registers the heat. Pair that cold center with a pan hot enough to brown the sesame in 30 to 45 seconds and the window is forgiving; pair a room-temperature block with a merely-warm pan and the heat reaches the center before the crust is set, and you get gray, sad, fully-cooked tuna with a pale crust.

  The sesame is structural, not decorative. Beyond flavor, the sesame coat does real work: it insulates the fish surface slightly, browns visibly as a doneness cue, and gives the knife something to bite through so the slices hold their shape. Toast the white seeds (the black ones are along for color and do not read by color anyway), because raw sesame on the crust tastes flatly grassy against the toasted note the sear gives the white seeds. Press the coat on firmly, a loose coat sheds into the pan and burns in the oil.

  Slicing and the grain. Tuna muscle has a clear grain, the parallel lines of myomere segments. Slice across it, perpendicular to those lines, so each slice is short-fibered and tender on the tongue; a with-grain slice reads stringy and chewy even on raw fish. A sharp knife and a single confident pull-stroke per slice keep the cut face clean and the ruby center crisp-edged; sawing tears the soft flesh and muddies the cross-section that is the entire visual point of the dish.

  Holding and leftovers. Seared rare tuna is a serve-immediately dish. As the block sits, residual heat keeps migrating into the center and the cool-ruby band shrinks, so a plate that was perfect at the cutting board is medium by the time stragglers arrive. There is no good reheat. Leftover seared tuna is best repurposed cold the next day, sliced into a salad or a rice bowl, where the now-fully-set texture is an asset rather than a disappointment.
