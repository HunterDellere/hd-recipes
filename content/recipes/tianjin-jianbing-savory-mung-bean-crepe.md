desc: 'A paper-thin mung-bean-and-millet crepe spread with a wooden T-bar on a hot round griddle, cracked with one egg and seeded with black and white sesame while the batter is still wet, flipped once, then painted with tianmianjiang (sweet bean sauce), fermented bean curd sauce, and a brushed-on roasted chili paste before a sheet of bao cui (thin shatter-crisp wheat cracker) is laid across the center and the whole disc is folded into a hand-held rectangle. source:
  name: 'Tianjin street-vendor canon; the Hebei Lu and Nanshi (南市) breakfast tradition'
  note: 'Jianbing guozi (煎饼馃子) is the Tianjin street breakfast par excellence and a dish with an unusually defended canon: in 2018 the Tianjin Catering Association published a non-binding standard that specifies mung bean flour as the dominant grain, a single egg, sesame seeds, scallion, cilantro, tianmianjiang and fermented bean curd as the sauce base, and bao cui (薄脆, the thin fried wheat cracker) as the canonical crispy filling. The Beijing-style variant substitutes you tiao (the puffy fried dough stick) for the bao cui and is widely available across northern China but is treated by Tianjin purists as a different dish. This recipe follows the Tianjin canon: mung bean and millet flour blend, bao cui, three sauces.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The batter needs a 30 minute hydration rest before the first crepe goes onto the griddle. Mung bean flour is gluten-free and the starches must fully hydrate or the crepe cracks under the T-bar sweep. Build the batter first, then use the rest window to make the three sauces and prep the egg-aromatic mise.'
  - 'A T-bar (wooden batter spreader) or a flat-backed offset spatula is structural for the sweep. The single continuous spiral motion is what produces the 28 cm paper-thin disc; a ladle and a tilt-the-pan technique produces a thicker uneven crepe that cannot hold the egg layer.'
  - 'Bao cui must be at room temperature when it meets the hot crepe. If yours is frozen, defrost on the counter for 10 minutes during the batter rest; cold bao cui shatters on contact with the hot disc and the wrap loses its glass-shatter crunch.'
  - 'Eat each crepe within 90 seconds of folding. The entire dish is engineered around freshness; the bao cui softens from steam within 2 to 3 minutes and the egg layer cools quickly. Cook one crepe, plate it, eat it, then cook the second.'

steps:
  - text: 'Build the batter, rest, and the sauces in parallel. In a medium bowl, whisk together the 80 g / ½ cup + 2 tbsp mung bean flour, 40 g / ¼ cup + 1 tbsp millet flour, 30 g / 3 tbsp + 1 tsp AP flour, 2 g / ⅓ tsp fine sea salt, and 1 g / ¼ tsp five-spice powder. Pour in the 300 ml / 1¼ cups cool water in a slow stream while whisking; the batter should pour like heavy cream off the whisk, with no lumps. Rest covered at room temperature for 30 minutes. This hydration rest is non-negotiable: mung bean flour is gluten-free and the starches need time to fully hydrate, otherwise the crepe cracks under the T-bar sweep. While the batter rests, build the sauces.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Build the three sauces. In a small bowl, whisk together the 40 g / 2½ tbsp tianmianjiang, the 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, and the 4 g / 1 tsp granulated sugar until smooth and brushable; this is the foundational sweet-savory sauce. In a second small bowl, mash the 15 g / 1 tbsp red fermented bean curd with the back of a spoon until paste-smooth (no chunks); this is the funk-and-glutamate sauce. The 20 g / 1½ tbsp jianbing chili paste lives in its own bowl with a small brush. Three small bowls, three small brushes (or one brush rinsed between sauces). Rest the sauces 10 minutes so the sugar dissolves into the tianmianjiang and the furu hydrates from its mashing.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Prep the egg-and-aromatic mise. In one wide ramekin, beat the 100 g / 2 eggs lightly with a fork, just enough to break the yolks; do not whip in air. Combine the 8 g / 2 tsp white sesame seeds and 4 g / 1 tsp black sesame seeds in a small dish. Combine the 30 g / 2 scallions, finely chopped, and the 12 g / ¼ cup cilantro, finely chopped, in a second small dish. Have the 2 sheets of bao cui ready on a plate; if frozen, defrost on the counter for 10 minutes so they reach room temperature before they meet the hot crepe. Have a small bowl with the 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil and a folded paper towel for wiping the griddle between crepes.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Heat the griddle. Place a 28 cm flat carbon steel or non-stick crepe pan (or a wide cast iron griddle, or the bottom of a 28 cm carbon steel wok inverted on the burner) over medium heat for 3 minutes. The cue for ready: a drop of water flicked onto the surface skitters and evaporates in under 2 seconds, with no aggressive smoke. Surface temperature 180 to 200°C / 360 to 390°F. Fold the paper towel, dip lightly in the 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil, and wipe the surface in two passes; the pan should look damp, not glossy. A pool of oil will fry the crepe rather than letting it set thin.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Pour and sweep the first crepe. Stir the batter from the bottom (the heavy flours will have settled), pour a 200 ml / scant 1 cup ladle of batter into the center of the hot griddle, and immediately use a wooden T-bar (or the flat back of a wide offset spatula or a sushi-rice paddle) to sweep the batter from the center outward in a single continuous spiral, ending at the edge of the disc. The motion is one continuous, even pressure sweep; pause and the crepe gets thick spots and tears. Aim for a 28 cm disc, paper-thin (1.5 to 2 mm), with the surface losing its glossy wet shine and turning matte within 15 seconds of the sweep finishing. If holes appear during the sweep, drip a few drops of batter into them and sweep across once more; do not pour a fresh ladle on top.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Crack the egg and seed-aromatic the surface (the wet-egg phase). Within 5 seconds of the crepe turning matte, crack 1 egg (50 g) directly into the center of the disc and use the back of a soup spoon or the T-bar edge to sweep the yolk and white outward in a thin even layer across roughly two-thirds of the crepe surface, leaving a 2 cm bare-crepe rim at the edge. The egg must hit the crepe while the crepe is still hot and the egg-protein hits a 180°C / 360°F surface immediately so it sets in seconds rather than pooling and weeping. Immediately scatter half the sesame seeds, half the scallion, and half the cilantro evenly across the egg layer while the egg is still glossy-wet on the surface; everything sticks to the setting egg, becoming a single embedded layer.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Flip. After the egg surface has set from glossy to matte (about 30 seconds; the underside of the crepe is now mid-golden and lifting cleanly from the pan), slide a wide flat spatula fully under the disc and flip the entire crepe in one motion. The egg-and-aromatic side is now down on the pan, finishing in direct contact with the heat for 20 seconds; the original crepe side, now facing up, is the sauce-receiving side. Do not press; the goal is to lightly brown the egg surface, not to flatten the disc.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Paint the sauces and lay the bao cui. Working fast across the now-up-facing crepe surface: brush a thin even layer of the tianmianjiang-sesame-oil-sugar sauce across the central two-thirds of the disc (use about half the sauce, roughly 22 g / 1½ tbsp per crepe). Dab a teaspoon of the mashed furu sauce in three dots across the same area and smear lightly. Brush a small amount of the jianbing chili paste in a single 8 cm stripe down the center; this is the heat axis the diner bites into mid-wrap. The sauce should be applied generously enough to taste at every bite but not so heavy that it pools; the bao cui will absorb the surface tackiness. Lay 1 sheet of bao cui (about 30 g) flat across the center of the crepe, oriented with the long edge running across the disc.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Fold the wrap and serve immediately. Fold the left and right thirds of the crepe inward over the 30 g / 1 sheet bao cui, then fold the top and bottom edges inward to make a 12 cm × 8 cm rectangle packet. The packet should be tight enough to hold but loose enough that the bao cui inside does not crack from compression. Slide the packet onto a cutting board and cut diagonally in half with a sharp knife (or serve whole, eaten by hand). Plate immediately; the entire dish is engineered to be eaten within 90 seconds of folding, while the bao cui is still glass-shatter and the egg layer is still warm. Wipe the griddle with the oiled paper towel and cook the second crepe.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Re-oil and repeat for the second crepe. Re-oil the griddle with a single wipe of the oiled paper towel. Stir the remaining batter (it will have settled and thickened slightly during the first cook; whisk it back to pouring consistency, adding 1 to 2 tsp water if needed). Ladle, sweep, crack the second 50 g / 1 egg, scatter the remaining sesame seeds, scallion, and cilantro, flip, sauce, lay the second 30 g / 1 sheet bao cui, fold. Total cook time per crepe is roughly 3 minutes from pour to plate; the two crepes are easier to cook back-to-back than to attempt in parallel on two pans.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why this specific flour blend. The Tianjin canon names mung bean flour as the dominant grain because mung bean is high in amylose-rich starch and very low in protein, which produces a crepe that sets fast, stays tender, and has the distinctive pale-greenish-yellow color that distinguishes a Tianjin jianbing on sight from a Shandong or Beijing version. Millet flour layers a brighter yellow color and a faint cereal-sweetness that bridges the bean and the egg. A small fraction of AP wheat flour (around 17% of total flour weight here) is the structural concession of the home kitchen: without it, the gluten-free mung-millet batter cracks under the T-bar sweep and produces a ragged disc rather than a continuous one. Pure mung-millet works on a properly seasoned commercial saji griddle at very high heat but is unreliable at home.

  The T-bar sweep is the technique that defines the dish. A jianbing crepe is not poured to thickness; it is poured-then-swept, and the sweeping action with a wooden T (the guazi, T字刮子) thins the batter to 1.5 to 2 mm in one continuous motion. The home cook substitute is the flat back of a wide offset spatula, a small wooden sushi-rice paddle, or even the back of a wide soup ladle: any flat-faced tool wide enough to cover a 6 cm arc of batter, swept in a single continuous spiral from center to edge. The motion must be continuous. Pausing or lifting mid-sweep produces thick spots where the batter pools, and those spots will be rubbery rather than lacy at finish.

  The wet-egg phase is the dish's signature texture. Where a Western crepe is cooked, then folded around fillings, a jianbing has the egg cracked directly onto the still-wet batter and spread immediately, so the egg and the crepe set together as a bonded two-layer fabric. The sesame seeds, scallion, and cilantro are scattered onto the wet egg surface and are physically locked into the egg as it sets. The flip then briefly crisps the egg-and-aromatic side on the griddle for 20 seconds; this is what produces the slight lace-edge browning that street vendors achieve and home cooks usually undershoot. If your jianbing reads pale on the egg side, you are flipping too soon or your griddle is not hot enough.

  Why three sauces, not one. The Tianjin sauce build is genuinely a three-axis system. Tianmianjiang carries the fermented-wheat sweetness and is the foundational umami; fermented bean curd (furu) layers a yeasty, almost cheese-funky glutamate kick; the chili paste contributes the heat and the garlic aromatic. Each is brushed on its own area of the crepe rather than premixed, because the diner experiences them sequentially across the bite. A jianbing made with only tianmianjiang is one-note; with all three, the wrap reads layered and dimensional. The street vendors of Hebei Lu in Tianjin will adjust the three sauces by hand-signal from the customer (more spice, less salt, no furu) and the resulting personalized wrap is part of the dish's appeal.

  The bao cui timing problem. Bao cui must stay shatter-crisp at the moment of biting; it has an effective hot-window of about 90 seconds from when the folded wrap leaves the griddle. This is the one element a home cook cannot stockpile. Fresh-fried bao cui (homemade or bought from a Tianjin-style takeout that fries to order) is the gold standard. Frozen supermarket bao cui works but loses crisp within 4 to 6 hours of opening; bring it to room temperature on the counter for 10 minutes before assembly, never microwave it. If you cannot get bao cui, fried wonton wrappers are the closest home substitute; you tiao moves the wrap into Beijing-style territory and is its own legitimate dish but is not Tianjin canon.

  Eat immediately. A jianbing held longer than 5 minutes loses what makes it a jianbing: the bao cui sogs from the sauces, the crepe steams limp from its own trapped heat, and the egg layer cools to leather. The dish is engineered for the 90-second window between fold and bite. If you must hold (rare; jianbing is a cook-and-eat dish), wrap loosely in parchment, never foil, and never in an insulated bag; the trapped steam is the death of the texture. Better to cook the second crepe while the first eater is still on their first bite.

  Beverage and accompaniment. Jianbing is breakfast street food and the canonical accompaniments are warm soy milk (douxiang, 豆浆), a small bowl of mung bean porridge, or a cup of chrysanthemum tea. A glass of cold Tsingtao or a cup of black tea works for non-breakfast service. The dish does not want coffee; the bitter astringency clashes with the sweet bean sauce. Pickled mustard greens or a small dish of cold zhacai on the side is a Tianjin home-kitchen pairing that cuts the richness.
