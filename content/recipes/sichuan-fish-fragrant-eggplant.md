steps:
  - text: 'Cut the 600 g eggplant into 1.5 cm × 6 cm batons. Do not salt, Chinese and Japanese eggplant cultivars are already low in water and salting tightens the texture too much. The dry-fry technique handles moisture removal from the heat direction.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Mix the fish-fragrant sauce. In a small bowl, whisk together the 20 ml / 1½ tbsp soy sauce, 20 ml / 1½ tbsp Chinkiang vinegar, 18 g / 1½ tbsp sugar, 5 g / ½ tbsp cornstarch, and 60 ml / ¼ cup water until the sugar and cornstarch dissolve. The sauce is going in as a single pour; pre-mixing prevents a scramble during the high-heat finish.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Dry-fry the eggplant. Heat a wok over high heat until smoking lightly. Add the 30 ml / 2 tbsp oil and the eggplant batons. Stir-fry continuously for 4 to 5 minutes; the eggplant will progress through three stages, releasing steam, collapsing and shrinking by 30 to 40%, then browning at the cut faces. Pull when the cut faces are golden-brown, the structure has collapsed inward, and the surface is dry to the touch. Transfer to a plate.'
    technique: 'dry-frying-eggplant'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Render the aromatic base. Wipe the wok clean and return to medium heat (140 to 160°C / 285 to 320°F). Add the 30 ml / 2 tbsp oil and the 25 g doubanjiang. Cook 90 seconds, breaking up the paste with a spatula, until the oil turns brilliant red and smells distinctly of chili-bean paste. Add the 15 g minced ginger, 20 g minced garlic, and 30 g chopped scallion whites. Cook 30 seconds until fragrant; do not exceed medium heat or the aromatics scorch.'
    technique: 'red-oil-rendering'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Build the dish. Return the dry-fried eggplant to the wok. Toss for 30 seconds in the rendered red oil to coat. Whisk the prepared sauce mixture once (the 5 g cornstarch settles) and pour around the edge of the wok. Toss continuously for 60 to 90 seconds; the sauce will boil, thicken, and coat the eggplant. Pull when the sauce has thickened to a glossy coat that clings to each piece, with no pooled liquid at the bottom of the wok.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish. Pull off heat. Add the 20 g reserved scallion greens and 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil. Toss once. Transfer to a serving plate and serve immediately with steamed jasmine rice.'
    time_min: 1

modifications:
  - for: 'cultivar'
    to: 'globe eggplant (American / Mediterranean)'
    how: 'Globe eggplant has 50% more water than Chinese eggplant and the cell structure is spongier. Cut into smaller batons (1 cm × 5 cm), salt at 1.5% by weight (9 g salt per 600 g eggplant), let stand 30 minutes, blot completely dry. Then proceed with dry-frying for 6 to 8 minutes (longer than Chinese eggplant). The salt-and-press is the move that lets globe eggplant approach the texture Chinese eggplant gets without it.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'cultivar'
    to: 'Italian or fairy-tale eggplant'
    how: 'Cut into 1 cm × 5 cm batons (smaller than Chinese cultivars to compensate for slightly higher water). Salting is optional; if you have time, salt at 1% by weight (6 g per 600 g) for 20 minutes and blot. Dry-fry 5 to 6 minutes. The end result is closest to Chinese-eggplant texture of any non-Asian cultivar.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  The "fish-fragrant" name. Yu xiang (鱼香, "fish fragrance") is a Sichuan flavor category, not an ingredient list. The flavor profile, sweet, sour, hot, salty, with strong ginger-garlic-scallion aromatics, was historically used to season fish in inland Sichuan where fresh ocean fish was unavailable; the seasoning style took the name. The flavor profile is now applied to many ingredients (eggplant, pork shreds, tofu, even chicken), and the dish is named "yu xiang [main ingredient]." None of the dishes contain fish; "yu xiang qie zi" translates roughly as "fish-fragrant-style eggplant," not "eggplant with fish."

  The sweet-sour-spicy balance is the spine. Yu xiang is one of the named "compound flavors" of Sichuan cooking (along with mala, lychee, strange-flavor, and others), and the balance is precise: roughly equal parts sugar and vinegar by weight, with the doubanjiang providing the chili and salt foundation, and the ginger-garlic-scallion providing the aromatic top. Adjust one variable at a time when troubleshooting: if the dish reads flat-sweet, more vinegar; if it reads harsh-sour, more sugar; if it reads salty-without-flavor, more aromatics. Restaurant versions sometimes go heavier on the sugar (up to 25 g) to make the dish more crowd-pleasing; the home version above hits a more balanced register.

  Why not salt the Chinese eggplant. Salting drives water out osmotically; dry-frying drives water out through evaporation. Doing both produces dehydrated, chewy, tough eggplant. Chinese eggplant has low enough starting water that dry-frying alone produces the right structural collapse. Globe eggplant, in contrast, has so much water that dry-frying alone struggles to drive enough out before the surface burns; salt-and-press is the necessary preparation step for globe eggplant in this dish.

  The dry-fry sequence vs the deep-fry shortcut. Many restaurant versions of yu xiang qie zi deep-fry the eggplant in 2 to 3 cm of oil at 175°C / 350°F for 90 seconds, which collapses the cells faster than dry-frying does. This is the easier-but-greasier version; the eggplant absorbs significantly more oil even with the deep-fry technique because the contact time is short but the oil contact is total. The dry-fry technique above produces less-greasy results and is the home-cooking standard. Choose deep-fry for restaurant-style results; choose dry-fry for the cleaner, more controllable outcome.

  Eating mechanics. Like most Sichuan stir-fries, this is part of a multi-dish meal: serve with steamed white rice and at least one non-spicy side (a stir-fried green vegetable, a clear soup). Each diner takes pieces of eggplant onto their rice; the sauce-coated eggplant flavors the rice. The dish is best within 10 minutes of plating; sitting longer, the cornstarch-thickened sauce can break and the eggplant goes soggy from condensation. Reheats acceptably the next day for personal meals.
