source:
  name: 'Lombard canon (Milan)'
  note: 'The dish is traditionally attributed to a 16th-century Belgian stained-glass artisan working in Milan who allegedly added saffron (a stained-glass pigment) to a wedding rice; the historical record is unreliable but the saffron-rice tradition is firmly Milanese by the 18th century. The structural elements are non-negotiable: short-grain high-amylopectin rice (carnaroli is the modern reference, vialone nano is the traditional Veneto-Lombardy alternative; arborio is the most common but reads less refined), a bone-marrow-and-onion soffritto in butter (the marrow is the canonical fat and provides the unctuous mouthfeel; pure butter is the accepted modern substitute), saffron bloomed in hot stock, dry white wine deglaze, hot stock added one ladle at a time with constant stirring, and the off-heat mantecatura (cold butter and parmigiano whisked vigorously to a glossy emulsion). The all-uppercase rule: never use parboiled rice, never use cold stock, never add the cheese while still on heat (it scrambles).'

before_you_start:
  - 'Get the chicken stock hot and held at a bare simmer in a separate saucepan before starting the rice; cold stock added to a hot pan drops the temperature and produces gummy rice.'
  - 'The mantecatura (final butter-and-cheese emulsion) must happen off-heat with cold butter; have 60 g of cold butter cubed and the parmigiano grated before the rice hits the al dente mark, or the timing falls apart.'
  - 'Plan to serve risotto immediately; the dish is at its peak texture for 3 minutes after plating, then begins to thicken and lose the canonical all''onda (wave-like) consistency.'

steps:
  - text: 'Set the 1200 ml / 5 cups chicken stock in a saucepan over low heat; bring to a bare simmer and hold there for the entire cook. In a small bowl, ladle out about 120 ml / ½ cup of the hot stock; add the 0.4 g / ½ tsp saffron threads. Steep 10 minutes until the stock is deeply orange-red and the threads have surrendered their pigment and aroma. Keep the saffron-infused stock at the side; it goes in midway through the cook.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'In a wide heavy-bottomed saucepan or sauté pan (28 cm / 11 in is ideal, the wide surface allows even heating and evaporation) over medium-low heat, melt the 40 g / 3 tbsp butter. Add the 80 g / ½ onion (finely diced); sweat 5 to 6 minutes, stirring frequently, until completely translucent and soft but not browned. Browning the onion is the most common failure mode; the soffritto for risotto is sweet, never colored.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Add the 320 g / 1½ cups carnaroli rice all at once. Stir to coat every grain in the butter and onion. Toast the rice 2 to 3 minutes, stirring constantly, until the grains are translucent at the edges with an opaque white core (the visual cue is the inner core staying white while the outer husk goes glassy). This toasting step (la tostatura) is structural; it sets the surface starch and ensures the rice will cook to al dente rather than collapsing to mush.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pour in the 120 ml / ½ cup white wine all at once; stir constantly until the wine has been fully absorbed and the alcohol has evaporated (about 90 seconds; the smell shifts from raw wine to a clean tang). Season with the 3 g / ½ tsp salt.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Begin adding the simmering plain chicken stock one ladle (about 120 ml / ½ cup) at a time, stirring frequently with a wooden spoon. Wait until each ladle is almost fully absorbed before adding the next; the rice should always be visible at the surface but never dry. After about 8 minutes of this (about 4 ladles), add the saffron-infused stock all at once. The rice should turn a vibrant orange-yellow.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Continue adding the remaining stock one ladle at a time, stirring frequently, for another 8 to 10 minutes. The total cook time from when the rice hit the pan is 18 to 20 minutes; taste a grain at the 16-minute mark. The canonical risotto is al dente: each grain has a small firm core (about 1 mm) at the center but yields under tooth pressure, and the surrounding starch has released into a creamy suspension. If you run out of stock before the rice is al dente, switch to hot water for the last addition.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'When the rice is at al dente, the consistency should be looser than the final plate; the rice continues to absorb liquid after heat is off, so pull while the texture is still all''onda (wavy, just-pouring rather than mounding stiffly). Pull the pan off the heat. Wait 30 seconds for the temperature to drop slightly below the boil; this prevents the butter from breaking on contact.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'The mantecatura (the off-heat butter-and-cheese emulsion): add the 60 g / 4 tbsp cold cubed butter and the 60 g / ¾ cup grated parmigiano all at once. Stir vigorously with a wooden spoon for 60 to 90 seconds, beating the rice firmly against the side of the pan to mechanically emulsify the butter and cheese into the residual stock. The texture transforms: the loose risotto thickens into a glossy creamy sauce that coats every grain, the surface develops a slight sheen, and the spoon leaves a soft trail when drawn through. Add the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. Cover the pan and let stand 2 minutes; the rest lets the texture stabilize.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Plate immediately into warm shallow bowls. Bang the bottom of each bowl on the counter once: the canonical Milanese service is the risotto spread out flat to the edge of the bowl in a thin layer (about 1 cm deep), not mounded in the center. The all''onda texture should make a soft wave when the plate is tilted slightly. Serve with no additional garnish; the dish is structurally complete.'
    time_min: 1

substitutions:
  - for: 'carnaroli rice (or vialone nano)'
    use: 'arborio'
    note: 'Carnaroli is the modern restaurant reference for its high amylopectin content and resistance to overcooking; vialone nano is the traditional Lombardy-Veneto alternative with a slightly smaller grain. Arborio is the most widely available risotto rice in the US and produces a slightly more starchy, slightly less precisely al dente risotto; use it freely if carnaroli is unavailable. Do not use long-grain rice, parboiled rice, or sushi rice; the amylose-amylopectin ratio is wrong for the canonical creamy texture.'
  - for: 'chicken stock'
    use: 'homemade veal stock, vegetable stock, or a 50/50 blend of chicken stock and water'
    note: 'Chicken stock is the most common modern Milanese choice; traditional preparations sometimes use veal stock for greater depth. Vegetable stock produces a lighter, more delicate risotto that reads as a vegetarian preparation. A 50/50 blend of chicken stock and water is acceptable if your stock is heavily concentrated. Do not use bouillon cubes; the salt level is wrong and the dish will read as a salty crash.'
  - for: 'saffron threads'

    note: 'Saffron threads are the canonical choice for visible pigment release and flavor; high-quality powdered saffron (purchased from a reputable Iranian or Spanish supplier, not a supermarket spice jar that has degraded) works in a pinch but the flavor is more diffuse and less bright. Do not substitute turmeric; it provides color but no flavor and the dish will read as wrong.'

notes: |
  Risotto is a constant-stirring dish for a structural reason: each stir drags amylose starch off the surface of the rice grain into the cooking liquid, where it suspends and thickens the broth into the creamy sauce that defines the dish. A risotto cooked without stirring (the so-called "lazy risotto" technique that has become trendy in some modern kitchens) produces a different dish: the grains remain dry and the surrounding liquid is thin. The constant-stirring rule is non-negotiable for the canonical mantecatura emulsion at the finish.

  The mantecatura is the structural moment that separates risotto from "rice in stock." The off-heat addition of cold butter and grated parmigiano is whisked vigorously into the residual hot liquid; the butter melts and the cheese melts, and the residual starch in the broth emulsifies the fat into a glossy cream. Three failure modes: (1) adding the butter while still over heat (the butter breaks and pools as oil), (2) using room-temperature butter (the butter melts too fast and does not emulsify), and (3) under-stirring at the mantecatura (the cheese clumps and the sauce reads as broken). The 60 to 90 seconds of vigorous wooden-spoon beating is the moment the dish comes together; do not rush it.

  The all''onda texture (literally "on the wave") is the visual cue for done risotto. The plated rice should pour slowly when the bowl is tilted, leaving a soft trail rather than mounding stiffly or running like soup. Risotto thickens dramatically as it cools; pulling the pan at the moment the consistency is correct on the heat produces a too-stiff plate by the time it reaches the table. Pull while the texture is still slightly looser than your target; the 2-minute covered rest at the end of the mantecatura tightens the texture into the canonical all''onda.

  Saffron is the structural flavor, not just the color. Cheap supermarket saffron that has degraded in the spice jar produces yellow stain but no aroma; the dish reads as bland rice in yellow stock. Spend on real Iranian or Spanish saffron from a reliable spice merchant (Burlap and Barrel, Diaspora Co., or a traditional Middle Eastern grocery); the difference is the dish.

  Storage. Risotto does not store well; the texture stiffens dramatically in the refrigerator and reheats to a gluey paste. The canonical leftover preparation is risotto al salto (see modifications), in which the cold risotto is pressed into a pancake and pan-fried to a crispy crust; this is the structurally correct second-day form. Do not microwave leftover risotto; the rice continues to absorb moisture and the result is mush.
