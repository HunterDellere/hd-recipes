steps:
  - text: 'Heat oven to 220°C / 425°F with a rack in the middle. Spread the 2000 g beef bones on a heavy roasting pan in a single layer. Roast 30 minutes, then turn each bone with tongs and continue roasting 25 to 30 minutes more, until the bones are deeply mahogany-brown all over and any clinging meat has crisped. The roast color is the entire flavor signature of brown stock; pale-roasted bones produce pale stock.'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'Add the carrots and celery (3 carrots, 3 celery, in large chunks) and the halved garlic head to the roasting pan in the last 25 minutes. Toss with the rendered fat and continue roasting until the vegetables char at the edges. In the last 15 minutes, brush the bones with the 30 g / 2 tbsp tomato paste; the paste roasts and contributes the deep red-brown color and umami that distinguish a great brown stock from a competent one.'
    time_min: 0
  - text: 'Char the onions separately. While the bones finish, halve the 2 onions cross-wise (cut-side flat). Heat a dry heavy skillet over medium-high heat and place the onions cut-side down without oil. Cook 8 to 10 minutes without moving until the cut faces are deeply blackened and slightly carbonized. The blackened-onion technique borrows from Vietnamese pho and Korean seolleongtang; even in classical French stock-making, charred onion deepens the broth in a way unavailable from raw onion.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Transfer to stockpot. Lift the bones and roasted vegetables into an 8-quart stockpot with tongs. Add the 2 charred onion halves. Pour off the rendered fat from the roasting pan (reserve for cooking; this is excellent rendered beef fat) and place the pan over medium heat on the stovetop. Pour in the 250 ml / 1 cup red wine and scrape up the dark fond stuck to the pan. Boil 2 minutes to cook off most of the alcohol, then pour the wine deglaze into the stockpot.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add water and aromatics. Pour the 4000 ml / 16 cups cold water over the bones and vegetables in the stockpot. Add the 3 bay leaves, 8 g / 1½ tsp peppercorns, 5 thyme sprigs, and 10 g parsley stems. Bring to the threshold of a simmer over medium heat (about 90°C / 195°F). Skim any gray foam (there will be less than chicken or pork stocks because the roast pre-coagulated most surface proteins).'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Simmer 8 to 10 hours. Adjust heat to a bare tremor of a simmer; cover loosely with the lid askew. Beef bones release their gelatin slowly and the longer simmer is what produces the body that defines a good beef stock. Top up water as needed to keep the 2000 g bones submerged.'
    time_min: 480
  - text: 'Strain. Lift bones and vegetables out with tongs. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a second pot, then again through cheesecloth.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Cool quickly and defat. Divide between wide shallow pans in an ice bath, stirring every 5 minutes. Refrigerate. Once cold, the fat layer on top will be substantial and amber; lift it off in pieces and reserve. The defatted stock below should be deep amber-brown, glossy, with noticeable body that turns to soft gel in the fridge.'
    time_min: 35

modifications:
  - for: 'cuisine'
    to: 'pho-style beef broth (Vietnamese)'
    how: 'Skip the tomato paste. Use the same bones (heavily skewed to oxtail and beef shank if available; trim 200 g of fatty short rib for body). Char the onion and add a 75 g knob of charred ginger (held whole over open flame or under broiler 8 minutes until the skin blackens, then peel partially). Skip the carrots, celery, and Western herbs entirely. Add to the simmer: 4 star anise, 1 cinnamon stick (8 cm), 4 cloves, 2 g / 1 tsp coriander seed, 2 g / 1 tsp fennel seed, 30 g rock sugar, 30 ml fish sauce. Simmer 6 to 8 hours. The result is the beef broth that goes under pho bo; aromatic, sweet-spice-driven, structurally distinct from European brown stock despite using the same bones.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'cuisine'
    to: 'Korean seolleongtang (milky beef bone broth)'
    how: 'Skip roasting the bones entirely; instead soak the bones in cold water 2 hours, drain, blanch 5 minutes in boiling water, drain, and rinse. Cover with fresh cold water and bring to a rolling boil; maintain a hard rolling boil for 8 to 10 hours, topping up water. The result is the cream-white milky broth that goes under seolleongtang and gomtang. Skip Western mirepoix and aromatic herbs. Optionally add 200 g daikon and a few crushed garlic cloves in the last 2 hours. Salt is added at the table by the diner, not in the broth.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'time-shortcut'
    to: 'pressure-cooker version'
    how: 'Roast the bones and vegetables as written (no shortcut on the roast; this is the entire flavor signature). Then transfer to an Instant Pot with the same liquid quantities. High pressure 4 hours, natural release. The broth will be slightly cloudier than the long simmer; flavor and gelatin extraction are 90% of the full version.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Brown vs white beef stock. Western tradition splits stocks into white (un-roasted bones, lighter color) and brown (roasted bones, darker color). For chicken and veal, both versions exist and have specific applications. For beef, the brown version dominates so completely that "beef stock" without further qualification means brown. White beef stock exists in some Italian and Spanish traditions but is rarely worth the effort versus chicken stock for similar use cases.

  Pho note. Vietnamese pho broth is technically distinct from Western beef stock despite the same starting bones. The cuisine difference shows in the aromatic palette (Asian sweet spices vs European mirepoix and herbs), the cook time (6 to 8 hours pho vs 8 to 10 hours brown stock), and the seasoning approach (fish sauce and rock sugar in the broth vs unseasoned for stock). Either can be made on the same kitchen day with overlapping prep; choose by intended use.

  Marrow bones note. Marrow bones contribute both flavor and the unique buttery texture of melted bone marrow. Save 2 to 3 marrow bones from the roast and serve them on toast as an appetizer (canonical bistro presentation: roasted marrow bones, parsley salad, sea salt, sourdough toast); the rest of the bones go into the broth. The two preparations work together, the marrow course opens the meal, the broth-based main follows.

  Storage. Refrigerated, defatted: 5 to 7 days (beef stock keeps slightly longer than chicken because of the higher fat-and-gelatin content). Frozen in 500 ml containers: 8 months. Frozen in ice cube trays for sauce-work portions: 6 months in zip-top bags after the cubes set. Reheated stock should be brought to a simmer briefly to kill bacteria before use; reduce as needed for sauce-work (a typical demi-glace reduces 1 L of brown stock to about 250 ml).

  Use cases. The neutral roasted version is the foundation under: French braises (boeuf bourguignon, daube provençale, short ribs braised in red wine); French sauces (espagnole, demi-glace, sauce bordelaise); French onion soup; pot roasts and beef stew; risotto al barolo; lasagna and ragu (replaces water in the simmer); deglaze for beef pan sauces. The pho version is for pho bo specifically. The seolleongtang version is for Korean milky-broth dishes; it does not substitute well for the other two.
