steps:
  - text: 'In a small bowl, whisk the 20 ml / 1½ tbsp light soy, 8 ml / ½ tbsp Chinkiang vinegar, 12 g / 1 tbsp granulated sugar, 2 g / ¼ tsp kosher salt, and 2 g / 1 tsp Sichuan peppercorns if using until the sugar dissolves. The glaze is going onto hot peanuts in a single pour; pre-mixing prevents a scramble at the critical moment.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Heat the 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil and the 200 g raw peanuts together in a wide, heavy skillet over medium-low. Cold-oil-and-cold-peanut start is the rule: this lets the peanuts toast through gradually rather than scorching the outside before the inside cooks. Stir every 60 seconds for the first 5 minutes, then every 30 seconds after that.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Watch the peanuts closely from minute 8 onward. They are done when the skins darken to a deep mahogany, the kernels inside have gone pale gold, and the smell shifts from raw-grassy to deeply roasted (peanut-skin volatiles and pyrazines). The textural cue is sound: a finished peanut snaps cleanly when bitten, an under-toasted one is starchy and chewy. Total cook time 9 to 11 minutes; pull a peanut at minute 9 and bite-test.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Pull the pan off the heat. Pour the glaze in a single pour around the edge of the pan; it will hiss aggressively as the soy and vinegar hit the residual heat. Toss continuously with two wooden spatulas for 30 seconds, until the glaze reduces visibly and lacquers onto every peanut as a glossy thin coat. Pull immediately when the bottom of the pan is dry and the peanuts are coated; another 15 seconds of heat will burn the sugar.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Spread the peanuts in a single layer on a sheet pan and let cool to room temperature, 15 to 20 minutes. The glaze sets brittle as it cools and the peanuts go fully crisp. Once cool, store in an airtight glass jar at room temperature; they keep 3 weeks (the glaze keeps moisture out of the kernel).'
    time_min: 20

notes: |
  How to use. A small dish of soy-glazed peanuts on the table beside a bowl of plain congee, where each spoonful gets two or three peanuts for crunch and salt-savory contrast against the smooth porridge. Folded into rice bowls. Tossed into cold cucumber salads (smashed cucumber, peanut, vinegar, sesame oil). Crushed and sprinkled over noodles. Eaten by themselves with a cold beer.

  The cold-oil-and-cold-peanut start is non-negotiable. Hot oil into cold peanuts scorches the skins before the kernels cook through, producing a peanut that tastes burnt-bitter on the outside and starchy-raw at the center. Cold-oil start lets the temperature climb gradually and the toast develop evenly. Same principle as the cold-oil shallot fry, lifted to a different ingredient.

  Why the off-heat glaze. The soy and vinegar contain enough water and sugar that hitting the pan over heat would drive the sugar straight to caramel and burn before the peanuts coat. Off-heat residual-heat glazing reduces the liquid just enough to lacquer without scorching. The cue is bottom-of-pan-dry-and-glossy, not bottom-of-pan-bubbling.
