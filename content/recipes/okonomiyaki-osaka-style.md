source:
  name: 'Osaka okonomiyaki tradition (the home and street-food canonical form)'
  note: 'Okonomiyaki (お好み焼き, "grilled how you like") has two distinct schools. Osaka-style mixes all ingredients into the batter and pan-fries as a single thick pancake; Hiroshima-style builds the layers (batter, cabbage, pork, noodles, egg) stacked separately on the griddle. The Osaka form is more common in home cooking and outside Japan; this recipe is the canonical Osaka direction. The dish became popular post-WWII as a way to stretch limited rice with cabbage and flour; teppanyaki-style restaurants featuring tableside okonomiyaki griddles are common throughout Kansai (Osaka, Kobe, Kyoto).'

before_you_start:
  - 'Source nagaimo (Japanese mountain yam, also called yamaimo). The grated nagaimo is the textural secret of proper okonomiyaki: its mucilaginous starch lightens the batter so the interior reads fluffy-tender rather than dense-doughy. Find at Japanese, Korean, or some Chinese groceries (long brown cylinder, white interior). Substitute 1:1 with peeled grated daikon plus a pinch of cornstarch if absolutely necessary, but the dish reads structurally different without nagaimo.'
  - 'Buy Otafuku brand okonomiyaki sauce, Kewpie mayonnaise, katsuobushi bonito flakes, beni shoga (red pickled ginger), and aonori (powdered green seaweed) at a Japanese grocery. These are the canonical toppings and substitutes do not produce the same dish. All are pantry items that last months.'
  - 'Cook one pancake at a time in a 24 cm nonstick or well-seasoned cast-iron skillet. Trying to flip a 30 cm pancake at home is the most common failure; a smaller pancake flips cleanly with a wide spatula.'

steps:
  - text: 'Mix the batter base. In a large bowl, whisk together the 150 g all-purpose flour and 180 g cold dashi until smooth, no lumps. Whisk in the 100 g grated nagaimo (the mixture will go gluey and viscous from the yam mucilage; this is correct). Whisk in the 2 eggs, 4 g salt, 8 g soy sauce, and 4 g sugar until uniform. The batter should be the consistency of slightly thick pancake batter; if it is too thick, splash in more dashi 30 g at a time.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Fold in the cabbage and mix-ins. Add the 500 g finely chopped cabbage, 60 g sliced scallions, 30 g chopped beni shoga, and 8 g katsuobushi flakes to the batter bowl. With a wide spoon or your hands, fold everything together until each cabbage shred is coated with batter. The mixture should look like a cabbage-heavy slaw barely held together with sticky batter; if it looks soupy, the batter-to-cabbage ratio is wrong (need more cabbage). Rest the mixed batter 5 minutes; the cabbage gives up a small amount of water that the batter absorbs, producing a more cohesive pancake.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Heat the pan. Heat 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil in a 24 cm nonstick or well-seasoned cast-iron skillet over medium heat until shimmering. The temperature is calibrated to medium, not high; high heat burns the outside before the cabbage cooks through.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Form pancake 1. Scoop about ¼ of the batter mixture (a generous 350 g) into the hot pan. Spread gently with a spatula into a thick disc about 2.5 cm tall and 20 cm across, leaving a 2 cm gap to the pan edge for flipping room. Do NOT press down hard; the pancake should remain lofty. Lay 4 to 5 thin slices of pork belly (about 50 g) over the top of the disc in a single layer.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Cook side 1. Cook undisturbed 4 to 5 minutes. The bottom should turn deep golden-brown and the edges should set; lift gently with a spatula at minute 4 to check color. The interior cabbage is still raw at this stage; the steam under the pancake is cooking it.'
    technique: 'shallow-frying'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Flip. Drizzle a little oil around the pancake edges. Slide a wide spatula (a 15 cm wide turner is ideal; use two spatulas if you have two) under the pancake. In one confident motion, flip the pancake over so the pork-belly side is now down. If the pancake breaks slightly, push it back together with the spatula; it will re-seal as the second side cooks. The pork belly will sizzle and start rendering its fat into the pan; this is the structural moment when the pork-fat-meets-batter interface develops.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Cook side 2 (the pork-belly side). Cook undisturbed 4 to 5 minutes. The pork belly will brown and crisp at its edges; the pancake will continue to steam from the inside, cooking the cabbage tender. Press gently in the center with the spatula at minute 4 to check; if the spatula meets springy resistance and the center feels firm, the pancake is done.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Flip back and finish. Flip the pancake one more time so the original side is up. Cook 60 seconds to re-crisp. Slide onto a plate, pork-belly-side up; the canonical Osaka presentation is pork-belly-up so the dressings layer over the meat.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Top and serve pancake 1. Drizzle 20 g okonomi sauce over the surface in a zig-zag pattern (use a squeeze bottle if you have one). Drizzle 10 g Kewpie mayo in a perpendicular zig-zag over the sauce. Scatter a generous handful of katsuobushi flakes over the top; the flakes will visibly dance and curl from the steam rising off the hot pancake (the "dancing bonito" is the canonical visual element). Sprinkle 1 g aonori in a stripe across the center. Top with a small pile of beni shoga off to one side. Cut into 6 to 8 wedges with the side of a spatula; the diners pull wedges directly off the plate with chopsticks.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Cook pancakes 2, 3, and 4 the same way. Wipe out the skillet between pancakes; add fresh 10 g oil per pancake. Total cook time is about 25 minutes across 4 pancakes; serve each one fresh and hot rather than holding them.'
    time_min: 18

notes: |
  Nagaimo is the textural secret. The single ingredient that distinguishes restaurant-quality okonomiyaki from home attempts is grated nagaimo (Japanese mountain yam). The yam''s mucilaginous starch (similar to okra slime) lightens the flour batter so the interior cooks fluffy-tender rather than dense-doughy. Most Western recipes omit nagaimo because it is hard to find; the resulting pancakes are recognizably heavier and less appealing. The hunt for nagaimo at a Japanese grocery is worth the trip.

  The cabbage ratio is the structural call. Okonomiyaki is roughly 70% cabbage by weight in the finished pancake; the batter is just the binder. New cooks frequently use too much batter and not enough cabbage, producing a flat dense pancake that reads as a savory pancake with cabbage in it rather than as cabbage-with-batter. The 500 g cabbage to 150 g flour ratio in this recipe is the canonical proportion; trust the volume.

  Why pork belly on top, not in the batter. Laying the thin pork belly slices on the surface of the pancake (and flipping so the pork hits the pan directly in side 2) produces a structurally specific result: the pork fat renders directly into the pan and caramelizes the contact surface to a deep golden crust, while the pork meat sits in direct contact with the cabbage interior, infusing it with pork flavor as it steams. Mixing pork into the batter produces a less dimensional dish.

  The dancing bonito is the visual centerpiece. Katsuobushi flakes scattered on the hot pancake immediately after topping with sauce and mayo will curl, twist, and visually "dance" from the steam rising off the surface. This is the canonical Japanese serving moment and is worth pausing to watch. The dancing slows as the pancake cools; serve immediately so the diner sees it.

  Okonomi means "how you like." The name "okonomiyaki" combines "okonomi" (how you like, your preference) with "yaki" (grilled). The Osaka tradition is highly customizable: the canonical pork-and-cabbage is the home default, but cooks add seafood, mochi, cheese, kimchi, corn, mountain vegetables, and dozens of other variations to taste. The dish is meant to flex with the cook''s preference; this recipe gives the canonical baseline.

  Osaka versus Hiroshima, the actual distinction. The two cities have a friendly rivalry over their okonomiyaki traditions. Osaka mixes everything into one batter and pan-fries; Hiroshima layers the components separately on a griddle, including yakisoba noodles as a structural layer. Both are valid; the Osaka style translates to home pans, while the Hiroshima style requires a flat griddle. Most okonomiyaki served in Tokyo and abroad is Osaka-style.

  Day-2 okonomiyaki is acceptable but not as good. The pancake reheats in a dry pan over medium-low for 4 to 5 minutes per side; the cabbage softens further and the texture goes a touch denser. The toppings (sauce, mayo, bonito, aonori) should be re-applied fresh after reheating since they degrade on the chill. Refrigerate up to 2 days.

  Eating mechanics. The pancake is cut into 6 to 8 wedges directly on the serving plate (the side of the spatula does the work; no need for a knife). Diners pull wedges with chopsticks from a communal plate, or each diner gets their own pancake. Some Osaka diners eat directly off the hot griddle at teppanyaki restaurants; the canonical home presentation is on a plate at the table.
