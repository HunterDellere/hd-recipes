steps:
  - text: 'Render the bacon. Lay the 115 g / 4 slices thick-cut bacon in a cold skillet and bring it up over medium heat. Cooking from cold renders the fat slowly and evenly so the bacon crisps without scorching, about 10 to 12 minutes, turning once, until deep brown and rigid. Transfer to a wire rack (not paper towel, which traps steam and softens it) and let it cool fully. Once cold and crisp, chop or crumble into rough 1 cm pieces.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Cut the wedges. Pull the ice-cold head of iceberg from the fridge. Remove any wilted outer leaves, then cut the 600 g head straight down through the core into 4 equal wedges, leaving the core intact on each so the leaves stay anchored in a wedge rather than falling apart into loose leaves. Pat the cut faces dry with a clean towel; surface water dilutes the dressing and makes it slide off. Set each wedge cut-side up on a chilled plate.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Sauce the wedges. Spoon the 240 g / 1 cup chunky blue cheese dressing generously over each wedge, about 60 g per wedge, letting it pool into the layered gaps between the leaves and run down the cut faces. The dressing should drape the wedge, not fully coat it; you want bare crisp lettuce in some bites and heavy dressing in others.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Build the layers. Scatter the bacon crumble, the 150 g / 1 cup halved cherry tomatoes, and the 40 g / ⅓ cup extra crumbled blue cheese over and around the dressed wedges, distributing evenly so every plate gets all three. Layering on top of the dressing rather than tossing keeps each component distinct, which is the structural logic of the wedge.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish and serve immediately. Shower the 8 g / 2 tbsp sliced chives over the top, then crack the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper coarsely over each wedge and pinch the 2 g / ½ tsp flaky salt across the cut surfaces, where the macro-flakes hit the tongue first and read as bright contrast to the long savory dressing. Serve with the lemon wedges alongside; a squeeze of fresh lemon at the table is the finishing acid that cuts the bacon fat and the blue cheese richness in the same stroke. Eat at once, while the lettuce is still cold and snapping.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  The wedge is a temperature dish disguised as a salad. Three of its five components are about thermal contrast: ice-cold iceberg, warm-rendered then cooled crisp bacon, and a cold dressing. Get the lettuce genuinely cold (chill the head, work fast, plate on chilled plates) and the dish has a crispness and refreshment that a room-temperature wedge never reaches. This is the single most-skipped step and the single biggest quality lever.

  Iceberg earns its place here, despite its reputation. The dense, tightly-packed leaves hold a high water content in turgid cells, which gives the glassy snap and the cold-water refreshment that the dish is built around. The leaves are also structurally stiff enough to hold a heavy chunky dressing in their layered gaps without collapsing, which delicate leaves cannot do. This is the one classic American salad where iceberg is the correct choice, not a budget compromise.

  Why bacon from a cold pan and onto a rack. Starting bacon in a cold skillet renders the subcutaneous fat slowly and evenly, so the meat crisps before it scorches; dropping it into a hot pan sears the outside and leaves flabby unrendered fat. Cooling it on a wire rack rather than paper towel lets steam escape from all sides so it sets shatter-crisp. Bacon laid hot onto the lettuce wilts the wedge instantly; cool it fully first.

  Acid is the line that holds the dish together. Between the rich dressing, the fatty bacon, and the salty blue cheese, the wedge is a wall of richness without intervention. The cherry tomatoes contribute one acid vector and the table lemon another; the dressing itself carries lemon and cultured buttermilk. Do not skip the lemon wedges at the plate, the squeeze is what keeps the third bite as appealing as the first.

  Build, do not toss. The defining structure of a wedge is that the components stay layered and discrete rather than tossed into uniformity. Each forkful crosses a different ratio of cold lettuce, dressing, bacon, tomato, and cheese, and that variation across bites is the experience. Tossing it homogenizes the contrast and turns it into an ordinary chopped salad.
