before_you_start:
  - 'The lentils must be dressed while still warm. Cold lentils bead the vinaigrette off their surface and read underseasoned no matter how much dressing you add. Build the vinaigrette while the lentils simmer so it is ready the moment they drain, and dress them within a couple of minutes of draining, before they cool past warm.'
  - 'Do not salt the lentil cooking water. Salt added early toughens the seed coat and stretches the cook time unpredictably; the lentils take their salt from the vinaigrette and a controlled pinch added at the end of the simmer. Add the kosher salt only in the last 5 minutes of cooking, once the lentils are nearly tender.'
  - 'This salad eats better after 30 minutes of rest and is genuinely good the next day, so it can be made ahead. Hold the fresh herbs, walnuts, and goat cheese back and fold them in just before serving so the herbs stay green and the nuts stay crisp.'

steps:
  - text: 'Build the vinaigrette first so it is waiting when the lentils drain. Combine the 40 g / 1 shallot fine-minced with the 30 g / 2 tbsp red wine vinegar in a large bowl (the bowl you will dress the lentils in) and let it macerate while the lentils cook, at least 10 minutes. The acid pulls the harsh raw-allium sulfur compounds out of the shallot and softens its bite; this short pickle is the difference between a sharp dressing and a balanced one. Whisk in the 15 g / 1 tbsp dijon mustard, the 5 g / 1 tsp honey, the 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper until smooth.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Rinse and cook the lentils. Rinse the 250 g / 1¼ cups lentilles vertes du Puy in a sieve until the water runs clear, picking out any small stones. Put them in a saucepan with the 1000 ml / 4 cups unsalted water, the 60 g / ½ onion half, the 6 g / 2 garlic cloves smashed, the 1 bay leaf, and the 3 g / 3 thyme sprigs. Bring to a boil, then drop to a bare simmer (a gentle tremor, not a rolling boil; a hard boil cracks the seed coats and turns the surface to mush). Simmer uncovered for 18 to 22 minutes.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Salt at the end and check for the right bite. In the last 5 minutes of the simmer, stir in the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt. Salting earlier toughens the seed coat and stretches the cook time; salting now seasons the interior without that penalty. The lentils are done when they are tender through but still hold a distinct, intact shape and give a slight resistance at the center on a bite, toothsome rather than soft. Start tasting at 18 minutes; Le Puy lentils go from underdone to perfect in a narrow window and overshoot into paste if walked away from.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Drain and dress while hot. Drain the lentils well in a sieve and pull out the spent onion half, the 2 garlic cloves, the bay leaf, and the thyme sprigs. Scrape the hot lentils straight into the bowl with the vinaigrette base. Whisking the dressing constantly, stream in the 75 g / ⅓ cup extra-virgin olive oil to build a loose emulsion (the dijon holds the oil and vinegar together), then fold the warm lentils through it. Dressing the lentils warm is the technique that defines the dish: the gelatinized starch on the hot seed surface drinks the vinaigrette into itself, where cold lentils would bead it off and read flat. Let the dressed lentils sit and absorb for 20 to 30 minutes, stirring once or twice.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Taste and correct before the herbs go in. The lentils will have absorbed most of the dressing and muted the acid. Taste now, while you can still adjust the base. It should read distinctly seasoned and a touch sharp; if it is flat, add a teaspoon of red wine vinegar and a pinch of fine sea salt and fold again. Get the base right here, because once the herbs are in you are committed.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Fold in the herbs and finish. Just before serving, fold in the 30 g / 1 cup chopped parsley, the 15 g / ¼ cup sliced chives, and the 10 g / ¼ cup mint ribbons. Fold gently so the herbs stay distinct and green. Scatter the 40 g / ⅓ cup toasted walnuts over the top, then crumble the 60 g / 2 oz goat cheese over in soft pieces so it stays in discrete creamy pockets rather than smearing through. Serve at cool room temperature; cold from the fridge dulls both the herbs and the seasoning, so if it has been refrigerated, let it come back toward room temperature and re-taste for salt and acid before serving.'
    time_min: 4

notes: |
  Why Le Puy specifically. Lentilles vertes du Puy are a small, dark-mottled green lentil grown on the volcanic soil of the Auvergne, carrying AOC protection. The thing that matters for a salad is their low starch-to-skin ratio and intact seed coat: they cook to tender without the interior dissolving, so the finished lentil holds a distinct shape and a faint resistance at the center. Brown lentils and the common green supermarket lentil have thinner coats and more loose starch; they cook into a soft purée-edged mass that is excellent in soup and wrong in a salad. Castelluccio (Umbria) and beluga lentils share the shape-holding trait and are the right substitutes; split red and yellow lentils are a different category entirely and disintegrate on purpose.

  Why dress them warm. This is the one non-negotiable technique. When lentils come off the heat, the starch at the surface of each seed is gelatinized and porous, and it pulls liquid into itself as it cools and retrogrades. Dress them in that window and the vinaigrette is drawn into the seed, seasoning the lentil from its surface inward and binding the dressing to the legume. Wait until the lentils are cold and the surface starch has set into a firmer, less permeable skin that beads the oil-and-vinegar off; the same quantity of dressing then sits on the outside and the dish reads underseasoned and greasy at once. Every correctly built lentil salad in the French canon dresses warm for exactly this reason.

  Salt timing and the cooking water. Salt added to the cooking water early in the simmer interferes with the seed coat softening and makes the cook time unpredictable, which is why the kosher salt goes in only in the last 5 minutes and most of the seasoning comes from the vinaigrette. Do not skip the aromatics in the water, though: the onion, garlic, bay, and thyme perfume the lentils from the inside in a way you cannot replicate by adding them later. They are lifted out and discarded before dressing; their job is done in the pot.

  The umami and acid structure. The savory anchor in a meatless lentil salad has to come from somewhere, and here it is the dijon mustard, whose glutamate-adjacent depth and the toasted walnuts and goat cheese carry the savory load that a piece of pork or duck would otherwise supply. The red wine vinegar is the finishing acid, doing double duty as the maceration medium that tames the raw shallot. Both phases are deliberate; a lentil salad without a real acid reads muddy and earthbound, and one without a savory backbone reads like plain dressed legumes.

  Make-ahead and holding. The lentil base is better after a rest and genuinely good the next day, since the absorption and the shallot maceration both improve with time. Hold the herbs, walnuts, and cheese back and fold them in within minutes of serving: the parsley and mint go dark and slack within a couple of hours in an acidic dressing, the walnuts soften, and the goat cheese smears into the lentils and muddies the look. Refrigerate the dressed lentil base if making it more than a few hours ahead, then bring it back to cool room temperature and re-taste for salt and acid before finishing, because cold flattens both.
