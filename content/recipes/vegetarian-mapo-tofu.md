before_you_start:
  - 'Steamed jasmine rice is the partner and must be on FIRST. Rinse 360 g / 1¾ cups rice three times, start in the cooker (or stovetop covered at a bare simmer 18 minutes, then 10 minute rest off heat). The 45 minute rice timeline overlaps the entire mushroom-and-tofu cook.'
  - 'Rehydrate the dried shiitake FIRST and reserve their soaking liquid. The 30 minute minimum soak (2 hours is better) produces the shiitake stock that is the structural umami foundation of this dish; rushed soaking leaves both flat mushrooms and weak stock. Start them at the same time as the rice.'
  - 'Toast the Sichuan peppercorns whole and grind them within 30 minutes of plating. Pre-ground peppercorn has lost most of its volatile oils to oxidation; the má (numbing) effect requires fresh-toasted-and-cracked powder as a finishing scatter.'
  - 'Set up the tofu blanch in parallel with the mushroom dry-fry. Have a wide saucepan of 80 degrees C salted water ready before the mushrooms hit the wok; the blanch holds the cut silken tofu for the 2 minutes the mushroom browning needs to finish.'

steps:
  - text: 'Rehydrate the dried shiitake. In a heat-safe bowl, pour the 500 ml / 2 cups near-boiling water over the 25 g dried shiitake. Cover with a plate and let stand 30 minutes minimum (2 hours is better). When ready to use, lift the mushrooms out (squeeze gently to release liquid), remove and discard the tough stems, and finely chop the caps to a 3 to 5 mm dice. Strain the rehydration liquid through cheesecloth or a coffee filter to remove any sediment; this is the "shiitake stock" and is the structural umami base of the dish. Reserve.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Brown the mushroom duxelles. Heat a wok over high heat until lightly smoking. Add 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil, the 250 g fresh shiitake dice, and the 50 g rehydrated chopped shiitake. Stir-fry continuously for 6 to 8 minutes; the mushrooms will release water in the first 2 to 3 minutes (do not drain, let it cook off). Once the water has evaporated, the mushrooms will begin to brown and crisp at the edges. Continue cooking until the granules are deeply browned, dry, and fragrant, they should look like ground meat that has been browned aggressively. Add 10 ml / 2 tsp Shaoxing and 5 ml / 1 tsp light soy. Toss 30 seconds. Transfer to a plate. The mushroom duxelles is the structural protein-substitute of the dish; under-browning produces a watery, flat result.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Blanch the tofu. While the mushrooms brown (or after, in parallel), bring 1500 ml / 6 cups water to a bare simmer (80°C / 175°F) with the 8 g / 2 tsp salt. Cut the 500 g silken tofu into 2 cm cubes. Slide into the salted water with a wide spatula, hold 2 minutes (do not boil), drain through a colander. Set aside.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Render the aromatic base. Wipe the wok and return to medium heat. Add 45 ml / 3 tbsp neutral oil. When shimmering (140 to 160°C / 285 to 320°F), add 35 g doubanjiang. Break up and stir-fry 90 seconds until the oil turns brilliant brick-red. Add 6 g crushed douchi, 12 g minced ginger, 16 g minced garlic. Stir-fry 45 seconds.'
    technique: 'red-oil-rendering'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Build the sauce. Pour in 240 ml / 1 cup strained shiitake stock, 18 ml / 1¼ tbsp light soy, 5 ml / 1 tsp dark soy, 3 g / ½ tsp sugar. Return the browned mushroom duxelles. Bring to a simmer; the liquid should be aromatic, deep brown, and structurally umami (taste it; it should read as substantively savory, not as light vegetable broth). Slide in the blanched tofu cubes, push gently to submerge, simmer 3 to 4 minutes.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Three-pour cornstarch thickening. Whisk 9 g cornstarch with 45 ml / 3 tbsp cold water. Increase wok to medium-high. Pour one-third around the edge, push the tofu to distribute, wait 20 seconds. Pour another third, wait 20 seconds. Pour the final third; the sauce should be glossy and clinging, not pooled.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish. Pull off heat. Add the 25 g scallion greens. Toss gently. Transfer to a wide serving bowl. Sprinkle the 3 g / 1½ tsp freshly toasted-and-ground Sichuan peppercorn over the top, do not stir in. Serve immediately with steamed jasmine rice.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The vegetarian Sichuan tradition. Buddhist temple cuisine in Sichuan has a long-established tradition of "mock meat" cooking that uses mushrooms, wheat gluten, and tofu to recreate dishes built around animal protein. The codified vegetarian mapo tofu (素麻婆豆腐, sù má pó dòu fu) typically uses a combination of mushroom duxelles and rehydrated dried shiitake as the protein substitute, with the dried shiitake stock as the cooking liquid. The result is not a compromise version, it is a genuinely different and equally valid version of the dish. Many Sichuan vegetarian restaurants serve nothing but vegetarian preparations of the Sichuan classics, and the mushroom mapo is often the signature.

  Why dried shiitake stock is the structural choice. Dried shiitake mushrooms produce a rehydration stock that is dramatically higher in glutamate, inosinate-equivalent guanylate, and aromatic compounds than fresh mushroom stock. The drying process concentrates the umami precursors and enzymatically generates new flavor compounds; the rehydrated stock has a meaty, brothy character that fresh mushroom stock simply does not produce. This is why most Asian vegetarian cooking traditions use dried shiitake as the foundational stock ingredient rather than fresh mushrooms or a vegetable medley. For this dish specifically, the shiitake stock provides the umami backbone that, in the meat version, would come from the beef and the chicken stock combined.

  The browning step is the critical move. The mushroom-duxelles browning step (6 to 8 minutes of high-heat stir-frying) is what converts the mushrooms from "vegetable" texture to "ground-meat-substitute" texture. Cooks unfamiliar with the technique often pull the mushrooms when they look done, at the 3 to 4 minute mark when they are wilted and shiny, and then the dish reads as wilted-mushroom-with-sauce rather than as mapo. The mushrooms must be cooked past wilted, past shiny, past softened, into the deeply-browned-and-dry territory where the granules look like ground-beef granules that have been aggressively rendered. Trust the process; the texture transformation happens in the last 2 minutes and the difference is dramatic.

  The seven-flavor target still applies. The vegetarian version targets the same seven flavors as the canonical Sichuan mapo (麻 numbing, 辣 spicy, 烫 hot, 香 fragrant, 鲜 savory, 嫩 tender, 酥 crumbly). The 鲜 (xiān, savory-fresh umami) is recovered through the dried-shiitake stock; the 酥 (sū, crumbly granular texture) is recovered through the well-browned mushroom duxelles. The other five flavors are unchanged from the meat version. A well-made vegetarian mapo tofu hits all seven flavors and reads as a complete Sichuan classic.

  Eating mechanics. Same as the canonical version: serve with steamed jasmine rice as part of a multi-dish meal. Reheats acceptably the next day; the silken tofu loses some structural integrity but the umami-laden mushroom granules and sauce often deepen overnight. The dish is a genuine option for vegetarian Chinese-meal diners, not a compromise.
