steps:
  - text: 'Choose the right pot. A small heavy saucepan (1.5 to 2 L / 6 to 8 cups) is ideal, small surface area means slower evaporation, longer run time. Avoid nonstick if you plan to run the pot for 8+ hours; the surface can degrade at sustained low heat. Stainless or enameled cast iron is the right choice.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Combine the orange peel, lemon peel (and squeezed juice from the lemon), and 200 g / 1 cup apple cores and peels in the pot. Add the 3 broken cinnamon sticks, 15 cloves, 2 star anise, the half cracked nutmeg, and the 4 cracked cardamom pods.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 1500 ml / 6 cups water, enough to fully submerge everything with at least 5 cm / 2 inches of headspace. Stir in the 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla extract if using.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Bring to a bare simmer over medium heat, then drop to the absolute lowest setting on your stove (or move to a small burner on its lowest setting). The water should produce occasional lazy bubbles, never a rolling boil, never quiet. The goal is sustained low evaporation that releases volatile aromatics into the air over hours rather than blasting everything out in 20 minutes.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Hold at this low simmer for 8 to 12 hours. Check the water level every 2 hours; top up with hot water from a kettle when the level drops below half. The pot will smell strongest in the first 2 to 3 hours, then settle into a softer ambient register that scents the whole house through the rest of the day.'
    time_min: 480
  - text: 'When done for the day, cool completely and either refrigerate (the pot will run again for one more day, weaker but functional) or compost the solids. The water itself can be poured down the drain, it is just citrus-spice tea.'

notes: |
  This is a non-edible preparation. The water is for evaporation, not drinking, extended cooking concentrates whatever else has touched the citrus peels (wax coating, conventional pesticide residue, packaging adhesive). Treat as a fragrance preparation, not a tea.

  Why this works better than scented candles or air fresheners. Commercial fragrance products release synthetic aromatic chemicals (phthalates, limonene derivatives, benzaldehyde isomers) that smell briefly intense but are uniform, flat, and chemically irritating to many people. A simmer pot releases dozens of natural volatile compounds (terpenes from citrus oils, eugenol from cloves, cinnamaldehyde from cinnamon, linalool from cardamom) at low sustained concentrations that the nose reads as complex and depth-layered. The smell also penetrates fabric and carpet less aggressively and dissipates within hours of pot-off, so the house does not retain residual fragrance the next morning.

  Sustained low heat is the technique. The aromatic compounds in cinnamon, clove, and citrus peel are mostly volatile at temperatures well below boiling, they evaporate steadily at 70 to 80°C over hours, which is exactly what a barely simmering pot delivers. A hard boil drives everything out in the first 30 minutes and the pot goes flat for the rest of the day; the lazy low simmer is the entire engineering.

  Never let the pot run dry. Even at the lowest setting, a 1.5 L pot evaporates roughly 200 ml per hour. The check-and-refill cadence keeps the spices from scorching and ruining the fragrance.
