desc: 'The Turkish miniature dumpling: a stiff egg-and-water dough rolled paper-thin, cut into 2 cm squares, each holding a pea-sized portion of seasoned ground lamb (or beef) and onion, pinched closed into tiny pyramids. The structural test of a Turkish home cook is the smallness; classical manti is 1 cm square, 40 to a spoon. The modern home standard is 2 cm, still tiny by dumpling standards globally.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the dough: in a medium bowl, whisk together the 300 g / 2½ cups flour and 4 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt. Make a well; add the 50 g / 1 egg and 90 g cold water. Stir with a fork until shaggy, then turn onto a clean surface and knead 8 to 10 minutes until smooth, firm, and slightly elastic. The dough should feel stiffer than pasta dough (it needs to roll paper-thin without tearing). Add 1 to 2 tbsp more water only if the dough refuses to come together; dryer is better than wetter here. Wrap tightly in plastic-free beeswax wrap or a damp kitchen towel; rest 30 minutes at room temperature.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Make the filling: in a medium bowl, combine the 250 g ground lamb, the 100 g / ½ onion (grated), 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt, 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper, and 1 g / ½ tsp cumin. Mix with a fork or your hands just until uniform; do not overmix or the meat tightens. The filling should look bound and slightly wet from the grated onion. Cover and refrigerate while you roll the dough.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Divide the rested dough into 4 portions. Working with one portion at a time (keep the others wrapped), roll on a lightly floured surface to a paper-thin sheet (1 to 2 mm, the thinnest you can roll without tearing; the dough should be nearly translucent). The sheet will be roughly 30 to 35 cm square. Use a pasta machine if you have one (set to 6 or 7 of 9) for consistency.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Trim the sheet to a clean rectangle. With a sharp knife or pizza cutter, cut into 2 cm squares (you will get roughly 50 squares per sheet, 200 total across all 4 portions). Work quickly; the dough dries as it sits, and a dry edge will not seal.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Fill the manti: place a pea-sized portion of filling (about ¼ tsp, no more) in the center of each square. To shape: lift the four corners up and pinch them together at the top to form a small pyramid; then pinch the four seams between the corners closed, working around so the dumpling is fully sealed. The classical Kayseri shape is a tiny four-sided pyramid; modern home cooks sometimes just pinch the four corners and leave the bottom seams partially open (this still seals during the boil because the dumpling sits flat). Repeat with all squares.'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'As you finish shaping, transfer the manti to a lightly floured sheet pan, spaced so they do not touch. The shaped dumplings can rest on the sheet pan up to 1 hour at room temperature, or be frozen on the pan and transferred to a freezer bag once solid (cook from frozen, adding 2 minutes to the boil time).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Make the garlic yogurt: pound the 8 g / 2 garlic cloves with a pinch of salt in a mortar to a smooth paste (or finely grate on a microplane). In a bowl, whisk the 400 g / 1¾ cups Greek yogurt with the garlic paste and 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt until smooth. Set aside at room temperature (cold yogurt on hot dumplings is a textural mistake; the dish wants the yogurt at body temperature, not refrigerator temperature).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Boil the manti: bring 4 L of well-salted water to a rolling boil. Add the manti in batches if needed (do not crowd; they should float freely). Cook 6 to 8 minutes from when they float to the surface; the dumplings are done when the dough is just-tender (no raw-floury bite) and the filling is cooked through. Drain in a colander; do not rinse.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'While the manti boil, make the chili butter: melt the 90 g / 6 tbsp butter in a small saucepan over medium heat. Cook past the foaming stage 3 to 4 minutes, swirling occasionally, until the milk solids turn deep amber and the butter smells of toasted hazelnut. Pull off heat. Stir in the 10 g / 2 tsp tomato paste and the 4 g / 2 tsp pul biber; the residual heat blooms the spices and the butter turns brick-red. Return briefly to low heat for 30 seconds to integrate, then off heat.'
    technique: 'brown-butter'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Plate: divide the hot drained manti among 4 shallow bowls. Spoon generous pools of the garlic yogurt over each portion (about 100 g per bowl). Drizzle the hot chili butter over the yogurt in dramatic streaks (the contrast of red butter on white yogurt is the visual signature). Sprinkle each bowl with the dried mint and sumac. Serve immediately; the contrast of hot dumpling, cool yogurt, hot chili butter, and bright dried herbs is the entire structural point of the dish.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  The size of manti is structural. The Kayseri tradition measures a cook by the smallness of their manti; the regional benchmark is 40 dumplings on a spoon, which requires 1 cm squares. The home standard worldwide is 2 cm. The reason the smallness matters: with tiny dumplings, the ratio of dough surface to filling is high, which means each bite carries more of the chili butter and yogurt sauce that defines the dish. Large dumplings (4 cm or bigger) drown the sauce in dough and the dish reads as a generic ravioli rather than as manti. If your first batch lands at 3 cm, that is still acceptable; aim for 2 cm on the second batch and 1.5 on the third.

  The temperature contrast is the dish. Manti is plated with three temperature layers: hot drained dumplings (just out of the pot), cool but room-temperature yogurt (not cold from the refrigerator), and hot just-finished chili butter (still steaming). The contrast of the three temperatures hitting the palate together is what makes the dish more than the sum of its parts. Cold yogurt on hot dumplings reads as confused; matched-temperature components read flat. Plan the timing so all three are at their target temperature when the bowl hits the table.

  Versatility. The chili butter alone is the structural sauce of many Turkish dishes and travels onto: drizzled over plain rice, brushed onto grilled bread, spooned over poached eggs, ladled over yogurt soup. The dough-and-filling logic transfers to other Turkish stuffed pasta (mantı bohça with broader sheets, içli köfte with bulgur shells).

  Pairs across Turkish (canonical, especially Kayseri and central Anatolia), Armenian (where similar dumplings exist), Central Asian Turkic (Uzbek, Uyghur manty are larger cousins), and contemporary fusion cookery.

  Storage. Shaped uncooked manti freeze excellently for 3 months on a floured sheet, then transferred to a freezer bag; cook from frozen with 2 minutes added to the boil. Cooked manti do not store well; the dough turns gummy in the refrigerator. Make ahead by freezing raw, then boil to order. Leftover yogurt sauce keeps 4 days; leftover chili butter keeps 2 weeks and is excellent on roasted vegetables, grilled bread, and eggs.
