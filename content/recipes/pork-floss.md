before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 3.5-hour weekend project, with 75 minutes of nearly continuous active stirring in the dry-fry phase. Do not start unless you can stand at the wok for the second half; the floss burns the moment you turn your back.'
  - 'Buy lean pork shoulder and trim aggressively before weighing. Any fat or skin left on will refuse to dry into fluff and instead reads as greasy clumps in the finished floss.'
  - 'Have a wide nonstick or carbon-steel wok (12-inch or larger) and a wooden or silicone spatula ready. A small skillet crowds the meat and traps moisture, the floss stays wet and never separates into strands.'
  - 'The finished floss stores up to a month in an airtight jar at room temperature. Plan a clean dry jar before you start, the floss must go in fully cooled or condensation will spoil it within days.'

steps:
  - text: 'Cut the 700 g lean pork shoulder along the grain into 4 cm strips. Cutting along the grain (not across) is what produces the long fibrous strands that shred into floss; cross-grain cuts produce short, mealy floss that turns to powder during dry-frying.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Combine the pork strips, 1500 ml / 6 cups water, 30 g smashed ginger, 3 scallion whites, 30 ml / 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine, and 2 star anise (~4 g) in a heavy pot. Bring to a boil, skim off the gray scum that rises in the first 10 minutes, then reduce to a bare simmer (just below 90°C / 195°F, with no active bubbles). Cover and simmer 90 minutes, until the pork pulls apart effortlessly between two forks.'
    time_min: 100
  - text: 'Drain the pork into a strainer set over a bowl (reserve the broth for soup). Discard the aromatics. Let the pork cool 10 minutes until you can handle it. While it is still warm, shred each strip by hand or pulse in a stand mixer with the paddle attachment on low for 60 seconds; the goal is fine, separated strands, not a paste. Pulled too cold and the meat fights the shred; warm pork gives easily.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Heat a wide, heavy wok or skillet over medium-low. Add the 15 ml / 1 tbsp neutral oil and the shredded pork. Spread into a single layer. Cook 5 minutes without stirring to begin driving moisture off the bottom layer. Once the pork is dry to the touch, stir in the 30 ml / 2 tbsp light soy, 8 ml / ½ tbsp dark soy if using, 25 g / 2 tbsp granulated sugar, 3 g / ½ tsp kosher salt, and 0.5 g / ¼ tsp white pepper.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Continue dry-frying over medium-low heat for 30 to 45 minutes total, stirring every 90 seconds with two wooden spatulas to keep the strands separated and to expose new surface area to the pan. The pork passes through three stages: wet (sticky, dark, glistening) at minute 5; tacky-dry (loose strands, deep brown, beginning to lighten) at minute 20; and floss (pale tan, dry to the touch, fluffy, the strands separate cleanly) at minute 30 to 45. Pull when the floss is fully dry and lifts in a cloud when you shake the pan.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Spread the finished floss on a sheet pan to cool completely; it will crisp further as it dries in air. Once fully cool, store in an airtight glass jar at room temperature; the low residual moisture combined with the seasoning concentration makes it shelf-stable for 4 to 6 weeks. Refrigerated, it keeps 3 months but absorbs ambient moisture; let it come to room temperature before opening to prevent condensation.'
    time_min: 60

  - for: 'protein'

    how: 'Replace the pork with skinless chicken breast or thigh. Simmer 45 minutes (chicken cooks faster than pork shoulder) and drop the dry-fry time to 25 to 35 minutes; chicken has less collagen so it dries faster. Drop sugar to 15 g for a Northern-leaning savory profile.'
    kind: 'protein'
  - for: 'texture'
    to: 'crisper, candy-like floss strands'
    how: 'Toward the end of dry-frying (minute 35), pull half the floss and toss it in 30 g warm honey-soy syrup (15 g honey + 15 g light soy reduced together to thread stage). Spread on a sheet pan to set crisp. Adds ~5 minutes; produces a contrast topping that reads more confection than pantry.'

notes: |
  Pork floss is the canonical Chinese congee topping that bridges into baozi, savory milk bread (rou song mian bao), tang yuan fillings, and the rice-rolled triangle snacks (fan tuan) that Taiwanese street vendors stuff with floss, pickled radish, youtiao, and sometimes a salted egg yolk. A jar of homemade floss expands the table by an order of magnitude.

  Why dry-fry, not bake or dehydrate. The Maillard reaction during the dry-fry develops the deep tan color and savory roasted notes that distinguish floss from merely shredded pork. A 110°C oven dry runs eight hours and produces a paler, less fragrant product. The wok dry-fry is the technique; substitute it and the dish loses its identity.

  The two failure modes. Pulling too early (still tacky-dry) produces floss that goes hard and clumpy in storage as residual moisture drives starch retrogradation. Pulling too late (over-fried) produces floss that has darkened and tastes burnt-sweet from caramelized sugar; the strands are brittle rather than fluffy. The cue is shake the pan: when finished floss lifts in a cloud and falls back as separate strands, it is done. If it falls back in clumps, give it 5 more minutes. If it falls back as fragments, you are 5 minutes past.

  How to use. A heaped tablespoon over plain rice or congee at breakfast turns a starch into a meal. Folded into the egg-and-scallion filling of fan tuan. Sandwiched into milk bread with a smear of mayonnaise (the Fujianese-Cantonese pairing that became the basis for the Taiwanese pork-floss-and-mayo bun). Stirred into mashed potatoes or scrambled eggs. The flavor is high-impact and dry, so it is almost always paired with something wet (porridge, mayo, soft egg) or starchy (rice, bread).
