source:
  name: 'Adapted from the North Indian tandoor-bread tradition, recalibrated for a home cooktop using the cast-iron-plus-broiler method that approximates the tandoor''s two heat sources (radiant ceiling heat and conductive wall contact).'
  note: 'A real tandoor cooks naan in 60 to 90 seconds: the wet dough is slapped onto the near-vertical clay wall, where conductive heat from the 400 °C-plus clay sets the contact face while radiant heat from the charcoal bed below blisters the exposed face. A home oven cannot reach that temperature, but a cast-iron skillet preheated to its smoke point supplies the conductive contact heat and a broiler element supplies the radiant top heat. The enrichment (yogurt, milk, a little sugar and fat) is what separates naan from a lean flatbread; the lactic acid relaxes the gluten for the characteristic tender chew and the milk solids and sugar drive the charred-bubble browning.'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a yeast-leavened dough that needs a 60 to 90 minute bulk proof at warm room temperature before you can shape it. Mix the dough first thing, then prep the garlic ghee and curry while it rises.'
  - 'The cooking method generates real smoke. The skillet runs at its smoke point and sits directly under an active broiler, so run the kitchen ventilation on high and pull the smoke alarm battery if it sits near the kitchen. The first naan will fill the oven with smoke within 30 seconds.'
  - 'Position one oven rack 4 to 5 inches below the broiler element before you preheat. The skillet must already be screaming hot when the first naan goes on, so preheat it under the broiler for a full 8 to 10 minutes while you roll the dough.'
  - 'Have the garlic ghee melted and a pastry brush ready before the first naan cooks. Each naan is brushed the instant it comes off the heat, while the surface is still hot enough to absorb the fat, so there is no time to melt ghee mid-batch.'

steps:
  - text: 'Mix the dough. In a large bowl, whisk together the 360 g / 3 cups bread flour, 7 g / 1¼ tsp fine sea salt, 12 g / 1 tbsp granulated sugar, and 5 g / 1½ tsp instant yeast, keeping the salt and yeast on opposite sides of the bowl as you add them so the salt does not deactivate the yeast on contact. In a separate bowl whisk the 120 g / ½ cup room-temperature yogurt, the 120 g / ½ cup warm milk (38 °C / 100 °F, bath-warm), and the 20 g / 1½ tbsp melted ghee until smooth.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Bring it together and knead. Pour the wet mixture into the dry and stir with a wooden spoon until no dry flour remains and a shaggy, sticky dough forms. Turn it onto a lightly floured counter and knead 8 to 10 minutes, adding flour a teaspoon at a time only as needed, until the dough is smooth, supple, and slightly tacky but no longer sticking to your hands. The target is a soft, extensible dough: the yogurt''s lactic acid has begun relaxing the gluten, so it should feel pillowy rather than tight. A windowpane test (stretch a piece thin enough to see light through it without tearing) confirms the gluten is developed.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Bulk proof. Shape the dough into a ball, place it in a lightly oiled bowl, and cover with a damp kitchen towel. Leave at warm room temperature (24 to 27 °C / 75 to 80 °F) until roughly doubled, 60 to 90 minutes. The enrichment slows the rise relative to a lean dough, so do not rush it; an under-proofed dough rolls back on itself and resists shaping, and the bubbles will not develop under the broiler.'
    technique: 'bulk-fermentation'
    time_min: 75
  - text: 'Make the garlic ghee. While the dough proofs, melt the 45 g / 3 tbsp ghee in a small saucepan over low heat. Off the heat, stir in the 12 g / 3 cloves grated garlic and let it steep in the residual warmth for 2 to 3 minutes; the gentle heat blooms the allicin without frying the garlic to bitterness. Keep warm and liquid. Have the 8 g / 2 tbsp chopped cilantro and the 4 g / 1½ tsp nigella seeds in separate small bowls within reach.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Divide and rest. Punch down the risen dough to deflate it and turn it onto a lightly floured counter. Divide into 6 equal pieces (about 110 g each); a bench scraper and a scale make this exact. Roll each into a tight ball, cover with the damp towel, and let them rest 15 minutes. This second short rest relaxes the gluten you just tightened during shaping, so the dough rolls out thin without snapping back, the single biggest cause of thick, bready naan.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Preheat the skillet under the broiler. Set an oven rack 4 to 5 inches below the broiler element. Place a heavy 30 cm / 12-inch cast-iron skillet on that rack and run the broiler on high for 8 to 10 full minutes. Cast iron has high thermal mass and preheats slowly; the skillet must reach its smoke point so that the dough''s contact face sets and chars on contact, mimicking the tandoor wall. The pan is ready when a flick of water instantly balls and skitters across the surface (the Leidenfrost effect, indicating well above 175 °C / 350 °F).'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Shape each naan. Working one ball at a time on the floured counter, press it flat with your fingertips, then roll it into a teardrop or oval roughly 20 cm / 8 inches long and 3 to 4 mm thick, leaving the center a touch thicker than the edges. Stretch it gently by hand to finish the teardrop shape if you like the classic look. Brush the top lightly with water (this helps the surface stay supple and helps the toppings adhere), and press a pinch of the 4 g / 1½ tsp nigella seeds into the surface.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Cook the contact face. Pull the rack out, lay one shaped naan onto the screaming skillet (nigella side up), and press it flat against the iron for 2 to 3 seconds with a folded towel or the back of a spatula to guarantee full contact. Within 30 to 60 seconds large bubbles will inflate across the surface and the underside will char in dark leopard spots. Do not move it; the conductive heat is setting the contact face exactly as the tandoor wall would.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Blister the top under the broiler. Slide the rack back under the broiler with the naan still in the skillet and broil 45 to 90 seconds, watching constantly, until the inflated bubbles char to dark brown-black leopard spots on the top face. This is the radiant heat doing the tandoor''s ceiling-char job. Pull the moment the bubbles blacken; a few seconds past that and the bread dries to a cracker. The total cook per naan is roughly 90 seconds to 2½ minutes across both faces.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Brush and finish. Transfer the cooked naan to a towel-lined basket or plate. Immediately, while the surface is hot and porous, brush it generously with the warm garlic ghee so the fat is drawn into the crumb rather than sitting on top, then scatter a pinch of the 8 g / 2 tbsp chopped cilantro over the top. Fold the towel over to keep it warm and pliable. Repeat from the shaping step with the remaining dough, re-preheating the skillet under the broiler for a minute or two between naan if it has lost its smoking-hot edge.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'The adjustment, and serve. Taste the first naan: it should read tender and chewy with a faint lactic tang from the yogurt, salty-sweet from the dough, and rich with garlic ghee, with bitter-char accents from the blistered bubbles. If it reads flat, the garlic ghee was under-salted or the bubbles under-charred; salt the ghee with a pinch of fine sea salt and push the broiler char further on the next one. The lactic tang of the yogurt is the built-in acid and the garlic ghee and charred milk solids carry the savory depth; serve hot, straight from the towel, alongside any North Indian curry or dal to mop the sauce.'
    time_min: 1

  - for: 'dairy-free'
    to: 'vegan naan'
    how: 'Swap the yogurt for an unsweetened cultured coconut or cashew yogurt (the live-culture lactic acid still does the gluten-relaxing work), the whole milk for an unsweetened oat or soy milk warmed to 38 °C, and the ghee for refined coconut oil or a neutral oil both in the dough and as the brushing fat. The browning is slightly less deep without milk solids, so push the broiler char a few seconds further.'

substitutions:
  - for: 'bread flour'
    use: 'all-purpose flour'
    note: 'All-purpose (about 10 to 11% protein versus bread flour''s 12 to 13%) makes a softer, slightly less chewy naan that still works well; reduce the milk by 10 to 15 g since lower-protein flour absorbs less liquid. Atta or whole-wheat flour shifts the bread toward a roti-like density and is not traditional for naan.'
  - for: 'plain whole-milk yogurt'
    use: 'Greek yogurt thinned with water, or buttermilk'
    note: 'Greek yogurt is too stiff and low in free whey to enrich the dough properly; whisk it with 1 to 2 tbsp water to loosen it to pourable yogurt consistency first. Buttermilk works as a 1:1 swap for the yogurt and brings a comparable lactic tang, though slightly thinner; reduce the added milk by 20 g to compensate.'
  - for: 'ghee'
    use: 'unsalted butter or neutral oil'
    note: 'Melted unsalted butter works in the dough and as the brush but browns and scorches faster than ghee under the broiler, so brush after cooking, never before. A neutral oil keeps the bread tender but loses the nutty browned-milk-solid flavor that defines ghee; it is the right call only for a dairy-free version.'
  - for: 'nigella seeds'
    use: 'black sesame seeds, or omit'
    note: 'Nigella (kalonji) is the canonical topping, oniony and faintly bitter. Black sesame seeds give a similar visual and a nutty note but lack the onion edge. Omitting them is fine; the naan is defined by the enrichment and the char, not the seed.'

notes: |
  Why naan is enriched and pita is not. A lean flatbread (pita, lavash) is flour, water, salt, and leavening; its whole structural goal is a thin wall that traps steam and balloons into a pocket. Naan is the opposite design. The yogurt, milk, sugar, and ghee are not optional richness; they are the recipe. The lactic acid in the yogurt partially hydrolyzes the gluten network, which is why naan tears with a soft, pillowy chew instead of a crisp snap. The milk fat and the ghee coat the gluten strands and shorten them, keeping the crumb tender for hours. The lactose and added sugar are reducing sugars that brown hard and fast under the broiler, which is why naan develops the dark leopard-spot bubbles that a sugar-free pita never will. Substituting water for the milk and yogurt does not give you a lean naan; it gives you a bad pita.

  Why cast iron plus broiler approximates the tandoor. A clay tandoor cooks naan with two simultaneous heat sources: conductive heat from the 400 °C-plus clay wall the wet dough is slapped against, and radiant heat from the charcoal bed below. The dough sets and chars on the wall side while the exposed face blisters from the radiant heat above. A home oven tops out far below tandoor temperature, so the trick is to split the two heat sources. The cast-iron skillet, preheated to its smoke point, is the wall; laying the dough on it and pressing it flat sets and chars the contact face in under a minute. The broiler element is the charcoal ceiling; sliding the skillet back under it blisters the top face. Doing both in sequence in the same pan gets within striking distance of a real tandoor naan, far closer than baking on a stone alone, which only ever browns one face well.

  The two failure modes. The first is under-charring: pulling the naan when it is golden and even rather than dark-spotted and blistered. Naan is supposed to have aggressive bitter-char accents; an evenly tan naan reads bland and bready. Push the broiler further than instinct says. The second is over-drying: leaving it under the broiler past the point where the bubbles blacken. Naan goes from perfectly blistered to cracker-stiff in about 15 seconds, because the thin enriched dough has little water to spare. Stand at the oven door and watch it; this is not a set-a-timer step.

  On the yogurt and the rest. The two rests do different jobs and both matter. The bulk proof is fermentation: the yeast inflates the dough and the lactic bacteria in the yogurt deepen flavor. The 15-minute rest after dividing is purely mechanical gluten relaxation, so the balls roll out thin without springing back. Skip that second rest and the dough fights you; the naan ends up thick and the bubbles never form because the surface is too taut to inflate.
