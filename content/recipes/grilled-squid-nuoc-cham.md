before_you_start:
  - 'If serving with steamed rice or rice vermicelli (the canonical pairings), start the rice on a PARALLEL track so it lands when the squid comes off the grill. Jasmine rice (20-minute soak, 12-minute simmer, 10-minute rest) finishes in the same window as the squid scoring, marinade, and grill. Vermicelli is a 4-minute boil and a cold rinse; start it during the last 10 minutes of grill prep.'
  - 'Light a charcoal grill 20 to 25 minutes before you want to cook. A chimney of lump charcoal needs that window to develop the white-ash bed that holds 250 °C / 480 °F under the grates. Gas grills need 10 minutes on high; an indoor grill pan needs 5 minutes on the highest setting.'
  - 'Mix the nuoc cham first. The dipping sauce improves over the first 30 minutes as the garlic and chili infuse the sweet-sour-salty base, and the dish is structurally as much about the sauce as the squid. Build it before scoring and marinating the squid so the bloom runs while you work.'
  - 'Hold the squid marinade to 15 minutes, no longer. Squid muscle is resistant to brine penetration and longer marinades toughen the protein through ionic dehydration rather than seasoning it more deeply. Bring the squid to near room temperature before it hits the grates; cold squid steams rather than chars.'

steps:
  - text: 'Score the squid. Lay each cleaned tube flat with the inside facing up. Cross-hatch score at 5 mm grid spacing, cutting only halfway through the wall. Leave the tube whole (do not cut into pieces, for grilling, the whole tube cooks more evenly than pieces). Leave the tentacles whole. Pat dry with paper towels.'
    technique: 'scoring-squid'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Marinate. In a wide bowl, whisk together 30 ml / 2 tbsp fish sauce, 25 g minced lemongrass, 16 g minced garlic, 20 g minced shallot, 12 g palm sugar (mash with a spoon to dissolve), 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil, and 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. Add the scored squid tubes and tentacles. Toss gently to coat, the squid should be evenly slicked with marinade. Set aside 15 minutes (no longer; over-marinated squid toughens). Bring the squid to near room temperature before grilling, cold squid steams rather than chars.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Make the nuoc cham. In a small bowl, dissolve 30 g / 2 tbsp palm sugar in 90 ml / 6 tbsp warm water (whisk until clear). Add 45 ml / 3 tbsp fish sauce and 45 ml / 3 tbsp fresh lime juice. Whisk to combine. Stir in 8 g minced garlic and 6 g minced chili. Taste; the sauce should land at sweet-sour-salty-spicy in roughly equal parts, with the fish sauce providing the deep savory backbone. Adjust: if too salty, more lime + a splash of water; if too sour, more sugar; if flat, more chili. Set aside; the sauce improves in 30 minutes as the flavors meld.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Heat the grill. Charcoal grill is traditional and best (lump charcoal lit with a chimney 20 to 25 minutes ahead). Gas grill works (preheat 10 minutes on high). Indoor grill pan or cast-iron skillet works (heat 5 minutes on highest setting until the surface is just smoking). The temperature target is 250°C / 480°F surface temperature, you should not be able to hold a hand 5 cm above the grates for more than 2 seconds.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Grill the squid. Lift the squid from the marinade (let excess marinade drip off, too much marinade causes flare-ups and steaming). Place the tubes on the grill scored-side down first; the scoring will curl the tube into a cup shape within 15 seconds, which is normal and what you want. Grill 60 to 90 seconds; the underside should char at the cut edges and the surface should turn opaque white. Flip with tongs (do not pierce with a fork, the punctures release juice). Grill the second side 45 to 60 seconds; the surface should be similarly charred and the squid should feel just-firm to the tongs (not rubbery-springy). Remove to a serving platter immediately. Grill the tentacles separately (45 to 60 seconds per side; they cook faster). Total grill time per piece is 90 seconds to 2 minutes; longer overshoots into rubber.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Plate and serve. Cut the grilled tubes into 2 cm rings or leave whole (Vietnamese tradition serves them whole; the diner cuts pieces with chopsticks). Arrange on a serving platter with the tentacles. Scatter the cilantro and mint over the top. Serve with the nuoc cham in small individual dipping bowls, and 4 to 6 lime wedges (1 lime cut) on the side. Eat by chopstick: pick up a piece of squid, dip generously in nuoc cham (the dipping sauce is the dish, be generous), eat with rice or vermicelli noodles. The piece should hit the tongue with the smoke-and-char of the grilling, then the lime-fish-sauce-chili of the dip, then the herbal note of the mint and cilantro.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why the brief marinade. Squid muscle is structurally resistant to brine penetration (see ingredients/squid), so marinating squid more than 30 minutes provides minimal additional flavor and longer marinades (over 2 hours) actively toughen the muscle through ionic dehydration. The 15-minute window is enough for the surface seasoning and the lemongrass-and-garlic aromatics to coat the cuts of the scoring; longer is counterproductive. This is why Vietnamese grilled squid recipes tend to be relatively uncomplicated marinades, the dish is built on the freshness of the squid and the dipping sauce, not on a deeply-marinated protein.

  The 90-second window over fire. Grilled squid is the textbook 90-second high-heat application on a grill rather than in oil. The scoring (see techniques/scoring-squid) curls the tube into a cup shape on contact with the grill, which both maximizes surface area for char and shortens the cooking time by reducing the diffusion distance for heat. Total grill time across both sides should be 90 seconds to 2 minutes; this seems aggressive to cooks accustomed to grilling fish steaks (which take 4 to 6 minutes per side), but for squid the short window is the rule. Most home grilled-squid failures come from over-cooking; if your grilled squid comes out rubbery, the answer is almost always less time on the heat next time.

  Why nuoc cham IS the dish. In Vietnamese cooking, the dipping sauce is not a "complement", it is structural to the eating experience. Nuoc cham is what makes plain grilled squid into a Vietnamese dish; without it, the squid is just charred squid with herbs. The four-element balance (sweet from sugar, sour from lime, salty from fish sauce, spicy from chili) is the classical Vietnamese flavor target, and the proportions matter. The home version above is calibrated to a 1:1:1 ratio of fish-sauce-to-lime-to-sugar by volume (with water diluting); this is the Saigon-leaning standard. Hanoi versions tend slightly less sweet (reduce sugar to 20 g), Mekong-delta versions tend slightly sweeter (increase sugar to 40 g), try the standard ratio first, then adjust.

  Lemongrass technique. Lemongrass is woody and fibrous in its raw stalk form; mincing it correctly is the difference between an aromatic marinade and a chunky stringy one. Trim the top half of the stalk (the dry green part) and discard. Trim the root end. Peel away the dry outer layers (1 to 2 layers, depending on freshness) until you reach the tender pale-green core. Slice the core thinly into rounds (2 mm), then run the knife through the rounds at 90 degrees to mince finely. The minced lemongrass should be in pieces no larger than 2 mm; anything larger reads stringy in the marinade. Frozen pre-minced lemongrass (Asian-grocery freezer aisle) is excellent and saves the 5 minutes of stalk-prep.

  Eating mechanics. Grilled squid with nuoc cham is part of a Vietnamese seafood-and-rice meal, often served alongside grilled prawns, grilled clams, and a vegetable preparation (morning glory stir-fried with garlic, or Vietnamese pickles). The herbs (mint, cilantro, sometimes Thai basil and perilla) are not garnish, they are eaten with each bite, picking herb leaves and folding them around the squid before dipping. Beer (especially Bia Saigon, Bia Hà Nội, or any cold lager) is the canonical pairing; a crisp dry white wine works at a sit-down dinner. The dish does not reheat well, squid that has been cooked once and refrigerated reheats into rubber. Eat fresh; portion the marinade and grilling for the meal at hand.
