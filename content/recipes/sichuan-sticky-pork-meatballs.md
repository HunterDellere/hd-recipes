steps:
  - text: 'Combine the 565 g / 1.25 lb ground pork with the 4.5 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt and the 1.5 g / ¼ tsp baking soda in a bowl. The salt extracts myosin from the muscle fibers and the baking soda raises pH, which keeps the proteins from gripping into a dry knot on cooking. Add the 12 g / 1 tbsp grated ginger, the 20 g / 3 tbsp minced scallion whites, the 1 g / ½ tsp white pepper, the 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy sauce, the 15 g / 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine, the 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, the 50 g / 1 egg, and the 9 g / 1 tbsp cornstarch.'
    technique: 'myosin-extraction'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Stir hard in a single direction for 2 to 3 minutes until the mass turns visibly tacky and pulls against the spoon. That drag is the soluble myosin cross-linking into the network that gives a Chinese meatball its bounce. Rest 15 minutes in the fridge to firm.'
    technique: 'myosin-extraction'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Roll into 16 balls of about 40 g each, wetting your palms to keep them from cracking.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Heat a heavy skillet over medium-high with the 14 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil to shimmering, around 190°C / 375°F. Lay the meatballs in without crowding and leave them undisturbed for 2 minutes per face. Moving them early tears the forming crust and forfeits the Maillard browning that anchors the dish''s savory depth. Roll to brown three or four sides, about 6 minutes total. They will not be cooked through; the glaze finishes them. Transfer to a plate. Pour off all but a thin film of fat, leaving the browned fond.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Lower heat to medium. Into the same pan add the 24 g / 1.5 tbsp doubanjiang with the 15 g / 3 cloves minced garlic and the 8 g / 2 tsp minced ginger. Bloom in the fat for 60 to 90 seconds until the oil stains red and turns nutty. Watch it: the fermented beans scorch fast and go acrid past that point.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Deglaze with the 30 g / 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine, scraping the fond up, then add the 120 g / ½ cup chicken stock, the 23 g / 1.5 tbsp light soy sauce, the 7 g / 1 tsp dark soy sauce, and the 40 g / 2 tbsp crushed rock sugar. Simmer to dissolve the sugar.'
    technique: 'deglazing'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Return the meatballs and any plate juices. Simmer at a steady bubble, 88 to 93°C / 190 to 200°F, turning to coat, until the meatballs read 71°C / 160°F internal and the glaze reduces to a syrup that sheets off a spoon, roughly 5 to 7 minutes. The dissolved sugar raises the boiling point and thickens the liquid into a lacquer that clings rather than runs.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Pull from the heat. Finish with the 10 g / 2 tsp Chinkiang vinegar and the few drops of toasted sesame oil off the flame: boiling the vinegar would drive off its volatile acetic and malty aromatics and leave the dish flat-sweet. Scatter the 14 g / 2 tbsp scallion greens.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'The Adjustment. If the glaze reads flat and one-dimensionally sweet, add Chinkiang vinegar in ½ tsp increments before more soy; the acid is what cuts the sugar, and that is the first thing missing. If it tastes sweet but hollow, a scant ½ tsp more doubanjiang stirred in at the end pushes the fermented-glutamate floor up. If the glaze breaks into a greasy slick, you reduced too hard and too far; splash in 1 tbsp stock and swirl off-heat to re-emulsify. If the meatballs read rubbery and tight, you took them past 74°C / 165°F and squeezed out moisture; next time pull at 70°C / 158°F and let carryover finish.'

notes: |
  The bounce in a Chinese pork meatball comes from myosin extraction, not from breadcrumbs. Salt is an ionic agent: it dissolves the myosin out of the muscle fibers, and the baking soda's higher pH keeps those proteins from over-tightening. Working the mince in one direction shears the freed myosin into aligned strands, and when those strands hit heat they denature and cross-link into a continuous elastic gel that traps fat and water. The result is the springy, snappy texture (the "Q" of the bite) that a loose, breadcrumb-bound Western meatball cannot produce.

  Build the glaze separately rather than pouring liquid over the meatballs in the pan: the doubanjiang has to bloom in fat to stain the oil red and turn nutty, and that only happens against the bare hot pan, not in a wet braise. Once it has bloomed, the deglaze and the sugar build the lacquer, and the meatballs finish cooking in it.
