before_you_start:
  - 'Mole poblano is a three-and-a-half hour project with over twenty distinct ingredients across five toasting phases. Read the full recipe and stage every ingredient on a sheet pan before turning on heat. Mid-cook ingredient hunting is the most common point of failure.'
  - 'The simmer-and-meld phase needs 90 minutes minimum at a tremor (small bubbles, not a boil). Time this so you are not pressed for service; the mole tastes meaningfully better at the 2-hour mark than at the 90-minute mark.'
  - 'Make the mole 1 to 3 days ahead if possible. The flavor integrates and deepens overnight in the refrigerator; this is how mole is made traditionally in Puebla and Oaxaca.'
  - 'Have a high-powered blender and a fine-mesh sieve ready. The sauce must be pureed in batches then strained through the sieve; a standard blender will leave the texture grainy and the grit ruins the dish.'

steps:
  - text: 'Stage every ingredient on a sheet pan before turning on heat. Mole has five toasting phases across twenty-plus ingredients; mid-cook ingredient hunting is the structural failure mode. Set out the 40 g ancho, 30 g mulato, 20 g pasilla, 6 g chipotle morita, 60 g almonds, 40 g peanuts, 35 g sesame (reserving 5 g for garnish), 30 g pepitas, 50 g raisins, 100 g plantain rounds, 30 g torn tortilla, 200 g chopped onion, 30 g whole garlic cloves, 200 g halved tomatoes, 100 g tomatillos, and the spice quartet (5 g canela, 1 g cloves, 3 g black pepper, 1 g allspice, 2 g cumin seeds, 2 g oregano).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Toast the chilies. Heat a comal or heavy dry skillet over medium-low. Toast the 40 g ancho, 30 g mulato, 20 g pasilla, and 6 g chipotle morita pieces in batches, 30 to 45 seconds per side, pressing down with tongs until fragrant and the surface darkens slightly. Do not burn; burnt chilies turn the mole bitter and are unrecoverable. Transfer to a large bowl, cover with 1 L of boiling water, weight down with a plate to keep submerged, and soak 25 minutes until pliable.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'While the chilies soak, char the vegetables. Char the 200 g halved tomatoes and 100 g tomatillos and 30 g whole garlic and 200 g onion (in chunks) directly over a gas flame, under a broiler, or on a hot comal until blackened in patches, 5 to 7 minutes total. Set aside; do not peel off the char.'
    technique: 'comal-charring'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Toast the nuts and seeds. In the same dry skillet over medium-low, toast the 60 g almonds 4 minutes until light golden and aromatic. Add the 40 g peanuts; toast 2 minutes. Add the 35 g sesame seeds and 30 g pepitas; toast 60 to 90 seconds until the sesame seeds turn golden and the pepitas puff. Transfer everything to a plate to cool.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Toast the spices. In the same skillet, toast the 5 g canela stick (broken into pieces), 1 g cloves, 3 g peppercorns, 1 g allspice, and 2 g cumin seeds together for 60 to 90 seconds until aromatic. Add the 2 g Mexican oregano off heat (it scorches quickly). Grind everything together to a fine powder in a spice grinder; set aside.'
    technique: 'spice-tempering'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Fry the plantain and tortilla. Heat 30 g of the 80 g lard in a small skillet over medium heat. Fry the 100 g plantain rounds 4 minutes per side until deeply golden; transfer to the nut plate. Fry the 30 g torn tortilla pieces 1 minute per side until dark brown and crisp; transfer to the nut plate. Add the 50 g raisins to the same pan; fry 30 seconds until plump and slightly toasted; transfer.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Blend phase one (the chili paste). Drain the soaked chilies; reserve 250 g of the chili soaking liquid. Combine the soaked chilies with 200 g of the soaking liquid in a high-powered blender; blend 2 minutes to a smooth paste. Pass through a fine-mesh sieve into a bowl, pressing with a spatula to extract all the puree (discard the solids; this is the structural grit-removal step). The yield is roughly 400 g smooth chili paste.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Blend phase two (the nut-fruit-vegetable paste). In the same blender, combine the toasted nuts and seeds (35 g sesame plus 30 g pepitas plus 60 g almonds plus 40 g peanuts), the fried plantain, fried tortilla, fried raisins, charred tomatoes, charred tomatillos, charred onion, charred garlic, and 500 g of the hot 1500 g chicken stock. Blend 3 minutes to a smooth paste; add more stock as needed to keep the blender moving. Pass through a fine-mesh sieve into a second bowl; this is structural.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Fry the chili paste. Heat the remaining 50 g lard in a large heavy-bottomed pot (5 L Dutch oven works well) over medium-high heat until shimmering. Pour in the 400 g chili paste all at once; it will sizzle aggressively (the canonical mole-frying technique; the splatter is real, use a splatter screen). Reduce to medium; fry 8 to 10 minutes, stirring constantly with a wooden spoon, until the paste has darkened to a deep mahogany and the oil starts to separate at the edges. This step develops the structural Maillard depth that distinguishes mole from a generic chili stew.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Combine and simmer. Add the nut-fruit-vegetable paste to the fried chili paste; stir to combine. Add the 2 g ground spice powder. Pour in the remaining 1000 g hot chicken stock, gradually, stirring with each addition. The sauce should be thick but pourable, similar to heavy cream. Bring to a bare simmer, reduce heat to low, and cook uncovered 60 to 90 minutes at a tremor (small bubbles, not a boil), stirring every 10 to 15 minutes to prevent scorching on the bottom. The mole reduces by about 20% and deepens in color and flavor through this window.'
    time_min: 90
  - text: 'Finish with chocolate and sugar. Add the 50 g Mexican chocolate (broken into pieces) and 20 g piloncillo to the simmering mole; stir until fully dissolved (3 to 4 minutes). Add the 12 g kosher salt. Taste and adjust: mole poblano should read deep, mahogany-colored, slightly sweet from the chocolate and fruit, with chili warmth at the back and a long complex finish. If flat, more salt; if bitter, more sugar; if too sweet, a splash of vinegar (5 g cider or red wine vinegar) to brighten. Continue simmering 10 minutes more for the chocolate to integrate fully.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Rest before serving. Pull from heat, cover, and rest 20 minutes (or refrigerate overnight, the canonical approach in Puebla; the mole tastes meaningfully better at 24 hours than at 2 hours). Serve over poached or roasted chicken or turkey, with a fistful of toasted sesame seeds (the reserved 5 g) on top, alongside white rice, warm corn tortillas, and refried beans. A pinch of toasted sesame goes on each plate; rice and tortillas are non-negotiable.'
    time_min: 20

notes: |
  Mole poblano is the longest, most architecturally complex sauce in the Mexican canon. Done right, it is a sauce of remarkable depth, the kind of dish that recalibrates the eater's understanding of what a sauce can carry. Done wrong (burnt chilies, under-fried paste, grainy texture, oversweet chocolate), it is a punishing 4-hour project that produces a forgettable brown gravy. The technique tolerates no shortcuts and the ingredient list tolerates no substitutions of major components.

  The chili-toasting step is where most home cooks fail. Dried chilies must be toasted briefly (30 to 45 seconds per side) until fragrant but not blackened. Burnt chilies turn the entire mole bitter and there is no rescue; the only path forward is to discard and restart. Work in small batches, press down gently with tongs, and pull the moment the surface darkens slightly past raw. The toast is structural to the flavor; under-toasted chilies produce a raw vegetal mole; correctly-toasted chilies produce the deep complex base.

  The sieve step is structural. Mole must be passed through a fine-mesh sieve at the puree stage to remove chili skins, nut grit, and tomato seeds; without it, the texture is unpleasantly gritty and reads as a failed sauce. Press the puree through with a flexible spatula; expect to discard about 15 to 20% of the volume as fibrous solids. The straining is non-negotiable; restaurant mole is always strained.

  The fry-the-paste step is where the mole develops its structural Maillard depth. The chili paste is poured into hot lard and fried 8 to 10 minutes until it darkens noticeably and the oil separates at the edges. This is the same technique that produces a refried bean (frying transforms a boiled paste into a Maillard-developed deep sauce) and the same technique that produces a salsa ranchera (raw salsa fried in hot oil develops structural depth). Skipping or shortening this step produces a raw-tasting mole that no amount of simmering can fix.

  The overnight rest is the secret. Mole tastes meaningfully better at 24 hours than at the immediate post-cook moment; the flavors integrate, the heat softens, the sweetness rounds, and the chocolate fully suspends through the sauce. Plan to make it the day before serving whenever possible. Refrigerated mole keeps 5 to 7 days and freezes 3 months; the texture is unchanged through freezing.

  The canonical service is mole over poached or roasted chicken or turkey (turkey is the structural Pueblan choice for feasts), with white rice, warm corn tortillas, and a fistful of toasted sesame seeds scattered over the plate. Some Pueblan cooks add a few sliced raw white onion rings and a sprinkle of crumbled queso fresco; both are acceptable additions. The mole is the structural ingredient and the protein is the vehicle; do not flip this balance by drowning the chicken in too little sauce or by garnishing aggressively.

  Storage. Refrigerated 5 to 7 days in a sealed glass jar; frozen 3 months in 250 g portions. Reheat gently with a splash of stock or water to restore the original consistency; the sauce thickens visibly in the refrigerator. Mole paste (the pre-stock state, after the fry step) is the traditional storage form in Mexico; tightly packed in a jar with a thin film of lard or oil on top, it keeps refrigerated 2 weeks or frozen 3 months, ready to thin with stock and use.
