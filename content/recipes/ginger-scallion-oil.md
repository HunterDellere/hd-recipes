steps:
  - text: 'Mince 60 g / 4-inch knob fresh ginger as finely as possible; the cuts release allyl compounds and soften the fibers so the oil can extract through the cell walls. The texture target is a coarse paste, not a brunoise. Knife technique: thin coins, then julienne, then crosswise mince. Microplaning works but produces a slightly wetter result.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Mince 100 g / 8-10 stalks scallion whites and pale greens to the same fineness. Combine with the ginger in a heatproof bowl (metal or thick ceramic; the oil will hit 190°C / 375°F).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Stir 4 g / ½ tsp kosher salt and the optional 8 g / ½ tbsp light soy sauce into the aromatic paste. The salt cure pulls a tablespoon of water out of the ginger and scallion in 5 minutes, which is what allows the oil to penetrate; without the cure, the high water content steam-cooks the aromatics into a soft mush rather than the gentle deep-fry the technique calls for. Rest 5 minutes.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Heat 200 ml / ¾ cup + 1 tbsp neutral oil in a small saucepan over medium-high until just smoking, about 190°C / 375°F. The visual cue: a wooden chopstick dipped in produces vigorous bubbles around it within a second. If you have a thermometer, use it; if you do not, count from the first wisp of smoke and pull within 30 seconds.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Pull the oil off heat and let it cool 10-15 seconds, just until the surface stops shimmering aggressively. Target temperature: 175-180°C / 350°F. Too hot and the aromatics go bitter; too cool and the oil never extracts.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour the hot oil over the aromatic paste in three stages, stirring with a heatproof spoon between each. The mixture will foam and crackle violently for 30 seconds; this is the moisture flashing off. The first pour does the deep extraction, the second sets the green color, the third (cooler) preserves the surface aromatics. The aromatics should sizzle vigorously but stay green-yellow rather than browning to deep gold.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Stir thoroughly. The bowl should look like a green-flecked aromatic suspension settling to a low fizz. Stir in 8 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame oil and the optional 5 g / 1 tsp rice vinegar off heat. Cool to room temperature on the counter, uncovered, 30-45 minutes.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Transfer to a clean glass jar (oil and aromatics together). Cover. Use immediately or refrigerate. The oil deepens over the first 24 hours as the aromatics continue to release flavor compounds.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  How to use ginger-scallion oil: as a finishing condiment, never a cooking oil. The classic deployments are over white-cooked Cantonese chicken (the dish the oil was invented for); over plain steamed rice as a near-instant lunch; tossed with cooked noodles and a splash of soy as cong you ban mian; spooned over the bowl of any congee variant; over silken tofu as a cold appetizer; over a softly fried egg with rice; as a dip alongside steamed dumplings.

  The temperature window is the technique. Below 175°C the oil never extracts, and the aromatics stay raw and harshly oniony. Above 200°C the aromatics caramelize and brown, losing the bright green color that signals a proper batch and turning fundamentally bitter. The 175-190°C window is wide enough that a careful cook with no thermometer can hit it by feel, but a thermometer turns the recipe into a precise reproducible operation.

  Why the brief salt cure: high-water aromatics dropped into oil flash-steam and lower the oil temperature instantly, which can drop you below the extraction threshold mid-pour. The 5-minute salt cure pulls about 15-20% of the surface water out of the ginger and scallion, which keeps the oil hot during the pour and lets the aromatics gently fry rather than boil.

  Storage: refrigerate in a sealed glass jar. The oil keeps two weeks refrigerated and the flavor peaks at days 2-5. Past two weeks, the aromatics begin to soften and the oil itself stays good but the bright green-onion note dulls. Pull from the refrigerator 10 minutes before serving so the oil regains its pourable consistency. Do not introduce water (wet spoon, condensation lid); that is what spoils any aromatic oil.
