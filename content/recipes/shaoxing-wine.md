steps:
  - text: 'Stub: soak 2000 g / 4.4 lb glutinous rice in cold water for 8 hours; drain. Steam over rapidly boiling water for 60 minutes (in a bamboo steamer with a damp cheesecloth liner, or in a metal steamer with similar humidity protection). The rice should be fully translucent and fully tender, with no white starch core remaining. Spread on a clean tray to cool to body temperature (30 to 35 °C / 86 to 95 °F); rice that is too hot kills the starter, rice that is too cool ferments slowly.'
    time_min: 540
  - text: 'Stub: when the rice is at body temperature, sprinkle 30 g / 2 tbsp qu starter evenly across the surface and toss gently with clean hands to distribute. Pack loosely into a clean fermentation crock or food-grade plastic bucket. Make a well in the center of the rice (this fills with the resulting wine over the next several days). Cover with a fitted lid that allows gas escape (a fermentation airlock or a loose-fitting lid).'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Stub: ferment at 25 to 30 °C / 77 to 86 °F for 5 to 7 days; the qu mold breaks down the rice starch into glucose, the yeast in the qu starter ferments the glucose into ethanol, and the well in the center of the rice fills with sweet rice wine. After 5 to 7 days, add the 3000 ml / 12 cups filtered water and stir to break up the rice mash; the resulting cloudy liquid is the moromi for the secondary ferment.'
    time_min: 8640
  - text: 'Stub: continue fermentation at 18 to 22 °C / 65 to 72 °F (cooler than the primary ferment) for 4 to 8 weeks. The yeast continues to ferment the residual sugars and the alcohol level rises to 14 to 16 percent. After the secondary ferment, strain the moromi through cheesecloth, transfer the clear liquid to clean glass bottles, and age in a cool dark place for 6 to 12 months. The aging develops complex aromatic compounds through slow oxidation and esterification; the finished wine is amber, smells distinctly winey-fruity, and tastes dry-savory with a faint sweetness.'
