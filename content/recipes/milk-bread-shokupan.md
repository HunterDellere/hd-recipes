title: 'Milk bread (shokupan)'
desc: 'Japanese tangzhong milk bread baked in a Pullman tin. A small portion of the flour is pre-cooked with milk into a thick paste (the water roux), which gelatinizes the starch ahead of mixing so the crumb holds far more water without going wet. The result is the pillowy, feather-soft, faintly sweet pain de mie loaf that defines Japanese bakery shokupan: a fine even crumb, a thin pale crust, and a shelf life measured in days rather than hours.'

  note: 'Tangzhong is a Chinese name (湯種, "soup starter") for a technique shared across the region; the Japanese cognate yudane uses boiling water rather than a cooked roux but solves the same problem. The shape and crumb target here are specifically Japanese shokupan: the square-cornered pain de mie loaf baked with the Pullman lid on, prized for a uniform fine crumb with no large irregular holes.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Make the tangzhong first and let it cool to room temperature (about 25 to 30 minutes) before mixing the dough. A hot roux will kill the yeast and slacken the gluten; the paste must be barely warm to the touch, around 25°C, when it goes into the mixer.'
  - 'This is a two-rise enriched dough. Budget roughly 3.5 to 4 hours start to finish: 35 minutes for the tangzhong, 60 to 90 minutes bulk fermentation, 10 minutes shaping, 45 to 60 minutes final proof in the tin, then 35 minutes baking. None of it is hands-on for long, but the loaf cannot be rushed.'
  - 'Pull the butter and milk out 30 minutes ahead so they come to room temperature. Cold butter will not laminate evenly into the dough during the slap-and-fold, and cold milk slows the yeast.'
  - 'Have a 9x4-inch / 23x10 cm Pullman (pain de mie) tin with its sliding lid. Without the lid the loaf domes and the crumb opens; the lid is what produces the square corners and the tight even crumb that define shokupan. A standard 9x5-inch loaf pan baked uncovered is the fallback, noted in the modifications.'

steps:
  - text: 'Cook the tangzhong. In a small saucepan off the heat, whisk the 20 g / 2½ tbsp bread flour with the 50 g / 3½ tbsp whole milk and the 50 g / 3½ tbsp water until completely smooth with no lumps. Set over medium-low heat and whisk constantly. At about 65°C the starch granules begin absorbing water and swelling; by 65 to 70°C the mixture thickens abruptly into a glossy paste that holds a line when you drag the whisk across the pan bottom. This is starch gelatinization: the amylose and amylopectin chains hydrate and lock water into a gel. Pull it the instant it reaches pudding thickness, about 2 to 3 minutes total, before it scorches. Scrape into a small bowl, press a sheet of parchment directly onto the surface to stop a skin forming, and cool to room temperature (around 25°C), about 25 to 30 minutes.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Combine the dry and wet, hold the butter back. In the bowl of a stand mixer, whisk the 290 g / 2⅓ cups bread flour, the 35 g / 2½ tbsp granulated sugar, and the 5 g / 1½ tsp instant yeast on one side of the bowl; add the 6 g / 1 tsp fine sea salt on the opposite side so it does not sit directly on the yeast. Add the cooled tangzhong, the 125 g / ½ cup room-temperature whole milk, and the 50 g / 1 beaten egg. Do not add the 30 g / 2 tbsp butter yet, fat added before the gluten forms coats the flour and stalls network development.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Mix to a shaggy mass, then develop the gluten. With the dough hook on low speed, mix until no dry flour remains, about 2 minutes, then raise to medium and knead 6 to 8 minutes. The dough is wet and sticky at first; resist adding flour. As the glutenin and gliadin proteins cross-link into gluten, the dough pulls away from the bowl sides and gathers around the hook. It will look smooth and feel tacky but no longer wet.'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Laminate in the butter. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp softened butter a few pieces at a time with the mixer on medium-low. The dough will break apart and look greasy and broken; keep kneading. Over 5 to 7 minutes the fat disperses into thin films between the gluten sheets (shortening them just enough for tenderness without collapsing the structure) and the dough comes back together smooth, glossy, and elastic. Test for the windowpane: stretch a walnut-sized piece between your fingers; it should pull into a thin translucent membrane you can almost see through before it tears. If it tears thick and ragged, knead another 2 minutes and test again.'
    technique: 'gluten-development'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Bulk ferment. Round the dough into a tight ball, place it seam-side down in a lightly oiled bowl, and cover with a damp kitchen towel. Ferment at warm room temperature (24 to 27°C) until doubled, 60 to 90 minutes. The tangzhong-enriched dough ferments a touch slower than a lean dough because the sugar and fat compete for water and slow the yeast; judge by volume, not the clock. It is ready when a finger poked 1 cm into the dough leaves a dent that springs back slowly and only partway.'
    technique: 'bulk-fermentation'
    time_min: 75
  - text: 'Divide and pre-shape. Turn the dough onto a lightly floured counter and gently deflate it. Divide into 3 equal pieces (about 220 g each); a scale is worth using here so the rolls fill the tin evenly. Round each piece into a smooth ball, cover with the damp towel, and rest 15 minutes. This bench rest relaxes the gluten so the dough rolls out without snapping back.'
    time_min: 17
  - text: 'Shape the spirals. Working one piece at a time, roll a ball into an oval about 20 cm long with a rolling pin. Fold the two long sides into the center to make a narrow strip, then roll it up tightly from the short end into a snug spiral, like a small cinnamon roll. Pinch the seam closed. The tight roll is what gives shokupan its characteristic swirled, fine-grained crumb. Place the three spirals seam-side down and side by side in the buttered Pullman tin, leaving small gaps between them; they will merge as they proof.'
    technique: 'loaf-shaping'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Final proof. Cover the tin with the damp towel (lid off for now) and proof at 24 to 27°C until the dough crowns to about 1 cm below the rim of the tin, 45 to 60 minutes. This is the critical window. Under-proofed dough tears and bakes dense; over-proofed dough collapses and the crumb goes coarse. The dough should look pillowy and feel airy and slow to spring back when pressed lightly. Twenty minutes before baking, set a rack in the lower-middle of the oven and preheat to 190°C / 375°F.'
    time_min: 55
  - text: 'Glaze and bake covered. Brush the dough tops lightly with the 10 g / 2 tsp whole milk for a soft matte crust (skip the egg wash that would brown it dark; shokupan crust stays pale). Slide the Pullman lid fully closed and bake 30 to 35 minutes. The closed lid forces the dough into square corners and a tight even crumb, and traps steam early for maximum oven spring. The loaf is done at an internal temperature of 90 to 93°C / 195 to 200°F; check by sliding the lid back and probing the center with an instant-read thermometer.'
    technique: 'enriched-bread-bake'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Depan immediately and cool fully. Slide off the lid, turn the loaf out of the tin onto a wire rack at once; left in the hot tin it steams and the crust goes soft and damp. The crumb is still setting from gelatinized starch and coagulated egg protein; slicing hot tears it and lets steam escape, drying the loaf. Cool at least 1 hour, ideally 2, before slicing. Slice thick for shokupan-style milk toast, thin for sandwiches.'
    time_min: 60

notes: |
  Why tangzhong works. The water roux is the entire reason this loaf stays soft for days. When you cook a small fraction of the flour with liquid to 65°C and above, the starch granules gelatinize: they swell and burst, and their amylose and amylopectin chains absorb water and lock it into a gel that holds far more liquid than raw flour can. Folding that pre-gelatinized paste into the dough lets you push the overall hydration higher (this dough runs around 75% hydration counting the roux) without a slack, unmanageable dough. More bound water in the baked crumb means a softer texture out of the oven and a dramatically slower stale rate, because staling is largely starch retrogradation (amylopectin recrystallizing and squeezing out water) and the gelatinized tangzhong starch resists that recrystallization. A tangzhong loaf is still tender on day three where a plain milk bread is dry by the next morning.

  The 6.5% rule. The standard tangzhong fraction is roughly 5 to 7% of the total recipe flour cooked into the roux, at a 1:5 flour-to-liquid ratio by weight. This recipe uses 20 g of the 310 g total flour (6.5%) cooked with 100 g of liquid. Below 5% the softening effect is faint; above about 10% the gel weighs down the crumb and the loaf bakes gummy. The 1:5 ratio is what produces a paste thick enough to hold a whisk line but loose enough to fold smoothly into the dough.

  Yudane versus tangzhong. The Japanese yudane method pours boiling water directly over a portion of the flour and rests it (often overnight) rather than cooking a roux on the stove. Both gelatinize the starch; the cooked tangzhong is faster and more controllable for a same-day bake, which is why it dominates outside Japan. If you want the most authentically Japanese version, mix 60 g flour with 60 g boiling water the night before, rest it covered in the refrigerator, and substitute it for the tangzhong (reduce the dough milk by about 30 g to compensate).

  Why the Pullman lid. Shokupan is defined as much by shape as by crumb. The sliding lid constrains the rising dough into a square cross-section with no dome, which forces a tighter, more uniform crumb with small even cells (no large irregular holes), and produces the thin pale crust that distinguishes shokupan from a Western enriched loaf. The lid also traps steam in the first minutes of the bake, extending oven spring. Bake without the lid and you get a perfectly good domed milk loaf, but it is no longer technically shokupan.

  Proof judgment is the whole game. Enriched doughs are slower and more forgiving than lean doughs in the bulk ferment but unforgiving at the final proof. The dough should crown to about 1 cm below the tin rim before the lid goes on; with the lid closed it will spring the rest of the way to fill the corners. Too early into the proof and the loaf tears at the seams and bakes dense; too late and it collapses or pushes the lid open. When a light press leaves a slow, partial dent, it is ready.

  Slicing and storage. Cool the loaf fully before slicing; cutting hot tears the still-setting crumb and vents the steam that keeps it moist. Store cut-side down on the board under a clean towel for a day, or wrap the cooled loaf in beeswax wrap or a fitted lidded container for 3 to 4 days. Shokupan freezes exceptionally well: slice first, freeze in a single layer, then bag the slices, and toast straight from frozen. Day-old shokupan makes the best French toast and the canonical Japanese milk-toast (thick slice, butter, sugar, brief broil).
