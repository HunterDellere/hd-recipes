before_you_start:
  - 'The dough must chill at least 1 hour before cutting. Sablé Breton is rolled thick, and a thick, butter-heavy dough has to be cold to cut a clean heart edge and to hold its shape rather than spreading into a blob.'
  - 'The signature top is two coats of yolk glaze with a fork-scored crosshatch between. Let the first coat dry before the second, then score; that is what bakes to the deep, lacquered gold a sablé Breton is known for.'
  - 'Salted butter is traditional here. If you use it, leave out the added fine salt; if you use unsalted, the recipe adds the salt back as fleur de sel for the same effect.'

steps:
  - text: 'Cream the butter, sugar, and salt. Beat the softened unsalted butter (10 tbsp / 140 g) with the granulated sugar (½ cup / 100 g) and the fine sea salt (¾ tsp / 3 g) until smooth and slightly pale, 2 to 3 minutes. Unlike a crisp shortbread, sablé Breton wants a little of this aeration; it is part of what gives the sandy, tender crumb rather than a hard snap. Beat in the egg yolks (2 / 34 g) one at a time, then the vanilla bean paste (1 tsp / 4 g), scraping the bowl until uniform and creamy.'
    technique: 'creaming-butter-sugar'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Sift the all-purpose flour (1⅔ cups / 200 g) with the baking powder (1 tsp / 4 g) and add to the bowl in two additions, mixing on low just until the dough comes together with no dry streaks. Stop the moment it clumps; the yolks make this a soft, rich dough, and overworking develops gluten that trades the sandy crumb for a tough one.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Press the dough into a disc, set it between two sheets of parchment, and roll it a generous 8 mm (⅓ in) thick. Sablé Breton is meant to be thick and substantial, not a thin snap. Slide the sheet onto a tray and chill at least 1 hour, until firm and cold throughout.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 350°F (175°C) and line two sheets with parchment. Cut hearts from the cold dough with a cutter and lift them onto the sheets with an offset spatula, spacing them 3 cm apart; they hold their shape and barely spread. Gather and re-chill scraps before re-rolling once. Whisk the wash yolk (1 / 18 g) with the heavy cream (1 tsp / 5 g) and brush a thin coat over each heart.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Let the glaze dry 5 minutes, brush a second thin coat, then drag the tines of a fork across each top to score a shallow crosshatch, the traditional Breton finish that bakes into raised, lacquered ridges. Bake 15 to 18 minutes, rotating once, until the tops are glossy deep gold and the edges are set and lightly browned. Unlike a matcha or a pale shortbread, a sablé Breton is meant to take real color; the gold is butter and yolk browning, and it is the flavor.'
    time_min: 16
  - text: 'Cool on the sheet 5 minutes; they are fragile and sandy straight from the oven and firm as they cool. Move to a rack to cool completely, where the crumb sets to its final tender-crisp sand. They keep their texture about a week in an airtight tin.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'The Adjustment: if the hearts spread and lose their edge, the butter was too warm or the dough undercooled; re-chill the cut shapes 15 minutes before baking. If they are hard rather than sandy, they were overbaked or the dough was overworked; pull at deep gold next time and mix the flour in only until it just clumps. If the tops are dull instead of lacquered, the glaze went on too thick in one coat or skipped the dry-between step; two thin coats with a rest is what builds the shine. If they taste flat, the salt is low; use the full ¾ tsp / 3 g fine salt (or salted Breton butter), which is doing real flavor work here.'

notes: |
  What makes a sablé a sablé. Sable is French for sand, and the word names the texture: a short, sandy, crumbling bite rather than the dense snap of Scottish shortbread or the crisp of a wafer. Three things build it here. The egg yolks add fat and lecithin that emulsify the dough and shorten it; the brief creaming pumps in just enough air to keep the crumb tender rather than hard; and the teaspoon of baking powder gives a small lift that opens the crumb into sand instead of letting it bake into a solid slab. Drop the baking powder and the yolks and you have shortbread; together they are a sablé Breton.

  Why salted butter, against the usual pastry rule. Most French pastry uses unsalted butter so the baker controls the salt, and that is the right default. Sablé Breton is the deliberate exception: it comes from Brittany, the heart of French salted-butter and fleur-de-sel country, and the salt is a defining flavor, not a seasoning to be hidden. Whether you use true beurre demi-sel or unsalted butter plus fleur de sel, the cookie should taste faintly, pleasurably salty against the butter and sugar. That salt-against-sweet tension is most of why the cookie is so easy to keep eating.

  The glaze and the fork. The twice-brushed yolk wash is not only decorative. The first thin coat soaks slightly into the surface and dries; the second sits on top and bakes to a glossy, deep-gold lacquer, richer than a single coat ever gets because the dried first layer keeps the second from sinking in. The fork-scored crosshatch raises ridges that catch the heat and brown a shade darker, the visual signature of the biscuit, and the scoring also relieves surface tension so the tops bake flat and even rather than doming.

  Roll thick, bake to gold. Two instincts to resist if you bake a lot of shortbread. Roll these thick, a full 8 mm, because the substantial, almost cakey-sandy bite is the point and a thin sablé Breton just shatters. And let them take real color: where a matcha or a delicate sablé is pulled pale to protect it, the Breton is meant to brown, because the gold is the Maillard browning of butter and egg yolk and that is exactly the toasty, rich flavor you are after.
