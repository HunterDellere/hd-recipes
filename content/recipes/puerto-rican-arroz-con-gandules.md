  name: 'Puerto Rican national dish canon'
  note: 'Arroz con gandules is the national dish of Puerto Rico and the centerpiece of every Christmas and Thanksgiving plate (alongside pernil). The structural elements are: medium-grain or long-grain rice cooked with sofrito (recaito); pigeon peas (gandules), the small green-brown Caribbean pulse; cured ham for umami; Spanish manzanilla olives and capers for the salt-acid balance; achiote oil for the orange color; and the canonical Puerto Rican spice trio of cumin, oregano, and bay. The signature pegao (the golden crust that forms at the bottom of the pot) is the most prized portion and is shared specifically among family at the table.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Achiote (annatto) oil is the structural color of the dish; the rice should turn deep orange. Make a small batch ahead by warming 60 ml neutral oil with 5 g whole annatto seeds over low heat 5 minutes, then straining. Or use 1 packet of Goya Sazon con culantro y achiote (~5 g) stirred into the rice cooking liquid; this is the modern home-cook shortcut and produces the same color and a slightly more savory profile.'
  - 'Pegao (the golden bottom crust) is structural and intentional. Use a heavy-bottomed pot (Dutch oven or caldero), do not stir the rice during the final 15 minutes of cooking, and trust the bottom-of-pot heat to crisp the rice without burning it. The pot must be wide rather than tall for the right pegao surface area.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the achiote oil. Heat the 45 ml / 3 tbsp neutral oil in a small saucepan over low heat. Add the 5 g whole annatto seeds; warm 4 to 5 minutes (do not exceed 80 °C / 175 °F or the seeds turn bitter). The oil should turn deep red-orange. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve, discarding the seeds. Use immediately, in a heavy-bottomed Dutch oven or caldero over medium heat.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'In the same Dutch oven or caldero with the warm achiote oil, add the 100 g diced ham; render 3 to 4 minutes until lightly golden and the oil has gained additional flavor.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add the 60 g / ¼ cup sofrito; bloom 90 seconds, stirring constantly. The kitchen should smell aggressively of recao and garlic. Add the 100 g tomato sauce and 15 g tomato paste; cook 2 minutes, stirring, until the paste darkens from bright to brick red and the mixture turns into a glossy red-orange base.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add the 250 g drained pigeon peas, 40 g sliced olives, 10 g capers, 3 g / 1½ tsp cumin, 2 g / ¾ tsp oregano, and 2 bay leaves. Stir 1 minute to combine.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 400 g / 2 cups rinsed rice; stir for 90 seconds in the orange sofrito-and-pea base until every grain is coated and the rice turns deep orange. Add the 700 ml / 3 cups water or stock and the 8 g / 2 tsp salt. Stir once to distribute.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Bring to a hard boil over medium-high heat, uncovered. Once boiling, stir once to lift any rice that has sunk to the bottom and ensure even distribution. Reduce heat to medium-low; let the rice cook uncovered until the liquid has reduced to just below the surface of the rice, about 8 minutes. Bubbles will rise through small craters across the rice surface.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Stir the rice once more, gently, scraping the bottom to distribute the bottom and top rice. Cover with a tight-fitting lid; reduce heat to the lowest setting. Cook covered without lifting the lid for 20 minutes. The bottom of the pot will be forming the pegao during this time; the steam from above continues to cook the rice.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Off heat, keep covered and rest 10 minutes. Uncover; fish out and discard the bay leaves. The pegao should be a golden-brown crust about 3 mm thick at the bottom of the pot; lift the rice with a spoon to see it. Scoop the soft rice from on top into a serving dish, then scrape and break up the pegao with the spoon and lay the crispy pieces on top of the soft rice. Serve hot.'
    time_min: 10

modifications:
  - for: 'shortcut'
    to: 'as same-day sofrito (no batch-paste required)'
    how: 'Skip the pre-made sofrito. Finely dice 100 g yellow onion, 80 g green bell pepper, 4 cloves garlic, 30 g cilantro, and 2 recao leaves. Sweat all in the achiote oil 5 minutes at the start of step 2 (before adding the ham). The result is a slightly less integrated sofrito-flavored rice but acceptable for a one-off cook without batch sofrito on hand.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'protein addition'
    to: 'with chicken (arroz con pollo y gandules)'
    how: 'Add 4 bone-in chicken thighs (skin-on, marinated 1 hour in 30 g sofrito plus 6 g salt) to the pot at step 2 (after the achiote oil and before the ham). Sear the chicken 4 to 5 minutes skin-side down to render the fat; remove and reserve. Proceed with the recipe as written, returning the seared chicken to the pot at step 5 (when the rice goes in), tucking the pieces into the rice before adding the liquid. Total cook becomes a one-pot Sunday-dinner dish.'
    kind: 'protein'

notes: |
  Why the pegao. The pegao (literally "stuck") is the golden-brown crust that forms at the bottom of the pot during the final covered cook. It is structural to the dish, prized, and traditionally shared among the family at the table; in Puerto Rican households the pegao is the most contested portion. The conditions for pegao formation are: a heavy-bottomed wide pot, sufficient fat (the achiote oil plus ham fat), low constant heat, and the discipline to not stir during the final 20 minutes. A non-stick pot or a thin-bottomed saucepan will not produce a real pegao; the bottom needs to conduct sustained low heat into the rice for 15+ minutes without burning the rest of the pot.

  The fluff-or-no-fluff rule. Unlike Persian rice (which is fluffed off the lid and gently mixed) or Indian basmati (which is fluffed with a fork), arroz con gandules is served in a specific way: scoop the soft top rice into the serving dish first, then break up the pegao and lay it on top. The pegao should not be mixed back into the soft rice; the textural contrast of crispy crust over soft pillowy rice is the point.

  The 3:2 liquid ratio. 700 ml liquid for 400 g rice is a slightly drier ratio than standard rice cooking (1.75:1 instead of 2:1); this is necessary for the pegao to form (excess liquid keeps the bottom from drying out and crisping). The pigeon peas contribute minimal extra water.

  Storage. Keeps 3 days refrigerated. Reheat with a splash of water in a covered pan over low heat; the pegao softens on storage and cannot be revived. Best eaten same day for the pegao experience.
