steps:
  - text: 'Score and prep the squid. Cross-hatch score the tube interior at 5 mm grid spacing, cutting only halfway through the wall. Cut the scored sheet into 3 cm × 3 cm squares. Cut the tentacles into 4 cm pieces. Pat dry with paper towels.'
    technique: 'scoring-squid'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Mix the sauce. In a small bowl, whisk together 35 g gochujang, 8 g gochugaru, 18 ml / 1¼ tbsp soy sauce, 15 ml / 1 tbsp mirin, 6 g / 1½ tsp sugar, 18 g minced garlic, 8 g minced ginger, and 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil. The sauce should be a thick paste; set aside. Pre-mixing prevents fumbling during the high-heat stir-fry.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Prep the vegetables. Slice the 150 g onion into 1 cm wedges, julienne the 80 g carrot into 5 cm × 3 mm matchsticks, cut the 50 g scallion into 4 cm pieces (separate whites from greens, whites go in earlier), and cut the 100 g cabbage into 4 cm squares. Have everything within arm''s reach of the wok.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Stir-fry the aromatics and vegetables. Heat a wok or wide skillet over high heat until lightly smoking. Add 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil. Add the onion wedges, carrot matchsticks, and scallion whites. Stir-fry 90 seconds; the onion should soften slightly and the edges should brown lightly. Add the cabbage if using; stir-fry 30 seconds.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the squid and sauce. Push the vegetables to one side of the wok. Add the squid pieces (tubes and tentacles) to the empty side. Spoon the gochujang sauce directly onto the squid and toss to coat. Stir-fry continuously for 90 to 120 seconds, the squid will curl into pinecone shapes from the scoring within the first 30 seconds, the sauce will reduce and glaze, and the dish should land at the moment when the squid is just opaque through and tender, not rubbery. The total squid contact time is 90 to 120 seconds; do not extend "to make sure it''s cooked." Squid that has been cooked into the rubber zone cannot be saved.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish. Pull off heat. Add the scallion greens, sprinkle with 4 g / 2 tsp sesame seeds, and drizzle with 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil. Toss once to combine. Transfer to a serving plate. Serve immediately with steamed short-grain rice; the dish is also excellent as ssam (lettuce wraps with perilla leaves, banchan, and rice).'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Korean stir-fry seasoning grammar. The sauce in this dish, gochujang + gochugaru + soy + mirin + sugar + garlic + sesame oil, is a textbook example of Korean stir-fry seasoning. It uses fewer components than Sichuan stir-fry seasoning (which typically includes doubanjiang, douchi, dried chilies, sometimes fermented chili oil, plus the alliums) but each component does heavier structural work. Gochujang is the central paste contributing salt, sweet, savory, and color. Gochugaru is the chili-flake top note for color and aromatic heat. Soy sauce is the salt depth. Mirin and sugar are the structural sweetness that defines Korean cooking. Garlic and sesame oil are the aromatic backbone. The pattern repeats across dakgalbi (chicken), jeyuk bokkeum (pork), bulgogi (beef), and many vegetable preparations; learning the grammar lets you riff across the cuisine.

  Why the squid pre-curls so dramatically. Korean squid stir-fry is the most visually dramatic of the cross-hatch-scored squid applications because the squid hits the wok with a thick gochujang paste that immediately glazes onto the cuts of the scoring; the curl-and-glaze happens together within the first 30 seconds. The pinecone bumps come up bright red and glossy from the sauce, and the visual is what defines the dish on Instagram and at Korean barbecue restaurants. Skip the scoring and the squid pieces lay flat in the wok and the sauce slides off the smooth surface; the dish is recognizably a different (less impressive) version.

  Why mirin specifically. Mirin (sweet rice cooking wine, real hon-mirin not the fake "mirin-fu chōmiryō") is structural to Korean stir-fry sauces because it contributes both sugar and ethanol-soluble aromatic compounds that integrate the gochujang, soy, and sesame into a unified flavor rather than three layered tastes. Sake with added sugar gets close but lacks the aromatic depth. The Korean home-cooking pantry has shifted in the last 30 years from rice wine (cheongju) plus separate sugar to mirin-as-default; both work, but mirin produces a slightly more polished result. Many Korean cookbooks specify "맛술" (matsul, "flavor liquor") which is the broad term that includes hon-mirin and mirin-fu products; either is fine for this dish.

  Eating mechanics. Ojingeo bokkeum is part of a Korean meal: rice bowl, soup or stew (a clear miyeokguk seaweed soup or a doenjang jjigae stew), and 2 to 4 banchan (kimchi, namul, pickles). Each diner takes spoonfuls onto rice; the spicy-sweet sauce flavors the rice. The lettuce-wrap (ssam) format is a major upgrade for a sit-down meal and worth doing when company is over. Beer (especially Korean lagers like Hite or Cass), soju, or makgeolli are the traditional alcohol pairings. The dish reheats acceptably the next day for lunch but is best fresh; squid that has been cooked once and reheated tends toward the rubber zone unless reheated very carefully (briefly, in a covered skillet with a splash of water).

  The traditional comma-shape cuts. Korean cooks sometimes cut squid into a comma-shape (one straight side, one curved side) instead of the cross-hatch cut squares; the comma-shape is cosmetic and produces a visually distinctive piece, but functionally the cross-hatch scoring is what does the work. Cross-hatch + square is the textbook approach and is what most Korean cooking tutorials show; comma-shape is a refinement for cooks who care about plate presentation.
