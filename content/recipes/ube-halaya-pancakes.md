before_you_start:
  - 'Pull the ube halaya from the fridge 15 minutes before mixing. Cold jam is too stiff to fold cleanly and will tear streaks of unincorporated batter; room-temperature halaya disperses in 5 to 6 folds without overmixing the gluten.'
  - 'Toast the desiccated coconut at the very start and tip it onto a cold plate. Pan carryover takes toasted coconut from gold to bitter in under 60 seconds, and the toasted coconut is the finishing aromatic; do not toast at the end with a hot pan and hurried plating.'
  - 'These pancakes cook on a lower-heat pan than classic buttermilk pancakes. The anthocyanin pigment in ube degrades above 80 °C in dry heat, and any aggressive browning on the underside flips brown-grey over the violet. Medium-low, butter foaming but not browning, is the target.'

steps:
  - text: 'Toast 8 g / 4 tsp desiccated coconut in a small dry stainless skillet over medium-low heat, shaking constantly, until the shreds turn uniform pale golden and smell intensely roasted-nutty, about 90 seconds. Tip immediately onto a cold plate; the pan carry-over takes it from gold to bitter in under 60 seconds.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Combine 200 ml / ¾ cup + 1 tbsp whole milk with 12 ml / 2½ tsp white vinegar in a measuring cup. Stir once and let stand 5 minutes at room temperature. The acid denatures casein micelles, causing visible curdling; that is correct. You now have soured milk that functions identically to buttermilk for leavening purposes.'
    technique: 'acid-milk-sour'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Whisk together in a large bowl: 145 g / 1¼ cups all-purpose flour, 8 g / 2 tsp granulated sugar, 5 g / 1¼ tsp baking powder, 2 g / ⅓ tsp baking soda, and 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt. The reduced baking soda is deliberate. Ube anthocyanin is a pH indicator: vivid violet at pH 5.5 to 7.0, drifting blue-grey above pH 7.5. The standard ½ tsp of baking soda in a pancake batter pushes pH past that line and the finished cakes read blue, not violet. Two-thirds the standard dose preserves color while still neutralizing enough acid to drive the second lift.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Whisk the 50 g / 1 large egg into the soured milk. Add 28 g / 2 tbsp melted cooled butter and 4 ml / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste. The butter must not be hot; above 70 °C it begins to cook the egg proteins and the batter will be lumpy from the outset.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour the wet into the dry. Fold with a spatula, 6 to 8 strokes, stopping at streaks of flour. Add the 110 g / scant ½ cup room-temperature ube halaya in a single dollop on top of the streaky batter, then fold 4 to 5 more strokes only, until the violet is distributed but the batter still shows lighter swirls. Adding halaya before the wet meets the dry weighs the leavening down; adding it last preserves the gas-cell network the soured-milk reaction has already started. Let the batter rest 3 minutes while you heat the pan; starch granules hydrate and the initial CO2 reaction builds the first wave of structure.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Set a wide skillet or griddle over medium-low heat (one notch lower than classic pancakes). Add a 2 g pat of butter and let it melt completely. When the foam subsides but the butter has not yet darkened toward amber, the pan is at roughly 160 °C / 320 °F. This is below the threshold for visible Maillard browning, which is what you want: aggressive browning on the underside will read brown-grey through the violet crumb. The pancakes will still set and rise; they just stay lighter colored.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour 60 to 70 ml / ¼ cup batter per pancake. Cook undisturbed until bubbles break the surface and the edges set dry, 2½ to 3 minutes (slightly longer than classic, since the lower heat slows the surface set). Flip once with a wide spatula. Cook the second side 90 seconds to 2 minutes until the center springs back when pressed lightly. The cooked pancake should be a pale lavender on both faces with darker violet flecks where halaya pooled.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Wipe the pan between batches and add a fresh 2 g pat of butter each time. Burnt milk solids from the previous batch lower browning temperature and will char the bottom of the next round before the interior sets. Transfer finished pancakes to a 95 °C / 200 °F oven on a wire rack set over a sheet pan; stacking them traps steam and makes them soggy.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Plate four pancakes per serving. Dollop 30 g / 2 tbsp of the chilled coconut cream over the top, then drizzle the warmed 30 g / 2 tbsp ube halaya across the cream in thin ribbons. Shower the toasted desiccated coconut over the whole stack. Skip the maple syrup; halaya is already at 35 to 45% sugar by weight, and the coconut-cream-and-jam finish is the canonical Filipino pairing for ube-flavored breakfast dishes (it echoes the *suman sa lihiya* finish of grated coconut over coconut sauce on sticky rice).'
    time_min: 1

  - for: 'occasion'

    how: 'Fold 40 g / ¼ cup grated mild cheddar or queso de bola into the batter alongside the halaya. The salty-sharp cheese against the sweet violet jam is the same logic as ensaymada (Filipino sweet bread topped with grated cheese); it sharpens the halaya rather than competing.     kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Why fold the halaya in last rather than whisk it into the wet phase. Ube halaya is roughly 35 to 45% sugar by weight and 8 to 14% fat by weight; it is dense and viscous. Whisking it into the wet ingredients before they meet the flour weighs down the leavening from the first second, slowing the soured-milk-plus-baking-soda CO2 release and producing a flatter, more leaden cake. Folding it in after the wet meets the dry preserves the gas-cell network the chemical reaction has already started and gives you a noticeably taller stack with visible violet veining instead of a uniformly tinted batter.

  Why under-leaven with baking soda. The pH-color rule for anthocyanins is the operative constraint here. Violet at pH 5.5 to 7.0; drift toward blue-grey above pH 7.5; pink-red at pH below 4. A normal pancake batter with ½ tsp baking soda lands at pH 7.6 to 7.8 after the reaction completes, which is exactly the zone that reads blue-grey on a pigment as sensitive as ube anthocyanin. Cutting the baking soda to ⅓ tsp lands the finished batter around pH 6.8 to 7.0, which is the upper edge of the vivid-violet zone. The lost leavening from the smaller dose is recovered by the baking powder (which provides a second thermal lift in the pan).

  Why no maple syrup. Maple syrup is the default North American pancake finish, but its woody-caramel aromatics fight ube and the brown color visually muddies the violet. The canonical Filipino sweet-breakfast finish is coconut-based: latik (toasted coconut curds), grated young coconut, or coconut caramel sauce. Coconut cream plus toasted desiccated coconut is the streamlined version of that pairing and lets the halaya read clean.
