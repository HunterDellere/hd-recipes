source:
  name: 'Adapted from the South German Laugenbrezel tradition (Bavaria, Swabia), where the defining step is a dip in food-grade sodium hydroxide solution (Natronlauge) before baking.'
  note: 'The authentic Bavarian and Swabian pretzel is bathed in a 3 to 4% food-grade lye (sodium hydroxide) solution, not baking soda. The lye raises the surface pH to roughly 13 to 14, which is what drives the fast, dark Maillard browning and the characteristic pretzel aroma. Baked baking soda (sodium carbonate, pH ~11) is the standard home substitute and gives 80% of the result with none of the caustic-handling hazard. This recipe specifies the lye method as the best version and the baked-soda bath as the accessible fallback.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Lye safety, read before deciding which bath to use. Food-grade sodium hydroxide (lye) is caustic and will burn skin and eyes on contact. If you use the 3% lye bath: wear nitrile gloves and eye protection, work in a ventilated space, ALWAYS add the lye to the water and never water to lye (the reaction is exothermic and can spatter), use a stainless steel or glass bowl (never aluminum, which lye corrodes), and keep a bowl of vinegar nearby to neutralize any splash. Lye on raw dough is fully neutralized by the heat of baking and is food-safe once baked. If any of this gives you pause, use the baked-baking-soda bath in Phase 3b instead; it is genuinely good and carries none of the risk.'
  - 'Total time is about 2 hours 15 minutes, most of it passive. Plan a 60-minute bulk proof and a short 15-minute bench rest after shaping. The dough is a stiff, low-hydration lean dough (about 55% hydration); it will feel firmer and less tacky than a sandwich dough, and that is correct.'
  - 'If using the baked-soda bath, bake the baking soda first (Phase 3b), it takes an hour in the oven and must cool before you dissolve it. Start it during the bulk proof so it is ready when the pretzels are shaped, or bake it the day before.'
  - 'Bring the bath ingredients and the shaped pretzels to room temperature before dipping, and have the salt, a slotted spoon or skimmer, and parchment-lined sheet pans staged. The dip is fast and you do not want to be searching for tools with a caustic or alkaline solution in front of you.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the dough. Whisk the 7 g / 2¼ tsp instant yeast and the 12 g / 1 tbsp light brown sugar into the 275 ml / 1 cup plus 2 tbsp water warmed to 35 °C. Add the 500 g / 3¾ cups bread flour, the 9 g / 1½ tsp fine sea salt, and the 30 g / 2 tbsp softened butter. Mix to a shaggy mass, then knead 8 to 10 minutes by hand or 6 minutes in a stand mixer with the dough hook on medium. This is a stiff, low-hydration dough at about 55% hydration; it should come together smooth and firm, slightly tacky but not sticky. The strong gluten network is what lets you roll thin ropes that hold their twist without tearing.'
    technique: 'enriched-dough'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Bulk proof. Shape the dough into a ball, place it in a lightly oiled bowl, and cover with a damp towel. Let it rise at room temperature until roughly doubled, about 60 minutes. A stiff dough proofs a little slower than a wet one; judge by volume, not the clock. While it proofs, if you are using the baked-soda bath, start Phase 3b now.'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'Shape the pretzels. Turn the dough onto an unfloured counter (a slightly tacky surface helps the rope grip and roll). Divide into 8 equal pieces of about 100 g each. Roll each piece into a rope about 50 cm long, thick in the middle and tapering to thin ends. To form the knot: lay the rope in a U, cross the two ends over each other twice to make a twist, then fold the twisted ends down onto the bottom of the U and press to seal. Set the shaped pretzels on parchment-lined sheet pans and let them rest uncovered 15 minutes; a brief skin-drying rest helps the bath cling evenly.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Phase 3a, the lye bath (authentic). Read the safety note in Before You Start first. Put on nitrile gloves and eye protection. Pour the 1000 ml / 4¼ cups cold water into a stainless steel or glass bowl, then slowly add the 30 g / 2 tbsp food-grade lye to the water (never water to lye) and stir gently with a stainless spoon until dissolved; the solution will warm slightly. One at a time, lower each shaped pretzel into the bath with a stainless slotted spoon, submerge for 15 to 20 seconds, lift, let it drain, and return it to the parchment. Do not let the bath touch bare skin. This is the step that delivers the true Laugenbrezel skin and flavor. Skip to Phase 4. (When finished, neutralize leftover bath with a splash of vinegar before pouring it away.)'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Phase 3b, the baked-soda bath (safe alternative). First, bake the baking soda: spread the 80 g / ⅓ cup baking soda on a foil-lined sheet pan and bake at 120 °C / 250 °F for 1 hour, then cool completely. Baking drives off water and carbon dioxide, converting sodium bicarbonate into the more alkaline sodium carbonate (washing soda) and raising the bath pH from about 8 to about 11; the higher pH is what pushes the Maillard browning. Dissolve the cooled baked soda into the 1000 ml / 4¼ cups water just off the boil in a wide stainless or glass pot. Wearing gloves (the solution is mildly caustic), submerge each pretzel for 20 to 30 seconds with a slotted spoon, drain, and return to the parchment.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Slash and salt. With a sharp paring knife or a razor, score one shallow slash along the thick belly of each pretzel (the fat part of the bottom curve). The slash splits open in the oven into the classic pretzel mouth and controls where the crust bursts. Immediately sprinkle the wet, dipped surface with the 15 g / 2 tbsp coarse pretzel salt; the alkaline film is tacky and grips the crystals. Salt now, while the surface is damp from the bath; a dried surface sheds the salt.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Bake. Bake at 230 °C / 450 °F for 12 to 15 minutes, until the crust is deep mahogany brown and glossy. The dark color is the visual proof that the alkaline surface did its job: the raised pH accelerates the Maillard reaction between the dough sugars and amino acids, so the crust browns far faster and darker than ordinary bread at the same temperature, and the same reaction generates the roasted, faintly bitter pretzel aroma. Rotate the pans halfway through for even color. Pull when the tops read true mahogany, not merely golden; a pale pretzel is an under-developed pretzel.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Cool and serve. Transfer to a rack and cool at least 10 minutes; the crust crisps and the crumb sets as it cools. Soft pretzels are best within a few hours of baking, while the skin still has its sheen and snap and the interior is tender. Serve warm with grainy or sweet-hot mustard, the alkaline-savory crust and the sharp acidic mustard are the canonical pairing, the mustard supplying the bright acid that the rich crust wants.'
    time_min: 12

notes: |
  Why the alkaline bath is the whole dish. An ordinary bread dough sits around pH 5 to 6, slightly acidic from the yeast fermentation. At that pH the Maillard reaction (the browning between reducing sugars and amino acids) runs slowly, which is why ordinary bread browns to gold over a long bake. Raising the surface pH dramatically accelerates Maillard: the amino groups are deprotonated and far more reactive, so browning runs fast and dark even in a short bake. That is the entire reason a pretzel browns to mahogany in 12 minutes while a baguette takes much longer to reach a paler gold. The bath also gelatinizes the surface starch, which is what gives the skin its characteristic glossy sheen and chewy snap. The pretzel flavor itself, that roasted, faintly bitter, savory note, is a direct product of running Maillard hard at high pH.

  Lye versus baked baking soda. Food-grade sodium hydroxide at 3% takes the surface to pH 13 to 14 and is the authentic Bavarian and Swabian method; nothing else matches its color, gloss, and flavor depth. Baked baking soda (sodium carbonate, pH ~11) is the safe home standard and gets you most of the way there. Plain unbaked baking soda (pH ~8) is the weakest and gives a pale, slightly soapy-metallic result. The hierarchy of result tracks the hierarchy of pH exactly: higher pH, darker crust, deeper flavor. The lye is fully neutralized by the heat of baking and the finished pretzel is completely food-safe; the hazard is entirely in the handling of the raw solution, which the safety note addresses.

  Why bread flour and low hydration. The pretzel dough is deliberately stiff and lean (about 55% hydration, no enrichment beyond a little fat). The low water and high protein give a dough strong enough to roll into thin ropes that hold a tight twist without tearing or springing back, and a crumb that is chewy rather than fluffy. A wetter, softer dough makes a pretzel that sags out of its twist and bakes into something closer to a roll. The small amount of butter is the one concession to tenderness; leave it out entirely and the pretzel is more rustic and chewy, which is closer to a true Bavarian Brezn.

  The malt question. Barley malt syrup is the traditional sweetener and contributes both flavor (the malty, roasted note that reads as quintessentially pretzel) and extra maltose for the crust to brown. Light brown sugar is the accessible stand-in used here; it feeds the yeast and adds a faint molasses note. If you want the most authentic result and can find it, use barley malt syrup, and if you want maximum crust color, add a pinch of diastatic malt powder for its browning enzymes.

  Slashing and salt timing. The shallow slash on the belly is functional, not decorative: it gives the expanding crust a controlled place to burst, producing the open pretzel mouth instead of random cracking. Salt the moment the pretzel comes out of the bath, while the alkaline film is still tacky; that film is what glues the coarse crystals to the crust. A pretzel salted after the surface has dried sheds most of its salt in the oven.
