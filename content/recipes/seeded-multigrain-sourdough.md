  name: 'Modern home sourdough tradition, multigrain seeded-loaf branch'
  note: 'The seeded multigrain sourdough extends the white-and-whole-wheat country loaf (Tartine and Forkish lineages) with two Northern and Central European bread-craft techniques: the soaker (a hot-water seed soak, the German Quellstück / Brühstück) and the porridge or scald (cooked grain folded into the dough, the German Kochstück). Both exist to solve the same problem in seed-and-grain breads: dry seeds and broken grains are powerful water sinks that, added straight, draw water out of the developing gluten matrix and yield a dry, tight, crumbly crumb. Pre-hydrating the seeds and pre-gelatinizing the oat starch loads the water into those components before mixing, so the dough hydrates the gluten as intended and the finished loaf holds moisture for days.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the seed soaker (the night before, alongside the levain). Combine the 70 g sunflower seeds, 50 g pepitas, 35 g flax seeds, and 25 g sesame seeds in a heatproof bowl. Pour the 180 g boiling water directly over them and stir once. The reasoning is the entire premise of this loaf: dry seeds are aggressive water sinks. Added straight to the dough, they pull free water out of the gluten matrix over the bulk ferment, and the loaf bakes up dry, tight, and crumbly. Hydrating them in boiling water first loads the water into the seeds before they ever touch the dough, and the heat softens the hulls and starts the flax mucilage. Cover the bowl with a plate or a damp towel and leave it on the counter overnight; the seeds keep absorbing as they cool.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Make the oat porridge / scald (the night before). Combine the 60 g rolled oats and 180 g water in a small saucepan. Bring to a simmer over medium heat, then cook, stirring, for 4 to 5 minutes until the oats collapse into a thick porridge and the starch gelatinizes (the mixture turns glossy and pulls away from the pan in a mass). Gelatinized starch holds far more water than raw flour and releases it slowly during and after the bake; this is the technique that keeps a multigrain loaf moist for days rather than going dry by the second morning. Scrape the porridge into a bowl, cover, and leave on the counter overnight to cool fully. It must be at room temperature before it goes into the dough.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Build the levain (the night before, 8 to 10 hours before mixing). Combine the 30 g active starter, 100 g bread flour, and 100 g room-temperature water in a clean jar. Stir to a smooth paste, cover loosely so gas can escape, and leave at 22 to 24°C / 72 to 75°F until tripled, domed, bubbled, and pleasantly tangy. Float test: a spoonful dropped in cold water should float, confirming the levain is mature and aerated enough to leaven the heavier seeded dough.'
    time_min: 540
  - text: 'Autolyse the flours. Combine the 700 g bread flour, 150 g whole wheat flour, 50 g rye flour, and 560 g warm water in a large bowl. Mix by hand or with a Danish dough whisk until no dry flour remains; the dough will be shaggy. Cover with a damp towel and rest 45 minutes. During the autolyse the flour fully hydrates, the gluten begins to crosslink passively, and the flour enzymes start converting starch to sugar. Hold the soaker and porridge back until after this rest: adding them now would let the seeds and gelatinized starch interfere with clean gluten development.'
    technique: 'autolyse'
    time_min: 50
  - text: 'Mix in salt, honey, and levain. Add the 22 g fine sea salt, the 15 g honey, and the 200 g matured levain to the autolysed dough. Pinch and fold vigorously by hand for 3 to 4 minutes using a slap-and-fold motion (lift, slap down, fold over) until the salt and levain are fully distributed and the dough turns smoother and stretchier. Developing the gluten now, before the seeds and porridge go in, matters: a strong gluten network is what suspends the heavy seed load without the loaf collapsing dense.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Fold in the soaker and porridge. Add the full 305 g cooled seed soaker and the 230 g cooled oat porridge (broken into clumps) to the dough. Fold and squeeze with a wet hand until the seeds and porridge are evenly dispersed, 2 to 3 minutes. The dough will loosen noticeably as the porridge integrates, then firm back up over the bulk; this slackening is expected and is not a sign to add flour. Cover with the damp towel.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Bulk ferment with stretch-and-folds. Ferment at 22 to 24°C / 72 to 75°F for 4 to 4.5 hours. Perform stretch-and-folds at minutes 30, 60, 90, and 120 (4 sets): wet one hand, reach under one side, lift the dough to full stretch, fold it over the top, rotate the bowl 90 degrees, and repeat 3 more times per set. The folds rebuild the strength that the seeds and porridge interrupt. After the 4th set, leave the dough undisturbed for the remaining time. The rye and whole wheat ferment faster than white flour, so watch the dough rather than the clock: it should rise about 50% with visible surface bubbles and a slightly domed, jiggly mass.'
    technique: 'stretch-and-fold'
    time_min: 255
  - text: 'Pre-shape and bench rest. Lightly flour a clean work surface. Turn the dough out with a wet bench scraper. Pre-shape into a loose round: pull the edges into the center, flip seam-side-down, and drag the dough in tight circles toward you to build surface tension on the bottom. The seeded dough has less stretch than a plain loaf, so handle it firmly but without tearing. Cover with the damp towel and rest 25 to 30 minutes to relax the gluten.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Final shape. Lightly flour the surface. Flip the pre-shaped round so the smooth side is down. Stretch gently into a rough rectangle, fold the bottom third up and the top third down like a letter, then roll into a tight cylinder from one side. Pinch the seam closed, flip seam-side-down, and drag in tight circles to set the surface tension. Transfer the shaped loaf seam-side-UP into a banneton dusted with the 30 g rice flour or semolina (a 10-inch round banneton, or a bowl lined with a flour-dusted towel).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Coat the crust in sesame. Have the reserved 30 g sesame seeds spread on a small plate. Lightly mist or wet-brush the exposed underside of the dough now facing up in the banneton (this will become the top crust after the turn-out), then sprinkle the sesame over it and press gently so the seeds adhere. The water gives the seeds something to grip; pressed onto a wet surface, they toast into the crust during the bake instead of falling off.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Cold proof overnight. Cover the banneton with a shower cap or a damp towel and refrigerate at 4°C / 40°F for 12 to 16 hours. The cold slows the yeast to a fraction of its activity while the lactobacilli keep building acidity and aromatic complexity. On a seeded multigrain loaf the long cold proof does double duty: it develops flavor and it gives the soaker and porridge final hours to equilibrate their moisture evenly through the crumb.'
    technique: 'cold-proof'
    time_min: 780
  - text: 'Preheat the oven and Dutch oven. Preheat the oven with the Dutch oven inside at 245°C / 475°F for 45 minutes. The vessel must be fully hot before the dough goes in; a cold start gives a pale crust and weak oven-spring.'
    time_min: 45
  - text: 'Score and load. Cut a 30 cm square of parchment. Pull the banneton straight from the refrigerator (cold dough scores and springs best; do not let it warm). Turn the dough out onto the parchment so the sesame-coated side is up and the seam is down. Score the top with one decisive slash about 1 cm deep using a lame or razor blade. Score off-center of the seeded surface if you can, so the slash opens through dough rather than tearing through the seed crust. The score controls where the loaf expands and prevents the crust bursting at a random weak point.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Bake covered for the oven-spring. Pull the hot Dutch oven out and remove the lid. Lower the parchment-and-dough in using the parchment edges as handles. Cover and bake 22 minutes. The trapped steam keeps the crust supple while the interior expands into full oven-spring; on a heavier seeded dough this covered phase is what buys the rise the seed load would otherwise suppress.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Uncover and finish. Pull the lid off with heavy mitts (steam releases hard). Reduce the oven to 230°C / 450°F and bake another 24 to 30 minutes uncovered until the crust is deep mahogany, the sesame on top is toasted golden, the loaf sounds hollow tapped on the bottom, and the internal temperature reads 96 to 99°C / 205 to 210°F. Multigrain loaves with a porridge inside benefit from the longer end of the bake, since the gelatinized starch holds heat and the center sets more slowly than a plain country loaf.'
    time_min: 27
  - text: 'Cool fully before slicing. Transfer to a wire rack and cool at least 2 hours before slicing, longer than a plain loaf. The crumb keeps cooking from residual heat, and the porridge-held moisture needs time to redistribute and set; slicing warm gives gummy, doughy slices and lets the held water steam off, undoing the moisture retention the soaker and scald were for. This cooling time is non-negotiable for the intended texture.'
    time_min: 120

notes: |
  What the soaker actually does, and why it is not optional. A seed is a dense package of fiber, protein, and oil with a dry, intact hull. Mixed straight into dough, the seeds sit in the gluten matrix and slowly wick free water out of it across the four-hour bulk and the overnight cold proof. The visible result is a loaf that looks fine out of the oven and is dry and crumbly by the next day, with a tight crumb and seeds that read as hard grit. Pouring boiling water over the seeds the night before front-loads the absorption: the seeds drink their fill before mixing, the heat softens the hulls so they eat tender rather than crunchy-hard, and the flax begins forming the soluble mucilage gel that binds the crumb. By the time the soaker goes into the dough, the seeds are passengers carrying their own water, not thieves taking the dough's. The boiling temperature is the operative detail; a cold soak hydrates more slowly and never fully softens the hulls.

  Why a porridge as well as a soaker. The soaker handles the whole seeds; the porridge (the German Kochstück, a cooked-grain scald) handles moisture retention in the crumb itself. Cooking rolled oats until the starch gelatinizes converts crystalline starch granules into a swollen, water-holding gel. That gel holds far more water than raw flour and releases it slowly during the bake and for days afterward, which is the entire reason this loaf stays moist to the fourth and fifth slice while a plain country loaf is dry by the second day. The porridge also adds a soft, slightly sweet oat character and lightens the crumb, offsetting the density the seed load would otherwise impose.

  Effective hydration accounting. The headline number is misleading if you only count the 560 g of dough water. The soaker carries 180 g of water and the porridge carries 180 g, so the dough sees roughly 920 g of water against 900 g of flour, plus the levain's contribution, landing the effective hydration near 75%. This is why the dough water is set lower than a comparable plain country loaf at the same hydration: the soaker and porridge are doing part of the hydrating. Reading the dough is more reliable than the math; it should be soft and tacky but hold a shape, not slack and soupy. If it pours rather than mounds, the porridge was too wet, so cook the next batch's porridge a minute longer.

  Levain strength matters more here than on a plain loaf. The seeds and the gelatinized oat starch add weight and interrupt the gluten network, so the dough needs more leavening power to lift it. Use the levain only at full maturity (tripled, domed, passing the float test); a sluggish levain that would still raise a plain white loaf can leave a seeded multigrain dense and gummy. If your starter has been neglected, give it two or three feedings over a couple of days before building the levain for this bake.

  Crust and crumb cues. The deep mahogany crust is structural, not cosmetic: it signals that the crust has dehydrated and developed the Maillard and caramelization compounds that make sourdough crust taste of more than flour, and it is the textural counterpoint to the soft seeded crumb. Pull the loaf too pale and the crust is leathery rather than crisp. The sesame coat toasts in the last covered-to-uncovered transition; watch it in the final minutes, since seeds go from golden to scorched quickly once the lid is off.

  Storage. Cooled completely, the loaf keeps at room temperature in a paper bag or wrapped in a kitchen towel for 4 to 5 days; the porridge-held moisture extends its useful life past a plain country loaf by a day or two. The crust softens progressively, which is expected. Freezing: slice the cooled loaf, wrap individual slices in parchment, store in a paper-lined freezer bag, and freeze up to 3 months; toast slices directly from frozen. Do not store at room temperature in a sealed container, since trapped moisture softens the crust and accelerates mold. A seeded crumb toasts exceptionally well, so day-old slices are best refreshed in the toaster rather than reheated whole.
