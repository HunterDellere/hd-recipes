before_you_start:
  - 'Cook the mung bean filling first, it needs to cool fully before shaping, or it will be too soft to roll into balls that hold their shape inside the dough. Steam, mash, and set aside while you do everything else; about 25 minutes of mostly passive time.'
  - 'Have two cooking vessels ready: one wide pot of plain water at a rolling boil for cooking the rice balls, and a separate saucepan with the ginger syrup at a low simmer. The cooked balls move from the boiling water directly into the simmering syrup.'
  - 'Shape all 16 balls before starting the boil. Cooking them in batches is fine; trying to shape while balls are boiling is how filling escapes into the water and the dough cracks.'

steps:
  - text: 'Filling. Drain the soaked 100 g / ½ cup split mung beans. Steam over rising water for 20 minutes until completely tender. While still warm, mash to a smooth paste with the 30 g / 2½ tbsp sugar, the 1 g pinch salt, the 10 g / 1 tbsp chopped fried shallots, and the 3 g / ½ tsp toasted sesame oil. The fried shallots are the signature; they pull the sweetness back and add a faint allium savory note that distinguishes Vietnamese trôi nước from Chinese tang yuan. Cool fully. Roll into 16 balls of about 12 g / 2 tsp each. Set aside.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Dough. Place the 200 g / 1½ cups glutinous rice flour in a bowl. Stream in the 150 ml / ⅔ cup warm water while stirring with chopsticks. Once the mixture comes together as shaggy clumps, knead by hand 30 seconds into a smooth dough. The texture should feel like soft modeling clay, firm enough to hold a ball, soft enough to give slightly. If sticky, sprinkle in 1 tsp more flour; if cracking, wet hands and knead again. Cover with a damp towel; do not let the dough dry.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Wrap. Divide the dough into 16 equal pieces of about 22 g / 1½ tbsp each. Working one at a time and keeping the rest covered, flatten a dough piece into a disc with your fingers (about 6 cm / 2½ inch across). Place a mung bean ball in the center. Bring the dough up around the filling, pinch the seam closed, then roll between palms into a smooth ball. The ball should be slightly heavier than a golf ball, perfectly sealed, no visible filling. Repeat for all 16.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Syrup. While shaping the last balls, combine the 720 ml / 3 cups water, the 120 g / ½ cup palm sugar, the 40 g sliced fresh ginger, and the 2 knotted pandan leaves in a wide saucepan. Bring to a simmer and hold 10 minutes, the ginger blooms slowly; rushed it tastes raw and harsh.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Cook the balls. Bring a separate large pot of water to a rolling boil. Slip the rice balls in 4 at a time so the water never stops boiling hard. The balls sink, then rise to the surface in about 4 minutes, let them bob another 2 minutes once floating, then transfer with a slotted spoon directly into the simmering ginger syrup. Repeat with remaining balls. Do not crowd the boil; underboiled water yields chewy-tough centers.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Hold the balls in the ginger syrup over the lowest heat for 5 minutes, they absorb the gingery sweetness as they finish setting. Fish out the pandan leaves; keep the ginger slices in for service (most Vietnamese cooks eat them).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Coconut topping. In a small saucepan combine the 240 ml / 1 cup coconut cream, the 15 g / 1 tbsp sugar, and the 1 g pinch salt. Warm over low heat just until the sugar dissolves. Whisk in the 5 g / 2 tsp tapioca slurry; stir gently 1 minute as it thickens to a pourable consistency. Do not boil.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Serve in shallow bowls: 4 balls per portion in a generous pool of the warm ginger syrup, a couple of ginger slices for the brave, then 2 to 3 tbsp coconut cream poured over the top. Finish with a pinch of the 5 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame seeds.'

notes: |
  The hot-water hydration of the glutinous rice flour is the technique that separates a good chewy ball from a brittle, cracking, leaky one. Cold water gives a stiff dough that fissures during shaping and lets filling escape during the boil; water at 50°C / 120°F partially gelatinizes a fraction of the starch on contact, which acts as a binder and gives the dough plasticity. Boiling water (over 70°C) over-gelatinizes the surface and the dough turns gummy. Aim for clearly warm, not hot to the touch.

  Sealing the filling is the make-or-break step. A poorly closed seam splits during the boil and dumps the mung bean into the water. The dough must wrap fully around the filling with no thin spots; pinch the seam closed firmly, then roll between palms to redistribute thickness. If you see filling through the dough surface, you have not used enough dough, start over with a bigger disc.

  Ginger steeping in cold water and bringing up to a simmer (rather than adding to already-boiling water) extracts the rounder gingerol fragrance without releasing the sharper shogaol compounds that develop under sustained high heat. The result is warmly aromatic rather than sharply hot.
