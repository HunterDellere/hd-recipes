source:
  name: 'Mexican Mesoamerican tradition (central Mexico, pre-Columbian)'
  note: 'Tamales are pre-Columbian Mesoamerican; the technique of grinding nixtamalized corn into masa, wrapping in husks, and steaming dates back at least 5000 years across Mexico and Guatemala. The chicken-in-salsa-verde filling is a colonial-era adaptation (chickens arrived with the Spanish), but the masa-and-husk vehicle is indigenous. Regional Mexican tamale variations are vast: Oaxacan tamales use banana leaves and red mole, Yucatecan cochinita pibil tamales use achiote and pork, Veracruz tamales include shrimp and chipotle. This recipe is the central-Mexican (Mexico City, Puebla, Hidalgo) chicken-and-green-salsa version, which is the most everyday and accessible expression.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Soak the dried corn husks 8+ hours or overnight in warm water. Husks fresh out of the bag are dry and brittle; they will crack when folded around the tamale. The soak is structural; do not skip or shortcut.'
  - 'The masa mixing window is critical. Once the masa is mixed with the lard and broth, it must rest 20 minutes, be tested for the float (a small spoonful dropped in cold water should float; if it sinks, whip the masa another 5 minutes and test again), and be used within 2 hours. Beyond 2 hours, the masa starts to lose its aeration and the tamales will be dense.'
  - 'The full process is a 4-hour cook session (90 minutes prep + 90 minutes steam + 30 minutes rest). Plan accordingly; this is a Saturday-afternoon project, ideally with a second pair of hands for the assembly line.'

steps:
  - text: 'The night before: soak the 30 dried corn husks in warm water. Cover with a plate to keep them submerged. Soak 8 to 12 hours at room temperature. The husks should be uniformly pliable, deep yellow-tan, and easy to fold without cracking.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Poach the chicken. Combine the 700 g chicken, 1500 ml water, 100 g halved onion, 15 g garlic, 12 g salt, and 2 bay leaves in a large saucepan. Bring to a simmer over medium-high; reduce to medium-low, cover, simmer 18 to 20 minutes until the chicken reaches 75°C / 165°F. Lift the chicken out; let cool. Strain and reserve 600 ml of the broth for the masa. Shred the chicken with two forks.'
    time_min: 27
  - text: 'Char and blend the salsa verde. Arrange the 500 g tomatillos and 30 g jalapeños on a sheet pan; broil 6 to 8 minutes per side until blistered and charred. Transfer to a blender with the 60 g raw onion, 10 g raw garlic, 20 g cilantro, and 4 g salt. Blend until smooth (45 seconds). Heat 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide saucepan; pour in the blended salsa (it will spit); cook 5 minutes to fry the salsa. Reserve.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Mix the chicken filling. Combine the shredded chicken with about 400 ml of the warm salsa verde in a bowl (reserve 200 ml of salsa for serving alongside). The chicken should be well-coated and slightly saucy but not soupy; the filling needs to hold its shape inside the tamale during the steam. Reserve.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Whip the masa dough. In a stand mixer with the paddle attachment (or a large bowl with an electric hand mixer), beat the 250 g lard on medium-high for 3 minutes until light, fluffy, and almost white. The aeration of the lard is the structural step that produces fluffy tamales; rushing this step produces dense ones.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Combine the dry ingredients. In a separate bowl, whisk together the 500 g masa harina, 8 g baking powder, and 10 g salt.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Build the dough. Add the masa-harina mixture to the whipped lard in alternating additions with the 600 ml warm chicken broth, starting and ending with the masa mixture (3 additions of masa, 2 of broth). Beat on medium-low between each addition for 30 seconds, then on medium-high for another 30 seconds. After all additions, beat on medium-high for 5 minutes total; the dough should be light, fluffy, slightly sticky, and pale tan. The texture is structurally what defines a great tamale; the masa should look like soft frosting, not like dense polenta.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Test the float. Drop a small spoonful (about 5 g) of the masa into a glass of cold water. If it floats, the masa is properly aerated and ready to use. If it sinks, beat for another 5 minutes and test again; the float test is the structural canonical doneness check for tamale masa, used in Mexican kitchens for centuries.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Assemble the tamales. Drain the soaked husks; pat dry on a kitchen towel. Hold a husk in your palm with the smooth side up and the wider end at the top, narrower end at the bottom. Spread about 50 g of masa on the upper two-thirds of the husk in a 8 to 10 cm wide rectangle, leaving the bottom third and the side edges bare. Spoon about 30 g of the chicken-salsa filling in a vertical line down the center of the masa. Fold both side edges of the husk over the filling (the masa-on-masa edges seal together), then fold the bottom narrow end up. The top wider end stays open; this is structural to the canonical central-Mexican tamale shape. Repeat for 24 tamales total.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Set up the steamer. Line the bottom of a tamale steamer (or a large stockpot with a steamer rack) with a few of the extra corn husks. Add 3 to 4 cm of water to the bottom (the water level should be just below the rack but not touching the tamales). Place a small heatproof bowl or a coin in the bottom (the coin rattling tells you when the water has boiled off and needs replenishing). Stand the assembled tamales upright in the steamer, open end up, packed tightly so they support each other and do not unfold during the steam.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Steam. Cover the steamer with a damp kitchen towel under the lid (the towel catches condensation and prevents water dripping back onto the tamales). Bring to a boil over high heat; reduce to medium to maintain a vigorous steam. Steam 90 minutes, checking the water level every 30 minutes and adding more boiling water if needed (the coin will rattle differently when the water is low). The tamales are done when the masa pulls away cleanly from the husk when one is unwrapped; if the masa sticks to the husk, steam another 15 minutes and test again.'
    time_min: 90
  - text: 'Rest and serve. Pull the steamer off heat; let the tamales rest covered in the steamer for 20 to 30 minutes. The rest is structural; tamales pulled hot immediately read as too soft and slightly mushy, but rested tamales firm up to the canonical fluffy-but-set texture. Serve hot with the reserved 200 ml of warm salsa verde on the side, plus Mexican crema, queso fresco, and lime wedges. Each diner unfolds the husk on their plate and eats the tamale with a fork (the husk is the wrapper, not the food).'
    time_min: 25

notes: |
  The float test is canonical. The cold-water float test for properly aerated masa is the structural Mexican doneness check, used for centuries before modern equipment. Masa that floats indicates that the lard has been whipped enough to incorporate sufficient air pockets that the dough is less dense than water. Masa that sinks is under-aerated and will produce dense, leaden tamales. Modern stand mixers make the whipping easier (8 to 10 minutes of total beating typically gets there); traditional Mexican kitchens used hand-mixing in a large bowl with a wooden paddle, taking 30 to 45 minutes of physical effort to reach the same point.

  Why lard is structural. Lard (manteca) provides the characteristic pillowy, fluffy, slightly meaty-savory texture that defines real tamales. The fat is mechanically suited to being whipped to a high-volume foam (its fatty acid composition holds air pockets well), and the flavor adds a subtle savory depth that contributes to the dish without being identifiable as pork. Vegetable shortening works as a substitute (the Crisco-tamale is common in Mexican-American households and entirely valid); coconut oil and butter are not good substitutes (coconut adds a competing flavor, butter does not whip as high). For canonical tamales, use lard.

  The fold-and-stand technique. Tamales are folded with the open end up and stood vertically in the steamer for a structural reason: the steam rises through the open top, cooks the masa from the inside, and the closed bottom keeps the filling contained. Tamales laid horizontally in the steamer often unfold and the filling leaks out; the vertical orientation is structural. Pack the tamales tightly so they support each other; loose packing causes tamales to fall over during the steam and unfold.

  The 90-minute steam. The masa needs at least 75 minutes to cook through; less than that and the dough is still raw at the center, more than 105 minutes and the masa starts to overcook and dry out. The 90-minute window is the structural target. The masa-pulls-cleanly-from-husk test is the doneness check; if the masa sticks, the tamales are not done; if the masa is dry and crumbly, they are overcooked.

  The rest after steaming. Tamales pulled hot immediately from the steamer are too soft; the masa needs 20 to 30 minutes of rest to firm up to the canonical fluffy-but-set texture. This is structural; rushing this step results in tamales that read as mushy. Mexican home cooks know to let the steamer rest covered after pulling from heat; the residual heat finishes the set without overcooking.

  Storage and reheating. Cooked tamales keep 4 days refrigerated in the husk, or 6 months frozen in the husk (in a zip-top bag). Reheat by re-steaming for 15 minutes (refrigerated) or 25 minutes (frozen); the tamales rehydrate to nearly fresh-made quality. Do not microwave; the masa goes rubbery. Tamales are a classic make-ahead dish; many Mexican households make 50 to 100 at a time at Christmas (the canonical tamalada, a tamale-making party) and freeze for the season.
