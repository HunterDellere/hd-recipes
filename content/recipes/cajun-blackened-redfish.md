source:
  name: 'Paul Prudhomme, K-Paul''s Louisiana Kitchen, New Orleans (1980)'
  note: 'Blackened redfish is the Cajun dish that put Cajun cooking on the global culinary map. Chef Paul Prudhomme of K-Paul''s Louisiana Kitchen in the French Quarter invented the technique in 1980 (or codified an older Louisiana fisherman tradition, depending on the source); the dish became so popular through the early 1980s that redfish populations off the Louisiana coast crashed and the species had to be federally protected. The technique is structural: a screaming-hot dry cast iron skillet (260 to 320 °C / 500 to 600 °F surface temperature), butter-dipped fillet, dry spice dredge, sear for 90 seconds per side. The blackened crust is the structural sensory event of the dish; it is not burned, it is rapidly Maillard-and-pyrolysis-charred under controlled conditions.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The cast iron skillet must be heated empty over high heat for 8 to 10 minutes until it is visibly white-hot at the bottom and smoking aggressively. A cooler skillet (the most common home error) produces fish that steams in melted butter rather than blackening. Cook outdoors on a grill side-burner or under a powerful range hood; the kitchen will fill with smoke.'
  - 'Redfish is the canonical species (a Gulf-coast drum); availability outside Louisiana is limited. Best substitutes: thick fillets of red snapper, grouper, sea bass, or Pacific snapper. Avoid thin delicate fish (tilapia, sole, flounder); they overcook before the crust forms.'
  - 'The spice blend is structural to the technique; do not skip ingredients. The full Prudhomme blend (paprika, cayenne, garlic powder, onion powder, dried oregano, dried thyme, black pepper, white pepper) layers heat (cayenne, peppers) on Maillard depth (paprika, garlic, onion) on aromatic backdrop (oregano, thyme). Commercial "blackening seasoning" is the home shortcut; use 30 g of the McCormick or Cajun-brand blend if not making the spice mix yourself.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the spice blend. Combine all of Phase 1 (15 g paprika, 6 g cayenne, 6 g garlic powder, 6 g onion powder, 3 g oregano, 3 g thyme, 3 g black pepper, 2 g white pepper, 6 g salt) in a small bowl. Whisk to fully blend. Yields about 50 g; you will use about 30 g, the rest keeps 3 months in a sealed jar.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Heat the cast iron skillet (30 cm / 12 inch is ideal) empty over the highest heat your stove provides for 8 to 10 minutes. The skillet is ready when it is visibly smoking aggressively and a drop of water flashes to steam in under a second. Open windows; turn on the range hood at full power; if you have an outdoor side burner or a high-output grill, this is the moment to use it. The kitchen will fill with smoke.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'While the skillet heats, melt the 120 g butter in a wide shallow bowl. Spread 30 g of the spice blend on a wide plate. Have the 4 fish fillets, the bowl of butter, and the plate of spice in a single line near the stove. Once the spice goes on the fish, the fish needs to hit the skillet in under 30 seconds; pre-position everything.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Dip the first fillet fully in the melted butter, coating both sides. Lift; let excess butter drip off for 1 second. Dredge in the spice blend, pressing down so the spice fully coats every surface; flip and dredge the other side. The fillet should be heavily black-red with spice on every visible surface. Immediately carry to the hot skillet and lay the fillet down flat (away from you to avoid splatter).'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Sear 90 seconds. Do not move the fish during the sear; the bottom is forming the structural blackened crust and any disturbance breaks the crust off and leaves a pale spot. The kitchen will be smoking dramatically; this is correct. After 90 seconds, lift the corner of the fillet with a thin spatula; the bottom should be deep black with audibly crispy edges. If the bottom is still red and not blackened, the skillet was not hot enough; continue 30 to 60 seconds more.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Flip the fillet using a wide thin spatula (the spice crust is fragile; flip carefully). Sear the second side 90 seconds to 2 minutes, depending on the thickness. The fish is done when a knife inserted into the thickest part shows opaque white flake all the way through; the center should be just barely set with no translucent raw spot. Transfer to a warm plate.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Repeat for the remaining 3 fillets. The skillet may need 60 seconds between fish to recover heat; the spice residue from the previous fillet will smoke aggressively but adds to the next crust rather than burning. If the skillet residue starts smoking with a sharp acrid smell (not the cleaner spice smoke), scrape it out with a wooden spatula and continue.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Plate the fish, sprinkle with 10 g chopped parsley, and serve with 2 lemon wedges per fillet. Squeeze the lemon over the fish at the table; the bright acid cuts the spice and butter and is the structural finishing element. Serve with dirty rice, étouffée, or simple buttered green beans.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why screaming hot. The structural blackening reaction happens between 230 and 320 °C / 450 to 600 °F surface temperature: hot enough to instantly drive off the surface moisture of the butter-coated fish, char the spices to a black crust, and Maillard-sear the protein underneath, all in 90 seconds. A skillet at 200 °C steams the fish in the butter instead of charring it; a skillet at 350 °C burns the spices to acrid bitter ash before the protein cooks. Cast iron is structural because it can hold extreme heat without warping or losing its structure; stainless steel can also work but is less forgiving.

  Why butter as the binder. The melted butter on the fish does two things: it coats the spice and makes it adhere uniformly to every surface, and it provides the water and milk-solid moisture that flash-vaporizes into the spice crust and creates the structural blackening reaction (the milk solids in the butter Maillard-brown rapidly under the hot spice). Olive oil works but produces a less structural crust; oil-only fish reads more grilled-spiced than blackened.

  Why ventilation matters. Blackening produces aggressive aromatic smoke; this is the spice's volatile compounds reaching their pyrolysis point and is structurally what flavors the fish. The kitchen will fill with smoke and the smoke detectors may go off. A working range hood at full power is necessary; an outdoor side burner or grill is ideal. Prudhomme cooked over wood-fired grills at K-Paul''s for exactly this reason.

  Why "not burned." Blackening is a controlled high-heat charring of the spice crust; the protein underneath stays cooked-through but moist. Burning is when the same crust continues to char past the structural point and becomes acrid-bitter ash. The distinction is the smoke smell: blackening smoke smells aggressively spiced; burning smoke smells acrid and chemical. Pull the fish at the moment of black-crust, not 30 seconds later.

  Pairings. Canonical with dirty rice (rice cooked with chicken livers, holy trinity, and Cajun spice), or with étouffée and white rice on the side. Some Louisiana restaurants serve over a lemon-butter cream sauce; this is the modern home approach. Lemon wedges are non-negotiable. A cold beer (Abita Amber from Louisiana, or a Mexican lager) on the side.

  Storage. Blackened fish does not store well; the crust softens within 30 minutes and the smoky aroma fades. Best eaten immediately. Leftover spice blend keeps 3 months in a sealed jar; double or triple the spice recipe for repeat cooks.
