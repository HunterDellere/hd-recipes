  - 'This dish requires 2 to 4 day old sourdough. If you only have fresh bread, tear it into chunks and dry on a sheet pan in a 120 C / 250 F oven for 20 minutes before starting, fresh moist bread turns to paste in the tomato-water soak.'
  - 'Salt-cure the heirloom tomato chunks at 1 percent salt by weight 30 minutes before the bread soak. The cure liquid is the soaking medium for the bread, you cannot shortcut this and substitute plain water without losing the glutamate spine.'
  - 'The final assembled salad needs a 20-minute rest at room temperature before serving so the bread hydrates evenly and the flavors integrate. Plan plating time accordingly.'
  - 'This is a peak-summer recipe. Out-of-season supermarket tomatoes will not yield enough tomato water or carry enough flavor, do not attempt in winter with grocery-store fruit.'

  note: 'The dish has a structural rule older than the recipe: never throw away stale bread, and never throw away ripe-tomato water. The standard preparation soaks dry bread in cold water plus vinegar to rehydrate, drains, then tosses with raw tomato, onion, and basil. The version here pulls the bread soak away from plain water and toward the tomato-cure liquid produced when the heirloom flesh is salted; the bread is then carrying glutamate from the start rather than picking up only what leaches off raw tomato during the rest. Adding the charred-cherry-tomato component is a modern split (the canonical version is fully raw); the blistered tomato contributes Maillard and pyrazine notes that the raw fruit cannot, and the contrast between cured-raw and dry-charred flesh is the signature of the rebuilt dish.'

steps:
  - text: 'Salt-cure the heirloom tomatoes. Place the 500 g / 2 large cored and chunked heirloom tomatoes in a wide non-reactive bowl (glass or ceramic, never aluminum which reacts with the tomato acid). Scatter the 5 g / 1 tsp fine sea salt evenly across the surface, distribute by gentle hand-toss, and let stand uncovered at room temperature for 30 minutes. The salt creates an osmotic gradient that pulls water out of the tomato cells, and along with the water comes dissolved free glutamate (the umami driver), citric and malic acid, sugars, and the volatile aromatics; what pools at the bottom of the bowl is concentrated tomato water, the most flavor-dense raw seasoning the kitchen produces. The flesh that remains is firmer, sweeter, and more umami-dense than raw tomato. This is the structural move of the recipe.'
    technique: 'tomato-salt-cure'
    time_min: 32
  - text: 'While the tomato cures, crisp the bread. Heat the oven to 180 °C / 350 °F. Toss the 300 g / torn sourdough chunks with the 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil on a sheet pan, spread to a single layer, and bake 10 to 12 minutes until the surfaces are dry and lightly golden but the interiors are still pliant when pressed; the bread should snap-then-yield at the bite, not shatter. Pull and let cool. Fully crisp croutons are wrong here; the bread needs interior elasticity to absorb the tomato water without crumbling into mush. If using already-stale bread (4 days old, dry through), reduce the oven time to 5 to 7 minutes; the goal is the same texture endpoint, not a fixed time.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Blister the cherry tomatoes. Heat a heavy carbon-steel or cast-iron skillet over medium-high for 3 to 4 minutes, until a flick of water snaps and evaporates within 2 seconds. Add the 8 g / 2 tsp olive oil; it should shimmer and barely smoke at the edges. Add the 250 g / 2 cups patted-dry cherry tomatoes in a single uncrowded layer (work in two batches if your skillet is small; a crowded pan steams the tomatoes instead of blistering them). Cook 3 to 4 minutes without moving, until the underside skin is dark-spotted, blistered, and split in places; flip with tongs and cook 2 to 3 minutes more on the second face. The skins should look black-spotted and the interior gel should bubble visibly through the splits. Pull to a plate, season with the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt, and rest. The Maillard browning on the skins contributes pyrazines and furans that the raw cured tomato cannot supply; this is the second umami axis of the salad.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Build the vinaigrette. While the bread cools and the tomato cures, in a small bowl combine the 30 g / 2 tbsp red wine vinegar, the 4 g / ¾ tsp dijon mustard, the 4 g / 1 small mashed garlic clove, the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp cracked black pepper. Whisk with a fork until the salt fully dissolves; the salt has to dissolve in the vinegar phase before the oil goes in or it pockets in the dressing as gritty hot spots. Stream in the 90 g / 6 tbsp olive oil in a thin steady pour while whisking vigorously; the dijon mucilage and the mashed garlic provide the surface-active emulsifiers, and the slow oil addition gives the colloid time to form. The dressing should look thick, opaque, and slightly creamy when finished; if it stays clearly two-phase after the oil is in, whisk harder for another 30 seconds. Set aside.'
    technique: 'vinaigrette-emulsion'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Drain the cured tomato. Tip the salt-cured heirloom tomatoes and their accumulated water into a fine-mesh sieve set over a bowl. Let drain 5 minutes; gently shake to release any pockets but do not press the flesh (pressing extracts pulp as well as water and the dressing turns muddy). You should have 80 to 120 g of clear-to-pale-pink tomato water in the bowl; reserve. The drained tomato flesh is now firmer, more concentrated, and pre-seasoned; transfer to a wide mixing bowl.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Soak the bread in tomato water plus vinaigrette. Add the cooled crisped sourdough chunks and the prepared red onion half-moons to a separate large mixing bowl. Pour the reserved 80 to 120 g tomato water over the bread, then add half the vinaigrette (about 70 g / 5 tbsp). Toss with hands or a wide spoon until every chunk is glistening and the bread has absorbed most of the liquid (about 60 seconds of gentle turning). Let stand 5 minutes; the bread will continue to drink and will plump from dry crouton to chewy-but-intact texture. The bread is now carrying tomato umami and vinaigrette acid as its primary flavor; this is what separates panzanella from croutons-in-salad.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Combine and dress. Add the drained heirloom tomato flesh, the blistered cherry tomatoes (and any cherry-tomato juice that pooled on the resting plate), the 200 g / 1 cup cucumber half-moons, and the 15 g / 1½ tbsp drained capers to the bread bowl. Pour the remaining vinaigrette (about 70 g / 5 tbsp) over the top. Toss gently with a wide rubber spatula or salad spoons, 6 to 8 turns, just enough to integrate the dressing through the components without breaking down the bread; the salad should look glossy, with the vinaigrette pooled in the bottom of the bowl rather than fully absorbed. Taste a piece of bread; it should read tomato-saturated, lightly sharp from the vinegar, salt-forward but not over. Adjust salt only by 1 g pinch increments; the cured tomato and the vinaigrette and the capers are all carrying salt and additional pinches stack quickly.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Rest 10 to 15 minutes before plating. The rest is structural: the bread continues to absorb the dressing and the tomato water, the onion mellows in the acid, the cured tomato releases a final round of flavor into the bowl, and the basil aromatic released in the next step lands on a salad that is already in equilibrium. A panzanella served immediately reads as bread and tomato in the same bowl; a panzanella rested 10 minutes reads as a single composed dish.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Finish and plate. Tear the 15 g / 1 packed cup basil leaves into the salad and fold gently 2 to 3 turns; do not over-mix at this stage or the basil bruises and oxidizes. Tip onto a wide shallow platter (not a deep bowl, the bread bottom layer goes soggy past 30 minutes in a deep vessel), drizzle with the 15 g / 1 tbsp finishing olive oil, and crack additional black pepper across the top. Serve within 30 minutes of plating; panzanella is at its peak in the 15 to 45 minute window after assembly and degrades past 90 minutes as the bread oversoaks and the tomato weeps. Leftovers refrigerate poorly; the bread turns to wet sponge overnight. If anticipating leftovers, hold back a portion of unsoaked bread and dressed tomato separately, combine at second-day mealtime.'
    time_min: 4

notes: |
  The thesis is that bread is a flavor sponge engineered to deliver tomato to the eater, and that the dish lives or dies on the quality and treatment of the tomato. The classical Tuscan version uses water-and-vinegar to rehydrate the bread, which is mechanically correct but flavor-flat; the bread carries acid and bulk to the salad but does not carry tomato until it has sat in the bowl for 20 minutes and absorbed by passive contact. The salt-cure-first approach reorders the build so that the bread is soaked in tomato water from the start, which means every bite of bread reads tomato-saturated rather than just tomato-adjacent. The blistered cherry tomatoes are the second axis: raw tomato has glutamate and acid and aromatics; charred tomato adds Maillard-derived pyrazines and furans that the raw fruit cannot supply. The dish has both, in deliberate proportion (twice as much raw cured as charred, by weight), so the salad reads as fresh-tomato-with-char-accent rather than the inverse.

  The umami source is the tomato itself, in two forms (cured raw heirloom, blistered cherry), reinforced by the capers (mustard-oil glucosinolates, brining-derived glutamate) and the small but structural raw garlic in the dressing. The finishing acid is the red wine vinegar in the dressing, with secondary acid from the cured tomato water (citric and malic) and a small contribution from the capers. There is no anchovy or parmesan in the canonical Tuscan version; the salad is engineered to read fully savory through the vegetable chemistry alone, which makes it strict-vegetarian by default and a counterpoint to the cheese-and-cured-meat-driven northern Italian salads.

  Why salt at 1 percent and rest 30 minutes. The 1 percent salt ratio (5 g per 500 g tomato) is the inflection point where extraction is meaningful (15 to 25 percent water yield) without over-seasoning the cured flesh. Lower than 0.5 percent, the osmotic gradient is too gentle and the cure does little; higher than 1.5 percent, the flesh reads salty before any other seasoning hits and the dish loses the ability to layer salt elsewhere. The 30-minute rest is the diminishing-returns sweet spot: useful extraction begins at 5 minutes (a glaze of liquid appears), reaches peak yield around 30 minutes (the flesh has given up roughly 20 percent of its mass as water), and degrades past 45 (the flesh turns leathery, the flavor flattens). See techniques/tomato-salt-cure for the full chemistry.

  Why blister the cherry tomatoes dry rather than oven-roast. Dry-skillet blistering at 230 to 260 °C concentrates the tomato sugars and acids in place (the skin acts as a vessel), produces aggressive Maillard browning on the contact patches, and keeps the interior gel raw and bursty: bite-into-it-and-it-pops texture. Oven-roasting at 200 °C for 20 minutes produces softer, sweeter, more concentrated tomatoes but loses the textural contrast that defines this version of the salad against a slow-roast tomato preparation. The right oven version is broiling at maximum heat for 4 to 5 minutes, which approximates the dry-skillet result; the dry-skillet method is faster and cleaner.

  Why the cold-water onion soak. Raw red onion contains aliinase enzyme and S-alkenyl cysteine sulfoxides that produce volatile thiosulfinates when the cell walls are cut; these are the harsh sulfurous front-note that turns a lovely salad into a salad-of-onion. Soaking sliced onion in cold water for 10 minutes leaches the water-soluble sulfoxides out without fully cooking the onion; the result is an onion that retains crunch, color, and a milder sweet-pungent character. The soak is structural for any raw-onion salad application, including kachumber, fattoush, ceviche, and panzanella; skip it and the dish reads as raw-onion-with-supporting-vegetables rather than as a balanced composition.

  Bread quality is the second hidden lever. A fresh bakery-quality sourdough that has been left out 3 days, crusts intact, is the right bread; the slow stale produces the open dry crumb that absorbs liquid uniformly without immediate disintegration. Day-old supermarket sandwich bread will not work; the tight crumb soaks unevenly and the dressing pools at the bottom of the bowl rather than entering the bread. Brioche and challah are wrong; the egg richness clashes with the tomato and the tighter crumb does not absorb. If the only bread on hand is fresh sourdough, the 12-minute oven crisp at 180 °C / 350 °F simulates the staling closely enough; a fresh sandwich loaf cannot be saved.

  How panzanella is served. Panzanella is a midday or early-evening summer dish across Tuscany, served as antipasto, as a primo (first course) instead of pasta, or as a contorno (vegetable side) alongside grilled meat and fish. Portion is generous (a panzanella is rarely a polite quarter-cup ramekin; it is a wide platter at the center of the table). The dish does not appear in winter and is not built around hothouse tomatoes; the August-September vine-ripe window is the entire window. The leftover-fridge problem is real: the salad does not improve overnight, and the bread turns to wet sponge by morning. The right move is to dress only the portion being eaten and hold back the bread and tomato separately for second-day service.
