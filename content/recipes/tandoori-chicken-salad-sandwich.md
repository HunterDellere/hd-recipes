before_you_start:
  - 'The chicken needs to poach, then cool, then shred before it can be combined with the mayo, a 30 to 35 minute lead before the salad comes together. The tandoori mayo also wants a 10 minute spice-bloom rest, and the assembled salad benefits from a 15 minute fridge integration before serving. Plan a 75 minute total window or make the salad up to 3 days ahead.'
  - 'Soak the diced red onion in cold water for at least 10 minutes before draining. Raw red onion in cold water loses the sulfurous edge while keeping the textural crunch; un-soaked red onion overwhelms the tandoori spice with allium pungency.'
  - 'Toast the croissants only at the moment of assembly. Toasted croissants soften within 15 minutes of contact with the salad and the crisp-creamy contrast is the structural eating experience; pre-toasting kills the dish.'
  - 'The chicken salad alone holds 3 days refrigerated and the flavors deepen overnight. If serving for guests, make the salad the day before and assemble sandwiches just before service.'

steps:
  - text: 'Poach the chicken. Combine the 2000 ml water, 20 g salt, 2 bay leaves, 3 g peppercorns, 15 g sliced ginger, and 12 g smashed garlic in a 4-quart saucepan. Bring to a low simmer (about 80 °C, surface tremor not bubbling). Add the 600 g chicken thigh in a single layer; submerge by 2 cm. Adjust heat to maintain the bare simmer; poach 18 to 22 minutes until the chicken reaches an internal temperature of 75 °C and the texture is just firm to the touch. Boiling rather than simmering toughens the proteins and produces dry, stringy chicken; the temperature window matters.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Cool the chicken in the poaching liquid. Pull the saucepan off the heat. Let the chicken rest in the warm poaching liquid for 10 minutes; the carryover cook plus the slow cooling produces a more uniformly tender, more deeply seasoned final result than yanking the chicken out hot. Remove the chicken to a cutting board; cool 5 minutes more before shredding.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Whisk the tandoori mayo. In a medium bowl, whisk the 200 g mayonnaise, 60 g yogurt, 12 g tandoori masala, 15 ml lemon juice, 1 g garlic powder, 3 g salt, 1 g black pepper, and 1 g optional cayenne until smooth and uniformly orange-pink in color. Let stand 10 minutes for the spices to bloom into the mayo (the dry spices need time to hydrate and integrate; freshly-mixed tandoori mayo reads dustier and less integrated than rested). Taste; the mayo should taste assertively spiced, slightly tangy, with the chili and cumin reading clearly.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Toast the walnuts. Spread the 50 g walnuts on a small dry skillet over medium heat. Toast 4 minutes, shaking every 60 seconds, until the walnuts smell deeply nutty and have darkened a shade. Pull from heat immediately; walnuts go from toasted to bitter in a 90-second window. Transfer to a cutting board; cool 2 minutes; chop roughly into 5 mm pieces.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Shred the chicken. Tear the cooled poached chicken into rough 1.5 cm pieces with two forks or with your hands. The torn-by-hand texture (irregular shapes, varying piece sizes) produces a better salad mouthfeel than the uniform fine dice some recipes specify; the salad reads more substantial and less industrially processed.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Assemble the salad. In a large mixing bowl, combine the shredded chicken, 100 g diced celery, 80 g grated carrot, 50 g toasted chopped walnuts, 8 g chopped cilantro, and 30 g cold-water-soaked diced red onion (drained). Pour the rested tandoori mayo over the top. Fold gently with a rubber spatula until everything is uniformly coated; do not over-mix or the chicken will break down further and the salad will read as a paste rather than as bound shreds. Taste; adjust salt, lemon juice, and tandoori masala if needed. Refrigerate 15 minutes for the flavors to integrate; longer resting produces an even more cohesive salad and is recommended if time allows.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Toast and butter the croissants. Slice each croissant horizontally; place cut-side up on a sheet pan. Toast under the broiler 90 seconds (watch closely; croissants burn fast) until the cut surfaces are golden and crisp. Spread the warm cut surface of each croissant half with about 7 g (1½ tsp) of softened butter; the heat melts the butter into the toasted surface and creates a moisture-and-flavor barrier between the bread and the salad.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Build and serve. Place a large lettuce leaf on each croissant bottom. Mound about 150 g of the chicken salad onto each leaf-lined croissant bottom, distributing evenly. Cap with the croissant top. Slice diagonally if serving plated, or wrap in parchment for handheld eating. Serve immediately; toasted croissants soften within 15 minutes of assembly, and the textural contrast between crisp croissant and creamy salad is the structural eating experience.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why poach, not roast or sauté. The chicken-salad form depends on shredded chicken with no Maillard browning, the dish wants pure poached-chicken flavor as the canvas for the spiced mayo. Roasted or sautéed chicken brings caramelized notes that fight with the tandoori spice profile and produce a salad that reads as a roasted-chicken sandwich rather than a chicken salad. Poaching is the structural technique; the mild flavor of poached chicken is the point.

  Why bloom the spices in the mayo. Tandoori masala is a dry spice blend that needs hydration time to develop its full flavor. Mixing the masala into the mayo and resting 10 minutes lets the dry chili powder, cumin, and other spices hydrate against the mayo''s water phase; the resulting mayo tastes more integrated, more deeply spiced, and less dustily-superficial than freshly-mixed mayo used immediately. The same rule applies to any dry spice in any cool emulsion (yogurt-based dressings, sour-cream dips, Greek-yogurt sauces). Ten minutes is the minimum; thirty produces an even better integration.

  Why yogurt in the mayo. The tang from a small dose of yogurt (60 g into 200 g mayo) is structural to the dish identity. Pure mayo reads as flat and one-dimensional under the high spice load; yogurt''s lactic acid lifts the spice and brightens the overall flavor profile. The yogurt also references the tandoori-marinade tradition, where yogurt is the canonical spice-carrying medium. The 30 percent ratio (yogurt:mayo) is the kitchen-tested floor that gives the tang without going so far that the salad reads as yogurt-bound rather than mayo-bound.

  The cold-water onion soak. Raw red onion has a sharp, sulfurous bite that can dominate a chicken salad. Soaking the diced onion in cold water for 5 minutes leaches out a meaningful fraction of the volatile sulfur compounds and produces a milder, sweeter onion that integrates with the salad rather than punctuating it. Drain thoroughly before adding to the salad. The same technique applies to any raw onion in any cold preparation, sandwiches, salads, tartines, ceviche.

  Why day-old croissants. Fresh-from-the-bakery croissants are too tender and too laminated-flaky to hold up under the chicken salad, the salad''s moisture migrates into the layers and the croissant goes soggy in 5 minutes. Day-old croissants have firmed up, the layers have set, and the toasted-and-buttered preparation creates a structural crisp surface that holds the salad without falling apart. The freshness sweet spot is 24 to 48 hours after baking; older croissants lose flavor and start tasting stale.

  The textural rule. Three textural elements define the dish: tender shredded chicken, crisp celery and carrot, and crunchy toasted walnut. Skip any one of the three and the salad reads as monotone. The celery and walnut both function structurally, celery provides cool crisp moisture, walnut provides hot crunch. Substituting both with cucumber or both with seeds compromises the textural architecture; substituting one with another acceptable crunch (sliced almonds for walnut, jicama for celery) is fine.

  Hunter''s household standard ratio. The mayo:chicken ratio of 200 g mayo per 600 g chicken (roughly 1:3 by weight) is the lower end of what reads as "bound chicken salad" and the higher end of what reads as "lightly dressed shredded chicken." More mayo and the salad reads as soggy and heavy; less and the chicken reads as dry and underseasoned. Adjust by 10 g either direction based on whether you''re serving on bread (lower mayo, the bread holds moisture) or on lettuce wraps (higher mayo, no bread to absorb).

  Make-ahead and storage. The chicken salad holds 3 days refrigerated in a sealed container and actually improves overnight as the spices fully integrate. Do not assemble the sandwiches more than 30 minutes before serving; the toasted croissants soften under the salad''s moisture and the sandwich loses its structural integrity. Pack the components separately for a brown-bag lunch and assemble at the table.
