before_you_start:
  - 'Fold the batter, then let it rest 5 minutes while the pan heats. The rest is where the starch hydrates and thickens, so the batter traps gas instead of leaking it, and the leftover buttermilk acid keeps the crumb from tasting soapy. Do not skip it and do not mix ahead and hold much longer; the soda-and-buttermilk reaction is largely spent within 10 minutes.'

steps:
  - text: 'Whisk together in a large bowl: 225 g / 1¾ cups + 2 tbsp all-purpose flour, 25 g / 2 tbsp granulated sugar, 6 g / 1½ tsp baking powder, 4 g / ¾ tsp baking soda, and 5 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt. Buttermilk is genuinely acidic (pH about 4.4), meaningfully more so than vinegar-soured milk, so this batter carries more baking soda than a classic recipe: the soda both neutralizes that acid for a big CO2 release and, once neutralized, raises the batter pH to push deeper Maillard browning in the pan.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'In a second bowl, whisk the 100 g / 2 large eggs until broken up, then whisk in the 360 ml / 1½ cups well-shaken buttermilk, 42 g / 3 tbsp melted cooled butter, and 6 ml / 1½ tsp vanilla bean paste. The butter must be barely warm; above 70 °C it cooks the egg proteins into visible flecks and can reharden into shards on contact with cold buttermilk.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour the wet into the dry. Fold with a spatula, 10 to 12 strokes maximum, stopping while the batter is still lumpy with visible flour streaks. Overmixing develops gluten and gives you tough, flat, rubbery pancakes; the lumps hydrate and vanish during the rest. Let the batter rest 5 minutes while the pan heats. The starch swells and thickens, the batter turns from pourable to slowly falling off the spatula, and that viscosity traps the CO2 the buttermilk-and-soda reaction is already generating.'
    technique: 'acid-milk-sour'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Set a wide skillet or griddle over medium heat. Add a 2 g / small pat of butter and let it melt fully; the foam is water cooking off. When the foam subsides and the surface just shimmers, the pan is at roughly 175 °C / 350 °F: hot enough for Maillard browning, below the smoke point of butter. Lower the heat a notch if the butter browns before you pour.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour 70 ml / ⅓ cup batter per pancake. Cook undisturbed until bubbles open across the surface and the edges look set and dry, 2 to 2½ minutes. Those bubbles are CO2 escaping as the interior sets; they mark structure, not just doneness. Flip once with a wide spatula and cook the second side 1 to 1½ minutes, until the center springs back when pressed lightly. Never press down; you are crushing the gas cells that give the height.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Wipe the pan with a paper towel between batches and add a fresh 2 g pat of butter each time. Burnt milk solids from the last round lower the browning point and will scorch the next pancake before its interior sets. Hold finished pancakes on a wire rack set over a sheet pan in a 95 °C / 200 °F oven; stacking them straight off the pan traps steam and turns the bottoms soggy.'
    time_min: 8

modifications:
  - for: 'occasion'
    to: 'chocolate chip pancakes'
    how: 'Do not fold the chocolate into the batter, where it sinks and scorches on the pan. Pour each pancake, then scatter 8 to 10 mini chocolate chips (about 15 g per pancake, 120 g / ⅔ cup total) onto the wet surface before the bubbles set. They anchor into the top face and stay put through the flip, and the brief second-side contact melts them without burning. Drop the pan heat by one notch, since the sugar in the chips browns fast.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'texture'
    to: 'extra-fluffy whipped-white pancakes'
    how: 'Separate the 2 eggs. Whisk the yolks into the wet as written. Beat the whites to soft peaks and fold into the finished batter in two additions after combining wet and dry. The whipped white adds a foam-based lift on top of the chemical leavening for a noticeably taller, more tender stack.'
    kind: 'texture'
  - for: 'occasion'
    to: 'blueberry pancakes'
    how: 'Pour each pancake, then press 8 to 10 fresh or unthawed frozen blueberries (about 120 g / ¾ cup total) into the wet surface, same as the chocolate-chip method. Folding them through the batter bleeds color and creates soggy patches; dropping them on top keeps the crumb clean. Lower the heat a notch, since the berry sugar browns fast.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Why real buttermilk over soured milk. The classic-pancakes recipe on this site sours whole milk with vinegar as a stand-in, and it works. Cultured buttermilk earns its own recipe because it brings two things the substitute cannot fully replicate: a lower, more consistent pH (about 4.4 versus roughly 5.5 for a quick soured milk), which fires the baking soda harder and more reliably; and diacetyl, the buttery aromatic compound the culture produces, which reads as a rounder, tangier flavor than plain acid. It is also thicker, so the batter bodies up faster and holds gas better.

  Why both leaveners. Baking soda is the acid-dependent one and does the heavy lifting here because buttermilk gives it so much acid to react with; that reaction fires immediately when wet meets dry, which is why you rest and then cook promptly rather than holding the batter. Baking powder is the thermally triggered one and provides a second lift in the pan as it heats. Using both means the rise happens in two waves, and the soda pulls double duty by raising batter pH for deeper browning.

  Why the rest, and why not longer. Five minutes lets the starch granules swell and bind water, turning a thin pourable batter into a slightly gelatinous one that traps gas instead of venting it. But the buttermilk-and-soda reaction is mostly spent within about 10 minutes, so a batter held far past the rest goes flat. Mix, rest 5 minutes, cook. If you must work ahead, keep the dry and wet mixtures separate and combine just before cooking.
