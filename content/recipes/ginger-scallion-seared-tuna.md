title: 'Ginger-scallion seared tuna'

desc: 'Sashimi-grade tuna seared bare and fast, sliced across the grain, and finished Cantonese-style with a fresh ginger-scallion relish bloomed by smoking-hot oil poured over it at the table. Here it lands on seared-rare tuna, with a light soy drizzle underneath.'

source:
  name: 'The Cantonese steamed-fish finish (清蒸魚), where raw ginger and scallion on top of cooked fish are bloomed by smoking-hot oil poured over them, transposed onto a seared-rare tuna block in place of a whole steamed fish.'
  note: 'The hot-oil pour is the whole technique. before_you_start:
  - 'The tuna is served rare, so it must be sashimi-grade or commercially flash-frozen for parasite kill (a home freezer cannot do this). Ask the counter "was this commercially flash-frozen." If you cannot source it, sear the block through to a cooked tuna-steak doneness, see the modification.'
  - 'Two pans, two heats, running close together. The tuna sears in one hot pan; the finishing oil heats in a small separate pan or pot to smoking right as the sliced fish is plated. Have the ginger and scallion minced and piled on the fish before the oil goes on to heat, the oil must hit the aromatics the moment it smokes, or it cools and the bloom falls flat.'
  - 'Sear from cold. A fridge-cold block holds a wider raw center through the sear; do not temper the fish.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the relish base. Pile the 20 g / 2 tbsp minced ginger and the 30 g / scallion whites and pale greens in a small heatproof bowl, toss with the 1 g / ¼ tsp kosher salt, and let sit 10 minutes. The salt draws out a little moisture and softens the raw bite so the hot-oil bloom lands evenly. Slice the dark-green scallion tops thin and set aside for garnish.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Mix the soy drizzle. Dissolve the 3 g / ½ tsp rock sugar in the 20 g / 4 tsp light soy sauce with a teaspoon of hot water (rock sugar needs the heat to dissolve; granulated sugar dissolves cold). Stir in the optional 2 g / ¼ tsp toasted sesame oil. Set aside.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Sear the tuna fast. Pat both 350 g / 12 oz blocks bone-dry and season all faces with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt. Heat a heavy skillet over high for 3 to 4 minutes until smoking, add the 15 g / 1 tbsp sear-oil, and swirl. Lay the first block in and sear 30 to 45 seconds per face, rolling around all four long sides plus a 15-second touch on each end, total about 3 minutes. The cooked-opaque band should creep in only 2 to 3 mm, leaving a wide cool ruby center. Sear the second block. Pull both to a board.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Slice and plate, with the soy underneath. Rest the blocks 60 seconds to halt carryover, then slice each across the grain into 1 cm slabs. Spoon the soy drizzle across a serving plate as a thin pool, lay the tuna slices over it cut-face up (soy under the fish, not over it, so the slices do not go soggy and the ruby center shows). Pile the salted ginger-scallion relish in a neat mound on top of the slices.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Bloom the relish with smoking oil. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp bloom-oil in a small pan or pot over high heat until it is genuinely smoking and shimmering hard, 1 to 2 minutes, it must be hotter than the sear oil, hot enough to sizzle violently on contact. Pour it directly and evenly over the ginger-scallion mound; it will hiss and foam and the kitchen will fill with the smell of bloomed ginger and allium. This is the moment the dish comes together: the raw aromatics cook just enough to lose their sharp edge and release their fragrant oils onto the fish.'
    technique: 'hot-oil-finish'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Garnish and serve immediately. Scatter the reserved sliced scallion greens and the optional 5 g cilantro over the top. Serve right away, while the bloomed oil is still fragrant and the tuna center is cool; spoon some of the now-aromatic oil and soy from the bottom of the plate over each portion as you serve.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why the hot-oil bloom, and why it has to smoke. Raw ginger and scallion carry sharp, volatile, slightly harsh compounds (gingerol, the allium thiosulfinates) that read aggressive on delicate fish. Pouring smoking-hot oil over them does in two seconds what gentle cooking does in minutes: it flash-heats the surface of the minced aromatics, driving off the harshest top-notes and releasing the rounder, fragrant oils into the pooling oil that then dresses the fish. The oil has to be genuinely smoking (well above 200 °C) for this to work, a merely-warm oil soaks into the aromatics without blooming them and the relish stays raw and sharp. This is the exact technique Cantonese kitchens use to finish a steamed whole fish, and it is the lightest, cleanest way to dress any delicate protein.

  Soy under, oil over. The plating order is deliberate. The soy goes down first as a thin pool, the fish sits on it, and the bloomed oil goes over the top. Soy poured over the slices would soak in and the cut faces would go soggy and dark, hiding the ruby center; pooled underneath, it seasons each bite from below as you lift it without drowning the fish. The bloomed aromatic oil over the top is where the fragrance lives, so it belongs on the surface where the nose meets it first.

  Restraint is the register. This is the lightest of the three seared-tuna preparations on this site: no crust, no glaze, just aromatics, hot oil, and a touch of sweetened soy. The whole point is to taste the fish, with the ginger-scallion as a clean fragrant frame. Resist the urge to add more, a heavier sauce would bury the delicate seared tuna that the technique exists to showcase.

  Source still gates the dish. As with all seared-rare tuna, the center is functionally raw; the fish must be sashimi-grade or commercially flash-frozen for parasite kill, which a home freezer cannot achieve. If you cannot confirm flash-freezing with the counter, cook the block through or steam a whole fish instead (the modification above), both carry the ginger-scallion finish just as well.
