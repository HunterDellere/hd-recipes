steps:
  - text: 'Phase 1, make the epis marinade. Combine the 8 g scotch bonnet, 30 g garlic, 80 g scallions, 30 g parsley, 6 g thyme leaves, 100 g bell pepper, 90 g lime juice, 60 g sour orange juice, 30 g olive oil, 1 g ground cloves, 12 g salt, and 3 g black pepper in a blender or food processor. Blend 90 seconds to 2 minutes until smooth and aromatic, scraping down sides as needed. The epis should read deeply green, bright, and aggressively herbal. The yield is roughly 380 g epis.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Phase 2, marinate the pork. Place the 1500 g pork cubes in a large bowl or zip-top bag. Pour over about 280 g of the epis (reserve 100 g of epis; it will be added to the braise later); mix to coat each cube evenly. Refrigerate 12 hours minimum, ideally 24 hours. The long marinade is the structural step that defines Haitian cooking; do not skip.'
    time_min: 720
  - text: 'Phase 3, braise the pork. Transfer the marinated pork with all the marinade into a large Dutch oven or wide heavy pot. Add the 500 g water, 2 bay leaves, and the reserved 100 g epis; stir to combine. Bring to a strong simmer over medium-high heat; reduce to medium-low, partially cover, and braise 60 to 75 minutes until the pork is tender (a fork inserted in a cube should meet little resistance) and most of the liquid has reduced to a thick aromatic glaze coating the meat. Stir occasionally to prevent sticking. The pork should still hold its cubed shape; do not let it fall apart.'
    technique: 'braising'
    time_min: 70
  - text: 'Drain and rest. Lift the braised pork cubes out of the Dutch oven with a slotted spoon onto a plate. Discard any remaining braising liquid (or reserve for a future dish; the epis-flavored broth makes a brilliant cooking liquid for rice or beans). Let the pork cubes air-dry 10 minutes; this is structural for the crisp fry to follow (wet meat does not crisp).'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Phase 4, fry the pork. Heat the 500 g neutral oil in a heavy deep skillet or wide Dutch oven (3 cm of oil) over medium-high heat to 180°C / 360°F (test with a small piece of pork; it should sizzle aggressively on contact). Fry the pork cubes in batches (do not crowd; crowding drops the oil temperature and produces steamed rather than fried pork) 5 to 7 minutes per batch until the surface is deeply crisp and mahogany-dark. Transfer to a paper-towel-lined plate; salt lightly. Repeat with the remaining pork; the fry should produce 3 batches.'
    technique: 'deep-frying'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Phase 5, assemble and serve. Pile the crisp griot onto a serving platter; spoon a generous portion of 200 g pikliz over the top or arrange in a small bowl alongside. Serve with the 60 g lime wedges, fried sweet plantains, and rice and beans on the side. The diner eats the griot in chunks, pairing each bite with pikliz for the structural heat-and-acid contrast that defines the Haitian flavor balance.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Haitian griot is the structural celebration dish of Haiti and one of the great two-stage meat preparations of the African-Caribbean diaspora. The technique (marinate, braise, then fry to crisp) is structurally Haitian and developed from West African slow-cook traditions combined with French colonial frying methods; the resulting dish carries both the depth of a long-cooked stew and the surface character of a deeply fried protein. Master griot and the technique transfers to chicken, goat, and any other slow-cook-then-crisp meat preparation.

  The epis is the structural Haitian flavor foundation. Epis (literally spice in Haitian Creole) is the green seasoning paste that underlies most Haitian cooking, similar in function to Caribbean sofrito but structurally distinct (epis is herbier, more aggressively garlic-and-parsley-driven, and almost always includes scotch bonnet). Make epis in a large batch (double or triple the recipe) and freeze in 100 g portions; it is the structural starting point for Haitian rice, beans, soups, and braises. The 12-to-24-hour marinade in epis is the structural step that distinguishes Haitian-cooked meat from generic braised meat.

  The two-stage cook is structural. Braising tenderizes the pork through collagen-to-gelatin conversion; frying develops the structural crisp surface that defines griot. Either stage alone produces a different dish: braised-only is structurally pork stew; fried-only (without the braise) is structurally tough fried pork. The braise-then-fry sequence is the canonical Haitian technique and is what produces the structural griot texture (tender inside, crisp outside).

  The fry temperature matters. 180°C / 360°F is the structural window; lower temperatures produce greasy pork without proper crisping, higher temperatures burn the surface before the interior reheats from the braise. Use a thermometer; if no thermometer, test with a small piece of pork (should sizzle aggressively on contact, develop crisp surface in 5 to 7 minutes, not turn dark too quickly).

  The pikliz pairing is structural. Pikliz (the fiery pickled cabbage-carrot-scotch-bonnet relish) is non-negotiable for canonical griot service. The structural Haitian flavor balance depends on the acid-and-heat-and-crunch of the pikliz cutting the rich-fried-fat of the griot; without pikliz, the dish reads as good fried pork but not as canonical griot. Make the pikliz at least 24 hours ahead; the canonical pikliz matures over several days and is structurally complete at the 48-hour mark.

  Versatility. The epis-marinate-and-braise technique transfers to chicken (griot di poul), goat (griot di kabrit), and beef (griot di bef). The fry-to-crisp finish works on any of these proteins. The pikliz pairing carries; serve any griot variant with pikliz and the structural Haitian flavor balance holds. Beyond griot, epis is the structural starting point for Haitian rice (riz djon-djon, with black mushrooms), Haitian red beans (sos pwa), and Haitian soup (soup joumou, the squash soup served on Haitian Independence Day).

  The canonical Haitian plate. Griot is served with pikliz, riz collé aux pois (rice cooked with red beans), bannann peze (fried green plantains pounded flat then re-fried), and avocado wedges. This is the structural Sunday or celebration plate in Haiti; the four components together produce a complete meal with structural acid, heat, starch, fat, and fresh elements. A glass of Barbancourt rum or Prestige beer (the Haitian lager) is the canonical drink pairing.

  Storage. Marinated raw pork keeps 48 hours refrigerated in epis. Cooked griot keeps 4 days refrigerated; freezes 2 months. Reheat in a 200°C / 400°F oven 10 to 12 minutes uncovered to re-crisp the surface; do not microwave (loses the structural crisp). Pikliz keeps 1 month refrigerated and reads better at 1 week than at 1 day.
