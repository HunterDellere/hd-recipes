  - 'This is a 3-week project, minimum. Active work is about 40 minutes spread over two sessions; the rest is the jar sitting at cool room temperature lacto-fermenting. Start it at least 21 days before you want to make lahpet thoke, and know that 4 to 6 weeks tastes better than 3.'

steps:
  - text: 'Soften the leaf. If using fresh or frozen tender green tea leaf, rinse it and steam it over simmering water for 3 to 5 minutes until limp and dark, the way you would blanch a green. If using dry sencha, pour just-boiled water over the 100 g / about 1¼ cups loose leaf to cover, let it sit 10 minutes to rehydrate fully, then drain hard. Either way you want soft, pliable, fully hydrated leaf with no raw crunch left, and you want it well drained, surface water dilutes the brine.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Rinse out the bitterness. Tip the softened leaf into a bowl of cool water, swish, and drain; repeat twice more with fresh water. Raw green tea leaf is aggressively astringent and tannic, and these rinses pull out the harshest catechins so the ferment starts from a cleaner base. Squeeze the rinsed leaf firmly in your hands or in a clean towel to wring out as much water as you can, the drier the leaf, the more concentrated and stable the ferment.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Pound and salt. Transfer the squeezed leaf to a mortar, or chop it coarsely on a board, and pound or press it into a rough, cohesive paste, this bruises the cells and releases the juices the lactic bacteria will feed on. Work in the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt, the crushed 6 g / 2 cloves garlic if using, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp lime juice, kneading until the leaf feels slick and wet and the salt has fully dissolved into the released liquid. The mixture should be glossy and just barely weeping brine when squeezed.'
    technique: 'lacto-fermentation'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Pack airtight under pressure. Press the salted leaf hard into a small, scrupulously clean glass jar, tamping down with a spoon or muddler to drive out air pockets, until the leaf is compacted and its own brine rises to just cover the surface. The leaf MUST sit below the liquid line. If it does not weep enough brine to submerge itself, top with a teaspoon or two of 2.5% salt water (2.5 g salt per 100 ml). Lay a small water-filled zip bag or a clean weight directly on the surface to hold the leaf down.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Cap with oil and seal. Pour the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil over the surface so it floats as a thin film, a second anaerobic barrier on top of the brine. Close the jar loosely (fermentation produces gas that must escape; a tight seal can build pressure) or use a fermentation lid. Label it with the date.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Ferment cool and slow. Leave the jar at cool room temperature (18 to 22°C / 64 to 72°F) out of direct light for at least 3 weeks, ideally 4 to 6. For the first week, check daily: push any floating leaf back under the brine, and if you see a thin white film (kahm yeast, harmless) skim it; fuzzy or colored mold means air got in and the batch is lost. Over the weeks the leaf darkens, softens further, and turns from harsh-astringent to mellow, sour, and savory as the pH drops below 4.'
    time_min: 30240
  - text: 'Taste and store. From week 3 on, taste a pinch: it should read pleasantly sour and faintly bitter, soft, almost olive-like, with the raw tea harshness gone. When you like it, it is done. Keep the jar refrigerated, always with the leaf submerged under its brine and oil, where it holds for several months and only deepens. Drain and lightly squeeze a portion before dressing it into lahpet thoke.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why ferment tea at all. Across nearly all of the world tea is brewed and the leaf discarded, but in Myanmar and parts of the Shan hills the tender leaf is also a food: lacto-fermented into lahpet and eaten. Fermentation does to tea what it does to cabbage or olives, lactic-acid bacteria convert the leaf's sugars to acid, dropping the pH, preserving the leaf, and transforming a harsh, tannic, undrinkable-raw green into something soft, sour, savory, and faintly bitter. The harsh catechins that make raw green tea astringent mellow over the weeks; what is left is closer to a pickled green or a mild olive than to a cup of tea.

  Why salt and submersion are non-negotiable. This is a true anaerobic lacto-ferment, identical in safety logic to sauerkraut or kimchi. The 2.5% salt concentration selects for salt-tolerant lactic-acid bacteria and suppresses spoilage microbes; keeping every scrap of leaf under brine and oil keeps oxygen-loving molds and yeasts out. Do not cut the salt and do not let leaf poke above the liquid. A white film on the surface is harmless kahm yeast and can be skimmed; fuzzy, green, black, or pink growth means the batch breathed and must be discarded.

  Why rinse before fermenting. Raw green tea leaf carries a punishing tannin load, far more than you would ever brew into a cup, because you are about to eat the whole leaf rather than a dilute infusion. The triple cold-water rinse strips the most aggressive catechins up front so the finished lahpet reads pleasantly bitter rather than mouth-puckering. Skip it and even a well-fermented batch stays harsh.

  The from-scratch caveat. Genuine lahpet starts from a specific fermentation-grade leaf, picked young for pickling and pressed for months in bamboo or pits, and it is not the same plant material sold as drinking tea. This home version, built from steamed drinking-grade green leaf and a faster jar ferment, is a faithful approximation rather than an exact reproduction; it gets you most of the way to the real thing with what is actually buyable outside Myanmar. If you can source true fermentation leaf, use it, and ferment longer.
