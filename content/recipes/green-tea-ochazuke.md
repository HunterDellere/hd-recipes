steps:
  - text: 'Warm the rice into a bowl. Mound the 180 g / 1 cup cooked short-grain rice in a deep individual bowl. If it is fridge-cold, sprinkle a few drops of water over it and warm it through (30 to 40 seconds in the microwave) so the hot tea will not cool instantly against cold rice.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Brew the sencha gently. Bring the 250 ml / 1 cup water to a boil, then pull it off the heat and let it sit 60 to 90 seconds to drop to about 80°C / 175°F. Pour it over the 5 g / 2 tsp sencha (add the 2 g kombu square now if using, for a dashi-leaning broth) and steep just 60 seconds, no longer, then strain. Over-steeping or boiling water pulls bitter catechins and makes the broth harsh.'
    technique: 'tea-steeping'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Season the broth. Stir the 1 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt into the strained hot tea until dissolved, then taste. The broth should be lightly savory, like a delicate seasoned tea, not aggressively salty; it is meant to season the rice, not to be drunk like soup on its own. Adjust salt by the pinch.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Top the rice. Scatter the 2 g / ¼ sheet snipped nori, the 5 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame seeds, and the 15 g / 1 sliced scallion over the rice. Add the 15 g umeboshi torn into pieces and a 2 g / ¼ tsp dab of wasabi to the side if using; their salt and sharpness are what make the bowl taste complete.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour and eat at once. Pour the hot seasoned sencha around and over the topped rice until it pools about halfway up the mound, you want the rice partly submerged, not drowned. Eat immediately with chopsticks and a spoon, scooping rice and broth together; the heat fades fast and the dish is best piping hot, the nori half-dissolving into the tea.'

notes: |
  The tea is the dish. Ochazuke (お茶漬け, literally "steeped in tea") is one of the few preparations where brewed tea is used as a savory broth rather than a beverage: hot green tea poured directly over rice to make a fast, light, restorative one-bowl meal. Historically it was the frugal, end-of-day way to use up cold rice, and it remains a late-night, hangover, no-appetite comfort food across Japan. Because the tea is doing the job of a soup, brewing it well matters as much here as in a cup: gentle, sub-boiling water and a short steep keep the broth fragrant and faintly sweet rather than bitter.

  Why below boiling, always. Sencha's catechins (bitter, astringent) extract fast at high temperature while its L-theanine (sweet, savory) extracts slowly. Boiling water tips that balance hard toward bitterness, which in a drinking cup is unpleasant and in a broth you are eating becomes a harsh, drying background to every bite. Letting the just-boiled water fall to about 80°C and steeping only a minute gives a broth that reads clean and aromatic. If you only have a hotter pour, switch to genmaicha or hojicha, whose roasted character forgives the heat.

  The role of the toppings. The tea-rice base is deliberately plain; the toppings are what season it and give it dimension. Nori brings marine umami and dissolves slightly into the broth, sesame adds nutty fat, scallion adds a fresh allium bite, and the umeboshi or pickle supplies the salt-sour jolt that makes the whole bowl snap into focus. Keep them light, ochazuke is meant to be delicate and quick, the opposite of a heavy donburi.
