source:
  name: 'Mumbai street food tradition (mid-20th century)'
  note: 'Pav bhaji (पाव भाजी, "pav" = soft Portuguese-derived bread roll, "bhaji" = vegetable curry) was developed in Mumbai in the 1850s to 1860s as a fast vegetarian meal for textile mill workers; cooks took whatever vegetables were on hand and mashed them into a quick spiced curry, served with the Portuguese-introduced soft bread. The dish became a Mumbai street-food signature in the 20th century and is now a household staple across India.'

steps:
  - text: 'Boil the vegetables. Bring 1500 ml / 6 cups water to a boil with 8 g salt in a 4-quart pot. Add the 400 g potato chunks and 200 g cauliflower florets. Cook 10 to 12 minutes until both are fork-tender. Add the 100 g frozen peas in the last 2 minutes. Drain through a colander, reserving 200 ml of the cooking water. Set the vegetables aside.'
    time_min: 13
  - text: 'Mash the vegetables. Transfer the drained vegetables to a large bowl. Mash with a potato masher until mostly smooth with some chunky bits remaining (the texture target is 80% smooth, 20% chunky, over-mashing produces a gluey dish, under-mashing produces a too-chunky one). Set aside.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Bloom and brown the aromatics. Heat the 60 g butter and 15 ml oil in a wide heavy pan or wok over medium heat until the butter melts and starts to foam. Add the 4 g cumin seeds; bloom 30 seconds until fragrant. Add the 200 g chopped onion and 100 g chopped bell pepper. Stir-fry 6 to 7 minutes over medium heat until the onion goes from white to deep golden-brown and the bell pepper softens. The browning is structural to the dish color and depth.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the spice masala. Stir in the 20 g ginger-garlic paste and 8 g minced green chilies; stir-fry 60 seconds until the raw garlic smell goes off. Add the 15 g pav bhaji masala, 8 g Kashmiri chili powder, and 1 g turmeric; stir-fry 30 to 45 seconds, splashing with 1 to 2 tbsp water if the spices threaten to scorch. The spices need brief contact with hot fat to bloom but cannot sit in dry hot fat for long.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Build the tomato base. Add the 400 g chopped tomatoes, 30 g tomato paste, and 6 g salt. Increase heat to medium-high. Stir-fry 8 to 10 minutes; the tomatoes will release water in the first 3 minutes (do not drain, let it cook off), then break down into a glossy red paste, and finally the oil and butter will pool at the edges of the masala (the visual marker that the bhuna has worked). The kitchen smells deeply of tomato-and-spice. This step is the foundational character of the dish; do not shortcut.'
    technique: 'red-oil-rendering'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Add the mashed vegetables and simmer. Add the mashed vegetables and 200 ml water (or reserved cooking water for richer dish) to the masala. Stir thoroughly. Bring to a simmer; reduce heat to medium-low. Simmer 12 to 15 minutes, stirring every 2 to 3 minutes. Use the back of a wooden spoon or potato masher to further break down any remaining vegetable chunks during cooking; the bhaji should reach a smooth-with-some-texture consistency. Add additional 100 ml water if the dish goes too thick.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Finish the bhaji. Stir in the 15 ml lemon juice and half the chopped cilantro. Taste; adjust salt, chili, and acid. The dish should land at deeply tomato-spiced, faintly buttery, with a clear lemon lift. Pull off heat. Stir in the 30 g additional butter and let it melt into the surface, this is the Mumbai-street butter-on-top signature. Sprinkle the remaining cilantro over the top.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Toast the pav. Heat a wide skillet or griddle over medium heat. Melt the 60 g butter for the pav. Split each pav roll horizontally (do not separate fully, leave hinged at one edge if possible). Place the pav cut-side-down in the buttery pan. Toast 90 seconds to 2 minutes; the cut surfaces should turn golden-brown and crisp. Flip and briefly toast the outside (30 seconds; just to warm). Remove to a plate.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Plate and serve. Spoon the bhaji into wide individual serving bowls (about 200 to 250 ml per bowl). Top each portion with a small additional pat of butter (5 g per bowl). Serve immediately with 2 toasted pav per person, the diced raw onion in a small side bowl, lemon wedges, and additional cilantro. Eat by tearing pieces of pav, scooping bhaji, topping with raw onion and a squeeze of lemon. The combination of butter-toasted soft pav, deeply spiced vegetable bhaji, raw onion crunch, and bright lemon is the structural Mumbai eating experience.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why pav bhaji masala is non-negotiable. The pav bhaji masala blend has a specific aromatic profile that defines the dish, coriander-fennel forward, with amchur (dried mango powder) for tang and cloves for warmth. Substituting garam masala (which has different proportions and additional cardamom) produces a recognizable but wrong-flavored dish. The commercial blends (MDH, Everest, Shan, National) are widely available at Indian groceries and are the right choice; the custom-blend substitution above approximates but is not equivalent.

  Why the butter is structural. Pav bhaji is a deliberately butter-rich dish; one of the Mumbai street-stall signatures is the cook visibly slapping a generous pat of butter onto the cooking griddle and onto each plated portion. The butter is not a finishing decoration; it is part of the structural calorie load and mouthfeel. Reduced-butter versions (using less than 90 g total across the recipe) produce a noticeably leaner, less-Mumbai dish. Diners who avoid dairy can use ghee or vegan butter; the dish reads slightly different but works.

  The Mumbai street technique vs the home technique. Mumbai street-stall pav bhaji is cooked on a giant flat griddle (tawa) where the cook continuously mashes and re-mashes the cooking bhaji, splashes it with water, and adds butter pats throughout. The home pan-cooked version above approximates the technique but with less flat-surface area; the bhaji texture comes out slightly thicker and less integrated than the street version. Both are correct for their context.

  Eating mechanics. Pav bhaji is a casual one-bowl meal, eaten by hand with the toasted pav as the primary tool. The diner tears pieces of pav, scoops bhaji, tops with raw onion and a squeeze of lemon, and eats. A spoon is acceptable for the bhaji itself but the pav-scoop technique is the textbook approach. Reheats well, day-two pav bhaji is widely considered as good as day-one or better as the spices integrate. The pav must be freshly toasted; do not toast ahead and keep, the toasted pav softens within 30 minutes of toasting.
