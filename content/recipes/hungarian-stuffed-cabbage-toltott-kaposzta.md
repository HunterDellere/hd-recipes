  name: 'Hungarian Christmas Eve and Sunday-dinner canon'
  note: 'Töltött káposzta is the structural Hungarian stuffed cabbage and a centerpiece of Hungarian Christmas Eve, Sunday dinners, and special-occasion meals. The defining elements are: blanched green cabbage leaves as the wrapper (not raw, the leaves must be soft enough to roll without tearing); a filling of ground pork-and-beef plus partially-cooked rice (raw rice swells too much during the braise and bursts the rolls); aggressive sweet Hungarian paprika in both the filling and the broth (paprika is structural to all Hungarian cooking); a bed of sauerkraut underneath the rolls that contributes the structural tang; smoked sausage (kolbász) and bacon tucked between the rolls for additional smoky-fat depth; tomato in the braise liquid; and a sour cream finish stirred into the hot braising liquid. The dish improves overnight and reheats beautifully; in Hungarian homes it is often made the day before for Christmas Eve service.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The total cook is 2.5 hours: 45 minutes prep (blanching cabbage, mixing filling, rolling), 90 minutes braise. Plan an afternoon cook.'
  - 'The rice in the filling must be partially cooked (about 60 percent done; 5 to 8 minutes in salted boiling water from raw). Fully cooked rice gives a mushy filling; fully raw rice swells dramatically during the braise and bursts the cabbage rolls.'
  - 'Hungarian sweet paprika (édes paprika) is structural; use a fresh bright-red Hungarian-imported paprika (Szegedi or Pride of Szeged brand). Old or low-quality paprika reads brown and tasteless; Hungarian cooking lives or dies on the paprika quality.'

steps:
  - text: 'Bring a large stockpot of salted water to a boil. Cut a deep cone out of the cabbage core. Submerge the whole head core-side up; blanch 3 minutes, then carefully peel off the softened outer leaves with tongs (they should come off easily without tearing). Continue blanching and peeling until you have 12 large pliable leaves. Shave off the thick central rib from each leaf (cut at a shallow angle to remove the thick part without going through the leaf). Set aside the leaves. Shred the remaining cabbage core into thin ribbons; reserve.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Par-cook the rice. Add the 100 g rice to fresh salted boiling water; cook 5 to 8 minutes until firm at the bite (still chewy, not fully cooked). Drain; spread on a plate to cool.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Sauté the filling onion. Heat 15 ml oil in a small skillet over medium heat. Sauté the 150 g diced onion 4 to 5 minutes until softened and translucent. Cool slightly.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Mix the filling. In a large bowl, combine the 350 g ground pork, 350 g ground beef, cooled rice, sautéed onion, 15 g garlic, 1 egg, 8 g paprika, 3 g marjoram, 10 g salt, and 3 g pepper. Mix thoroughly with gloved hands until uniformly distributed and slightly tacky (mixing develops myosin and binds the filling; mix for 60 seconds).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Form the rolls. Lay a cabbage leaf flat on the cutting board, rib-side away. Place about 80 g of filling in a log shape near the rib end. Fold the rib end up over the filling; fold both sides in; roll up tightly into a 10 cm log. Place seam-side down on a plate. Repeat for all 12 leaves.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Prepare the braise base. In a large heavy Dutch oven over medium-high heat, render the 100 g diced bacon for 4 minutes until lightly crispy. Add the 200 g diced onion; cook 5 minutes until softened. Add the 200 g sliced kolbász; cook 2 minutes more to lightly brown.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Add the 10 g / 1 tbsp paprika; stir 30 seconds, off-heat if needed to prevent burning (paprika burns rapidly above 150 °C and turns bitter). Add the 30 g tomato paste; cook 90 seconds until darkened.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Spread the 800 g drained sauerkraut and the shredded fresh cabbage core (from step 1) over the bacon-onion-kolbász mixture; toss gently to distribute. Add the 240 g tomato sauce, 800 ml stock, and 2 bay leaves. Bring to a simmer.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Nestle the 12 cabbage rolls seam-side down into the sauerkraut mixture, arranged in a single layer if possible. The liquid should come about two-thirds up the rolls. Add more stock if needed. Cover with the lid slightly ajar; transfer to a 160 °C / 325 °F oven (or maintain a steady stovetop simmer). Braise 90 minutes.'
    technique: 'braising'
    time_min: 90
  - text: 'Make the sour cream slurry. In a small bowl, whisk the 200 g sour cream with the 15 g / 2 tbsp flour until smooth. Ladle about 250 ml of the hot braising liquid into the slurry, whisking continuously to temper. Pour the tempered cream back into the pot around the rolls; stir gently to integrate. The sauce should thicken slightly and turn pale pink-orange.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Bring to a gentle simmer for 2 minutes to cook out the raw flour taste. Fish out the bay leaves. Taste; adjust salt (the kolbász and sauerkraut contribute most of the salt). Sprinkle with 10 g chopped dill if using. Serve 2 rolls per plate with a generous spoon of the sauerkraut-broth around them, plus boiled potatoes or thick slices of crusty bread on the side.'
    time_min: 4

notes: |
  Why par-cooked rice. The rice in the filling will continue to cook during the 90-minute braise; starting with fully raw rice means the rice swells to 2.5 times its starting volume during the cook and bursts the cabbage rolls. Par-cooking the rice to 60 percent done lets it finish cooking to fully tender during the braise without bursting. Starting with fully cooked rice produces mushy filling. The 5 to 8 minute par-cook is the structural compromise.

  Why a sauerkraut bed. The sauerkraut underneath the cabbage rolls contributes the structural tangy base note that makes the dish read Hungarian rather than generic-stuffed-cabbage. The sauerkraut also catches the rendered fat and meat juices from the rolls during the braise, becoming a deeply flavored side that is structurally part of the dish. Some Hungarian households serve more sauerkraut alongside than rolls themselves.

  Why the sour cream slurry. The sour cream finish is the universal Hungarian sauce finisher; it transforms the braising liquid into a structurally Hungarian sauce. Adding the cream alone curdles in the hot liquid; mixing with flour first stabilizes it and gives the sauce additional body. The result is a pale pink-orange sauce that coats the rolls and the sauerkraut.

  Why the dish improves overnight. The 90-minute braise integrates the filling, cabbage, sauerkraut, and broth, but overnight refrigeration deepens this further: the flavors meld, the cabbage becomes more tender, and the broth thickens slightly as the gelatin from the meat sets. Hungarian Christmas Eve homes traditionally cook the dish the day before; reheating produces a dramatically better dish than same-day.

  Pairings. Canonical with boiled potatoes or thick crusty bread for sopping the sauce. A glass of cold Hungarian wine (Tokaji Furmint for the white, Egri Bikavér for the red).

  Storage. Improves overnight; keeps 5 days refrigerated. Reheat gently in a covered pan over low heat or in a 160 °C / 325 °F oven for 25 minutes. Freezes well; portion into 2-roll containers with sauerkraut and broth.
