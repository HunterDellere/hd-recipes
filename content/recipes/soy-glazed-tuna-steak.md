steps:
  - text: 'Mix and marinate. In a shallow dish whisk the 45 g / 3 tbsp light soy sauce, the 30 g / 2 tbsp mirin, the 15 g / 1 tbsp honey, the 8 g / 1 tbsp grated ginger, and the 6 g / 2 cloves grated garlic. Lay the two 340 g / 12 oz tuna steaks in and turn to coat. Marinate 15 minutes (up to 30), turning once. Then lift the steaks out and pat the surface dry, reserving all the marinade; a wet surface steams instead of searing.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Sear the tuna. Heat the 14 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a wide skillet over medium-high until just smoking. Lay the steaks in and sear undisturbed about 2 minutes per face, until well-browned and an instant-read thermometer reads 55 to 58 °C at the thickest point. The honey in the marinade left on the surface will catch and color fast, so watch it. Pull the steaks to a plate to rest; they finish to a moist medium off the heat.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Reduce the marinade into a drizzle. Pour the reserved marinade into the hot pan and boil hard for 1 to 2 minutes, scraping up the browned bits, until it thickens to a glossy, pourable glaze that coats a spoon. The hard boil cooks the marinade through, so it is safe despite having touched raw fish. If it gets too thick, loosen with a teaspoon of water.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Drizzle and top. Set each steak over rice, spoon the warm glaze over the top, then scatter the 15 g / 2 scallions and the 6 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame seeds and finish with a few drops of the 3 g / ½ tsp toasted sesame oil. Serve right away while the glaze is glossy and warm.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why reduce the used marinade. The marinade is mostly soy, mirin, and honey, all of which concentrate into a classic teriyaki-style glaze when boiled down, so throwing it out wastes the best part. Boiling it hard for a minute or two cooks it through completely, which makes it safe even though it touched raw fish; the heat is more than enough. You get a glaze and a marinade out of one bowl of ingredients with no extra work.

  The honey does double duty. Beyond sweetness, the honey (or any sugar you swap in) is what lets the glaze thicken and lacquer, and it is what makes the seared surface of the fish color quickly. That fast coloring is why you sear over medium-high rather than screaming-high heat and watch the pan closely: a sugar glaze goes from caramelized to burnt in seconds.

  Cooked through, on purpose. This is built for fish you cannot or do not want to eat raw, so it asks nothing of the source beyond freshness. The trade with fully-cooked tuna is that it dries fast, which is exactly what the glaze is for: pull the steak a few degrees early, let it finish off the heat, and let the sweet-salty drizzle carry the moisture and flavor the lean flesh lacks on its own.
