title: 'Gravlax'
desc: 'A side of raw salmon buried in a dry cure of salt, sugar, crushed white pepper, and a heavy blanket of dill, optionally laced with aquavit, then weighted and turned over 48 to 72 hours until the flesh firms to a translucent, sliceable density. The cure is osmotic, not thermal: salt and sugar draw water out of the muscle and drive their own ions in, lowering water activity until the fish is preserved and the texture shifts from soft and wet to dense and silky. No smoke, no heat. Sliced paper-thin on the bias and served with a sweet-sharp mustard-dill sauce (hovmästarsås) over dark rye.'

source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Scandinavian curing tradition; gravlax (literally "buried salmon" from the medieval practice of fermenting salt-packed fish in shoreline pits) is the salt-sugar-dill cure common to Sweden, Norway, and Denmark.'
  note: 'Gravlax is cured, not smoked, and that is the line that separates it from lox (brine-cured then cold-smoked) and from hot-smoked salmon (cooked). The cure here is a pure osmotic and enzymatic process: no heat ever touches the fish. The 60/40 salt-to-sugar ratio and the 48-to-72-hour window are calibrated to firm the flesh and lower water activity enough for safe, sliceable density without over-salting the interior or turning the surface to leather.'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 2-to-3-day project with no active cooking. Plan to start the cure 48 to 72 hours before you want to serve. The fish cures untouched in the refrigerator; your only job after day one is to flip the package and re-weight it every 12 hours.'
  - 'Freeze the salmon first for parasite safety unless you are buying sushi-grade / previously-frozen fish. The FDA standard for raw-consumed wild salmon is 7 days at minus 20 C in a home freezer, or 15 hours at minus 35 C commercially. Farmed Atlantic salmon raised on pelleted feed is the lower-risk choice and is what most commercial gravlax uses; if buying farmed sushi-grade, it has typically already met the freeze standard. Thaw fully in the refrigerator before curing.'
  - 'You need a non-reactive vessel and a flat weight. A glass or ceramic baking dish with a second dish or a foil-wrapped board on top, loaded with two large cans, applies the even pressure that drives the cure liquid out and compacts the flesh. Set the dish on a rimmed tray; the cure will weep a surprising volume of brine over three days.'
  - 'Slice cold, with a long thin blade. Gravlax slices cleanly only when fully chilled and cured; a warm or under-cured side tears. A long, flexible slicing knife (a salmon slicer or a sharp carving knife) drawn at a low angle gives the wide translucent sheets the dish is known for.'

steps:
  - text: 'Prep the fish. Run a fingertip along the centerline of the 900 g / 2 lb salmon side and pull any remaining pin bones with fish tweezers; a stray bone ruins a thin slice. Blot the flesh and skin fully dry with paper towels. Trim any thin belly flap that is dramatically thinner than the rest of the side, it cures faster and over-salts; reserve it for tartare or freeze it.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Mix the cure. Combine the 75 g / 6 tbsp kosher salt, the 50 g / ¼ cup granulated sugar, the 6 g / 1 tbsp coarsely crushed white peppercorns, and the 4 g / zest of 1 lemon in a bowl. This is a 60/40 salt-to-sugar ratio by weight: the salt firms and preserves, the sugar pulls water just as hard while keeping the flesh from going leather-stiff. Stir in the 30 g / 2 tbsp aquavit if using, the mixture will clump into a wet sand.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Build the cure package. Lay a sheet of parchment in a glass or ceramic dish and scatter a third of the chopped 60 g / 1 bunch dill across it. Set the salmon skin-side down on the dill. Pack the salt-sugar cure evenly over the entire flesh surface, pressing it into the thick shoulder more heavily than the thin tail since thickness dictates cure time. Blanket the top with the remaining two-thirds of the 60 g / 1 bunch dill. The flesh should disappear under a green carpet.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Weight and chill. Fold the parchment over the fish, then set a second dish or a flat board on top and load it with two large cans or a 1 to 2 kg weight. Even pressure drives the drawn-out brine away from the flesh and compacts the muscle into a sliceable density. Set the whole rig on a rimmed tray to catch the weep and refrigerate. The cure has now started; nothing happens by heat, only by the salt and sugar gradient pulling water out of the muscle cells and driving their ions in (osmotic dehydration), which simultaneously denatures the surface proteins and firms the texture.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Flip every 12 hours. Twice a day, pour off the accumulated brine from the tray (it can be a half cup by day two), flip the whole salmon package over so the side that was facing up now faces down, re-cover, and re-weight. Flipping bathes both faces evenly in the migrating cure liquid and keeps the salt penetration uniform top to bottom. Cure 48 hours for a silky, just-set texture or up to 72 hours for a firmer, more traditionally dense and well-salted result. A thick center-cut side wants the full 72; a thinner side is done at 48.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Test for doneness and rinse. The fish is cured when the flesh has gone from raw-translucent and soft to a deeper, more uniform translucency that feels firm and springs back when pressed, no longer soft like raw fish. Scrape off the dill and cure, then rinse the side briefly under cold water to remove surface salt and pat completely dry. Optional but classic: paint the surface with a thin coat of 15 g / 1 tbsp dijon mustard and re-blanket with a little fresh chopped dill for the final hour to set a green-flecked crust.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Make the mustard-dill sauce. Whisk the 45 g / 3 tbsp dijon mustard, the 15 g / 1 tbsp whole-grain mustard, the 25 g / 2 tbsp granulated sugar, and the 20 g / 4 tsp white wine vinegar in a bowl until smooth. Drizzle in the 120 g / ½ cup neutral oil in a slow thin stream while whisking constantly, exactly as you would build a vinaigrette; the mustard mucilage stabilizes the emulsion into a loose, glossy, pourable sauce. Fold in the 15 g / 3 tbsp finely chopped dill and the 2 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt. Taste: it should read sweet-sharp and bright, the vinegar acid cutting the coming richness of the fish. Refrigerate; it holds a week.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Slice and serve. With the cured side skin-side down on a board, hold a long thin slicing knife at a shallow 20-degree angle and draw it across the grain in long strokes, cutting wide translucent sheets and releasing each from the skin at the bottom of the stroke. Always slice toward the tail. Fan the slices over dark rye or crispbread, spoon the mustard-dill sauce alongside (do not drown the fish), and set out the lemon wedges from the 1 lemon for a squeeze of finishing acid at the table. Serve cold.'
    time_min: 8

notes: |
  Why cure and not smoke. Gravlax, lox, and smoked salmon are routinely conflated and they are three different products. Gravlax is dry-cured raw fish: salt and sugar pull water out, lower water activity, and firm the flesh without any heat. Lox is brine-cured (wet salt) and then usually cold-smoked. Hot-smoked salmon is cooked through by smoke heat and flakes apart. The defining experience of gravlax is the silky, dense, still-raw texture and the clean dill-and-cure flavor, with nothing masking the fish. If you want smoke, you want a different recipe.

  The salt-sugar ratio is the whole game. The 60/40 salt-to-sugar split is the workable center of the tradition. More salt firms faster and preserves harder but risks a leathery, over-salted exterior; more sugar gives a softer, sweeter, more delicate cure that some Danish styles prefer. Both salt and sugar are humectants that draw water out by osmosis, but salt also denatures and tightens the muscle proteins, which is why it does the structural firming. Keep them measured by weight, not volume, since kosher salt brands differ by nearly half in density.

  Why weight and flip. The pressure from the weight forces the brine that the cure draws out away from the flesh so it does not sit and dilute the cure at the surface, and it compacts the muscle into the dense, cohesive block that slices into wide unbroken sheets. Flipping every 12 hours keeps the salt gradient even top to bottom; an unflipped side cures saltier on the bottom face where the brine pools. Skip neither step.

  Doneness, timing, and thickness. Cure time is governed by thickness, not weight. A thick center-cut side wants 60 to 72 hours; a thinner side or one tapering to the tail is done at 48 and will over-salt if pushed to 72. The fish is ready when the flesh is uniformly firm and springy and the translucency has deepened, no soft raw give remaining. Under-cured fish is wet and tears when sliced; over-cured fish is dry and aggressively salty. When in doubt, cut a test slice off the tail end at 48 hours.

  Storage and safety. Keep the parasite-freeze rule unless the fish is verified previously-frozen or labeled sushi-grade farmed Atlantic, which is the lowest-risk and most common commercial gravlax fish. Once cured, the salmon keeps wrapped in parchment in the coldest part of the refrigerator for up to one week; the cure itself is a preservation method, but this is lightly cured table gravlax, not shelf-stable salt cod. For longer storage, slice and freeze in portions. The mustard-dill sauce holds a week refrigerated and actually improves after a day as the mustard mellows.
