title: 'Whole fried flounder with nuoc cham'
desc: 'A whole flounder, scaled and gutted, cross-scored to the bone on both sides, salted and dried bone-dry, dredged in a thin cornstarch-and-rice-flour coat, and fried in hot oil until the broad flat skin shatters crisp and the sweet white flake lifts off the frame. source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Vietnamese ca chien whole-fried fish canon'
  note: 'Ca chien is the family of whole-fried fish across the Vietnamese table, from street stalls to home kitchens, served whole over herbs with nuoc cham. The flatfish version exploits the geometry of the flounder: the broad flat body presents far more skin to the oil than a round fish of the same weight, so the crisp-skin yield is maximal. The dish is structurally a frying problem (a dry, well-scored fish in hot oil) and a balance problem (the nuoc cham supplies all of the salt, sweet, sour, and umami the lean fish lacks on its own). Gluten-free as written: the dredge is cornstarch and rice flour, and the nuoc cham is built on fish sauce rather than wheat-based soy, so a gluten-free fish sauce makes the whole plate gluten-free.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Make the nuoc cham first and let it rest. The sauce wants 5 to 10 minutes for the garlic and chili to infuse, and it must be poured the instant the fish comes out of the oil. Build it before you touch the fish so the frying phase is uninterrupted.'
  - 'Score and dry the fish thoroughly before anything goes near the oil. A wet fish does two bad things: the surface water flashes to steam and prevents the skin from crisping, and water hitting 190 C oil throws violent spatter. Cross-score to the bone on both sides, salt, then pat bone-dry inside, out, and into every cut. Air-drying uncovered in the refrigerator for 30 minutes is the single biggest upgrade to the crust.'
  - 'Manage the oil temperature with a thermometer, and clear the area around the pot. Fry at 185 to 190 C; below 175 C the fish absorbs oil and goes greasy instead of crisp, above 200 C the dredge scorches before the spine cooks. A whole fish displaces a lot of oil and can boil over a too-full pot, so fill no more than halfway and have a lid and a sheet pan within reach.'
  - 'Build the herb plate while the fish air-dries. Shred the romaine, pick the cilantro and mint, rinse the bean sprouts, and slice the scallion so the platter is ready to receive the fish the moment it drains. Cook the jasmine rice on a parallel track so it finishes as the fish fries.'

steps:
  - text: 'Build the nuoc cham first. In a bowl, whisk the 35 g / 2½ tbsp granulated sugar into the 90 g / 6 tbsp warm water until fully dissolved. Whisk in the 45 g / 3 tbsp fish sauce and the 45 g / 3 tbsp lime juice; the sauce should read in equal balance across salty, sweet, and sour. Stir in the 6 g / 1 to 2 minced garlic cloves and the 3 g / 1 to 2 sliced Thai chilies, which will float on the surface (correct, do not strain). Set aside to rest at least 5 minutes so the aromatics infuse.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Prep the fish. Rinse the 600 g flounder inside and out and scrape any dark blood line from the cavity with the back of a spoon; the blood line carries the strongest fishy flavor. Lay the fish flat and, with a sharp knife held at a 45-degree angle, cut parallel cross-scores 2 cm apart all the way down to the bone across the whole flat body, on both sides. The deep scoring is structural: it lets hot oil reach the dense flesh near the spine so the thick center cooks through before the thin edges burn, and it multiplies the crisp surface area.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Salt and dry. Rub the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt over the skin, into every score, and through the cavity. Let the fish sit 10 minutes, then pat it completely dry with paper towels, working into the cuts. For the best crust, set the fish on a rack and air-dry uncovered in the refrigerator for 30 minutes. A bone-dry fish is the difference between a skin that shatters and one that steams limp, and a dry surface fries far more safely.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Dredge. Whisk the 40 g / ⅓ cup cornstarch and the 30 g / ¼ cup white rice flour together on a wide plate. Press the dry fish into the mixture on both sides and into the scores, coating it in a thin, even, dry film; lift and shake off all excess so the coat is barely visible. A thick wet dredge gums up and fries pale; a thin dry one shatters.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Heat the 1000 g / about 4 cups neutral oil in a wide deep pan or wok to 185 to 190 C / 365 to 375 F, filling the vessel no more than halfway. Use a thermometer; below 175 C the fish soaks oil and goes greasy, above 200 C the dredge scorches before the spine cooks. The oil is ready when a pinch of the dredge flour dropped in sizzles vigorously and rises at once.'
    technique: 'deep-fry'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Fry the fish. Lay it into the oil away from you to limit spatter and fry without moving it for 4 to 5 minutes, until the down side is deep golden and rigid. Slide two spatulas under it and turn once, carefully, then fry the second side 4 to 5 minutes more. Total fry is 8 to 10 minutes for a 600 g flounder; the skin should be uniformly crisp and the flesh in the scores opaque and flaking. Spoon hot oil over any thick part that sits above the oil line to crisp it.'
    technique: 'deep-fry'
    time_min: 9
  - text: 'Drain. Lift the fish out, let it drain a few seconds over the pot, then set it on a rack or paper towels for 1 minute. Resting on a rack rather than a flat plate keeps the bottom skin crisp; a fried fish left on a flat plate steams its own underside soft.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Build the platter. Spread the 120 g / ½ head shredded romaine across a large platter and scatter over the 80 g / 1 cup mung bean sprouts, the 20 g / 1 cup cilantro sprigs, the 15 g / ¾ cup mint leaves, and the 20 g / 2 stalks sliced scallion. Lay the hot fried fish on top of the herb bed so its residual heat lightly wilts the herbs underneath.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish and serve. Pour about a third of the nuoc cham over the fish so it runs into the crisp scores and seasons the flake, and pour the rest into small individual dipping bowls. Serve immediately with the cooked 300 g / 1½ cups jasmine rice. Each diner lifts crisp flesh and skin off the frame with a herb leaf, dips into the nuoc cham, and eats it over rice; the lean sweet fish, the cool herbs, and the bright salty-sour sauce are the whole point of the dish.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why a flatfish. The whole point of ca chien with flounder rather than a round fish is geometry. A flounder is a broad flat panel, so for a given weight it presents far more skin to the oil than a branzino or a snapper, and crisp skin is the prize. The thin body also fries through fast and evenly, and the lean white flesh under the crackling skin reads sweet and clean against the bright sauce. Fluke (summer flounder) is the closest interchangeable fish; any sole or plaice works the same way.

  Why dry, dry, dry. Three things crisp a fried fish: a dry surface, a thin starch coat, and hot enough oil. The dry-salt cure pulls surface moisture and firms the skin, the 30-minute uncovered air-dry in the refrigerator finishes the job, and the thin cornstarch-and-rice-flour dredge gives the crisp a scaffold without gumming. Skip the drying and the skin steams limp under its own water, and the wet surface throws dangerous spatter when it hits the oil. This is the single highest-leverage step in the recipe.

  Why cornstarch and rice flour, not wheat. Cornstarch and rice flour are pure or near-pure starch with little protein, so they fry to a glassy shatter rather than the bready, gluten-bound crust wheat flour gives, and they hold that crisp longer as the fish sits on the platter. The mix also keeps the whole plate gluten-free, which a wheat-flour dredge or a wheat-based soy sauce would break. Potato starch is the most shatter-crisp option of all and swaps in directly.

  Where the umami and acid live. The flounder itself is lean and mild, with almost no fat and only a faint natural sweetness, so it cannot carry the dish alone. Both the glutamate-rich umami and the finishing acid are supplied by the nuoc cham: the fish sauce brings the salt and the deep fermented umami, the lime brings the bright finishing acid, the sugar balances both, and the raw garlic and chili add the aromatic top. This is the canonical Vietnamese structure, a clean fried or grilled protein finished by a separate balanced sauce, rather than seasoning built into the protein. Taste the nuoc cham and adjust it to equal balance before it goes near the fish.

  Oil temperature and safety. Fry at 185 to 190 C. Too cool and the fish absorbs oil and turns greasy instead of crisp; too hot and the starch coat scorches before the dense flesh at the spine is cooked. A whole fish displaces a large volume of oil and can boil the pot over if it is more than half full, so use a wide vessel, keep a lid and a sheet pan nearby, lower the fish in away from your body, and never add a wet fish to hot oil. A thermometer is not optional here.

  The herb plate is the dish, not a garnish. In the Vietnamese frame the pile of cilantro, mint, bean sprouts, scallion, and shredded lettuce is a primary component eaten by the handful, not a decoration. Diners wrap crisp fish in a lettuce or herb leaf, dip it in nuoc cham, and eat it with rice; the cool raw herbs against the hot crisp fish and the bright sauce are the contrast the whole dish is built on. Serve generously and replenish.
