steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 230 °C / 450 °F with a heavy half-sheet pan inside on the middle rack. The pan needs to preheat for at least 10 minutes; the 900 g / 1 large head of cauliflower must hiss on contact when added. A cold pan plus cold florets means the system spends 15 minutes climbing to roasting temperature and the vegetable steams before it browns.'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Cut the 900 g / 1 large head cauliflower from the underside (where the stems hold the florets together) into 3 to 4 cm florets. Smaller pieces overcook before they brown; larger pieces stay raw at the center. Save any pale-yellow tender leaves; they crisp into something close to vegetable chips.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'In a wide bowl, toss the cauliflower with the 35 g / 2½ tbsp olive oil, the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt, and the 3 g / 1 tsp lightly crushed cumin seeds. Every floret should glisten; pooled oil at the bottom of the bowl means too much, drag the florets through it. The crushed (not powdered) cumin holds its aromatic punch through the roast; ground cumin scorches and turns acrid.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pull the preheated sheet pan out of the oven and tip the dressed 900 g / 1 head of cauliflower onto it in a single layer, cut faces down where possible. The florets should sizzle audibly. Spread the pieces so each has 2 to 3 cm of clear pan around it; if it does not fit, use a second preheated pan rather than crowding one. Crowded vegetables steam each other, the surface stays wet, and Maillard never starts.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Roast 25 to 30 minutes without disturbing for the first 18 to 20 minutes; the underside contact develops the deepest browning during this phase, and tossing too early breaks the seal. At 18 to 20 minutes, check the underside of one floret with tongs; it should be deep mahogany, almost burnt-looking. If it is still pale, push to 25 minutes. Then toss once with a spatula, scraping any browned bits from the pan, and return for 7 to 10 minutes more until the previously-up surfaces also have color and the florets are tender at a fork (the stems should yield with a small push).'
    technique: 'high-heat-dry-roasting'
    time_min: 28
  - text: 'While the cauliflower roasts, build the lemon-tahini emulsion. In a medium bowl, stir the 100 g / ½ cup tahini to integrate the oil layer, then whisk in 40 g / 2½ tbsp of the ice water all at once. The tahini will seize ; it tightens into a thick, putty-like mass that resists the whisk. This is correct. Continue whisking 20 to 30 seconds; the paste lightens in color (a sign the proteins are hydrating and gelling).'
    technique: 'tahini-emulsion'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, the 4 g / 1 clove microplaned garlic, and the 3 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt. Whisk vigorously for 30 to 45 seconds; the paste begins to relax noticeably. The acid here is non-negotiable for the emulsion; the lemon brings citric acid (which finishes the protein gel) and the garlic'' allicin reacts with sulfur compounds in the tahini to round out the flavor. Whisking through this stage incorporates air; the sauce starts to look fluffy.'
    technique: 'tahini-emulsion'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Stream in the remaining 35 g / 2½ tbsp ice water in 1 tbsp / 15 g additions, whisking between each, until the sauce reaches a pourable-creamy consistency that ribbons off the whisk and slowly settles back into the surface. Cold water (refrigerator-cold) makes a slightly thicker, fluffier sauce than room-temperature water; the colder water slows the protein hydration and gives the gel network time to organize before relaxing. Taste; the emulsion should read sesame-led, lemon-bright, garlic-present, and fully salted; if it tastes flat, more salt; if it tastes harsh, another splash of water.'
    technique: 'tahini-emulsion'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Toast the 40 g / ¼ cup pistachios in a dry small skillet over medium heat for 4 minutes, swirling, until fragrant and one shade darker. Cool on a plate; do not chop until cool or the residual heat steams the surrounding parsley.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'When the cauliflower is roasted, transfer it directly from the pan to a wide warm bowl. Do not let it sit on the pan; the residual heat overcooks the bottom side. While the cauliflower is still warm (not hot), drizzle two-thirds of the lemon-tahini emulsion (~140 g / ⅔ cup) over the florets and toss gently with tongs to coat every surface. Warm cauliflower picks up the dressing differently than cool cauliflower; the cellular surfaces are more porous and the tahini reads slightly into the florets rather than just sitting on top.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Turn the dressed cauliflower onto a wide platter or shallow bowl. Drizzle the remaining tahini emulsion (~70 g / ⅓ cup) in zigzag stripes over the top; the visible white-tan ribbons are part of the dish'' visual structure, not just a flavor delivery. Scatter the 90 g / ½ cup pomegranate seeds, the 25 g / 1 medium bunch chopped parsley, and the cooled chopped pistachios across the surface in that order; layering gives the eye a path through the dish.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Finish with a thin drizzle of the 10 g / ½ tbsp pomegranate molasses (zigzag stripes again, sparingly ; the molasses is a structural acid, not a sauce), the 10 g / 2 tsp finishing olive oil, and a sprinkle of the 2 g / 1 tsp urfa biber across the top. The urfa biber should land visibly as dark-burgundy flakes against the pale tahini; the cocoa-raisin profile of the cured chili reads naturally against the brassica sweetness. Serve warm or at room temperature within 30 minutes; the cauliflower stays in the right textural register for that window.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why 230 °C and not 200 °C. Cauliflower is the brassica that rewards the highest practical home-oven temperature most clearly. At 200 °C / 390 °F, the florets cook through tender but the surface never browns past pale gold; the flavor stays in vegetal-sweet territory and the salad reads as roasted-cauliflower-and-tahini, fine but not striking. At 230 °C / 450 °F, the cut faces hit Maillard temperature within 8 to 10 minutes of pan contact, the natural sugars caramelize, and the florets develop a deep mahogany browning that reads almost meaty. The same dish at the lower temperature is a different dish; the high heat is the entire flavor argument.

  The umami source is the cauliflower itself (the dry-roasted brassica generates substantial free glutamate during the cook, plus the Maillard browning produces additional savory compounds) and the tahini (sesame is glutamate-rich); the finishing acid is the lemon in the tahini emulsion plus the pomegranate molasses drizzle plus the inherent citric-and-malic acidity in the pomegranate seeds. Three acid sources at three different concentrations, layered through the dish.

  Why warm-not-hot for dressing the cauliflower. Hot cauliflower (straight off the pan) seizes the tahini emulsion in a way the cook does not want; the heat denatures the protein gel and the dressing reads grainy. Warm cauliflower (rested 5 minutes off the pan) is the right temperature; the surfaces are porous, the dressing absorbs slightly, and the emulsion stays creamy. Cold cauliflower (made ahead and refrigerated) reads as a different dish; the dressing sits on top rather than into the florets and the brassica sweetness flattens. The warm window is 5 to 30 minutes off the pan.

  The salad keeps poorly. Tahini-dressed cauliflower at room temperature for more than 90 minutes degrades visibly; the cauliflower softens, the tahini absorbs into the florets, and the parsley wilts against the warm vegetables. Plate within 30 minutes of dressing; if making components ahead, hold the cauliflower and the tahini emulsion separately and combine immediately before serving.
