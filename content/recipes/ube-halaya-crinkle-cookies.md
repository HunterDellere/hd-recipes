before_you_start:
  - 'The dough needs at least 4 hours of fridge chill before scooping, ideally overnight. Halaya carries water, and the dough is wetter than a standard cookie dough straight from the mixer; without the chill the cookies spread flat and lose the cracked crinkle top. Plan to mix the dough the night before or first thing in the morning.'
  - 'Pull the eggs and butter to room temperature 30 minutes before starting. Cold eggs slow the sugar-egg whip and prevent the mixture from reaching the pale ribbon stage that builds the structure for the crinkle. Cold butter resists creaming with sugar and produces lumps that never fully homogenize.'
  - 'Use jarred Good Shepherd ube halaya if you can. Fluorescent-purple jars (Tropics or store brand) have added blue and red dye and will bake to a fluorescent crinkle instead of the muted, jam-rich violet you want. The dye is also pH-sensitive in a different way than natural anthocyanin and tends to read more blue against the white sugar dredge.'

steps:
  - text: 'In a stand mixer with the paddle, cream 90 g / 6 tbsp softened unsalted butter, 110 g / ½ cup + 1 tbsp granulated sugar, and 60 g / ¼ cup + 1 tbsp packed light brown sugar on medium for 3 minutes until pale, fluffy, and visibly aerated. Scrape down twice. The creaming step beats microscopic air pockets into the butter that later expand in the oven; without it the cookies set dense.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add 130 g / scant ⅔ cup ube halaya to the creamed butter and mix on low until fully combined, about 45 seconds. The mixture turns from pale yellow to muted lavender. Scrape down. Add 2 room-temperature large eggs one at a time, mixing on low for 20 seconds after each, then 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste and (optional) 3 ml / ½ tsp ube extract. Mix until uniform.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'In a separate bowl, whisk together 220 g / 1¾ cups + 1 tbsp all-purpose flour, 18 g / 2 tbsp cornstarch, 5 g / 1¼ tsp baking powder, and 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt. The cornstarch is the tenderness lever here: it cuts the gluten-forming protein percentage of the flour mix by about 8% without lowering total dry weight, which produces a noticeably more tender bite against the jam moisture.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the dry mixture to the wet in two additions, mixing on low just until no flour streaks remain. Do not overmix; the dough should look shaggy and slightly tacky. Scrape into a covered container or wrap with plastic. Refrigerate at least 4 hours, ideally overnight. The chill is non-negotiable: warm halaya-laden dough spreads flat in the oven and the crinkle never forms.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Heat oven to 175 °C / 350 °F. Line two sheet pans with parchment. Combine 60 g / ¼ cup + 1 tbsp granulated sugar in one wide bowl and 80 g / ⅔ cup powdered sugar in another wide bowl. Scoop the chilled dough into 28 g / 1.5-tbsp balls (a small ice cream scoop is the cleanest tool). Roll each ball lightly in the granulated sugar to coat the surface, then drop into the powdered sugar and coat heavily, pressing the powder onto the surface so it stays put through the bake. Arrange 8 cookies per pan with 5 cm / 2 in between them; they spread to about 6 cm / 2½ in across.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Bake one pan at a time on the center rack for 10 to 11 minutes. The cookies are done when the edges are set, the tops have cracked open into the canonical crinkle pattern, and the centers still look slightly soft. The interior should read jam-dark violet through the cracks, with the 80 g / ⅔ cup dredge of powdered sugar staying snow-white on the unbroken surfaces. Cool on the pan for 5 minutes (centers finish setting), then transfer to a rack.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Store at room temperature in an airtight container with a piece of parchment between layers (the sugar dredge smudges if cookies touch). They are best on day 1 when the contrast between crisp edge and soft jam-rich center is at its widest; on day 3 the cornstarch has fully equilibrated moisture and the cookies are still excellent but uniformly chewy throughout. Five days maximum before they go from chewy to leathery.'
    time_min: 0

notes: |
  Why halaya in cookies, structurally. Halaya brings four things at once that a normal cookie dough builds from separate ingredients: violet color, sweet jam flavor, fat (from butter and condensed milk in the halaya), and starch (from the cooked yam). Treating it as a replacement for melted chocolate in a chocolate crinkle is the cleanest mental model. The chocolate brings color, flavor, fat, and a small amount of cocoa solids that act as starch in the dough; halaya does the same with violet, yam, dairy, and yam starch. The dough behaves remarkably similarly, which is why the crinkle protocol transfers cleanly.

  Why cornstarch is non-negotiable here. Halaya is 30 to 40% water by weight (mostly in the cooked yam and the evaporated milk). That moisture, against a normal all-purpose flour dough, develops more gluten than you want for a crinkle, and the cookies bake up dense and slightly chewy-tough. Cornstarch is pure starch with zero gluten-forming protein; substituting 2 tbsp of it for some of the flour reduces effective protein percentage and produces a noticeably more tender bite. Skip it and you have a fine cookie but a structurally tougher one than the chocolate version.

  Why the double sugar dredge. The granulated layer is a moisture barrier; the powdered sugar above it stays white and snowy as the cookie spreads and cracks open. Straight powdered sugar on the bare dough absorbs surface moisture and goes patchy, giving you a wet-spotted top instead of clean snowdrift contrast. The granulated layer also adds a thin sugar shell on the cookie surface that crackles slightly under the teeth, which doubles down on the crinkle aesthetic with a textural bite.

  Why optional ube extract. Anthocyanin is heat-sensitive: pigment degradation begins at 80 °C and accelerates above 100 °C. A 175 °C oven for 11 minutes fades color by roughly 15 to 20% from the dough state. The natural color from good halaya is still pretty after the bake, but if you want the violet to read closer to the dough color, 3 ml of ube extract (which is dye-based and heat-stable) restores most of the lost intensity. The flavor change is negligible at that dose. Skip it if you want an honest color report from the halaya alone; add it if the cookies are going on a plate next to non-ube things and you want the violet to compete.
