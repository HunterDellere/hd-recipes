steps:
  - text: 'The night before. Place 250 g jameed stones in a non-reactive bowl with 1500 g (6 cups) cold water. Cover and refrigerate 12–24 hours. The stones soften from the outside in and dissolve into a chalky off-white slurry; this is correct. The slow soak rehydrates the protein gradually so it integrates cleanly into the sauce later. A hot reduction (90 minutes simmer with crushing) is acceptable when time is short, but the cold soak gives a smoother sauce.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Day of cooking. Rinse the 1500 g lamb shoulder pieces under cold water. Place in a heavy stockpot, cover with 3000 g (12 cups) cold water, and set over medium heat. Critically, cold-water start: a hot start seals myoglobin and trace blood into the surface and gives a cloudy broth. The cold start lets the proteins coagulate gradually and rise as scum.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Bring to a bare simmer (about 88 °C, when the surface ripples and breaks lazily). For the first 20 minutes, skim aggressively with a wide spoon. The grey-tan foam carries the off-flavors that ruin a yogurt-based sauce later. Skim until the foam stops forming and the surface clears.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Once the broth runs clear, add the 250 g halved yellow onion, 3 g (1) cinnamon stick, 3 g cracked cardamom pods, 1 g (3) bay leaves, 4 g whole black peppercorns, and 0.5 g mastic if using. Add 8 g kosher salt for the braise. The salt seasons the lamb and the broth in the same motion; jameed will bring more salt later, so this dose is deliberately moderate.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Hold at 88–92 °C for 2.5 to 3 hours, partly covered, until a fork slides into the lamb with no resistance and the bones begin to release from the meat. The sustained 80–95 °C window is what hydrolyzes the connective-tissue collagen into gelatin, which is why the cut reads tender rather than chalky. Carefully transfer the lamb pieces to a covered tray and reserve.'
    time_min: 165
    technique: 'cooked-yogurt-sauce'
  - text: 'Strain the lamb broth through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean wide pot. You should have roughly 2.2 L of clear amber broth. Reduce uncovered over medium heat to about 1.5 L, 15 minutes, to concentrate the gelatin and the lamb flavor before the jameed goes in.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Blend the soaked jameed slurry smooth with an immersion blender, then pass through a fine-mesh sieve into a bowl, pressing the solids to extract maximum liquid. Discard any grit caught in the sieve. You should have about 1.5 L of pasty white-tan jameed liquid. Whisk 12 g cornstarch into 60 g cold water to a smooth slurry, then whisk that into the jameed liquid. The cornstarch is non-negotiable: even though jameed casein is more heat-tolerant than fresh yogurt, a 30-minute simmer without a starch matrix will still produce surface curdling.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Temper the jameed liquid before it goes into the hot reduced broth. With the broth at a gentle tremor (88 °C), ladle 250 ml hot broth into the jameed bowl, whisking constantly. Add another 250 ml the same way. The jameed liquid should now be hot to the touch and roughly doubled in volume; this thermal-bridge step is what prevents the casein from shocking on contact.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Pour the warmed jameed back into the stockpot with the rest of the broth in a slow stream, stirring continuously with a wooden spoon (one direction throughout, no reversing — the regional rule has no chemistry behind direction itself, but the no-reverse rule prevents shear zones where curdling nucleates). Bring slowly back to a tremor (~90 °C); the surface should bubble lazily but never roll. Hold 20 minutes, stirring frequently. The sauce should coat the back of a spoon thinly. Taste; the jameed contributes most of the salt, so adjust only after this hold.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Return the reserved lamb pieces gently to the sauce. Hold at 85–90 °C while you finish the rice; if the sauce thickens past coating consistency, splash in 100 g hot water at a time. Never let it boil; jameed sauce held above 95 °C breaks even with the cornstarch in place.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'For the rice: rinse the 450 g basmati in a fine-mesh sieve under cold water until the water runs nearly clear (3–4 changes), then cover with cool water and soak 30 minutes. The soak hydrates the grain core in advance so the cooking phase does not have to overshoot the surface to get the center done. Drain thoroughly.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'In a heavy 4-quart saucepan, melt 60 g samna over medium heat. Add 3 g ground turmeric and bloom 30 seconds; fat-soluble turmeric pigments and curcumin extract into the samna. Add the drained rice and toast 60–90 seconds, stirring gently to coat every grain in the spiced fat. The toast gelatinizes the surface starch and locks the grains separate during cooking.'
    time_min: 3
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
  - text: 'Pour in 750 g hot water and 6 g kosher salt. Bring to a boil, then immediately drop to the lowest heat, cover with a tight lid, and cook 15 minutes without lifting the lid. Pull off heat, leave covered, rest 10 more minutes. Fluff gently with a fork; the grains should have elongated to roughly 1.7× their dry length and stay separate.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'For the garnish: melt 30 g samna in a small skillet over medium heat. Add 40 g pine nuts and 40 g slivered almonds together, swirl constantly, and toast until the pine nuts are deep gold and the almonds are amber-edged, 90 seconds to 2 minutes. The window from gold to burnt is roughly 15 seconds; do not look away. Tip immediately onto a plate to halt the carryover.'
    time_min: 3
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
  - text: 'Assembly. On a wide shallow platter, tear the 200 g shrak bread into rough hand-sized squares and lay them as a single overlapping base layer. Ladle 200 g of the jameed sauce evenly over the bread to soak it; the bread should darken and soften without disintegrating. Mound the turmeric rice over the soaked bread in a low dome.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Arrange the lamb pieces on top of the rice, bone-side up where possible for visual cue. Spoon another 150 g of jameed sauce over the lamb to glaze. Scatter all 80 g of toasted nuts over the surface. Sprinkle the 10 g chopped parsley as a green note. Pour the remaining jameed sauce into a warmed serving bowl and pass at the table; diners ladle additional sauce over their portion to taste. Serve immediately.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'The Adjustment: if the jameed sauce reads too sour or too aggressive, the soaked stones held more salt and acid than the bag suggested; thin with 100 g hot lamb broth or hot water at a time. If the sauce reads thin or pale, you reduced the lamb broth too little before the jameed went in; reduce a future batch closer to 1.3 L. If the rice grains clumped and read soft, the soak was skipped or the toast step was too brief; both lock the grain separation. If the sauce broke into curds, the simmer crossed 95 °C or the temper was too fast; an immersion blender will recover most of it off heat, though the texture stays faintly grainy.'

notes: |
  Why no browning step on the lamb. The Western reflex is to sear the meat for Maillard depth, but mansaf is built on a clear, light-bodied broth and the jameed itself supplies the deep umami. Browning the lamb fouls the broth (cloudy and heavy) and competes with the jameed funk on the palate. The bare-simmer cold-water-start technique is doing what searing does in other braises, just through a different chemistry: gradual protein coagulation, gelatin extraction, and aggressive scum removal.

  Why the cold-water start matters for a yogurt-based dish more than for other braises. Jameed sauce is unforgiving of off-flavors. Trace blood, denatured surface proteins, and anything that would normally hide under a Maillard sauce reads obvious in a clear yogurt-bathed dish. Skim until the surface is clean, then add aromatics; the order matters because aromatics added too early float in the scum and are trapped when you skim.

  Why jameed and not yogurt. Read the jameed page. The short version: the long air-ferment hydrolyzes casein into smaller peptides and frees glutamate, which is why a small mass of jameed delivers more umami and more stable heat behavior than any quantity of fresh yogurt. The dish is a different dish without it. The yogurt-and-cornstarch version in modifications is a workable last resort, not an equivalence.

  Why turmeric instead of saffron. Saffron is the Persian rice fat-extraction move; the Levantine and Bedouin tradition uses turmeric, which is cheaper, available everywhere in the region, and reads as a different yellow on the plate (more amber-orange than the rosy gold of saffron). Some commercial mansaf restaurants in Amman use both. The turmeric bloom in samna is the technique; saffron would need to be steeped separately in hot (not boiling) water and added to the rice cooking liquid.

  Why the rice goes under the lamb instead of beside it. Mansaf is built as a single shared platter, eaten by all diners scooping with the right hand from one mound, in the older Bedouin manner. The architecture is bread-then-rice-then-lamb because that is the order in which a hand reaches: the lamb on top is what the eater pulls first; the rice underneath catches what falls; the bread at the bottom soaks the sauce that drips through. Plating it differently (lamb beside rice, sauce as a side) misses the structural logic of the dish.

  Why you stir in one direction. There is no chemistry to stirring direction itself, but reversing direction creates shear zones where the casein can curdle locally. The cooks who pass the no-reverse rule down are giving a practical instruction wrapped in tradition. Use a wooden spoon flat-bladed against the pot floor and walls; whisks aerate and produce surface foam that makes it harder to read the simmer.

  On scaling. Mansaf scales linearly up to roughly 3 kg of lamb (10–12 servings) using the same 6:1 jameed-to-water soak ratio. Past that, multiple platters are easier to assemble than one heroic platter, and the jameed sauce becomes harder to keep at a tremor in a single pot. For a smaller cook (3 servings), halve everything except the cornstarch, which scales at 80% (because the surface-area-to-volume ratio of a smaller pot reduces edge curdling risk).
