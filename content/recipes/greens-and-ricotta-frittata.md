title: 'Greens and ricotta frittata'
desc: 'The Italian open-faced baked omelet: wilted greens (lacinato kale, chard, spinach, or a mix) folded into beaten eggs with whole-milk ricotta, parmigiano, lemon zest, and aggressive black pepper, started in an oven-safe skillet on the stove and finished in the oven until just set with a custardy center. Structurally distinct from a frittata that is fully cooked on the stove (which produces a denser drier texture); the oven-finished version sets evenly across the depth and produces the canonical jiggly center. Eaten warm or at room temperature, sliced into wedges or squares; the structural Italian make-ahead egg dish and one of the great flexible weeknight dinners.'

source:
  name: 'The Italian regional canon. Documented across Italian regional cookbooks; frittata di verdure (vegetable frittata) is structurally an everyday Italian home dish across the peninsula, with regional variations using whatever greens or vegetables are in season. The dish predates documentation and is structurally a peasant preparation (a way to extend a small number of eggs across a meal for many people with whatever vegetables and cheeses are at hand).'
  note: 'Two structural schools: the fully-stove-cooked frittata (cooked on the stove from start to finish, flipped onto a plate and slid back into the pan to finish the second side, the older preparation) and the oven-finished frittata (started on the stove to set the bottom, then transferred to the oven to set the top, the modern preparation that produces a more uniformly cooked texture). The version here is the oven-finished form, which is structurally more reliable for home cooks and produces a better texture for a thick frittata with vegetables and cheese. Distinct from a quiche (which has a pastry crust and a more cream-heavy egg base) and from a Spanish tortilla (which has potato as the structural primary ingredient and uses a plate-flip rather than an oven finish).'

before_you_start:
  - 'Preheat the oven to 200 °C / 400 °F with the rack in the upper third before starting the greens; the pan moves from stovetop to oven mid-cook and a not-yet-hot oven means the top sets unevenly. The preheat takes about as long as the onion sweat and greens wilt.'
  - 'Pull the eggs to room temperature 30 minutes ahead. Cold eggs slow the bottom set when they hit the warm pan and produce a streaky cook line; room-temperature eggs flow evenly and set as one cohesive layer.'
  - 'Beat the egg-and-cheese base while the greens cool in the pan. The greens need 2 minutes off heat after the wilt so they do not scramble the eggs on contact; that window is exactly enough time to whisk the eggs, fold in the ricotta, parmigiano, lemon zest, salt, and pepper.'
  - 'Confirm the skillet is oven-safe before starting. Cast iron, well-seasoned carbon steel, and oven-safe non-stick all work; plastic-handled pans will fail in the oven and there is no recovery once the eggs are in.'

steps:
  - text: 'Preheat the oven to 200°C / 400°F. Set the rack in the upper third of the oven (the higher position browns the top of the frittata; a middle rack works but reads less developed on top).'
    time_min: 0
  - text: 'Heat the 25 g / 2 tbsp olive oil in a 25 to 28 cm (10 to 11 inch) oven-safe heavy skillet (cast iron, well-seasoned carbon steel, or oven-safe nonstick) over medium heat. Add the 100 g / ½ onion (diced); sweat 4 to 5 minutes, stirring occasionally, until translucent and lightly golden. Add the 8 g / 2 garlic cloves (minced) and 1 g / ¼ tsp red pepper flakes; cook 30 seconds until fragrant.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the 300 g / 1 bunch kale (torn) and the 2 g / ¼ tsp salt to the pan in 2 or 3 additions, stirring after each to wilt before adding the next (a full bunch of kale fills the pan above the rim before wilting; staged additions prevent steam buildup). Cook 4 to 6 minutes, stirring, until the kale is fully wilted, tender, and most of the water has evaporated (the pan should be visibly dry at the bottom with no pooled liquid). Remove from heat and let cool 2 minutes; the eggs added to a too-hot pan will scramble on contact with the pan and produce a streaky frittata.'
    technique: 'greens-wilt-and-dry'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'While the greens cool, prepare the egg-and-cheese base: in a large bowl, beat the 400 g / 8 eggs with a fork or whisk for 30 seconds until just uniform (no streaks of white). Add the 150 g / ⅔ cup ricotta, 40 g / ½ cup parmigiano, 3 g / 1 tsp lemon zest, 4 g / ¾ tsp salt, and 2 g / 1 tsp cracked black pepper; whisk to combine; the ricotta should integrate but small soft lumps are acceptable and structural (they remain visible in the finished frittata as soft white pockets).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pour the egg-and-cheese mixture (the 400 g / 8 eggs base) over the warm (not hot) greens in the skillet; use a wooden spoon or rubber spatula to gently fold the greens into the eggs so the vegetables distribute uniformly through the depth of the pan. Return the pan to medium-low heat for 2 to 3 minutes, without stirring, until the bottom of the frittata has visibly set (a thin layer of cooked egg around the edge of the pan; the top is still completely liquid). This stove-set step is structural; it prevents the bottom from undercooking in the oven and produces a clean release from the pan when slicing.'
    technique: 'stove-set-then-oven'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Transfer the skillet directly to the preheated oven. Bake 10 to 12 minutes, checking at 10 minutes, until the top of the 400 g / 8-egg frittata is set (no visible liquid; a gentle nudge of the pan shows the top moving as a single cohesive layer rather than wobbling like uncooked egg), the edges are puffed and golden, and an instant-read thermometer in the center reads 75 to 78°C / 165 to 170°F. The center should still have a slight wobble when the pan is gently shaken; a fully rigid center is overcooked.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Remove the skillet from the oven; let rest 5 minutes on a heatproof surface. The frittata continues to set during the rest and the slicing is cleaner once the 400 g / 8-egg matrix has firmed slightly. Run a thin spatula around the edges to release; slide the frittata onto a serving plate or cutting board (the well-seasoned cast iron or oven-safe nonstick releases cleanly; if it sticks, the pan was not well-greased before adding the egg mixture and a small spatula slide works around the edges).'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Slice into 6 wedges (or 12 small squares for tapas-style service); drizzle each slice with a small amount of the 10 g / 2 tsp finishing olive oil and a small sprinkle of the 1 g / ¼ tsp flaky sea salt. Serve warm (immediately after cooking, with the center still slightly trembling), at room temperature (the canonical Italian make-ahead preparation; lets the flavors integrate), or cold straight from the refrigerator (a canonical Italian picnic food). Pairs with a green salad, crusty bread, and a glass of dry white wine for a complete light dinner; or cut into smaller pieces and served as part of an antipasti or aperitivo spread.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The greens wilt is structural and the most common point of failure. Greens carry a significant amount of water (kale is about 84 percent water; spinach is 92 percent water); if the greens are not fully wilted and the pan is not visibly dry before the eggs are added, the residual water leaches into the eggs during cooking and produces a watery, weeping frittata with a soggy bottom layer. Cook the greens until the pan is visibly dry at the bottom (a tip of a spoon dragged across the pan should leave a clear track, not a wet smear); this can take 4 to 6 minutes with fresh kale and longer if the greens are particularly wet. Frozen spinach must be aggressively squeezed dry in a clean tea towel before use; even thawed spinach holds enough water to ruin a frittata if not pre-dried.

  The stove-set-then-oven technique is structural and is the difference between a reliable home frittata and a Russian roulette of overcooked edges and undercooked center. The 2-to-3-minute stove-set sets the bottom and the lower edges (preventing them from staying liquid or undercooking in the oven), while the 10-to-12-minute oven cook sets the top and the center evenly across the depth of the pan. A frittata cooked only on the stove (the classical fully-stove method) requires a plate-flip and produces a denser, drier texture; a frittata cooked only in the oven sets unevenly (the bottom undercooks while the top overbakes). The hybrid stove-set-then-oven is the modern reliable technique.

  The temperature target matters. A frittata is done when the center reads 75 to 78°C / 165 to 170°F on an instant-read thermometer (above 80°C the eggs over-coagulate and squeeze water out, producing a rubbery texture). The visual cue (a slight wobble when the pan is gently shaken) is the home-cook reference; the thermometer is the precision check. The 12-minute oven time is the upper end of the range; check at 10 minutes and pull early if the surface looks set.

  Versatility. A frittata is one of the most structurally adaptable egg dishes in any cuisine; the basic architecture (eggs plus dairy plus seasoning plus filling) accommodates almost any vegetable, protein, or cheese combination. Use the same technique to make: any seasonal vegetable frittata (asparagus and goat cheese in spring; tomato and basil in summer; mushroom and gruyère in autumn; roasted root vegetables and feta in winter); a frittata with leftover roasted vegetables (the canonical way to use leftover roast chicken vegetables); a frittata with leftover pasta (the Italian frittata di pasta or frittata di maccheroni, a Roman tradition where cold pasta is folded into the eggs and cooked the same way); a frittata as the structural component of an antipasti spread (cut into small squares); a frittata as a picnic food (sliced cold and packed in a tin); a frittata as a make-ahead breakfast (sliced and reheated in the oven 5 minutes for individual servings throughout the week); a frittata sandwich (a thick wedge between two slices of bread with a smear of pesto or harissa); and a frittata as a brunch centerpiece (with a green salad, crusty bread, and a glass of wine for a complete meal).

  Pairs across Italian (canonical), Italian-American, broader Mediterranean (the Spanish tortilla is structurally adjacent; the Iranian kuku sabzi shares architectural DNA; the French quiche is the pastry-crust cousin), and contemporary American Italian-influenced kitchens.

  Storage. Keeps 3 days refrigerated, wrapped tightly; the flavor improves over the first 24 hours as the eggs, dairy, and vegetables integrate further. Eat cold straight from the refrigerator (the canonical Italian picnic preparation) or reheat in a 150°C / 300°F oven for 5 to 8 minutes (microwave reheating turns the eggs rubbery). Freezing is acceptable for up to 1 month (wrap individual slices tightly); thaw overnight in the refrigerator and reheat in the oven. The make-ahead capability is one of the structural strengths of the dish.
