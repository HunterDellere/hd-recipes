before_you_start:
  - 'Start the ñora pepper soak first. 20 minutes in hot water before anything else; the dried peppers must rehydrate fully so the flesh scrapes cleanly off the skin. Reserve the soaking liquid, it is the structural thinner for the sauce.'
  - 'The finished sauce is best after a 2-hour rest at room temperature, so the smoked paprika, sherry vinegar, and toasted nut oils integrate. Plan to make it at least that far ahead of the meal; same-day morning for a dinner is ideal.'
  - 'Source dried ñora peppers (Spanish or Mediterranean grocery, or online). The roasted red bell pepper substitute works but the sauce reads markedly more vegetal and less sweet; if substituting, lean harder on smoked paprika.'

source:
  name: 'The Catalan canon (Tarragona and broader Catalonia, northeastern Spain). Documented as the canonical sauce for the calçotada (winter grilled spring onion festival) and the romescada (Catalan fish stew); stabilized in the modern form (toasted nuts, ñora peppers, charred tomato, garlic, sherry vinegar, smoked paprika, olive oil) in the 19th and early 20th centuries.'
  note: 'Two structural variants: the calçotada romesco is thicker and more concentrated (a dipping consistency for the grilled spring onions); the broader Catalan romesco is slightly thinner and more pourable (a sauce consistency for fish and grilled vegetables). The version here is the medium-body all-purpose form. Distinct from salbitxada (a Catalan cousin with more parsley and less smoked paprika) and from xató (a sauce-and-salad with the same logic but more anchovy and chili). All three are romesco family.'

steps:
  - text: 'Soak the 15 g / 2 to 3 dried ñora peppers in 200 ml hot water for 20 minutes until softened. Scrape the flesh from the skin with the back of a spoon, discarding the skin (the skin reads tough and does not break down in the processor). Reserve 60 ml of the soaking liquid; the soaking water is sweet and pepper-flavored, structural for thinning the sauce. If using a roasted red bell pepper instead, see substitutions.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'In a dry medium skillet over medium heat, toast the 50 g / ⅓ cup almonds 4 to 5 minutes, shaking the pan continuously, until deeply golden and fragrant; the toast should go past pale and into a noticeable brown. Transfer to a plate. Add the 30 g / ¼ cup hazelnuts to the same dry pan and toast 5 to 6 minutes until the skins blister and crack. Pour the hazelnuts into a clean tea towel, fold over, and rub vigorously 30 seconds to remove the skins (most skins will release; perfect skin removal is not required). The deep nut toast is the structural flavor base of romesco; pale nuts produce a flat sauce.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Char the 100 g / 1 medium tomato directly over a gas flame on a low burner, turning with tongs, until the skin is fully blackened and blistered (4 to 5 minutes). Alternatively, broil 4 inches from the broiler element until the skin chars (5 to 6 minutes). Cool 2 minutes, peel off the blackened skin, and roughly chop. In the same dry skillet, dry-roast the 8 g / 2 garlic cloves over medium heat 5 minutes, turning, until the skins are golden and the cloves are soft; cool and mash with the back of a knife. The char-and-roast pair builds the smoky-sweet aromatic depth that defines romesco.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'In the bowl of a food processor (4 to 7 cup capacity), combine the toasted almonds and hazelnuts; pulse 10 to 12 times to a coarse-medium grind (the texture should be like coarse breadcrumbs, not a paste; some structural texture is canonical to romesco). Add the ñora flesh (or roasted red pepper), charred tomato, mashed roasted garlic, 4 g / 2 tsp smoked paprika, and 25 g / 5 tsp sherry vinegar; pulse 8 to 10 more times to combine into a coarse paste, scraping the sides between pulses.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'With the processor running on continuous low, drizzle in the 100 g / ½ cup olive oil through the feed tube in a steady stream, then add 30 to 60 ml of the reserved ñora soaking liquid (or warm water if using bell pepper) to thin to a thick spoonable consistency, somewhere between a dip and a pourable sauce. Add the 4 g / ¾ tsp salt, pulse to combine, taste and adjust: romesco should read deeply nutty with smoky paprika, sweet pepper-and-tomato, and a clean sherry-vinegar lift; if flat, more salt and vinegar; if sharp, more oil; if too thick, more soaking liquid. Transfer to a serving bowl; the sauce can be served immediately but improves significantly with 2 hours rest at room temperature.'
    time_min: 3

substitutions:
  - for: 'dried ñora peppers'
    use: '1 medium roasted red bell pepper (jarred is acceptable)'
    note: 'Roast a fresh red bell pepper directly over a gas flame or under a broiler until the skin is fully blackened (5 to 7 minutes); cool, peel, seed. Or use 1 medium jarred roasted red pepper (about 100 g drained), patted dry. The bell pepper version reads slightly more vegetal and less sweet than the ñora; the smoked paprika is more important in the bell pepper version because the pepper itself is less complex. Skip the soaking liquid; thin with warm water instead.'
  - for: 'sherry vinegar'
    use: 'red wine vinegar or aged balsamic'
    note: 'Sherry vinegar is the canonical Catalan choice and reads more elegant than red wine vinegar; red wine vinegar is the practical substitute and the standard supermarket alternative. Aged balsamic reads sweeter and is acceptable but pulls the sauce in a less Catalan direction.'
  - for: 'fresh charred tomato'
    use: '30 g good-quality tomato paste'
    note: 'When fresh tomatoes are out of season (winter), substitute 30 g of double-concentrated tomato paste. Brown the paste in a dry pan with a drop of olive oil for 1 minute over medium heat to deepen the flavor; the result reads slightly more umami-concentrated and less fresh, useful in winter when ripe tomatoes are unavailable.'

notes: |
  Romesco occupies a unique position in the global sauce repertoire as a structurally nut-thickened sauce in the same family as Indian curry pastes, West African groundnut sauces, Mexican mole, and Georgian satsivi. The nut grind is the body, the pepper and tomato are the flavor, the vinegar is the lift, the olive oil is the carrier. The Catalan version is unique in pairing toasted nuts with smoked paprika and sherry vinegar; the smoke-and-vinegar combination is the structural signature that distinguishes romesco from any other nut-thickened sauce in the world.

  The toast depth on the nuts matters more than any other variable. Pale toasted nuts produce a flat, slightly raw-tasting sauce; deeply toasted (golden-brown, aggressively fragrant) nuts produce the characteristic Catalan depth. The toast goes from pale to fragrant to too-dark in about 90 seconds at the end; pull the moment the nuts smell aggressively roasted, before any visible burning. Burned nuts ruin the sauce and there is no rescue; toast carefully but commit to the depth.

  Versatility. Romesco is one of the most cuisine-flexible sauces in the Mediterranean pantry. Use it as: the canonical dip for calçots and grilled spring onions (the Catalan calçotada pairing), grilled or roasted leeks, asparagus, fennel, broccoli, cauliflower, eggplant, zucchini, charred carrots; a sauce for grilled fish (especially salmon, halibut, branzino, monkfish; the romescada Catalan fish stew tradition), grilled shrimp and shellfish; a sauce for roast chicken and roast pork; a sauce for sliced grilled steak and lamb; a spread for grilled bread (the canonical Catalan pa amb tomàquet upgrade); a stir-in for fried or scrambled eggs (a tablespoon stirred into the eggs at the end of cooking); a drizzle for poached eggs and shakshuka; a dressing thinned with more olive oil and water for grain bowls and farro salads; a finish for white-bean stews and chickpea braises (a spoonful stirred in at service); and as a sandwich spread for grilled vegetable, roast chicken, and lamb sandwiches.

  Pairs across Catalan, broader Spanish, Mediterranean, southern French, North African, contemporary American grill cookery, and modern Israeli (the nut-and-pepper logic also runs through muhammara, the Syrian and Turkish red-pepper-and-walnut sauce; treat romesco and muhammara as cousins).

  Storage. Keeps 5 days refrigerated in a sealed jar with a thin film of olive oil over the surface (the oil seal prevents oxidation; the sauce darkens visibly without it). Bring to room temperature 30 minutes before service; the cold sauce reads dull, the room-temperature sauce reads vibrant. The flavor peaks at day 2 to 3 (the nuts and peppers fully integrate); after day 5 the freshness fades and the sauce reads slightly stale.
