  - 'Warm the milk to about 35 C with a thermometer. Above 40 C kills the yeast; below 30 C the dough takes much longer to rise. Whisk the honey into the warm milk first so it disperses evenly and the yeast can reach it.'
  - 'Mix the garlic butter brush at the start of the bulk rise so the pressed raw garlic has an hour to mellow in the butter. Cold-mixed garlic butter brushed on a hot breadstick reads sharp and harsh.'
  - 'Honey browns faster than cane sugar because its fructose caramelizes at a lower temperature. Watch the crust from minute 10 and pull the breadsticks the moment the tops are golden; they can go from golden to too-dark quickly.'

steps:
  - text: 'Build the dough. Whisk the 21 g / 1 tbsp honey and the 14 g / 2 tsp barley malt syrup into the 240 ml warmed (35 °C) milk until dissolved, then whisk in the 7 g / 2¼ tsp instant yeast and let stand 5 minutes to hydrate and disperse (instant yeast is designed to go in dry and will not always foam in 5 minutes, so do not read a quiet surface as dead yeast; if you want a live-yeast check, use active dry yeast, which does foam, see the substitution below). In a stand mixer bowl, add the 460 g / 3½ cups bread flour, the 30 g / 2 tbsp melted-and-cooled butter, and the 8 g / 1¼ tsp fine sea salt, then pour in the milk mixture. Fit the dough hook; mix on low 2 minutes until shaggy, then medium 8 minutes until smooth, elastic, and pulling cleanly from the bowl. By hand: combine everything in a large bowl, turn out, and knead 10 to 12 minutes until smooth and elastic. The dough will feel slightly stickier than a cane-sugar dough because honey is hygroscopic and holds more water; resist adding flour beyond a light dusting.'
    technique: 'enriched-dough'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'First rise (bulk fermentation). Transfer the dough to a lightly oiled bowl and cover with a damp tea towel. Set in a warm spot (24 to 26 °C) and rise 60 to 90 minutes until roughly doubled. This dough may rise a touch faster than a cane-sugar dough because the invert sugars from the 21 g / 1 tbsp honey are immediately available to the yeast. The dough is correctly proofed when a finger-poke leaves a partial dent that springs back slowly; a dent that fills instantly means underproofed, a dent that holds without springing means overproofed (shape and bake immediately if so).'
    time_min: 75
  - text: 'Shape into 12 batons. Turn the dough out onto a clean, unfloured surface (a lightly damp surface helps prevent sticking; flour here produces uneven shaping). Divide into 12 equal portions of about 65 g each, using a scale, and roll each under your palm into an 18 cm baton, slightly thicker in the middle and tapering at the ends. Hand-rolling is structural; a rolling pin produces a flatter, less pillowy breadstick.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Second rise (proof). Arrange the 12 batons on two parchment-lined sheet pans, 6 per pan, at least 4 cm apart. Cover loosely with a damp tea towel and proof at room temperature 30 to 40 minutes until visibly puffed but not fully doubled. Overproofed breadsticks collapse in the oven and bake dense; the finger-poke should show a slow partial spring-back.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Bake. Position racks in the upper and lower thirds and preheat to 200 °C (400 °F) for at least 15 minutes. Bake 12 to 15 minutes, swapping the pans top-to-bottom and rotating front-to-back at the 7-minute mark. Because the 21 g / 1 tbsp honey browns faster than cane sugar, start checking at minute 10: pull the breadsticks when the tops are golden-brown, the bottoms deep golden, and a probe reads 88 to 90 °C. Underbaking leaves a gummy interior; overbaking, easy to hit here, gives a hard crust and dry crumb.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Brush with garlic butter while hot. While the breadsticks bake, finish the brush you started at the beginning of the bulk rise: the 60 g / 4 tbsp melted butter whisked with the 6 g / 2 cloves pressed-and-rested garlic, the 2 g / ½ tsp garlic powder, the 1 g / ¼ tsp dried oregano, and the 1 g / ¼ tsp dried parsley flakes. The moment the breadsticks come out of the oven, brush each one generously on all surfaces, then sprinkle the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt over the tops while the butter is still wet so the grains stick.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Serve hot. These are a hot-from-the-oven bread; the texture compromises noticeably within 10 minutes of cooling. Serve immediately in a cloth-lined basket (the cloth holds heat) alongside marinara, a ragu, or soup. Cooled breadsticks reheat acceptably in a 175 °C oven for 4 minutes.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why honey instead of cane sugar. The job the sugar does in an enriched dough is threefold: feed the yeast, brown the crust, and hold moisture in the crumb. Honey does all three, and does two of them better. It is an invert sugar (mostly glucose and fructose already split apart), so the yeast can metabolize it immediately without waiting for its own invertase to break down sucrose. Its fructose caramelizes and browns at a lower temperature than sucrose, so the crust colors faster (which is why the bake needs watching from minute 10). And honey is strongly hygroscopic, so it holds water in the crumb and keeps the breadsticks soft a little longer than a cane-sugar version. The only adjustment the baker makes is respecting the faster browning and the slightly stickier, wetter dough.

  Why the barley malt. Commercial bakery breadsticks and bagels are almost always fed with barley malt syrup, not plain sugar, and that maltose-and-malt-flavor note is a real part of why bakery bread tastes like bakery bread rather than home bread. A small dose here (2 tsp) adds that malty depth behind the honey without making the dough taste of malt. It is optional; the recipe works on honey alone. Use non-diastatic (plain) barley malt syrup, not diastatic malt powder, which carries active enzymes that would slacken this dough.

  The pressed-and-rested garlic in the butter. Crushing garlic and resting it 5 to 10 minutes before it hits the butter lets the alliinase enzyme convert dormant alliin into allicin, the sharp-garlic compound. Garlic mixed into butter the instant it is pressed reads soft and flat; rested garlic reads sharp and defined. The double-garlic move, fresh plus powder, covers two parts of the garlic spectrum: fresh gives immediate bite, powder gives a slow toasted background.

  Why brush hot. The breadsticks must be brushed within 30 seconds of leaving the oven. The hot crust wicks the seasoned butter into its outer couple of millimeters, integrating the garlic and salt into the bread rather than leaving them sitting greasy on top. The same applies to any hot enriched bread: brioche, dinner rolls, hot cross buns.

  A note on cane-free integrity. The two sweeteners here are genuinely cane-free, but read the labels: buy honey whose only ingredient is honey (some cheaper "honey" products are cut with cane or corn syrup), and a barley malt syrup that lists only malted barley (a few blends add cane sugar). Plain garlic powder, oregano, and parsley are clean, but a pre-mixed "garlic salt" or spice blend can carry dextrose or an anti-caking sugar, so use single-ingredient spices if you are strict about it.

  The freezer strategy. Shape the breadsticks fully, freeze on the sheet pan, then bag for up to 2 months. To bake from frozen, arrange on parchment, cover, and proof at room temperature 90 to 120 minutes until visibly puffed, then bake as written. They read identical to fresh-shaped, and it is the canonical way to keep cane-free breadsticks on hand for a pasta night.
