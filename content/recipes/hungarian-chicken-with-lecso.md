The defining elements: yellow Hungarian wax peppers (slightly sweet, slightly hot, the canonical pepper); ripe tomatoes; onion; sweet Hungarian paprika; bacon fat for body (or substitute neutral oil). before_you_start:
  - 'Hungarian wax peppers (sárga paprika) are the canonical lecsó pepper: long, yellow-green, mildly sweet with a faint heat. Available at Hungarian and Eastern European groceries, some farmers markets in the summer. The closest substitutes are Cubanelle peppers (mild Italian frying peppers, common in U.S. supermarkets) or banana peppers (slightly sweeter). Avoid bell peppers (too sweet and thick-walled) or hot peppers (too spicy).'
  - 'Bacon fat or lard is structural to lecsó for body and depth. The dish can be made with neutral oil but reads less integrated. If you do not have rendered bacon fat saved, render 80 g of bacon at the start of the cook and reserve the bacon for the topping.'

steps:
  - text: 'Season the 1200 g chicken with the 8 g / 2 tsp salt and 3 g / 1½ tsp pepper. Rest 15 minutes at room temperature.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Render the bacon. Heat a heavy-bottomed Dutch oven over medium heat. Add the 80 g diced bacon; render 5 to 6 minutes until crispy with 30 to 60 ml of rendered fat in the pot. Lift the crispy bacon out with a slotted spoon to a paper-towel-lined plate; reserve for topping. Leave the rendered fat in the pot.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Sear the chicken. Add the seasoned chicken pieces skin-side down to the hot bacon fat in a single layer (2 batches if needed). Brown 5 to 6 minutes skin-side down until deep mahogany; flip and brown 3 more minutes on the second side. Transfer to a plate.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Reduce heat to medium. Add the 250 g sliced onion to the pot; sauté 6 minutes in the bacon-and-chicken fat until softened with light gold edges.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Add the 600 g pepper rings; cook 5 minutes until starting to soften.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Reduce heat to medium-low (paprika burns easily at high heat). Add the 20 g / 5 cloves garlic, 15 g sweet paprika, and 3 g hot paprika if using; stir 30 seconds to bloom, off heat if needed to prevent burning. Add the 500 g tomatoes and 3 g sugar.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour in the 200 ml / ¾ cup chicken stock. Stir to combine. The lecsó base is now built; the peppers should be partially softened, the tomatoes broken down, the broth deep red-orange.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Return the seared chicken to the pot, skin-side up, nestling the pieces into the lecsó. Any accumulated juices from the plate go in too. The liquid should come about two-thirds up the chicken (not fully submerged; the exposed skin stays slightly crisp). Bring to a steady simmer.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Cover with the lid slightly ajar (10 percent evaporation); reduce heat to maintain a steady simmer. Braise 35 to 40 minutes, until the chicken is fork-tender and the lecsó has thickened around the chicken into a deep orange-red sauce. The peppers should be soft but still distinguishable as pieces; the tomatoes should have fully broken down.'
    technique: 'braising'
    time_min: 40
  - text: 'Uncover; if the sauce is still thin, raise heat to medium-high for 3 to 5 minutes to reduce. Taste; adjust salt (rarely needed at this stage). Off heat, sprinkle with the reserved crispy bacon and 10 g parsley. Serve over white rice or fresh egg noodles, with the chicken on top and the lecsó spooned generously around.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  Why the bacon fat. Hungarian lecsó is structurally a bacon-fat dish; the rendered pork fat carries the paprika and adds bass-note depth that neutral oil cannot match. Many Hungarian home kitchens keep a jar of rendered bacon fat or lard (zsír) for this and other dishes. The bacon-fat version reads dramatically more Hungarian than the oil version.

  Why the paprika goes in off heat. Paprika is a powdered red pepper that burns rapidly at temperatures above 150 °C / 300 °F; burnt paprika turns bitter and the bright red color goes brown. The Hungarian discipline is to add paprika off heat (or to the just-warm bottom of the pot after reducing heat), stir to bloom in residual warmth, then add liquid to dilute. This is a universal Hungarian cooking rule.

  Why no flour or cream. Unlike chicken paprikash (which finishes with sour cream and sometimes a flour slurry for thickening), lecsó-and-chicken relies on the natural reduction of the lecsó base and the rendered chicken juices for body. The dish reads brighter and more vegetable-forward than paprikash; the structural distinction is the no-cream finish.

  Pairings. Canonical with white rice or fresh egg noodles (the canonical Hungarian "tojásos tészta"). A glass of dry Hungarian white wine (Olaszrizling or Furmint) on the side.

  Storage. Improves overnight. Keeps 4 days refrigerated. Reheat gently in a covered pan over low heat. Freezes well; portion into 500 g containers.
