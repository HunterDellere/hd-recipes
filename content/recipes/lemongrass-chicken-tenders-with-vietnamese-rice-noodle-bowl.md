title: 'Lemongrass chicken tenders with Vietnamese rice noodle bowl'
desc: 'Chicken tenderloins marinated 30 minutes in a paste of pounded lemongrass, garlic, shallot, fish sauce, palm sugar, and black pepper, then hard-seared in a smoking carbon-steel pan until the sugar lacquers into a mahogany crust. Served bun-thit-nuong style over room-temperature rice vermicelli with a fresh-herb plate (mint, cilantro, Thai basil), shredded romaine, julienned cucumber, quick-pickled carrot, crushed roasted peanuts, and a small bowl of nuoc cham to spoon over at the table. Vietnamese in the structural sense: the protein is one component of five, the herbs are not garnish but a primary ingredient eaten by the handful, and the dressing is built in the bowl bite by bite rather than tossed in advance. Lean, vegetable-forward, and naturally gluten-free.'

The substitution of chicken tenderloin for pork shoulder is a weeknight pragmatism (the tenderloin cooks in 6 minutes against the pork shoulder''s 30); the marinade and the bowl structure are unchanged.'
  note: 'The structural insight, drawn from the Vietnamese cuisine reference: a Vietnamese composed bowl is not a salad with chicken on top, it is five independent components in dialogue. Each one has a job. The lemongrass marinade and the hard sear give the chicken aromatic depth and caramelized edges. The room-temperature rice vermicelli is a starch that absorbs the dressing without diluting it. The herb plate (mint, cilantro, Thai basil) is the primary aromatic, eaten by the handful, not a garnish. The pickled carrot is the acid-and-crunch foil. The nuoc cham is the universal seasoning, spooned over at the table so each bite balances differently. Skipping or shortcutting any component flattens the dish.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'Pulling chicken tenders at 70 to 72 °C / 158 to 162 °F internal (below the USDA published-safe 165 °F for poultry)'
    ref: 'safety/meat-doneness#poultry'
    why: 'The USDA published-safe instantaneous target is 165 °F (74 °C). The USDA-FSIS pasteurization table publishes the equivalent holds: 160 °F (71 °C) held 14 seconds achieves the same 7-log Salmonella reduction. Tenders pulled at 70 to 72 °C off the pan continue to climb to 75 to 78 °C during the 3-minute rest before slicing and during the time on the plate before eating, spending well over 14 seconds above 160 °F. The carryover plus the residual pan contact clears pasteurization comfortably. Cooks who prefer the published-safe number should pull at 165 °F (74 °C), accepting noticeably drier meat. Ground chicken, mechanically tenderized cuts, and pre-marinated commercial chicken should always be cooked to the instantaneous 165 °F since the surface bacteria may be distributed through the mass.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Marinate the chicken at least 30 minutes before cooking, ideally 1 to 2 hours. The lemongrass paste needs time to drive its citral and the fish-sauce glutamates into the lean tender; under 30 minutes the dish reads surface-flavored rather than infused. Plan accordingly: pound the lemongrass paste and rest the chicken in it while you build the rest of the bowl.'
  - 'Make the pickled carrot 30 minutes ahead at minimum. The carrot needs time in the vinegar to soften from raw-crunchy to pickle-crisp; under 20 minutes it still tastes raw, past 4 hours it goes limp. The 30-to-90-minute window is the sweet spot.'
  - 'Make the nuoc cham at the start of the session. The dressing benefits from 10 minutes of rest so the raw garlic and chili mellow into the lime-and-fish-sauce base. The recipe is on this site at recipes/nuoc-cham; the 5-minute build is part of the bowl structure, not an optional add-on.'
  - 'Cook the rice vermicelli last (after the chicken comes off the heat), then rinse aggressively under cold water and toss with a few drops of neutral oil to prevent clumping. Bun is meant to be room-temperature in this bowl; cold-from-the-rinse is correct.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the lemongrass marinade and start the chicken. Peel the dry outer layers off the 30 g / 3 stalks of lemongrass, trim away the upper two-thirds (the woody green), and slice the pale lower third paper-thin against the grain. Transfer the slices to a mortar with the 12 g / 3 cloves garlic and pound until reduced to a coarse paste; the fiber should break down enough that no large lemongrass shards remain. (No mortar: use a microplane on the garlic, then chop the thin-sliced lemongrass as fine as you can with a heavy knife.) Scrape the paste into a bowl. Whisk in the 30 g / 1 medium minced shallot, the 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce, the 20 g / 1½ tbsp palm sugar, the 10 g / 2 tsp light soy sauce, the 2 g / 1 tsp cracked black pepper, the 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, and the 10 g / 2 tsp lime juice. Stir until the sugar dissolves. Add the 600 g chicken tenderloins, massage briefly so every piece is coated, and refrigerate 30 to 120 minutes. Past 2 hours the fish sauce starts to break down the surface texture; do not exceed 4 hours.'
    technique: 'mortar-pounding'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Quick-pickle the carrot. In a small bowl, dissolve the 20 g / 1½ tbsp granulated sugar and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt in the 60 g / ¼ cup warm water, then stir in the 60 g / ¼ cup rice vinegar. Tip the 200 g / 2 medium julienned carrots into the brine, press them gently below the liquid (a small plate weighed down with a glass works), and let stand at room temperature 30 to 90 minutes. The pickle is correct when the carrots have just lost their raw crunch and read translucent at the edges, still firm and bright orange at the center. Drain and reserve in the brine in the refrigerator until plating.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Make or assemble the nuoc cham. Follow the recipe at recipes/nuoc-cham (about 5 minutes, no cook): whisk 60 g / ¼ cup fish sauce with 60 g / ¼ cup fresh lime juice, 60 g / 5 tbsp granulated sugar, and 120 g / ½ cup warm water until the sugar dissolves, then stir in 8 g / 2 cloves minced garlic and 4 g / 1 finely chopped Thai or bird-eye chili. Let stand 10 minutes for the raw aromatics to integrate. Transfer to a small pitcher or 4 individual bowls. This is the table sauce; do not toss it through the bowl, each diner spoons it over their own bowl.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Build the herb plate and vegetable mise. While the chicken marinates, pick the herbs (20 g mint, 15 g cilantro, 15 g Thai basil) into separate piles on a serving platter or one shared plate. Julienne the 200 g cucumber into 3 mm matchsticks; shred the 150 g romaine into 5 mm ribbons. Crush the 40 g / ¼ cup roasted peanuts with the side of a heavy knife into rough pieces (not powder; the texture is the point). Slice the 20 g / 2 scallions thin. Have all components staged in small bowls or piles on the platter; this is the bowl-builder mise. The herb plate is the structural component the Vietnamese eat by the handful; do not skimp.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Cook the rice vermicelli. Bring a 4-quart pot of water to a hard boil (no salt; the bun comes already salted by the dressing later, and salted boiling water can over-firm the rice noodle). Slip the 250 g / ½ nest of rice vermicelli into the pot, stir once to separate the strands, and boil 2 to 3 minutes until just tender (bite a strand; it should yield with a faint resistance, not paste-soft). Drain immediately into a colander and rinse aggressively under cold running water for 30 seconds, lifting and tossing the noodles with your hands or tongs so every strand gets cold rinsed. The rinse stops the cook and washes off the surface starch that would otherwise gum the noodles. Drain well, then transfer to a wide bowl and toss with the 5 g / 1 tsp neutral oil. The noodles hold at room temperature for 2 hours.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Heat the pan and prep the chicken for the sear. Pull the marinated chicken out of the refrigerator. Lift each tender out of the marinade, letting excess drip back into the bowl, and lay them on a paper-towel-lined plate; the surface should be tacky-coated but not dripping wet (the surface marinade is what caramelizes and the bowl-marinade is what runs off into the pan and burns). Place a 30 cm / 12-inch carbon-steel or cast-iron skillet over medium-high heat for 2 to 3 minutes until a drop of water beads and dances on the surface (about 175 °C / 350 °F). Add the 20 g / 1½ tbsp neutral oil and tilt to coat.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Hard-sear the chicken. Lay the tenders in the pan in a single layer, leaving 1 cm between each one (work in two batches if needed; the marinade sugars need open pan space to caramelize). The chicken will sizzle aggressively and the marinade sugar will start to lacquer within 30 seconds. Sear undisturbed 3 minutes on the first side. The cue for the flip is the underside: lift a corner with tongs, the surface should be a deep mahogany-brown with visible caramelization from the marinade sugars and salts, not pale tan. Flip to the second side and sear 2 to 3 more minutes. The target internal temperature is 70 to 72 °C / 158 to 162 °F at the thickest point; the chicken is correct when the interior reads just-set opaque white with no translucent pink.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Rest and slice. Transfer the tenders to a cutting board and let rest 3 minutes; the rest is non-negotiable on tender, the meat will give up 30 to 40 percent more juice if sliced immediately. After the rest, slice each tender across the grain into 1 cm pieces. Across-the-grain shortens the muscle fibers to bite size and reads dramatically more tender than along-the-grain.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Build the bowls. In each of 4 wide bowls, scatter a base of the 150 g shredded lettuce (about 40 g per bowl). Pile a quarter of the 250 g cooked rice vermicelli over the lettuce. Arrange a quarter of the 200 g cucumber matchsticks and a quarter of the drained pickled carrot in two parallel piles to one side of the noodles. Lay a quarter of the sliced chicken (about 150 g per bowl) across the top. Scatter generous handfuls of the 20 g mint, 15 g cilantro, and 15 g Thai basil over the bowl, the herb layer should be visible-thick, not a sprinkle. Top with a quarter of the 20 g sliced scallions and a quarter of the 40 g crushed peanuts. Set a lime wedge on the rim.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Dress at the table. Serve immediately with the nuoc cham pitcher (or individual small bowls) alongside. Each diner spoons 50 to 75 g of nuoc cham over their bowl, squeezes the lime wedge over the top, and tosses briefly with chopsticks or a fork to coat. The dressing should pool at the bottom; each forkful drags noodles, herbs, and chicken through a different dressing-to-meat ratio. This is the structural eating method of bun thit nuong, do not pre-toss in the kitchen, the bowl flattens within 5 minutes if dressed too early and the herbs go limp.'
    time_min: 1

substitutions:
  - for: 'chicken tenderloins'
    use: 'boneless skinless chicken thigh, cut into 2 cm strips'
    note: 'Thigh substitutes well and produces a more forgiving dish; the higher fat tolerates the high-heat sear without drying. Trim the visible fat and connective tissue first; the chunked-cooking application reads gristly otherwise. Cooking time is the same; pull at 75 °C internal rather than 70 °C since the thigh wants a touch more time for the connective bands to soften.'
  - for: 'palm sugar'
    use: 'light brown sugar, dark brown sugar, or jaggery'
    note: 'Light brown sugar substitutes 1:1 and is the standard accessible swap; the dish reads slightly less caramel-deep but is honest. Dark brown sugar pushes the molasses note forward and is closer to palm sugar in depth. Indian jaggery (gur) is structurally the same product, an unrefined cane or palm sugar, and substitutes 1:1. Avoid white granulated sugar, which strips the caramel backbone the dish is built on.'
  - for: 'Thai basil'
    use: 'Italian basil or perilla (shiso)'
    note: 'Italian basil is the next-best swap but reads wrong; the anise-forward profile of Thai basil is structural to the Vietnamese herb plate. If only Italian is available, increase the mint by 5 g to compensate for the missing aromatic register. Korean perilla (shiso) is more closely related and works honestly. Holy basil is not the substitute; it is peppery and changes the dish into something Thai-Isaan rather than Vietnamese.'
  - for: 'rice vermicelli (bun)'
    use: 'thin rice stick noodle (banh pho 1 mm) or angel hair pasta'
    note: 'Thin rice stick (banh pho) works honestly, flatter shape but similar mouth-feel and cooking time. Angel hair pasta is the emergency wheat-noodle substitute; cook to al dente, rinse cold identically, the dish loses its gluten-free status but the bowl still works structurally. Avoid glass noodles (mien) or rice paper sheets, both are different products.'
  - for: 'fish sauce (nuoc mam)'
    use: 'light soy sauce + a few drops of mushroom powder, or vegan fish sauce'
    note: 'Fish sauce is the structural salt and umami of the marinade; substituting it changes the cuisine. For a pescatarian-free or vegan version, use 30 g of light soy sauce plus 3 g of mushroom powder (dried shiitake ground in a spice grinder) to mimic the glutamate density. Commercial vegan fish sauce (Three Crabs Vegan, Wegan Whisper) is the closest 1:1 swap and is rapidly improving in quality.'
  - for: 'roasted peanuts'
    use: 'cashews or sunflower seeds (for nut-free)'
    note: 'Cashews substitute 1:1 and read slightly sweeter, less aromatic. For a nut-free version, use roasted sunflower seeds; the texture is similar and the dish stays structurally honest. Avoid almonds and pecans, neither is the right register for a Vietnamese bowl. Salted-and-roasted is the canonical form; do not use honey-roasted, the added sugar throws the bowl balance.'

notes: |
  Why the lemongrass paste over chopped lemongrass. The aromatic profile of lemongrass lives in cellular oil pockets locked behind a fibrous wall; pounding fractures those cells in a way that chopping cannot. A pounded paste releases the citral, geraniol, and pinene volatiles in proportions that the recipe is calibrated against. Chopped lemongrass also leaves recognizable fiber chunks that read unpleasant on the seared chicken surface; pounded paste integrates and disappears. The 8 to 10 minutes of pestle work is the structural opening move of the recipe, do not shortcut it with a food processor (which whips air into the paste and changes the texture) or pre-jarred lemongrass paste (which is aromatically dead).

  Why fish sauce in the marinade and not the bowl. Fish sauce on raw chicken does two things over the 30 to 120 minute marinade: the high-glutamate content drives the savory depth into the meat surface, and the salt component (fish sauce is about 25 percent salt by weight) acts as a dry-brine analog, equalizing seasoning through the lean tender. In the bowl, fish sauce is the structural base of the nuoc cham at the table, where it is diluted with lime and water and sugar into a pourable seasoning that touches every bite. The two uses are distinct; the marinade fish sauce never reaches the diner directly because it cooks off in the sear, the nuoc cham fish sauce is what the bowl is dressed with.

  The herb plate is structural, not optional. The Vietnamese cuisine reference is explicit on this point: herbs in a Vietnamese bowl are a primary ingredient eaten by the handful, not a garnish. A bun thit nuong served with a sprinkle of cilantro and basil on top is structurally not the same dish as one with three small herb piles totaling 50 g per bowl. The herbs contribute aromatic volatility (mint cools, Thai basil pushes anise-forward, cilantro brightens) that the cooked components cannot supply; without them the bowl reads heavy, one-note, and Westernized. Buy the full bunches, pick the leaves into separate piles, eat by the handful.

  Why room-temperature noodles and not hot. The bun is the starch that carries the dressing; at room temperature it absorbs the nuoc cham without diluting it (cold noodles would shed the dressing), and at hot temperature it would wilt the herbs and warm the cucumber. Room-temperature is the structural correct, not a compromise. The cold-rinse after cooking is non-negotiable; it stops the cook (rice noodles overcook in 30 seconds in residual pot heat), it washes off the surface starch that turns gummy at room temperature, and it brings the noodles down to the right serving temperature in one move.

  The 30-minute pickle window. The pickle is structurally correct between 30 and 90 minutes. Under 30 minutes, the carrot is raw-crunchy and the vinegar has not penetrated; the pickle reads as raw carrot in sweet vinegar rather than a unified pickle. Past 4 hours, the carrot cell walls break down enough that the matchsticks go limp, the texture goes soft, and the bowl loses the structural crunch the pickle is supposed to provide. The 30-to-90-minute window is the sweet spot; the pickle can hold in the brine in the refrigerator for 3 to 5 days after that, with the texture progressively softening but the dish still functional.

  Doneness on the chicken. Same as for any chicken tender: the USDA-recommended 74 °C (165 °F) is the safe-without-question target, but tenders pulled at 70 °C and held for 90 seconds during the rest are equally safe by the time-and-temperature equivalence rule, and read noticeably moister. The marinade sugars caramelize aggressively on the pan surface; do not push the sear past the 3-minute first-side window or the surface goes bitter-black while the interior is still climbing. An instant-read thermometer is the right tool.

  Why no chili in the marinade. The southern Vietnamese tradition keeps the marinade aromatic and lets the chili come from the nuoc cham at the table. This is a deliberate structural choice: each diner dials their own heat through the dressing rather than having a single heat level baked into the protein. The bird-eye chili in the nuoc cham is the canonical heat carrier; cooks who want a hotter dish can mince a second chili directly into the dressing.

  Storage and reheating. The components hold separately for 2 to 3 days in the refrigerator: cooked chicken (slice before storing), cooked noodles (toss with a touch of oil), pickled carrot (in its brine), herbs (wrap in a damp paper towel, store in a sealed bag), nuoc cham (keeps 1 week). Assemble at room temperature; do not microwave the chicken (the marinade sugars go rubbery and the meat dries). The dish is structurally a cold-noodle bowl with warm-room-temperature chicken; reheating the chicken to fully hot is the wrong move, the bowl is meant to be at one temperature throughout. For the next-day lunch version, slice the chicken cold and let it temper to room temperature on the counter for 20 minutes while you build the bowl.
