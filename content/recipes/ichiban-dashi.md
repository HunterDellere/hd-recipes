before_you_start:

The lift point is structural; the kombu must come out just before the water reaches a boil (about 80 to 85°C / 175 to 185°F, small bubbles forming at the bottom but no rolling boil). Continued heating with the kombu in the pot releases the bitter compounds.'

steps:
  - text: 'Cold-soak the kombu. Place the 10 g kombu in a 2-quart saucepan; pour the 1000 ml cold water over it. Let stand at room temperature for 30 minutes (or refrigerated overnight, 8 to 12 hours, for the deepest extraction). The kombu will rehydrate, soften, and slowly release glutamate into the cold water during this rest. The water should take on a faint amber color and a subtle savory smell by the end of the soak.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Heat the kombu water. Place the saucepan over medium heat. Bring slowly to just under boiling, about 8 to 10 minutes; the water should reach approximately 80 to 85°C / 175 to 185°F, with small bubbles forming at the pot bottom but no rolling boil. Watch the water carefully; the lift point is structural.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Lift the kombu. Just before the water boils (when you see strings of small bubbles rising and the water is steaming actively but not yet rolling), use chopsticks or tongs to lift the kombu out of the water. Reserve the kombu on a plate. Continuing to heat the water with the kombu in it releases alginate compounds that produce a slimy, bitter dashi; the lift point is non-negotiable. The reserved kombu can be used for niban dashi (the second extraction), sliced into ribbons for vegetable simmers, or chopped finely for furikake.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Bring to just below boiling for the katsuobushi steep. Increase heat slightly; bring the water to about 95°C / 200°F (a faint simmer, with consistent small bubbles rising). Sprinkle the 20 g katsuobushi evenly across the surface of the water. The flakes will absorb water within 30 to 60 seconds and sink to the bottom of the pot. Pull the pot off heat immediately as soon as the flakes are mostly submerged.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Steep off heat. Let the dashi stand off heat for 3 to 4 minutes. The katsuobushi releases inosinate (the umami nucleotide that synergizes with the kombu glutamate to produce dashi''s characteristic deep savory taste) during this brief steep. A longer steep (5 to 8 minutes) produces a stronger but slightly more bitter dashi; the ichiban style targets the 3 to 4 minute window for the cleanest extraction. Suimono (clear soup) and refined kaiseki applications get the 3 minute steep; everyday miso soup gets the 4 minute steep.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Strain. Set a fine-mesh sieve lined with a single layer of cheesecloth (or a clean unscented paper towel for an everyday substitute) over a clean pot or large measuring cup. Pour the dashi through the sieve. Do not press the katsuobushi against the sieve to squeeze out the last of the liquid; pressing extracts bitter and astringent compounds and turns clear dashi cloudy. Let the katsuobushi drain naturally; the small amount of dashi left in the flakes is structural, not worth saving. You should have about 800 ml of clear amber dashi.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Use immediately or refrigerate. For peak flavor, use the dashi within 4 hours of making. Refrigerated in a sealed container, dashi keeps 3 days; it loses subtle aromatics over time but the structural umami stays intact. For longer storage, freeze in ice-cube trays (each cube is about 30 ml, convenient for individual portions); transfer cubes to a zip-top freezer bag and use within 2 months. Frozen dashi is dramatically better than commercial powder for any application where the dashi is a primary flavor.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why two ingredients produce such a deep broth. The kombu releases glutamate (free L-glutamic acid) into the cold soak, which delivers the base umami sensation on the human tongue (the 5th taste, identified by Kikunae Ikeda at Tokyo Imperial University in 1908 specifically through kombu research). The katsuobushi releases inosinate (5''-IMP, inosine monophosphate) into the warm steep, which is a different umami molecule. When glutamate and inosinate are present together at the right ratio, they produce a synergistic umami sensation 8 times stronger than either alone (a 1979 study by Yamaguchi quantified this synergy). This umami synergy is the structural reason why dashi can produce such a deep savory broth from two ingredients in 50 minutes; the combination is mathematically and biochemically optimized.

  The kombu cultivar differences. Rishiri kombu (from northern Hokkaido) produces a clear, refined dashi favored for kaiseki and suimono. Hidaka kombu (from southern Hokkaido) produces a slightly more vegetal, everyday dashi favored for home cooking. Ma-kombu (from southern Hokkaido) produces a sweet, rich dashi favored for more complex preparations. Naga kombu (long kombu, from various Hokkaido coasts) produces a more aggressively flavored dashi suitable for stronger preparations like Osaka-style udon broth. Most US-market kombu is unidentified by cultivar; any quality Japanese kombu produces good dashi.

  Why pressing the katsuobushi is wrong. The katsuobushi flakes hold residual dashi after the strain; pressing them against the sieve to extract this residual liquid extracts bitter and astringent compounds (mostly tannins and oxidation products) that turn the clear dashi cloudy and ruin the clean profile. The clean strain technique is structural; let the flakes drain naturally and accept that a small amount of dashi stays in the flakes (about 30 ml out of 800 ml). The strained katsuobushi can be re-used in niban dashi or for okaka furikake.

  Ichiban dashi vs niban dashi by application. Ichiban dashi (the first extraction, this recipe) is the structurally refined version, reserved for suimono (clear soup), kaiseki cooking, chawanmushi (savory egg custard), and any preparation where the dashi flavor is dominant. Niban dashi (the second extraction, see modifications) is the everyday version, used for miso soup (where the miso paste covers the dashi anyway), simmered vegetables, udon broth, and tempura dipping sauce. Many Japanese households use ichiban for special-occasion suimono and niban for everyday cooking; the two-tier system is structural to Japanese kitchen economy.

  The umami baseline. Ichiban dashi tastes savory, marine, slightly sweet, with a clean finish and no fishy aftertaste. If your dashi tastes fishy or bitter, the issue is either: the kombu was boiled with the water in the pot (releases bitter alginates), the katsuobushi was steeped too long (releases bitter compounds), the katsuobushi was pressed (releases bitter compounds), or the water was tap with high chlorine. The fix for each is direct: lift the kombu before boiling, steep the katsuobushi only 3 to 4 minutes, do not press, use filtered water. With these four rules respected, every batch produces clean canonical dashi.

  Storage. Refrigerated in a sealed container, 3 days at peak quality; the dashi slowly loses subtle aromatics over time but retains the structural umami. Frozen in ice-cube trays then bagged, 2 months; the frozen dashi is structurally indistinguishable from fresh after thawing. Do not store at room temperature for more than 2 hours; the protein-rich broth grows bacteria readily.
