steps:
  - text: 'Press the tofu. Wrap the 400 g block of firm tofu in a clean towel, set it on a plate, and weight it with a second plate and a couple of cans for 30 minutes to drive out water. Pressing is essential: a wet tofu steams rather than takes on smoke, and it will not develop the firm, dense bite you want. Pour off the expelled water.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Brine for flavor and color. Stir the 40 g / 2½ tbsp light soy sauce, 10 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt, and 10 g / 2 tsp granulated sugar into the 500 ml / 2 cups water until dissolved. Submerge the pressed tofu and brine 30 minutes, turning once. The brine seasons the tofu through and tints the surface so it burnishes attractively under smoke. Lift it out and pat it bone-dry, a dry surface takes smoke; a wet one beads it off.'
    time_min: 32
  - text: 'Build the smoking rig. Line a wok (or a deep heavy pot) completely with two layers of heavy foil, letting it overhang the rim. Pile the 30 g / ½ cup genmaicha, 50 g / ¼ cup raw rice, and 30 g / 2 tbsp brown sugar in the center. Set a wire rack or a steamer trivet above the mixture, leaving a few centimeters of clearance, and lay the dried tofu on the rack. Have a tight lid ready.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Start the smoke. Set the wok over medium-high heat with the lid off until the mixture begins to smoke steadily, 3 to 5 minutes, you will see wisps rise and smell toasted tea and caramel. As soon as it is smoking in earnest, put the lid on and crimp the overhanging foil up and over the lid edge to seal in the smoke.'
    technique: 'tea-smoking'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Smoke covered, then rest off heat. Drop the heat to medium and smoke, sealed, for 12 minutes. Then turn off the heat and leave the wok sealed and undisturbed another 5 minutes so the residual smoke keeps working as it cools, do not lift the lid early or you vent the smoke and lose color. The tofu should emerge burnished amber-brown and smell distinctly of tea smoke.'
    time_min: 17
  - text: 'Finish and serve. Lift the tofu out and brush it with the 10 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame oil while warm, which adds gloss and a nutty top note. Let it cool at least to warm (the smoke flavor settles and deepens as it rests). Slice into thick slabs or batons, scatter with the 15 g / 1 sliced scallion, and serve. It is excellent cold, sliced over rice or into the green tea ochazuke, or quickly seared cut-side-down for a crisp edge.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why tea makes a gentle smoke. Hot-smoking over smoldering tea leaves, rice, and sugar is a classic Chinese and Japanese technique (the Sichuan tea-smoked duck is its most famous expression), and it produces a fundamentally different smoke from burning wood. Tea leaves and rice smolder at a lower temperature and throw a finer, sweeter, more aromatic smoke than hardwood, while the sugar caramelizes into the smoke stream and lacquers the surface with color and a faint sweetness. Genmaicha, which is green tea already blended with roasted rice, is the most forgiving choice: its built-in toasted-rice character makes the smoke read warm and nutty rather than sharp.

  Why press and brine first. Tofu is mostly water, and water is the enemy of smoke uptake: a wet surface steams and beads the smoke off, while a dry, seasoned surface drinks it in and burnishes. Pressing drives out the free water and firms the texture so the tofu holds together on the rack and develops a denser, meatier bite; the brine then seasons it through (smoke is a surface flavor and cannot season the interior) and the soy tint gives the surface something to burnish against. Patting bone-dry after the brine is the final, easily-skipped step that decides whether you get color or a pale, damp slab.

  Why seal, then rest off heat. The crimped-foil seal traps the smoke in contact with the tofu instead of letting it escape into the kitchen, and the off-heat rest is where a lot of the flavor and color actually develop: as the wok cools, the dense smoke continues to settle onto the surface without any risk of the sugar scorching to bitterness. Lifting the lid early vents that working smoke and gives you a paler, milder result. The whole technique needs no special smoker, just a wok, foil, a rack, and a strong exhaust fan.
