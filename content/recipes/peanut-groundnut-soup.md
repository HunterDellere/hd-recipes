source:
  note: 'The peanut-based stew exists across West Africa under different names: mafé in Senegalese Wolof, domoda in Gambian Mandinka, groundnut soup in Ghanaian English. The structural elements are constant: protein (beef, lamb, or chicken) slow-braised in a smooth peanut paste thinned with tomato and stock, with scotch bonnet for heat, ginger and garlic as aromatics, and a long enough cook for the natural oil to render out of the peanut paste and pool on the surface as a visible sheen. Most West African peanut stews use natural unsweetened peanut paste rather than American commercial peanut butter; the recipe specifies "natural unsalted unsweetened" to avoid the added sugars and stabilizers that throw the balance off.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Use natural unsalted unsweetened peanut butter (the only ingredient is peanuts, sometimes with salt). American commercial peanut butter (Jif, Skippy) contains added sugar and stabilizers that throw the dish toward dessert; if you only have commercial, reduce or omit the small honey addition and accept the slightly off-balance result.'
  - 'The peanut paste needs to be thinned with broth before going into the pot; adding it dry to hot liquid clumps the proteins. Whisk the paste with 250 ml of warm broth in a separate bowl first, then pour the thinned mixture in.'
  - 'The 60 to 75 minute braise is what lets the natural peanut oil render out and pool on the surface; this glossy red-brown sheen is the structural visual signature of the dish. Cooks who shorten the braise produce a peanut stew that reads pasty rather than glossy.'

steps:
  - text: 'Pat the 800 g / chuck or chicken pieces dry with paper towels and season all sides with the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt and the 2 g / ¾ tsp black pepper. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy 5 to 6 quart Dutch oven over medium-high heat until shimmering, about 1 minute. Sear the meat in two batches, 3 minutes per side, until deeply browned. Transfer to a plate. The Maillard crust on the meat is the structural flavor anchor; do not crowd the pot or the meat steams instead of searing.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Drop the heat to medium. Add the 250 g / 1 large diced yellow onion to the hot pot (with the rendered meat fat still in it) and cook 6 to 8 minutes, stirring every minute, until the onion is deep golden brown.'
    technique: 'dry-sweat'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the 20 g / 5 cloves microplaned garlic, the 20 g / 4 cm knob microplaned ginger, and the 8 g / 1 to 2 scotch bonnets minced. Cook 1 minute, stirring constantly, until fragrant.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Push the aromatics to one side. Add the 60 g / 4 tbsp tomato paste to the empty side and fry 3 minutes in the residual oil, stirring constantly, until it darkens from bright red to brick red and the oil takes on its color. Stir the aromatics back through.'
    technique: 'bloom-spice'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'In a separate bowl, whisk the 300 g / 1¼ cups natural peanut butter with 250 ml / 1 cup of the chicken or beef stock until completely smooth, no lumps. This pre-thinning is critical; adding dry peanut butter to hot liquid causes the proteins to clump.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour the thinned peanut mixture into the pot and stir to combine with the aromatic base. Add the remaining 750 ml / 3 cups stock, the 400 g / 1 can crushed whole tomatoes (broken up by hand), the 4 g / 1 Maggi cube crumbled in, the 3 g / 1 tsp fresh thyme, and the 2 bay leaves. Stir well; the mixture should be the consistency of thick gravy. Return the seared meat (and any juices on the plate) to the pot, nestling the pieces into the liquid so they are mostly submerged.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Bring to a vigorous simmer, then drop to low and cover with the lid slightly ajar (1 cm gap to let steam escape). Simmer for 60 minutes (chicken) or 90 minutes (beef chuck), stirring gently every 20 minutes so the peanut base does not catch on the bottom. The stew should look glossy and red-brown by the end with a visible sheen of natural peanut oil pooling on the surface; this oil rendering out of the peanut paste is the structural signature of a properly cooked peanut stew.'
    technique: 'braise'
    time_min: 75
  - text: 'If using the 300 g / 1 medium sweet potato, add it for the final 25 to 30 minutes of the braise (drop it in when the meat has 25 minutes left to go). The sweet potato softens to fork-tender and absorbs the peanut sauce; it makes the stew more substantial as a one-pot meal.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Uncover and taste the stew. It should read deeply nutty, faintly tangy from the tomato, warm from the scotch bonnet. If too thick, splash in 100 ml water; if too thin, simmer uncovered another 10 minutes. Adjust salt; the Maggi contributes salt, so taste before adding more.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pull from heat. Stir in the 15 g / 1 tbsp fresh lemon juice off heat. Discard the bay leaves. Rest covered for 5 minutes off heat so the flavors integrate.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Serve in wide shallow bowls over steamed long-grain rice (the canonical Senegalese service), or with fufu or pounded yam (the Ghanaian service), garnished with the 30 g / ¼ cup chopped roasted peanuts and the 10 g / ¼ cup torn cilantro if using.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why natural peanut butter. Commercial American peanut butter contains added sugar (4 to 8 g per 2 tbsp serving) and partially hydrogenated oils for stability. Both wreck a peanut stew: the sugar pushes the dish toward dessert and the stabilizers prevent the natural peanut oil from rendering out during the long braise. Natural unsweetened peanut butter (only ingredient: peanuts, sometimes with salt) is what every West African kitchen uses and what this dish needs. Smucker''s Natural, Whole Foods 365, Trader Joe''s, and most natural-food brands all make working versions.

  Why the long braise matters. The 60 to 90 minute simmer does two things: it tenderizes the meat (collagen converts to gelatin) and it renders the natural peanut oil out of the peanut paste, where it pools on the surface as a visible glossy sheen. A short cook leaves the peanut base reading pasty and dull; the long cook produces the glossy red-brown stew that is the structural visual signature of a real West African peanut stew.

  The finishing lemon. The acid lift at the end is structural; the rich peanut-and-tomato base reads heavy without an acid finish. The Senegalese tradition uses lime; lemon is the closer Western equivalent. Vinegar works in a pinch (1 tsp white wine vinegar).

  The swallow. Senegalese mafé is traditionally served over long-grain rice; Ghanaian groundnut stew is more often served with fufu or pounded yam. Both work; rice is the accessible default for Western kitchens.

  Make-ahead and reheat. Peanut stew improves dramatically overnight; the spices integrate, the peanut deepens, and the sauce thickens further. Reheat gently with a splash of water to loosen, covered, over low heat for 15 minutes. Holds 4 days refrigerated, 3 months frozen.
