source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Korean jogi-gui canon, the salted pan-fried whole-croaker preparation that anchors the everyday Korean rice table.'
  note: 'The dish reads as plain fried fish but turns on two controls: a salt cure plus a thorough pat-dry so the thin skin can dehydrate and crisp instead of steam, and a moderate, patient oil temperature that crisps the skin in step with the flesh setting to the bone rather than scorching it ahead of a raw interior. The flour dredge is optional in the tradition; here it earns its place by building an even, shatter-prone shell on a fish whose own skin is thin enough to tear.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Salt the fish and let it rest 15 to 20 minutes, then pat it bone-dry just before it hits the pan. Dry skin is the entire game: a wet croaker steams, sticks, and tears instead of crisping, and the salt rest both seasons the flesh and pulls the surface water that would otherwise prevent the skin from shattering. Do not skip the second pat-dry after the rest.'
  - 'Start short-grain rice FIRST as a parallel track. The fish fries in under 12 minutes total and is best eaten the moment it leaves the pan while the skin is at its crispest; the rice (a roughly 15 minute simmer plus 10 minute rest) should be finished and resting before the first fish goes into the oil. Jogi-gui is a rice-table fish and goes cold and limp if it waits.'
  - 'Whisk the cho-ganjang dip before you fry. It is a no-cook sauce and takes one minute, and you want both hands free for the single confident flip once the fish is in the pan.'

steps:
  - text: 'Prep the fish. The grocer should have gutted, scaled, and gilled the 450 g / 3 whole croakers; verify all three. Rinse each fish inside and out under cold water and scrape any dark blood line from the spine cavity with the back of a small spoon, the blood line carries the strongest fishy note. With a sharp knife cut two or three diagonal slashes 1 cm deep into each side of every fish through the skin to the flesh; the slashes let the salt and heat reach the spine and give the skin somewhere to release tension instead of curling. Pat the fish completely dry, inside and out.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Salt-cure. Rub the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt evenly over the skin of all three fish, working it into the slashes and the cavities. Rest the salted fish on a rack, uncovered, for 15 to 20 minutes. The salt seasons the flesh and draws surface moisture into beads on the skin; this is the cure that makes a crisp skin possible. Meanwhile, with the rice already started, build the dip.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Mix the cho-ganjang dip. In a small bowl whisk the 30 g / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, the 15 g / 1 tbsp rice vinegar, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp water, then stir in the 15 g / 1 stalk minced scallion, the 3 g / 1 clove grated garlic, the 2 g / 1 tsp gochugaru, the 4 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, the 2 g / 1 tsp crushed sesame seeds, and the 8 g / ½ green chili if using. The soy is the glutamate anchor and the rice vinegar is the finishing acid; taste and adjust so the dip reads bright and salty, not flat. Set aside.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Dry and dredge. After the cure, pat the fish bone-dry a second time with paper towels, blotting the beaded moisture off the skin and out of the slashes; residual water is what makes a fish steam, stick, and tear. Spread the 30 g / ¼ cup all-purpose flour on a plate and press each fish into it on both sides for a thin, even coat, then lift and tap off all excess. The dredge should be a sheer dusting, not a crust; a heavy coat goes gummy in the pan.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Heat the oil. Film a nonstick or well-seasoned skillet with the 45 g / 3 tbsp neutral oil over medium heat until it shimmers and a pinch of flour dropped in sizzles steadily, about 170 to 180 °C. Moderate heat is the rule: too hot and the thin skin scorches and the flour burns before the flesh sets; too cool and the fish absorbs oil and the skin goes greasy-soft instead of crisp. The oil should be hot enough to sizzle on contact but not smoking.'
    technique: 'shallow-fry'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Fry the first side. Lay the fish into the oil away from you to avoid splatter, giving each room; work in two batches if the pan is crowded, since a crowded pan drops the oil temperature and steams the skin. Fry undisturbed for 4 to 5 minutes. Do not move or poke the fish; the skin needs uninterrupted contact to dehydrate and release on its own. The first side is done when the lower edge of the fish has gone opaque and golden and the fish slides freely when you nudge the pan.'
    technique: 'shallow-fry'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Flip once. Slide a thin spatula fully under each fish and turn it in one confident motion; a tentative half-flip is how the delicate skin tears. Fry the second side 3 to 4 minutes, until the skin is crackling-crisp and deep gold and a chopstick slipped into the thickest slash meets flesh that is opaque to the bone and flakes cleanly. The small fish cooks fast, so the risk here is overcooking past moist into dry; pull the moment the spine flesh turns opaque. Drain briefly on a rack, not paper, so the underside stays crisp.'
    technique: 'shallow-fry'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Serve immediately, while the skin is at its crispest. Plate the fish over or beside the cooked 300 g / 2 cups short-grain rice, scatter the 5 g / fine ginger julienne over the top if using, and add a 50 g / lemon wedge per diner. Each bite goes through the cho-ganjang dip; squeeze the lemon over the fish at the table for a second, sharper hit of acid against the fried richness. Eat the crisp skin and the sweet flesh straight off the frame.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why the small fish is the value play. A 150 g whole croaker is thin through the body, so the ratio of crisp skin to flesh is high and the fish cooks through in under ten minutes. You are buying a lot of the best part, the shattering skin, for very little cooking risk, which is exactly why the small salted croaker became an everyday Korean rice-table fish rather than a special-occasion one. Scale the recipe by fish count, not weight: two diners eat two to four of these comfortably depending on appetite and how many other banchan are on the table.

  The dry-skin doctrine. Everything about the crisp depends on getting the skin dry before it meets the oil, and the salt cure is the mechanism, not just the seasoning. Salt draws cell water to the surface during the rest, you blot that water away, and the now-dehydrated surface protein can sear and crisp instead of spending the first minutes of frying boiling off water. A fish that goes into the pan wet will steam, stick to the metal, and tear on the flip every time. The two pat-dry passes, before the cure and after, are the most important non-obvious moves in the recipe.

  Umami and acid. The glutamate source is the soy sauce in the cho-ganjang dip, which seasons the mild sweet flesh from the outside and supplies the savory depth the fish itself is too delicate to carry. The finishing acid arrives twice: the rice vinegar whisked into the dip, which cuts the fried richness on every bite, and the lemon wedge at the table for a brighter, sharper second hit. A fried fish without an acid counter reads heavy and flat; this one is built to stay bright.

  Temperature discipline over heat. The instinct with fried fish is to crank the burner, but the thin croaker skin and the flour dredge both scorch well before a hot pan would set the interior. Moderate heat, around 170 to 180 °C, crisps the skin in step with the flesh cooking to the bone. If the skin is browning faster than the flesh is setting, the oil is too hot; pull the pan off the heat for a few seconds rather than fighting it. The single confident flip, with a thin fish spatula slid all the way under the fish, is what protects the fragile skin; flip once, never twice.

  Sourcing and the dye caution. Buy whole croaker at H Mart, Hannam, or any Korean or Chinese grocer from the iced case. A genuine fish has a soft, natural pale-gold blush along the belly that fades to silver on the back, clear domed eyes, bright red gills, and firm springy flesh. Be wary of a fish dyed an even, saturated, vivid yellow to fake freshness; if the color rubs off on a wet finger or stains the ice, choose another. Frozen-at-sea small croakers are often the more reliable buy than thawed fish that has sat in the case, and they thaw overnight in the fridge with no loss for this preparation.

  Gluten-free path. Swap the all-purpose flour for rice flour or potato starch and use a tamari or certified gluten-free soy in the dip; the fish fries up if anything crisper, and the dish loses nothing.
