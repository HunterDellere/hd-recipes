before_you_start:
  - 'This is a melted-butter dough carrying ube halaya, which is 30 to 40% water, plus an extra yolk for chew. It spreads readily, so the dough needs a 60-minute fridge chill before scooping or the cookies bake out thin and the white chocolate weeps. Plan the chill into your timing.'
  - 'Chop a white chocolate baking bar rather than reaching for chips. A chopped couverture bar (Valrhona Ivoire, Callebaut, Lindt) melts into wide, lightly browned pools; chips are formulated to hold their shape and give you discrete nubs instead. Use whichever look you want, but the pooled bar is the better partner for the soft violet crumb.'
  - 'Use muted-violet jarred halaya (Good Shepherd) over a fluorescent dye-loaded jar, so the crumb bakes to a jam-rich violet rather than a garish one.'

  - text: 'Brown the 115 g / 1 stick unsalted butter in a light saucepan until it foams, quiets, and smells of toasted hazelnut, about 4 minutes. Scrape every brown fleck into a large heatproof bowl and cool 5 minutes.'

  - text: 'Whisk the 80 g / ⅓ cup + 1 tbsp granulated sugar and 80 g / ⅓ cup + 1 tbsp packed light brown sugar into the warm butter for a full minute, then whisk in the 110 g / ½ cup ube halaya until the mass is even and muted lavender.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Whisk in the 1 large egg, 1 egg yolk, 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste, and the optional 3 ml / ½ tsp ube extract. Beat 30 to 60 seconds until glossy and emulsified.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Fold in the 200 g / 1½ cups + 2 tbsp all-purpose flour, 15 g / 2 tbsp cornstarch, 4 g / ¾ tsp baking soda, and 4 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt until almost no streaks remain, then fold in the 120 g / ¾ cup chopped white chocolate, reserving a small handful to press on top later.'

  - text: 'Cover and chill 60 minutes. The dough firms from soft to scoopable and the fat re-sets, which is what keeps the cookies from flooding flat and the mix-ins from weeping out.'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 350°F (175°C) and line two sheets with parchment. Scoop the chilled dough into 45 g / 3-tbsp balls, space them 2½ inches apart, and press 2 or 3 reserved white chocolate pieces onto the top of each.'

    time_min: 13

notes: |
  Why white chocolate is the right chocolate for ube. White chocolate has no cocoa solids, only cocoa butter, milk solids, and sugar. That means it brings the fat, sweetness, and creamy melt of chocolate without the brown color or the assertive cocoa flavor that would fight the yam for the lead. Dark or milk chocolate would dominate; white chocolate supports. It is the same logic that makes white chocolate the standard base for matcha and citrus confections, applied to ube.

  Why brown butter plus halaya plus white chocolate is a coherent stack. Three caramelizations sit on top of one another here: the Maillard-toasted milk solids of the brown butter, the cooked-down condensed milk inside the halaya, and the milk solids of the white chocolate that brown lightly where the pools sit exposed. They are all toasted-dairy notes in different registers, so they read as depth rather than as competing flavors. Caramelizing the white chocolate first (see modifications) pushes that stack further.

  Why the structural compensations. As with the brown butter sugar cookie version, halaya's 30 to 40% water would over-develop gluten and flatten a melted-butter dough; cornstarch lowers the effective protein for tenderness and the 60-minute chill re-sets the fat so the cookies hold height and the white chocolate stays pooled instead of weeping out. The extra egg yolk adds fat and lecithin for a chewier, more cohesive set that stands up to the heavy mix-in.

  On chips versus a chopped bar. Chips hold their shape by design and give you discrete nubs of white chocolate; a chopped couverture bar melts into wide, lightly browned pools that integrate with the crumb. The pooled bar is the better partner for the soft violet dough, but both work, so pick the look you want.
