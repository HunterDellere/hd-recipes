steps:
  - text: 'Crush the cracked 6 cardamom pods, 1 cinnamon stick (broken into 2 or 3 pieces), 4 cloves, 3 peppercorns, and 1 g / ¼ tsp fennel seeds together briefly in a mortar, break them open without grinding to powder. The break exposes the volatile-oil-rich interior; intact spices steep slowly and underdeliver.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Place a small heavy-bottomed saucepan over medium-low heat. Add the cracked spices dry, no oil, and toast for 30 to 45 seconds, swirling the pan, until you smell warm cardamom and cinnamon rising. This pre-toasting blooms the spice oils in dry heat before water dilutes them; the difference in final flavor depth is substantial.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the 240 ml / 1 cup water and the smashed 15 g ginger to the spices. Bring to a hard rolling boil and hold there 4 minutes. This is the spice decoction phase, water at boiling temperature extracts the tough fibers and woody aromatic compounds in cardamom seeds, cinnamon bark, and ginger root. The water will reduce slightly and turn pale amber with a strong gingery-cardamom perfume rising from the pot.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the 12 g / 2 heaping tbsp CTC tea. Continue boiling hard for 1 minute, short, sharp extraction pulls color and body without dragging out the bitter tannins that a long steep produces. The liquid turns dark mahogany.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour in the 360 ml / 1½ cups whole milk. Stir once. Bring back to a rolling boil, watch carefully because the milk-tea will rise dramatically as it foams. Just before it threatens to boil over, lift the pot off the heat for 5 seconds, then return it; repeat this lift-and-return motion 3 times over the next 2 minutes. Each lift mixes air into the chai and integrates the milk proteins with the tea; the final color should be a deep, even tan, not patchy. This is the "ubaal" (boil-up) technique that defines proper chai.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Drop the heat to low. Add the 20 g / 1½ tbsp sugar; stir until dissolved. Taste from a small spoon, should read aggressively spiced, deeply tea-tannic, and clearly sweet, with the milk binding everything into a creamy body. Adjust sweetness by ½ tsp at a time. Strain through a fine sieve directly into preheated cups (rinse cups with hot water first; cold cups drop the temperature of the chai noticeably).'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Serve immediately. Chai cools fast in thin cups; the experience window is the first 5 minutes when the steam still carries the aromatic top notes.'

notes: |
  The order of operations is the whole technique. Water → spices → boil → tea → boil → milk → boil → sugar. Adding milk to cold spices and bringing everything up together produces a flat, scummy chai because the milk proteins set before the spices have extracted. Adding tea to milk at the start makes the leaves slime up and refuse to release color. Each ingredient enters at the phase where its chemistry actually works.

  The "ubaal" (boil-up) is not aesthetic; it is functional. The repeated rise and fall mixes the milk-tea, aerates it, and lets the dairy proteins integrate with the tea tannins to produce that distinctive creamy, full-bodied chai mouthfeel. A chai that is just simmered tastes thin and separated.

  Sugar must go in at the end, not the beginning. Sugar competes with spices for water during extraction, slows everything down, and partially caramelizes against the pan bottom under sustained heat, giving a faint burnt-sugar note that does not belong here.
