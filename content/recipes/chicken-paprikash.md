source:
  note: 'Chicken paprikash (csirkepaprikás) is the canonical Hungarian Sunday-lunch dish: bone-in chicken legs braised in a paprika-onion base, finished with sour cream tempered into the sauce, served over nokedli (small spätzle-style egg dumplings) or buttered egg noodles. The structural moves are constant: heroic paprika quantity, off-heat blooming to prevent scorching, slow-cooked onion base, bone-in chicken for the gelatin contribution to the sauce, and the final off-heat sour-cream temper that produces the creamy brick-red gravy that defines the dish. The temper is the only technical risk; if you stir cold sour cream into a boiling sauce, it breaks (the casein curdles and the fat separates). The off-heat temper plus the small addition of hot sauce to the cold sour cream before reintroducing prevents breaking.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Use full-fat sour cream (at least 14% fat, ideally 18 to 20%). Low-fat or fat-free sour cream breaks instantly when heated. Hungarian-style smetana (sold at Eastern European groceries) is the ideal version; American Daisy or Cabot full-fat sour cream is the standard substitute. See content/ingredients/sour-cream.'
  - 'Bring the sour cream to room temperature before tempering. Cold sour cream is more prone to breaking when mixed with hot sauce.'
  - 'Off-heat paprika bloom is structural; paprika scorches at 130 °C and turns aggressively bitter. Pull the pot off the burner before adding the paprika.'
  - 'Make the nokedli (Step 9) in parallel with the final 15 minutes of the chicken braise. The dumplings need 10 minutes of mixing and 5 minutes of cooking; if you start them after the chicken is done the dish goes lukewarm waiting.'

steps:
  - text: 'Pat the 1200 g / 4 whole chicken legs dry and season all sides with the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt and the 2 g / ¾ tsp black pepper. In a heavy 5-quart Dutch oven over medium-high heat, melt the 30 g / 2 tbsp lard until shimmering. Sear the chicken in two batches, skin-side down first, 4 to 5 minutes per side, until the skin is deep golden brown. Transfer to a plate.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Drop the heat to medium-low. Pour off all but about 3 tbsp / 40 g of the rendered chicken fat from the pot. Add the 400 g / 2 medium diced yellow onions and cook 18 to 22 minutes, stirring every 3 minutes, until deeply golden brown and very soft. The long onion cook is structural; rushing it produces shallow paprikash.'
    technique: 'dry-sweat'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Add the 15 g / 4 cloves microplaned garlic and the 150 g / 1 medium diced bell pepper. Cook 3 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the pepper softens slightly.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pull the pot off the burner. Sprinkle the 25 g / 3 tbsp + 1 tsp Hungarian sweet paprika and the 1 g / ¼ tsp hot paprika (if using) over the onions and stir constantly for 30 seconds, off heat. The paprika dissolves into the fat and turns the onion mass deep brick-red. Working off heat is structural; paprika scorches almost instantly on direct heat. Once integrated, return to medium-low for 10 seconds.'
    technique: 'bloom-spice'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the 100 g / 1 diced peeled tomato (or the 15 g / 1 tbsp tomato paste) and stir to combine. Pour in the 400 ml / 1⅔ cups chicken stock and add the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt. Stir to dissolve any browned bits on the bottom of the pot.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Return the seared chicken legs and any accumulated juices to the pot, nestling the pieces skin-side up into the sauce. The chicken should be mostly submerged with the tops just showing. Bring to a simmer over medium heat, then drop to low. Cover with the lid slightly ajar (1 cm gap to let steam escape) and simmer for 35 to 40 minutes, until the chicken is fully cooked (internal 75 °C / 165 °F) and tender enough to pull from the bone with light fork pressure.'
    technique: 'braise'
    time_min: 38
  - text: 'Make the nokedli in parallel with the final 15 minutes of the chicken braise (Phase 5). In a bowl, whisk together the 200 g / 1½ cups flour, the 2 eggs, 80 ml / ⅓ cup water, and the 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt to form a thick sticky batter, the consistency of stiff cake batter. Adjust water if too dry. Bring a large pot of salted water (4 liters with 20 g / 4 tsp salt) to a hard boil. Using a spätzle maker or a slotted spoon, press the batter through the holes (or scrape thin strands off a small cutting board with a wet knife) directly into the boiling water. The nokedli sink at first, then rise within 60 seconds; cook another 90 seconds after rising. Drain in a colander, transfer to a bowl, toss with a knob of butter to prevent sticking.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Once the chicken is tender, transfer the chicken pieces to a plate using tongs, leaving the sauce in the pot. Pull the pot off the burner; the sauce must cool slightly before the sour cream temper.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'In a medium bowl, whisk together the 250 g / 1 cup room-temperature full-fat sour cream and the 15 g / 2 tbsp flour until smooth. Slowly whisk in 4 to 5 ladles (about 250 ml) of the hot sauce from the pot, ladle by ladle, whisking constantly. This off-heat temper raises the sour cream temperature gradually and prevents the proteins from shocking and curdling.'
    technique: 'tempering'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pour the tempered sour cream mixture back into the pot of sauce and whisk to combine into a uniform creamy brick-red gravy. Return to low heat for 2 to 3 minutes, stirring constantly, until the sauce thickens slightly and warms through; do not let it return to a boil (boiling can still break the emulsion even after tempering). Return the chicken pieces to the pot, spooning sauce over the tops to glaze.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Serve immediately. Plate the nokedli (or buttered egg noodles) first, then arrange 1 to 2 chicken pieces on top per plate, and ladle generously with the creamy paprika sauce. Some Hungarian cooks dollop additional sour cream on top; others consider this gilding. A small bowl of additional hot paprika on the table lets diners adjust their own heat.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why the sour cream temper matters. Cold sour cream stirred directly into boiling sauce shocks the proteins; the casein curdles and the fat separates, producing a broken sauce with white flecks and oily pools. The off-heat temper (pour hot sauce gradually into the cold sour cream while whisking) raises the sour cream temperature in a controlled way; the casein stays in solution and the emulsion holds. The flour whisked into the sour cream first adds additional stability; the starch granules absorb water and physically prevent the casein from collapsing.

  Why full-fat sour cream. The fat in sour cream coats the casein and protects it from shocking. Low-fat sour cream (less than 14%) does not have enough fat to provide that coating and breaks even with a careful temper. Hungarian smetana (sold at Eastern European groceries, typically 20 to 30% fat) is the most stable; American full-fat sour cream (around 18%) is the standard substitute.

  Why bone-in chicken. The bones release collagen during the 35 to 40 minute braise; the gelatin builds body in the sauce that boneless cuts cannot provide. The skin contributes additional fat and color. Skinless boneless chicken thighs work as a quicker variation but produce a recognizably thinner sauce.

  Make-ahead and reheat. Paprikash is best made fresh; the sour cream sauce can break or thin on reheating. If you must make ahead, stop after Step 6 (the braised chicken in unfinished sauce), refrigerate covered up to 2 days, then reheat the sauce and chicken gently in the pot and proceed with the sour cream temper at serving time. The sour-cream-finished version holds 2 days refrigerated; reheat gently, stirring constantly, over very low heat (do not boil).
