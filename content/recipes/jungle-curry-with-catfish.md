  name: 'Adapted from the Central Thai jungle curry (kaeng pa) tradition.'
  note: 'Kaeng pa is the forest curry of inland Thailand, built without coconut because coconut palms grow on the coast, not in the hills. Catfish (and freshwater fish and game generally) is a canonical protein, and fingerroot (krachai) is the aromatic that both flavors the broth and tames the muddy back-note of freshwater fish. The dish is defined by what it lacks, coconut and sweetness, and what it leans on instead, a fierce paste, a thin stock base, and a tangle of fresh aromatics.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Start the jasmine rice on a PARALLEL track so it lands when the curry does. Rinse 360 g / 1¾ cups rice three times, then cook covered at a bare simmer for 12 minutes and rest off heat 10 minutes; that timeline overlaps the whole curry build.'
  - 'Cure the catfish with salt and rice vinegar 10 minutes before cooking. Catfish carries a slight earthy back-note (geosmin) from its freshwater biology; the salt-and-vinegar rub mobilizes it for rinsing and firms the protein so the steaks hold together in the simmer. Both effects need the full 10 minutes.'
  - 'This is a brothy, fat-free curry, so it has no emulsion to break, but it does poach a delicate fish: dial the heat down to a bare simmer (about 85 to 90 °C) before the catfish goes in. A hard boil shreds the steaks.'
  - 'Have the finishing handful of holy basil and the sliced fresh chilies within reach of the stove; they go in off heat at the very end and there is no time to fetch them once the fish is poached.'

steps:
  - text: 'Cure the fish. Rinse the 650 g / 4 catfish steaks in cold water rubbed with 1 tbsp salt and the 15 g / 1 tbsp rice vinegar, working it gently over skin and flesh, then rinse clean and pat fully dry. Dust lightly with the 4 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt and set aside for 10 minutes. The salt-and-acid rub mobilizes the earthy geosmin in farm catfish for rinsing and firms the surface protein so the steaks hold in the simmer.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Bloom the paste. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide pot or deep skillet over medium heat. Add the 75 g / 5 tbsp jungle curry paste and fry, pressing and folding it across the pan floor, for 3 to 4 minutes until it darkens one shade and the oil runs clear and red-tinged around the edges. With no coconut cream to crack, the oil is the bloom medium, and under-frying here is the single biggest reason a coconut-free curry tastes raw and flat.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Build the broth. Pour in the 1000 ml / 4 cups water (or light stock) and stir hard to lift the paste off the pan floor and dissolve it. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce, the 6 g / 1.5 tsp palm sugar, and the 3 g / 4 bruised makrut lime leaves. Bring to a simmer and taste the broth: it should read salty, fierce, and barely sweet, a thin liquid rather than a sauce.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add the firm vegetables. Drop in the 20 g fingerroot matchsticks, 200 g / 4 to 5 Thai eggplant quarters, 120 g yardlong beans, 120 g / ¾ cup bamboo shoots, and the 20 g / 3 to 4 stems of fresh green peppercorns. Simmer 6 to 8 minutes until the eggplant is tender and curry-stained and the beans are crisp-tender. The fingerroot perfumes the broth as it goes, and the whole peppercorn stems plump and release their resinous heat.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Poach the catfish. Lower the heat to a bare simmer (about 85 to 90 °C, small bubbles only). Slide the 650 g cured catfish steaks into the broth in a single layer, spooning liquid over the tops rather than stirring. Poach 6 to 8 minutes until the flesh is opaque to the bone and flakes at a gentle press. Do not let it boil or stir hard, or the steaks shred.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Finish off heat. Pull the pot from the heat. Stir in the 12 g / 2 tsp lime juice, then fold in most of the 15 g / 1 cup holy basil and the 8 g / 2 to 3 sliced Thai chilies; the residual heat wilts the basil and releases its clove-peppery oils without bruising it. Taste the broth as a complete system: it should land salty, hot, herbaceous, and only faintly sweet and sour. Adjust with fish sauce in ½ tsp increments for salt and lime by the few drops for lift. Scatter the remaining basil over the top and serve in bowls over the 600 g / 3 cups cooked jasmine rice, or with rice alongside so the broth stays thin.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'The Adjustment: jungle curry should read fierce and clean, not rich. If it tastes flat, it is almost always under-salted or under-bloomed, so reach for fish sauce before anything else; if it tastes harsh or one-note hot, a touch more palm sugar (½ tsp) rounds it without making it a sweet curry. If the catfish reads muddy despite the cure, add a few more fingerroot matchsticks, which is exactly the job krachai does in this dish. If the broth is thinner than you like, simmer it uncovered for 5 minutes before adding the fish.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  What makes it a jungle curry. Kaeng pa is defined by the absence of coconut. The forest cooks of inland Thailand had no coconut palms, so the curry is built on water or stock, which makes it thin, fierce, and clean where a coconut curry is rich and rounded. Everything else follows from that: with no fat to carry flavor and no sugar to soften the edges, the dish leans entirely on a hot paste, a generous load of fresh aromatics, and a final hit of holy basil and chili. If you find yourself wanting to add coconut milk, you are making a different curry.

  Why catfish, and why the krachai. Freshwater fish and game are the traditional jungle-curry proteins, and both carry an earthy, sometimes muddy back-note. Fingerroot (krachai) is the classic answer: it perfumes the broth with its peppery-medicinal aroma while chemically subduing the off-notes of the fish. This is not optional seasoning; krachai is the reason a freshwater-fish curry reads clean. The salt-and-vinegar cure does the same job from the other direction, mobilizing the surface geosmin for rinsing before the fish ever hits the pot.

  Why bloom the paste in oil rather than coconut cream. A coconut curry begins by cracking thick coconut cream until its oil separates, then frying the paste in that oil. With no coconut here, plain neutral oil does the same job: the paste must fry in hot fat until it darkens and smells cooked before any liquid goes in, or it tastes raw and harsh in the finished broth. The bloom is the one rich step in an otherwise lean dish, and it cannot be skipped.

  Why so little sugar and acid. Jungle curry sits at the hot, salty, herbaceous corner of the Thai four-flavor balance, with sweetness and sourness deliberately restrained. The small amounts of palm sugar and lime here are for rounding and lift, not to make a sweet-and-sour curry. Taste before adding more of either; the dish should read fierce first.

  Serving. Because the curry is thin, serve it as a brothy bowl over rice or, more traditionally, in a bowl alongside a plate of jasmine rice so the rice stays separate and the broth stays clean. It is meant to be eaten in small, intensely flavored spoonfuls against plenty of plain rice.
