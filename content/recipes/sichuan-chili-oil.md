  - text: 'Once all the oil is in, stir thoroughly. The bowl should look like a vivid red slurry with crackling settling to a low fizz. notes: |
  The three temperatures do three different jobs: the slow infusion (130°C) extracts fat-soluble aromatics (the volatile oils in star anise, cassia, Sichuan peppercorn) without breaking them down. The high pour (175°C) hydrolyzes the chili pigments into the oil and triggers the Maillard reaction on the flake surfaces, giving you the deep red rather than the flat orange of a single-temperature oil. Skip the staging and you get either weak, flat oil or burnt, bitter oil.

  Why two grinds of chili: the fine flakes/powder dissolve into the oil and color it deep red instantly. The coarse flakes hold their shape, give you the sediment to spoon out for noodles and dumpling sauce, and provide a slow-release bite as they continue to infuse over the next week. One grind only gives you one register of heat.

  Why no garlic: garlic goes acrid above 140°C and bitter below 110°C. The temperature window for clean garlic infusion is narrow, and chili oil's three-temperature staging doesn''t respect it. If you want garlic, fry it separately in cooler oil (110°C) and add it to the bowl just before the first hot pour.

  Why salt at the end with the chili flakes, not in the oil: salt is hygroscopic; it pulls moisture and competes with the oil for the chili surfaces. It belongs in the dry mix where it can season the flakes directly as the hot oil hits.

  The three-band visual is the test: a properly made chili oil settles into clearly visible layers within 24 hours. Top oil for stir-fries and scallion-oil noodles where you don't want sediment; middle oil for vinaigrettes; bottom sediment-rich scoops for dan dan, mapo tofu, dumpling dips.

  Storage: room temperature, sealed, dark cupboard. 3 months easily. The oil deepens and rounds in the first 2 weeks; after a month, the heat plateaus and only flavors slowly. Refrigeration is unnecessary and clouds the oil. Do not introduce water (wet spoon, condensation lid); that's what spoils chili oil.
