steps:
  - text: 'Dry-brine the pork: pat 600 g / 1.3 lb bone-in pork shoulder dry, rub all over with 8 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt and 15 g / 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine. Rest uncovered on a rack at room temperature 20 minutes while the rice prep happens. The salt draws moisture to the surface and the alcohol evaporates off any pork-blood notes.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Rinse 200 g / 1 cup jasmine rice three times until the water runs from milky to faintly hazy. Drain five minutes. Massage with 7 g / ½ tbsp neutral oil and 3 g / ½ tsp kosher salt for 30 seconds. Rest fifteen minutes; the oil shell forces the grain to break inside-out during the simmer.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'In a heavy 5-quart pot, combine 2200 ml / 9 cups cold water, the brined pork, the 15 g / half of the ginger as smacked coins, the white parts of the 4 scallions, and the 8 g / 2 tbsp optional dried scallop. Bring to a hard boil over high heat.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Skim the gray scum that rises in the first 4-5 minutes with a fine spider; this is coagulated blood proteins and would cloud the broth and read iron-y. Once the surface skims clean, add the rinsed and oiled rice. Bring back to a boil.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Drop the heat to a steady tremor (small bubbles, never a rolling boil). Crack the lid. Simmer 50 minutes, stirring with a wooden spoon every ten minutes to scrape the bottom and check that nothing is sticking.'
    time_min: 50
  - text: 'At the 50-minute mark, fish out the pork pieces with tongs into a bowl. Pull the bone, shred the meat with two forks into bite-sized strands, and discard the bone and any tough connective tissue. Return the shredded meat to the pot.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Roughly chop one of the 3 century eggs (the half allocated to the simmer) into 1 cm pieces and stir into the pot. Continue simmering uncovered another 15-20 minutes. The pidan dissolves partially into the broth, transferring its sulfur-savory aroma and a deep grey-amber tint to the rice. The remaining two pidan stay as wedges for the bowls.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'After roughly 70-75 minutes total simmer, the rice should be fully fragmented and the body the consistency of single cream. Fish out the smacked ginger coins and the scallop if used (chop the rehydrated scallop fine and stir back in). If thick, splash 100 ml / ½ cup hot water; if loose, simmer another 5-10 minutes uncovered.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Season the pot with 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy sauce and ¼ tsp white pepper. Taste. The base should read savory and warm, with the pidan funk in the background but not aggressive. Adjust salt if needed (kosher salt or more soy).'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Build each bowl: ladle congee, top with 2-3 wedges of the reserved 2 century eggs, a small fistful of the 15 g / ginger matchsticks, the green parts of 4 scallions, optional 10 g / ¼ cup cilantro leaves, a few drops (~2 g / ½ tsp) toasted sesame oil, a final dust of 1 g / ½ tsp white pepper, and 2-3 drops of 10 g / 2 tsp rice vinegar at the rim of the bowl. Eat immediately; the pidan wedges should still be cool to the touch when they hit the hot porridge.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  The two-stage pidan move is the structural insight. One egg dissolved into the simmer transfers the alkaline-fermented aroma into the broth; two eggs reserved as wedges for the bowls preserve the textural drama, the cool jammy yolk against the hot porridge. Halving the pidan and dumping it all in at the end is a common shortcut that flattens the dish into a cleaner, less complex flavor.

  The bone-in pork is the stock. Substituting boneless pork plus chicken stock works mechanically but loses the slow connective-tissue gelatinization that gives the broth its silky body. The Cantonese tradition is one-pot for a reason: the dish builds its own stock in the same vessel that finishes it. If you can only find boneless, simmer it whole and pull at the same point as the bone-in version; the broth will be lighter but the meat texture survives.

  White pepper is the canonical pepper here. Black pepper reads pine-and-citrus-forward against the pidan, which clashes; white pepper is fermented-funk-and-warmth and harmonizes with the egg cure. This is the dish white pepper was born for.

  The rice-vinegar drop at the rim of the bowl is the finishing acid. Cantonese tradition does not always include this and many home cooks skip it; the small amount cuts the richness of the pork-and-pidan broth and brightens the cilantro. Pickled mustard greens or a chili-vinegar dip on the side fill the same role at a higher intensity.

  Storage: leftovers thicken in the refrigerator. Reheat over low heat with a splash of hot water; the pork shreds will dry out at high heat, so reheat gently. Three days in the refrigerator. The pidan wedges should not be reheated; if you have leftover congee, top with fresh wedges from a new egg.
