source:
  name: 'Japanese yaki-gyoza tradition (descended from Chinese jiaozi but Japan-adapted)'
  note: 'Gyoza (餃子) arrived in Japan from China via returning soldiers after WWII, particularly through the Manchurian and Northeast Chinese routes. The Japanese version evolved smaller and thinner-skinned than the Chinese jiaozi/guotie, with a more delicate filling and the canonical hane (lacy starch wing) added at the steam phase. This recipe is the Japanese yaki-gyoza (pan-fried gyoza) tradition; sui-gyoza (boiled) and age-gyoza (deep-fried) are minority variants in Japan.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Salt the cabbage 30 minutes ahead to draw out its water. Skipping this step produces wet filling that leaks during the cook and weakens the wrapper. The salted cabbage releases 30 to 50% of its weight in water; squeezing it dry is structural.'
  - 'Use Japanese gyoza wrappers (Mochiya, Twin Marquis, or any Japanese-brand round 8 to 9 cm wrappers), not Chinese dumpling wrappers (which are thicker and rounder-bottomed). Japanese gyoza wrappers are thinner and made specifically for the pan-fry-then-steam technique. Find at Japanese groceries or via Asian grocery delivery.'
  - 'The pleated half-moon fold takes practice. The first 5 dumplings will look rough; by dumpling 15 the technique clicks. The fold is more important visually than functionally; even poorly pleated gyoza cook fine.'
  - 'Gyoza can be assembled and frozen RAW. Lay wrapped gyoza on a parchment-lined sheet pan, freeze 2 hours until solid, then transfer to a zip-top bag. Cook from frozen directly (do not thaw); add 60 seconds to the steam phase. This is the make-ahead path that makes gyoza a 25-minute weeknight dish.'

steps:
  - text: 'Salt and drain the cabbage. Place the 400 g fine-minced napa cabbage in a colander set over a bowl. Sprinkle with 6 g kosher salt; toss to distribute. Let stand 30 minutes; the salt draws water out of the cabbage cells via osmotic pressure. Press the cabbage with the back of a spoon every 10 minutes to encourage drainage.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Squeeze the cabbage dry. After 30 minutes, transfer the salted cabbage to a clean kitchen towel (cheesecloth or a thin tea towel works). Gather the towel around the cabbage and squeeze hard over the sink, wringing out as much water as possible. The cabbage should reduce from a soft pile to a dense compact ball; you should extract 100 to 150 g of liquid. The dry-squeezed cabbage is now the structural texture base of the filling.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Mix the filling. In a large bowl, combine the 400 g ground pork, 15 g grated ginger, 12 g grated garlic, 40 g sliced scallion, 15 g soy sauce, 10 g sake, 10 g sesame oil, 5 g sugar, 4 g kosher salt, 1 g white pepper, and 8 g cornstarch. Mix with chopsticks or a fork in one direction (always one direction; this aligns the meat protein strands) for 60 to 90 seconds until the mixture goes from loose ground meat to a sticky cohesive paste. Add the squeezed cabbage; fold in until evenly distributed. Do NOT over-mix at this stage; just integrate.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Pleat the gyoza. Set up a wrap station: the wrapper package (kept covered with a damp towel), the filling bowl with a small spoon, a small bowl of water for sealing, a parchment-lined sheet pan for the finished gyoza, and a damp towel to cover the sheet pan as you work. Take one wrapper. Hold it flat in your non-dominant palm. Place 15 g / about 1 tablespoon of filling in the center (do not overfill; the wrapper will tear). Dip your finger in water; trace a thin water line along half the wrapper''s edge. Fold the wrapper in half over the filling without sealing yet. Starting from one end, make 4 to 6 pleats along the front (top) edge of the wrapper, pressing each pleat against the flat back edge to seal. The pleats should curve the gyoza into a crescent with the flat bottom toward you. Press the pleated seam firmly to seal. Set on the parchment, flat-side down, with the crescent standing up like a small tent. Cover with the damp towel. Repeat with all 40 wrappers; the first 5 will look rough, the rest will improve.'
    technique: 'dumpling-wrap'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Pan-fry, batch 1. Heat 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a 30 cm nonstick or well-seasoned cast-iron skillet over medium-high heat until shimmering. Arrange 12 to 15 gyoza flat-side down in a spiral or rows in the pan (touching is fine; they will not stick together). Cook undisturbed 2 to 3 minutes until the flat bottoms are a deep golden-brown; lift one with a spatula to check.'
    technique: 'shallow-frying'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Steam phase. Pour 200 g / ¾ cup water into the hot pan in one go; it will steam aggressively and create a small geyser. Immediately cover with a tight-fitting lid. Reduce heat to medium. Steam 5 to 6 minutes; the water cooks off, the steam cooks the pork filling through, and the wrapper hydrates and turns translucent on top. Check by lifting the lid at minute 5; if water remains in the pan, replace the lid for 60 more seconds.'
    technique: 'steaming'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Crisp the bottom. Once the water has fully evaporated, the gyoza will hit the dry hot pan again and the bottoms will re-crisp. Continue uncovered 60 to 90 seconds; the bottoms should be deep golden-brown and audibly crispy when tapped. Pull from the heat.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Plate and serve batch 1. Slide a thin spatula under the gyoza in groups; invert onto a serving plate so the crispy golden bottoms face UP (this is the canonical Japanese plating, called "kapparei" or "crown-up"). The gyoza should look like a flower of crescent moons with the crispy bottoms on top. Serve immediately while you fry batch 2.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Fry batches 2 and 3. Repeat the fry-steam-crisp sequence for the remaining gyoza, 12 to 15 per batch, with fresh 30 g oil and 200 g water each batch. Total: about 25 minutes of active fry time across 3 batches.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Mix the dipping sauce and serve. Whisk together 60 g soy sauce, 30 g rice vinegar, 8 g rayu, and 3 g sesame oil in a small bowl. Pour into 4 small dipping dishes (one per diner) or set out as a communal dip. The canonical Japanese ratio is 2 parts soy to 1 part vinegar plus chili oil to taste; many diners prefer more vinegar (1:1) to cut the rich pork.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  The salt-drain on the cabbage is structural. Skipping the salt-and-drain step is the single most common home-gyoza failure. Raw napa cabbage is 95% water by weight; ground pork mixed with un-drained cabbage produces a filling that weeps liquid during wrapping (the wrappers tear), during the freeze (the dumplings stick together), and during the cook (the wrappers go soggy from the inside out). The 30-minute salt draw plus the hard squeeze extracts 100 to 150 g of water from the cabbage; the resulting filling is texturally cohesive and the dumplings cook cleanly.

  The fry-steam-crisp sequence is the Japanese signature. Chinese boiled jiaozi go directly into water; Japanese yaki-gyoza follow a three-phase cook: (1) pan-fry the flat bottom in oil for the golden Maillard crust, (2) splash water and cover to steam-cook the filling and hydrate the top of the wrapper, (3) uncover and re-crisp the bottom in the dry pan as the water cooks off. The result is a dumpling with a shatter-crisp golden bottom and a tender translucent top, a textural contrast that defines yaki-gyoza.

  The pleat is for show, not for function. The canonical Japanese pleated half-moon fold with 4 to 6 pleats along one edge is the aesthetic standard, but a poorly pleated gyoza cooks identically to a perfectly pleated one. New cooks should not stress the pleat; press the seam well, do not overfill, and the cook is the same. The visual difference between the first 5 and the last 5 wrapped is dramatic; that is normal.

  Mixing direction matters. Mixing the ground pork in ONE direction (clockwise or counter-clockwise, just consistently) for 60 to 90 seconds aligns the actin and myosin proteins into the springy gel that holds the filling together. This is the same technique behind well-bound meatballs and burgers. Random stirring or over-mixing produces a less cohesive filling that tends to crumble when bitten.

  Cornstarch in the filling does two things. The 8 g / 1 tbsp cornstarch is structural: it binds the small amount of free moisture left in the filling (after the cabbage drain), and it gels when cooked into a slight juicy interior that simulates the broth-and-aspic filling of soup dumplings (xiao long bao) without the gelatin technique. Many home cooks skip the cornstarch and end up with drier gyoza; do not skip.

  Freezing raw is the make-ahead path. Assembled raw gyoza can be frozen on a parchment-lined sheet pan, then transferred to a zip-top bag once solid (2 hours). Cook from frozen directly: add 60 seconds to the steam phase. This is the path that makes gyoza a 25-minute weeknight dish; do the work of wrapping on a weekend and pull a dozen from the freezer on a Tuesday night. Gyoza freeze 2 to 3 months.

  Eating mechanics. The canonical Japanese eating order: take one gyoza with chopsticks, dip the crispy bottom into the sauce (NOT the soft top, which would soak too much), bite half, look at the cross-section to see the filling, eat the other half. The dipping sauce should accent, not overwhelm; many Japanese diners use a 2:1 soy-to-vinegar ratio with rayu, but the 1:1 vinegar-heavy ratio is also common. Serve over rice with a small bowl of miso soup for a complete meal.

  Gyoza-ya in Japan. Specialist gyoza restaurants (gyoza-ya) are a common Japanese dining-out category; Gyoza no Ohsho (national chain), Tencho (Kyoto), and Utsunomiya Minmin (Utsunomiya, the gyoza capital of Japan) are the canonical references. Most serve gyoza, fried rice, and beer; some add ramen. The dish translates to home kitchens better than most Japanese restaurant categories because the technique is fundamental rather than equipment-dependent.
