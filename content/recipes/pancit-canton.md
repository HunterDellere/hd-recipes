  - 'Soak the dried pancit canton noodles in cold water for 5 minutes if your brand specifies pre-soak. Some brands cook directly in the wok with the sauce (the modern Filipino style); others (like Excellent or Wai Wai) need a brief pre-soak. Read the package.'

source:
  name: 'Adapted from Filipino home-cook and fiesta canon'
  note: 'Pancit (from the Hokkien pian-i-sit, "something easily cooked") is a broad family of Filipino noodle dishes brought by Chinese traders centuries ago and adapted into specifically Filipino preparations. Canton refers to the wheat-flour egg noodle (from Cantonese gōn-min, dried noodle); other major pancit are bihon (rice vermicelli), miki (fresh wheat noodle), and palabok (rice noodle with shrimp sauce). Pancit canton is the most-recognized export version and is the fiesta noodle of Filipino birthdays and celebrations; the long noodles symbolize long life.'

steps:
  - text: 'Mix the sauce in advance. Combine the 500 ml / 2 cups chicken stock, 45 g / 3 tbsp light soy sauce, 30 g / 2 tbsp oyster sauce, 15 g / 1 tbsp fish sauce, and 2 g / ½ tsp black pepper in a bowl. Whisk to combine. Set aside.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a 30 cm / 12 inch wok or wide skillet over high heat until shimmering and just starting to smoke. Add the 200 g shrimp; sear 30 seconds per side until just pink, pull from the pan to a plate (they finish cooking in residual heat later). Add the 200 g char siu and 150 g chicken (if using); toss 60 seconds to brown the edges and warm the char siu. Pull the meats to the same plate as the shrimp.'
    technique: 'wok-sear'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add another 15 g / 1 tbsp oil to the wok if dry. Add the 20 g minced garlic and the scallion whites; stir constantly for 20 seconds until fragrant and just turning gold (do not brown the garlic, it goes bitter). Add the 100 g sliced onion; toss 60 seconds until translucent.'
    technique: 'wok-stir-fry'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 100 g julienned carrot; toss 90 seconds to slightly soften. Add the 200 g shredded cabbage and the 100 g snow peas; toss 60 seconds until the cabbage just starts to wilt and turn translucent at the edges.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pour in the sauce mixture. Bring to a hard simmer (the wok should be bubbling vigorously). Add the 250 g dried pancit canton noodles directly into the simmering sauce, pushing them under the liquid with tongs or a spatula. The noodles will be stiff for the first 60 seconds; do not stir aggressively, just push under and let the steam soften them.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'After 90 seconds, the noodles soften enough to toss. Use two spatulas or tongs to toss the noodles through the sauce-and-vegetable mixture; the goal is even hydration. Continue tossing for 4 to 5 minutes; the noodles absorb the liquid, soften to al dente, and the wok contents go from soupy to glossy-coated. By the end the noodles should be tender but with a slight bite at the center, and almost no free liquid should remain in the wok.'
    technique: 'noodle-absorption-stir-fry'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Return the cooked proteins to the wok. Add the scallion greens. Toss 30 seconds to warm through. Taste, adjust with more soy sauce, fish sauce, or pepper as needed.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Transfer to a wide serving platter (the canonical Filipino fiesta presentation). Scatter fried garlic chips over the top (if using). Arrange lime or calamansi wedges around the platter. Serve immediately; diners squeeze citrus over their individual portions at the table.'

notes: |
  Why the noodle goes directly into the sauce. The structural identity of pancit canton is the noodle absorbing the seasoned cooking liquid as it cooks; the noodle hydrates with sauce, not water, which is why every strand emerges deeply flavored rather than coated in surface sauce. Pre-cooking the noodles in plain water (the Western convention for noodle stir-fries) produces a fundamentally different dish, the noodle ends up coated rather than infused. Read your noodle package; if it absolutely requires pre-cooking, do a brief 2-minute parboil and reduce the sauce liquid to 350 ml.

  Why high heat is non-negotiable. The wok needs to maintain a hard simmer (close to a boil) for the duration of the noodle absorption phase; if the heat drops, the noodles soften in lukewarm liquid and end up gummy. The cabbage and snow peas should still have a slight crunch at the end; this only happens if the cook time is short and the heat is high. Low heat produces a soft, soggy pancit; high heat produces the al-dente, glossy-coated canonical version.

  Why fish sauce on top of soy and oyster. The Filipino accent on the Chinese sauce base is the patis (fish sauce). Skipping it produces a noodle stir-fry that reads as Cantonese, not Filipino; the funky-salt note of fish sauce is what makes pancit canton specifically Filipino. The tablespoon is small but structural.

  Why lime or calamansi at the table. The finished pancit reads salt-savory-heavy with no built-in acid (unlike the Vietnamese noodle bowls that build acid into the sauce). Each diner squeezes citrus over their portion at the table; the squeeze of acid lifts the heavy soy-oyster-fish-sauce mix and balances the richness. Skipping the citrus wedge is the most common Western adaptation mistake; the dish reads incomplete without it.

  Why the protein trinity. Shrimp, char siu, and chicken thigh (or some combination of two of three) is the canonical pancit canton protein loadout. The varied textures and flavors (sweet-smoky char siu, sweet-bouncy shrimp, savory-tender chicken) give each forkful something different to land on. A single-protein pancit canton reads flat; the variety is the point.

  Storage. Pancit canton holds 3 days in the fridge but the noodles soften further; reheat in a hot wok with a splash of stock to revive the texture. The dish is also acceptable cold (a common Filipino lunch-box use); the texture is different but the flavor holds.

  Why long noodles. Filipino tradition holds that pancit noodles should not be cut, the long strands symbolize long life. Filipinos serve pancit at birthdays specifically for this reason. The practical implication: do not cut the noodles when serving or eating; respect the symbolism and the eating texture by leaving them long.
