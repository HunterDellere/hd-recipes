source:
  name: 'Jamaican Sunday dinner and wedding-feast canon'
  note: 'Curry goat is the centerpiece dish of Jamaican wedding meals and Sunday family dinners, descended from Indian curry brought by indentured laborers to Jamaica in the 19th century and adapted through Jamaican spice tradition (heavy allspice, scotch bonnet, thyme, scallion). The structural method: marinate overnight, dry-brown the spice paste with the meat ("burn the curry"), water-braise long, finish with potato to thicken the gravy. Goat is canonical; lamb and beef chuck are the standard substitutes.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The goat needs a 12 to 24 hour cold marinade for the spices to penetrate and the connective tissue to start breaking down. Plan the night before; same-day curry goat reads as bland and chewy.'
  - 'Bone-in cuts are structural. The bones release marrow and collagen into the gravy that gives the dish its characteristic glossy body. Boneless goat or beef produces thinner gravy and less depth.'
  - 'Goat is widely available at halal butchers, Caribbean groceries, and many Mexican markets (sold as "cabrito"). If unavailable, bone-in lamb shoulder is the closest substitute and works at the same time and method.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'Bone-in goat braised to fully tender (internal 90 to 95 °C / 195 to 205 °F) rather than the USDA published-safe 145°F whole-cut target'
    ref: 'safety/meat-doneness#beef-and-lamb-whole-muscle'
    why: 'Curry goat is a braise; the meat cooks well past the USDA published-safe instantaneous target of 145°F for whole-muscle red meat. The braise endpoint is 90 to 95°C, the temperature at which collagen has fully converted to gelatin and the meat slides from the bone. The 2+ hour simmer above 80°C provides massive pasteurization equivalency. The food-safety attention in a braise is on rapid cooling of leftovers (within 2 hours) and reheating to 75°C / 165°F throughout before serving.'

steps:
  - text: 'Day before: combine the 1400 g goat chunks in a large bowl with all of Phase 1 (30 g curry powder, 4 g allspice, 6 g thyme, 80 g scallion, 25 g garlic, 20 g ginger, 12 g minced scotch bonnet, 14 g salt, 3 g pepper, 15 ml soy sauce). Mix thoroughly with gloved hands until every chunk is coated. Cover and refrigerate 12 to 24 hours. The marinade penetrates the meat through the bones, dyes it deep yellow, and starts the protein breakdown.'
    time_min: 720
  - text: 'Day of: heat the 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy-bottomed Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Add the 15 g / 1½ tbsp dry curry powder; toast 60 to 90 seconds, stirring constantly, until the powder turns from bright yellow to deep golden-brown and the kitchen smells aggressively of toasted spice. This is the "burning" of the curry; the canonical depth signature of Jamaican curry goat. Do not let it go black, which produces bitter gravy.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 200 g diced onion to the bloomed curry; saute 3 to 4 minutes until softened and uniformly stained yellow.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Add the marinated goat in a single layer (you may need to work in 2 batches if the pot is crowded; do not crowd, crowding steams instead of browns). Sear 8 to 10 minutes total, turning the chunks every 3 minutes, until the outside has dry-browned to deep mahogany with crispy edges. The dry brown stage is structural; it converts the marinade paste into a Maillard crust that becomes gravy body.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Add the 800 ml / 3⅓ cups water or stock; scrape the deeply browned fond off the pot bottom with a wooden spoon. The liquid should come about halfway up the meat (not fully submerged; the exposed tops baste in steam). Float the whole intact 12 g scotch bonnet on top.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Bring to a hard boil, reduce to a steady simmer, cover with the lid slightly ajar (10 percent evaporation), and braise on the stovetop or in a 160 °C / 325 °F oven for 2 hours. Check at the 90-minute mark; the meat should be approaching tender and the gravy should have reduced by about a third.'
    time_min: 120
  - text: 'At the 2-hour mark, add the 350 g potato chunks and the 3 g / 4 sprigs whole thyme. Push the potatoes down so they are mostly submerged in the gravy. Continue to braise 30 to 40 minutes more, uncovered now to allow reduction. The dish is done when a fork inserted into a goat chunk meets no resistance and slides easily back out, the potatoes are fully tender, and the gravy has thickened to coat the back of a spoon (the potatoes contribute starch as they cook, which gives the gravy its glossy body).'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Off heat. Carefully fish out and discard the whole scotch bonnet (do not pierce it now) and the thyme sprigs. Taste; adjust salt (rarely needed, the marinade-soy combination usually carries). Rest covered 10 minutes before serving; this lets the gravy thicken further as it cools slightly. Serve over rice and peas or white rice with a side of fried plantain or steamed cabbage.'
    time_min: 10

notes: |
  Why burn the curry. Dry-toasting curry powder in hot oil (the Jamaican "burning the curry") drives the Maillard reaction on the spice powder''s starches and proteins, deepens the color from bright yellow to mahogany, and converts raw harsh spice flavors into the warm cooked depth that defines Jamaican curry. Skipping this step produces a dish that tastes like raw curry powder mixed with meat; the depth signature is missing.

  Why the marinade is structural, not optional. Goat is a tough meat with thick connective tissue. The overnight marinade does two things: the salt and acid begin protein breakdown before the cook, shortening the effective braise time and producing more tender meat; and the spices penetrate deep into the meat through the long cold soak, producing a goat that tastes curry-saturated all the way through rather than just on the surface.

  The potato-as-thickener mechanism. Waxy potatoes release amylose into the braising liquid as they cook; the amylose gelatinizes in the hot gravy and contributes the glossy body that distinguishes proper curry goat from a thin meat-and-water stew. Without the potato, the gravy stays thin and reads like a soup. Cornstarch slurry is not a substitute; the texture is wrong.

  Pairings. Canonical with rice and peas; everyday with plain white rice. Fried plantain and steamed cabbage with bell pepper round out the Sunday-dinner plate. A small bowl of mango chutney or Jamaican pickled scotch bonnet on the side is common at restaurant service.

  Storage. Keeps 4 days refrigerated and improves on day 2 (the gravy integrates and the spice depth rounds out). Reheat over low heat with a splash of water. Freezes well; portion into 500 g containers, freeze flat, keeps 3 months. Thaw overnight in fridge, warm gently.
