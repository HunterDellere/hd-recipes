source:
  name: 'Indonesian Javanese tradition (Jakarta and West Java)'
  note: 'Gado-gado (literally "mix-mix" in Indonesian) is the canonical Indonesian composed salad, with strongest ties to Jakarta and West Java but found across the Indonesian archipelago. The dish is structurally a vehicle for the peanut sauce; the vegetable selection and the protein add-ons (tempeh, tofu, egg, lontong) vary by region, season, and household. The Jakarta version is the most internationally recognized and is what this recipe targets. Distinct from karedok (Sundanese raw-vegetable cousin) and pecel (Javanese cousin with a slightly different peanut sauce).'

steps:
  - text: 'Toast the 200 g raw peanuts. Spread on a sheet pan; bake at 175°C / 350°F for 10 to 12 minutes, shaking the pan once at the halfway mark, until pale golden and intensely aromatic. Cool fully (about 10 minutes).'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Build the peanut sauce. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide skillet over medium heat. In a small food processor or mortar, pound the 60 g shallots, 15 g garlic, and 10 g red chili into a smooth paste. Add the paste to the hot oil; stir-fry 4 to 5 minutes until the moisture has cooked off, the paste has darkened slightly, and the smell has gone from raw to deeply aromatic.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Grind the toasted peanuts. Transfer the cooled peanuts to the food processor; pulse 8 to 12 times into a coarse-ground texture (like coarse breadcrumbs; not yet peanut butter). Add the ground peanuts to the skillet with the bloomed paste. Add the 40 g palm sugar, 15 g tamarind, 30 g kecap manis, 2 finely shredded kaffir lime leaves, and 4 g fine sea salt. Stir to integrate.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Loosen the sauce. Gradually add the 250 ml / 1 cup hot water, stirring constantly, until the sauce reaches a thick spoonable consistency, like loose peanut butter (the cook should be able to ribbon the sauce over the salad without it pooling flat). Simmer 3 to 4 minutes; the sauce will thicken further as the peanuts hydrate. Add the 30 ml / 2 tbsp lime juice off heat. Taste; adjust salt and sugar. The sauce should be sweet-savory-spicy-tangy in balance; if flat, add more lime or tamarind; if too sweet, add more salt or tamarind; if too thick, add more hot water. Reserve covered.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Hard-boil the 4 eggs. Cover with cold water in a small saucepan; bring to a simmer; cook 9 minutes; transfer to ice water for 5 minutes; peel; halve lengthwise. Reserve.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Cook the rice (or use pre-made lontong). Rinse the 200 g jasmine rice in cold water until the water runs clear. Combine with 280 ml water and a pinch of salt in a small saucepan; bring to a simmer; reduce to lowest heat; cover; cook 15 minutes. Off heat, let stand covered 10 more minutes. Pack into a small loaf pan or container while warm; press firmly with the back of a spoon to compact; refrigerate 20 minutes to set. Cut into 2 cm cubes.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Fry the tofu and tempeh. Heat the 200 ml / ¾ cup neutral oil in a deep skillet to 175°C / 350°F. Fry the 200 g tofu cubes in 2 batches for 3 to 4 minutes each, turning once, until golden on all sides. Drain on paper towels. Fry the 200 g tempeh slabs in the same oil for 3 minutes per side until golden and crisp at the edges. Drain on paper towels; cut tempeh into 2 cm cubes.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Blanch the vegetables. Bring a large pot of salted water to a boil. Blanch each vegetable separately and time them precisely: 200 g long beans for 3 minutes (still snappy), 200 g napa cabbage for 1 minute (just-wilted), 150 g bean sprouts for 30 seconds (just-warm), 150 g carrot matchsticks for 90 seconds (al dente). Lift each batch out with a spider or slotted spoon; shock briefly in ice water; drain. Boil the 250 g potato cubes separately for 12 minutes until fork-tender; do not shock the potatoes (warm potato absorbs the sauce better than cold).'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Assemble the salad. On a large platter or 4 individual plates, arrange the components in distinct sections: blanched long beans, blanched cabbage, bean sprouts, boiled potato, carrot matchsticks, raw cucumber rounds, fried tofu, fried tempeh, halved hard-boiled eggs, and lontong (or rice) cubes. The composed-salad presentation is structural; the diner sees each component before mixing.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Serve. Spoon a generous quantity of warm peanut sauce over the entire platter (about 80 ml per portion; pass extra sauce on the side). Scatter the 40 g krupuk crackers and 20 g fried shallots over the top. Each diner mixes their own portion with chopsticks or a fork at the table; the "gado-gado" name literally means "mix-mix," and the mixing is part of the eating experience. The components combine into the structural composed-salad bite: cool vegetable, hot fried protein, soft starchy rice cake, all coated in the rich peanut sauce.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  The peanut sauce is the dish. Gado-gado is structurally a vehicle for the peanut sauce; the vegetable selection and proteins are infinitely variable, but the sauce is non-negotiable. The four-flavor target for the sauce is sweet (palm sugar), savory (kecap manis, shallot-garlic base), spicy (red chili), and tangy (tamarind + lime); all four must register on the first taste, with the peanut as the carrier. A sauce that tastes only of peanut butter is missing the structural Indonesian character; a sauce that tastes too sweet is over-corrected toward Thai satay sauce.

  Why the vegetables are blanched separately. Each vegetable in gado-gado has a different optimal cook time: bean sprouts need 30 seconds (a structural Indonesian crunch), cabbage needs 1 minute (just-wilted), long beans need 3 minutes (still snappy), carrots need 90 seconds (al dente). Cooking them together produces some overcooked and some undercooked; cooking each separately gives every vegetable its correct texture. The technique is a structural Indonesian approach that distinguishes gado-gado from less-considered versions.

  Tempeh is structural. Tempeh (fermented soybean cake, a distinctly Indonesian invention) is what gives gado-gado its nutty fermented backbone. Tofu provides creamy soybean protein; tempeh provides the more aggressive fermented-and-meaty soybean character. Both are present in canonical gado-gado; substituting more tofu for tempeh loses the dish-defining note. Tempeh is widely available in US supermarkets (Lightlife, Westsoy, Tofurky are reliable brands).

  Composed-salad geometry. The presentation of gado-gado is structural: each component is arranged in its own distinct section on the platter or plate, so the diner sees the variety before mixing. A pre-mixed gado-gado is a structurally different dish (more like a thrown-together stir-fry salad); the visual presentation of distinct sections is part of the eating experience. The mixing is done by the diner at the table, and the "gado-gado" name literally means "mix-mix," referring to this final step.

  Storage. The components store well separately for 2 days refrigerated; assemble fresh just before serving. The peanut sauce thickens dramatically on chilling; reheat gently with a splash of hot water to loosen. Blanched vegetables lose their snap on refrigeration; the dish reads significantly fresher when blanched the same day. Fried tofu and tempeh keep 2 days refrigerated and reheat well in a 175°C / 350°F oven for 5 minutes.
