source:
  name: 'Adapted from the classical French brioche method (Escoffier-lineage enriched dough, the cold-butter-into-developed-gluten technique standardized in modern bakery practice).'
  note: 'Brioche is defined by fat load, not by shape. At 50% or more butter to flour it is a brioche riche; the technique below sits at ~55%, rich enough to read luxurious but still structured enough to slice clean and toast. The single decision that separates good brioche from a greasy, dense brick is mixing order: gluten is developed on the flour-egg-milk-yeast mass FIRST, and only then is cold butter beaten in, so the fat lubricates an already-built network instead of waterproofing the flour before it can hydrate.'

before_you_start:
  - 'This is a two-day bake. Day 1 is the mix, the bulk ferment (1.5 to 2 hours), and then a long overnight cold proof in the refrigerator (12 to 16 hours). Day 2 is shape, final proof (2 to 3 hours at room temperature), and bake. Do not try to compress it into one day; the cold proof is what firms the butter and makes the 55%-butter dough handleable, and it deepens flavor by slowing fermentation.'
  - 'Eggs and milk go in cold, straight from the refrigerator, and the butter is used cold but pliable (15 to 16°C, bendable but not greasy). Brioche dough heats itself through long mixing; friction can drive it from 21°C to 28°C, and above 26°C the butter weeps out of the dough and the structure collapses. Starting cold buys you the mixing time you need. If your kitchen is warm, chill the mixer bowl and the dough hook for 20 minutes before you start.'
  - 'A stand mixer with a dough hook is effectively required. Brioche needs 15 to 25 minutes of mixing to develop gluten and then emulsify the butter; doing this by hand is possible but punishing, and hand mixing tends to overheat the dough through body contact. Cube and re-chill the butter while the gluten phase runs so it is cold the moment you need it.'
  - 'Weigh everything. Brioche is an emulsion held together by structure, and the ratios (hydration, fat, egg, salt, yeast) are tighter than in a lean bread. Volume measures introduce enough error to push the dough out of the workable window.'

steps:
  - text: 'Day 1, build the dry and wet base. In the bowl of a stand mixer fitted with the dough hook, whisk together the 360 g / 3 cups bread flour, the 45 g / 3½ tbsp granulated sugar, and the 7 g / 2¼ tsp instant yeast. Keep the 8 g / 1¼ tsp fine sea salt to one side for now, direct contact between concentrated salt and yeast at this stage can slow the rise; it goes in once the flour buffers it. Add the 250 g / 5 cold eggs and the 60 g / ¼ cup cold whole milk.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Develop the gluten BEFORE any butter. Mix on low (speed 2 on a KitchenAid) for 2 minutes to bring the shaggy mass together, then add the 8 g / 1¼ tsp fine sea salt. Raise to medium (speed 4) and mix for 8 to 10 minutes. The dough will climb from sticky and slack to a smooth, elastic, cohesive mass that wraps the hook and slaps the side of the bowl. This is the non-negotiable order: the gluten network must be fully built on the flour, egg, and milk while the dough is still lean, because once fat coats the flour the strands can no longer link. Check the window: stretch a walnut of dough thin between your fingers; it should pull to a translucent membrane without tearing. If it tears, mix 2 to 3 minutes more.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Beat in the cold butter, cube by cube. With the mixer on medium-low (speed 3), add the 200 g / 14 tbsp cold cubed butter a few cubes at a time, waiting until each addition has disappeared into the dough before adding the next. This takes 8 to 12 minutes total and the dough will look alarming midway: it breaks, goes slack and greasy-looking, and seems ruined. Keep going. The yolk lecithin and the developed gluten are emulsifying the fat into the structure; the dough re-gathers into a glossy, satiny, cohesive mass that pulls cleanly off the bowl sides. Stop the moment it comes back together and looks smooth and shining. The dough temperature should still be at or below 26°C; if it climbs higher and the butter starts weeping, refrigerate the bowl 15 minutes, then finish.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Bulk ferment at room temperature. Scrape the dough into a lightly buttered bowl, cover with a damp towel or a fitted lid, and let it rise at room temperature (21 to 23°C) until it has grown by about half again its size, 1.5 to 2 hours. It will not double like a lean dough; the fat weighs it down and the rise is slower and gentler. You are looking for a visibly puffed, domed mass that springs back slowly when pressed, not a full doubling.'
    time_min: 105
  - text: 'Deflate and cold-proof overnight. Gently deflate the dough by folding it over on itself three or four times in the bowl; this redistributes the yeast and degasses the large bubbles. Cover with a fitted lid or a damp towel and refrigerate 12 to 16 hours. This long cold proof does three jobs at once: it firms the butter so the slack dough becomes handleable, it slows fermentation to a clean low-acid profile that deepens flavor, and it relaxes the gluten so the dough shapes without fighting back. Do not skip this; warm 55%-butter dough is impossible to shape and bakes greasy.'
    time_min: 720
  - text: 'Day 2, shape into the mold. Butter a 900 g Pullman loaf pan (or a large fluted brioche mold). Turn the cold dough onto a lightly floured surface; it should be firm and cool, like cold modeling clay. For a classic tearable pull-apart crumb, divide the dough into 4 equal pieces (about 200 g each), shape each into a tight ball by cupping and rolling on the bench until the surface is taut, and set them in a row in the buttered pan. For a fluted brioche à tête, shape a large ball and a small ball, set the large one in the mold and press the small one into a dimple on top. The tight surface tension is what gives the loaf a clean rise and an even crumb.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Final proof at room temperature. Cover loosely with a damp towel and proof until the dough has risen to fill the pan and crests just over the rim, 2 to 3 hours at 21 to 23°C. Cold dough out of the fridge proofs slowly at first; do not rush it with extra warmth above 27°C or the butter softens and weeps before the structure sets. The dough is ready when a gentle fingertip press in a corner springs back slowly and leaves a faint dent, not when it springs back fast (underproofed) or collapses and stays dented (overproofed).'
    time_min: 165
  - text: 'Egg wash and bake. Heat the oven to 175°C / 350°F with a rack in the lower third. Strain the egg wash (the 50 g / 1 egg beaten with a pinch of salt) and brush a thin, even coat over the entire exposed surface, getting into the seams between the dough balls but not pooling at the base where it would scorch. If making a savory loaf, scatter the optional pinch of flaky salt now. Bake 35 to 45 minutes, rotating the pan halfway, until the crust is a deep mahogany and the internal temperature at the center reads 88 to 91°C / 190 to 195°F on an instant-read thermometer. Brioche is enriched and browns fast; if the top is dark by 25 minutes, tent loosely with parchment or foil and finish to temperature. Pull it at temperature, not by color alone, an underbaked center stays gummy.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 40
  - text: 'Cool fully before slicing. Turn the loaf out of the mold onto a wire rack within 5 minutes of leaving the oven so steam does not condense and soften the crust. Cool completely, at least 1.5 hours, before slicing. Cutting hot brioche tears the crumb and compresses the still-setting gluten into a dense streak; the structure needs to finish setting as it cools. The crumb should pull apart in tender, buttery, slightly stringy strands with an even fine grain, and the crust should be thin and lacquer-deep.'
    time_min: 90

notes: |
  The one rule that makes brioche work: develop gluten first, add fat last. Gluten is a network of glutenin and gliadin proteins that cross-link only when flour is hydrated and worked. Butter is a waterproofing fat; if it coats the flour before the proteins hydrate and link, the gluten never develops and you get a dense, crumbly, cakey loaf that cannot hold the air the yeast produces. So the method is strict: mix flour, eggs, milk, and yeast to a fully developed, windowpane-stage dough while it is still lean, then beat the cold butter in cube by cube so the fat lubricates and tenderizes an already-built network. The dough breaking and going greasy halfway through the butter phase is normal and correct; it re-emulsifies as the yolk lecithin and the worked gluten pull the fat back into suspension.

  Why cold, and why overnight. At 55% butter to flour, this dough is a fat-in-dough emulsion held together by structure and temperature. Above about 26°C the butter melts out of suspension and weeps, the dough goes slack and oily, and it bakes greasy and flat. Cold eggs and cold milk buy mixing time; the long mix generates friction heat, and starting near refrigerator temperature keeps the finished dough under the failure threshold. The overnight cold proof then does triple duty: it solidifies the butter so the otherwise-unmanageable slack dough firms to cold-modeling-clay consistency you can actually shape, it slows the yeast to a long, clean, low-acid ferment that builds flavor without the sour edge of a fast warm rise, and it relaxes the gluten so the dough shapes without snapping back. Skipping the cold proof is the single most common reason a home brioche comes out greasy and misshapen.

  Hydration and the role of egg. The eggs are the main liquid in this dough, not the milk. Egg provides water (eggs are ~75% water), protein for structure, and yolk lecithin, the emulsifier that lets a fat load this high integrate without breaking. That is why a lean-dough baker's first brioche often fails: they treat it like bread with butter added, but it is closer to a structured emulsion that happens to also ferment. Respect the egg as both hydration and emulsifier and the rest follows.

  Baking to temperature, not color. Enriched dough browns fast because sugar, egg, and milk proteins all Maillard and caramelize at lower temperatures than lean dough. The crust can read done while the center is still gummy. Pull the loaf at 88 to 91°C / 190 to 195°F internal, full stop; tent it with parchment if the top darkens before the center is set. An instant-read thermometer is the difference between a tender, fully-set crumb and a dense underbaked streak down the middle.

  Storage and the best use of day-old brioche. Fresh brioche is best within a day; the high fat keeps it soft longer than a lean loaf but it still stales. Wrap the cooled loaf in a clean towel or beeswax wrap and keep at room temperature 2 to 3 days, or slice and freeze for up to 2 months. Day-two brioche is the ideal French toast and bread pudding base, the dense buttery crumb soaks custard without dissolving, and the slight staling actually improves the structure for soaking. Toasted with butter and jam is the simplest and arguably the best use of all.
