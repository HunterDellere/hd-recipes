before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 7 to 14 day fermentation, not a sauce ready the same day. Active hands-on time is about 30 minutes split between the brine-and-pack (day 1) and the blend-and-bottle (day 7 to 14).'
  - 'Equipment: a 1 L wide-mouth glass jar with an airlock lid or loose-fitting lid, a glass fermentation weight or improvised weight (a small zip-top bag filled with brine works), a high-speed blender or food processor for the final blend, fine-mesh sieve or cheesecloth for straining, two 350 ml glass bottles with tight-sealing lids for storage. Avoid aluminum, copper, and untreated cast iron throughout; the chili-and-acid environment corrodes reactive metal.'
  - 'Water must be non-chlorinated. Chlorine and chloramine kill lacto-fermentation bacteria; use filtered water, bottled spring water, or tap water that has been boiled for 10 minutes and cooled to room temperature.'
  - 'Salt must be non-iodized. Iodine inhibits the lactobacillus. Kosher salt, fine sea salt, or pickling salt all work; avoid Morton iodized table salt.'
  - 'Brine concentration is 3% by total weight (salt + water + chilies). This is the canonical hot sauce fermentation brine, high enough to suppress aerobic spoilage but low enough for full lactobacillus activity. Higher salt produces a saltier sauce with less complexity; lower salt risks mold.'
  - 'Hold at 18 to 22 °C for 7 to 14 days. Cooler stalls; warmer runs too fast and can produce off-flavors. Submersion under brine is non-negotiable; floating chilies grow mold within 3 days.'
  - 'Wear nitrile gloves when handling and seeding the chilies. Capsaicin transfers to skin and persists for hours; never touch your eyes after handling chilies without thorough hand-washing with soap and oil.'

steps:
  - text: 'Day 1: prepare the brine and pack the jar. Wearing nitrile gloves, prepare the 400 g chilies according to the heat preference: deseed for a milder sauce or leave seeds in for the maximum heat. Combine the prepared chilies, the 30 g whole garlic cloves, the 15 g ginger if using, and the 50 g halved shallot in a clean 1 L wide-mouth glass jar. The chilies should fill the jar to about 70% capacity.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'In a separate bowl, whisk the 30 g / 2 tbsp salt into the 500 g / 2 cups filtered water until fully dissolved. Pour the brine over the chilies in the jar; the brine should fully submerge the chilies with about 2 cm of headspace at the jar top. If the chilies float (they typically do), weigh them down with a glass fermentation weight, a clean small ceramic dish that fits the jar diameter, or an improvised weight (a zip-top bag filled with extra brine works; rest it on top of the chilies). The chilies must stay below the brine line throughout the ferment.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Cap with a loose-fitting lid (a quarter-turn back from sealed) or an airlock lid. Place the jar on a small tray to catch any brine that bubbles over during active fermentation. Hold at 18 to 22 °C (room temperature, away from direct sunlight) for the duration of the ferment. Check daily; within 2 to 4 days the brine will start to bubble, the chilies will turn slightly duller in color, and a slight sour-fizzy aroma will develop. This is the lactobacillus succession kicking in.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Watch for kahm yeast or mold. Kahm yeast (a thin white film on the brine surface) is harmless but off-flavored; if it appears, skim it off with a clean spoon, press the chilies back down, and continue. Mold (fuzzy growth, any color, often green or black) means the chilies were not fully submerged and the batch should be discarded; restart with closer attention to submersion. Properly submerged chilies under a 3% brine should not develop mold.'
    time_min: 0
  - text: 'Ferment for 7 to 14 days. Most home cooks like 7 to 10 days for a balanced sauce; longer ferments (14+ days) produce a more aggressively funky, more deeply sour sauce. The ferment is done when the brine has gone clearly cloudy, the chilies have lost their bright fresh color and turned olive-pink, the bubbling has slowed significantly (peaks around day 4 to 6), and the sauce smells aggressively chili-funky with a clear sour edge.'
    time_min: 0
  - text: 'Day 7 to 14: blend the sauce. Lift the chilies out of the jar with tongs and transfer to a high-speed blender. Reserve the brine. Add the 60 g / ¼ cup vinegar to the blender. Blend on high for 60 to 90 seconds, starting with no added brine; the chilies should puree into a thick paste. Add reserved brine in 30 g / 2 tbsp increments while blending until the sauce reaches the consistency of loose ketchup or commercial Tabasco-style hot sauce; this typically takes 90 to 180 g (½ to ¾ cup) of the reserved brine, depending on how thick or thin you want the final sauce.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Strain the sauce. Pour the blended sauce through a fine-mesh sieve set over a clean bowl, pressing the solids with the back of a spoon to extract every last drop of liquid. Discard the strained solids (or save for a chili-and-pulp paste for stir-fries). The strained sauce should be a vibrant red-orange, smooth, with the consistency of loose ketchup. If thicker than desired, thin with more reserved brine; if thinner than desired, simmer briefly (5 minutes over low heat) to reduce.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Bottle and store. Pour the strained sauce into two clean 350 ml glass bottles or one 700 ml bottle. Cap tightly and refrigerate. The sauce continues to develop slowly in the bottle (the vinegar largely halts the active fermentation but residual lactobacillus and enzymatic activity continues at refrigerator temperatures); the flavor peaks at week 2 and stays good for 6 to 12 months refrigerated.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  Why lacto-fermentation beats vinegar-pickling for hot sauce. A vinegar-only hot sauce (combine fresh chilies, vinegar, salt, garlic in a blender; bottle immediately) is a perfectly serviceable preparation and is roughly how generic supermarket hot sauces (Frank''s, original Tabasco-clones) are made. A lacto-fermented hot sauce uses the same ingredients but adds a 7 to 14 day lactobacillus succession before the vinegar goes in. The fermentation produces lactic acid (which contributes a different acid character than acetic acid from vinegar), small amounts of esters and other volatile flavor compounds, and breaks down the harsh fresh-chili capsaicin compounds into rounder, more complex chili-heat with a longer back-of-throat finish. The taste difference is substantial; a lacto-fermented hot sauce reads as a different category of condiment than a quick blender hot sauce, the way kimchi reads as a different category than fresh cabbage slaw.

  The chemistry. The 3% salt brine selectively suppresses aerobic spoilage bacteria (which die at salt levels above 2.5%) and yeasts while preserving the salt-tolerant lactobacillus species native to the chili surface. Over the 7 to 14 day ferment, Leuconostoc mesenteroides dominates the first 2 to 4 days (producing the initial fizz and CO2), then Lactobacillus plantarum takes over and drives the pH steadily lower (to about 3.5 by day 7). The vinegar addition at the end pushes the pH below 3.4 (the threshold below which most pathogens cannot survive) and halts active fermentation for stable bottling.

  Salinity math. 3% brine targets 30 g salt per 1000 g total weight (chilies + aromatics + water). The recipe values (400 g chilies + 95 g aromatics + 500 g water = 995 g total) produce a 30 g salt addition for a clean 3.0% brine. Adjusting the ingredient quantities means recalculating the salt; a hot sauce with 600 g chilies and 700 g water and 100 g aromatics needs 42 g salt (1400 g total × 3%). The 3% number is the structural fixed point; everything else adjusts around it.

  Chili choice and heat. The chili selection is the single biggest variable in the finished sauce. The recipe defaults to Fresno or red jalapeño for a medium-heat Louisiana-adjacent workhorse sauce. For mild and fruity: red bell pepper with one or two jalapeños for shape and a hint of heat. For aggressive and complex: habanero, scotch bonnet, or ghost pepper for the fruit-and-fire profile. For smoky depth: rehydrated dried chipotle blended in at the end. Mixing chilies (a 60/30/10 split of fresh, medium-heat, and a small amount of super-hot) typically produces the most complex sauce; pure single-chili sauces read flatter.

  Color stability. Fresh red chilies start vibrant red and dull to olive-pink over the ferment as the cell walls break down and the carotenoid pigments shift. The finished blended sauce is a vibrant red-orange (the blender re-distributes the pigments evenly); the duller chili appearance in the jar mid-ferment is not a sign of failure. Green chili ferments stay green throughout because chlorophyll is more stable than the red carotenoids; both are normal.

  Failure modes. Mold (fuzzy growth on the chilies above the brine line): the chilies were not fully submerged; discard the batch and restart with a heavier weight. Pink film on the brine surface (kahm yeast that has gone red): a sign of yeast contamination; skim, press chilies back down, and add a splash of vinegar. No fizz or bubbles after 4 days: the room is too cold, the salt was iodized, or the water was chlorinated. Salty-flat finished sauce: the chilies were old (low natural sugar content) or the ferment was too short; add additional vinegar and a pinch of sugar to balance.

  Service and use. Lacto-fermented hot sauce is the upgrade for any application where commercial hot sauce is called for: tacos and burritos, fried chicken and chicken wings, eggs (canonical on huevos rancheros and breakfast burritos), grilled meats, Bloody Marys, marinades, finishing sauces, anywhere a clean chili-and-acid hit is wanted. The home product reads significantly more complex than commercial hot sauce; a teaspoon goes further on most dishes.

  Storage and stability. The finished sauce is stable for 6 to 12 months refrigerated (the pH below 3.4 and the salt content make it hostile to pathogens). Sauce that develops a yeasty smell or a fizzy quality during storage has residual live lactobacillus; this is harmless and often desirable but can build CO2 pressure in a sealed bottle (open carefully, leaning the bottle away). For longer storage, pasteurize the bottled sauce: heat to 70 °C / 160 °F for 15 minutes, then cool; this halts all microbial activity and stabilizes the sauce for 2+ years.

  The "set and forget" vs "watch closely" question. A sauerkraut or kimchi ferment is forgiving; the cabbage stays under brine reliably and the timing is wide. A hot sauce ferment requires a bit more attention because the chilies are buoyant and tend to bob above the brine line if the weight slips or shifts; check daily for the first week and re-weight as needed. Once you have the technique down (consistent submersion, reliable weight), the process is essentially the same complexity as sauerkraut.
