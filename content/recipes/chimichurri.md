source:
  name: 'The Argentine and Uruguayan asado canon. Documented across Río de la Plata cookbooks since the late 19th century; the modern stabilized form (parsley, oregano, garlic, red wine vinegar, olive oil, chili flake) hardened in the mid-20th century as the standard finishing sauce for grilled beef.'
  note: 'Two regional schools: the Argentine version leads with flat-leaf parsley and dried oregano, with red wine vinegar as the dominant acid; the Uruguayan version often adds bell pepper and reads slightly sweeter. The version here is the Argentine canon. Chimichurri rojo (a distinct preparation with smoked paprika, tomato, and more chili) is a separate sauce; this is chimichurri verde, the green and dominant form.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The sauce must rest at room temperature 30 minutes before serving. The vinegar needs that window to pull chlorophyll and aromatic oils from the chopped herbs into the oil, which is the structural step that distinguishes chimichurri from a fresh herb oil. Sauce-on-the-table immediately reads thin and disconnected.'
  - 'Chop the herbs by hand, not in a food processor. The processor pulverizes the parsley into a green paste that bleeds bitter chlorophyll; hand-chopped herbs stay in distinct flakes that release flavor over the 30 minute rest without going olive-drab.'

steps:
  - text: 'Chop the 40 g / 1 packed cup parsley and 5 g / 2 tbsp oregano on a board with a heavy chef knife to a coarse-fine cut: each piece 1 to 2 mm across, with structure still visible. Avoid the food processor; the blades bruise the leaves and release chlorophyll into a green slurry that loses the herb-clarity that defines chimichurri. Mince the 12 g / 3 to 4 garlic cloves to a fine even cut and combine all three on the board.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Transfer the herb-garlic mix to a small bowl. Add the 5 g / ¾ tsp salt and 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper, then pour in the 60 g / ¼ cup red wine vinegar and the 2 g / ½ tsp red pepper flakes. Stir to dissolve the salt; the vinegar will visibly pull green color from the herbs within a minute, the leaching reaction that builds the sauce flavor.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour in the 150 g / ¾ cup olive oil in a steady stream, stirring with a wooden spoon to disperse the oil through the herb-vinegar suspension. Chimichurri is not an emulsion: the oil and vinegar separate on standing and the cook stirs to recombine before serving. Let the sauce rest at room temperature 30 minutes before service so the vinegar can fully extract the garlic and herb flavor; the resting step is non-negotiable for flavor depth.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Taste and adjust: chimichurri should read aggressively herbal with sharp acid, a noticeable garlic bite, and gentle heat. If flat, an extra 1 g / pinch of fine sea salt; if too sharp, a touch more oil; if the herbs are overwhelmed, more vinegar to bring them forward. Serve at room temperature, spooned over grilled or seared meat, fish, or vegetables; or as a dip for grilled bread; or stirred into rice and grain bowls; or as a finish for fried eggs and roasted potatoes.'
    time_min: 2

  - for: 'cuisine pivot'
    to: 'as Uruguayan chimichurri with bell pepper'
    how: 'Add 30 g of finely diced red bell pepper (skin and ribs removed). The pepper adds a vegetal sweetness and a slight body to the sauce; common in Uruguay and the eastern Argentine coast. The result reads slightly less sharp and more rounded; pairs better with grilled chicken and pork than the parsley-forward Argentine version.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'as a marinade'
    to: 'used as a pre-grill marinade rather than a finishing sauce'
    how: 'Reduce the oil to 100 g and the salt to 8 g; coat the protein (skirt steak, chicken thighs, pork tenderloin) and refrigerate 2 to 24 hours before grilling. The acid tenderizes the surface and the herbs and garlic bake into the crust during high-heat searing. Reserve a separate batch of fresh chimichurri for service; the marinade chimichurri is denatured by the raw protein and should not be reused.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Chimichurri is not pesto and not salsa verde. Pesto is an emulsion (basil, pine nut, garlic, parmigiano, olive oil) bound by cheese fat and ground to a paste. Italian salsa verde is parsley-anchovy-caper-lemon ground to a coarse paste and bound by olive oil. Chimichurri is structurally a chopped-herb suspension in a vinegar-and-oil bath, with no emulsifier and no grinding; the textural integrity of the chopped herbs is the defining feature. Treating it as any of the others (running the herbs through a processor, mounting it as an emulsion, omitting the rest period) produces a different and worse sauce.

  The 30-minute rest is non-negotiable. The vinegar needs time to penetrate the chopped herbs and pull the chlorophyll, garlic compounds (allicin), and oregano essential oils into the liquid phase; this is what gives chimichurri its characteristic grassy depth and aggressive aromatic carry. A freshly mixed chimichurri tastes flat and disjointed; the same sauce after 30 minutes reads integrated and sharp. The sauce continues to develop for 4 to 6 hours; after 24 hours the herbs darken visibly and the sauce reads more pickled than fresh, still good but a different character.

  Versatility. Chimichurri is the broadest finishing sauce in the herb-acid-oil family. Use it on: grilled steak (the canonical pairing), grilled chicken, grilled fish (especially oily fish like salmon and mackerel), grilled or roasted vegetables (asparagus, broccoli, eggplant, sweet potato), fried eggs (over easy, with the yolk), grain bowls (farro, rice, lentils), boiled or roasted potatoes, grilled bread (as a dip), poached chicken or fish (as a brightener), roast leg of lamb, and as a marinade pre-grill (with reduced oil, see modifications). Pairs across Argentine, Uruguayan, broader Latin American, Mediterranean, North American grill, and Levantine table contexts.

  Storage. Keeps 1 week refrigerated in a sealed glass jar; the oil firms in the cold and the sauce needs 30 minutes at room temperature to soften and re-suspend before service. The flavor peaks at day 2 to 3 (the herbs fully integrated with the vinegar); after day 5 the sauce darkens and the brightness fades, still serviceable but no longer at peak.
