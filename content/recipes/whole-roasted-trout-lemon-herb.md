source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Mediterranean whole-roasted trout tradition'
  note: 'The whole-roast trout is the leanest version of the Mediterranean whole-fish play: a small farmed trout, one per diner, roasted hot enough that the moderate subcutaneous fat renders the skin crisp before the thin flesh overcooks. Two moves carry it. Roast dry and hot so the skin blisters rather than steams, and pull the fish the moment the flake just sets, around 52 to 54°C, so it stays glossy and moist. The cavity aromatics (lemon, garlic, dill, parsley, thyme) perfume the flesh from inside as it roasts; the finish is the engineered counterpoint, lemon and olive oil for clean acid, or an anchovy-caper-lemon dressing when you want an external glutamate-and-salt vector against the mild flesh.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'rainbow trout at 52 to 54°C / 126 to 129°F internal (below the USDA published-safe 145°F for fish)'
    ref: 'safety/seafood-doneness#cooking-targets-for-fin-fish'
    why: 'The Mediterranean whole-roasted fish tradition pulls trout at 52 to 54°C / 126 to 129°F so the fine flake just sets and stays glossy; past 60°C the thin flesh dries, the muscle blocks separate, and the surface goes chalky. The published 145°F is the conservative line for the immunocompromised. Whole trout from a reputable counter, sourced fresh and roasted hot until the skin renders, follows long-standing practice at the lower temperature, with the hot oven surface pasteurizing the exterior where the bacterial load lives. Cooks who want the USDA-published-safe number should roast to 145°F / 63°C internal, accepting drier, more separated flake.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Preheat the oven fully to 230°C / 450°F with a rack in the upper third BEFORE the fish goes near it. A small whole trout lives or dies on the oven being at full temperature at the moment the fish enters: a cold or still-heating oven steams the skin instead of crisping it, and a thin fish has no margin to wait. Give it a full 20 minutes to come up.'
  - 'Get the trout bone-dry and tempered. Pull the fish to room temperature for 15 to 20 minutes, then pat it completely dry inside and out right before it goes in; a thin trout overcooks if it has to spend extra oven time driving off surface water, and a wet skin will not crisp. Tempering draws surface moisture, so the final pat is the one that matters.'
  - 'Stage the herbs and lemon before you start. The cavity stuffing (lemon slices, smashed garlic, dill, parsley, thyme) should be cut and within reach, and if you are making the anchovy-caper-lemon dressing, build it while the fish roasts so the chopped herbs stay bright. There is no slow step here once the fish is in, so the work is all front-loaded.'

steps:
  - text: 'Pull the 600 g / 2 whole trout from the fridge 15 to 20 minutes ahead to temper, and set the oven to 230°C / 450°F with a rack in the upper third. Verify the fishmonger scaled and gutted the fish and removed the gills. Rinse each cavity and scrape any dark blood line along the inside of the spine with the back of a small spoon; the blood line carries the strongest fishy flavor and is the main source of "fishy fish" complaints. Pat both fish completely dry inside and out, since a wet skin steams instead of crisping.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'With a sharp knife, cut three diagonal slashes 1 cm deep into the flesh on each side of both fish, from the gill plate to the tail. On a thin trout the slashes do two jobs: they open channels for the salt and oil, and they give a visual doneness cue, since the flesh in the slashes opaques first. Keep them shallow, 1 cm at most, because a trout fillet runs only 1 to 2 cm thick and a deep cut reaches the spine. Rub the 7 g / 1¾ tsp kosher salt over the skin on both sides, into the slashes, and inside both cavities. Grind the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper over the skin.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Stuff the cavities. Divide the ~60 g / lemon rounds (one of the two lemons), the 16 g / 4 smashed garlic cloves, the 10 g / dill fronds, the 8 g / parsley, and the 4 g / thyme sprigs between the two fish, packing each cavity loosely. The aromatics perfume the flesh from the inside and steam the cavity as the fish roasts, and the lemon slices keep the cavity walls moist. Rub the 28 g / 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil over the skin of both fish and work a little into the slashes so the skin renders and browns rather than drying out.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Lay both trout on a rimmed sheet pan lined with parchment or a lightly oiled rack, spaced apart so hot air circulates around each fish and the skin crisps on all sides rather than steaming where two fish touch. Pat the top skin dry one more time; this is the last chance before the oven and it matters most for crisp. A rack lifts the fish so the bottom skin crisps too, but parchment on a hot pan also works for a small trout.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Roast the fish in the upper third of the fully preheated 230°C / 450°F oven, undisturbed, for 12 to 16 minutes depending on size. The ~300 g trout here run about 14 minutes. The skin should tighten, blister, and brown, and the flesh in the slashes should turn fully opaque. Begin checking at 12 minutes by inserting an instant-read thermometer into the thickest part of the back just behind the head: pull the fish at 52 to 54°C / 126 to 129°F, where the fine flake has just set and stays glossy. The flesh should lift cleanly from the bone with gentle pressure; if it still has a translucent core, give it 1 to 2 minutes more. A thin trout moves fast through the doneness window, so err toward the early pull and let carryover finish it.'
    technique: 'roasting'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'While the fish roasts, build the optional anchovy-caper-lemon dressing. In a small bowl, stir the 45 g / 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil with the 8 g / 2 anchovy fillets minced to a paste; the anchovy is the glutamate anchor, dissolving into the oil and carrying savory depth against the mild trout. Add the 15 g / 1½ tbsp chopped capers, the 20 g / juice and zest of ⅓ lemon, and the 4 g / 1 tbsp chopped dill. Taste: it should read sharp, salty, and herbaceous, with the lemon forward enough to cut the roasted skin. The anchovy and capers supply the salt, so add none until you have tasted.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Rest the fish 2 to 3 minutes on the pan; the flake firms slightly and carryover brings the spine to about 55°C. Transfer each trout to a warm plate with a wide spatula. To eat, run a knife down the spine and lift the top fillet off the bones, then lift the soft skeleton clear from the tail to free the bottom fillet; trout has a pliable rib cage and fine pin bones that strip away in one piece, which is what makes it the canonical fish to eat off the spine at the table (see the rainbow trout ingredient page). Spoon the anchovy-caper-lemon dressing over the flesh, or for the plain finish simply squeeze the reserved ~60 g / lemon wedges over each fish and drizzle with a little of the 45 g extra-virgin olive oil. Serve immediately, while the skin is still crisp.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why whole trout, one per person. A small farmed trout is the leanest, fastest member of the Mediterranean whole-roast family, and it portions naturally: one ~300 g fish is a single serving, so there is no carving a large fish into uneven shares. The bone, skin, and head insulate the thin flesh and baste it as the fish roasts, the cavity aromatics season from the inside, and the soft pliable skeleton lifts away in one piece at the table. Trout sits at a moderate 6 to 8% fat, lean enough to stay clean-tasting and mild enough to carry a simple lemon finish, with just enough subcutaneous fat to render the skin crisp. See the rainbow trout ingredient page for the fat profile and the soft-bone structure that makes it the fish to eat off the spine.

  The two technical moves. First, dryness and heat for the skin: the trout must be patted bone-dry and the oven fully preheated to 230°C, because surface water boiling off at the start steams the skin soft instead of letting it render and crisp, and a thin fish has no spare oven time to drive that water off. Second, temperature for the flesh: pull at 52 to 54°C and rest, because trout flake just sets across that window and begins to dry and chalk past 60°C. The window is narrow on a fish only 1 to 2 cm thick, so an instant-read thermometer in the back is worth more than any timer, and the early pull plus carryover is safer than chasing color.

  The umami and acid engineering. A roasted whole trout is mild and clean, and it needs a finishing acid to brighten it; whether it also needs an external glutamate source is the cook's call. The roasted skin itself carries a savory, lightly Maillard note that anchors the plain version, where lemon and olive oil are all the dish wants. When you want more depth, the anchovy-caper-lemon dressing supplies it: the anchovy is dense in free glutamate and the capers add a salty brine, together giving a savory vector against the mild flesh, while the lemon and the caper brine are the acid that cuts the richness of the crisp skin. Build the dressing fresh while the fish roasts so the chopped dill stays bright, and keep it at room temperature so the oil stays loose.

  Sourcing and serving. Whole farmed rainbow trout is widely stocked at the seafood counter, at H Mart, and at most fishmongers in the 250 to 500 g size that fits a single plate, fresh on ice or frozen. Look for clear, slightly bulging eyes, bright red gills, tight bright scales, firm flesh that springs back, and a clean cucumber-and-lake smell with no muddy or ammonia note; cook it the day you buy it. To serve, lift the top fillet off the bones, then lift the skeleton clear from the tail to free the bottom fillet, and dig out the small lobe of cheek meat behind the eye. A crisp salad and bread to mop the lemon-and-oil are all the sides it needs.
