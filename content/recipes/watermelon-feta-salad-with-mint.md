source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Greek and broader eastern-Mediterranean summer table, where cold watermelon and brined sheep-milk cheese are a canonical sweet-salt pairing.'
  note: 'The dish reads as a salad but behaves like a contrast study. Watermelon is roughly 92% water and 6% sugar by weight, which makes it both a sweetness source and a structural liability: cut too early it weeps and dilutes the dressing. The feta is the counterweight, a brined sheep-milk cheese carrying salt and milk-fat that the melon lacks entirely. The job of the cook is to keep the two components discrete until the last moment so the contrast lands per bite rather than collapsing into pink brine on the plate.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Soak the sliced red onion in cold water with a splash of the lime juice for 10 to 15 minutes before assembling. Raw red onion carries pungent thiosulfinates that read harsh against the sweet melon; the cold-water soak leaches them out and leaves the onion crisp and mildly sweet. Skipping this step is the single most common reason this salad reads aggressive rather than refreshing.'
  - 'If you want the optional pickled chili heat line, start the quick pickle first: it needs 20 to 30 minutes in the vinegar to soften and mellow before it goes on the plate. It can also be made hours ahead and kept cold.'
  - 'Chill the cubed watermelon in the refrigerator while you prep everything else. This salad is served cold, and a warm melon against cold feta reads muddy. Cut the melon last among the prep so it spends the least time weeping juice before it hits the plate.'

steps:
  - text: 'Soak the onion. Submerge the 60 g / ½ onion sliced into 2 mm half-moons in the 250 ml / 1 cup cold water with a splash of the lime juice taken from the dress allotment. Leave 10 to 15 minutes. The cold water leaches the pungent thiosulfinates that make raw red onion read harsh; the onion comes out crisp, milder, and bright pink. Drain well and pat dry on a clean towel before it goes near the salad, residual soak water dilutes the dressing.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Quick-pickle the chili, if using. Pack the 15 g / 1 chili sliced into thin rings into a small bowl. Warm the 30 g / 2 tbsp red wine vinegar with the 5 g / 1 tsp honey just until the honey dissolves (15 seconds in a small pan or a few seconds in a heatproof cup of just-boiled water set in it), then pour over the rings to submerge. Leave 20 to 30 minutes at room temperature. The acid softens the cell walls and the honey rounds the raw vinegar bite, so the chili lands as a sweet-sharp heat line rather than a raw burn. Drain when ready to plate.'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Build the dress. In a small bowl whisk the remaining lime juice (30 g / 2 tbsp total, minus the splash used in the onion soak) with the 40 g / 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil until it loosely emulsifies into a cloudy vinaigrette. Whisk in the 1 g / ½ tsp sumac and, only if the melon tasted underripe, the optional 5 g / 1 tsp honey. Do not salt the dress, the feta and the finishing flake carry all the salt this dish needs, and salting the dress on top of brined cheese oversalts it fast.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Cut and chill the melon. Remove the rind from the watermelon and cut the flesh into 3 cm / 1-inch cubes to yield 700 g / about ½ small melon of flesh. Cut on a board tilted slightly toward a lipped tray so the juice runs off rather than pooling under the cubes. Keep the cubes cold in the refrigerator until the moment of assembly; warm melon against cold feta reads muddy, and melon cut too far ahead weeps and dilutes the plate.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Compose, do not toss. Spread the cold 700 g watermelon cubes across a wide shallow platter in a single loose layer. Tuck the 150 g / 5 oz feta broken into 2 cm chunks between the melon, then scatter the drained soaked onion over the top. The salad is composed rather than tossed: tossing breaks the feta into paste and bruises the melon into juice. A platter, not a bowl, keeps the layers discrete so each forkful can take its own ratio.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Dress and finish. Spoon the lime-olive-oil dress evenly over the platter, about 60 to 70 g total, letting it pool in the gaps rather than drowning the melon. Tear the 8 g / ½ cup mint leaves directly over the top so the cut edges stay green, then scatter the optional 1 g / ½ tsp Greek oregano crumbled between your fingers and, if using, the drained pickled chili. Finish with the 2 g / ½ tsp flaky finishing salt scattered from height so it lands evenly, the 1 g / ½ tsp cracked black pepper, and the reserved lime zest. The flake is functional, not garnish: it sits on the cut surfaces and delivers the salt hit that defines the sweet-salt contrast.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Serve immediately, cold. This salad has a short window: within 10 minutes the salt and acid begin pulling water out of the melon and the plate turns to brine. Dress and finish at the table, or no more than a few minutes before serving. Taste one loaded forkful, melon plus feta plus dress, and adjust: flat means more flaky salt and a squeeze of lime; sharp means a thread more olive oil to round it.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why this works as a flavor system. The salad runs on three opposed axes held in tension. Sweet against salt: the watermelon's 6% sugar against the feta's brine and the finishing flake. Water against fat: the melon is 92% water, the feta and olive oil supply the lipid that coats the tongue and carries aroma. Soft against sharp: the yielding melon and mild soaked onion against the lime, sumac, and optional pickled chili. None of these is a garnish. Pull the flaky salt and the dish reads like fruit; pull the lime and it reads like dessert; pull the feta and there is no contrast left at all.

  The melon is the structural problem, not the cheese. Watermelon weeps the moment salt and acid touch it, by osmosis the cell water is drawn out into the dressing, and within 10 minutes a composed plate turns to pink brine. Every timing decision in this recipe serves that one constraint: cut the melon last, keep it cold, compose rather than toss, and dress at the table. If you must hold the salad, hold the components separately and assemble at the last second.

  Block feta, never pre-crumbled. Pre-crumbled feta is tumbled in anti-caking agents (cellulose or starch) that leave it dry and chalky, and it is cut from drier offcuts. Buy a block in brine, drain it, and break it by hand into rough 2 cm chunks. The torn surfaces are irregular and grab dressing; uniform machine-crumbles do not. Sheep-milk PDO feta is tangier and creamier than cow-milk imitations and is the right cheese for a dish where the cheese is half the contrast.

  On the onion soak. Raw red onion is sharp because cutting it ruptures cells and triggers an enzymatic reaction that produces volatile thiosulfinates, the same family of compounds that make you cry. A 10 to 15 minute cold-water soak leaches these water-soluble compounds out, leaving the onion crisp, faintly sweet, and bright pink instead of harsh. This is the difference between an onion that disappears into the salad and one that dominates it. Do not skip it, and dry the onion before it goes on the plate.

  The acid is a finishing seasoning, the Greek way. In Greek cooking lemon (here, lime) is added at the end as a brightener rather than cooked in. Keep the dress unsalted because the feta and the finishing flake carry the salt; salting the dress on top of brined cheese is the fastest way to oversalt the whole plate. Taste a fully loaded forkful before adding any more salt, melon plus feta plus dress together, because each component on its own misreads the balance.
