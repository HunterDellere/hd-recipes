steps:
  - text: 'In a medium bowl whisk together the 120 g / ½ cup mayonnaise, 120 g / ½ cup sour cream, and 80 ml / ⅓ cup buttermilk until completely smooth and the mixture has a thick pourable consistency. If it looks broken or weeping, add 1 tbsp more sour cream and re-whisk.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Whisk in the 1 grated garlic clove paste, the 8 g / 2 tbsp chives, the 6 g / 1½ tbsp dill, the 8 g / 2 tbsp parsley, the 10 ml / 2 tsp lemon juice, the 3 g / ½ tsp salt, the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper, the 0.5 g / ¼ tsp onion powder, and the 2 g / ¼ tsp Worcestershire sauce.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Taste from a small spoon. Adjust salt by ¼ tsp at a time, lemon by ½ tsp, and any single herb if it reads weak (dill is usually the variable; some people want twice as much). The Worcestershire should be invisible as flavor but registers as a faint umami depth that distinguishes the sauce from a plain sour-cream-and-herb mixture.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Transfer to a sealed jar. Refrigerate at least 2 hours before serving, fresh herb ranch is markedly better after a 2-hour rest than fresh-whisked; the herb oils integrate into the dairy and the garlic mellows. Holds 5 days refrigerated; the herbs slowly lose brightness past day 3.'

notes: |
  Fresh herbs are non-negotiable. The Hidden Valley packet uses freeze-dried herbs that lose most of their volatile aromatic compounds; the resulting sauce reads as a flavored white cream with herb specks but no true herb flavor. Fresh chives, dill, and parsley at the quantities above produce a sauce that actually tastes of herbs alongside the dairy. This is the entire reason to make ranch at home.

  The buttermilk-mayo-sour cream ratio (roughly 1:1.5:1.5 by weight) is the standard creamy ranch architecture. Adjusting up or down shifts the body: more buttermilk thins, more sour cream tightens and tangs, more mayo enriches. The 2-hour rest is critical, same as for all herb-based emulsions, the flavor integration is the dish.

  Worcestershire sauce is the secret umami. A ¼ tsp is invisible as flavor but adds a foundational savory depth that distinguishes the better ranch recipes. Some old-school southern recipes use a few drops of Tabasco for the same role; both work.
