  name: 'The Peruvian canon. Ají de gallina (literally hen chili) has colonial-era roots; the modern stabilized form (poached shredded chicken in ají amarillo and bread-thickened cream sauce) hardened in the 19th century as a Limeño home dish. The dish is canonically associated with Peruvian comfort food and family Sunday lunches; documented across Peruvian cookbooks and restaurant menus worldwide.'
  note: 'The traditional dish uses hen (gallina, an older stewing hen) for richer flavor; modern home cookery uses chicken breast or thigh as the everyday substitute. The pecan or walnut addition is structural (the nuts thicken and enrich the sauce); the parmesan is a 20th-century European addition that has become canonical in modern preparations. The bread-and-milk thickener is structurally African-Caribbean (similar to the bread thickening of Spanish ajoblanco and various West African stews) and reads as the structural feature of ají de gallina.'

steps:
  - text: 'Phase 1, poach the chicken. Combine the 700 g chicken breast, 200 g halved onion, 12 g garlic, 2 bay leaves, 9 g salt, and 1500 ml water in a medium pot. Bring to a boil; reduce to a bare simmer (small bubbles breaking the surface). Cook 20 to 25 minutes until the chicken is cooked through (internal temperature 74°C / 165°F). Remove the chicken; cool until handleable. Strain the broth through a fine-mesh sieve; reserve 500 g of broth for the sauce, refrigerate the rest for future use.'
    technique: 'low-and-slow-simmer'
    time_min: 28
  - text: 'Shred the chicken. Once cool enough to handle, pull the chicken meat from the bones; discard skin and bones. Shred the meat by hand into rough strands about 4 to 5 cm long (the structural ají de gallina texture; not chopped, not diced). Reserve.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Phase 2, soak the bread. While the chicken poaches, tear the 80 g crustless bread into pieces in a bowl; pour the 200 g evaporated milk over; let soak 10 minutes until the bread is fully saturated and breaks apart when squeezed.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Phase 3, build the sauce. Heat the 45 g neutral oil in a large heavy skillet or wide pot over medium heat. Add the 300 g diced onion; sweat 8 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the onion is soft, translucent, and very slightly golden at the edges. Add the 20 g minced garlic; cook 60 seconds. Add the 60 g ají amarillo paste; cook 2 to 3 minutes, stirring constantly, until the paste has cooked through and the oil starts to separate at the edges. Add the 2 g ground cumin; toast 30 seconds.'
    technique: 'sweating-aromatics'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Add the bread, broth, and nuts. Add the soaked bread (and any unabsorbed milk) and the 60 g ground nuts to the skillet; stir to combine. Add 250 g of the 500 g reserved chicken broth; cook 3 to 4 minutes, stirring, until the sauce thickens noticeably (the bread breaks down into the sauce and acts as the structural thickener). Transfer the sauce to a blender in 2 batches; blend each 60 to 90 seconds to a smooth thick puree (the blender step is structural for the canonical ají de gallina texture). Return the smooth sauce to the skillet.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Combine and finish. Add the shredded chicken and the remaining 250 g chicken broth to the smooth sauce; stir to combine. Simmer over low heat 8 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the chicken is heated through and the sauce reaches a thick spoonable consistency (similar to a Bechamel). Add the 35 g of the 50 g grated parmesan (reserve 15 g for garnish); stir until melted in. Add the 4 g salt and 2 g black pepper. Taste and adjust; ají de gallina should read deeply creamy, with the ají amarillo character bright at the front and the nut-and-cheese depth at the back. If too thick, more broth; if too thin, simmer longer.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Phase 4, plate and serve. For each plate: arrange 2 to 3 rounds of warm boiled potato as the base; mound about 150 g cooked rice next to the potatoes; ladle a generous portion of ají de gallina (about 200 g) over the potatoes. Garnish each plate with 1 hard-boiled egg half (yolk-side up), 2 black olives, and a small pinch of the reserved parmesan. Serve immediately; the structural Peruvian eating motion is to take a forkful of chicken-and-sauce-and-potato together, alternating with rice.'
    time_min: 4

notes: |
  Ají de gallina is the structural Peruvian comfort dish and one of the great cream-based stews of the Andean world. The technique (poach, shred, blend a creamy sauce, combine) is conceptually straightforward; the structural complexity comes from the ají amarillo as the flavor pillar and the bread-and-nut thickener as the structural texture base. Master ají de gallina and the technique transfers to ocopa (cold version), ají de mariscos (seafood version), and the broader Peruvian creamy-sauce tradition.

  The bread-and-milk thickener is structural. The crustless white bread soaked in evaporated milk and blended into the sauce is the canonical Peruvian thickener; without it, the sauce reads thin and loses its structural body. Flour or cornstarch produce a structurally different sauce that reads as a Bechamel-with-chili rather than as canonical ají de gallina. The bread also contributes structural carb-density that distinguishes the dish from a generic chicken stew.

  The blender step is structural. The sauce must be blended after the initial cook to produce the canonical ají de gallina texture (thick, smooth, with the bread, nuts, and chili paste fully integrated). A chunky un-blended sauce reads as a stew rather than as ají de gallina; the blender step is non-negotiable for the structural texture.

  The yellow potato base is structural. Peruvian yellow potatoes (papa amarilla, a specific Andean variety) are the canonical base for ají de gallina; Yukon Gold is the closest North American substitute and produces a structurally similar plate. Russet potatoes are too dry and starchy; red potatoes are too waxy. The structural Peruvian plate has a base of boiled potato rounds with the sauce-and-chicken spooned over and rice alongside; this is non-negotiable for canonical service.

  The hard-boiled egg, black olive, and parmesan garnish are structural. The Peruvian plate is structurally complete only with the three garnishes: the egg adds structural protein and visual contrast (the yellow yolk against the orange sauce); the black olives add structural brine and a sharp visual accent; the parmesan adds structural umami and saltiness. All three are canonical and not optional.

  The ají amarillo character is the structural Peruvian flavor pillar. The chili is more about flavor than heat; ají amarillo brings a fruity-floral character that defines Peruvian cooking and makes ají de gallina recognizable as a Peruvian dish rather than a generic creamy chicken stew. Use the structural amount (60 g paste for 700 g chicken); halving or doubling shifts the dish out of canonical proportions.

  Versatility. The ají de gallina sauce architecture transfers to: shredded turkey (excellent post-Thanksgiving Peruvian dish), poached white fish (a coastal Peruvian variation), and as a sauce for boiled cauliflower or sweet potato (vegetarian adaptation). The cold version (ocopa) uses the same flavor base as a dressing for boiled potatoes. Beyond Peru, the sauce works as a substitute for chicken pot pie filling, as a topping for baked sweet potato, or as a structural pasta sauce (toss with cooked penne).

  Storage. Refrigerated, 4 days. The dish tastes meaningfully better at 24 hours than at the immediate cook; the ají amarillo flavor integrates and the sauce thickens. Reheat gently with a splash of broth or milk to restore the original consistency. The dish freezes 2 months in 500 g portions; texture holds well through one freeze-thaw cycle.
