before_you_start:
  - 'Whisk-and-rest, not whisk-and-serve. The dressing needs at least 2 hours refrigerated before service so the blue cheese funk integrates into the dairy and the raw garlic mellows from sharp to round. Fresh-whisked blue cheese dressing tastes like cold mayonnaise with cheese floating in it; the same dressing two hours later reads cohesive and savory.'
  - 'Use a well-aged blue cheese (Roquefort, Stilton, Danish blue, or a domestic Maytag-style) and keep it cold until the moment it goes in. Cold cheese crumbles into clean chunks; room-temperature blue cheese smears and you lose the chunk-versus-cream contrast that defines the dressing.'

steps:
  - text: 'In a medium bowl whisk together the 120 g / ½ cup mayonnaise, the 90 g / ⅓ cup plus 1 tbsp sour cream, and the 80 ml / ⅓ cup buttermilk until completely smooth and the mixture has a thick pourable consistency. If it looks broken or weeping, whisk in 1 tbsp more sour cream to rebuild the body.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Whisk in the 1 grated garlic clove paste, the 12 ml / 2½ tsp lemon juice, the 3 g / ½ tsp Worcestershire sauce, the 2 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. The base should taste slightly under-seasoned at this point; the blue cheese carries significant salt and will bring the dressing up to level.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Stage one of the cheese, the melt-in half. Add 55 g / about ½ cup of the chilled crumbled blue cheese and whisk it hard against the side of the bowl until it breaks down into a fine, gritty suspension that tints the base gray-flecked and savory. This half is what gives the body its blue-cheese flavor all the way through, rather than only at the chunks.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Stage two of the cheese, the chunk half. Switch to a flexible spatula and fold in the remaining 55 g / about ½ cup chilled crumbled blue cheese with three or four gentle strokes only, just enough to distribute. These crumbles stay intact as cold, weighty chunks; the contrast between the smooth savory body and the discrete cheese pockets is the defining texture of a steakhouse blue cheese dressing.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Taste from a small spoon. Adjust fine sea salt a pinch at a time, lemon by ½ tsp if it reads flat or too rich, and black pepper to preference. Transfer to a sealed jar and refrigerate at least 2 hours before serving; the funk integrates and the garlic mellows. Holds 5 days refrigerated; whisk briefly to re-loosen before each use, as it tightens cold.'
    time_min: 120

notes: |
  The two-stage cheese addition is the whole point. A blue cheese dressing whisked entirely smooth tastes like ranch with a funky accent; one with the cheese only folded in as chunks tastes like cold mayonnaise hiding lumps of cheese. Half melted into the body and half held as cold chunks gives you both the all-the-way-through savory flavor and the textural pockets that define the steakhouse version. Skip neither stage.

  Undersalt the base on purpose. Blue cheese is one of the saltiest cheeses on the board (Roquefort runs near 4% salt by weight), so a base seasoned to taste before the cheese goes in will read aggressively salty after. Salt the base light, then correct at the very end once both stages of cheese are in.

  Worcestershire is the invisible umami anchor, the same role it plays in a Caesar or a proper ranch. A half teaspoon disappears as flavor but adds a foundational savory depth that distinguishes a made-from-scratch dressing from a bottled one. The cheese, the Worcestershire, and the cultured buttermilk are three separate glutamate and lactic sources stacking into one savory line.

  Cheese choice drives the character. Roquefort (sheep''s milk) is the sharpest and most mineral; Stilton (cow''s milk) is rounder and nuttier; Danish blue and domestic Maytag-style sit in the affordable middle and crumble cleanly. Gorgonzola dolce is the outlier, too soft to hold chunks, so it makes a smoother, milder dressing better suited to a creamy dip than a chunky wedge dressing.
