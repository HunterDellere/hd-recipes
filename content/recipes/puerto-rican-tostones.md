source:
  name: 'Caribbean canon (Puerto Rican / Dominican / Cuban)'
  note: 'Tostones (also called patacones in Colombian/Venezuelan tradition) are the canonical Caribbean fried-plantain side: green (unripe) plantains sliced into thick coins, fried twice with a smash in between. The double-fry-with-smash technique is structural; a single-fry produces soft plantain coins, and an unsmashed double-fry produces dense disks rather than the layered crispy tostones. Served with mojo (garlic-lime-olive-oil dipping sauce) in Puerto Rico and Cuba.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Use only green (fully unripe) plantains. The skin should be completely green with no yellow patches; the flesh should be pale white and starchy. A yellow plantain has begun to convert its starch to sugar and will not crisp; it will fry to a soft sweet maduro instead. If your plantains are green-with-yellow-edges, wait another day or use them for maduros.'
  - 'Twice-fried means two distinct fry steps at two different temperatures. The first fry at 150 °C / 300 °F gelatinizes the starch and softens the plantain so it can be smashed without shattering; the second fry at 190 °C / 375 °F dehydrates the smashed surface and crisps the exterior. Skipping the temperature differential produces inferior tostones.'
  - 'The smash should compress the coin to about 6 mm thick. Too thin and the tostone shatters; too thick and the interior stays dense. Use a tostonera (a hinged wooden press, available at Latin grocery stores for under $10), or a flat-bottomed glass or measuring cup pressed firmly on parchment paper.'

steps:
  - text: 'Peel the plantains. Cut off both ends. Score the skin lengthwise in 3 or 4 places, cutting only through the skin (not into the flesh). Lift the skin off in strips, working under hot tap water if it resists. Cut the peeled plantains crosswise into 3 cm coins (about 16 coins from 3 plantains). Drop the coins into a bowl of salted water to prevent oxidation; they will brown if exposed to air more than 5 minutes.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Make the mojo. Pound the 20 g / 5 cloves garlic with the 3 g / ¾ tsp salt in a mortar (or chop very fine on a cutting board) until you have a paste. Transfer to a small bowl. Whisk in the 60 ml / ¼ cup olive oil and 30 ml / 2 tbsp lime juice. Stir in the 5 g / 1 tbsp chopped parsley. Reserve for serving; mojo is best made fresh and used within an hour.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Heat the 600 ml / 2½ cups neutral oil in a deep skillet or Dutch oven to 150 °C / 300 °F (use a deep-fry thermometer; the oil should ripple gently and a wooden chopstick dipped in should produce small slow bubbles, not vigorous bubbles). The oil should be about 3 cm deep.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Drain the plantain coins and pat dry with paper towels. Fry in batches of 6 to 8 coins (do not crowd the pan; crowding drops the oil temperature and produces greasy plantains). Fry 4 to 5 minutes per batch, turning once, until the coins are pale gold with a soft yielding texture (a chopstick should pass through easily). Transfer to a paper-towel-lined plate. The plantains are now par-cooked, not done.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Smash each warm coin. Place a coin between two layers of parchment paper (or in a tostonera). Press down firmly with a flat-bottomed glass or measuring cup until the coin is about 6 mm thick (you should hear a soft crack as the starch structure compresses). The smashed disk will be slightly bigger in diameter than the original coin, with rough fractured edges; these edges are where the second fry creates the most crisp surface area.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Crank the oil to 190 °C / 375 °F (chopstick dipped in should produce vigorous fast bubbles). Fry the smashed disks in batches of 6 to 8, 2 to 3 minutes per batch, flipping once, until they are deep gold with shattering crispy edges and surface texture. Transfer to a fresh paper-towel-lined plate.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Immediately season the hot tostones with the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt, sprinkled evenly from height (the salt sticks best to the freshly-fried surface). Serve immediately with the mojo dipping sauce; tostones soften within 15 minutes of frying and should be eaten hot.'
    time_min: 2

substitutions:
  - for: 'green plantains'
    use: 'unripe bananas (only as last resort)'
    note: 'Unripe bananas are a poor substitute; the fruit is smaller, the starch content lower, and the texture more brittle. The tostones come out crumbly rather than crisp. Wait for true green plantains; they are at every Latin and Caribbean grocery and most U.S. supermarket produce sections.'
  - for: 'neutral oil'
    use: 'rendered lard (manteca), or coconut oil'
    note: 'Lard is the older Caribbean canonical frying fat and produces slightly crispier and more flavorful tostones. Coconut oil gives a faint coconut aroma that some find pleasant; not canonical. Avoid olive oil for the deep-fry, the smoke point is too low.'
  - for: 'fresh lime juice (for the mojo)'
    use: 'sour orange juice (Cuban-style mojo)'
    note: 'Sour orange (naranja agria) is the Cuban mojo base; lime is more Puerto Rican. Either works; the lime version is brighter and more accessible.'

notes: |
  Why twice-fried. A single fry produces soft plantain coins; the inside cooks but the outside has the same surface area as before. The double-fry-with-smash sequence does two things: the first low-temperature fry cooks the plantain through and gelatinizes the starch (so it can be deformed without shattering); the smash compresses the cell structure and fractures the surface (multiplying the surface area available for crisping); the second high-temperature fry dehydrates the smashed surface and creates the shattering crispy crust. Each step is essential; skipping any one produces a worse tostone.

  The temperature differential is structural. 150 °C for the first fry is hot enough to cook the plantain through without browning the outside; 190 °C for the second fry is hot enough to drive off surface moisture in 2 minutes and create the crispy crust. Using the same temperature for both fries (the most common error) either undercooks the plantain (if you stay at 150 for both) or burns the outside before the inside softens (if you go straight to 190).

  The smash compresses the cell structure. The peeled plantain is a dense matrix of parenchyma cells filled with starch granules. The first fry gelatinizes the starch (the granules absorb water and swell). The smash ruptures the cell walls and flattens the structure, exposing the gelatinized starch to the second fry, where it dehydrates and crisps. An unsmashed double-fry produces a dense plantain disk with a thin crust; the smashed-and-fried tostone has crispy throughout because the smash brought the gelatinized starch to the surface.

  Pairings. Canonical with mojo, but also with garlic mayo (mayoketchup, the Puerto Rican condiment of mayo, ketchup, and garlic). As a side with pernil, arroz con gandules, picadillo, ropa vieja, fried fish. As an appetizer with hummus or guacamole (Caribbean-Mexican fusion).

  Storage. Tostones do not store well; the crust softens within 30 minutes of frying. To make ahead, complete through the first fry only and refrigerate the par-cooked coins up to 2 days; smash and final-fry just before serving.
