  name: 'Haitian wedding and holiday canon (northern region origin)'
  note: 'Diri djon djon is the prestige rice of northern Haiti, reserved for weddings, Christmas, Independence Day, and special Sunday dinners. The djon-djon mushroom (Psathyrella sp.) is a small dried black mushroom that grows only in northern Haiti and parts of the Dominican Republic; the steeping liquid stains the rice deep grey-black, and the cooked mushrooms contribute a deep umami earthiness. Outside Haiti, dried djon djon is sold at Haitian groceries; the closest substitute is dried Polish or French black trumpet mushrooms (which produce a similar inky steep), or a combination of dried porcini and a small amount of squid ink for color.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Dried djon djon mushrooms need a 20 to 30 minute hot-water steep to release their black pigment and umami. Plan this first; the steeping liquid is the rice liquid, and the recipe falls apart if you steep at the wrong time. The mushrooms themselves are mostly discarded in the canonical version (they are tough and grainy); the steeping liquid is the prize.'
  - 'The whole intact Scotch bonnet rule applies. The chili floats on top of the simmering rice for fragrance only; do not pierce or chop, or the rice becomes inedibly hot. Fish out before serving.'

steps:
  - text: 'Place the 20 g dried djon djon in a heatproof bowl. Cover with the 900 ml hot water. Steep 25 to 30 minutes; the water will turn from clear to deep inky black. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a measuring cup, pressing the mushrooms gently to release the dark liquid. Reserve the liquid (you should have about 800 ml). Discard the spent mushrooms (or reserve 30 g, rough chopped, to fold back into the rice for textural body if desired).'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Heat the 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy-bottomed pot or wide saucepan with a tight lid over medium heat. Add the 120 g diced onion and the white parts of the 60 g scallions; sweat 4 to 5 minutes until soft and translucent.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the 12 g / 3 cloves garlic, 4 g / 6 sprigs thyme, and 1 g / 2 whole cloves. Cook 60 seconds until fragrant.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the 400 g / 2 cups rinsed rice. Toast in the aromatic oil 90 seconds, stirring, until the grains turn from translucent to opaque white and smell faintly nutty.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the reserved 800 ml djon djon liquid (the rice now turns deep inky grey on contact), the 100 g frozen peas, and the 8 g / 2 tsp salt. Stir once to distribute. Float the whole intact 12 g Scotch bonnet on top.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Bring to a hard boil over high heat, uncovered. Once boiling, reduce heat to the lowest setting, cover tightly (use a kitchen towel between the pot and the lid if the seal is loose), and cook 18 minutes without lifting the lid.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Off heat, keep covered and rest 10 minutes. Uncover; fish out and discard the Scotch bonnet (carefully, do not pierce now), the thyme sprigs, and the whole cloves. Fluff with a fork. Sprinkle the reserved scallion greens over the top. Serve hot.'
    time_min: 10

notes: |
  Why steep first. The djon djon mushroom''s value is entirely in the steeping liquid; the mushroom itself is tough and grainy after rehydration and contributes texture but not much flavor to the rice. The steep produces an inky umami-rich black liquid that replaces water as the rice''s cooking medium. Discarding the steep and trying to extract flavor from the mushrooms alone produces a pale rice with chewy mushroom bits, missing the point.

  The 2:1 liquid ratio. 800 ml of djon djon liquid for 400 g of long-grain rice is the structural ratio for an absorption-style covered cook. The frozen peas contribute almost no extra water (they thaw rather than release liquid in the closed pot). Reduce the liquid by 50 ml if using basmati or a less-thirsty rice.

  Why no aggressive browning. Diri djon djon is a quiet, elegant rice. The sofrito is a soft sweat (not a Maillard sear), the rice toasts only briefly, and the cook is gentle. Aggressive browning produces a heavier, smokier dish that misses the canonical clean black-mushroom-and-thyme balance.

  Pairings. Canonical with grilled chicken (poul griye), poul en sos (Haitian chicken in tomato-pepper sauce), or grilled fish. Also paired with sliced avocado and pikliz (the Haitian fermented cabbage-and-Scotch-bonnet condiment) for a complete plate.

  Storage. Keeps 3 days refrigerated. Reheat with a splash of water in a covered pan over low heat. The color remains; the flavor mellows slightly on day 2.
