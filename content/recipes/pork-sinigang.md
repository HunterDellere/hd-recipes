  note: 'Sinigang is a family of sour soups defined by the souring agent rather than the protein. Tamarind (sampalok) is the most common modern souring agent and the one this recipe teaches; older and regional versions use kamias (bilimbi), green mango (mangga), guava (bayabas), or batuan (a souring fruit unique to the Visayas). Each souring agent produces a structurally different sinigang; the tamarind version is the canonical Manila-area Filipino sinigang. The pork-rib protein (baboy) is the most common; chicken (manok), beef (baka), shrimp (hipon), and fish (isda) are all legitimate.'

steps:
  - text: 'Build the pork base. Place the 1200 g pork ribs (and belly, if using a mix), 2500 ml / 10 cups water, 10 g / 2 tsp kosher salt, and 150 g quartered yellow onion in a stockpot. Bring to a bare simmer over medium heat. Skim the gray foam that rises in the first 10 minutes (this is denatured blood protein, removing it keeps the broth clear). Reduce heat to maintain a gentle simmer, partially covered (lid cracked), for 45 to 60 minutes until the pork is fork-tender and just-shy-of-falling-off-the-bone.'
    technique: 'pork-simmer'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'While the pork simmers, soak the 80 g tamarind pulp in 240 ml / 1 cup of the hot pork broth (ladled from the pot) for 15 minutes. Then mash the softened tamarind through a fine mesh sieve over a bowl, pushing the pulp through with the back of a spoon and leaving the seeds and fiber behind. You should have about 200 ml of thick, deeply-sour tamarind extract. Discard the seeds and fiber.'
    time_min: 17
  - text: 'Once the pork is tender, add the tamarind extract back to the main pot. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce and the 200 g quartered tomato. Bring back to a simmer; the broth will go from clear-pale-yellow to a deeper brown-orange and the tamarind sourness will be immediately apparent. Taste, the broth should already read aggressively sour with a salt-savory backbone. If under-sour, add more tamarind (a tablespoon at a time); if over-sour, dilute with a cup of hot water.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the 300 g sliced daikon. Simmer 6 minutes. Add the 250 g sliced eggplant. Simmer 3 more minutes. Add the 200 g yardlong bean and 150 g whole okra. Simmer 4 more minutes. Add the 15 g bruised chili. The total accumulated simmer for the vegetables is about 13 minutes; daikon goes in first, water spinach last.'
    time_min: 13
  - text: 'Taste the broth again. The vegetables release some of their water and dilute the sour-salt slightly; adjust with 1 tbsp more fish sauce or 1 tbsp more tamarind extract as needed. The broth should still read aggressively sour with deep salt-front umami; this is not a subtle soup.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the 200 g water spinach. Push the leaves under the broth surface with a spoon; cook 90 seconds only until just wilted. Pull the pot off heat immediately, overcooking the water spinach turns it to mush and bleeds chlorophyll into the broth.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Serve. Each diner gets a wide shallow bowl with a generous portion of pork, an assortment of vegetables, and a deep ladleful of broth poured over. Steamed jasmine rice in a separate bowl (the canonical eating method is to take a spoon of broth over the rice and eat that bite, alternating with pieces of pork or vegetable). A small ramekin of fish sauce on the side for diners who want extra salt on the pork. Serve hot.'

  - for: 'protein'
    to: 'sinigang na hipon (shrimp sinigang)'
    how: 'Skip the long pork simmer entirely. Build the broth from 2000 ml shrimp stock (shells of 500 g shrimp simmered in water 20 minutes, then strained). Add the tamarind, fish sauce, and tomato; build the broth at full strength. Add 500 g head-on shrimp at the very end (90 seconds before pulling from heat). Vegetables go in 10 minutes before the shrimp. The Visayan canonical version, lighter than pork sinigang.'
    kind: 'protein'
  - for: 'cuisine'
    to: 'sinigang sa miso (Northern Luzon variant)'
    how: 'Add 30 g / 2 tbsp white miso to the broth along with the tamarind extract (step 3). The miso adds deeper umami and slight thickness to the broth; the dish reads richer and slightly cloudier. Common in Northern Luzon where Japanese influence layered onto the Filipino sinigang base; pairs especially well with the bangus (milkfish) version.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'souring agent'
    to: 'sinigang sa bayabas (guava sinigang)'
    how: 'Skip the tamarind. Add 6 to 8 ripe guavas (about 400 g), halved, to the broth at the same point the tamarind extract would go in. Simmer 10 minutes, then mash the softened guavas with a fork directly in the broth and strain out the skins and seeds with a sieve. The broth turns pinkish-cream and the sour-front shifts from tamarind-tart to a tropical, almost-floral sour. The Visayan and Mindanao regional version.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'souring agent'
    to: 'sinigang sa mangga (green mango sinigang)'
    how: 'Skip the tamarind. Peel and slice 400 g of green (unripe) mango; add to the broth at the tamarind point. Simmer 15 minutes, the mango softens but holds shape; do not strain. The sourness is brighter and more tropical-fruit-forward than tamarind. A Filipino spring-and-summer version when green mangoes are abundant.'
    kind: 'regional'

notes: |
  Why aggressively sour is the goal. Sinigang is one of a small set of world cuisines where the dish is defined by being unapologetically sour, not by sour as a balancing note. The acetic-bright Filipino sourness (also seen in adobo) is a structural identity, not a flavor accent. A timid, balanced sinigang reads as a poorly-made vegetable soup; an aggressive sinigang reads as itself. Calibrate the tamarind to where a non-Filipino diner would say "this is too sour" and a Filipino diner would say "this is right."

  Why the vegetable order matters. The five vegetables (daikon, eggplant, yardlong bean, okra, water spinach) have wildly different cook times, from 12 minutes (daikon) to 90 seconds (water spinach). Adding them all at once produces a soup of mush; adding them in reverse order (water spinach last, daikon first) preserves the textural identity of each vegetable. The water spinach must go in at the very end, even 3 minutes in the broth and it bleeds chlorophyll, turns slimy, and dies. Pull the pot from heat the moment the leaves wilt.

  Why whole okra (not sliced). Sliced okra releases mucilage (the slime) into the broth and turns the soup into a thick, gummy mess. Whole okra contains the mucilage inside the pod; the diner bites into the pod and eats the soft interior while the soup stays clear. This is a structural technique known across cuisines that use okra in soup; Filipinos universally use whole pods in sinigang.

  Why simmer the pork before adding the tamarind. Tamarind (and any strong acid) toughens collagen-rich meats and slows the breakdown of pork ribs to tender. Adding the souring agent at the start of the cook extends the cook time by 50 percent or more; adding it after the pork is already tender preserves the gelatin extraction and the velvety texture of properly-cooked pork. The pork goes in first for 45 to 60 minutes in plain water; the tamarind comes in only after the pork is fork-tender.

  Why fish sauce instead of soy sauce. Sinigang depends on the salt-and-funk axis of fish sauce (patis), not the salt-and-fermented-soy axis of soy sauce. Substituting soy sauce produces a darker, more savory broth that reads more like Chinese pork-noodle soup; the canonical Filipino flavor signature is lost. Use fish sauce, even if you usually keep less of it in the pantry than soy.

  How it is eaten. Sinigang is a one-bowl meal eaten with rice. The Filipino eating architecture: a spoon of broth over white rice, a forkful of pork or vegetable on the side, a small drizzle of additional fish sauce (often spiked with bird-eye chili) on the pork for diners who want it saltier. The broth is intentionally aggressive; the rice neutralizes; the alternation of bite and rinse is the structural eating rhythm. A non-Filipino encountering sinigang for the first time often finds the broth shockingly sour eaten alone; the rice integration is what makes the dish work.

  Storage. Sinigang holds 3 days in the fridge. The vegetables soften further on standing; the broth flavor improves. Reheat gently; do not boil hard or the water spinach disintegrates entirely. The leftovers are sometimes pulled apart for paksiw (the pork pieces, with broth reduced to a sauce, served on a separate plate).
