before_you_start:
  - 'This is an overnight steep, 8 hours minimum in the refrigerator. Active hands-on time is 5 minutes; the rest is passive cold extraction. Start the night before you want to drink it.'

steps:
  - text: 'Place the 12 g / 2 heaping tbsp genmaicha in a 1.5 L glass jar or carafe. Add the 1000 ml / 4 cups cold filtered water. The leaves and rice grains will float; press them down with a spoon so they hydrate evenly. Do not skip filtered water, chlorinated tap water pulls metallic notes from the toasted rice that ruin the brew.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Cap the jar loosely (a tight lid is fine but a loose cover prevents pressure buildup if any fermentation occurs during a long steep). Refrigerate for 6 to 8 hours, or overnight. Cold water extracts L-theanine (the sweet umami amino acid) and most aromatic compounds slowly but extracts the bitter catechins very slowly, the result is a tea that tastes round and full-bodied without the dry-mouth tannin of hot brewing.'
    time_min: 480
  - text: 'After steeping, strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean carafe. Press the wet leaves gently with the back of a spoon to extract the last 50 ml or so of tea trapped in the hydrated mass. The brew should be a pale gold-green with a faint suspended cloudiness from the rice flour, that cloudiness is correct; do not strain through a coffee filter or you remove the aromatic suspension.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Pour over ice in tall glasses. The tea is perfectly drinkable straight out of the steep but reads brighter and more aromatic over ice, the dilution from melting ice opens the rice notes. Holds in the refrigerator 3 days; aroma slowly fades after 24 hours.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The 6-to-8-hour window is the key. Under 6 hours the rice notes have not fully extracted and the tea reads watery. Over 12 hours bitterness starts to creep in even at refrigerator temperatures, and the tea takes on a slight tannic edge. Overnight (8 to 10 hours) is the standard sweet spot.

  Cold brew vs. iced brew. This recipe is the cold-brew method: cold water from the start, long slow extraction. The alternative iced brewing (hot water over ice into a chilled vessel) extracts faster, gives a brighter top note, but pulls more astringency. For genmaicha specifically the cold-brew method is significantly better because the rice flavor is delicate and slow to release; aggressive hot extraction overshoots and the rice gets lost behind the green tea.

  Reusing the leaves. The wet leaves after one cold-brew steep have a second extraction in them: fill the jar again with cold water and steep another 6 hours. The second brew is paler and slightly more aromatic-forward; some prefer it. After two steeps the leaves are spent.
