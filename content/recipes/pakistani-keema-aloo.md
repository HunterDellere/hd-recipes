steps:
  - text: 'Bloom the whole spices. Heat the 45 g ghee in a heavy 4 to 5 quart Dutch oven or wide pot over medium heat until shimmering. Add the 5 g / 2 tsp cumin seeds, 3 cracked cardamom pods, 3 g cinnamon stick, and 1 bay leaf. Bloom 30 to 45 seconds, until the cumin sizzles and darkens slightly and the kitchen fills with the toasted-spice aroma.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Brown the onion. Add the 250 g diced onion. Stir-fry over medium heat for 10 to 12 minutes, stirring every 30 to 60 seconds, until the onion has gone from white to gold to deep mahogany-brown. The onion is the structural color and depth of the dish; pale onions produce a pale-and-flat keema.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Add the 15 g grated ginger, 20 g minced garlic, and 10 g slit green chilies. Stir-fry 60 to 90 seconds; the rawness cooks off and the smell goes from sharp to warm.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Brown the keema. Push the onion base to the sides; add the 700 g ground beef into the cleared center. Break it up with a wooden spoon and cook 6 to 8 minutes over medium-high heat, stirring frequently, until the beef has released its juices, the juices have evaporated, and the beef is starting to brown at the edges. Do not skip the browning step; the Maillard reaction on the beef is structural to the depth of the dish, and unbrowned keema reads as boiled meat in gravy.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Add the ground spices. Reduce heat to medium-low. Add the 8 g / 1 tbsp ground coriander, 5 g / 2 tsp Kashmiri chili powder, 2 g / ½ tsp turmeric, half (2 g / 1 tsp) of the garam masala, and the 12 g / 1 tbsp salt. Stir-fry 60 to 90 seconds, splashing with 2 tbsp water if the spices threaten to scorch. The ghee should pool slightly red-orange at the edges (the visual marker of the bhuna).'
    technique: 'red-oil-rendering'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the 300 g diced tomato. Cook 5 to 6 minutes over medium heat, stirring occasionally, until the tomato has fully broken down and the mixture looks pasty with visible ghee pooling around the edges.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Braise. Add the 500 g cubed potatoes and the 400 ml / 1⅔ cups water. Stir to integrate. Bring to a simmer, cover, and cook 18 to 22 minutes over medium-low heat, stirring every 6 minutes to prevent the bottom from scorching, until the potatoes are tender to a fork-poke and the gravy has reduced to a thick clinging consistency that just coats the keema and potatoes.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Finish. Uncover; if the gravy is still too loose, simmer uncovered another 3 to 5 minutes to reduce. Stir in the remaining 2 g / 1 tsp garam masala. Taste; adjust salt. The dish should be deeply spiced, slightly sweet from the browned onion, with the potatoes fully tender and the keema clinging.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Plate and garnish. Transfer to a serving dish. Scatter the 20 g chopped cilantro and 10 g julienned ginger across the top. Serve with naan, roti, or basmati rice and the lemon wedges on the side. Each diner squeezes lemon over their portion; the acid lifts the dish.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why the onion goes deep brown. Pakistani gravies are structurally onion-based; the brown-onion stage is what gives the dish its color, depth, and inherent sweetness. Pale onions (cooked only to translucent) produce a watery, flat gravy; deep-brown onions (cooked to mahogany) produce the thick, glossy, deeply savory base that defines authentic Pakistani home cooking. The 10 to 12 minute brown is non-negotiable. Do not rush.

  The bhuna stage and red-oil rendering. After the spices are added and the tomato has reduced, the canonical Pakistani visual marker is the ghee pooling separately from the masala paste at the edges of the pan, with a distinct red-orange color from the Kashmiri chili and turmeric blooming into the fat. This separation (called bhuna in Urdu) is the structural signal that the spices have properly cooked and integrated; if the ghee is still mixed homogeneously with the masala, the bhuna is incomplete and the dish will taste raw-spiced. Patience here pays off.

  Two-stage garam masala. Adding half the garam masala during the bhuna and half at the end is the structural Pakistani technique: the first half integrates into the gravy and provides body, the second half provides the bright aromatic top-note that defines fresh-cooked Pakistani food. Pre-ground garam masala loses its volatile aromatics within months; the second-half addition compensates by providing fresh aroma at the moment of service.

  The brown-color heat. Kashmiri chili powder is structurally a colorant first and a heat second; the chili variety produces a deep red color with only moderate heat. The 5 g in this recipe is what gives the dish its rich red-orange color without making it aggressively spicy. For more heat, add 1 g of cayenne or hotter Indian chili powder; do not increase the Kashmiri chili (it changes the color but adds little heat).

  Storage. Refrigerated in a sealed container, 4 days; the dish improves significantly overnight as the spices integrate. Freezes well for 3 months; thaw overnight in the refrigerator and reheat gently. The potatoes can go slightly mushy on freezing; this is acceptable. Reheat with a splash of water if the gravy has thickened too much.
