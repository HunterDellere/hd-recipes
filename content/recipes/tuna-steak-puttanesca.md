before_you_start:
  - 'This is a one-pan sauce that uses the tuna fond, so the order is fixed: sear and remove the fish FIRST, build the sauce in the same pan SECOND, then return the fish to finish. Have every sauce ingredient chopped and within reach before the tuna hits the pan, because once the fish is out you move straight into the sauce with no pause to mince garlic.'
  - 'The fish is cooked through, so this works with any fresh tuna steak from a normal fish counter, no sashimi or sushi grade needed. The trade is that cooked tuna dries fast: pull the steak at an internal 55 to 58 °C while it still looks slightly underdone in the very center, because it climbs to a moist medium as it rests in the warm sauce. Past 65 °C it turns chalky and no sauce will rescue it.'

steps:
  - text: 'Salt and sear the tuna. Pat the two 340 g / 12 oz tuna steaks completely dry and season both faces with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt. Heat the 14 g / 1 tbsp olive oil in a wide stainless or cast-iron skillet over medium-high until it shimmers and just begins to smoke. Lay the steaks in and sear undisturbed for about 90 seconds per face, until each face is deep golden-brown. Pull them to a plate while the centers are still distinctly underdone; they will finish in the sauce. Do not cook them through here.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Build the sauce in the same pan. Lower the heat to medium. Add the 28 g / 2 tbsp olive oil, the 12 g / 4 cloves sliced garlic, the 12 g / 4 chopped anchovy fillets, and the 1 g / ½ tsp red pepper flakes. Cook 1 to 2 minutes, stirring, until the garlic is pale gold and the anchovies have dissolved into the oil; this melted-anchovy oil is the savory base, so let them break down completely.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the tomatoes and simmer. Tip in the 400 g / 14 oz hand-crushed San Marzano tomatoes, scraping up the browned tuna fond from the pan bottom. Add the 60 g / ⅓ cup halved olives and the 20 g / 2 tbsp rinsed capers. Simmer briskly 6 to 8 minutes, stirring now and then, until the sauce thickens and the raw-tomato edge cooks off. Taste: the anchovies, olives, and capers bring most of the salt, so season cautiously if at all.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Finish the tuna in the sauce. Nestle the seared steaks back into the sauce along with any juices on their plate. Spoon sauce over them, cover loosely, and let them poach in the simmer for 2 to 3 minutes, just until the centers turn opaque and an instant-read thermometer reads 55 to 58 °C at the thickest point. Pull the pan off the heat the moment they hit that; carryover in the warm sauce brings them to a moist medium.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Brighten and serve. Off the heat, scatter the 8 g / 2 tbsp chopped parsley over the pan and squeeze in the 15 g / ½ lemon of juice; stir it through the sauce around the fish without breaking up the steaks. Taste once more for acid, the lemon should make the whole pan read brighter and saltier. Serve each steak with a generous spoon of sauce over the top, with bread or a starch to catch the rest.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why sear-then-finish instead of sear-through. A tuna steak this thick, cooked entirely in a dry pan to the center, almost always overshoots into chalky territory because the window between opaque-and-juicy and dry-and-gray is about thirty seconds at sear heat. Splitting the cook solves it: a hard dry sear builds the browned flavor and crust on the outside, then the gentler, wetter simmer in the sauce brings the center to doneness slowly and surrounds the lean flesh with fat and moisture so it finishes moist rather than dry. This is the opposite of the seared-rare preparation, where you fight to keep the center cold; here you want it cooked through, just not overcooked.

  Why the anchovies are not optional. Cooked tuna is lean and mild, and without a deep savory anchor the dish reads flat. The anchovies are not there to taste of fish, they melt completely into the oil and disappear as anything recognizable, leaving pure glutamate depth that makes the tomato taste more savory and the whole sauce taste seasoned from within. Skipping them gives you a thinner, more obviously acidic tomato sauce. If you genuinely cannot use them, a teaspoon of fish sauce or a spoon of miso covers some of the same glutamate ground.

  The salt comes from the sauce, not the fish. Anchovies, capers, and olives are all aggressively salty, so the tuna itself gets only a light seasoning before searing and the sauce gets essentially none added. Taste before you reach for the salt cellar; this is a dish that is far more often over-salted than under. The lemon at the end is what makes it read seasoned rather than merely salty, so do not skip the finishing acid.

  Holding. Unlike seared-rare tuna, this dish holds and even reheats reasonably because the fish is already cooked through and protected by the sauce. Gently warmed the next day, the flaked tuna folds into the puttanesca like a quick ragu over pasta; that is arguably the better second life for leftovers than a reheated whole steak.
