steps:
  - text: 'In a microwave-safe mug of at least 350 ml / 12 oz, whisk the 60 g / 3 tbsp ube halaya, the 1 egg, the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil, the 15 g / 1 tbsp whole milk, the 2 g / ½ tsp vanilla bean paste, and the optional 2 ml / ¼ tsp ube extract with a fork until completely smooth and uniform violet.   - text: 'Add the 35 g / ¼ cup + 1 tbsp all-purpose flour, the 2 g / ½ tsp baking powder, and the pinch of fine sea salt directly into the mug. Whisk with the fork just until the batter is smooth and no dry flour remains, scraping the bottom corners of the mug where flour hides. Do not overbeat past smooth; a little restraint keeps the crumb tender. The batter should fill the mug no more than halfway, it will rise to the rim as it cooks.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Microwave on full power for 80 to 100 seconds (1000 W oven; add 10 to 20 seconds for a 700 to 800 W oven). The cake rises dramatically, then settles slightly. It is done when the top looks set and springs back to a light touch and a toothpick comes out with just a moist crumb. notes: |
  Why halaya makes this so fast. A normal cake balances flour, sugar, fat, eggs, dairy, flavoring, and color from separate ingredients, which is why it needs a mixer, multiple bowls, and an oven. Ube halaya collapses four of those into one spoonable ingredient: it brings the violet color, the sweet yam-vanilla flavor, the dairy and butter fat, and a dose of cooked-yam starch all at once. That is why this batter needs almost nothing added, just enough flour and leavener for structure and lift, an egg to bind, and a little oil and milk for moisture, and why it comes together with a fork in one mug.

  Why oil, not butter. Butter sets firm as it cools, which is fine for a cake you eat warm from the oven but works against a mug cake that you might eat over a few minutes. Oil stays liquid at room temperature, so the crumb reads moist and soft even as the mug cools on the desk. If you only have butter, use it but eat the cake hot.

  The overcook cliff. The single thing that ruins a mug cake is fifteen extra seconds. The microwave drives water out of a small batter fast, and the difference between a moist, tender cake and a dry, bouncy, rubbery one is a narrow window right at the end. Start low (80 seconds), check, and add time in 10-second bursts only if the center is still wet. Pull it the instant the top sets and springs back; the standing minute afterward finishes the center with residual heat without drying the crumb.

  Color holds better in the microwave than the oven. Ube''s anthocyanin fades with sustained heat above 80 °C, which is why oven bakes lose 15 to 20% of their violet. A mug cake cooks in 90 seconds, so the pigment has far less time to degrade and the natural halaya color reads brighter out of the microwave than out of an oven. The optional ube extract is genuinely optional here, more so than in any of the oven bakes; add the few drops only if you want maximum violet.
