before_you_start:
  - 'Cook the short-grain rice the day before and refrigerate overnight. Day-old rice is structural, the grains dry out and harden enough to fry as discrete particles instead of clumping into mush. Fresh hot rice produces a gummy chikin raisu.'
  - 'If you forgot and only have fresh rice, spread it on a sheet pan and chill uncovered 30 minutes in the fridge. The texture is still inferior to overnight but workable.'
  - 'The omelet is cooked in 60 to 90 seconds while still soft and underset, then wrapped immediately. Have the chikin raisu fully finished and warm on a plate before any egg hits the pan, you cannot stop mid-omelet to stir rice.'

source:
  name: 'The Japanese yōshoku canon (yōshoku is the body of Western-influenced Japanese cuisine that emerged during the Meiji Restoration of the late 19th and early 20th centuries; it integrates Western ingredients and techniques into a structurally Japanese flavor logic). Omurice is documented from the early 20th century in Tokyo restaurants (the Renga-tei restaurant in Ginza, founded 1895, is often credited with the first popular omurice preparation in the 1920s and 1930s, though competing origin stories exist across Osaka and other cities). The dish became a national childhood comfort food during the 20th century and is structurally embedded in Japanese family restaurants (famiresu), school cafeterias, casual diners, and home cookery.'
  note: 'Two structural visual schools: the classical envelope omurice (the omelet is cooked thin, the ketchup rice is placed on one half, and the omelet is folded over into a sealed envelope shape with the seam tucked under for service) and the modern Kyoto-style tornado omurice (popularized by the Kyoto restaurant Kichi Kichi in the 2010s, where the omelet is cooked into a soft custardy half-set, placed over a dome of rice, and split open at the table with a knife so that the soft eggs unfold and drape over the rice in a structural soft custard fall). The version here is the envelope canonical preparation; the tornado technique is documented in modifications. The defining elements are: the ketchup fried rice (chikin raisu, structurally distinct from Chinese chao fan in that the ketchup is added to the pan and reduced down rather than added at the end, producing a Maillard browning on the sugar in the ketchup and a deeper structural flavor), the thin soft omelet (cooked at medium-high heat but pulled while still soft and slightly underset for the wrap), and the finishing ketchup zigzag (a small artistic flourish that is structural to the canonical visual presentation). Distinct from omuhayashi (omurice with demi-glace sauce instead of ketchup, a more adult preparation) and from omu-soba (an omelet over yakisoba noodles instead of rice).'

steps:
  - text: 'Prepare the rice the day before: cook 200 g of Japanese short-grain rice (about 1 cup uncooked) by the standard method (rinse 3 times in cold water until the water runs nearly clear; cook in a rice cooker with 220 ml water, or on the stove in a covered pot for 18 minutes with a 10-minute rest); spread on a sheet pan to cool; refrigerate overnight uncovered or loosely covered. The day-old refrigerated rice is structural for proper fried rice; the starch has had time to retrograde and the grains separate cleanly under the spatula rather than clumping.'
    time_min: 0
  - text: 'Make the chikin raisu: heat 15 g / 1 tbsp of the 20 g neutral oil in a wide carbon-steel or nonstick skillet (25 to 28 cm / 10 to 11 inch) over medium-high heat. Add the 180 g / 1 chicken thigh (cubed); season with a small pinch of the 2 g salt; cook 4 to 5 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the chicken is just cooked through and lightly golden on the surface (no longer pink at the center, with some Maillard color). Push the chicken to one side of the pan.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the remaining 5 g / 1 tsp neutral oil to the cleared space; add the 100 g / ½ onion (diced) and 60 g / ½ bell pepper (diced); sauté 3 to 4 minutes, stirring, until the onion is translucent-gold and the bell pepper is just-softened. Stir the chicken back into the vegetables. Add the 400 g / 2 cups day-old rice; break up any clumps with the back of a wooden spoon or a flat spatula; toss to combine and heat through, 2 minutes.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Push the rice mixture to one side of the pan; in the cleared space, add the 60 g / ¼ cup ketchup directly to the pan (not stirred into the rice yet). Let the ketchup cook on the hot pan surface for 60 to 90 seconds without stirring; the ketchup will visibly darken from bright red to a deeper rust color as the sugars in the ketchup undergo Maillard browning. This is the structural fried-ketchup step that separates a canonical chikin raisu from a generic ketchup-stirred-in fried rice; the browned ketchup reads deeper, less sweet, and more complex.'
    technique: 'ketchup-fry-and-brown'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Stir the browned ketchup into the rice mixture vigorously, coating every grain in the rust-red sauce. Add the 40 g / ¼ cup thawed peas, 6 g / 1 tsp soy sauce, the remaining 1.5 g / pinch salt, and the 0.5 g / pinch white pepper; toss to combine and heat through, 1 to 2 minutes more. Taste and adjust seasoning. The finished chikin raisu should be uniformly red-orange, with visible chicken, onion, pepper, and peas distributed through the rice, and the grains should be separate and glistening with ketchup. Divide into 2 mounds (about 240 g each) and shape each mound into a football (American oval, not a sphere) on its serving plate; the football shape is the canonical Japanese omurice rice base.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Make the omelets (one per plate, made one at a time): in a small bowl, beat 100 g / 2 eggs with 7.5 g / 1½ tsp milk and a pinch of the 1 g salt for 30 seconds until just uniform (no streaks of white; do not whip air into the eggs). Heat a small nonstick skillet (20 to 22 cm / 8 to 9 inch) over medium-high heat for 1 minute; add 7.5 g / ½ tbsp of the 15 g butter and swirl to coat. When the butter is foaming but not yet browning, pour in the beaten eggs all at once; immediately use a chopstick or rubber spatula to stir the eggs gently in tight circular motions for 5 to 8 seconds (this builds small soft curds throughout the omelet rather than letting it set as a single sheet). Stop stirring; let the omelet finish cooking undisturbed for 15 to 20 seconds until the bottom has set but the top is still visibly wet and just barely set.'
    technique: 'soft-set-omelet'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'The wrap: slide the wet-topped omelet partially out of the pan onto a clean kitchen towel (or directly onto the rice football). Using the towel (or the pan edge), fold the front of the omelet up and over the long edge of the rice football, then roll the entire omelet over so the seam is on the bottom (under the rice). The omelet should fully envelope the rice in a smooth oval blanket; any visible seams can be smoothed with a paper towel pressed gently against the top. Place a clean kitchen towel over the wrapped omurice and gently mold with your hands to shape into a smooth taut football. Repeat with the remaining 100 g / 2 eggs, 7.5 g / ½ tbsp butter, and 7.5 g / 1½ tsp milk for the second plate.'
    technique: 'omelet-wrap'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'The finish: squeeze the 15 g / 1 tbsp finishing ketchup in a thin zigzag line across the top of each wrapped omurice (a steady continuous squeeze in a single zigzag pattern from one end to the other; the zigzag is the canonical Japanese visual presentation and the structural artistic flourish). Sprinkle the 4 g / 1 tbsp chopped parsley across the top. Serve immediately while the omelet is still warm and soft; omurice is structurally a hot dish and the omelet firms quickly as it cools. The diner cuts the omelet open with a fork from the side, revealing the red ketchup rice inside, and eats forkfuls of egg-and-rice together.'
    time_min: 1

modifications:
  - for: 'visual technique'
    to: 'as a Kyoto-style tornado omurice (the modern split-and-drape preparation)'
    how: 'Skip the wrap step. Instead, cook each omelet to a softer set (pull the omelet from the pan when the eggs are still visibly wet on top and the bottom has barely set, about 30 seconds total cook). Slide the soft custardy omelet directly onto the rice football on the plate, centered, draped slightly over the sides. At the table, use a knife to cut the omelet down the center lengthwise; the soft eggs unfold and drape over the rice in a structural soft custard fall. The result is the Kyoto-style tornado omurice popularized by Kichi Kichi; reads dramatically more soft and custardy than the envelope version; harder to execute (the timing is more precise) but visually striking.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'cuisine pivot'
    to: 'as omuhayashi (omurice with hayashi rice and demi-glace sauce instead of ketchup)'
    how: 'Replace the ketchup fried rice with hayashi rice: beef stew (200 g sliced beef sirloin browned with 100 g sliced onion, 100 g sliced mushroom, 30 g tomato paste, 200 g beef stock, simmered 20 minutes until thickened into a demi-glace consistency); skip the rice-in-pan technique and instead serve the omelet over plain steamed white rice, with the demi-glace beef ladled over and around. The result is omuhayashi, the adult sibling of omurice; reads more European (closer to a French demi-glace) and is the canonical dinner-grade yōshoku preparation. Pairs especially well with a glass of red wine.'
    kind: 'occasion'
  - for: 'simpler home version'
    to: 'as a fast home omurice (without the wrap, with the omelet folded on top)'
    how: 'Skip the wrap. Cook the omelet to a soft set (slightly more cooked than the wrap version; the top should be just-set with no visible wet egg). Slide the omelet onto a flat surface; fold the omelet in half over itself into a half-moon shape. Place on top of the rice football. Finish with the ketchup zigzag and parsley. The result is the home-cook fast omurice that does not require the wrap technique; reads acceptable and is widely served in Japanese homes and casual restaurants when the cook does not want to commit to the wrap. The envelope wrap is the canonical restaurant presentation; the folded-over version is the structural home shortcut.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Omurice is one of the most structurally interesting Japanese egg dishes because it sits at the intersection of two cuisines (Japanese and Western) and integrates both flavor logics into a single coherent preparation. The chikin raisu is structurally a Japanese fried rice (uses day-old short-grain rice, the soy-sauce finish, the white-pepper rather than black) but is built around a Western ingredient (ketchup) as the structural seasoning; the omelet is structurally a French preparation (butter-cooked, soft set, wrapped) but is built around a Japanese visual presentation (the football shape, the ketchup zigzag, the small parsley sprinkle). The dish is one of the clearest examples of how yōshoku is not simply Western food imported to Japan, but Western ingredients reorganized through a Japanese culinary grammar.

  The day-old rice is structural and non-negotiable. Fresh hot rice is too wet and starchy for proper fried rice; the grains stick together in a gluey clump and the ketchup does not coat individual grains. Day-old refrigerated rice has had time to retrograde (the starch molecules have recrystallized and the grains have firmed); the result is rice that breaks apart cleanly under the spatula and coats individually with ketchup. If you must use same-day rice, spread the freshly cooked rice on a sheet pan and refrigerate uncovered for 30 minutes minimum; the refrigerator cool concentrates the rice and approximates the day-old texture. Skipping this step is the most common home-cook failure mode for omurice.

  The ketchup-fry step is the structural technique that separates a canonical chikin raisu from a generic ketchup-stirred-in fried rice. Pouring the ketchup directly onto the hot pan surface (rather than mixing it into the rice immediately) lets the sugars in the ketchup undergo Maillard browning, deepening the color from bright red to rust red and converting some of the simple sugars into more complex aromatic compounds. The 60-to-90-second hot-pan reduction transforms the ketchup from a sweet condiment into a structural sauce that integrates with the rice. The technique is short and easy but is often skipped by home cooks who simply stir the ketchup into the warm rice; the difference in the finished dish is immediately tasted.

  The omelet technique is structurally a soft-set French preparation but is pulled at a softer stage than a typical French omelet. The eggs should be still visibly wet on top when the omelet leaves the pan (for the wrap version) or barely set on top (for the tornado version); the residual pan heat and the contact with the hot rice continue to cook the eggs slightly during the wrap or drape. A fully set omelet reads dry and rubbery against the soft fried rice; the just-set omelet provides a structural contrast (firm but trembling outside, soft creamy inside) that defines the canonical omurice texture.

  The wrap technique requires practice but is straightforward. The structural move is the simultaneous fold-and-roll: the front of the omelet folds up and over the rice football, then the whole omelet rolls back so the seam ends up on the bottom. The kitchen towel is the practical aid that lets the cook commit to the motion without burning hands; the molding-after-wrapping smooths any visible seams. First attempts often produce uneven wraps with visible seams or torn omelets; the simpler folded-over version (see modifications) is the structural home alternative for cooks who do not want to commit to the wrap.

  Versatility. Omurice is structurally a single dish (the Japanese yōshoku ketchup-rice-omelet preparation) and does not travel cross-cuisine in the way a sauce-only recipe does, but the technique combines several useful skills that travel onto other preparations. Use the same techniques to make: chikin raisu (the ketchup fried rice on its own, without the omelet; a popular Japanese bento and lunch component); omuhayashi (the demi-glace beef omurice, see modifications); omu-soba (an omelet over yakisoba noodles instead of rice; a structural Japanese street-food preparation); omu-curry (an omelet over Japanese curry rice; a popular family-restaurant variant); chikin raisu doria (the ketchup fried rice topped with bechamel and cheese and baked; a casserole-style Japanese-Western preparation); the soft-set wrap omelet for any rice or noodle dish where a soft egg blanket is the structural finish; and the ketchup-fry technique for any sauce or stir-fry where caramelized tomato is the structural goal.

  Pairs across Japanese yōshoku (canonical), Japanese family restaurant culture (where omurice is structurally embedded as a child-friendly comfort food), Japanese-American (where omurice has become a popular crossover preparation in modern American Japanese-influenced cooking), and contemporary international Japanese restaurants (especially in cities with large Japanese diaspora communities).

  Storage. The chikin raisu (without the omelet) keeps 3 days refrigerated and reheats well in a hot skillet with a splash of water; the omelet should always be made fresh. The combined dish (rice plus omelet) does not store well; the omelet firms in the cold and the rice loses its glistening texture. For meal prep, make the rice ahead and warm just before service; the omelet wrap takes 5 minutes from start to finish and is structurally a service-time preparation.
