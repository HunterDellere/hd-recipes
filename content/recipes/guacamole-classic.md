source:
  name: 'Mexican Aztec-Pueblan tradition'
  note: 'Guacamole (from Nahuatl ahuacamolli, "avocado sauce") is pre-Columbian Aztec; the Spanish encountered it on contact in the 16th century. The canonical preparation is five ingredients: avocado, white onion, green chile (jalapeño or serrano), cilantro, salt, with lime as the brightening acid in modern versions. The molcajete (Mexican basalt mortar) is the structural tool; the texture difference between pounded-paste aromatics and mashed-chunky avocado is what distinguishes real guacamole from a blender approximation. American additions (peas, Greek yogurt, garlic, cumin, tomato salsa, sour cream) are not guacamole; they are different dishes with avocado as an ingredient.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Avocado quality is the dish. A ripe Hass avocado yields gently to firm thumb pressure on the stem end and feels heavy for its size; it should not have any soft mushy spots or stringy brown flesh inside. Under-ripe avocados are inedible (waxy and bitter); over-ripe avocados are inedible (brown and rancid). Test 1 avocado from each batch before assuming all are good; pull the small brown stem button and check the color underneath (bright green is perfect, brown is rotten, yellow-green is slightly under-ripe).'

steps:
  - text: 'Pound the aromatic paste. Combine the 60 g diced white onion, 20 g diced jalapeño, 15 g chopped cilantro, and 4 g kosher salt in a molcajete (or in a wide bowl if no molcajete). Pound with the tejolote (molcajete pestle), or work with the back of a wooden spoon if using a bowl, for 60 to 90 seconds, until the mixture has broken down into a wet aromatic paste with no large pieces of onion or chile remaining. The salt is the structural abrasive that helps the paste form; the pounding releases volatile aromatics from the cilantro and the chile capsaicin that would otherwise stay locked in the cell walls. This step is the structural difference between real guacamole and chopped-avocado-with-toppings.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Prepare the avocados. Halve each of the 3 ripe Hass avocados lengthwise around the pit. Twist to separate the halves; remove the pit with a careful knife-tap-and-twist (or just scoop it out with a spoon, safer). Scoop the flesh out of the skin with a large spoon, transferring directly to the molcajete or bowl with the aromatic paste. The avocado flesh should be uniformly green with no brown patches or stringy fibers; if it has any brown patches, cut them out before adding.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Mash to controlled chunky. Mash the avocado gently with the molcajete pestle or a fork, working from the center outward and folding the aromatic paste into the avocado as you go. The target texture is chunky-smooth: about 70% mashed with about 30% remaining as 1 cm chunks of avocado that retain their shape. Do not over-mash; a fully smooth guacamole is a Mexican-American shortcut, not the canonical preparation. The chunks provide textural contrast and are structurally important to the eating experience.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Brighten with lime. Pour the 30 ml / 2 tbsp lime juice over the mashed mixture; fold gently 4 to 5 times to integrate. The lime brightens the avocado, balances the salt, and provides modest protection against oxidation (the acid slows the enzymatic browning at the surface).'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Taste and adjust. The guacamole should taste bright, salty, chile-warm, with the cilantro and onion clearly present but not dominating, and the avocado as the structural body. If flat: add the additional 2 g / ¼ tsp salt. If too rich: add another 5 ml of lime juice. If too tart: add another mashed avocado quarter. Adjust before serving; the dish does not improve sitting.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Serve immediately. Transfer to a serving bowl (or serve directly from the molcajete; the canonical Mexican presentation). Press a piece of plastic wrap directly onto the surface only if not eating immediately (within 10 minutes); this slows the oxidative browning. Serve with hot corn tortilla chips, as a topping for tacos, burritos, tostadas, or as a side. Eat within 30 minutes for peak flavor; the dish degrades visibly as the avocado oxidizes (the surface browns, the flavor flattens).'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why pound the aromatics first. The molcajete pounding releases volatile aromatics from the cilantro (through cell-wall rupture and the release of aldehyde-rich essential oils), extracts capsaicin from the jalapeño (which moves into the salt-and-aromatic paste before being distributed through the avocado), and slightly cures the onion in salt (mellowing its raw sulfur compounds). The aromatic paste then distributes evenly through the avocado, providing flavor in every bite. A guacamole made by simply chopping the aromatics and folding them into mashed avocado will have aromatic islands rather than aromatic distribution; the dish reads less integrated. The technique is canonical and structural.

  Why no garlic, no cumin, no tomato in the canonical form. The five-ingredient guacamole (avocado, onion, chile, cilantro, salt, with lime as the modern acid addition) is the pre-Columbian Aztec preparation. Garlic and cumin are Spanish colonial additions that taste wrong in this context; the dish becomes a Spanish-Mediterranean avocado paste, not Mexican guacamole. Tomato is a common Mexican home addition (acceptable; the with-tomato version is a different but valid expression) but is not in the canonical molcajete form. American additions (peas, Greek yogurt, sour cream, mayonnaise) are bastardizations that should be politely declined; they make a different dish.

  Avocado ripeness as the dish. Guacamole made with under-ripe avocado is inedible: waxy texture, bitter background, no structural creaminess. Guacamole made with over-ripe avocado is also inedible: brown surface and rancid taste. The window for peak avocado ripeness is about 48 hours; buying avocados 3 days before needed and letting them ripen on the counter at room temperature is the standard household technique. To accelerate ripening, store in a paper bag with a banana or apple (ethylene gas from the other fruit triggers faster ripening). To slow ripening, refrigerate once ripe; the cold pauses the process and extends the window by 4 to 5 days.

  The two-texture principle. Real guacamole has two textures: the pounded aromatic paste (smooth and uniform, fully integrated) and the mashed avocado (chunky-smooth, with visible 1 cm chunks). Fully smooth guacamole (the texture of avocado butter) is a Mexican-American shortcut for sandwich spreads and is structurally different from the eating-with-chips version. The chunky texture is what makes guacamole grip the chip and what provides the textural variation through the bite.

  Lime is for oxidation, not flavor. The lime juice provides modest flavor brightening but its primary structural role is slowing the enzymatic browning that occurs when avocado flesh meets oxygen. The browning is purely cosmetic (the guacamole remains safe and edible) but unpleasing. Press plastic wrap directly onto the surface, or the avocado pit pressed into the surface, to slow contact with oxygen further. The dish does not store well; serve within 30 minutes for peak appearance and within 2 hours for acceptable appearance.

  Storage. Best within 30 minutes of making; acceptable up to 2 hours with plastic wrap pressed to the surface. Refrigerated covered, 24 hours (the surface browns significantly; scrape off the brown layer before serving, the underneath is fine). Freezing destroys the texture; do not freeze. The dish does not improve with sitting; make fresh each time.
