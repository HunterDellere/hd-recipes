before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 90 minute pot: a 15 minute rice rest after the oil-and-salt massage, then 60 to 75 minutes of bare tremor simmer with the chicken. Active hands-on is about 25 minutes; the rest is passive but the pot needs a stir every 10 to 15 minutes to keep grains off the bottom.'
  - 'The oil-and-salt rice massage and 15 minute rest is structural, not optional. Without it the polished grains swell intact instead of fragmenting into the cloudy starch suspension that defines congee.'
  - 'Pre-prep the triple-stage ginger before the simmer goes on: 25 g smacked coins for the pot, 15 g microplaned for juice at the finish, 10 g fine julienne raw for the bowl. Mid-simmer is the wrong time to be peeling and slicing a ginger knob.'

steps:
  - text: 'Dry-brine the chicken: pat 700 g / 1.5 lb chicken thighs dry, rub all over (under and over the skin) with 8 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt and 15 g / 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine. Rest uncovered on a rack at room temperature 20 minutes while the rice prep happens. The cure firms the meat and the alcohol takes off any chicken-blood notes.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Rinse 200 g / 1 cup jasmine rice three times until the water runs from milky to faintly hazy. Drain five minutes. Massage with 7 g / ½ tbsp neutral oil and 3 g / ½ tsp kosher salt for 30 seconds. Rest fifteen minutes.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'In a heavy 5-quart pot, combine 2200 ml / 9 cups cold water, the brined chicken thighs, the 20 g / smacked ginger coins, the white parts of the 4 scallions, and the 8 g / 2 tbsp optional dried scallop. Bring to a hard boil over high heat.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Skim the gray scum that rises in the first 4-5 minutes with a fine spider; this is coagulated blood proteins from the chicken bones. Once the surface skims clean, add the rinsed and oiled rice. Bring back to a boil.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Drop the heat to a steady tremor (small bubbles, never a rolling boil). Crack the lid. Simmer 35 minutes, stirring every ten minutes to scrape the bottom.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'At the 35-minute mark, fish out the chicken thighs with tongs. Pull the bone, discard the skin (or chop it fine and stir back in if you want the rendered-fat richness), shred the meat with two forks into bite-sized strands. Return the meat to the pot and continue simmering. The bones can go back in for another 20 minutes of broth-deepening if you have a sieve handy at the end.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Continue simmering 20-25 minutes uncovered. The rice should be fully fragmented and the body the consistency of single cream. Fish out the bones (if returned), the smacked 20 g / 1-inch ginger coins, the scallop if used (chop it fine and stir back in), and the scallion whites.'
    time_min: 23
  - text: 'Just before serving, squeeze the 20 g / grated ginger through cheesecloth or a fine sieve into the pot, capturing the bright juice and discarding the spent fiber. The ginger juice carries the volatile aromatics that long simmer drives off; this is what restores the bright ginger nose to the finished bowl. Stir in 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy sauce and ¼ tsp white pepper. Taste; adjust salt or soy.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Build each bowl: ladle congee, top with a small fistful of the 20 g / ginger matchsticks, the green parts of 4 scallions, optional 15 g / ½ cup cilantro leaves, a few drops (~2 g / ½ tsp) toasted sesame oil, a final dust of 1 g / ½ tsp white pepper, optional ½-1 tbsp / 7-15 g chili oil with sediments, and 2-3 drops of 10 g / 2 tsp rice vinegar at the rim of the bowl. Stir once and eat immediately.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Why the triple-stage ginger: each form delivers a different fraction of the rhizome. Smacked coins in the long simmer give back the heat-stable warm gingerone notes that build the broth's depth. Pressed juice added off-heat at the end carries the volatile gingerol that drives off in the first ten minutes of any simmer; this is the bright top note the bowl needs. Raw matchsticks scattered on the bowl provide a sharp peppery bite that contrasts with the soft porridge. Each step does work the others cannot.

  Why this is the healing version: the high ginger load is functionally medicinal in the southern Chinese tradition (warming, anti-nausea, palate-cleansing), the broth is gelatin-rich and easy to digest, and the seasoning curve runs gentle. This is the bowl Cantonese parents make when a child has the flu, and the bowl western traditions reach for in the form of chicken soup. The mechanism is similar; the technique is more refined.

  The dry-brine on the chicken is twenty minutes minimum, but you can push it overnight in the refrigerator for deeper salt penetration and a firmer cooked texture. Longer cures call for a lower salt load (5 g / ¾ tsp instead of 8 g / 1 tbsp) since the cure is more efficient.

  Storage: three days refrigerated; the chicken shreds dry out faster than the rice does. Reheat with a splash of water and stir gently. Add the bowl-stage ginger juice fresh on each reheat; the existing ginger note will have flattened by then.
