source:
  name: 'The Mexican central and western canon (especially Jalisco, Guerrero, Michoacán). Documented across pre-Columbian Mesoamerican religious tradition (pozole was historically a ritual dish made with maize and meat); the modern pork-based pozole rojo stabilized in the colonial era after the Spanish introduction of pork. The dish is served at Independence Day, weddings, baptisms, and Saturday family lunches across central and western Mexico.'
  note: 'Three regional schools: pozole rojo (this version, with guajillo and ancho, Jalisco and Guerrero), pozole verde (with pumpkin seeds, tomatillo, and roasted chilies, Guerrero), and pozole blanco (no chili broth, served with the chilies and aromatics on the side, Michoacán). Pork is the dominant protein modern; chicken pozole is an acceptable substitute. The traditional Guerrero version uses pork head and trotters for the structural gelatin; pork shoulder is the modern home-cook shortcut.'

steps:
  - text: 'Phase 1, simmer the pork. Combine the 1500 g pork shoulder chunks, 200 g halved onion, 40 g halved garlic head, 2 bay leaves, 2 g dried oregano, 18 g kosher salt, and 3500 ml water in a large heavy pot (8 L works well). Bring to a boil over high heat, skim off the gray scum that rises in the first 10 minutes (this is structural for a clean broth). Reduce to a bare simmer (small bubbles breaking the surface, not a rolling boil; boiling produces a cloudy broth), partially cover, and cook 90 to 120 minutes until the pork shreds easily with two forks. Strain the broth; reserve 2.5 L of broth. Pull the pork from the bones if bone-in; discard the spent aromatics. Shred the pork into bite-size pieces; reserve.'
    technique: 'low-and-slow-simmer'
    time_min: 120
  - text: 'Phase 2, make the chili broth. While the pork simmers, toast the 40 g guajillo and 20 g ancho chilies (stems and seeds removed) in a dry skillet over medium-low heat 30 seconds per side until fragrant; transfer to a bowl. Toast the 3 g árbol chilies separately for 20 seconds; add to the bowl. Cover all chilies with 500 ml of the hot pork broth (or boiling water); soak 20 minutes until pliable. Toast the 2 g cumin seeds in the same skillet 30 seconds until fragrant.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Blend the chili paste. Transfer the soaked chilies plus 250 g of their soaking liquid to a blender; add the 80 g chopped onion, 12 g garlic cloves, and 2 g toasted cumin. Blend 2 minutes until completely smooth. Pass through a fine-mesh sieve into a bowl, pressing with a spatula (this removes the chili skins and seeds; the strain is structural for a smooth pozole broth). The yield is roughly 600 g chili paste.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Fry the chili paste. Heat the 30 g neutral oil in the same large pot over medium-high heat until shimmering. Pour in the 600 g chili paste all at once; it will sizzle aggressively. Reduce to medium; fry 6 to 8 minutes, stirring constantly with a wooden spoon, until the paste darkens to a deep brick-red and the oil separates at the edges. The salsa-fry is structural; it develops Maillard depth and concentrates the chili flavor.'
    technique: 'salsa-fry'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Combine. Add the 2.5 L reserved pork broth to the fried chili paste; whisk to combine. Add the shredded pork and the 750 g drained hominy. Bring to a bare simmer, reduce heat to low, and cook uncovered 45 to 60 minutes for the flavors to integrate and the hominy to absorb the broth flavor. Taste and adjust salt; pozole rojo should read deeply pork-and-chili-flavored with a bright tomato-and-fruit chili note at the front and a long warm finish. If flat, more salt (start with 3 g increments); if too thick, more water; if too thin, simmer another 20 minutes uncovered.'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'Stage the garnish. Set out small bowls with the 200 g shredded cabbage, 100 g sliced radish, 60 g diced raw onion, 90 g lime wedges, 4 g dried oregano (the garnish portion), 60 g crumbled chicharrón, and the 8 tostada shells. Each diner builds the bowl: ladle pozole into a wide soup bowl, top with a small handful of cabbage, sliced radish, raw onion, a generous pinch of oregano, a squeeze of lime, a sprinkle of chicharrón. Eat with a tostada on the side, broken and dunked. The build-your-own assembly is the structural point of the dish; do not garnish in the kitchen.'
    time_min: 5

notes: |
  Pozole rojo is one of the structural festival dishes of Mexico and one of the easiest of the long Mexican projects (the components are conceptually straightforward, the technique tolerates inattention through the simmer phase, and the dish forgives most adjustments). The two structural skills are the chili-toast-and-strain (which produces a smooth deeply-flavored broth from raw dried chilies) and the salsa-fry (which transforms the chili paste from a raw vegetal puree into a structurally complete sauce base). Master these two techniques and pozole rojo, mole, enchiladas, and most of the deep-red Mexican sauce tradition are within reach.

  The pork-broth-then-chili-broth sequence is structural to the dish. The pork shoulder simmers first in plain water with aromatics; the resulting broth carries pork depth that the chili paste then layers onto. Skipping the long simmer (browning the pork in oil first, for example, then proceeding to the chili paste) produces a structurally different dish that reads more like a chili stew than a true pozole; the gentle long simmer is what produces the broth character that defines the dish.

  The hominy texture is the second structural feature. Properly cooked hominy is tender through but holds a slight chew at the kernel center; the kernel should yield to a spoon but not collapse. Canned hominy is pre-cooked and softer than the canonical version; do not overcook it in the final simmer or the kernels turn mushy. Dried hominy (soaked overnight, simmered 4 to 6 hours separately, drained, added at the same point as canned would be) produces the structurally superior texture and is worth the time for festival-grade pozole.

  The garnish is the dish, not a decoration. The shredded cabbage adds structural crunch; the sliced radish adds peppery freshness; the lime is the structural finishing acid; the oregano adds a floral-camphoraceous high note; the diced raw onion adds bite; the chicharrón adds salty-rich crunch. Each diner builds the bowl to taste, and the structural completeness of pozole comes from the layered tableside assembly. A pozole served pre-garnished from the kitchen loses the structural interactive character that defines the dish.

  The tostada is structural. The diner breaks the tostada into the broth, dunks the broken pieces, and eats them as the broth-soaked carbohydrate alongside the meat-and-hominy bowl. Without the tostada, the dish reads as a soup; with it, the structural completeness as a full meal becomes apparent. Sliced fresh corn tortillas or fried tortilla strips work as substitutes; a piece of crusty bread does not.

  Storage. Refrigerated, 5 days; freeze in 500 g portions for 3 months. The pozole tastes better at 24 hours than at the moment of cooking; many Mexican households make it the day before any festival or family lunch. Reheat gently over medium heat; do not boil, which toughens the pork.
