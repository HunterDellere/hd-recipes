steps:
  - text: 'Stub: build the dough. Whisk the 250 g flour, 2 g baking soda, and 3 g salt in a bowl. Drizzle in the 10 g neutral oil and rub through the flour with your fingers until evenly distributed. Pour in the 130 ml warm water in three additions, mixing between, until a stiff shaggy dough forms. Knead on the counter for 5 minutes until smooth. Cover and rest at room temperature for 30 minutes; the rest hydrates the gluten and makes the dough roll thin without springing back.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Stub: roll and cut. Divide the dough into 8 pieces (about 50 g each). On a lightly floured surface, roll each piece into a paper-thin rectangle, roughly 18 × 12 cm and about 1.5 mm thick. Aim for translucent; you should be able to see the counter through the dough when held to the light. Score each rectangle with a sharp knife in 3 to 5 short slashes lengthwise (not cutting all the way through); the slashes prevent the cracker from puffing into a closed pocket during frying and ensure even shatter-crisp texture.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Stub: shallow-fry. Heat the 800 g / 4 cups frying oil in a wok or deep skillet to 180°C / 360°F. Slide one rolled sheet at a time into the oil; it will bubble vigorously and curl as it fries. After 30 to 45 seconds, flip with chopsticks or tongs; the underside should be pale gold and shatter-crisp when tapped. Fry the second side another 30 to 45 seconds, until both sides are the color of light honey and the cracker is rigid all the way through. Lift onto a wire rack (not paper towels, which trap steam and soften the bottom) and let cool 1 minute before using or stacking. Each sheet takes about 90 seconds total.'
    time_min: 15

notes: |
  This is a stub. To flesh out: the lye-water (jian shui, 碱水) tradition that street vendors use instead of baking soda, the chemistry of the alkaline rise (CO2 from sodium bicarbonate decomposition at frying temperature, plus the pH shift that gives wheat its golden Maillard color and the characteristic alkaline-noodle aroma), the rolling-pin technique for paper-thin sheets (Chinese cooks use a long narrow rolling pin and a rolling-and-stretching motion), the air-pocket problem (if you don't score, the cracker puffs into a sealed bubble and softens once cool), the storage limitation (bao cui must be eaten within 4 hours of frying; sealed in a cookie tin it stays crisp 8 to 12 hours, but quickly takes on humidity), and the canonical thickness (vendors aim for 1 mm; home cooks at 1.5 mm produce a slightly more substantial cracker that holds up to sauces better).

  Bao cui is one of those dishes where the canonical version is exceptionally hard to source outside its home region; this recipe is the version the home cook actually needs in order to make a proper Tianjin jianbing if frozen bao cui is unavailable. The Beijing-style you tiao (fried dough stick) is the common substitute but produces a different dish; bao cui shatters, you tiao chews. Both are legitimate but they are not interchangeable for purposes of the Tianjin canon.

  Failure modes: cracker too dark (oil too hot, or fried too long; aim for honey-pale, not amber); cracker not crisp (oil too cool, fried too short, or rolled too thick); cracker puffed into a sealed bubble (forgot to score, or scored too shallow); cracker soft after 30 minutes (held on paper towels rather than a wire rack, or stacked while still warm). The fix for soft bao cui is a 60-second re-fry at 180°C, which restores most of the original crisp; stale bao cui never quite returns.
