steps:
  - text: 'Macerate the shallot. In the bottom of a large salad bowl, stir together the 25 g / 1 small shallot fine-minced, the 30 g / 2 tbsp red wine vinegar, and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. Let it sit 10 minutes. The acid and salt soften the shallot raw bite and pull its flavor into the vinegar, which then seasons the whole dressing.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Build the vinaigrette in the bowl. Whisk the 8 g / 1 tsp dijon mustard into the macerated shallot-vinegar until smooth. Then add the 60 g / ¼ cup olive oil in a thin steady stream while whisking constantly, so the oil breaks into droplets suspended in the acid and the dressing thickens to a cohesive, slightly creamy emulsion that coats the back of a spoon. Whisk in the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. The 2:1 oil-to-acid ratio reads sharper than a standard vinaigrette on purpose; taste it, it should make you wince slightly on its own, because it will be tamed by the leaves.'
    technique: 'vinaigrette-emulsion'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Dress and toss at the last second. Pile the 200 g / 8 cups of washed, thoroughly dried greens and the optional 10 g torn herbs on top of the vinaigrette in the bowl. Do not toss until you are ready to serve, acid wilts tender leaves fast. When the plate is going out, toss with clean hands or tongs, lifting from the bottom, until every leaf is lightly and evenly coated, 15 to 20 seconds. The dressing should cling, not pool; if leaves look wet and heavy you used too much, dress lighter next time.'

  - text: 'Taste a leaf and serve. Eat one dressed leaf: it should read bright and sharp, just barely on the right side of too-acidic, which is exactly what cuts a rich main. If it is genuinely too sharp, the fix is more greens or a thread more oil, not more time. Serve immediately, while the leaves are crisp and lively.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why this salad is acid-forward on purpose. A standalone salad dressing runs about 3 parts oil to 1 part acid, balanced to eat pleasantly on its own. This one runs closer to 2:1, deliberately sharper, because its job is not to be the centerpiece but to cut through and reset against a rich main: seared tuna, buttered mushrooms, a roasted brassica glossed in oil. Tasted alone, the vinaigrette should make you wince slightly; tasted on a leaf alongside a fatty bite, that extra acid is exactly what scrubs the palate clean for the next forkful. Tune it back toward 3:1 only if you are serving the salad as a dish in its own right.

  Why macerate the shallot. Raw minced shallot is sharp and aggressive and can read harsh and oniony in a dressing. Soaking it in the vinegar and salt for ten minutes does two things: the acid and salt soften and partly pickle the shallot, taming its raw bite into a gentle savory sweetness, and the shallot in turn flavors the vinegar so it seasons every drop of the finished vinaigrette. This is a five-minute-of-patience step that is the difference between a vinaigrette that tastes professionally seasoned and one that tastes like oil and vinegar with raw onion in it.

  Why the mustard, and why whisk the oil in slowly. Oil and vinegar do not mix; left alone they separate into layers. Dijon mustard is an emulsifier, its mucilage coats the oil droplets and holds them suspended in the acid, so the dressing thickens to a cohesive, slightly creamy texture that clings to the leaves instead of sliding off and pooling at the bottom of the bowl. Adding the oil in a thin stream while whisking is what lets the emulsion form; dumping it in all at once gives the mustard no chance to disperse it and the dressing breaks. A jar-and-shake works too if you shake hard.

  Why dress at the last second. Acid and salt wilt tender greens fast, drawing water out and collapsing the cell structure into a limp, soggy heap. A salad dressed five minutes early is already past its best. Build the vinaigrette in the bottom of the bowl, pile the dry greens on top undressed, and toss only as the salad goes to the table. Thoroughly dried greens matter for the same reason in reverse: water clinging to wet leaves dilutes the dressing and keeps it from coating.
