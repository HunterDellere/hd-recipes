Chicken is one of its everyday proteins (kaeng pa gai), and bone-adjacent dark meat suits the thin, fierce broth far better than breast: thigh collagen hydrolyzes into the stock and gives the curry a body it has no coconut fat to supply. The dish is defined by what it lacks, coconut and sweetness, and what it leans on instead, a hot paste and a tangle of fresh aromatics.'

steps:
  - text: 'Bloom the paste. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide pot or deep skillet over medium heat. Add the 75 g / 5 tbsp jungle curry paste and fry, pressing and folding it across the pan floor, for 3 to 4 minutes until it darkens one shade and the oil runs clear and red-tinged around the edges. With no coconut cream to crack, the oil is the bloom medium, and under-frying here is the single biggest reason a coconut-free curry tastes raw and flat.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Sear the chicken in the paste. Add the 550 g sliced chicken thigh and stir to coat every piece in the bloomed paste, letting it cook 2 to 3 minutes until the exterior turns opaque. Searing the thigh in the paste before liquid goes in builds a layer of fond and seasons the meat from the surface in.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Build the broth. Pour in the 1000 ml / 4 cups water (or light stock) and stir hard to lift the paste and any fond off the pan floor. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce, the 6 g / 1.5 tsp palm sugar, and the 3 g / 4 bruised makrut lime leaves. Bring to a gentle simmer and cook 8 to 10 minutes, until the chicken is cooked through to 74 °C and the thigh collagen has started to lend the broth a faint body. The broth should read salty, fierce, and barely sweet, a thin liquid rather than a sauce.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Add the aromatics and vegetables. Drop in the 20 g fingerroot matchsticks, 200 g / 4 to 5 Thai eggplant quarters, 120 g / 1 cup baby corn, 120 g yardlong beans, and the 20 g / 3 to 4 stems of fresh green peppercorns. Simmer 6 to 8 minutes until the eggplant is tender and curry-stained and the beans are crisp-tender. The fingerroot perfumes the broth as it goes, and the whole peppercorn stems plump and release their resinous heat.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Finish off heat. Pull the pot from the heat. Stir in the 12 g / 2 tsp lime juice, then fold in most of the 15 g / 1 cup holy basil and the 8 g / 2 to 3 sliced Thai chilies; the residual heat wilts the basil and releases its clove-peppery oils without bruising it. Taste the broth as a complete system: it should land salty, hot, herbaceous, and only faintly sweet and sour. Adjust with fish sauce in ½ tsp increments for salt and lime by the few drops for lift. Scatter the remaining basil over the top and serve in bowls over the 600 g / 3 cups cooked jasmine rice, or with rice alongside so the broth stays thin.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'The Adjustment: jungle curry should read fierce and clean, not rich. If it tastes flat, it is almost always under-salted or under-bloomed, so reach for fish sauce before anything else; if it tastes harsh or one-note hot, a touch more palm sugar (½ tsp) rounds it without making it a sweet curry. If the broth is thinner than you want, simmer it uncovered for 5 minutes before the vegetables go in, which also concentrates the thigh collagen for more body.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  What makes it a jungle curry. Kaeng pa is defined by the absence of coconut. The forest cooks of inland Thailand had no coconut palms, so the curry is built on water or stock, which makes it thin, fierce, and clean where a coconut curry is rich and rounded. With no fat to carry flavor and no sugar to soften the edges, the dish leans entirely on a hot paste, a generous load of fresh aromatics, and a final hit of holy basil and chili.

  Why thigh, not breast. The thin coconut-free broth has no fat to keep lean breast moist, and the only body the curry can develop comes from the chicken itself. Thigh, with its collagen and intramuscular fat, both stays succulent through the simmer and lends the broth a faint gelatin body as it cooks. Searing the sliced thigh in the bloomed paste before the liquid goes in also builds a layer of fond that deepens the whole pot.

  Why bloom the paste in oil rather than coconut cream. A coconut curry begins by cracking thick coconut cream until its oil separates, then frying the paste in that oil. With no coconut here, plain neutral oil does the same job: the paste must fry in hot fat until it darkens and smells cooked before any liquid goes in, or it tastes raw in the finished broth. The bloom is the one rich step in an otherwise lean dish, and it cannot be skipped.

  Why so little sugar and acid. Jungle curry sits at the hot, salty, herbaceous corner of the Thai four-flavor balance, with sweetness and sourness deliberately restrained. The small amounts of palm sugar and lime here are for rounding and lift, not to make a sweet-and-sour curry. Taste before adding more of either; the dish should read fierce first.

  The role of krachai. Fingerroot is not a generic aromatic here; it is the ingredient that most distinguishes a jungle curry from a thin red curry. Its peppery-medicinal aroma is part of the dish's identity, and the matchstick cut keeps it present as both flavor and texture. If you can only find it frozen or brined, use it anyway; do not substitute ginger.
