steps:
  - text: 'Trim and halve the 680 g / 1.5 lb sprouts, pulling off the loose outer leaves and reserving them (they fry into the crispiest chips). Spread everything on a clean towel and let it air-dry, or pat aggressively dry; surface water makes the oil erupt and steams the leaves soft.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Heat the 1000 ml / 4 cups neutral oil in a wide heavy pot to 180 °C / 360 °F (a sprout leaf dropped in should bubble vigorously and crisp in under a minute). While it heats, make the caramel base. In a separate small saucepan, melt the 70 g / ⅓ cup granulated sugar over medium heat without stirring, swirling the pan, until it turns a deep amber (just shy of the first wisp of smoke); the deep color is where the bitter-edged complexity lives, so do not stop at pale gold.'
    technique: 'dry-caramel'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Stop the caramel. Off the heat, stand back and carefully pour in the 30 ml / 2 tbsp water and the 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce; it will sputter and seize violently, which is normal. Return to low heat and stir until any hardened caramel re-dissolves into a smooth, glossy, pourable sauce, 30 to 60 seconds. Pull off the heat and stir in the juice of one lime (about 20 g / 4 tsp), the 9 g / 3 cloves grated garlic, and the 4 g / 1 to 2 sliced thai chilies. The residual heat takes the raw edge off the garlic without cooking it. Leave the sauce in a large mixing bowl ready to receive the sprouts.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Fry the sprouts in batches. Lower the halved sprouts into the 180 °C oil in batches that do not crowd the pot (crowding drops the temperature and steams them), cut-face down to start. Fry 3 to 5 minutes per batch until the leaves are blistered crisp and dark and the cut faces are deep brown. Lift out with a spider to a rack or paper, and fry the reserved loose leaves last; they take well under a minute, so watch them and pull at the first dark edges.'
    technique: 'deep-fry'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Toss and serve immediately. While the last batch is still hot, tip all the fried sprouts and crisp leaves into the bowl of caramel and toss fast to coat every piece. Taste a sprout against the four flavors: it should hit salty, sweet, sour, and spicy at once; adjust with a squeeze more citrus or a pinch of chili. Pile onto a platter and serve immediately with the wedges from 1 lime, before the crisp leaves soften under the sauce.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why this dish converts sprout skeptics. The deep-fry does two things no other method does as completely: it blisters the thin outer leaves into shatter-light, almost potato-chip-crisp shards, and it drives the cut faces to a deep, sweet, faintly bitter brown. On that crisp, charred canvas the fish sauce caramel lands all four of the flavors the Vietnamese palate balances at once, salty from the fish sauce, sweet from the amber sugar, sour from the lime, spicy from the chili, and the brassica sulfur that reads acrid when boiled instead reads savory against the sweet-salt-sour. It is the single most persuasive way to serve a brussels sprout.

  The caramel is the kho technique. Cooking sugar to a deep amber and stopping it with fish sauce is nuoc mau, the caramel base under Vietnamese braises like thit kho (caramelized pork) and ca kho (caramelized fish). The deep amber color is essential; a pale caramel reads only sweet, while a deep amber one carries the slightly bitter, roasted complexity that balances the fish sauce. Cook it past gold to the edge of smoking, but pull it before it actually smokes and turns acrid-burnt. The water and fish sauce stop the cooking and dissolve it into a glaze; adding them is violent and the steam is scalding, so stand back and keep the liquid measured and ready.

  Dryness and batch size are the two fry rules. Wet sprouts make hot oil erupt and steam the leaves soft instead of crisping them, so dry them completely first. Crowding the pot drops the oil temperature, which means the sprouts absorb oil and go greasy-soft rather than frying crisp, so work in batches that keep the oil hot. The reserved loose outer leaves fry into the best chips in the batch but take seconds, so fry them last and watch them.

  Timing is unforgiving at the end. Fried sprouts begin to soften the moment they are sauced and within a couple of minutes of leaving the oil. Have the caramel finished and waiting in the bowl, toss the moment the last batch is out, and carry the platter straight to the table. This is not a dish to plate slowly or to hold; the contrast between the crisp leaf and the wet sauce is the entire point and it is fleeting.
