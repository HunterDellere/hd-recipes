source:
  name: 'Korean banchan and anju tradition; the Busan and Jeolla-do coastal canon'
  note: 'Pajeon (파전, "scallion pancake") and its seafood-loaded sibling haemul pajeon (해물파전) are the canonical Korean batter-fried discs. The Busan tradition treats haemul pajeon as a rainy-day anju (drinking food eaten with makgeolli, the cloudy rice wine); the Jeolla coastal tradition treats it as a homestyle banchan with a higher seafood-to-batter ratio. The technique sits between Japanese chijimi (slightly thicker batter, less crisp) and Chinese cong you bing (no batter, layered dough); the defining Korean move is the cold-batter shallow-fry with edge-basting oil for shatter crisp.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Mix the dipping sauce (cho-ganjang) FIRST and let it rest 10 minutes while you build the pancake. The gochugaru needs that window to hydrate and bloom into the soy; sauce mixed and served immediately reads gritty and flat, and the pancake without proper sauce is half a dish.'
  - 'Make the batter and refrigerate it for 10 to 15 minutes before pouring. Cold batter is structural; the gluten relaxes, the temperature gradient at the oil interface steepens, and the disc sets glass-crisp rather than bready. Skipping the chill produces a chewy pancake.'
  - 'Pat the seafood very dry on paper towels before it goes onto the batter. Wet squid, shrimp, and mussels release water that steams under the disc and prevents the shatter-crisp bottom from setting.'
  - 'Hold 2 to 3 tbsp of the neutral oil aside before pouring the batter; this is the edge-basting oil that flows under the disc during the last minute of the first-side cook and produces the dark lacquered rim that defines well-cooked pajeon. Without it, the pancake is competent but flat.'
  - 'Serve the pajeon within 5 minutes of cutting. Held longer than that the crisp bottom softens; a wire rack in a 90 °C oven extends the window to 10 minutes if you must hold.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'Mussels held alive until immediately before cooking and discarded if unresponsive'
    ref: 'safety/seafood-doneness#bivalves-oysters-clams-mussels'
    why: 'Mussels are sold live; the shells should be tightly closed or close sharply when tapped. Any mussel that is gaping and unresponsive to a tap, or has a broken or cracked shell, is dead and a bacterial hazard. Discard before cooking, not after. For pajeon, the mussels are shucked from their shells before adding to the batter, so the live-shell check happens at purchase and at shucking time; any mussel whose meat smells of ammonia or appears slimy beyond the normal mantle moisture should be discarded.'

steps:
  - text: 'Mix the batter. In a medium bowl, whisk together the 100 g / ¾ cup AP flour, 35 g / ¼ cup potato starch, and 3 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. Add the 50 g / 1 beaten egg and 200 ml / ⅞ cup ice-cold water; whisk just until smooth, no lumps. Stop the moment the batter is uniform; over-whisking activates gluten and produces a bready, chewy shell rather than the crisp delicate disc the dish wants. Place the bowl in the refrigerator while you prep the solids; the cold rest (10 to 15 minutes) further relaxes any gluten that did form and steepens the temperature gradient at the pan interface.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Prep the scallion bed. Trim the 200 g / 2 bunches scallions of the dry root tips and any wilted leaves. Cut to 26 cm lengths (slightly under the pan diameter; you want a tight raft, not stalks hanging over the edge). For thicker stalks (white base over 1 cm thick), split lengthwise so all stalks lay flat without rocking. Pat dry on paper towels; wet scallions splatter when they hit hot oil.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Prep the seafood. Cut the 120 g cleaned squid into 1 cm rings; separate the tentacles. Halve the 100 g shrimp lengthwise through the back. If using whole live mussels, place 10 mussels in a small covered pot with 30 ml / 2 tbsp water over high heat for 2 minutes; pull as soon as they open; shuck the meat from the shells, pull off the byssal threads (beards) and discard, and pat the meat very dry. Or use pre-shucked mussel meat patted dry. Place all three seafood elements (squid, shrimp, mussel) in one small bowl. Pat the whole bowl dry one more time with paper towels; wet seafood will steam in the batter rather than crisp into it.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Heat the pan. Place a 28 to 30 cm non-stick or carbon steel skillet over medium-high heat for 2 minutes. Add 50 g / ¼ cup of the neutral oil; tilt to coat the pan in a 3 to 4 mm film (the oil should fully wet the bottom and pool slightly at the edges when tilted). Bring the oil up to 180 to 190°C / 360 to 375°F; the cue is a faint shimmer, the first whisper of smoke, and a drop of batter sizzling and bubbling vigorously within 2 seconds of contact. Hold the additional 2 to 3 tbsp oil in a small jug nearby for edge-basting during the cook.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Assemble in the pan. Lay the 200 g / 2 bunches trimmed scallions across the hot oil as a tight raft, all whites pointing the same direction (the raft pattern is canonical and produces uniform cooking; a haphazard scatter works but cooks unevenly). The scallions will sizzle aggressively on contact; let them sit for 20 seconds to take on color on the underside. Then quickly pour the cold batter from the refrigerator evenly over the scallion raft, working from the center outward in a slow spiral; the batter should just cover the scallions, with the dark green tops visible through the batter at the surface. Scatter the seafood mixture (squid, shrimp, mussel) evenly across the top of the batter, pressing each piece lightly into the batter surface so it sits embedded rather than balanced on top.'
    technique: 'batter-frying'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'First-side cook. Reduce heat to medium-high and cook undisturbed for 4 to 5 minutes. The cues for done-on-the-bottom are: edges of the pancake turning dark golden and beginning to lift slightly from the pan, the surface batter setting from shiny-wet to matte (no more pooling), the seafood whitening as it cooks through from the heat conducting up through the batter, and the disc releasing cleanly when a spatula is slid under the edge. In the last 60 seconds, drizzle 1 to 2 tbsp of the reserved oil around the outside edge of the pancake; the oil flows under the disc, fries the bottom edge harder, and produces the shatter-crisp rim that is the signature of properly cooked haemul pajeon. Do not move or peek before 3 minutes; the bottom needs uninterrupted contact to set.'
    technique: 'batter-frying'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Flip. Slide a wide spatula (or two flexible spatulas) fully under the pancake to confirm clean release. In one decisive motion, flip the pancake onto its uncooked side. The flip can be done two ways: (a) directly with the spatula if the pan is wide enough, sliding the disc onto the spatula and tipping back into the pan; (b) by sliding the disc onto a plate, inverting the empty pan over the plate, and flipping the pair together (this works for very large or very loaded pancakes). The hot pan will be slightly drier on the second side; drizzle the last tbsp of oil around the edges. Cook the second side for 2 to 3 minutes, pressing firmly with the spatula during the last 30 seconds to maximize pan contact and crisp the seafood-side surface.'
    technique: 'batter-frying'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Finish and plate. Slide the pancake onto a cutting board, seafood-side up. Scatter the 3 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds across the surface. Let rest for 60 seconds to let the residual steam escape (cutting immediately makes the bottom soft). Cut into 8 to 12 wedges with a pizza wheel or sharp chef knife. Serve immediately on a warmed plate with the 80 g / ⅓ cup pajeon dipping sauce in a small bowl alongside; the diner picks up each wedge with chopsticks, dips into the sauce, and eats while the crisp bottom is still glass-shatter. Pajeon held more than 5 minutes loses crisp; if you must hold, place on a wire rack (never a plate or paper towel, both trap steam) in a 90°C / 200°F oven for up to 10 minutes.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why this batter, this temperature, this technique. Haemul pajeon is a 30-minute dish that fails when any one of three things is wrong: a batter that is too warm or too gluten-developed (produces a bready, chewy disc rather than the shatter-crisp shell the dish wants), a pan that is not hot enough at pour (produces a greasy, slow-setting pancake with a soft bottom), or a flip that is hesitated or done too early (produces a torn or folded disc with raw batter in the middle). The technique is a chain of three commitments: cold batter (refrigerator-rested, ice water, do not over-whisk), hot pan (180 to 190°C oil film, smoking just barely at pour), and one decisive flip at the right moment (edges golden and lifting, surface matte). Get all three right and the pancake is glass-crisp at the rim, custardy-tender at the center, and visually defined by the scallion raft showing through.

  The umami source is the seafood (squid and shrimp contribute free glutamate and the inosinate from the muscle protein, the mussels contribute additional glutamate from the bivalve liquor) plus the soy in the dipping sauce. The finishing acid is the rice vinegar in the dipping sauce, which is the entire reason the sauce is non-negotiable on pajeon; the pancake without the acid reads heavy and one-dimensional, the pancake with the cho-ganjang reads vivid and balanced. The home cook who skips the dipping sauce ("the pancake is enough") has missed half the dish.

  The potato starch fraction is the crisp-determining choice. At 35 g potato starch to 100 g AP flour, the dry blend is 26% starch by weight, which sits at the high end of the canonical pajeon range (20 to 30%). Lower starch produces a softer, more bready pancake (Japanese chijimi style); higher starch produces a more fragile, glass-shatter pancake but increases the risk of the disc breaking during the flip. 26% is the home-kitchen sweet spot for the single-flip technique. Restaurant pajeon often pushes to 35 to 40% starch and uses a one-side-only technique (the pancake never gets flipped, the seafood side is finished under a broiler or with a torch) for maximum crisp; this is harder to execute at home.

  The edge-basting move. The signature texture of well-cooked haemul pajeon is the dark, almost lacquered, glass-shatter rim that frames the disc. This is produced by drizzling additional oil around the perimeter during the last minute of the first-side cook; the oil flows under the disc by capillary action through the gaps in the batter at the edge, deep-fries the bottom edge to a darker, harder crisp than the center, and produces the textural contrast that distinguishes pajeon from a soft pancake. Skipping the edge-basting produces a pajeon that is competent but flat; the move is worth the 60 seconds of attention.

  Sourcing notes. Korean and Japanese groceries (H Mart, Mitsuwa, Han Ah Reum) carry pre-shucked frozen mussel meat in 200 g packs that work well here; defrost overnight in the fridge, pat very dry. Pre-cleaned squid from the seafood counter saves 10 minutes of work; if buying whole, see content/ingredients/squid for the 5-step cleaning protocol. For the shrimp, the H Mart standard 31/40 count head-on shrimp gives the cleanest yield after peeling; defrost in cold water for 15 minutes if buying frozen.

  Beverage pairing. Haemul pajeon is the canonical anju for makgeolli, the cloudy Korean unfiltered rice wine; the slight sweetness and effervescence of makgeolli cuts through the fried richness and complements the seafood. The Busan tradition specifies that haemul pajeon and makgeolli are eaten together on rainy days, the percussive sound of rain through the restaurant window matching the rhythmic frying sounds and the cracking of crisp pancake under chopsticks. Light Korean lagers (Cass, Hite, OB) work; sparkling wine and dry crisp white wines (Riesling, Albariño, Grüner Veltliner) work for non-Korean drink pairings. Rice ales and saisons also pair well.
