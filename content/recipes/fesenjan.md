before_you_start:
  - 'The walnuts need 90 to 120 minutes of slow simmering before the chicken goes in. This is non-negotiable; the walnut oil releases gradually and the meal transforms from a gritty paste to a silken cream over hours. Rushed walnuts make a grainy stew with separated oil on top.'
  - 'Serve over chelow or tahdig (the canonical Persian pairing). Start the rice soak when you start the walnut toast and the rice parboil 90 minutes before serving.'
  - 'Quality pomegranate molasses is structural. Look for one with one ingredient (pomegranate juice), no added sugar or citric acid; brands like Cortas, Al Wadi, or Sadaf. Sweetened or adulterated versions overshoot the sweet axis and the dish reads jammy instead of sour-sweet-savory.'

source:
  name: 'The Iranian khoresh canon. Fesenjan is documented across northern Iranian (Gilan, Mazandaran) cookery, where the canonical protein is wild duck; the chicken version is the modern standard outside duck season.'
  note: 'The northern Persian original uses wild duck and aged sour pomegranate juice (rather than pomegranate molasses); the dish reads more sour, more gamey, and significantly darker. The modern home version uses chicken thigh or whole chicken pieces and bottled pomegranate molasses, with a small addition of sugar to balance the molasses concentration. Some traditional recipes go fully savory (no sugar) and lean very sour; others sweeten with sugar or saffron-bloomed honey to a sweet-and-sour register. The version here lands in the middle: sour-forward with a small sweet round, the most common modern Iranian profile.'

steps:
  - text: 'Toast the 400 g walnut halves in a dry wide skillet over medium heat, tossing every minute, 5 to 7 minutes until fragrant and a shade darker. Cool 5 minutes, then pulse in a food processor 15 to 20 times to a coarse meal (cornmeal texture; not a paste). Do not over-process; the slow simmer turns the meal into a sauce, not the food processor.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'In a heavy 5-quart Dutch oven, heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil over medium heat. Add the 200 g / 1 onion (diced) and cook 12 to 15 minutes, stirring frequently, until deep golden brown with crispy edges. Add the 3 g / ½ tbsp turmeric; cook 30 seconds.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Add the ground walnut meal directly to the onions; stir well to coat with the onion oil. Pour in the 1500 ml / 6 cups water and stir. Bring to a steady simmer; reduce heat to medium-low. Cover partially (leave the lid askew so steam can escape) and simmer 90 to 120 minutes, stirring every 15 minutes to prevent the walnut meal from sticking. The mixture will look gray and thin at first; over the simmer it will gradually thicken, the walnut oil will release and rise to the surface, and the color will deepen to mocha-brown. Skim and discard the oil that rises in the first 30 minutes (this is the bitter surface oil); leave the oil that releases later (this is the flavorful walnut fat that enriches the sauce).'
    technique: 'walnut-slow-sweat'
    time_min: 105
  - text: 'When the walnut base is the texture of a thin pudding (coats a spoon with a slow drip) and the color is deep mocha, stir in the 150 g pomegranate molasses, 30 g / 2 tbsp sugar, and 1 g / ½ tsp cinnamon. The color will shift dramatically from mocha to deep mahogany-chocolate, and the sauce will smell sweet-sour-savory. Taste: the sauce should read sour-forward with a small sweet round and a savory backbone; adjust with more sugar (1 tsp at a time) if too sour, or more molasses (1 tbsp at a time) if too sweet or flat. Add 6 g / 1½ tsp of the salt.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Nestle the 1200 g chicken thighs into the sauce, skin-side up, pressing gently so the meat is mostly submerged but the skin tops poke above the sauce. The skin out of the sauce stays slightly crisp; the meat braises in the walnut-pomegranate base. Reduce heat to low; cover fully and simmer 45 to 60 minutes, basting the exposed skin with sauce every 15 minutes, until the chicken is fork-tender (the meat pulls easily from the bone) and the sauce has thickened further to a glossy gravy that coats the back of a spoon and pours in a slow ribbon.'
    time_min: 55
  - text: 'Taste the finished sauce again; adjust salt (in 2 g increments) and final sourness. The sauce should be deep mahogany, glossy from the released walnut oil, and read sour-sweet-savory with the chicken integrated.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Plate over chelow or tahdig in a shallow bowl: place 1 or 2 chicken pieces, ladle sauce generously over and around, drizzle the saffron-bloom liquid in random streaks across the top, and scatter the 60 g pomegranate seeds. The visual contrast of the bright red seeds against the dark mahogany sauce is the canonical fesenjan plate.'
    time_min: 2

modifications:
  - for: 'duck variant (the canonical northern Iranian original)'
    to: 'with duck legs instead of chicken'
    how: 'Substitute 6 duck legs (about 1.4 kg) for the chicken. Brown the duck legs skin-side down in a separate pan first to render the fat (8 to 10 minutes), then proceed; use the rendered duck fat in place of the neutral oil for the onion. Extend the chicken braise to 90 minutes. The dish reads richer, more gamey, more aromatically deep; this is the canonical northern Iranian fesenjan from Gilan and Mazandaran, where duck hunting was historical and the original protein.'
    kind: 'protein'
  - for: 'vegetarian variant'
    to: 'as vegetarian fesenjan with butternut squash and chickpeas'
    how: 'Skip the chicken entirely. Add 500 g cubed butternut squash and 400 g cooked chickpeas in step 5 in place of the chicken; simmer covered 30 minutes until the squash is tender and the chickpeas have absorbed the sauce. The walnut base carries the dish; the squash adds body and a faint sweetness that pairs beautifully with the pomegranate. A modern Iranian vegetarian standard.'
    kind: 'dietary'
  - for: 'meatball variant (koofteh fesenjan)'
    to: 'with small lamb-and-rice meatballs in place of chicken pieces'
    how: 'Make small (3 cm) meatballs from 500 g ground lamb, 50 g cooked basmati rice, 30 g grated onion, 3 g salt, 1 g pepper, ½ tsp cinnamon; roll into 18 to 20 balls. Brown in oil, then nestle into the sauce at step 5 and simmer 30 minutes. The meatball version reads more intimate and is common in some Iranian regional traditions.'
    kind: 'protein'

notes: |
  The walnut-sweat technique. The 90 to 120 minute slow simmer is what transforms walnut meal into a sauce body. Three transformations happen during the sweat: (1) the walnut cell walls break down and release intracellular oil (skim the bitter surface oil in the first 30 minutes; leave the later oil that contributes flavor), (2) the walnut starches and proteins hydrolyze into a thickening agent (the sauce body builds from thin to pudding-textured), and (3) the bitter tannins in the walnut skins mellow significantly (raw walnut meal in cold water tastes harsh; the same meal after a 2-hour sweat tastes mild and toasty). Skip this phase or shorten it and the dish reads gritty, oily, and bitter; do it correctly and the walnuts disappear into a deep sauce that coats every bite.

  Why the molasses goes in late. Adding the pomegranate molasses early in the walnut sweat slows the walnut breakdown (the acid resists the structural transformation of the walnut starches). The structural sequence is: walnut-and-water first to build the sauce body, then pomegranate molasses to flavor and color, then chicken to braise. Reversing the order produces a thinner, less integrated sauce.

  Versatility. Fesenjan is the most distinctive flavor profile in the Persian canon (sweet-sour-savory-nutty) and the sauce travels well to other applications: serve the sauce over roasted duck breast, drizzle over grilled lamb chops, fold into a wild-rice pilaf, or use as a glaze for roasted butternut squash. The walnut-pomegranate combination is structurally portable.

  Pairs across Persian (canonical), broader Iranian diaspora, Azerbaijani (where similar walnut sauces exist), and contemporary fine-dining contexts where the sauce is borrowed onto Western proteins.

  Storage. Fesenjan improves dramatically with a day in the refrigerator; the walnuts continue to thicken the sauce and the pomegranate-sour mellows into a more integrated savory note. Keeps 4 days refrigerated and freezes 2 months. Reheat gently with a splash of water to loosen; the sauce thickens significantly cold and needs help to return to pourable consistency.
