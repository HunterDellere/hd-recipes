steps:
  - text: 'Equipment check. You need two heatproof pots or jugs (about 1 liter each), a fine cloth bag (a coffee filter sock, a clean nut-milk bag, or a long fabric tea filter, the "silk stocking" of the dish name). A fine wire mesh strainer will work but is the inferior version; the cloth physically absorbs tannins as the brew passes through, smoothing the bite.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Combine the 15 g CTC Ceylon, 6 g orthodox tea, and 3 g pu-erh inside the cloth bag. Tie or fold the bag closed so leaves stay contained, they should have room to expand.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Bring the 600 ml / 2½ cups water to a rolling boil. Place the loaded cloth bag in the first pot and pour the boiling water through and over it. Cover the pot and let steep 5 minutes at high heat (set the pot back over the lowest flame to maintain temperature above 90°C). High-temperature, full-leaf-contact extraction is what gives Hong Kong tea its signature depth.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Now the silk-stocking pour-back. Lift the tea bag (with the leaves still inside) into the second empty pot. Pour the hot brewed tea over and through the bag, the liquid filters through the leaves and into the second pot. Then lift the bag back into the first pot and pour everything back through. Repeat this back-and-forth pour 4 times total. Each pass strips additional rough tannin compounds onto the fabric and aerates the brew; by the fourth pass the tea is noticeably smoother and slightly darker.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pre-warm two cups by rinsing with hot water. Discard the warming water. Add 10 g / 1¼ tsp sugar to each cup.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour 60 ml / ¼ cup evaporated milk into the bottom of each cup over the sugar. Stir briefly to dissolve.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pour the hot tea into each cup over the evaporated milk and sugar, about 240 ml / 1 cup of tea per cup. The tea hits the cold milk and immediately turns the characteristic deep tan-mahogany of cha chaan teng milk tea. Stir once. Taste, should be strongly tea-forward, distinctly sweet, with the evaporated milk carrying body without making the drink milky. Adjust sugar by ½ tsp if needed.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Serve immediately. Hong Kong milk tea is best in the first 5 minutes when the tea is still hot and the milk-tannin emulsion is fresh. For iced milk tea (dong nai cha), pour the hot tea-milk mixture over a full glass of ice and stir until the outside of the glass frosts.'

notes: |
  The blend matters. A single tea makes a fine milk tea but lacks the depth that defines the Hong Kong style. The standard cha chaan teng formula is 70 percent CTC for body, 20 percent finer leaf for top notes, and 5 to 10 percent pu-erh for earth. Many shops keep their exact blend secret; the principle of layered teas is universal.

  The pour-back is the engineering. Each pass through the cloth filter strips additional astringent tannin compounds (catechins, theaflavins) by physical adsorption onto the fabric. After four passes the brew has lost the chalky-dry mouthfeel of a single-steep tea and gained a rounded, silky texture. The brewed tea also drops slightly in temperature with each pour, which is desirable, too-hot tea curdles evaporated milk slightly on contact.

  Black & White brand evaporated milk is the cha chaan teng standard; Carnation works but tastes slightly sweeter and less mineral. The brand matters more here than in most applications because evaporated milk is half the drink.
