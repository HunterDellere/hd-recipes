before_you_start:
  - 'This is a 4 to 6 week mellow, not a recipe ready to use the same week. Active hands-on time is about 25 minutes; the rest is patience and the occasional jar-shake to redistribute the salt-and-juice brine.'
  - 'Equipment: a clean 1 L wide-mouth glass jar with a tight-fitting lid (a Weck or Le Parfait jar with a glass lid is canonical; standard mason jar lids work but the metal reacts slightly with the salt over months, so use plastic mason jar lids or stick a square of parchment between the jar mouth and the metal lid). A small wooden spoon or a citrus reamer for packing.'
  - 'Use only thin-skinned lemons. Meyer lemons are the upgrade (sweeter, more floral, thinner pith); standard Eureka lemons (the supermarket default) work fine if scrubbed well. Thick-skinned varieties (Lisbon, some Sicilian lemons) take longer to mellow and read more bitter.'
  - 'Salt must be non-iodized. Iodine inhibits the lacto-fermentation that develops over the 4-week mellow. Use kosher salt, coarse sea salt, or Moroccan harsha salt; avoid iodized table salt.'
  - 'Hold at room temperature (18 to 24 °C) away from direct sunlight for the first 4 weeks of mellow. After the rind has softened and translucent, transfer to a cool pantry or the refrigerator for indefinite storage.'

steps:
  - text: 'Scrub and cut the lemons. Under cold running water, scrub the 1000 g lemons with a vegetable brush to remove any wax coating (supermarket lemons are typically waxed for shelf life) and surface dirt. Pat fully dry with a clean towel; surface water dilutes the salt-cure and slows the brine formation. Stand each lemon stem-side up on a cutting board and cut lengthwise into quarters from the stem end downward, stopping about 1 cm short of cutting all the way through so the quarters stay attached at the blossom end. The lemon should open like a flower when squeezed gently from the sides.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Pack the lemons with salt. Place 20 g / 2 tbsp of the 200 g salt in the bottom of the clean 1 L glass jar. Take the first lemon, gently spread the four quarters open over a small bowl, pack about 20 g / 2 tbsp of salt into the cut surfaces (getting salt onto all four cut faces and into the center), close the lemon back up around the salt, and press it into the jar. Repeat with each lemon, packing them tightly into the jar; the lemons should compress under the pressure of the lemon above. As you press each lemon down, juice will release and begin to pool in the bottom of the jar.'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Add the aromatics. Tuck the 2 bay leaves, the 1 cinnamon stick, and the 3 cloves between the lemons if using; these are optional and the preserved lemons are equally canonical without them. The Moroccan baseline is salt and lemon only; the spice variants are regional.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Top up with fresh lemon juice. The jar should now have 6 to 9 lemons compressed inside with a pool of released juice at the bottom and the top lemons mostly above the liquid line. Add the 200 g additional fresh lemon juice (from 3 to 4 reamed lemons), pouring it over the top of the lemons to fully submerge them; the lemons should be completely covered by liquid with about 1 cm of headspace at the jar top. If the lemons float and refuse to stay submerged, weigh them down with a small piece of cabbage leaf or a clean stone (which will be removed in 1 to 2 weeks once the lemons soften and pack down).'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Cap tightly and shake. Seal the jar with a tight-fitting lid (plastic preferred over metal; metal corrodes slowly in the salt-and-acid environment over months). Invert the jar once to mix the salt and juice, then return to upright. Place on a small saucer (the jar will weep a small amount of brine during the first week; this is normal).'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Mellow at room temperature for 4 to 6 weeks. Hold at 18 to 24 °C (room temperature, dark cupboard or pantry shelf) for the full mellow window. For the first week, invert and shake the jar once daily to redistribute the salt-and-juice brine and ensure even curing. After the first week, shake the jar every few days. The brine will start clear and yellow, then turn cloudy and pale-yellow as the salt-cure breaks down the pith proteins and the lacto-fermentation cultures bloom in the juice. The lemons themselves will go from firm-bright-yellow to soft, pliable, translucent, with the rind taking on a darker, jammier appearance.'
    time_min: 0
  - text: 'Taste at week 4. Pull out one lemon quarter, rinse briefly under cold water to remove surface salt, and taste a thin strip of rind. Properly mellowed rind reads soft and pliable (you should be able to bite cleanly through it without resistance), deeply salty, intensely lemony with the harsh-bitter notes of fresh pith fully resolved, and faintly funky-floral from the lacto-fermentation that develops in parallel with the salt-cure. If the rind still reads raw or bitter, return the lemon to the jar and continue mellowing for another 1 to 2 weeks. The preserved lemons are at peak flavor between week 4 and month 3; they continue to deepen for another 6 months and remain usable for 1+ years.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Storage and use. Once the lemons have mellowed, transfer the jar to the refrigerator or a cool pantry for indefinite storage; the high salt content and the acid pH make the jar hostile to spoilage organisms. When using, fish out a lemon with a clean utensil (never with fingers; introduces spoilage organisms), rinse off the surface brine, and scrape the flesh out from the rind with the back of a knife. The flesh is generally discarded (it has turned to a salty, broken pulp); the rind is the use case. Slice the rind into thin ribbons or fine dice and add to tagines, dressings, grain salads, roasted vegetables, or as a finishing element on grilled fish or chicken.'
    time_min: 0

notes: |
  The dual chemistry: salt-cure plus lacto-fermentation. Preserved lemons are not pure salt-cured pickles, and they are not pure lacto-fermented pickles; they are both at once. The 20% salt cure draws water out of the lemon flesh, denatures the bitter pith proteins (the white spongy layer that reads aggressively bitter when raw), and softens the cell walls of the rind into a pliable, jammy texture. In parallel, salt-tolerant lactobacillus species native to the lemon surface and the air bloom in the brine over the 4-week mellow, producing small amounts of lactic acid and floral esters that give properly mellowed preserved lemons their characteristic funky-floral depth distinct from a pure salted lemon. The combination is what makes the finished product taste meaningfully different from just chopping raw lemon rind into a dish.

  Why 4 weeks. The pith breakdown is the rate-limiting step. Salt diffuses into the lemon at roughly 1 cm per 7 to 10 days at room temperature, and the bitter compounds in the pith (limonin and related limonoids) only break down or leach out once the salt has penetrated the full thickness of the rind. Standard supermarket lemon rind is 4 to 6 mm thick (rind plus pith), which takes about 28 days to fully penetrate. Thinner-skinned Meyer lemons hit the mellow point closer to day 21; thicker-skinned commercial lemons can take 35 to 42 days. Tasting at week 4 and giving it another week if the rind still reads bitter is the canonical workflow.

  Why so much salt. The 20% salt-by-weight ratio looks aggressive on paper but most of the salt ends up in the brine, not in the finished rind. The lemon rind itself absorbs salt to a steady-state of about 4 to 6% by weight, which is the salinity range that pure preserved-lemon rind reads at when tasted. The remaining salt sits dissolved in the surrounding brine and is rinsed off when the rind is used. The high salt is structural: it inhibits all aerobic spoilage organisms (preventing mold), selectively encourages the salt-tolerant lactobacillus, and keeps the jar shelf-stable for 1+ years.

  The flesh-vs-rind question. The rind is the use case for preserved lemons in 95% of applications. The flesh turns to a salty, broken pulp during the cure and reads aggressively sour-and-salty without any of the complexity of the rind. Some North African cooks add a small amount of the pulp to tagines for extra acid; most discard it. The brine is also occasionally used (a teaspoon in dressings adds an interesting salt-and-funk note) but is mostly discarded with the spent flesh.

  Service and uses. Preserved lemon is a Moroccan and broader Mediterranean pantry staple. Canonical uses: chicken tagine with preserved lemon and olives (the dish that introduced the ingredient to most Western cooks), in spice rubs for roasted lamb and fish, finely diced into bulgur or couscous salads, whisked into vinaigrettes for bitter greens, scattered over roasted carrots or fennel, layered into hummus and other dips, blended into compound butters, or as a finishing condiment on grilled or seared white fish. The amount is typically small: 10 to 20 g of diced rind per serving is enough to season a tagine for 4.

  Storage. Refrigerated preserved lemons keep 1+ years; some Moroccan home cooks keep a jar going for 2 to 3 years, topping up with fresh lemon juice as the brine level drops. The flavor deepens through month 6 and stays good through year 1; beyond that the rind can turn slightly dark and the flavor can read overly funky for delicate applications (still usable for robust stews). White film on the brine surface (kahm yeast) is harmless but should be skimmed off; mold (any color, fuzzy growth) means the lemons were not fully submerged and the batch should be discarded if extensive.

  Why thin-skinned lemons matter. The pith (the white layer between the yellow zest and the flesh) is the source of the bitter compounds that the salt-cure must break down. Thick-pithed lemons have more bitter mass to resolve and take longer to mellow; the finished rind also retains a slight bitterness because the salt-cure cannot fully neutralize the limonin in a 5 mm pith layer in 4 weeks. Meyer lemons have a noticeably thinner pith and produce the cleanest, most floral preserved lemons; for serious cooks this is the upgrade worth seeking out.

  The "supermarket wax" issue. Most US supermarket lemons are coated with food-grade carnauba or shellac wax to extend shelf life; the wax does not interfere with the preserve dramatically but contributes a slightly off mouthfeel to the finished rind. Scrubbing with a vegetable brush under hot water removes most of the wax; for the cleanest result, source unwaxed lemons (organic lemons are usually unwaxed; some specialty grocers carry "for-cocktail" unwaxed lemons).
