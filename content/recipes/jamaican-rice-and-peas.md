  name: 'Jamaican Sunday dinner canon'
  note: 'In Jamaica the dish is "rice and peas," and the peas are red kidney beans (or in older households gungo peas, the local pigeon pea). The structural method is the same: bean cooking liquid extended with coconut milk becomes the rice liquid, a whole intact scotch bonnet is floated on top during the simmer for aromatic capsaicin and fruit notes without piercing heat, and the seasoning is anchored by fresh thyme and scallion.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Dry kidney beans need an 8 to 12 hour cold soak before cooking, then 60 to 75 minutes of boiling to reach tender. Plan the soak the night before, or use the quick-soak (cover boiled beans with water, hold off heat 1 hour). Canned kidney beans work but require the substitution branch, since the bean broth is structural to the dish.'
  - 'The scotch bonnet stays whole and unpierced throughout the cook. A pierced or chopped scotch bonnet releases its capsaicin into the rice and the dish becomes inedibly hot; whole and intact, it releases only the fruit-floral volatiles through the skin. Fish it out before serving.'

steps:
  - text: 'Soak the 200 g / 1 cup dried kidney beans in 1500 ml cold water for 8 to 12 hours (overnight is easiest). Drain.'
    time_min: 720
  - text: 'Combine the drained beans with 1500 ml / 6 cups fresh cold water and the 12 g / 3 cloves smashed garlic in a heavy-bottomed pot. Bring to a hard boil, then reduce to a steady simmer and cook 60 to 75 minutes, partially covered, until the beans are fully tender (a bean crushed between two fingers should mash with no resistance). Do not salt the bean cook; salt before tender slows hydration and produces tough skins.'
    time_min: 70
  - text: 'Drain the beans, reserving the bean cooking liquid. Measure the reserved liquid; combine it with the 400 ml coconut milk to total exactly 800 ml of liquid (add water if short, discard excess if over). The bean broth carries dissolved bean proteins and starch that give the rice color, body, and umami; throwing it out is the single most common error.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'In the same heavy-bottomed pot (or a wide saucepan with a tight lid), combine the 400 g rinsed rice, the cooked beans, the 800 ml bean-broth-and-coconut liquid, the 4 g / 6 to 8 thyme sprigs, the 50 g smashed whole scallions, the 12 g / 3 cloves smashed garlic, the 3 g / 1 tsp whole allspice berries, and the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt. Stir once to distribute. Float the whole intact 12 g scotch bonnet on top; do not push it down, do not pierce it.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Bring to a hard boil over high heat, uncovered. Once boiling, reduce heat to the lowest setting, cover tightly (use a kitchen towel under the lid if the seal is loose), and cook 18 to 20 minutes without lifting the lid. The rice is steaming under the lid; lifting the lid releases the steam and produces unevenly cooked rice.'
    time_min: 20
  - text: 'Off heat, keep covered and rest 10 minutes; this finishes the rice with residual steam and gives the grains time to firm up so they fluff cleanly rather than crushing into starch. Uncover, fish out and discard the scotch bonnet (carefully, with a spoon, do not pierce it now either), the thyme sprigs, the scallions, and the whole allspice berries. Fluff the rice with a fork. Serve immediately as a side.'
    time_min: 10

modifications:
  - for: 'the bean'
    to: 'as Jamaican rice and gungo peas (the older canonical version)'
    how: 'Replace the 200 g dried kidney beans with 200 g dried pigeon peas (gungo peas). Pigeon peas cook in 45 to 55 minutes rather than 70, and have a slightly grassier, less sweet flavor than kidney beans. This is the older Jamaican canonical version; kidney beans replaced gungo peas in many home kitchens in the 20th century because they were more widely available.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'the canned-bean shortcut'
    to: 'as same-day rice and peas with canned kidney beans'
    how: 'Skip the bean soak and cook. Use 1 can (~440 g) drained kidney beans plus the canning liquid extended with vegetable stock or water to total 400 ml (this replaces the bean broth in step 3). The dish reads slightly less dense and slightly less umami than the dried-bean version, acceptable for weeknight cooking.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Whole, unpierced scotch bonnet. The scotch bonnet is the fragrance instrument of the dish, not the heat instrument. The chili sits intact on top of the rice; the steam and gentle heat draw out the fruit-floral aromatics (citrus, apricot, mango notes from the chili's distinctive aroma compounds) through the skin without rupturing the seed-and-placenta capsaicin reservoir. A pierced chili turns the dish inedibly hot in 5 minutes.

  Why reuse the bean broth. The bean cooking liquid contains dissolved bean proteins, oligosaccharides, and starch. These give the rice its characteristic pink-brown color, its slightly creamy body, and a bass-note umami that water plus coconut milk cannot replicate. The most common home shortcut, draining the beans and using fresh water for the rice, produces a paler, blander dish.

  The 800 ml liquid target. This is a 2:1 liquid-to-rice ratio by volume, which is correct for parboiled long-grain rice in a covered pot. Reduce to 750 ml if using basmati (basmati needs slightly less water). Do not exceed 850 ml; the rice goes mushy.

  Storage. Keeps 3 days refrigerated. Reheat with a splash of water in a covered pan over low heat, or microwave covered with a damp paper towel.
