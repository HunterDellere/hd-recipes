source:
  name: 'The Yemenite Jewish canon, brought to Israel through the mid-20th-century Yemenite Jewish diaspora migration (Operation Magic Carpet, 1949 to 1950); the sauce is older than the documentation in Israel and is structurally embedded in Yemenite Jewish home cookery from the 18th century or earlier. The Yemenite name is skhug (or s''hug, sahawiq, sahawik); the Israeli pronunciation zhoug became the international reference as Israeli cookery spread globally. The dish is one of the most successful examples of a regional ethnic condiment becoming a national Israeli icon and then an international staple.'
  note: 'Two structural schools: green zhoug (zhoug yarok, the most international form, with green chili and packed fresh cilantro and parsley) and red zhoug (zhoug adom, with dried red chili, tomato, and the same herb-and-spice base). The version here is the green canonical form. The Yemenite original is mortar-pounded; the modern food-processor preparation is the widespread home and restaurant technique. Distinct from Mexican green salsas (tomatillo-based, different acid profile) and from chimichurri (vinegar-based, different aromatic logic with oregano instead of cardamom-coriander-cumin); zhoug is structurally a Yemenite spice paste in a fresh herb suspension, with the cardamom-cumin-coriander signature being the defining flavor.'

steps:
  - text: 'In a small dry skillet over medium heat, toast the 2 g / 6 to 8 cracked cardamom pods (seeds extracted), 3 g / 1½ tsp cumin seeds, 3 g / 1½ tsp coriander seeds, and 0.5 g / 2 whole cloves for 60 to 90 seconds, shaking the pan continuously, until fragrant and slightly darker. Transfer to a spice grinder or mortar and grind to a fine even powder; the toast deepens the volatile aromatic compounds and integrates the spices into a single unified flavor.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'In the bowl of a food processor (4 to 7 cup capacity), combine the 60 g / 1½ cups cilantro, 40 g / 1 cup parsley, 60 g / 4 to 5 serrano chilies (chopped), 15 g / 3 to 4 garlic cloves, and the ground spice mix. Pulse 10 to 12 times in 1-second bursts, scraping the sides between pulses, until the herbs are reduced to a uniform coarse-medium chop with no visible whole leaves but with structural texture preserved (about the consistency of a coarse paste, not a smooth puree).'
    technique: 'knife-chop-not-processor'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add the 6 g / 1 tsp salt and 20 g / 4 tsp lemon juice; pulse 3 to 4 more times to integrate. With the motor running on continuous low, drizzle in the 90 g / ⅓ cup plus 1 tbsp olive oil through the feed tube in a slow steady stream; the oil incorporates into the herb-and-chili suspension over about 15 seconds and the sauce takes on a glossy unified appearance. Do not over-process; zhoug is meant to retain visible herb structure, not become a smooth slurry.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Transfer to a small bowl. Taste and adjust: zhoug should read aggressively herbal with structural chili heat, deep cardamom-cumin-coriander warm spice, gentle garlic, and a clean lemon-and-salt finish; if flat, an extra 1 g / pinch of fine sea salt and 5 g / 1 tsp lemon juice; if too sharp from the chilies, more cilantro and parsley; if the spices are muted, the grind was insufficient (regrind the spices 30 more seconds and re-fold into the sauce). Let rest 30 minutes at room temperature for the flavors to integrate; the rest is structural for depth.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Serve as a condiment alongside hummus and tahini (a spoonful spooned onto the surface of a bowl of hummus, with a swirl of olive oil and a sprinkle of paprika; the canonical Israeli mezze service), grilled meat (lamb kebabs, kafta, grilled chicken, beef shawarma), grilled or roasted fish (especially salmon, branzino, mackerel, sardines); as a stir-in for soups (chickpea, lentil, vegetable, white bean; a teaspoon stirred into each bowl at service brings structural herbal-and-spice depth); as a spread for sandwiches and wraps (especially with grilled chicken, lamb, hummus, falafel, roasted vegetables); as a topping for eggs (fried eggs, shakshuka, omelets, soft-boiled eggs; the eggs-and-zhoug pairing is canonical across modern Israeli breakfast cookery); as a sauce for falafel (the canonical pairing alongside tahini sauce); as a finish for grain and rice bowls; as a flavor base for marinades (mix 30 g zhoug with 30 g olive oil and 15 g lemon juice); and as a raw counterpoint to slow-cooked or rich dishes (a spoonful on a bowl of lamb stew, a roast chicken, a slow-braised vegetable dish brings the structural raw herbal lift that the long-cooked dish lacks).'

The result is structurally red zhoug, the warm-weather and tomato-leaning Yemenite cousin of the green version; reads deeper and more chili-forward, less herbal-fresh; pairs especially well with grilled lamb, slow-cooked stews, and rice dishes.'

notes: |
  Zhoug is one of the most aromatically distinctive sauces in any cuisine because of the cardamom. The single defining ingredient that distinguishes Yemenite zhoug from every other green chili-and-herb sauce in the world (Mexican salsa verde, Argentine chimichurri verde, Italian salsa verde, Indian green chutney, North African chermoula) is the green cardamom. The slight floral-eucalyptus-camphor note of cardamom, paired with the warm cumin and coriander, gives zhoug its characteristic Yemenite warmth that no other sauce quite reproduces. Without the cardamom, zhoug is a green chili-and-herb sauce; with it, it is unmistakably Yemenite.

  The spice toast is structural. Cardamom, cumin, coriander, and cloves toasted whole in a dry pan and then ground produce a meaningfully more aromatic spice base than pre-ground spices or untoasted whole spices ground at the same moment; the toast deepens the volatile aromatic compounds and integrates the spices into a unified flavor. The 60-second toast is short but non-negotiable; pre-ground spices in zhoug produce a sauce that is recognizable but reads flatter and less aromatic.

  The texture is structural. Zhoug is meant to be a coarse paste with visible herb structure, not a smooth puree. Over-processing in the food processor (more than 12 pulses, or running continuously) reduces the herbs to a slurry that loses textural integrity and oxidizes faster; the coarse-chop texture preserves the bright green color and the structural integrity of the herb leaves. Some Yemenite cooks still use a mortar instead of a processor; the mortar version is even more textural and is the most traditional preparation.

  The chili-and-herb ratio matters. Canonical zhoug is structurally chili-forward, with the herbs supporting the chili rather than dominating it. The 60 g chili to 100 g combined herbs (60 g cilantro plus 40 g parsley) ratio produces a sauce that reads structurally hot and herbal in balance; reducing the chili below 30 g produces a herb sauce that has lost its zhoug character, while increasing the chili above 90 g produces an aggressive chili paste that overwhelms applications. The 60 g chili amount is the structural reference point.

  Versatility. Zhoug is one of the most cuisine-flexible green chili-herb sauces in the global pantry, and the only one that combines the structural Yemenite cardamom-cumin-coriander base with fresh chili heat and fresh herbs. Use it as: the canonical condiment for hummus (a spoonful spooned onto the surface of a bowl of hummus, with a swirl of olive oil and a sprinkle of paprika; the canonical Israeli mezze service); a sauce for falafel (the canonical pairing alongside tahini sauce; the chili-and-herb cuts the rich tahini); a sauce for grilled and roasted meat (lamb kebabs, kafta, grilled chicken, beef shawarma, roast lamb, grilled steak; the bright herbal-spicy cut against the rich grilled protein); a sauce for grilled and roasted fish (especially oily fish like salmon, mackerel, sardines; and white fish like branzino, sea bass, halibut); a stir-in for soups (chickpea, lentil, vegetable, white bean, tomato; a teaspoon stirred into each bowl at service brings structural herbal-and-spice depth that finishes the soup); a topping for eggs (fried eggs, shakshuka, omelets, soft-boiled eggs; the eggs-and-zhoug pairing is canonical across modern Israeli breakfast cookery); a spread for sandwiches and wraps (chicken shawarma wraps, lamb pita pockets, halloumi sandwiches, grilled vegetable wraps); a finishing drizzle for grain bowls (farro, freekeh, bulgur, brown rice) with roasted vegetables; a marinade for grilled chicken or fish (mix 30 g zhoug with 30 g olive oil and 15 g lemon juice; the chili-and-herb crust grills beautifully); a condiment for cheese (especially with feta, halloumi, fresh mozzarella, ricotta; the bright herbal-spicy cuts the rich dairy); a stir-in for yogurt to make a spicy yogurt sauce (fold 30 g zhoug into 100 g Greek yogurt for a herb-yogurt sauce); and as a raw counterpoint to slow-cooked or rich dishes (a spoonful on a bowl of lamb stew, a roast chicken, a slow-braised vegetable dish brings the structural raw herbal lift that the long-cooked dish lacks).

  Pairs across Yemenite (canonical), Israeli (where zhoug has become a national condiment), broader Levantine, modern Middle Eastern cookery, contemporary American Middle Eastern and Mediterranean cooking, and modern European cookery (especially British, French, and Italian kitchens where zhoug has been deeply integrated through chef cooking over the past decade). The cardamom-forward signature reads slightly less natural on dishes that already have a strong eastern Mediterranean or North African spice register (Persian, Indian, Moroccan); pair zhoug with cuisines that benefit from a structurally distinct aromatic counterpoint, rather than cuisines that already share its aromatic vocabulary.

  Storage. Keeps 4 to 5 days refrigerated in a sealed glass jar with a thin film of olive oil over the surface (the oil seal prevents oxidation; without it the sauce darkens visibly within 24 hours and loses its bright green color). Bring to room temperature 20 minutes before service; the cold sauce reads dull, the room-temperature sauce reads vibrant. Freezes well in small portions (ice cube trays); thaw at room temperature and re-fold to restore the texture. Frozen zhoug keeps 3 months and is a meaningful improvement over the no-zhoug winter alternative; fresh cilantro is less reliable in winter and the freezer is a useful preservation strategy.
