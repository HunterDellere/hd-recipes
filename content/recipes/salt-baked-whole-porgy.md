source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Mediterranean salt-crust whole-fish canon, the pesce al sale of coastal Italy, the orata al sale of the Adriatic, and the Spanish pescado a la sal.'
  note: 'The dish is a single technique executed precisely: a thick crust of coarse sea salt bound with egg white is packed around a whole gutted fish and baked. The crust does not salt the fish; left whole and unscaled, the skin and scales block salt migration into the flesh. What the crust does is insulate and seal. It hardens into a rigid clay-like shell that holds the fish in a pocket of its own escaping steam, so the heat arriving at the flesh is gentle, even, and self-basting rather than the dry radiant blast of an open oven. The result is a fish cooked edge to spine at a near-uniform temperature, pulled at just-set, with nothing to dry it out. The only real failure modes are a wet fish or a wet crust that steams loose instead of setting, an under-preheated oven that lets the crust soften before it locks, and overshooting the pull temperature.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Three things must be ready before the crust goes on, and all three are non-negotiable. Whip the egg whites to soft peaks so the salt packs into a moldable, cohesive crust rather than a loose pile that sloughs off. Pat the porgy bone-dry inside and out, since surface water on the fish keeps the crust from sealing against the skin and lets steam escape. And bring the oven fully to 220 C / 425 F before the fish goes in, because a crust that bakes up from a cold start softens and slumps before it can harden into a rigid shell.'
  - 'Leave the fish whole and unscaled, gutted only. The intact skin and scales are the barrier that stops the salt crust from penetrating and over-salting the flesh; a scaled fish in a salt crust comes out aggressively salty. Ask the counter to gut the fish and remove the gills but to leave it unscaled, and verify both.'
  - 'Have a serving platter, a small heavy spoon or the back of a knife to crack the crust, and a pastry brush ready before baking. The crust is cracked and the loose salt brushed away tableside while the fish is hot; a fumbled crack drives salt shards into the flesh, so clear the workspace first.'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the oven to 220 C / 425 F with a rack in the center, and let it come fully up to temperature before the fish goes in. The crust has to meet a hot oven so it sets and hardens quickly; a crust that bakes up from a cold or under-heated oven softens and slumps before it locks into a rigid shell. Line a sheet pan with parchment.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Prep the porgy. Confirm the counter gutted and de-gilled the 650 g / 1 whole fish but left it unscaled; the scales are the barrier that keeps the 1300 g salt crust from over-salting the flesh. Rinse inside and out and scrape any dark blood line from the spine cavity with the back of a small spoon, since the blood line is the strongest source of fishy flavor. Pat the fish completely dry inside and out with paper towels. A wet fish keeps the crust from sealing against the skin and lets steam leak out, so dry is non-negotiable here.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Stuff the cavity. Tuck the thin lemon rounds (from half the 60 g lemon), the 4 whole thyme sprigs (half the 6 g), the fennel fronds and 2 shavings (from the 40 g fennel bulb), and the 2 smashed garlic cloves (9 g) into the cavity. These aromatics perfume the flesh gently from the inside as the fish steams inside the crust; they do not need to be packed tight, just laid along the cavity.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Whip the egg whites. Beat the 105 g / 3 egg whites to soft peaks, by hand or with a mixer; you want them billowy and holding a soft droop, not stiff and dry. The whipped whites give the salt enough air and protein to pack into a cohesive, moldable crust rather than a loose pile. Whipping also means you use less binder, so the crust stays rigid rather than wet.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Build the salt mixture. In a large bowl, combine the 1300 g / 4½ cups coarse sea salt, the 3 g / 1 tsp crushed fennel seed, the lemon zest strips (from the zested half of the 60 g lemon), and the thyme leaves stripped from the remaining 4 sprigs (from the 6 g thyme). Fold in the whipped egg whites until the salt has the texture of packable wet sand that clumps when squeezed. If it is still too dry and crumbly to clump, work in the 30 g / 2 tbsp cold water a little at a time until it just holds together.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Pack the crust. Spread a 1 cm / ½-inch bed of the salt mixture on the parchment in the shape of the fish, and lay the stuffed porgy on top. Pack the remaining salt mixture firmly over and around the fish, mounding it 1 to 1.5 cm / ½ to ¾ inch thick everywhere and pressing it into a smooth, sealed dome; leave the tail fin poking out as a handle and a doneness probe point if you like. Press out any gaps. The shell must be continuous, since a crack or a thin spot vents the steam the technique depends on.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Bake the fish. Slide the sheet pan into the fully heated 220 C / 425 F oven and bake 25 to 32 minutes by size: about 25 minutes for a 600 g fish, 28 to 30 for a 650 to 700 g fish, adding 4 to 5 minutes per additional 100 g. The crust will harden, turn pale gold, and may craze with fine cracks. Inside the sealed shell the fish steams in its own moisture at a gentle, even heat, cooking edge to spine without the dry radiant blast of an open oven; this self-basting steam is the entire point of the salt crust.'
    technique: 'salt-crust-baking'
    time_min: 28
  - text: 'Check doneness, then rest. Push a thin metal skewer or cake tester through the crust into the thickest part of the flesh behind the head and leave it 5 seconds; on withdrawal it should feel hot to the lip, or read 52 to 57 C / 126 to 135 F at the spine on an instant-read probe pushed through a small hole. Porgy is lean and dries fast past 60 C, so pull at the low end and let carryover finish it. Rest the fish in its crust 5 minutes out of the oven; the temperature evens out and the flesh sets enough to lift clean.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Build the finishing oil while the fish rests. In a small saucepan over low heat, warm the 45 g / 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil with the 4 g / 1 sliced garlic clove until the garlic is fragrant and just barely colored, about 2 minutes. Add the 6 g / 2 anchovy fillets and mash them into the oil; they sizzle and melt away in 30 to 45 seconds into a savory, salty base. This melted-anchovy oil is the umami anchor of the dish, the glutamate that the gently cooked lean flesh needs. Pull off heat and stir in the 8 g / ¼ cup chopped parsley.'
    technique: 'fond-deglaze'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Crack the crust tableside. Carry the fish to the table on a platter. Tap firmly around the perimeter of the dome with the back of a heavy spoon or a knife handle to fracture the shell, then lift the top crust away in large pieces; it should come off in slabs, taking the skin and scales with it and exposing the flesh. Brush every loose salt crystal off the fish with a dry pastry brush before serving, since stray crystals are intensely salty and will spike any bite they land in.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Lift, dress, and serve. Run a thin knife or spoon along the spine and lift the top fillet off the bones in clean pieces; remove the skeleton by the tail to free the bottom fillet, peeling away the bottom skin. Spoon the warm anchovy-parsley oil over the flesh and squeeze the reserved 15 g / ¼ lemon over the top for the finishing acid that lifts the gently seasoned fish. Taste a piece: the crust seasons only the surface lightly, so add a pinch of flaky finishing salt at the plate only if a portion reads underseasoned. Serve with the lemon wedges and the rest of the fennel bulb dressed simply alongside.'
    time_min: 3

  - for: 'coarse sea salt'
    use: 'coarse kosher salt'
    note: 'Coarse sea salt (gros sel) is canonical for its large, irregular crystal that interlocks into a strong shell. Coarse kosher salt works and packs well with the egg white; avoid fine table salt, which sets into a dense, hard-to-crack slab and risks pushing salinity into the flesh, and avoid flaky finishing salt, which is too delicate to build a rigid crust.'
  - for: 'egg whites'
    use: 'cold water alone'
    note: 'The egg white is the structural binder; without it, dampen the salt with about 200 g / ¾ cup cold water until it packs like wet sand. The crust is workable but more fragile and prone to cracking during the bake, so pack it slightly thicker and handle the pan gently. Whipped whites give the most reliable, rigid shell.'
  - for: 'anchovy fillets'
    use: 'colatura di alici or a few drops of fish sauce'
    note: 'A teaspoon of colatura (Italian anchovy syrup) or a scant ½ teaspoon of fish sauce warmed into the oil delivers the same melted-anchovy glutamate without the fillets. To keep the finish strictly free of small fish, omit the anchovy and lean on a heavier hand of lemon and a pinch of flaky salt; the oil reads cleaner and less savory.'

notes: |
  Why the crust does not salt the fish. The intuition that packing a fish in over a kilo of salt must make it inedibly salty is wrong, and understanding why is the key to the dish. Salt migrates into flesh only through direct contact and a moisture gradient, and the whole unscaled fish presents neither: the intact scales and skin are a near-impermeable barrier, and the crust itself is bound and largely sealed by the egg-white protein, so very little free salt brine ever reaches the surface. The cure on a steamed or poached fish seasons through bare scored flesh; here the flesh is never exposed to the salt at all. The crust seasons only faintly at the surface, which is exactly why the finishing oil, the lemon, and an optional pinch of flaky salt at the plate are doing the real seasoning work, not the kilo of salt on the pan.

  The crust is an oven, not a seasoning. Mechanically the salt shell does two things. It insulates: salt has low thermal conductivity, so heat arrives at the fish slowly and evenly rather than as the dry radiant blast of an open oven, which means the temperature difference between the thin belly and the thick shoulder stays small and the whole fish reaches doneness together. And it seals: as the fish heats, the water in its flesh and cavity turns to steam that cannot escape the rigid shell, so the fish effectively steam-cooks in a pocket of its own moisture, self-basting the entire time. The combination is why salt-baked fish comes out so reliably moist and evenly set. It is the gentlest whole-fish cooking method short of sous vide, with none of the equipment.

  The umami and acid structure. Lean white fish supplies its own modest glutamate, but the dish needs a savory anchor and a bright finish to keep the gently cooked flesh from reading flat. The umami comes from the two anchovy fillets melted into the warm finishing oil, dissolving into a salty, savory carrier that coats every piece of flesh. The acid is the lemon squeezed over the lifted fillets at the very end, plus the lemon zest and rounds that perfumed the flesh from the cavity during the bake. The salsa-verde modification folds both jobs into one green spoonful. Without the anchovy-and-lemon finish the salt-baked fish is clean but slightly austere; with it the plate is complete.

  Doneness and the lean-fish window. Porgy runs 3 to 6% fat, lean enough that the doneness window is narrow: the connective tissue between muscle blocks gelatinizes at 55 to 60 C and the flesh dries and turns cottony fast past 60 C at the spine. The salt crust widens the margin by cooking gently and evenly, but it also hides the fish, so you cannot watch the flesh opaque the way you can in a steamer. Trust the probe. Pulling at 52 to 57 C and resting 5 minutes lands a moist, just-set fish after carryover; if you prefer or need the USDA-safe 63 C target, bake a few minutes longer and accept a firmer flake. Either way, rest before cracking so the flesh sets enough to lift clean off the bone.

  Sourcing and the unscaled rule. The single sourcing instruction that matters for this dish is to leave the fish unscaled. Most counters scale by default, so say so explicitly: gutted, gills out, scales on. The scales are load-bearing here, the barrier that keeps the crust from over-salting the flesh, and a scaled fish salt-baked in this crust does come out too salty. Otherwise judge the porgy as any whole fish: clear convex eyes, bright red gills, tight scales with a clean sheen rather than gummy slime, firm springy flesh, and a clean ocean smell with no ammonia. Porgy is the high-leverage choice here, the same Sparidae structure as the premium orata and branzino at a fraction of the price, and salt-baking is a technique that rewards the cheaper whole fish because the method, not the species cachet, carries the dish. A 600 to 700 g fish fits one sheet pan and serves two to three as a shared centerpiece.
