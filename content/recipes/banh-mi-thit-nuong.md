before_you_start:
  - 'Make the do chua (pickled carrot and daikon) at least 1 hour ahead, ideally 4 to 24 hours. The pickle improves dramatically as the vegetables soften and absorb the vinegar-sugar brine; the 1-hour minimum is just functional, the 4-hour mark is where the dish reads as it should.'
  - 'Marinate the pork at least 2 hours, ideally 6 to 12 hours. The fish-sauce-and-lemongrass marinade needs time to penetrate; under 1 hour the pork tastes coated rather than infused.'
  - 'The baguette is half the sandwich. Source a Vietnamese baguette from a Vietnamese bakery or banh mi shop if at all possible (shorter, thinner crust, fluffier interior than French baguette; rice flour blended into the dough). The standard French baguette from a supermarket is workable but produces a different sandwich (more crust-to-crumb ratio, chewier). Warm the bread in the oven before assembly; cold bread is structurally wrong.'
  - 'Have all the components prepped and arranged before starting assembly. A banh mi is built fast (60 to 90 seconds per sandwich) and the warm baguette and warm pork are the time-sensitive elements.'

source:
  name: 'Adapted from Saigon and broader Vietnamese tradition (banh mi thit nuong)'
  note: 'Banh mi (the bread itself) is a Vietnamese adaptation of the French baguette, developed during French colonial rule in the late 19th century and modified into a shorter, thinner-crusted, rice-flour-blended loaf better suited to Vietnamese hot-and-humid climate and ingredient context. The sandwich (banh mi sandwich, often just called banh mi by metonymy) evolved in mid-20th-century Saigon as a street-food fusion of French bread, French pâté and mayonnaise, Vietnamese pickled vegetables, Vietnamese herbs, and Vietnamese grilled or braised meats. The grilled-pork (thit nuong) version is one of the most-recognized; cold-cut (thit nguoi) and pork-belly (thit kho) versions are also canonical.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the do chua. Combine the 200 g carrot and 200 g daikon in a medium bowl. Sprinkle with 5 g / 1 tsp kosher salt and toss; let sit 10 minutes (this draws excess water out of the vegetables and improves the pickle texture). Drain off any liquid and gently squeeze handfuls of the vegetables. In a separate bowl, whisk the 120 g / ½ cup rice vinegar, 120 ml / ½ cup water, and 50 g / ¼ cup sugar until the sugar dissolves. Pour the brine over the salted vegetables; toss to coat. Pack into a glass jar; the vegetables should be submerged. Refrigerate at least 1 hour, ideally 4 hours, before serving.'
    technique: 'quick-pickle'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Marinate the pork. Combine the 30 g minced lemongrass, 40 g minced shallot, 20 g minced garlic, 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce, 15 g / 1 tbsp soy sauce, 25 g / 2 tbsp palm sugar, 15 g / 1 tbsp oil, and 3 g / 1 tsp black pepper in a large bowl. Whisk to dissolve the sugar. Add the 600 g sliced pork shoulder; toss aggressively to coat every slice. Refrigerate covered for 2 to 12 hours.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Heat the grill. Charcoal grill (canonical) over hardwood charcoal heated to medium-high until coals are glowing white-orange with a thin gray ash. Gas grill on high (260°C / 500°F) works. Cast iron grill pan on the hottest stovetop burner for indoor cooking; the lemongrass-and-sugar marinade burns aggressively, ventilate the kitchen.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Grill the pork. Lay the marinated pork slices on the grill in a single layer; do not crowd. Cook 90 seconds on the first side until charred at the edges and deeply caramelized; flip, cook 60 to 90 seconds on the second side. The sugar in the marinade caramelizes fast at high heat; pull individual pieces as they reach char rather than leaving the whole batch on. The pork should be cooked through (75°C / 165°F internal) and visibly lacquered on both sides. Transfer to a plate and tent with foil to rest 3 minutes.'
    technique: 'high-heat-grill'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Warm the baguettes. Heat the oven to 175°C / 350°F. Place the 4 baguettes directly on the rack for 5 minutes until the crust is hot and shatters when squeezed. The bread must be warm and crisp-shelled at the moment of assembly; cold or soft bread ruins the textural identity of the sandwich.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Build each sandwich. Slice each warm baguette horizontally with a serrated knife, three-quarters of the way through (leaving a hinge on the back so the sandwich opens like a book). Pull out about a tablespoon of the soft interior bread from the top half to make room for filling (the discard becomes croutons or breadcrumbs another day). Spread 15 g / 1 tbsp mayonnaise across the bottom interior. Layer in this order: 4 to 5 slices grilled pork, a strip of cucumber, a generous handful (about 80 g) of do chua, a handful of cilantro sprigs, 4 to 5 jalapeno rings. Drizzle 4 g / 1 tsp soy sauce and 1 g / 4 drops Maggi (if using) over the top. Close the sandwich, press gently.'
    technique: 'sandwich-build'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Serve immediately, wrapped in a sheet of parchment or wax paper for ease of eating. The sandwich is meant to be eaten right away; the warm pork, warm crust, and cold pickles are at peak contrast at the moment of assembly. A banh mi left to sit for 30 minutes loses the textural contrast as the pickles weep moisture into the crumb.'

notes: |
  Why the four-component contrast. The structural identity of banh mi is the contrast between four distinct elements in each bite: the shattering crust of the warm baguette, the soft interior crumb, the smoky-sweet warm grilled pork, and the bright cold vinegar-pickled vegetables. Any banh mi that loses one of these contrasts loses the dish. Cold bread mutes the crust contrast. Pre-cooked or non-grilled pork misses the char depth. Skipping the pickled vegetables (substituting fresh vegetables alone) misses the acid-cold-bright moment that lifts the rich pork and crumb. All four elements must be present and at their respective temperature-and-texture peaks for the sandwich to work.

  Why do chua specifically (not just pickled vegetables). The vinegar-sugar-water-salt brine for do chua is calibrated to a sweet-tart balance that is distinctly Vietnamese. American-style pickles (more vinegar, less sugar) read too sharp; sauerkraut and other fermented vegetables are texturally and flavorally wrong. The 50 g sugar in 120 g vinegar plus 120 ml water gives a brine pH around 3.5 to 3.8, bright enough to read pickled, sweet enough to balance the rich pork and mayo. The 4-hour minimum rest is what gives the vegetables their characteristic softened-but-still-crisp texture; same-day do chua is acceptable but day-old is canonical.

  Why the pork is sliced thin. The marinade-and-grill technique depends on the slice thickness. At 5 mm the slices marinate efficiently (the fish-sauce-and-lemongrass aromatic penetrates fully in 2 hours), grill in 3 minutes total (90 seconds per side), and stay juicy. Thicker slices (10 mm and above) take longer to grill and the sugar-marinade surface chars to bitterness before the interior cooks. Thinner slices (under 3 mm) overcook to dry-and-stringy. The 5 mm slice is the working sweet spot.

  Why the soy and Maggi finish. The drizzle of soy sauce (and optionally Maggi seasoning sauce) over the assembled sandwich just before closing is the canonical Vietnamese-American banh mi finish. It contributes a final salt-and-savory note that integrates the bread, mayo, pork, and vegetables; without it the sandwich reads slightly under-seasoned and the components feel separate. Maggi is the more authentic touch; the wheat-fermented seasoning sauce was popularized in Vietnam during French rule and became part of the canonical banh mi flavor.

  Why warm bread. The structural crust-and-crumb contrast of canonical banh mi only happens with bread that is warm enough that the crust is crisp and the crumb is just-tender. Cold bread (straight from the bag) has a softer crust that loses the shatter and a tighter crumb that does not absorb mayo properly. The 5-minute warm in a 175°C / 350°F oven brings the bread to its peak texture; do not skip this step.

  Storage. Banh mi does not store well as an assembled sandwich; the pickles weep into the crumb and the crust softens within 30 minutes. The components store well separately: do chua holds 2 weeks in the fridge (improves with age); marinated raw pork holds 24 hours in the fridge; grilled pork holds 3 days. Assemble each sandwich at the moment of eating. For lunch portability, wrap the warm assembled sandwich tightly in parchment within 15 minutes of assembly; eat within 2 hours.

  Why mayonnaise (and not butter). The mayonnaise is structural to the sandwich, contributing both moisture and binding emulsion that holds the pickle-and-herb layer in place. Butter (the French-baguette tradition) is structurally wrong for banh mi; the temperature and water content are different, and the butter solidifies into a layer that fights the soft-crumb texture. Kewpie mayo (Japanese, with a sweeter and umami-richer profile from the rice vinegar and MSG) is the modern Vietnamese-American canonical; American mayonnaises work but are slightly off.
