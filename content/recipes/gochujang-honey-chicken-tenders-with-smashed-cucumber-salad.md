desc: 'Chicken tenderloins quick-brined in soy, ginger, and garlic, then dusted in cornstarch for a thin Maillard crust and seared in a smoking carbon-steel pan. safety_notes:
  - for: 'Pulling chicken tenders at 70 to 72 °C / 158 to 162 °F internal (below the USDA published-safe 165 °F for poultry)'
    ref: 'safety/meat-doneness#poultry'
    why: 'The USDA published-safe instantaneous target is 165 °F (74 °C). The USDA-FSIS pasteurization table publishes the equivalent holds: 160 °F (71 °C) held 14 seconds achieves the same 7-log Salmonella reduction. Tenders pulled at 70 to 72 °C off the pan continue to climb to 75 to 78 °C during the 30 to 60 seconds of off-heat glaze toss and the 1 to 2 minutes between plating and eating, spending well over 14 seconds above 160 °F. The carryover plus the residual pan contact clears pasteurization comfortably. Cooks who prefer the published-safe number should pull at 165 °F (74 °C), accepting noticeably drier meat. Ground chicken, mechanically tenderized cuts, and pre-marinated commercial chicken should always be cooked to the instantaneous 165 °F since the surface bacteria may be distributed through the mass.'

steps:
  - text: 'Start the cucumbers. Lay the 400 g cucumbers on a cutting board and smash each one along its length with the broad side of a heavy knife or a rolling pin until the skin fractures and the cucumber splits open. Tear them apart by hand into rough 3 cm pieces, irregular shapes are correct. Transfer to a colander or sieve set over a bowl, scatter with the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt, toss to coat, and let drain at room temperature for 10 to 15 minutes while you mise the rest. The cucumber will release 30 to 50 g of water; that water is what you do not want in the salad.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Mix the cucumber dressing. In a serving bowl, whisk together the 20 g / 4 tsp rice vinegar, the 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, the 4 g / 1 small clove microplane-grated garlic, the 4 g / 2 tsp gochugaru, and the 4 g / 1 tsp granulated sugar until the sugar dissolves. Let stand at room temperature 5 minutes so the raw garlic mellows into the acid.'

  - text: 'Dry-brine and dust the chicken. Pat the 600 g chicken tenderloins fully dry with paper towels. Lay them flat in a shallow bowl or rimmed plate. Let stand at room temperature for 5 to 8 minutes; the ginger protease starts breaking down the surface proteins while the salt draws a thin layer of moisture into the meat. If pockets of dry cornstarch persist, drizzle a teaspoon of water in and toss again.'

  - text: 'Mix the glaze. In a small bowl, whisk together the 45 g / 3 tbsp gochujang, the 30 g / 1½ tbsp honey, the 15 g / 1 tbsp rice vinegar, the 10 g / 2 tsp light soy sauce, the 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, and the 8 g / 2 cloves of grated garlic until smooth and pourable.   - text: 'Finish the cucumber salad. Tip the salt-drained cucumbers into the bowl of dressing (do not rinse the cucumbers, the residual salt seasons the salad). Toss to coat. The first side builds the starch-and-protein Maillard crust in one uninterrupted contact with the hot iron, and lifting at 90 seconds breaks the contact and restarts the clock.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Flip and finish the sear. After 3 minutes, lift the corner of one tender with tongs; the underside should be deep golden-brown with the starch crust looking lacquered, not pale. Sear undisturbed another 2 to 3 minutes; the second side wants less time because the interior is mostly cooked through from the first sear. notes: |
  Why the cornstarch dust over flour, breadcrumbs, or bare protein. Cornstarch is pure amylose-and-amylopectin starch with no protein; the granules gelatinize and brown in 60 to 90 seconds of hot pan contact, producing a thin glassy crust that locks the chicken interior moist while the glaze adheres to a rough surface rather than smooth wet protein. Flour produces a similar crust but reads doughier (the gluten develops as the flour hydrates), and the crust competes with the glaze for surface attention. Breadcrumbs (panko) produce a thicker, crunchier crust that is structurally different, more chicken-katsu than yangnyeom; reserve it for a different dish. Bare protein with no dusting produces an unsticky glaze surface, the gochujang slides off rather than lacquering.

  Why ginger in the brine and not garlic. Ginger contains zingibain, a cysteine protease that gently hydrolyzes muscle proteins (the same enzymatic class as bromelain in pineapple and papain in papaya, but slower and more controllable). A 5-to-10-minute ginger exposure on chicken breast or tender produces a noticeably more tender bite without crossing into mushy over-tenderized territory; longer exposures (30 minutes or more) can over-soften the surface. Garlic contributes no protease activity to the brine and is wasted on raw protein contact at this duration; reserve it for the glaze where its allicin volatiles cook off briefly into the warm pan heat.

  Why off-heat glaze and not pan-reduction. The classic Korean fried chicken glaze is built in the pan: gochujang plus aromatics simmered down before the chicken hits the pan. That works for deep-fried chicken (the fry crust is already hard set, the wet glaze does not soften it). For seared tender, the off-heat toss preserves the cornstarch crust integrity; if the chicken sits in simmering glaze for more than 30 seconds, the crust softens and the dish loses the contrast between lacquered surface and crisp interior. The 30-second off-heat toss is the right window: long enough for the glaze to reduce slightly from the chicken juices that release into the pan, short enough that the crust stays intact.

  The smashed cucumber as structural foil, not garnish. The Sichuan and northern Chinese pai huang gua technique (smashing the cucumber to fracture rather than slicing) was built around exactly this problem: a rich savory protein needs an acid-and-water counterpoint that holds its texture against the heat and fat of the main dish. The fractured surface exposes 40 to 50 percent more cell wall than a clean knife cut, which means the salt-and-vinegar dressing penetrates faster and the cucumber stays crisp longer in the dressed state. A sliced cucumber salad goes limp in 10 minutes; a smashed cucumber salad stays crisp for 30. Time the cucumber so it hits the table 5 to 15 minutes after dressing for the peak texture window.

  Doneness on chicken tenders. The pectoralis minor (the tender) is the smallest, leanest muscle on the bird and the easiest to overcook into chalky dry meat. The USDA-recommended internal temperature of 74 °C (165 °F) is the safe-without-question target, but tenders pulled at 70 °C and held for 90 seconds (which they will be, the carryover heat in the glaze toss plus the residual pan contact does the rest) are equally safe by the time-and-temperature equivalence rule. Pulling at 70 °C gives a noticeably moister, more tender eat. An instant-read thermometer is the right tool; visual cues alone produce too much variance on a cut this thin.

  The pairing with rice and the carb dial. Steamed short-grain rice is the canonical Korean pairing and the structural starch that the glaze is calibrated against (the rice absorbs the surplus glaze that pools on the plate). For a lower-carb plate, skip the rice and serve the tenders in romaine or butter lettuce wraps (see modifications); the lettuce supplies the water-and-crunch that the rice would have supplied as starch, and the plate eats lighter. The cucumber salad is the structural side either way; do not skip it, the dish lives on the warm-cool, sweet-acid contrast it provides.

  Storage and reheating. The glazed tenders hold in the refrigerator 3 days; the cucumber salad holds 1 day before the cucumbers go limp. Reheat the tenders in a 175 °C / 350 °F oven on a wire rack for 6 to 8 minutes (the wire rack prevents the bottom of the glaze from steaming into a sticky film); the microwave works but softens the cornstarch crust into something less interesting. Do not freeze the glazed tenders, the glaze breaks on thaw and the cornstarch crust goes mealy.
