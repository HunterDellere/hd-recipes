source:
  name: 'The Ligurian canon (Genoa and the broader Ligurian coast). Documented in the modern stabilized form (small-leaf basil, pine nuts, garlic, parmigiano, pecorino, olive oil, pounded in a mortar) by Giovanni Ratto in La cuciniera genovese (1863); the dish is older than the documentation. The Consorzio del Pesto Genovese codified the canonical preparation in the 21st century around the Genovese DOP basil, the Vessalico garlic, and the Sardinian pecorino paired with parmigiano reggiano.'
  note: 'Pesto alla genovese is structurally a cheese-bound emulsion, not a chopped herb sauce. The mortar grind crushes the basil cell walls slowly without generating the heat that a food processor produces (high-speed shear above 50°C oxidizes the chlorophyll and turns the sauce brown within minutes); the cheese fat binds the basil oils, nut oils, and olive oil into a cohesive paste that clings to pasta. The modern food-processor version is widely tolerated as a time-saving substitute, but a side-by-side tasting reveals the mortar version reads brighter, greener, and more stable. The canonical pesto holds its color for 3 to 4 hours at room temperature; the processor version browns within 30 minutes.'

steps:
  - text: 'Wash the 60 g / 4 packed cups basil leaves in cold water, swirl to remove any grit, and dry thoroughly on a clean tea towel or in a salad spinner. Water clinging to the leaves breaks the emulsion at the end of the grind; the leaves must be visibly dry before they enter the mortar. Set aside while the garlic and pine nuts are processed.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'In a heavy marble mortar (the canonical Ligurian vessel; a heavy ceramic or stone mortar is an acceptable substitute), pound the 6 g / 1 to 2 garlic cloves with the 3 g / ½ tsp coarse salt for 1 minute, working in slow circular grinds with the pestle, until the garlic is reduced to a smooth even paste. Add the 30 g / 3 tbsp pine nuts; continue pounding 1 to 2 minutes until the nuts are fully crushed into the garlic paste, producing a thick pale cream.'
    technique: 'mortar-pounding'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add the 60 g / 4 packed cups dried basil leaves in 3 to 4 additions (about 15 g per addition); for each addition, grind the leaves against the mortar wall in slow circular motions with the pestle (not pounding straight down; the rotating-pressure grind is the structural difference between a Genovese pesto and a bruised herb paste). Each addition takes about 90 seconds of patient grinding; the basil progressively releases its oils and the paste turns a brilliant emerald green. Total basil grinding time should be 4 to 6 minutes; the finished basil paste should be uniform and bright green with no visible leaf structure.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Add the 50 g / ½ cup parmigiano and the 25 g / ¼ cup pecorino; grind to incorporate, 1 minute, until the paste is uniform. Begin streaming the 90 g / ⅓ cup plus 1 tbsp olive oil into the mortar in a slow steady stream while continuing to grind with the pestle; the oil and the cheese fat bind the basil and nut oils into a cohesive emulsion. The finished pesto should be a thick spoonable paste, brilliant green, with a slight gloss from the emulsified oil; it should hold a soft mound on a spoon and slowly slump rather than pour.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Taste and adjust: pesto alla genovese should read basil-forward with assertive cheese, gentle garlic, and a clean fruity-olive finish; the pine nuts should be background sweetness, not foreground crunch. If flat, an extra 1 g / pinch of coarse salt; if too sharp, a bit more parmigiano to round; if the basil is muted, the grind was insufficient (regrind 1 more minute). Use immediately or transfer to a clean jar, smooth the surface, cover with a thin film of olive oil (the oil seal prevents oxidation; the pesto darkens visibly without it), and refrigerate up to 4 days. For pasta service, thin with 30 to 60 ml of starchy pasta water just before tossing; the water lifts the emulsion and helps it coat the pasta.'
    time_min: 1

  - for: 'food-processor speed'
    to: 'in a food processor instead of a mortar'
    how: 'Combine the garlic, salt, and pine nuts in a small food processor; pulse 8 to 10 times to a coarse paste. Add the basil in 3 additions, pulsing 5 to 6 times after each (do not run continuously; the heat and shear oxidize the chlorophyll and turn the pesto brown). Add the grated cheeses; pulse 3 to 4 times. With the motor off, scrape down the sides and add the olive oil; pulse 5 to 6 times in short bursts to incorporate. The processor version reads slightly less bright and oxidizes faster (browning within 30 minutes vs the mortar pesto holding color for 3 to 4 hours), but is acceptable when time is short. Chill the processor bowl in the freezer for 15 minutes before starting to slow the oxidation.'

notes: |
  Pesto alla genovese is the most structurally precise of the Mediterranean herb sauces. The mortar grind is the canonical technique because it preserves the basil chlorophyll: the slow rotating pressure of the pestle against the marble wall ruptures the basil cell walls without generating the heat (above 50°C) that triggers chlorophyll oxidation and converts the brilliant green pesto to a brown dull paste. The food processor produces an acceptable pesto in 90 seconds, but the high-speed blade shear heats the basil to 60°C or higher within those 90 seconds, and the pesto browns visibly within 30 minutes of being made. The mortar version made by hand stays brilliant green for 3 to 4 hours at room temperature, which is the operational difference between a sauce that serves well at a dinner table and one that has to be served immediately.

  The basil drying matters. Wet basil leaves (water clinging from washing) break the emulsion as the oil and water phases separate during grinding; the pesto reads thin and slightly grainy instead of cohesive and creamy. Dry the leaves thoroughly on a clean tea towel or in a salad spinner before they enter the mortar; the few extra minutes of patience pay off in the texture of the finished sauce.

  The cheese blend is structural. Pure parmigiano reads sweet and mild and produces a pesto that is recognizable but lacks the sharp sheep-milk depth that pecorino brings; pure pecorino reads aggressive and salty and produces a pesto that overwhelms its applications. The canonical 2:1 parmigiano-to-pecorino ratio is the balance that gives Ligurian pesto its characteristic flavor; reduce the pecorino by half if you find the sheep-milk character too strong for your palate, but do not eliminate it.

  Versatility. Pesto alla genovese is structurally a finishing sauce for cooked starches and grilled or roasted ingredients. Use it as: the canonical sauce for trofie al pesto (the Ligurian potato-and-green-bean pasta), trenette al pesto (the flat-noodle version), and other Ligurian pasta shapes (linguine, gnocchi, pansoti); a stir-in for minestrone alla genovese (a tablespoon stirred into each bowl at service brings the pesto signature to the soup); a sauce for fresh potato gnocchi and ricotta gnocchi; a finishing drizzle for grilled or roasted vegetables (eggplant, zucchini, tomato, sweet pepper, asparagus); a sauce for grilled fish (especially salmon, branzino, sea bass, swordfish, and white fish in general) and grilled shrimp; a sauce for fried eggs (a small spoonful stirred into scrambled eggs at the end of cooking; spread on a toasted English muffin under a poached egg); a spread for sandwiches and panini (especially with grilled chicken, mozzarella, tomato, prosciutto); a topping for pizza (drizzled over the finished pie, not baked) and flatbreads; a stir-in for ricotta and other fresh cheeses to make herb spreads; a dressing thinned with extra olive oil and a squeeze of lemon for pasta salads and grain bowls; and a finish for soups (a spoonful in white bean soup, vegetable soup, or tomato soup brings a structural herbal lift).

  Pairs across Ligurian (canonical), broader Italian, Italian-American, Mediterranean French, modern Mediterranean diaspora, and contemporary American kitchens. Less cuisine-flexible than chimichurri or salsa verde because the cheese-bound emulsion is structurally Italian and reads incongruous in non-Mediterranean contexts; pesto on a Mexican grain bowl or a Vietnamese rice plate reads disjointed in a way that chimichurri on the same dish does not.

  Storage. Keeps 4 days refrigerated in a sealed jar with a thin film of olive oil over the surface (the oil seal is structural for preventing oxidation; without it the pesto darkens visibly within 24 hours). Freezes well: portion into ice cube trays (about 30 g per cube), freeze solid, transfer to an airtight glass container; thaw cubes individually at room temperature or stir directly into hot pasta. Frozen pesto keeps 3 months and is a meaningful improvement over the no-pesto winter alternative; basil is a summer ingredient and the freezer is the only way to carry it forward through the year.
