  name: 'Cantonese / overseas-Chinese adaptation of Sichuan mapo'
  note: 'When mapo tofu emigrated to Hong Kong, Singapore, and the diaspora restaurants of North America in the 20th century, it was adapted to local pantries and palates: pork (the Cantonese default protein) replaced beef, oyster sauce was added for the slightly sweet-savory finish that defines Cantonese cooking, and the numbing-spicy levels were dialed down for diners not raised on má-là. before_you_start:
  - 'Start jasmine rice FIRST as a PARALLEL TRACK. The mapo cooks in about 12 minutes once aromatics hit the wok, and the rice needs 12 minutes simmer plus 10 minutes covered rest. Rinse and put rice on before you marinate the pork; the timing lines up so both finish together.'
  - 'Marinate the pork 10 minutes before cooking. The 3 g cornstarch coat (velveting principle on ground meat) needs the rest to hydrate or it sloughs off in the wok.'
  - 'Have every aromatic minced and every sauce ingredient pre-measured into small bowls before the wok goes on heat. From doubanjiang in the oil to plate is under 6 minutes of continuous high-heat cooking; stopping to mince garlic mid-stir-fry burns what is already in the pan.'

steps:
  - text: 'Marinate the pork. In a small bowl, mix the 200 g ground pork with 10 ml / 2 tsp Shaoxing, 5 ml / 1 tsp light soy, and 3 g / ½ tsp cornstarch. Set aside 10 minutes; the cornstarch coats the pork granules and produces a more velvety bite (Cantonese velveting principle applied to ground meat).'
    technique: 'velveting'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Brown the pork. Heat a wok over high heat. Add 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil and the marinated pork. Stir-fry, breaking into granules, for 5 to 6 minutes until the pork is no longer pink, dry, and lightly browned. Pork takes less browning than beef; pull when the granules are pale-tan rather than mahogany. Transfer to a plate.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Blanch the tofu. While the pork browns, bring 1500 ml / 6 cups water to a bare simmer (80°C / 175°F) with the 8 g / 2 tsp salt. Cut the 450 g medium-firm tofu into 2 cm cubes. Slide into the salted water, hold 90 seconds, drain through a colander.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Render a gentler aromatic base. Wipe the wok and return to medium heat. Add 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil. When shimmering, add 20 g doubanjiang. Break up and stir-fry 60 seconds (less time than canonical mapo because there is less paste, the goal is still red oil, but the dish is meant to be lighter). Add 12 g minced ginger, 16 g minced garlic, and 25 g chopped scallion whites. Stir-fry 30 seconds.'
    technique: 'red-oil-rendering'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Build the sauce. Pour in 240 ml / 1 cup chicken stock, 18 ml / 1¼ tbsp oyster sauce, 10 ml / 2 tsp light soy, 5 ml / 1 tsp dark soy, 4 g / ¾ tsp sugar. Return the browned pork. Bring to a simmer; the oyster sauce contributes the Cantonese sweet-savory shimmer that distinguishes this from Sichuan mapo. Slide in the blanched tofu cubes, push gently to submerge, simmer 3 minutes.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Single-pour cornstarch thickening. Whisk 9 g cornstarch with 45 ml / 3 tbsp cold water. Bring the wok to medium-high. Pour the slurry around the edge of the wok in a slow stream while gently pushing the tofu to distribute. The sauce will thicken to a glossy coat; this is the Cantonese single-pour standard and is fine for this version (Sichuan three-pour is a more refined technique that does not need to be applied here).'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Finish. Pull off heat. Add 20 g scallion greens and 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, sesame oil is the Cantonese finishing move and is the small detail that places this version on the Cantonese side of the canon. Toss gently. Transfer to a serving plate. Sprinkle with 1.5 g / ¾ tsp Sichuan peppercorn powder, half the canonical amount, present but not aggressive. Serve immediately with steamed jasmine rice.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  Why this version exists. This is the mapo tofu most North American Chinese restaurant menus list, and it is a defensible cuisine in its own right rather than a compromised Sichuan classic. The Cantonese cooks who emigrated and brought Chinese food to North America adapted Sichuan dishes to their own palate (Cantonese cooking favors sweet-savory balance over numbing-spicy intensity), to their pantry (oyster sauce is the Cantonese seasoning paste that doubanjiang is to Sichuan), and to local diner expectations. The result is its own thing: lighter, sweeter, more polished. A diner who has only ever had this version has not had Sichuan mapo, but they have had a real and complete dish.

  When to make this version vs the canonical Sichuan version. Make this version when: cooking for diners who find canonical Sichuan mapo too aggressive; cooking on a weeknight when the simpler approach earns its time; using ingredients you have on hand without specialty Yongchuan douchi; or simply preferring this flavor profile (many cooks do, sweet-savory polish is its own pleasure). Make the canonical Sichuan version (recipes/sichuan-mapo-tofu) when: cooking for someone who knows the dish and wants the original; cooking for the technique itself (the má-là combination, the three-pour cornstarch, the fresh-toasted peppercorn finish); or wanting the seven-flavor target Sichuan codified.

  The Cantonese signatures. Three small details place this version on the Cantonese side of the canon: oyster sauce in the cooking liquid (the structural Cantonese seasoning), velveted pork (the cornstarch-coat ground-meat technique that produces a more silky bite), and toasted sesame oil at the finish (the Cantonese closing aromatic). All three are absent from canonical Sichuan mapo and would be considered Cantonese drift if added there. Together they shift the dish recognizably toward Cantonese cooking.

  Eating mechanics. Serve with steamed jasmine rice; reheats acceptably the next day. The lower spice level makes this a defensible main-course mapo for cooks who want the dish but not the full mala assault, the sauce-to-rice ratio is similar to other Cantonese braised tofu dishes (mapo here behaves more like Cantonese fish-and-tofu hot pot than like Chengdu street-vendor mapo).
