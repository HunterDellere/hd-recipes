source:
  name: 'Adapted from the Greek hortopita tradition, the wild-and-cultivated-greens filo pie that is the rustic country cousin of spanakopita, where whatever leafy greens are in season (chard, beet greens, nettle, dandelion, amaranth) are wilted, squeezed dry, bound with cheese and egg, and baked between layers of filo.'
  note: 'Two pieces of beet-green chemistry drive the build. First, the leaves hold a lot of water (88 percent raw) and a heavy oxalate load; wilting and then squeezing them bone-dry is non-negotiable, because residual moisture steams the filo limp from the inside and the pie goes soggy. The feta and egg in the filling, plus the calcium-rich greens themselves, supply the calcium that pairs with the oxalate at the table. Second, the cooked greens drift toward a flat earthy register, so lemon zest folded into the filling and the bright dill-and-scallion aromatics keep the iron-mineral leaves lifted under the rich buttered pastry. The fat brushed between every filo sheet is structural, not a garnish; it is the only reason the crust shatters rather than baking into a leathery cracker.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Thaw the filo correctly and ahead: overnight in the refrigerator, then 1 to 2 hours at room temperature still sealed in its plastic. Filo thawed too fast tears and sticks into an unusable block. Unroll it only when the filling is made and the butter-oil is melted, and keep the stack covered with parchment or a barely-damp (never wet) towel the entire time you work, exposed sheets dry to brittle shards in 2 to 3 minutes.'
  - 'The squeeze-dry step is the dish. After wilting, the greens must be wrung out hard in a clean towel or pressed in a sieve until almost no liquid releases; budget time for it to cool enough to handle. Skipping or rushing this is the single most common way the pie fails, the crust goes soggy from below.'
  - 'Have everything for the filling cooked, squeezed, cooled, and mixed before you open the filo. Once the filo is unrolled the assembly must move steadily so the sheets do not dry out; this is not a stage to stop and chop.'

steps:
  - text: 'Wilt the greens. Wash the 500 g beet greens in two changes of cold water. Warm the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil in a wide pot over medium-high. Add the finely chopped stems and cook 2 minutes; add the 60 g sliced scallions and the 9 g / 2 to 3 minced garlic, cook 1 minute until fragrant. Add the chopped leaves in handfuls with the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt, tossing each addition down, and cook 3 to 4 minutes until fully wilted and collapsed. Tip into a sieve set over a bowl and let cool enough to handle, 10 minutes.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Squeeze the greens bone-dry. This is the step the whole pie depends on. Working in two batches, gather the wilted greens into a clean kitchen towel, twist hard over the sink, and wring until almost no liquid releases; a 500 g raw bunch should give up a startling amount of water and reduce to a tight dense ball. Pull the squeezed ball apart with your fingers so the greens are loose again rather than a compressed clump. Residual water here steams the filo soggy from inside, so squeeze harder than feels necessary.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Make the filling. In a large bowl, combine the squeezed greens, the 250 g crumbled feta, the 60 g / ¼ cup Greek yogurt, the 2 beaten eggs, the 15 g / ½ cup chopped dill, the 3 g / zest of 1 lemon, the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper, and the 0.5 g / ⅛ tsp nutmeg. Mix thoroughly with your hands or a fork until the filling is cohesive and the feta is evenly distributed. Taste a small pinch (the egg is raw, taste judiciously): it should read salty-savory from the feta, bright from the lemon and dill, with the greens as the backbone. Add salt only if it reads flat, the feta usually carries enough.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Set up the filo station. Heat the oven to 190°C / 375°F with a rack in the lower-middle. Melt the 80 g butter and stir in the 40 g / 3 tbsp olive oil to make one brushing fat. Brush a 23x33 cm / 9x13 inch baking pan with the fat. Unroll the thawed filo and lay it flat near the pan; immediately cover the stack with a sheet of parchment topped with a barely-damp towel, and uncover only to lift one sheet at a time. A torn sheet is fine, the next brushed layer bonds over it; patch and proceed rather than discard.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Build the bottom crust. Lay one filo sheet in the pan, letting the edges overhang, and brush it all over with the butter-oil. Repeat, stacking and brushing, for 8 sheets total (if your sheets are smaller than the pan, alternate the direction of overhang so the base is fully covered). Brush each sheet, the fat between layers is what makes the crust shatter; an unbrushed sheet bakes into a leathery layer. This is the base the filling sits on.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Fill and fold. Spread the entire filling evenly over the bottom crust, pressing it into a level layer to the edges. Fold the overhanging filo edges in over the filling to partly enclose it, brushing the folded flaps with fat as you go. This seals the filling and reinforces the rim.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Build the top crust and score. Lay and brush the remaining filo sheets (8 sheets) over the top one at a time, brushing each with the butter-oil, tucking the edges down inside the pan rim to seal the pie like a parcel. Brush the very top generously. With a sharp knife, score through only the top few layers of filo (not down into the filling) into 12 squares; pre-scoring lets the pie bake through cleanly and makes it cut neatly without shattering the whole crust. Sprinkle the optional 6 g / 2 tsp sesame seeds over the top and flick a little water across the surface (the steam helps the top layers separate and crisp).'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Bake. Bake at 190°C / 375°F for 40 to 50 minutes, until the top is deep golden-brown and crisp and the filo sounds hollow and shatters when tapped. If the top browns before 40 minutes, tent loosely with foil and continue; the pie needs the full time for the egg-bound filling to set and the bottom crust to dry and crisp. A pie pulled early has a set top but a pale, soggy base.'
    time_min: 48
  - text: 'Rest and cut. Let the pie rest 15 to 20 minutes before cutting. This is functional: the egg-bound filling continues to set as it cools, and a pie cut straight from the oven slumps and weeps while a rested one holds clean square slices. Cut along the score lines with a sharp serrated knife. Serve warm or at room temperature, the latter is how it is most often eaten in Greece, with a tomato-cucumber salad and lemon wedges.'
    time_min: 18

    note: 'All olive oil (replace the butter gram-for-gram) is the rustic-village choice and makes a vegan-adjacent, slightly coarser, more savory crust, very traditional for hortopita. Ghee gives an even more brittle, nutty crust and does not burn as readily. A butter-and-oil blend (as written) is the balance of brittle shatter and savory depth.'

notes: |
  Why the squeeze-dry step is the entire dish. Beet greens are 88 percent water raw, and even after wilting they hold a great deal of it. Filo is so thin (under half a millimeter per sheet) that any moisture released from the filling during baking steams the bottom crust limp from the inside, and the pie that should shatter instead bends and weeps. Wringing the wilted greens bone-dry in a towel, hard enough that almost no liquid releases, is what lets the filo crisp through to the base. This is the single step that separates a hortopita that cuts into clean crisp squares from one that collapses into a soggy mess. Squeeze harder than feels reasonable; you cannot over-do it.

  Why the fat between every layer is structural. Filo contains no laminated butter and no leavening; it is just stretched wheat dough. The shatter comes entirely from the fat the cook brushes between the sheets at assembly. Each brushed sheet bakes into a crisp plane mechanically isolated from its neighbors by a film of set fat, so the crust fractures cleanly instead of baking into one continuous leathery layer. An unbrushed sheet anywhere in the stack reads as a chewy band in the finished crust. The butter gives the finest brittle shatter; the olive oil blended into it adds a savory note and keeps the butter from setting rock-hard, which is the right balance for a savory pie. All-olive-oil is the rustic village version and equally correct.

  The calcium pairing and the lemon lift, in this format. As in any beet-green dish, the heavy oxalate load wants a calcium partner in the same meal; here the feta, the Greek yogurt, the eggs, and the calcium-rich greens themselves cover it without any special effort, which is part of why a cheese-bound greens pie is such a natural format for this leaf. The lemon zest folded into the filling does the second job every cooked-beet-green dish needs: cooked greens drift toward a flat earthy register, and the limonene in the zest plus the bright dill and scallion keep the iron-mineral leaves lifted under the rich buttered pastry. Zest rather than juice in the filling, the juice would add water the squeeze-dry step worked to remove.

  Filo handling is the part that intimidates and shouldn''t. The two rules cover almost everything: thaw it slowly (overnight in the fridge, then to room temperature still wrapped), and keep the working stack covered at all times so the exposed sheets do not dry to shards in the two or three minutes they sit in open air. Beyond that, tears do not matter in a layered pie, the next brushed sheet bonds over any tear, so patch and keep moving rather than discarding torn sheets. Cooks new to filo should buy the thicker country-style (horiatiko) sheets, which are sturdier and far more forgiving at a small cost to the final delicacy.

  Make-ahead, serving temperature, and leftovers. The pie is excellent warm and arguably better at room temperature, which is how it is most often eaten in Greece, as a meze, a lunchbox slice, or a light supper with salad. It is a strong make-ahead dish: assemble it fully (unbaked), refrigerate up to a day, and bake straight from the fridge with 5 to 10 extra minutes; or bake it and serve at room temperature within the day. Store baked leftovers at room temperature loosely covered rather than refrigerated, because the cold sets the fat hard and softens the crust; re-crisp slices in a hot oven (200°C / 400°F for 8 to 10 minutes) rather than a microwave, which steams the filo limp. Unbaked triangles (the meze modification) freeze well and bake straight from frozen.
