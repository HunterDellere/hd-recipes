before_you_start:
  - 'Macerate first, build later. Mince the shallot, combine it with the sherry vinegar and fine sea salt, and let the mixture sit 10 minutes before anything else goes in. The acid and salt pull water out of the shallot cells and quench the raw allyl sulfide pungency; skip this rest and the dressing reads sharp and oniony for the first hour. This is the only timed step, so start it as soon as you walk into the kitchen.'
  - 'Walnut oil is fragile. If using walnut oil, keep it refrigerated until the moment it streams in and use it within the day; its polyunsaturated fats oxidize fast and a rancid bottle turns the whole dressing bitter. Smell it first: fresh walnut oil reads sweet and toasted, rancid oil reads of paint and crayon.'

steps:
  - text: 'Macerate the shallot. Combine the 30 g / 1 medium minced shallot, the 30 g / 2 tbsp sherry vinegar, and the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt in a small bowl. Stir once so the salt dissolves into the vinegar and the shallot is fully submerged. Let it sit undisturbed for 10 minutes. The acid and salt pull water out of the shallot through osmosis and quench the alliinase reaction that generates the raw allyl sulfides; what comes out the far side is a softened, pickle-bright shallot rather than a harsh aromatic. The vinegar also goes faintly pink, which is correct.'
    technique: 'vinaigrette-emulsion'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Whisk in the emulsifier base. Add the 5 g / 1 tsp dijon mustard, the 4 g / ½ tsp honey, and the 1 g / ½ tsp cracked black pepper to the macerated shallot and vinegar. Whisk with a fork until the mustard and honey fully dissolve into the acid and the mixture looks uniformly cloudy. The water phase must be complete and homogeneous before any oil enters; salt, mustard mucilage, and dissolved honey all need to be in solution first so the oil shears into a finished base rather than a half-mixed one.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Combine the oils, then stream and shear. Stir the 30 g / 2 tbsp walnut oil and the 60 g / 4 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil together in a separate small cup. Stream the combined oil into the vinegar base in a thin steady pour while whisking vigorously and continuously. The dijon mucilage (a seed-coat polysaccharide) and the suspended seed particles coat each oil droplet as it enters and hold it in suspension; the slow pour gives the emulsifier time to coat one droplet before the next arrives, and the hard whisking shears the oil into fine droplets that stay suspended longer. The dressing should turn opaque, pale, and visibly thickened, coating the whisk.'
    technique: 'vinaigrette-emulsion'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Taste and balance. The finished vinaigrette should read sharp and aromatic from the sherry, savory and faintly sweet underneath, with the shallot present but no longer raw-hot. The dijon supplies the glutamate-adjacent savory tang and the emulsion body; the sherry vinegar is the finishing acid. If it reads too sharp, whisk in 5 to 10 g / 1 to 2 tsp more olive oil; if it reads flat, a few extra grains of fine sea salt and a half teaspoon more sherry vinegar lift it. Use within the hour for the tightest emulsion. It will keep a day in the refrigerator and rebuilds with a few seconds of whisking, though the shallot continues to mellow and the dressing reads softer on day two.'
    time_min: 1

substitutions:
  - for: 'sherry vinegar'
    use: 'red wine vinegar, champagne vinegar, or aged balsamic'
    note: 'Red wine vinegar is the closest everyday swap (a touch less complex, slightly lower acetic, so use the same weight). Champagne vinegar is milder and cleaner; push the ratio toward 1:2.5 oil-to-vinegar since it reads softer. A few grams of aged balsamic stirred into red wine vinegar approximates the oaked depth of sherry. White wine vinegar works but reads thinner and benefits from an extra few grams of dijon for body.'
  - for: 'walnut oil'
    use: 'hazelnut oil, toasted-walnut oil, or all extra-virgin olive oil'
    note: 'Hazelnut oil is a 1:1 swap with a rounder, sweeter nut note. Toasted (roasted) walnut oil is more intense; cut it to 20 g and make up the difference with olive oil. Dropping the nut oil entirely and using 90 g / 6 tbsp olive oil gives a cleaner, more neutral all-purpose dressing that loses the Burgundian nut character but keeps everything else.'
  - for: 'honey'
    use: 'maple syrup, a pinch of granulated sugar, or omit'
    note: 'Maple syrup is a 1:1 swap that reads slightly woodier. A pinch of granulated sugar dissolved in the vinegar does the same acid-rounding job without flavor. Omitting the sweetener entirely is acceptable on a very fresh, mild sherry vinegar, though most aged sherry vinegars benefit from the trace of sweetness against their high acid.'
  - for: 'dijon mustard'
    use: 'whole-grain mustard'
    note: 'Whole-grain mustard emulsifies nearly as well (the same seed-coat mucilage) and leaves visible seed flecks in the dressing, which reads more rustic. Use the same 5 g. The dressing holds a touch less tightly since fewer particles are ground fine, so whisk in the oil a little more slowly.'

notes: |
  Why maceration is the whole recipe. Raw shallot owes its harsh bite to allyl sulfide compounds generated when the alliinase enzyme contacts sulfur precursors at the moment of cutting. Submerging the minced shallot in salted acid does two things at once: the salt draws water out of the cells through osmosis, and the low pH slows the enzymatic reaction that keeps generating pungent volatiles. Ten minutes is enough to take a shallot from sharp-and-oniony to soft-and-pickle-bright. A shallot dropped into a finished dressing at the end, by contrast, keeps reading raw-hot for the first hour and is the most common reason a homemade vinaigrette tastes aggressive. The pink tint the vinegar takes on is anthocyanin leaching from the shallot skin and is correct.

  Why sherry vinegar over red wine vinegar. Sherry vinegar (vinagre de Jerez) is made from sherry wine and aged in oak by a solera system, which gives it a higher acetic load (around 7% against red wine vinegar's 6%) and far more aromatic complexity: nutty, faintly caramel, with an oxidized oak depth that red wine vinegar lacks. That complexity is why it carries a bistro house dressing where red wine vinegar reads simpler. This dressing is deliberately distinct from a plain red-wine vinaigrette on three axes: the sherry's oaked depth, the macerated-shallot aromatic, and the toasted-walnut oil backbone.

  Why a third of the oil is walnut oil. All-olive-oil vinaigrette is clean and correct, but a fraction of walnut oil ties this dressing to its Burgundian home, the warm frisée-aux-lardons and the endive salads of bistro tradition, where toasted-nut notes pair with bitter greens and aged cheese. Walnut oil is too fragile and too assertive to carry the whole dressing, so it rides at one-third while olive oil carries the body and the keeping quality. Keep the walnut oil cold and fresh; its polyunsaturated fat oxidizes quickly and a rancid bottle ruins the dressing.

  The 3:1 ratio and where to move it. Ninety grams of oil to thirty grams of vinegar is the classical 3:1 by weight, calibrated to a salad where the dressing is a discreet partner to the leaves rather than a sharp slap. Because sherry vinegar is more acidic than most, this ratio already leans bright; cooks who want a softer plate can push to 4:1 (add 20 to 30 g more oil), and the creamy crème-fraiche modification softens it further. Bitter greens (frisée, endive, radicchio) and rich composed plates (a warm goat-cheese toast, lardons, a poached egg) take the dressing at full sharpness; tender butter lettuce wants it pulled milder.

  Where it goes. This is the everyday French bistro house dressing: it dresses a frisée-aux-lardons, a green salad alongside a roast chicken, an endive-and-walnut salad with blue cheese, a composed plate of poached leeks or asparagus, or a warm lentil salad where the acid cuts the earthiness. Build a small batch fresh per meal rather than holding a jar; a freshly built vinaigrette and a two-day-old jar are different dressings, and the shallot maceration in particular is at its best in the first hour.
