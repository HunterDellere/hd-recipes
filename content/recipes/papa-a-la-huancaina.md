steps:
  - text: 'Phase 1, boil the potatoes and eggs. Bring a large pot of water (3 L) to a boil with the 15 g salt. Add the 800 g whole potatoes (skins on); reduce to a steady boil; cook 15 to 20 minutes until tender when pierced with a fork (the cook time depends on the potato size; check at 15 minutes). Drain; cool 5 minutes; peel while still warm (the skins lift easily on warm potatoes), then slice into 1 cm thick rounds. Set aside at room temperature. Meanwhile boil the 6 eggs: cover with cold water in a small pot, bring to a boil, cook 10 minutes, transfer to an ice bath. Peel and halve.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Phase 2, blend the huancaína sauce. In a blender, combine the 60 g neutral oil, 60 g ají amarillo paste, 200 g crumbled queso fresco, 150 g evaporated milk, 30 g soda crackers, 8 g garlic, 3 g salt, and 15 g lime juice. Blend 60 to 90 seconds at high speed until completely smooth and creamy. Scrape down the sides at the midpoint. The sauce should read deeply orange-yellow, thick enough to coat the back of a spoon, and structurally creamy without being heavy. Taste and adjust salt; the cheese provides most of the salt so adjust carefully. If too thick, add 15 g more milk; if too thin, add 5 g more crackers and blend again.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Phase 3, assemble the plates. For each plate: lay 1 lettuce leaf flat on the plate; arrange 3 to 4 potato slices on the lettuce (slightly overlapping). Spoon a generous portion of the huancaína sauce (about 80 g per plate) over the potatoes, covering them completely. Garnish each plate with 1 hard-boiled egg half (yolk-side up), 2 black olives, and a small pinch of chopped parsley.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Serve immediately or refrigerate up to 4 hours. Papa a la huancaína is structurally a cold dish and reads better at cool room temperature than at refrigerator-cold; pull from the refrigerator 15 minutes before serving if held cold. The structural eating motion is to take a forkful of potato-and-sauce together, with bites of egg, olive, and lettuce alongside.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Papa a la huancaína is the structural Peruvian appetizer and one of the most successful cold-potato preparations in any cuisine. The technique is conceptually simple (boil potato, blend sauce, assemble) but the structural complexity comes from the ají amarillo and queso fresco sauce, which is structurally Peruvian and one of the most distinctive sauce preparations of South America. Master papa a la huancaína and the sauce transfers to ocopa, causa rellena, and dozens of other Peruvian cold-and-warm dishes.

  The huancaína sauce is the dish. The sauce, not the potato, is what defines papa a la huancaína; the potato is the structural vehicle. Make the sauce with care: blend until completely smooth, taste for salt and acid balance, adjust the consistency to a thick coating sauce that holds on the potato without running. A thin sauce slides off the potato; a thick sauce reads pasty; the structural canonical sauce is the consistency of warm Bechamel.

  The cracker is structural. Soda crackers (saltines) are the canonical Peruvian thickener and contribute structural body, salt, and a faint cracker-flavor that integrates into the cheese-and-chili sauce. Bread and panko work as substitutes; flour and cornstarch produce a different sauce with a recognizably wrong texture. Use saltines when possible.

  The queso fresco is structural. The Peruvian queso fresco (or Mexican queso fresco; the two are nearly interchangeable) is structurally the cheese for huancaína; the slightly salty, slightly tangy, soft-crumbly character produces the structural sauce flavor. Feta is the closest international substitute and works mechanically (reduce added salt). Avoid hard cheeses (parmesan, pecorino) which produce a structurally wrong sauce.

  The yellow potato base is structural. Peruvian yellow potatoes (papa amarilla) are the canonical base; their creamy texture and faint sweetness pair structurally with the chili-cheese sauce. Yukon Gold is the closest North American substitute. Boil the potatoes whole with the skins on (peeling after boiling produces a structurally creamier texture than boiling peeled), then slice into 1 cm rounds at warm temperature (easier to handle than cold).

  The hard-boiled egg and black olive garnish are structural. The structural Peruvian plate is incomplete without both: the egg adds structural protein and visual contrast; the olive adds structural brine. The lettuce leaf base adds structural color and a fresh element. Skipping any of the three reads as structurally incomplete service.

  Versatility. The huancaína sauce works far beyond the canonical potato preparation. Use it as: a sauce for boiled cauliflower or broccoli (cold or warm), a dressing for cold pasta salad, a dipping sauce for grilled vegetables (asparagus, bell pepper), a topping for grilled chicken or fish, a spread for sandwiches, or a base for a Peruvian chicken salad (mix shredded poached chicken with the sauce, plate over lettuce). The sauce is structurally one of the most versatile Peruvian condiments.

  Storage. The assembled plate is best within 4 hours of assembly. The sauce alone keeps 5 days refrigerated; thickens slightly in the cold and may need a splash of milk to restore the original consistency. The boiled potato keeps 2 days refrigerated; bring to cool room temperature before assembly. Make the sauce in advance (double or triple batch) and assemble plates fresh.
