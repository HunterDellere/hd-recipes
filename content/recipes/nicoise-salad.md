before_you_start:
  - 'This is a composed salad served at room temperature, not warm and not chilled. Plan for every cooked component (tuna, eggs, potatoes, haricots verts) to come off the heat at least 20 minutes before plating so they cool to room temp without going cold-from-the-fridge.'
  - 'Pull the tuna loin from the fridge 45 minutes ahead to temper. A cold loin dropped into the oil bath drops the bath temperature below the 60 C poach window and overcooks the exterior before the center warms.'
  - 'Salt-cure the sliced tomatoes 20 to 30 minutes ahead on a rack so they shed water. Unsalted tomatoes water down the plate at room temperature and dilute the vinaigrette.'
  - 'Build the anchovy-dijon vinaigrette first so the shallot has 15 minutes to mellow in the acid. Raw shallot dropped onto the plate at the last moment reads harsh against the oil-poached tuna.'

steps:
  - text: 'Pull the 400 g / 14 oz tuna loin from the fridge 30 minutes before cooking and rub it with the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt. The salt initiates surface cure while the loin warms toward 18 °C; an ice-cold loin entering 60 °C oil drops the bath temperature past the target window.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'In a small saucepan that fits the loin snugly, combine the 500 g / 2 cups extra-virgin olive oil, the 2 bay leaves, the 4 g lemon zest strips, and the 2 smashed garlic cloves. Bring the oil to 60 °C / 140 °F over low heat, monitoring with an instant-read thermometer. The aromatics infuse during the climb.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Lower the tuna into the oil. Hold the bath at 60 °C / 140 °F for 12 minutes for rare-medium centre (still translucent, lightly flaking). The protein denatures at this temperature without driving off the oil-soluble glutamates that read as savory depth at room temperature. Lift the loin onto a rack, reserve the oil for cooking another day.'
    technique: 'oil-poaching'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'While the tuna poaches, set a small saucepan of water to a hard boil. Lower the 4 fridge-cold eggs in with a slotted spoon, set a timer for 6 minutes 30 seconds. The fixed water mass and the cold-egg start make the timer reproducible; deviating by 30 seconds shifts you from jammy to set yolk.'
    technique: 'jammy-egg'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'When the timer hits, transfer the eggs straight into an ice bath. Hold 5 minutes; the cold shock arrests carryover and contracts the white away from the shell, which is what makes peeling clean.'
    technique: 'ice-bath-shock'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Bring the seasoned 2 L water with the 20 g / 2 tbsp kosher salt to a gentle boil. Add the 400 g / 14 oz waxy potatoes whole; reduce to a tremor. Cook 14 to 18 minutes until a paring knife meets the centre with no resistance. Cooking gently (not at a hard boil) keeps the skins intact and the cells from rupturing into chalky mashed-potato territory.'
    time_min: 18
  - text: 'Drain the potatoes and let them dry on the rack 5 minutes; cut larger ones into halves while the surface starch is still warm and tacky, which lets a spoonful of the vinaigrette grip later.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'In a wide pot, bring the 3 L water with the 30 g / 3 tbsp kosher salt to a hard rolling boil. Drop the 250 g / 9 oz haricots verts; cook 90 seconds for crisp-tender centre. Brief contact with vigorously boiling salted water sets the chlorophyll without giving the cell walls time to break down.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Lift the 250 g / 9 oz haricots verts directly into the same ice bath the eggs vacated. Hold 2 minutes, then drain on a towel. The shock locks the chlorophyll green; without it, residual heat shifts the beans toward gray-olive within 10 minutes.'
    technique: 'ice-bath-shock'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Toss the 400 g / 14 oz tomato wedges with the 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt in a colander. Let them drain 15 minutes. The salt pulls cellular water out and concentrates glutamate; without this step the tomato weeps watery juice into the salad and dilutes the vinaigrette.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Build the vinaigrette in a small bowl. Whisk the 12 g / 4 minced anchovy fillets, 15 g / 1 tbsp dijon mustard, 25 g grated shallot, 30 g / 2 tbsp red wine vinegar, and 10 g / 2 tsp lemon juice until the anchovy fully dissolves into the acid. Stream in the 90 g / 6 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil while whisking; the mustard provides the surface-active agents that hold the temporary emulsion. Crack in the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper.'
    technique: 'vinaigrette-emulsion'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Slice the rested tuna across the grain into 1 cm / ⅜-inch slabs. Peel the eggs and halve them lengthwise — the yolk should bead at the cut, not run.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Compose, do not toss. On a wide platter, lay the warm potatoes, the haricots verts, and the drained tomato wedges in distinct piles. Tuck the tuna slabs and halved eggs alongside. Scatter the 80 g nicoise olives, the 15 g / 1 tbsp capers, and the 10 g basil. Spoon the vinaigrette over each pile so the dressing reads on each component, not pooled at the bottom of the platter.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Taste at the table. The dish wants to land room-temperature, salty from anchovy and capers, acidic from vinegar and lemon, with the tuna and yolk providing the only soft notes against the snap of the beans and the give of the potatoes. If a single component reads bland, salt that component directly with a 1 g / pinch of fine sea salt rather than adding more vinaigrette.'

notes: |
  The structural argument: niçoise is a salting curriculum disguised as a salad. The tuna gets a dry brine, the potatoes get a heavily salted boil, the haricots verts get a heavily salted blanch, the tomatoes get a 15-minute salt cure, and the vinaigrette carries anchovy-and-caper salt. Each component arrives at the platter pre-seasoned to its appropriate level for its own texture; the vinaigrette is finishing acid, not seasoning rescue.

  Why oil-poach the tuna at 60 °C: myosin denatures around 50 °C and actin around 66 °C; staying between drives a uniform translucent-pink centre with intact moisture. Higher and the loin reads chalky and dry; lower and the cure is perceptibly raw. Oil is the heat-transfer medium because it is gentler than water and because the oil-soluble flavor compounds infuse rather than diffuse out.

  No cream, no mayonnaise, no mustard-cream emulsion. The dressing is a temporary vinaigrette held by the dijon's mucilage and the anchovy's surface-active proteins; it will break in the bowl after 20 minutes, which is correct. Spoon over individual components rather than tossing.

  Plate room-temperature, not chilled. Cold tomato is bland; cold tuna is leathery; cold potato is starchy. Composed at 18 to 20 °C, every component reads at its volatility peak.
