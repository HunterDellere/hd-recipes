source:
  name: 'Original, built on the yōshoku katsu dredge and the French sole-meunière tradition'
  note: 'The dish borrows the panko dredge from the Japanese katsu canon and the thin-fillet pan-fry from French sole meunière, but resolves the central problem of crusting a flatfish: a flounder fillet is too thin and too delicate to breade on both faces and flip without shattering the flake. The fix is a one-sided crust. Only the skinned top face is pressed into panko and parmigiano; the fish goes into the pan crust-down, fries undisturbed until the coating is set and deep gold, and is lifted out crust-up without ever being flipped onto its bare side. The parmesan is doing double duty, it is a third of the crust by weight and the primary seasoning, since the fish itself contributes almost no salt or glutamate. The mushrooms and the cabbage are not garnishes; the dry-seared oyster-and-shimeji tangle is the umami anchor of the plate and the caraway cabbage is the acid-and-sweetness counter to the rich fried crust.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The three components cook on separate tracks and the fish is last. Braise the cabbage first (it holds warm happily for 20 minutes and even improves), sear the mushrooms second (they hold warm 10 minutes), and fry the fish last so it hits the table crust-crisp. Working the reverse order leaves you frying delicate fish while cabbage steams and mushrooms weep.'
  - 'The fish is thin and fast. A flounder or sole fillet sets in 3 to 4 minutes total, all of it crust-side-down, and overcooks in seconds past that. Have the crust mixed, the fillets dredged, and the pan hot before anything goes in; there is no time to stop and grate cheese mid-fry.'
  - 'The crust goes on one side only, the skinned top face. Do not flour or crumb the underside. The bare side sits against the pan for a brief final set and the crumbed side stays up, so the fish is lifted out, never flipped.'

steps:
  - text: 'Braise the cabbage first. Melt the 30 g / 2 tbsp butter in a wide lidded skillet over medium heat, add the 2 g / 1 tsp crushed caraway seed, and let it sizzle 30 seconds until fragrant. Lay the 400 g cabbage wedges cut-side down in a single layer and let them take a little color for 2 minutes. Season with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt, pour in the 80 ml / ⅓ cup water or stock, cover, and steam-braise over medium-low for 8 to 10 minutes until a knife slides through the thick core end with only slight resistance. Uncover, raise the heat, and cook off any remaining liquid so the wedges glaze in the butter. Off heat, spoon the 8 g / 1½ tsp cider vinegar over the top and turn the wedges once. Hold warm; the cabbage improves as it sits.'
    time_min: 14
  - text: 'Sear the mushrooms while the cabbage braises. Heat the 12 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a separate skillet (cast iron or stainless) over medium-high until it shimmers. Add the 150 g torn oyster mushrooms and 100 g separated shimeji in a single layer and, critically, do not touch them or salt them for the first 2 minutes. The mushrooms release their water first; only once that water has boiled off and the pan is dry do the cut faces begin to brown. Sear 4 to 5 minutes total, tossing only after the first browning, until the edges are deep gold and the tangle has shrunk by half. Now add the 15 g / 1 tbsp butter, the 6 g / 2 sliced garlic cloves, the 1 g / ½ tsp thyme, and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt; toss 60 seconds until the garlic is fragrant and the butter browns lightly. Pull off heat and hold warm.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Set up the crust and dredge the fish. In a shallow bowl combine the 60 g / 1 cup panko, the 40 g / ½ cup microplaned parmigiano, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper; rub the cheese into the crumbs with your fingers so it distributes evenly. Put the 30 g / ¼ cup flour on a small plate and the beaten egg in a second shallow bowl. Season the four 400 g fillets on both sides with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt. Now crumb one side only, the smoother skinned top face of each fillet: press the top face into the flour and shake off the excess, brush that same floured face with beaten egg, then press it firmly into the panko-parmesan so a dense even layer sticks. Leave the underside completely bare. Set the dredged fillets crust-side-up on a plate.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Fry the fish crust-side-down. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide nonstick or well-seasoned skillet over medium-high until it shimmers. Lay the fillets into the pan crust-side-down and press each gently flat with a fish spatula for 5 seconds to seat the crust against the oil. Fry undisturbed for 2½ to 3 minutes: the crust should turn deep gold and you should see the flesh at the edges turning opaque and creeping up from the bottom. When the crust is set and browned, add the 20 g / 1½ tbsp butter to the pan around the fillets and let it foam and brown; tilt and spoon the foaming butter around (not over) the crust for 30 seconds. The flesh should now be opaque nearly to the top.'
    technique: 'pan-fry'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Set the top and lift out, never flip. The fish is thin, so the crust-side-down fry has already cooked it most of the way through. If the very top surface still looks translucent, either flip the fillets bare-side-down for a final 20 to 30 seconds (only if your crust is fully set and you have a confident spatula), or simply cover the pan off the direct heat for 30 seconds and let residual steam finish the top. The goal is opaque, just-set flesh and an intact crust. Lift each fillet out crust-side-up onto a warmed plate. A flounder fillet pulled the instant it turns opaque is silky; 30 seconds past that it goes chalky, so err early.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Plate. Set two crusted fillets on each warmed plate, crust facing up. Pile the seared oyster-and-shimeji mushrooms over or beside the fish so their browned-butter juices run into the crust. Lay a couple of butter-braised cabbage wedges alongside and spoon a little of their glaze over them. Scatter the 5 g / 2 tbsp chopped parsley across the plate. Serve with the lemon wedges; a firm squeeze of lemon over the crust and fish at the table is the finishing acid that lifts the whole plate, the parmesan and mushrooms carry the salt and umami, and the lemon is what makes the mild fish sing.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why the crust goes on one side only. A flounder fillet is 1 to 1.5 cm at its thickest and made of short, delicate muscle blocks that shear apart under almost no force. A conventional both-sides breading requires flipping the fillet in the pan to brown the second face, and that flip is where a crumbed flatfish fillet comes apart, the flake breaks along its natural seams and half the crust sloughs into the oil. The one-sided crust sidesteps the problem entirely. The crumbed top face fries against the oil, sets into a rigid golden shell, and the fish is lifted out crust-up without ever landing on its bare side. The thin fillet is cooked through by the single crust-side-down contact plus a brief top-set, so the second-side browning a katsu needs is unnecessary here.

  Why parmesan is in the crust, not just on top. Flounder contributes almost no salt and very little glutamate; it is the mildest common food fish. A plain panko crust would fry crisp but taste of little. Grating parmigiano into the crumb at a third of the crust weight solves the anti-bland problem structurally: the cheese browns alongside the panko, contributes its own Maillard nuttiness, and seasons every bite of crust with salt and glutamate so the crust itself is the flavor engine of the dish. This is why the fish gets only a light direct salting; the crust is doing most of the seasoning work.

  The mushrooms are the umami anchor, not a garnish. The dry-sear is the whole technique: oyster and shimeji mushrooms are 80 to 90% water, and that water must boil off completely before the cut faces can reach the Maillard threshold and brown. Salting early pulls the water out osmotically and traps the mushrooms in a steaming, gray, rubbery state; salting only after they have browned is non-negotiable. The browned mushrooms bring the deep savory floor that the lean fish lacks, and finishing them in butter with garlic and thyme ties them to the same browned-butter register as the fish crust. Piling them over the fish lets their pan juices soak into the crust, which is the intended eat.

  Why butter-braised cabbage and not a raw slaw. A rich fried-crust main wants a warm, sweet, acid-cut vegetable rather than a cold crunchy one. Braising green cabbage wedges in butter with caraway develops the cabbage''s natural sugars and softens the sulfur funk into something round and sweet, while leaving enough structure that the wedge holds on the plate. The cider vinegar off the heat is the counterweight: it cuts the butter, brightens the sweetness, and echoes the lemon on the fish, so the whole plate reads balanced rather than uniformly rich. Caraway is the classic central-European cabbage partner and its carvone note pairs cleanly with both the browned butter and the mild fish.

  Timing and holding. The only component that must hit the table hot and fresh is the fish, so it is fried last. The braised cabbage genuinely improves held warm for 15 to 20 minutes as the flavors marry, and the seared mushrooms hold well for 10 minutes off heat and can be given a 30-second reheat in their own pan just before plating. Fry the fish into that finished mise; a crusted flounder fillet is at its peak in the first two minutes off the heat, when the crust is still shattering-crisp and the flesh is silk.

  Sourcing the fish. Flounder, sole, and fluke fillets are reliable at Western fish counters (Whole Foods, Northeast US markets) and at Asian seafood markets, where whole flatfish is cheaper and fresher; ask the counter to fillet and skin a whole flounder if you want the best price and the freshness cues a whole fish shows. The fillets should be bright white, faintly translucent, springy to the touch, and smell of clean sea; pass on any that are gray-soft, dull, or carry an ammonia note. Pat them bone-dry before dredging, because a wet fillet will not hold the flour base layer and the crust will slide off in the pan.
