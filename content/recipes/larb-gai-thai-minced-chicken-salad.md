before_you_start:
  - 'Sticky rice is the partner and must start FIRST. Soak 200 g / 1 cup Thai glutinous rice in cold water at least 4 hours ahead (overnight is better), then steam in a bamboo basket while you build the larb. A 30-minute steam finishes within minutes of the larb hitting the plate.'
  - 'Toast the raw glutinous rice for khao khua early, it must cool fully (about 15 minutes) before grinding to powder. Run the toast in parallel with the chicken hand-chop.'
  - 'Pull mint, cilantro, scallion, and culantro from the fridge and dry them before anything else. Cold wet herbs drop the dish temperature when they hit the warm chicken and dilute the dressing.'
  - 'Larb does not hold. Once dressed, the toasted rice powder absorbs liquid every minute and the herbs wilt within 20 to 30 minutes. Plate and serve immediately.'

steps:
  - text: 'Make the toasted chili powder. Set a small dry skillet (cast iron or stainless, no oil) over medium heat. Add 6 to 8 dried red Thai bird chilies (about 5 g) and toast, shaking constantly, for 60 to 90 seconds until the chilies turn slightly darker, smell smoky-fruity, and become brittle. Pull at the first wisp of acrid smoke, past that and the chilies turn bitter. Cool, then grind coarse (mortar or spice grinder) to yield 3 g / 1½ tsp prik pon. Set aside.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Make the toasted glutinous-rice powder (khao khua). In the same dry skillet over medium-low heat, add 25 g / 2 tbsp raw glutinous rice. Toast 6 to 8 minutes, shaking the pan every 30 seconds, until the grains turn from chalk-white to even amber and smell like popcorn-and-toasted-bread. The color cue is the entire grain reading the same medium-amber, not patchy white-and-brown. Cool 2 minutes, then pound in a mortar or pulse in a spice grinder to a coarse powder, grain texture between sand and fine sea salt, never to a fine flour. Yields 15 g / 2 tbsp khao khua. Set aside.'
    technique: 'dry-toasting'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Hand-chop the 500 g chicken thigh on a heavy cutting board with a sharp chef knife. Working through the meat in a rocking motion, chop to a coarse mince of 4 to 5 mm pieces. The hand-chopped texture is the structural identity of larb, the meat reads as discernible bite-sized pieces with rough surfaces that hold dressing, against a food-processed paste which goes wet-and-uniform like a meat-pâté. Allow 5 to 7 minutes for hand-chopping; do not rush.'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Heat a wide skillet (28 to 30 cm / 11 to 12 inch) over medium-high heat. Add the 60 ml / ¼ cup water and bring to a bare simmer. Add the chopped chicken in a thin layer, breaking up clumps with a wooden spoon. The technique is low-water poaching, not searing, larb chicken is cooked in its own moisture so it stays juicy and tender; a hard sear toughens the lean thigh and produces a different dish (gai pad). Stir constantly, breaking up clumps, for 3 to 4 minutes until the meat is opaque throughout, no pink remains, and the pan liquid has reduced to a glaze that coats the meat. Pull off heat. Internal temperature target is 75 °C / 165 °F.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'While the chicken is still hot in the pan, add the 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce, 6 g / 1½ tsp shaved palm sugar, and the 3 g / 1½ tsp toasted chili powder directly to the pan. Stir 30 seconds. The residual pan heat dissolves the sugar fully and blooms the chili powder into the chicken juices, the chili oils need fat or hot liquid to release; a cold-mixed chili powder reads dusty and one-dimensional. Transfer the chicken and pan liquid to a wide mixing bowl.'
    time_min: 1
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
  - text: 'Let the chicken cool 2 to 3 minutes to warm-not-hot, then add the 45 g / 3 tbsp lime juice. Adding lime to actively-hot chicken cooks the lime juice and dulls its top notes; lime added at warm-not-hot retains the bright citrus aromatic that defines the salad. Stir to combine.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add the 15 g / 2 tbsp toasted glutinous-rice powder. Stir thoroughly. The starch hydrates in the warm pan-juice-and-lime mixture and thickens it to a clinging slurry that coats every chopped meat piece, this is what makes larb structurally a salad rather than a wet meat dish. The rice powder also contributes a nutty-popcorn aromatic and a faint grainy mouthfeel that is the defining sensory signature of larb. Taste, the dish should read salty-front (fish sauce), sour-prominent (lime), warmly-sweet (palm sugar, but quiet), with the chili heat building in the throat after the swallow. If flat, add 5 g / 1 tsp more fish sauce; if too sharp, 5 g / 1 tsp more palm sugar. The salt-acid axis is the soul of larb; do not overdo the sweet.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Add the 60 g sliced shallot, 40 g sliced scallion, 20 g mint leaves, 20 g chopped cilantro, and 5 g culantro if using. Toss with two large spoons or clean hands until the herbs are evenly distributed through the meat, the herb-to-meat ratio in larb is high; the dish should look as much green as it does brown. Do not over-toss, the aromatic oils in the herbs are released by handling and excess tossing produces a slightly bruised, herbal-bitter character.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Pile the larb onto a flat plate (not a deep bowl, the dressing should not pool). Serve immediately with a basket of warm sticky rice, a plate of raw vegetables (cabbage wedges, cucumber spears, long bean, Thai eggplant if available), and the optional extra raw chili on the side. The eating method is canonical: pinch a small ball of sticky rice with the right hand, press into the larb to pick up meat and dressing, eat with a piece of raw cabbage or cucumber as the foil. The dish does not hold; the toasted rice powder absorbs more dressing every minute, and the herbs wilt within 20 to 30 minutes from the salt and warm meat.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The thesis is texture-by-starch. Larb is not a wet salad; it is a salad held together by the hydrated starch of toasted glutinous-rice powder. Every other element (the chopped meat, the herbs, the chili, the lime) lives or fails based on whether the rice powder thickens the dressing into a slurry that clings to each chopped meat piece. A larb made without toasted rice powder, or with rice powder added too late to absorb properly, reads as a wet meat-and-herb mixture with dressing pooled at the bottom; the textural signature is gone. This is the single most-skipped step in non-Lao adaptations of larb and the most consistent way the dish gets ruined.

  Why hand-chopped meat. The traditional tool is a heavy cleaver on a wooden round, hand-chopping the meat to 4 to 5 mm dice with rough surfaces. The rough surfaces hold dressing and rice powder in a way that smooth-cut or food-processed meat does not, the food processor produces a paste that wets and emulsifies the dressing into a meat-pâté texture; the hand-chop produces discrete bite-sized pieces with dressing sitting on rough faces. Buying pre-ground chicken from the supermarket is the Western shortcut; it works in a pinch but the meat is finer than ideal and tends toward the wet-paste failure mode unless you are aggressive with the rice powder.

  Why low-water poaching. The standard Western move would be to brown the chicken in a hot pan with a little oil; the Lao move is to cook the chicken in a small pool of water until the water mostly evaporates and the meat is tender. The reason: lean ground chicken (and pork shoulder, and chopped fish) toughens and dries when seared, and larb wants juicy-tender meat that absorbs dressing. The 60 ml / ¼ cup of water serves two purposes, gentle thermal carrier so the meat reaches 75 °C without surface drying, and a deglazed flavor base that becomes part of the dressing. By the time the water has reduced to a glaze, the meat is fully cooked and seasoned by its own juices.

  The four-flavor adjustment, larb-style. Larb does not run the full salty-sour-sweet-spicy-balanced palette of som tum; it skews salty-and-sour with a quiet sweet undertone and aggressive heat. The dressing as written (30 g fish sauce, 45 g lime, 6 g palm sugar, 3 g chili powder) is calibrated to a roughly 1:1.5 salt-to-sour ratio with a low sugar fraction and medium heat. For Isaan-canonical seasoning, push the chili powder to 5 to 8 g and the fish sauce to 35 g; the dish then reads more aggressive in heat and salt. For first-time Western eaters, dial the chili powder to 1 to 2 g.

  Why khao khua, specifically. Khao khua is the Lao name for the technique of dry-toasting raw glutinous rice and grinding it to a coarse powder; the same technique is shared with Vietnamese cooking (thinh, used in nem chua and bi cuon) and Cambodian cooking. Glutinous rice, as opposed to long-grain jasmine, is what gives khao khua its signature slightly-sticky mouthfeel and its tendency to bind dressing into a clinging slurry. The toasting develops Maillard products that contribute the popcorn-and-toasted-bread aromatic, and the coarse grind preserves a faint grainy textural signature in each bite. Pre-toasted khao khua sold in plastic bags at Thai markets is acceptable but dull; freshly toasted has a top note that fades within days.

  How larb is eaten. The Lao and Isaan eating method is canonical: a basket of warm sticky rice on the table, a flat plate of larb in the middle, a plate of raw vegetables (cabbage, cucumber, long bean, Thai eggplant) and herbs (mint sprigs, holy basil, sawtooth herb) for foil. Each diner pinches a small ball of sticky rice with the right hand, presses it into the larb to pick up meat-and-dressing, and eats it with a bite of raw vegetable. Eating larb with a fork from a deep bowl of rice misses the architecture; the dish is built to be hand-pinched.

  Salt safety on chicken. The 60 ml / ¼ cup water poach holds the pan temperature at boiling (100 °C / 212 °F) and the chopped chicken reaches 75 °C / 165 °F internal within 3 minutes. This meets the USDA chicken safety target. The glaze stage that follows (after the water reduces) is post-safety; the meat at that point is at sub-boiling temperatures because the bulk of the water has evaporated, but already past the safety threshold. The technique is structurally safe even though it does not look like a hard sear.
