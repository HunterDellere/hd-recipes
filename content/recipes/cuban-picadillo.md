steps:
  - text: 'Heat the 30 ml / 2 tbsp olive oil in a wide skillet or shallow Dutch oven over medium heat. Add the 150 g diced onion and 150 g diced green pepper; sweat 5 to 6 minutes until soft and translucent, with the pepper showing light golden edges. The sofrito should be soft and aromatic, not Maillard-browned.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Add the 20 g / 5 cloves minced garlic, 5 g / 2 tsp cumin, 3 g / 1 tsp oregano, and 1 bay leaf. Cook 60 seconds, stirring constantly, until fragrant and the cumin has darkened the oil to brick red.'
    technique: 'blooming-aromatics'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Increase heat to medium-high. Add the 500 g ground beef. Break it up with a wooden spoon and cook 6 to 7 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the beef has lost all pink and the bottom of the pan has developed a brown fond. Do not stir constantly; let the beef sit and Maillard-brown in patches. The fond is the structural source of the dish''s depth.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 7
  - text: 'Drain off all but about 30 ml / 2 tbsp of the rendered beef fat (an 80/20 ground beef yields about 60 ml of fat at this stage). Season the beef with the 6 g / 1½ tsp salt and 2 g / 1 tsp pepper. Push the beef to the edges of the pan; add the 15 g / 1 tbsp tomato paste to the center; cook 90 seconds, stirring the paste in place until it darkens from bright red to brick red. Stir the bloomed paste into the beef.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Add the 240 g tomato sauce, 80 ml / ⅓ cup white wine, and 120 ml / ½ cup water. Scrape the fond off the pan bottom with a wooden spoon. Bring to a steady simmer, partially covered, and cook 12 to 15 minutes. The liquid should reduce by about a third and the picadillo should look saucy but not soupy.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Stir in the 50 g raisins, 60 g sliced olives, and 20 g rinsed capers. Cover and cook 3 more minutes; the raisins plump in the steam and the olives release their brine into the sauce. Fish out and discard the bay leaf.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Off heat, stir in the 15 ml / 1 tbsp red wine vinegar. The acid lifts the dish; without it the picadillo reads as one-note sweet-salty. Taste; adjust salt if needed (rarely, since the olives and capers carry the salt budget).'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Serve over white rice, with a side of fried sweet plantain (maduros) and a small green salad. The canonical Cuban-Sunday plate adds black beans.'
    time_min: 1

notes: |
  The Cuban sweet-salty-acid triad. Picadillo is the canonical Cuban demonstration of the sweet-salt-acid balance: raisins for the sweet bass note, olives and capers for the salt-savor middle, vinegar for the high-acid finish. Skip any one and the dish flattens to a generic ground-beef stew. The raisins are not optional, and they are not "weird"; they are the structural Cuban move that distinguishes picadillo from Mexican picadillo (which omits them) or Tex-Mex picadillo (which substitutes potato).

  Why drain the fat. An 80/20 ground beef renders about 60 ml of fat by the end of step 3. Leaving all of it in the pan produces a greasy, heavy picadillo. Draining most of it (leaving about 30 ml) preserves enough fat to bloom the tomato paste and carry the spices, without making the finished dish feel oily.

  The pimento-stuffed olive rule. The red pimento inside the green manzanilla olive contributes a faint paprika-and-sweet-pepper note that complements the bell pepper sofrito. Olives without pimento read slightly less Cuban. Black olives (canned ripe California olives, Kalamata, Niçoise) are the wrong flavor entirely and will pull the dish out of its Cuban frame.

  Pairings. Canonical with white rice, black beans, and fried sweet plantain (maduros). The plantain sweetness reinforces the raisin sweetness; the black beans balance the salt; the rice carries the sauce.

  Storage. Improves overnight as the flavors integrate; the raisins plump further and the sauce thickens. Keeps 4 days refrigerated. Freezes well; portion into 500 g containers. Use leftovers as empanada filling or stuffed potato filling.
