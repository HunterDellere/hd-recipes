source:
  name: 'Tampa Cuban-exile canon (Ybor City, late 19th century)'
  note: 'The Cuban sandwich (Cubano, mixto) originated in the cigar-factory cafes of Ybor City, Tampa, and Key West around the 1890s as a workman''s lunch. The Tampa version includes Genoa salami (the Italian immigrant contribution); the Miami and Cuban-island version omits it. The structural elements are: Cuban bread (light, crackly, similar to French but with a lard-rich crumb), roast pork (canonically leftover from Cuban-mojo pernil), sweet baked ham, Swiss cheese, dill pickle, yellow mustard, all pressed flat in a plancha until the cheese is fully melted and the bread is crackly. No mayonnaise, no lettuce, no tomato; these are American-deli additions that pull the sandwich out of its Cuban frame.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The sandwich is at its best with leftover roast pork from Cuban-style mojo pernil. Plan to make pernil the day before; the cold sliced leftover is structural, both for the citrus-garlic Cuban flavor and the texture. Deli-counter "Cuban roast pork" is the next-best option. Plain rotisserie chicken or generic ham-only sandwich is not a Cubano.'
  - 'Cuban bread (pan cubano) is a soft white loaf with a thin crackly crust, traditionally made with lard for a more tender crumb than French bread. Look at Latin bakeries or Caribbean groceries. Substitute French baguette or Italian ciabatta if unavailable; American sandwich bread or sourdough is the wrong texture and will not press correctly.'
  - 'A panini press, plancha, or sandwich press is ideal. A weighted heavy skillet (cast iron with a second skillet on top) is the structural home substitute. The pressure compresses the sandwich and is the defining sensory feature of a Cubano; an unpressed sandwich is something else entirely.'

steps:
  - text: 'Set up the press: heat a cast iron skillet (or large stainless skillet) over medium heat and a second heavy skillet alongside (this will be the weight). Or preheat a panini press to medium. The press needs to be hot when the sandwich goes on.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Split the 2 Cuban bread loaves horizontally with a serrated knife. Brush the inside (cut side) of each loaf with no butter; the inside is the mustard-and-filling face. Spread 15 g / 1 tbsp of yellow mustard on the inside of each top half of bread (not the bottom; the mustard adheres better to the dry bread side, and stacking the wet filling on dry bread keeps the bottom crisper).'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Build each sandwich in this exact stacking order, bottom to top: bottom bread (no mustard), 100 g sliced roast pork (spread evenly), 60 g sliced sweet ham (overlapping), 45 g Swiss cheese (overlapping), 25 g dill pickle slices (single layer), top bread (mustard-side down). The Swiss goes on the ham, not the pork; this puts the cheese near the hot top bread side first for faster melt. Press the sandwich together firmly with both hands to start the compression.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Brush the outside of both top and bottom of each assembled sandwich with the 30 g / 2 tbsp softened butter (about 7 g per side per sandwich). The butter is what produces the dark golden crackly crust during the press.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Place the first sandwich on the hot skillet. Immediately place the second heated skillet on top, pressing down firmly. Press for 3 to 4 minutes (cast iron weight method) or 2 to 3 minutes (panini press) until the bottom bread is dark mahogany with crisp diagonal lines and crackly when tapped. Lift to check; if not yet there, give another 30 to 60 seconds.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Flip the sandwich. Replace the weight; press the second side 2 to 3 more minutes until the top is also dark mahogany and the sandwich has visibly compressed from its starting height of about 8 cm to about 2 to 2.5 cm. The cheese should be fully molten and beginning to ooze at the edges. Repeat with the second sandwich.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Transfer the pressed sandwich to a cutting board. Cut on a sharp diagonal into 2 pieces with a serrated knife (the diagonal cut maximizes the surface area visible and is the canonical Cuban-cafeteria presentation). Serve immediately; a cooled Cubano loses the textural distinction between the molten cheese and the crackly crust.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  The press is the dish. A Cuban sandwich is defined by the compression: the bread crackles, the cheese melts and fuses with the meats, and the height drops from 8 cm of loose stack to 2 cm of dense slab. Without pressing, the same ingredients on the same bread are a ham-and-cheese, not a Cubano. A panini press is fine; a hot cast iron skillet with a second cast iron weighting from above is the home solution that works just as well.

  Why butter on the outside, not the inside. Butter on the outside conducts heat into the bread and produces Maillard browning on the crust; the dark mahogany surface that crackles when tapped is the structural sensory marker of a properly pressed Cubano. Butter on the inside would just soak in and make the bread soggy without adding crust. Mayo on the outside (a common American shortcut) burns at lower temperatures than butter and produces an off flavor.

  Why no mayo, lettuce, or tomato. The Cubano is defined by what it is not. American deli additions (mayo, lettuce, tomato, sliced onion) push the sandwich into a generic deli frame and dilute the salt-fat-acid balance of pork-ham-Swiss-pickle-mustard. The sandwich is intentionally simple and intentionally Cuban; the canonical 5 fillings plus mustard are not negotiable.

  Storage. A Cubano is a fresh sandwich and does not store; the cheese congeals and the bread softens. Build sandwiches to order; the components themselves keep separately for 4 days (roast pork, ham, cheese in the fridge). Pre-press only at the moment of serving.
