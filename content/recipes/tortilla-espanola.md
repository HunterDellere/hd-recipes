title: 'Tortilla española'
desc: 'The Spanish potato-and-onion-and-egg cake: thinly sliced waxy potatoes and onions slow-poached in a generous bath of olive oil until completely tender (not browned), drained, folded into beaten eggs to rest, then set in a smaller skillet with just enough oil and finished with the canonical plate-flip to brown both sides. Texture is structural: the inside should be just-set, almost custardy at the center; a dry tortilla is a failed tortilla. Eaten warm or at room temperature, sliced into wedges; the canonical Spanish tapas anchor and one of the great egg dishes in any cuisine.'

source:
  name: 'The Spanish national canon. Documented across Spanish regional cookbooks since the 19th century (the modern with-onion form codified through the early 20th century; the without-onion form remains contested and partisan in Spanish home kitchens). The dish is one of the most consumed in Spain; nearly every household and bar has a tortilla in the case at any given moment.'
  note: 'Two structural schools: with onion (con cebolla) and without onion (sin cebolla); the partisanship between the two camps is genuine and the matter is treated as a question of taste rather than canon. The version here is the con-cebolla form, which is the more commonly served modern preparation and the more flavor-developed of the two. The defining technique is the slow olive-oil poach of the potato and onion (the potatoes should never brown; the goal is fully tender potato that breaks apart under fork pressure). The texture target is jugosa (juicy): the inside of the finished tortilla should be just-set, almost custardy; a fully cooked-through, dry tortilla is a regional minority preference and is not the canonical reference. Distinct from frittata (Italian baked open omelet, no plate-flip, generally larger and more structured) and from a flat omelet (French; thinner, browner, eaten hot rather than warm or room-temp).'

steps:
  - text: 'Heat the 250 g / 1 cup plus 2 tbsp olive oil in a wide 28 to 30 cm (11 to 12 inch) skillet (a slope-sided or straight-sided skillet works; not the final cooking pan) over medium heat until shimmering and about 130 to 140°C / 270 to 285°F (a slice of potato dropped in should release small steady bubbles, not aggressive sizzling). Add the 600 g / 3 potatoes (sliced) and 250 g / 1 large onion (sliced) all at once; stir to coat in the oil. Sprinkle the 4 g / ¾ tsp salt over the top.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Reduce heat to medium-low; poach the potato and onion 18 to 22 minutes, stirring gently every 4 to 5 minutes with a wooden spoon (to prevent sticking and to redistribute the cooking), until the potatoes are completely tender and break apart under fork pressure but have not browned (the surface should stay pale yellow and the onion should be soft and translucent-gold, not caramelized). The slow poach is the structural foundation of the dish; rushed potatoes that brown read wrong, undercooked potatoes that retain bite read worse. Trust the fork test: if a potato slice breaks easily under a fork, the poach is done.'
    technique: 'olive-oil-poach'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Set a fine-mesh sieve over a large heatproof bowl. Pour the potato-and-onion-and-oil through the sieve; the oil drains into the bowl below (reserve about 200 g for other cooking) and the potato-and-onion stays in the sieve. Let the potato drain 2 to 3 minutes; the oil should pool below and the potato should be glistening but not dripping.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'In a large mixing bowl, beat the 350 g / 7 eggs with the 3 g / ½ tsp salt until just uniform (no streaks of white, light yellow throughout; do not whip air into the eggs, which produces a souffléd texture). Add the drained potato-and-onion to the eggs; fold gently with a rubber spatula or wooden spoon until every potato slice is coated. Let rest 8 to 10 minutes at room temperature; this rest is structural and non-negotiable. The salt seasons the eggs evenly, the potato breaks down further into the egg matrix (the potato edges soften into the eggs and the mixture homogenizes), and the eggs warm to room temperature for a more even cook.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Heat a 22 to 24 cm (9 to 10 inch) nonstick or well-seasoned carbon steel skillet over medium-high heat. Add 15 g / 1 tbsp of the reserved oil; swirl to coat the bottom and the lower sides of the pan. When the oil is shimmering and just on the edge of smoking, pour in the egg-and-potato mixture all at once. Immediately reduce heat to medium-low. The eggs should sizzle around the edges and the bottom should set within the first minute; the goal is a slow cook on low heat from this point forward.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Cook the tortilla 5 to 6 minutes over medium-low heat, gently shaking the pan every minute to free the bottom (do not stir; the bottom is setting into a single cohesive layer). Use a flexible thin spatula to gently tuck the edges in toward the center as they set, rounding the sides of the tortilla; this builds the canonical disc shape. After 5 to 6 minutes, the bottom should be set and golden when you carefully lift an edge with the spatula, and the top should still be visibly wet (a substantial pool of uncooked egg in the center). The tortilla is now ready for the flip.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'The flip: take a plate slightly larger than the skillet (about 26 cm / 10 inch); invert the plate over the skillet. Holding the plate firmly against the pan with one hand and the skillet handle with the other (use a kitchen towel for grip; the pan is hot), in one confident motion flip the pan upside down so the tortilla lands cooked-side-up on the plate. Set the skillet back on the burner; add 5 g / 1 tsp more reserved oil; slide the tortilla back into the skillet uncooked-side-down. Use the spatula to tuck the edges in. Cook 3 to 4 more minutes over medium-low heat, shaking the pan gently every minute, until the bottom is set and golden and the center is just-set when you gently press the top with the back of a spoon (a slight wobble at the center is correct; firm and rigid is overcooked).'
    technique: 'plate-flip'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Slide the tortilla onto a serving plate. Let rest 5 to 10 minutes before slicing; the residual heat continues to set the center and the texture stabilizes during the rest. Serve warm or at room temperature, sliced into wedges (8 wedges for tapas, 4 wedges for a meal); the canonical Spanish service is on a small plate with a wedge of crusty bread and a small glass of beer or wine, or as part of a larger tapas spread. Tortilla travels especially well as a make-ahead dish: the texture and flavor improve after a 30-minute rest and remain excellent at room temperature for 3 to 4 hours.'
    time_min: 5

modifications:
  - for: 'cuisine pivot'
    to: 'as a tortilla sin cebolla (without onion, the regional minority preference)'
    how: 'Skip the onion entirely; increase the potato to 850 g (to maintain the same total weight). Reduce the egg to 6 large eggs (300 g). The result is structurally tortilla sin cebolla, the canonical pure potato-and-egg form; reads cleaner and more potato-forward, less complex than the with-onion version. The sin-cebolla camp argues that the onion masks the structural simplicity of the dish; the con-cebolla camp argues that the onion provides essential aromatic depth. Both are correct; the choice is a matter of regional and personal preference.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'flavor pivot'
    to: 'as a tortilla with chorizo and red bell pepper'
    how: 'Sauté 100 g of crumbled Spanish cooking chorizo and 150 g of finely diced red bell pepper in 30 g of the oil for 6 to 8 minutes until the chorizo has rendered its red oil and the pepper is softened; reserve. Proceed with the potato-and-onion poach as written; fold the chorizo-and-pepper into the egg mixture with the potato. The result is a more substantial dinner-grade tortilla; pairs especially well with a green salad and a glass of Tempranillo.'
    kind: 'protein'
  - for: 'oven-finished version'
    to: 'as an oven-finished tortilla (for cooks uncomfortable with the plate-flip)'
    how: 'After the egg-and-potato mixture has set in the smaller skillet for 5 to 6 minutes (end of step 6), transfer the pan directly to a preheated 180°C / 350°F oven for 8 to 10 minutes until the center is just-set. Slide onto a plate to serve. The result reads slightly more frittata-adjacent (more set-through, less custardy) and skips the dramatic plate-flip; useful for cooks new to the technique. The plate-flip version produces a more characteristically Spanish texture.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Tortilla española is one of the most structurally precise egg dishes in any cuisine because of the texture target. A tortilla is judged on a single primary axis (texture at the center), and a perfectly executed tortilla has a center that is just-set, almost custardy, with the eggs barely cohering around the soft potato. The wrong texture in either direction is a failed tortilla: a dry, fully-set-through tortilla reads like a potato cake and is the most common home-cook failure mode; a runny, under-set tortilla collapses on the plate and reads like an undercooked omelet. The 5-to-6-minute first cook plus 3-to-4-minute second cook produces the canonical jugosa texture; trust the visual cues (set bottom, just-wobbling top after the flip) and pull the tortilla early rather than late.

  The slow olive-oil poach is structural and non-negotiable. Some home-cook shortcuts attempt to sauté or fry the potato (which browns the surface) or to boil the potato in water (which loses flavor); the canonical Spanish technique is a slow poach in a generous bath of warm olive oil until the potato is fully tender without any browning. The oil temperature should be 130 to 140°C (270 to 285°F), which is below frying temperature; the potato should never sizzle aggressively. The poach takes 18 to 22 minutes and cannot be rushed; the entire dish depends on the potato being fully soft, well-seasoned, and oil-infused before it meets the egg.

  The rest between mixing the eggs and cooking is structural and often skipped. The 8-to-10-minute room-temperature rest serves three functions: the salt distributes evenly through the eggs; the warm potato edges soften further into the egg, building integration; and the cold-from-the-refrigerator eggs warm to room temperature, which means the cook proceeds at a more uniform rate when the eggs hit the hot pan. Skipping the rest produces a tortilla that reads disjointed (the potato and egg separate during cooking, instead of integrating into a single cohesive layer).

  The plate-flip is the canonical dramatic moment and the move that defines tortilla cookery. The technique requires a plate slightly larger than the skillet, confidence, and a willingness to commit to the motion; hesitation produces a half-flipped tortilla on the floor. Cooks new to the flip benefit from practicing with a dry tortilla-sized pan and a plate first; once the motion is comfortable, the actual flip is straightforward. The oven-finished version (see modifications) is a legitimate alternative for cooks who prefer not to flip; the texture difference is real but the dish is still recognizable.

  Versatility. Tortilla española is structurally a make-ahead room-temperature dish and travels brilliantly across applications. Use it as: the canonical Spanish tapas anchor (sliced into small wedges, served on a small plate with a wedge of bread and a glass of wine or beer); a light dinner (4 large wedges with a green salad and a glass of Tempranillo); a picnic dish (sliced and packed in a tin; the dish travels well and stays excellent at room temperature for 3 to 4 hours); a sandwich filling (the bocadillo de tortilla, a wedge of tortilla on a crusty roll with a smear of alioli or sliced tomato; a canonical Spanish handheld); a brunch centerpiece (with a side of pan con tomate, jamón, and Spanish cheeses); a cocktail-party appetizer (cut into small bite-sized squares on a platter); and as a meal-prep anchor (the dish keeps 3 days refrigerated and tastes excellent cold straight from the refrigerator).

  Pairs across Spanish (canonical national dish), Portuguese (a related preparation exists), Latin American Spanish-diaspora cookery, modern American Spanish-influenced kitchens, and contemporary brunch cookery globally. The dish is one of the most successfully exported Spanish foods and is now structurally embedded in modern European and American restaurant menus.

  Storage. Keeps 3 days refrigerated, wrapped tightly; the texture firms slightly in the cold but the flavor improves over the first 24 hours as the egg, potato, and olive oil integrate further. Eat cold straight from the refrigerator (the canonical Spanish late-night snack) or bring to room temperature 30 minutes before service. Do not microwave-reheat; the eggs turn rubbery. Freezing is not recommended; the texture degrades meaningfully on thawing.
