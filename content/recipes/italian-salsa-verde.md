steps:
  - text: 'Soak the 20 g / ½ slice crustless bread in the 30 g / 2 tbsp white wine vinegar for 1 minute, until fully saturated. Squeeze the bread firmly with the back of a spoon to remove the excess vinegar (the bread should be wet but not dripping). The bread provides body and absorbs the sharp acid; the vinegar that comes through is the sauce backbone.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'On a heavy cutting board, mound the 50 g / 1¼ cups parsley leaves, 8 g / 2 garlic cloves, 10 g / 3 anchovy fillets, and 15 g / 2 tbsp capers together. Chop with a heavy chef knife in long even strokes, gathering the pile back to center every 30 seconds. Continue until the mixture is uniform and the pieces are 1 to 2 mm across, with structure still visible (a coarse paste, not a slurry); about 4 to 5 minutes of steady chopping. The hand-chop preserves textural integrity that a food processor destroys.'
    technique: 'knife-chop-not-processor'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Transfer the chopped mixture to a small bowl and add the squeezed bread, breaking it apart with a fork into small pieces. Stir to combine. Add the 10 g / 2 tsp lemon juice, then pour in the 120 g / ½ cup plus 2 tbsp olive oil in a steady stream, stirring with a wooden spoon to disperse the oil through the herb-and-bread mixture. The sauce will not emulsify; salsa verde is structurally a chopped-herb suspension, not an emulsion.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Taste and adjust: salsa verde should read parsley-forward with assertive umami from the anchovy and capers, gentle garlic, and a clean lemon finish. The bread should not be detectable as bread but as background body. If flat, a pinch of the 1 g / ¼ tsp salt; if too aggressive, more oil to soften; if the parsley is overwhelmed, more lemon. Let rest 10 minutes before service so the bread fully integrates and the flavors marry. Serve at room temperature spooned over poached or grilled fish, sliced roast meats, boiled potatoes, white beans, fried or hard-boiled eggs, grilled or boiled vegetables, or grilled bread.'
    time_min: 3

modifications:
  - for: 'body type'
    to: 'as the egg-yolk-bound version (more common in central Italy)'
    how: 'Skip the bread and vinegar soak; replace with the mashed yolk of 1 hard-boiled egg, smashed to a paste with the back of a fork before adding to the chopped herb mixture. The yolk lecithin gives a slight emulsifying action and a more luxurious mouthfeel; common in central and southern Italian preparations. Pairs especially well with poached fish and boiled vegetables.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'cuisine pivot'
    to: 'as a sauce gribiche (the French cousin)'
    how: 'Add 1 hard-boiled egg, whites and yolks separately chopped fine; double the capers to 30 g; replace the bread with 1 tsp Dijon mustard for the binding action. The result is the French sauce gribiche, structurally adjacent and used identically for poached meats and fish; reads more aggressive and more textured.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'food-processor speed'
    to: 'in a small food processor instead of by hand'
    how: 'Combine the parsley, garlic, anchovy, capers, and lemon juice in a small food processor (1 to 2 cup capacity); pulse 8 to 10 times to a coarse-medium chop, scraping the sides between pulses. Add the soaked bread and pulse 2 more times to incorporate. Transfer to a bowl and stir in the olive oil by hand; do not run the oil through the processor (the high-speed shear pulverizes the herbs into a paste with no integrity). Faster than the hand-chop and acceptable; not as good.'
    kind: 'occasion'

notes: |
  Italian salsa verde occupies the same operational role as Argentine chimichurri (a raw, herb-forward, oil-mounted finishing sauce) but the architecture is fundamentally different. Chimichurri is a vinegar-and-oil suspension with chopped parsley and oregano floating in a brick-acidic bath; the herbs read fresh and the acid is the dominant flavor. Italian salsa verde is a chopped-herb-and-anchovy paste bound with olive oil, where the parsley provides volume, the anchovy and capers provide the umami spine, and the bread or yolk gives body; the acid is restrained (a finishing squeeze of lemon, not a vinegar bath), and the umami is the dominant flavor. Both sauces finish grilled and poached protein, but the flavor logic is opposite.

  This is also distinct from Mexican salsa verde (a tomatillo-based cooked or raw green salsa with cilantro, onion, jalapeño, and lime) and from German grüne soße (the seven-herb Frankfurt sauce, parsley and chervil and chives and sorrel and burnet and cress and borage, bound with sour cream and yogurt). All three are called "green sauce" in their respective languages but share no architectural DNA.

  Versatility. Italian salsa verde is the universal finishing sauce for plain protein. Use it on: poached or boiled meat (the bollito misto canonical pairing, especially boiled beef and tongue), poached or steamed fish (especially salmon, halibut, cod), grilled fish, roast leg of lamb, sliced roast pork, hard-boiled eggs (the egg-and-anchovy version), boiled or roasted potatoes (a classic Italian pairing), white beans (cannellini, butter beans, runner beans, dressed with salsa verde while still warm), boiled or grilled vegetables (asparagus, leeks, fennel, broccoli, cauliflower), grilled bread (as a dip or spread), and as a finish for soups (a spoonful stirred into bean or vegetable soup at service). Pairs across Italian, broader Mediterranean, French, North American grill, and Levantine table contexts.

  The bread-or-yolk decision is regional. The Piedmontese bagnetto verde uses bread (the canonical bollito misto pairing, documented in northern Italian regional cookbooks); central and southern Italian preparations more often use a hard-boiled egg yolk; some preparations use neither and accept a thinner sauce. All three are correct; the bread version reads softest and most absorbent, the yolk version reads richest and most luxurious, the no-body version reads cleanest and most parsley-forward.

  Storage. Keeps 4 days refrigerated in a sealed jar with a thin film of olive oil over the surface (oxygen exposure darkens the parsley); bring to room temperature 20 minutes before service. The flavor peaks at day 1 to 2; after day 4 the parsley darkens visibly and the sauce reads slightly pickled, still serviceable but past peak.
