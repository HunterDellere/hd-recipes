source:
  name: 'Adapted from the American steakhouse cast-iron canon and the French bistro red-wine shallot pan-sauce tradition, both of which converge on the same technique: high-heat sear, butter-baste, rest, sauce off the fond.'
  note: 'Chuck eye is one rib forward of the strip loin and one rib back of the ribeye, with the same longissimus dorsi cross-section that defines those cuts and a fraction of the price. The technique is calibrated to two things: getting the deep Maillard crust that the marbling rewards, and pulling the steak at 54 °C so the connective bands around the eye read tender rather than chewy. The pan sauce is the second half of the dish; the fond left in the pan after the sear is the entire reason to use cast-iron over a grill for this cut.'

safety_notes:
  - for: 'Pulling chuck eye at 52 to 57 °C / 126 to 135 °F internal (below the USDA published-safe 145 °F for whole-muscle beef)'
    ref: 'safety/meat-doneness#beef-and-lamb-whole-muscle'
    why: 'The USDA published-safe target for whole-muscle beef is 145 °F (63 °C) followed by a 3-minute rest. The American and French steakhouse canon pulls medium-rare steaks at 52 to 57 °C (126 to 135 °F) followed by a 3- to 5-minute rest, and this is the doneness range at which chuck eye eats best. Whole-muscle beef from a reputable source carries a substantially lower pathogen risk than ground beef (the bacterial load lives on the surface, which the hard sear pasteurizes; the interior of a whole muscle from a healthy animal is essentially sterile). Cooks who want the published-safe number should pull at 145 °F, accepting drier, chalkier meat. Ground beef, mechanically tenderized cuts, and blade-tenderized steaks should always be cooked to 71 °C / 160 °F since the surface bacteria are mixed throughout.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Dry-brine the steaks 12 to 24 hours ahead, salt them on both sides at 0.9% of weight (4 g kosher salt for two 225 g steaks), set on a wire rack over a sheet tray, and refrigerate uncovered. The surface needs to be visibly dry to the touch before searing; salt without overnight rest crusts but does not penetrate, and the steak weeps water into the pan instead of building Maillard.'
  - 'Pull the steaks out of the fridge 45 minutes before searing so the interior comes up toward 18 to 20°C. A cold-from-fridge interior overshoots the sear window (the surface goes black before the center hits 54°C), and the pan sauce window collapses chasing temperature.'
  - 'The pan sauce is built in the same hot cast iron while the steaks rest 5 minutes. Stage the minced shallot, red wine, broth, dijon, and the cold cubed butter on the counter before the sear; once the steaks come out you have a 6 minute window to build, reduce, and mount, no time to start mincing.'
  - 'Have the kitchen ventilation on high. A smoking cast iron at 230°C plus butter-basting fills the kitchen with smoke within 90 seconds; without the fan running you trigger smoke alarms and the cook becomes about the alarm rather than the steak.'

steps:
  - text: 'The night before. Pat both 225 g steaks dry on both sides with paper towels. Lay them on a wire rack set over a small sheet pan. Sprinkle 4 g / 1 tsp Diamond Crystal kosher salt evenly across both sides of both steaks; that is roughly 0.9% of the total steak weight, the canonical dry-brine ratio. The salt will draw beads of surface moisture in the first hour (visible as fine droplets on the meat), then re-absorb over the next 6 to 8 hours as the salinity gradient flips and the brine carries the salt back into the meat. Place the rack-and-pan setup uncovered in the refrigerator for 12 to 24 hours. Uncovered is the operative word; the cold dry fridge air pulls additional surface moisture and forms a tacky pellicle on the meat that sears dramatically faster than wet meat. By the morning the steak surface should look slightly matte-tacky and the flesh should feel firmer than raw.'
    technique: 'dry-brining'
    time_min: 720
  - text: 'One hour before cooking. Pull the steaks out of the refrigerator and let them temper at room temperature for 45 to 60 minutes, still on the rack. A cold-from-the-fridge steak takes 60 to 90 seconds longer per side to reach interior temperature, which gives the surface time to overshoot into a charred-black rather than mahogany crust. Room-temperature meat (around 18 to 21 °C surface, the center still cool) gives the cleanest crust-to-interior gradient. While the steaks temper, do every cut for the pan sauce, the 50 g / 2 shallots fine-minced, the 12 g / 3 garlic cloves side-smashed with skins on, the 4 g / 4 thyme sprigs and the 1 g / ½ tsp stripped thyme leaves separated, the 30 g of cold cubed butter for basting and the 30 g of cold cubed butter for mounting in two small bowls in the refrigerator. The mise needs to be complete before the pan goes on the heat, the sear is 4 minutes total and there is no time to chop mid-cook.'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'Preheat the pan and crack the pepper. Place a heavy 30 cm / 12-inch cast-iron skillet over high heat. Cast-iron preheats slowly; give it 4 to 5 full minutes with the burner on its highest setting. The cue for the right temperature: a drop of water flicked into the pan should immediately ball into a single rolling bead and dance across the surface (the Leidenfrost effect, indicating the pan is well above 175 °C / 350 °F), and the first faint wisps of dry heat-shimmer should be visible above the cooking surface. Crack the 2 g / 1 tsp coarse black pepper directly onto both sides of both steaks while the pan preheats; the pepper goes on now rather than during the dry-brine because pepper left on raw meat in the fridge for 12 hours oxidizes and reads stale by the time it hits the pan.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Sear, first side. Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil to the smoking cast-iron pan and tilt to coat the surface; the oil should ripple and shimmer within 3 to 5 seconds and just begin to smoke at 8 to 10 seconds. The pan is ready. Lay the 2 steaks in the pan, pulling each one toward you so the oil sprays away (oil splash safety). They will sizzle violently and immediately, that is the cue that the pan is hot enough, the steaks should be loud the second they touch the surface. Press each steak flat to the pan with the back of a fish spatula for 5 to 10 seconds to ensure full contact. Then leave them undisturbed for 90 seconds. Resist the urge to lift and peek, the crust forms in a single uninterrupted contact with the iron, and lifting at 30 seconds breaks that and starts the clock over.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Flip and sear, second side. After 90 seconds, lift the corner of one steak with the spatula; the crust should be a deep mahogany-bronze, with the cooked-flesh band climbing about a third of the way up the side of the steak. If the crust is still pale gray, give it another 20 to 30 seconds. When ready, flip both steaks to the second side. Lay them down in clean pan-territory (not the spot they were just searing on, which has lost surface temperature and accumulated rendered fat that will steam rather than sear). Sear undisturbed for another 90 seconds.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add aromatics and butter-baste to finish. Drop the 30 g / 2 tbsp cold cubed butter, the 12 g / 3 smashed garlic cloves (skins on), and the 4 g / 4 thyme sprigs into the pan around the steaks. The butter will foam and brown within 15 seconds; tilt the pan slightly so the foaming butter pools at one edge, and use a large spoon to scoop the herb-and-garlic-flavored butter onto the top surface of each steak repeatedly for 60 to 90 seconds. The baste does three things: it puts a thin film of milk-solid-browned butter onto the seared crust (the Maillard browning of the butter compounds the Maillard of the steak), it carries thymol and allicin (the volatile compounds in thyme and garlic) onto the surface, and it equalizes the steak temperature top-to-bottom so the second-side sear gets a head start before any flip. Check internal temperature in the thickest part with an instant-read thermometer; the target is 50 to 52 °C / 122 to 126 °F to come off the heat (the steak will climb 3 to 5 °C during the rest). For a 1-inch chuck eye starting from properly tempered meat, 60 to 90 seconds of butter-baste is the right window.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Rest. Pull the steaks from the pan onto a small board or warmed plate. Tent loosely with foil, do not seal, sealed foil traps steam and softens the crust the dry-brine spent 12 hours building. Rest 5 to 7 minutes. The rest is functional, not cosmetic; the muscle fibers that contracted under the heat relax back and the internal moisture redistributes. A steak cut immediately bleeds its juice onto the cutting board; the same steak rested 5 minutes holds 30 to 40% more juice when sliced. Internal temperature should climb to 52 to 57 °C / 126 to 135 °F (medium-rare to medium-rare-plus) during the rest. Remove the spent 4 g / 4 thyme sprigs and 12 g / 3 garlic cloves from the pan with tongs; the garlic should be deeply golden and edible (squeeze the soft clove out of the skin and eat it as the cook''s treat). Keep the rendered butter-fat in the pan, that is the sauce base.'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Pan sauce, the shallot sweat. Reduce the heat under the cast-iron pan to medium. The pan is still very hot from the sear; the residual butter-fat should be glossy and just below smoking. Add the 50 g / 2 shallots fine-minced and stir constantly with a wooden spoon for 60 to 90 seconds. The shallot should turn translucent and just begin to take on a pale golden color, no deeper. Color past blond is texture loss; the shallot turns from a soft aromatic into a crunchy bitter chip if it browns past gold.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Deglaze. Pour in the 120 g / ½ cup red wine; it will boil aggressively the second it hits the pan. Use the back of the wooden spoon to scrape every brown spot of fond off the bottom of the pan (the dark crusty bits from the steak sear are pure glutamate-driven flavor; the wine is the solvent that lifts them into the sauce). Reduce the wine over medium-high heat until it has roughly halved in volume and the alcoholic harshness has cooked off; the visual cue is a glossy syrupy puddle of dark red liquid coating the bottom of the pan, about 60 to 90 seconds. The wine is reduced when you can drag the spoon across the pan and the liquid takes a moment to flow back together rather than rushing in immediately.'
    technique: 'deglazing'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the broth and mustard. Pour in the 240 g / 1 cup beef broth and whisk in the 8 g / 1 tsp dijon mustard. If using the optional 5 g / 1 tsp Worcestershire, add it now. Bring to a hard simmer and let the liquid reduce to a sauce consistency: the volume should drop to roughly one-third of the original (a scant ½ cup), with bubbles becoming smaller and slower as the liquid concentrates, and the sauce should coat the back of a spoon with a visible drag-line when you run a fingertip through it (the classic nappant test). This reduction takes 3 to 5 minutes at a hard simmer. Taste the sauce, it should read strongly beefy, a touch sharp from the wine, with the mustard reading as background tang rather than mustard-front. Add a pinch of kosher salt only if needed; the seared fond and the reduced beef broth carry significant salt already.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Mount with cold butter, off heat. Pull the pan completely off the burner. The pan retains enough residual heat to melt cold butter without breaking the emulsion; this is the move on which the sauce''s gloss and body depend. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp cold cubed butter all at once and whisk continuously with a fork or a small whisk, moving fast, until the butter has fully integrated into the sauce and the surface has gone glossy-opaque rather than oil-slicked. This is monter au beurre, the French bistro butter-mount, and the cold-butter-into-warm-sauce direction is what produces a temporary emulsion of butterfat in reduced wine and broth. Hot butter into hot sauce produces a broken oily sauce; cold butter into off-heat sauce produces silk. Whisk in the 1 g / ½ tsp stripped thyme leaves at the end. Taste again, add a small splash of red wine vinegar (a teaspoon, off-pantry) if the sauce reads too rich and needs lift.'
    technique: 'pan-emulsion'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Slice and plate. Place each rested steak on a warmed plate. Slice across the grain (perpendicular to the visible muscle striation; the longissimus eye runs one direction and the smaller surrounding muscles may run another, follow the dominant grain through the eye) into 1 cm thick slices; the cross-grain slice shortens the muscle fibers to bite size and reads dramatically more tender than a with-grain slice. Spoon the shallot pan sauce generously over the slices, about 60 to 80 g of sauce per plate; the sauce should pool around the meat rather than fully coat it, so each forkful can take a different sauce-to-meat ratio. Scatter a pinch of the 2 g / ½ tsp flaky finishing salt over the slices, the macro-flake salt sits on top of the cut surface, hits the tongue first, and reads as a sharper bright contrast to the sauce''s long umami. Serve immediately, ideally alongside the sheet-pan roasted shallots, cauliflower, and delicata squash from the companion recipe on this site, with the same sauce spilling over the vegetables.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why this cut on cast-iron specifically. Chuck eye sits one rib forward of the strip loin and one rib back of the ribeye, carrying the same longissimus dorsi cross-section that defines those cuts. The marbling is roughly Select to low Choice grade, lower than a typical Choice ribeye but comparable on the eye itself. The cut runs 4 to 6 dollars per pound at most American supermarkets against 15 to 25 dollars per pound for ribeye, and the perceived flavor difference on a properly cooked sear is small enough that most home cooks would not pick the ribeye in a blind tasting. The trade-off is that chuck eye has a band or two of connective tissue at the edges of the eye, the silver-white sheets visible on the cross-section, which need the longer carryover into the 54 to 57 °C window to soften enough to eat tender. Cast iron is the right pan for the cut because the thermal mass holds temperature when two cold steaks hit the surface, and the rough seasoned-iron surface produces a more textural crust than the smoother carbon-steel pan that defines the French equivalent. Either works; cast iron is what the American steakhouse tradition built around.

  The dry-brine is the night-before lift that distinguishes a good cast-iron steak from a great one. A salted-30-minutes-before steak is fine; the salt has only worked the surface and the meat reads salted-on-the-outside. A 12-to-24-hour dry-brine equilibrates the seasoning through the entire cross-section, fundamentally changing the eating profile. The uncovered fridge rest does the secondary structural work: dry surface sears hotter and faster than wet surface, and the pellicle that forms over 12 hours of cold dry air is the foundation of the deep mahogany crust. If you only have 30 minutes, salt and proceed, the dish still works, but the difference between 30-minute-salt and overnight-dry-brine is the difference between a home steak and a steakhouse steak.

  Why the butter-baste matters and where it can be skipped. The 60 to 90 seconds of butter-basting at the end of the sear does three structural things: it browns butter on the pan surface (the milk solids in butter Maillard at the same temperature the meat does, producing nutty butterfat that compounds the meat''s own browning), it infuses the surface with thymol from the thyme and allicin from the garlic, and it equalizes the top-side temperature of the steak so the second side doesn''t need to re-sear from cold meat. The baste is a finish, not a foundation; if you''re cooking 4 steaks in two batches and the pan is overloaded, skip the baste and rely on the crust the dry sear built. The shallot pan sauce supplies the same butter-and-aromatic register the baste would have brought, so the dish is not poorer for the skip.

  Pan-sauce structure and where it can break. The shallot sweat, the wine reduction, the broth reduction, and the cold-butter mount are four discrete moves in sequence. Each one fails for a different reason. The shallot sweat fails when the pan is too hot and the shallot browns past blond into bitter brown chip; reduce the heat to medium the moment you scrape out the steak fat, not after. The wine reduction fails when the wine is over-reduced past syrup into a sticky lacquer that won''t loosen when broth hits; if you overshoot, add 30 g of water before the broth to bring it back. The broth reduction fails when the heat is too low and the sauce never concentrates into nappant; medium-high heat with the bubbles audibly hard-rolling is the right level. The cold-butter mount fails when the pan is too hot and the butter melts cleanly into oil-on-water rather than emulsifying into the sauce; pulling the pan completely off the heat for the butter mount is non-negotiable, residual cast-iron heat is plenty.

  Doneness targets and carryover. Pull the steaks at 50 to 52 °C internal in the thickest part. The carryover during the 5- to 7-minute rest will climb the temperature 3 to 5 °C into the 52 to 57 °C window, which is the sweet spot for chuck eye. Past 60 °C the eye dries chalky; under 50 °C the connective tissue at the edges reads chewy. The optimal-doneness window for chuck eye is narrower than the ribeye''s 52 to 60 °C window because the connective bands need temperature to soften. An instant-read thermometer is the right tool here; reading doneness from finger-press or visual cues alone produces too much variance on a cut this thin. The Thermapen ONE (or any sub-3-second instant-read at the same price point) earns its keep on every steak it touches.

  Why no bone-in option. Bone-in cuts hold the bone-side meat at a lower temperature during the sear (the bone acts as a heat sink), producing uneven doneness across the steak. Chuck eye is typically sold boneless and the dish is calibrated to that. Bone-in ribeye works for a different dish (the long, slow oven-finish reverse sear or a true steakhouse broiler approach), not for a 4-minute cast-iron sear.

  The American-French overlap. The American steakhouse tradition built this dish around the cast-iron pan, the dry-brine, the butter-baste, and the rest. The French bistro tradition built the same dish around a carbon-steel pan, an overnight salt, an off-heat baste with beurre noisette, and the same rest. The pan sauce, monter au beurre with shallot and red wine, is French in origin (the entire technique of mounting reduced wine sauces with cold butter off-heat is bistro classique) but has been absorbed wholesale into modern American steakhouse cookery. This recipe sits exactly at the overlap; an experienced French cook and an experienced American cook will both recognize the dish as their own canon.
