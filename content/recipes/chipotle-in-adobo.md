source:

  note: 'Chipotle in adobo (chipotles adobados) is the canonical Mexican preserved-chile condiment: smoked-and-dried jalapeños (chipotles) rehydrated and packed in a tomato-vinegar-spice marinade (adobo). The technique descends from Mexican preservation traditions where adobo-style marinades were used to extend the shelf life of meats and chiles in the pre-refrigeration era. Commercial canned chipotles in adobo (La Costeña, San Marcos, El Mexicano) are widely available and structurally functional, but the homemade version reads dramatically fresher with no metallic tinned-tomato note.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Two chipotle varieties exist: chipotle morita (smaller, darker, sweeter, the most commonly available US variety) and chipotle meco (larger, gray-brown, more wood-smoke aromatic, harder to source). This recipe uses morita; meco is acceptable and produces a more aggressively smoky version.'
  - 'The toasting and the frying are both structural. Skip the toast and the chiles read flat; skip the fry and the adobo reads raw. Both steps take only a few minutes and are non-negotiable for the canonical version.'

steps:
  - text: 'Bring the 500 ml / 2 cups water to a boil in a kettle or small saucepan; pull off heat and reserve hot.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Toast the chipotles. In a dry comal or cast-iron skillet over medium heat, toast the 60 g chipotle peppers for 30 to 60 seconds per side until they puff slightly, soften, and release a pronounced toasted-chipotle aroma. Press gently with a spatula to keep them in contact with the hot surface. Do not let them blacken; scorched peppers turn the adobo bitter (slightly darker spots are fine; uniform deep brown is right; uniformly black is wrong). Pull to a heatproof bowl.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Rehydrate the chipotles. Pour the just-boiled 500 ml water over the toasted chipotles in the bowl. Weight with a small plate to keep them submerged (the dried chiles float). Let stand 20 to 25 minutes; the peppers will rehydrate to fully soft and pliable, and the water will turn dark amber from the released chile pigments.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Drain and reserve. Drain the rehydrated peppers in a fine-mesh sieve, RESERVING 100 ml / ½ cup of the soaking water (this water carries dissolved capsaicin and chile aromatics and is structurally important to the adobo flavor; discarding it wastes a significant flavor component).'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Blend the adobo. In a blender, combine the rehydrated peppers, the reserved 100 ml soaking water, the 200 g canned tomatoes, the 80 g / ⅓ cup apple cider vinegar, the 100 g quartered onion, the 20 g smashed garlic, the 8 g kosher salt, the 12 g piloncillo or dark brown sugar, the 1 g Mexican oregano, the 1 g ground cumin, and the 1 bay leaf (snap in half before adding so it blends smoothly). Blend on high for 60 to 90 seconds until completely smooth; the mixture should be a uniform deep red-brown paste with no visible chunks of onion or pepper.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Fry the adobo. Heat the 20 g / 1½ tbsp avocado oil in a heavy 2 L saucepan over medium heat until shimmering. Add the blended adobo paste; it will spit aggressively as the moisture hits the hot oil (use a splatter guard if available). Reduce heat to medium-low; simmer 10 to 12 minutes, stirring frequently with a wooden spoon to prevent scorching at the bottom. The paste will deepen in color from bright red-brown to deep reddish-mahogany, the texture will concentrate to a thick spoonable paste slightly looser than tomato paste, and the kitchen will fill with the structural smoky-tangy-sweet adobo aroma.'
    technique: 'red-oil-rendering'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Cool and store. Pull from heat; cool to room temperature in the pan (about 30 minutes). Transfer to a clean glass jar; the adobo keeps 1 month refrigerated. For longer storage, freeze in 30 g / 2 tbsp portions in an ice cube tray; once frozen, transfer cubes to a zip-top freezer bag and label with date; frozen, keeps 6 months. Use spoonful-for-spoonful as a direct substitute for canned chipotle in adobo.'
    time_min: 35

notes: |
  The toasting chemistry. Dry-toasting the chipotle on the comal does two structural things: it volatilizes existing aromatic compounds (so they bloom in the rehydration), and it triggers a brief Maillard reaction on the chile surface that produces new aromatic compounds. Untoasted rehydrated chipotles read flat; toasted chipotles read deeply complex. The technique is canonical across Mexican dried-chile preparations; every Mexican grandmother knows to toast the chiles first.

  Why the soaking liquid matters. The rehydration water absorbs significant amounts of capsaicin, chile aromatic compounds, and water-soluble pigments from the chiles. Discarding this water and using fresh water in the adobo wastes 20 to 30 percent of the chile flavor. The standard technique is to reserve about half the soaking liquid (the deeply flavored amber portion) and use it in the adobo blend; discard the rest (or save for a chile-based marinade or salsa). Some cooks use all the soaking water; this produces a slightly more intense adobo but can over-concentrate the bitter compounds from the chile skins.

  The fry-the-adobo step. The hot-oil contact develops Maillard products in the adobo, concentrates the flavors by driving off water, and changes the color from bright red-brown to deep reddish-mahogany. Adobo that is not fried reads raw and flat; fried adobo reads as a complete condiment. The technique is canonical across Mexican sauces (the same "freír la salsa" technique used for enchilada sauces and salsas); skipping it produces an inferior product.

  Why the homemade reads fresher than canned. Commercial canned chipotle in adobo sits on the shelf for 6 to 12 months in a can lined with BPA-free epoxy that nonetheless contributes a faint metallic note; the canning process also requires the adobo to be sterilized at high temperatures that flatten the aromatics; the commercial adobo typically uses dehydrated onion and garlic powder for cost reasons, which read flatter than fresh. The homemade version skips all of these compromises; the result is a dramatically more aromatic, fresher-reading condiment that any cook who has used both will recognize immediately.

  Application notes. Use chipotle in adobo spoonful-for-spoonful as a direct substitute for the canned product in any recipe: marinades for grilled meats (pollo asado, carne adobada), tinga (Pueblan shredded chicken with chipotle and tomato), birria (Mexican spicy stew), chipotle mayo, chipotle-cream sauces, salsas, and as a finishing condiment for tacos. The 30 g / 2 tbsp portion (one ice cube from the freezer) is roughly equivalent to two canned chipotle chiles plus their sauce. Start small and add more; the heat level is significant and can dominate a dish if overused.

  Storage. Refrigerated in a clean glass jar, 1 month at peak quality; the flavors continue to integrate during the first week of storage. Frozen in 30 g ice-cube portions, 6 months; the cubes pop out of the tray cleanly when frozen and are convenient for portion-sized use. Do not store at room temperature; the relatively low acidity is structurally insufficient to prevent bacterial growth at warm temperatures. The dish reads structurally better after a 48-hour rest in the refrigerator; consider making a batch on Saturday for use the following week.
