  note: 'Four ingredients, no fat, no sugar, no dough conditioners. Every variable that a richer bread hides behind, the baguette exposes: hydration sets the crumb, the poolish sets the flavor and the extensibility, the autolyse sets the gluten without kneading, and the steam sets the crust. The recipe is calibrated to a home oven with a stone or steel and a covered steam phase rather than a steam-injected deck oven, which is the single hardest gap to close between a home baguette and a boulangerie one.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Build the poolish 12 to 16 hours ahead, the night before you bake. It is equal weights flour and water with a trace of yeast (0.1 g, a pinch), fermented at room temperature until it is domed, bubbled across the whole surface, and just beginning to recede in the center. Under 12 hours it has not developed the organic acids and extensibility that define the crumb; past 18 hours at warm room temperature it collapses and turns sharply sour. If your kitchen runs warm (above 24 C / 75 F), build it with cold water and check it at 10 hours.'
  - 'This is a same-day bake once the poolish is ready: roughly 4 hours from mixing to pulling loaves, of which only about 60 minutes is hands-on. Block out the afternoon. The bulk ferment and the final proof each need 60 to 90 minutes and cannot be rushed without flattening the crumb.'
  - 'Preheat the baking stone or steel for a full 45 minutes to 250 C / 480 F before the loaves go in. The thermal mass of a fully saturated stone is what drives oven spring; a stone given only 15 minutes reads hot on the surface but has a cold core and the baguettes spread instead of springing.'
  - 'Stage your steam method before you shape. A preheated cast-iron skillet or a metal tray on the oven floor that you pour boiling water into at load time, plus a spritz bottle, is the home-oven workaround for a steam-injected deck oven. Have the kettle boiled and the water measured before the loaves are scored, you have about 20 seconds between loading and sealing the steam.'

steps:
  - text: 'The night before, build the poolish. In a 2-liter container, whisk the 150 g / ⅔ cup room-temperature water with the 0.1 g pinch of instant yeast until the yeast disperses, then stir in the 150 g / 1¼ cups bread flour until no dry flour remains. It will be a thick batter, not a dough. Scrape down the sides, cover with a damp towel or a fitted lid set ajar, and leave at room temperature (ideally 20 to 22 C / 68 to 72 F) for 12 to 16 hours. It is ready when the surface is fully domed and pocked with bubbles and the center has just started to dip. This long, cool, low-yeast ferment is where the baguette gets its flavor: the wild lactic and acetic acids that build over the hours give the lean dough the aromatic depth that a fast straight dough cannot, and the slow proteolysis loosens the gluten into the extensibility you need to stretch a baguette without tearing.'
    technique: 'poolish-preferment'
    time_min: 840
  - text: 'Autolyse. In a large bowl, combine the ripe poolish, the 350 g / 2¾ cups bread flour, and the 225 g / scant 1 cup water. Mix with a wet hand or a wooden spoon just until no dry flour remains, about 1 minute; the dough will be shaggy, sticky, and rough. Do not knead. Cover and rest 30 to 45 minutes. During this autolyse the flour fully hydrates and the gluten proteins (glutenin and gliadin) self-assemble into a network with no mechanical work at all, driven by enzyme activity and water alone. The dough that emerges is smoother, more extensible, and far easier to develop than a dough mixed all at once, and the rest shortens the kneading the high hydration would otherwise demand.'
    technique: 'autolyse'
    time_min: 40
  - text: 'Add the yeast and salt. Sprinkle the 2 g / ½ tsp instant yeast and the 10 g / 1¾ tsp fine sea salt over the autolysed dough. Wet your hand, then pinch and fold the dough repeatedly to distribute them, about 2 minutes, until the dough tightens and the salt has dissolved with no gritty patches. The salt goes in now rather than at autolyse because salt tightens gluten and slows enzyme activity, and you want the autolyse to run loose and unimpeded first. The salt is also doing flavor and structure work: at 2% of flour it suppresses excess yeast activity for an even rise, tightens the crumb walls so they hold gas, and is the only seasoning a lean dough gets.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Bulk ferment with stretch-and-folds. Cover the dough and begin the bulk ferment at room temperature. Over the first 90 minutes, perform four sets of stretch-and-folds spaced about 30 minutes apart: with a wet hand, grab one edge of the dough, stretch it up and over to the opposite side, rotate the bowl a quarter turn, and repeat until you have gone around all four sides. Each set develops gluten strength by aligning the protein strands and traps gas without degassing the dough the way kneading would. After the folds, let the dough rest undisturbed to the end of bulk. The dough is ready when it has risen by about 50 to 70%, feels alive and jiggly, and shows a few bubbles at the surface and along the sides of the container. Total bulk is 90 minutes to 2 hours depending on room temperature.'
    technique: 'stretch-and-fold'
    time_min: 120
  - text: 'Divide and pre-shape. Turn the dough out onto a lightly floured bench, using a bit of the 30 g / ¼ cup dusting flour. Handle it gently from here on; the open crumb lives in the gas you are now protecting. Divide into three equal pieces, about 280 g each before baking loss. Pre-shape each piece into a loose, slack cylinder: fold the top third down, the bottom third up, and roll into a short log without forcing tension. Cover the logs with a damp towel and let them bench-rest 20 to 30 minutes. This relaxes the gluten so the final shaping does not fight back and tear; a baguette shaped straight off the bulk springs back and will not extend to length.'
    time_min: 30
  - text: 'Final shape into baguettes. Working one log at a time, flip it seam-side up on a lightly floured bench and gently pat it into a rough rectangle, deflating only the largest bubbles. Fold the top edge to the middle and seal with your fingertips, then fold the new top edge down to the bottom and seal the seam along its length with the heel of your hand, creating surface tension on the skin. Roll the cylinder under your palms from the center outward, walking your hands toward the ends to taper them, until each loaf is 35 to 40 cm long. Lay the loaves seam-side up in the pleats of a heavily floured couche or a flour-dusted, pleated kitchen towel, the pleats supporting the sides so they hold their shape rather than spreading.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Final proof, and preheat the oven. Cover the couched loaves with a damp towel and proof 45 to 75 minutes at room temperature, until they look puffed and pass the poke test: a floured fingertip pressed gently into the dough should leave a dent that springs back slowly and only partway. Meanwhile, set the baking stone or steel on a middle rack with a cast-iron skillet or metal steam tray on the rack below or on the oven floor, and preheat to 250 C / 480 F for a full 45 minutes. Under-proofed loaves tear at the scores and spring unpredictably; over-proofed loaves deflate when scored and bake flat and pale.'
    time_min: 60
  - text: 'Transfer and score. Boil the kettle for the steam. Gently roll the proofed loaves seam-side down off the couche onto a piece of parchment on a flipping board or an inverted sheet tray, two or three at a time depending on stone size. With a lame or a clean razor blade held at a low 30-degree angle to the surface, score each loaf with three to five long, overlapping slashes running nearly parallel to the length, each cut opening the next like roof tiles. The shallow angled scores are not decoration: they direct where the loaf bursts as it springs, so the crust opens cleanly into ears instead of rupturing at a random weak point. A confident, single-stroke cut beats a hesitant sawing one, which drags and tears the skin.'
    technique: 'scoring'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Load and steam. Slide the parchment and loaves directly onto the preheated stone. Immediately pour the 250 g / 1 cup boiling water into the preheated steam tray and shut the door fast to trap the steam, and give the oven walls two or three spritzes from a spray bottle on the way. The first 10 to 15 minutes of steam keeps the crust surface moist and elastic so the loaf can spring and expand to its full volume before the crust sets; steam also gelatinizes the surface starches, which is what gives the baked crust its glassy sheen. A dry oven sets the crust early and chokes the oven spring.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Bake. Bake at 250 C / 480 F for 10 minutes with the steam, then carefully remove the steam tray (or crack the door for a few seconds to vent), drop the temperature to 230 C / 450 F, and bake another 12 to 15 minutes dry. The dry second phase is what crisps and colors the crust through the Maillard browning of the surface proteins and sugars. The baguettes are done when the crust is a deep golden brown bordering on amber at the ears, the loaves feel light and hollow-sounding when tapped on the bottom, and the internal temperature reads 96 to 99 C / 205 to 210 F. Pale, blond crust means underbaked; pull too early and the crust softens to leather as it cools instead of crackling.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Cool, and listen. Transfer the baguettes to a wire rack and let them cool at least 20 to 30 minutes before cutting. As they cool you should hear the crust crackle and sing, the audible sign that the glassy crust is contracting and fissuring over the moist crumb, the textbook baguette signature. Cutting hot collapses the still-setting crumb into a gummy paste; the structure and the final moisture migration finish during the cool. Lean breads stale fast, so a baguette is at its peak within hours of cooling. To revive a day-old loaf, mist it with water and refresh 5 minutes in a 200 C / 400 F oven to re-crisp the crust.'
    time_min: 30

    how: 'After the stretch-and-folds, refrigerate the covered dough overnight (8 to 14 hours) instead of finishing bulk at room temperature. The cold ferment deepens the flavor further (more acetic tang, a more complex aroma) and turns the bake into a make-ahead: pull the dough, let it warm 30 minutes, then divide and shape. The crumb reads slightly more sour and the schedule decouples the long ferment from the bake day.'

    how: 'If your oven will not hold steam, bake the baguettes under an inverted roasting pan, a large stainless bowl, or a turned-over deep sheet tray for the first 12 minutes to trap the loaves own moisture, then uncover and finish dry. The covered phase mimics the boulangerie steam injection. The constraint is vessel size: most home covers fit two baguettes at most, or one at full length, so bake in batches.'

notes: |
  Why a poolish and not a straight dough. A baguette has nothing to hide behind: no butter, no milk, no sugar, no eggs. With only flour, water, salt, and yeast, the only place flavor comes from is fermentation time. The poolish, a 100% hydration preferment built with a trace of yeast and run 12 to 16 hours, develops a population of organic acids and aromatic byproducts that a 3-hour straight dough never reaches. It also does structural work: the long, slow proteolysis in the poolish loosens the gluten into the high extensibility a baguette demands, which is why a poolish baguette stretches to 40 cm without snapping back or tearing. The trade-off the poolish buys is the open, irregular, glossy-walled crumb that defines the loaf.

  Hydration is the lever for the crumb. At 75% hydration this dough is wet and slack, and that is the point. Water is what makes the large, irregular holes possible: a wetter dough has more steam to expand the gas cells in the oven, and looser gluten walls that stretch around them instead of constraining them into a tight, even crumb. The cost is handling difficulty, which is why this recipe leans on autolyse, stretch-and-folds, and gentle shaping rather than kneading. If you are new to wet dough, start at 72% (cut about 15 g water) and work up; do not start at 80% and fight a puddle.

  Why no kneading. High-hydration lean dough develops better through time and gentle folding than through aggressive kneading. The autolyse builds the gluten network passively through hydration and enzyme action; the four stretch-and-folds during bulk align and strengthen the strands while preserving the gas you want in the final crumb. Kneading a dough this wet would either require a stand mixer running long enough to warm and degas it, or a frustrating slap-and-fold session. The fold method is gentler on the gas and better suited to the open crumb.

  The umami-and-acid question for bread. A lean baguette has no added glutamate source and no added acid, and it should not: salt is its only seasoning by tradition and by French bread law. But the fermentation supplies both axes anyway. The long poolish builds free amino acids, including glutamate, through proteolysis, which is the savory backbone you taste in well-fermented bread, and the same ferment builds lactic and acetic acids that give the crumb its faint, clean tang. The flavor a good baguette has over a bad one is fermentation chemistry, not added ingredients. Pushing the poolish longer or adding a cold retard pushes both the savory depth and the acid up together.

  Steam is the home-oven bottleneck. A boulangerie deck oven injects steam on demand; a home oven leaks it. Everything in the load-and-steam step is a workaround for that gap: the preheated cast-iron steam tray, the boiling-water pour, the wall spritzes, and the optional covered-vessel method all exist to keep the crust surface moist and elastic for the first 10 to 15 minutes so the loaf can spring fully and the surface starches can gelatinize into that glassy, crackling crust. If your baguettes come out pale and dense with a dull, thick crust, the two usual culprits are insufficient steam and an under-preheated stone, in that order.

  Reading doneness. Pull at a deep golden-amber crust and an internal temperature of 96 to 99 C / 205 to 210 F, not before. Lean breads punish underbaking: the crust that looks set in the oven turns to soft leather as it cools instead of crackling, and the crumb stays gummy. Bake them darker than feels comfortable the first time. The audible crackle as they cool, the chant in French boulangerie slang, is the signal you nailed it.
