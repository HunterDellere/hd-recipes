source:
  note: 'Egusi soup is a Nigerian and broader West African staple, technically a "soup" in West African vocabulary (which means thick stew, eaten with a starch swallow) rather than a thin Western soup. The structural elements are: red palm oil as the cooking fat and color base, ground egusi (melon seeds) as the thickener that clumps into soft pebbles in the hot oil, scotch bonnet for heat, smoked or dried fish plus assorted meats for umami depth, and a dark leafy green (bitter leaf canonically, ugu pumpkin leaves regionally, frozen chopped spinach as the accessible substitute) folded in at the end. The funk from the smoked fish and the earthy weight of the palm oil are the entire character of the dish; substituting cleaner ingredients produces a different stew.'

before_you_start:
  - 'The smoked fish and assorted meats need to simmer for 30 to 45 minutes to give up their umami before the egusi goes in. Plan the timeline: meat broth first, then the egusi paste, then the greens at the end. The dish is technique-light but timing-heavy.'
  - 'Source real red palm oil (recipes/palm-oil) from a West African or African-Caribbean grocery; vegetable oil produces a different dish entirely. The color and the earthy-vegetal flavor are structural.'
  - 'Source real ground egusi from a West African grocery (the seeds are sold whole shelled or pre-ground, both work). The closest accessible substitute is raw shelled pumpkin seeds (pepitas) ground in a spice grinder or food processor; see substitutions.'
  - 'Source smoked fish (smoked catfish, smoked herring, or stockfish) from a West African grocery; the funk is non-negotiable. Smoked mackerel from a regular grocery is a working substitute.'

steps:
  - text: 'In a heavy 6-quart stockpot, combine the 700 g / 4 to 5 chicken thighs (or beef chuck), the 100 g / half of the diced onion (the half not reserved for Phase 3), the 4 g / 1 Maggi cube crumbled in, the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt, and the 800 ml / 3⅓ cups water. Bring to a boil, then drop to a low simmer and cook covered for 30 minutes for chicken, 60 minutes for beef. The meat should be tender and the broth should be flavorful.'
    time_min: 35
  - text: 'Add the 150 g / 2 fillets smoked fish (broken into pieces) and the 10 g / 1 tbsp crayfish powder to the meat broth. Simmer covered for another 15 minutes; the smoked fish releases its funk into the broth and softens. Do not stir aggressively; the smoked fish breaks apart easily and you want some recognizable pieces in the finished soup.'
    technique: 'simmer'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'In a small bowl, combine the 200 g / 1¼ cups ground egusi with the 200 ml / ¾ cup + 1 tbsp water and stir with a fork until a thick smooth paste forms, the consistency of stiff peanut butter. Set aside.'
    time_min: 3

Add the 100 g / reserved half of the onion (finely diced) and the 12 g / 2 to 3 scotch bonnet peppers and cook 5 minutes, stirring every minute, until the onion is soft and the chili is fragrant.'
    technique: 'bloom-spice'
    time_min: 6
  - text: 'Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp tomato paste (if using) and fry 2 minutes in the palm oil, stirring constantly, until the paste darkens from bright red to brick red and the oil takes on its color.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Add the egusi paste to the palm oil in small spoonfuls, not all at once. The paste will clump into soft yellow-white pebbles as it hits the hot oil. Stir gently with a wooden spoon, breaking up the largest clumps but leaving the smaller ones intact (the visible pebbles are the structural texture of the dish). Cook 5 minutes; the egusi releases its oil and the mixture goes from raw-pasty to nutty-aromatic.'
    technique: 'egusi-pebbling'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Transfer the palm-oil-and-egusi mixture into the meat-and-fish broth pot, scraping in everything from the skillet with a spatula. Stir gently to combine. The egusi pebbles distribute throughout the broth and absorb the liquid; the soup thickens visibly over 20 minutes of low simmer. Cover and simmer for 20 minutes, stirring every 5 minutes to prevent the egusi from settling and sticking to the bottom.'
    time_min: 22
  - text: 'Taste the soup. It should read deeply spiced, faintly funky from the smoked fish, thick enough to hold the shape of a spoon-drag. If too thick, splash in 100 ml water; if too thin, simmer uncovered another 5 to 10 minutes. Adjust salt; the Maggi and the dried shrimp contribute salt, so taste before adding more.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Add the 250 g / 4 cups fresh spinach (or thawed frozen spinach) and fold in gently. Cook another 5 minutes uncovered; the spinach wilts down dramatically and integrates into the soup. The greens go in last so they keep their bright color and slight bite.'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Pull from heat. Rest covered for 5 minutes off heat to let the flavors integrate further. Serve in wide shallow bowls with fufu, pounded yam, eba, or rice on the side.     time_min: 5

  - for: 'green'

    how: 'Replace the spinach with 100 g dried bitter leaf (sold at West African groceries). Soak the dried leaf in warm water for 30 minutes, drain, squeeze dry, and chop. Add at the same point as the spinach. The dish becomes more aggressively bitter and more authentically Nigerian; bitter leaf is an acquired taste but is what egusi soup canonically calls for.'
    kind: 'regional'
  - for: 'cuisine'
    to: 'Cameroonian-style egusi pudding'
    how: 'Reduce the water in the meat broth by half (400 ml), increase the ground egusi to 300 g, and reduce the simmer time after combining to 12 minutes. The mixture sets to a thick puddingy consistency that is the Cameroonian eru-and-egusi style, eaten in scoops rather than with a swallow.'
    kind: 'regional'

    note: 'The closest substitute by far; pepitas carry similar fat content and clump in hot palm oil similarly. The flavor is slightly cleaner and less earthy than real egusi. Quantity 1:1 by weight.'

notes: |
  Why the egusi pebbles matter. When ground egusi paste hits the hot palm oil, the proteins coagulate and the seeds clump into soft yellow-white pebbles roughly the size of small peas. These visible pebbles are the structural texture of the dish; egusi soup is not smooth, it is grainy, and the visible egusi clumps in every spoonful are what cooks and diners look for. Over-stirring breaks the clumps into a smoother but less authentic soup; gentle folding preserves the pebble structure.

  The umami architecture. Egusi soup carries an unusually layered umami profile: meat collagen from the chicken or beef simmer, smoke-and-funk from the smoked fish, oceanic glutamate from the crayfish powder, and the Maggi cube tying it all together. Most West African soups follow this pattern (meat-broth + smoked-fish + dried-shrimp + Maggi); the depth comes from the combination, not from any single ingredient.

  Why palm oil specifically. Red palm oil contributes color (the deep orange-red carotenoid pigment that tints the finished soup), flavor (the earthy-vegetal note that reads through every spoonful), and the structural cooking fat (the hot fat that pebbles the egusi). Substituting any other oil produces a recognizably different dish; the cuisine is built around this fat. See content/ingredients/palm-oil for sourcing.

  The swallow. Egusi soup is traditionally eaten with a starch swallow: fufu (pounded cassava), pounded yam, eba (toasted cassava granules), or amala (yam flour). The diner tears off a piece of the swallow, dips it into the soup, picks up a piece of meat or fish, and eats by hand. Rice is acceptable but breaks the tradition; the swallow-and-soup pairing is the canonical service.

  Make-ahead and reheat. Egusi soup improves the next day; the spices and the smoke integrate further. Reheat gently with a splash of water to loosen, covered, over low heat for 10 minutes. Holds 4 days refrigerated, 2 months frozen. The greens lose their bright color in storage but the flavor stays intact.
