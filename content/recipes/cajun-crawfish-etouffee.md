source:
  name: 'Acadiana spring crawfish-season canon'
  note: 'Étouffée (from French étouffer, "to smother") is the Cajun and Creole technique of smothering a protein in a sauce of holy trinity, roux, stock, and seasoning. The classic crawfish version is the spring dish of Acadiana, made during the February-to-May Louisiana crawfish season when wild crawfish are abundant. The structural elements are: light blond roux (a butter-and-flour roux cooked to peanut-butter color, lighter than the dark Cajun gumbo roux); the holy trinity; crawfish tail meat with its orange-pink fat; and the timing rule that the crawfish goes in last to warm through without overcooking into rubbery shellfish.'

before_you_start:
  - 'Louisiana crawfish tail meat is the structural protein; frozen Louisiana crawfish tails (with their orange fat included) are sold at most U.S. Asian markets, Latin markets, and online (Bayou Crawfish, Riceland, Acadia). Avoid Chinese imported crawfish tails (lower quality, less fat); they are typically the cheapest option but read meaningfully worse. If unavailable, shrimp is the substitute, but the dish becomes shrimp étouffée rather than crawfish.'
  - 'The roux for étouffée is blond, not dark. The Cajun gumbo roux is cooked to dark chocolate (15 to 30 minutes); the étouffée roux is cooked to peanut-butter or light caramel color (4 to 6 minutes) and contributes body and slight Maillard depth without the deep flavor of dark roux. Using a dark roux in étouffée produces a gumbo-flavored étouffée; not wrong but not canonical.'

  - text: 'Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp tomato paste; cook 90 seconds until darkened from bright red to brick red.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Gradually whisk in the 500 ml / 2 cups stock (add about 100 ml at a time, whisking smooth before adding more; gradual addition prevents lumps in the roux). Once all liquid is added, stir in the 8 g Cajun seasoning, 1 g cayenne, 3 g paprika, 3 g thyme, 2 bay leaves, 4 g salt, 2 g pepper, and 10 ml Worcestershire. Bring to a steady simmer.'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Simmer 12 to 15 minutes uncovered, stirring occasionally, until the sauce has thickened to coat the back of a spoon and reduced by about a third. The étouffée sauce should be thick enough to mound slightly on a plate, not soup-thin. Taste; adjust salt and cayenne.'
    time_min: 15
  - text: 'Stir in the reserved crawfish-fat liquid from the bag (about 30 ml of orange liquid that drained off the thawed crawfish, structural for color). Add the 450 g crawfish tail meat. Stir gently to distribute. Reduce heat to low. Cook 3 to 4 minutes only, just until the crawfish is heated through; do not boil the crawfish or simmer hard, the tails turn rubbery in under 5 minutes of overcooking.'
    time_min: 4
  - text: 'Off heat, stir in the 15 ml / 1 tbsp lemon juice. Fish out and discard the bay leaves. Taste; adjust salt.'
    time_min: 1
  - text: 'Spoon a 150 g mound of cooked white rice into the center of each shallow bowl. Ladle the étouffée over the rice, allowing the sauce to pool around the rice rather than fully covering it. Top with the 50 g sliced scallions and 10 g parsley. Serve with Louisiana hot sauce at the table.'
    time_min: 2

notes: |
  Why blond roux, not dark. The étouffée roux is one shade darker than just-melted-butter: light caramel or peanut butter. Cook beyond that and you have a gumbo roux, which has dramatically different flavor (deeply toasted, nutty, less starchy) and dramatically less thickening power (dark roux loses much of its starch-bonding capacity to extended Maillard reaction). The étouffée needs the structural thickening; the blond roux provides it without overshadowing the crawfish.

  Why crawfish goes in last. Crawfish tail meat is sold pre-cooked (the crawfish are boiled live in the harvest, peeled by hand, and frozen with their orange fat). All the cook does is warm them through. Cooking longer than 4 to 5 minutes at simmer-level temperatures shrinks and toughens the tails into rubbery shellfish. The discipline is to make the sauce fully ready first, then fold the crawfish in at the very end.

  The crawfish-fat rule. Frozen Louisiana crawfish bags include 30 to 60 ml of orange-pink crawfish fat that drains out as the meat thaws. This fat is the structural source of the étouffée's deep orange color and rich shellfish flavor; it should always be added to the sauce. Many home cooks discard it as "liquid" without realizing what it is.

  Why butter. Étouffée is a butter dish. The roux is butter-flour, not oil-flour (Cajun gumbo can use either; étouffée is butter only). The butter contributes dairy richness that supports the shellfish without overwhelming; oil-based étouffée reads thinner and less integrated.

  Pairings. Canonical over plain white rice (the rice catches the sauce). Side of cornbread or French bread for sopping. Crisp green salad to cut the richness. A cold beer (Abita Amber) or a dry white wine.

  Storage. Keeps 3 days refrigerated; the crawfish texture degrades on day 2 (becomes softer, less distinct from the sauce). Reheat over low heat with a splash of stock; do not boil. Does not freeze well; the crawfish texture becomes mealy on thaw.
