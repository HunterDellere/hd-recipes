steps:
  - text: 'Slice evenly and dry the garlic. Slice the 80 g / about 1 large head of peeled garlic an even 1 to 2 mm thick, or fine-mince it. Uniform thickness is the whole game: thick and thin pieces in the same pan means the thin ones burn before the thick ones crisp. Blot the slices dry on a towel, surface moisture spits in hot oil and steams rather than fries.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Start cold. Put the garlic and the 240 ml / 1 cup neutral oil in a small saucepan together, cold, then set it over medium-low heat. A cold-oil start is the key technique: the garlic dehydrates gradually as the oil comes up to temperature, cooking through evenly instead of searing the outside while the inside stays raw. Dropping garlic into already-hot oil browns it instantly and unevenly.'
    technique: 'confit-fry'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Fry slow and stir. As the oil reaches a gentle, steady bubble (around 140 to 150°C / 285 to 300°F), the 80 g / 1 head of garlic will foam as its water boils off. Stir often with a fork or chopsticks to keep the pieces separate and moving. Hold this gentle simmer-fry for 6 to 10 minutes. The garlic goes translucent, then pale straw, then begins to take on the faintest gold at the edges.'
    time_min: 8
  - text: 'Pull early, the instant pale gold appears. The moment the garlic is uniformly pale gold (NOT amber, not brown), kill the heat and immediately strain it through a sieve set over a heatproof bowl. Do not wait for golden-brown in the pan, it will get there from carryover. Spread the drained garlic on a paper-lined plate in a single layer and toss with the 1 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt while hot. It crisps as it cools over the next few minutes.'
    time_min: 2
  - text: 'Keep both. You now have two products from the 80 g / 1 head of garlic: shatter-crisp fried garlic on the plate (about 50 g), and amber garlic-infused oil in the bowl. Cool both completely. Once cold and fully crisp, store the fried garlic in an airtight jar; bottle the oil separately. Use the crisp garlic as a topping and the oil as a finishing or cooking fat anywhere garlic is welcome.'

notes: |
  Why a cold-oil start. The enemy of fried garlic is uneven cooking: garlic dropped into hot oil sears its outside dark while the interior is still raw, and burnt garlic is irredeemably bitter from the moment it browns. Starting the garlic in cold oil and bringing both up together dehydrates the slices slowly and uniformly, so every piece reaches crisp at the same moment. This is the same principle as a confit, gentle, even heat doing the work, just taken further until the garlic dries fully and crisps.

  Why pull it early. Garlic carries a large amount of heat in the hot oil clinging to it and keeps cooking after it leaves the pan. The visual you want in the pan is pale gold, distinctly lighter than the golden-brown you want to eat; by the time it has drained and cooled it will have darkened exactly one shade into perfect. Cooks who wait for golden-brown in the oil pull bitter, over-browned garlic every time. When in doubt, strain a few seconds sooner.

  Two products, one technique. The garlic oil is not a byproduct, it is half the point. The slow fry leaches garlic flavor into the oil, leaving you with an infused fat that is itself a finishing ingredient: spooned over rice, tossed into noodles, drizzled on a tea-leaf salad. Keep the two separate so the crisp garlic stays crisp (it would soften back in the oil) and use them as a pair.
