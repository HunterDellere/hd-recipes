steps:
  - text: 'Stub: build the wheat koji. Steam the 500 g flour mixed with 250 ml water into a stiff dough (similar to a steamed bun, about 30 minutes over high heat). Cool to body temperature on a clean tray, break apart into 2 cm clumps. Dust with the 2 g koji starter, mix to distribute evenly. Spread on a clean tray, cover with damp cheesecloth, and incubate at 28 to 30°C / 82 to 86°F for 48 hours; the surface should turn fluffy and pale yellow-green as the Aspergillus oryzae colonizes the wheat. Then sun-dry or oven-dry at 50°C / 122°F for 24 hours until the koji is dry and crumbles cleanly. This is your enzymatic engine for the salt cure that follows.'
    time_min: 10080
  - text: 'Stub: combine and salt-cure. Soak the 200 g soybeans overnight, then steam until very tender (45 to 60 minutes). Cool. Combine the cooked soybeans with the 500 g dried wheat koji and 120 g salt in a wide-mouth jar; mix to coat. Pour the 400 ml water over the top, stir to dissolve the salt fully and saturate the koji. Cover with a breathable cloth (not airtight) and leave at warm room temperature (22 to 28°C / 72 to 82°F) for 6 to 8 weeks, stirring weekly and tasting at week 4 onward. The paste is ready when the wheat and bean flavors have integrated into a single sweet-savory profile and the color has deepened from pale ochre to mahogany-brown.'
    time_min: 50400
  - text: 'Stub: finish and store. Once cured to desired depth, blend the paste smooth in a food processor (3 to 4 minutes; the texture should be just shy of peanut butter, with no visible bean or koji chunks). Pack into clean glass jars, refrigerate. Keeps 12 months refrigerated. Commercial tianmianjiang is often cooked briefly at the end to halt fermentation and stabilize; the home version stays raw and continues to slowly mature in the jar.'

notes: |
  This is a stub. To flesh out: the koji chemistry (Aspergillus oryzae enzymatic breakdown of wheat starch into glucose, which is the source of the sweetness in the finished sauce; protease activity breaks soybean proteins into free amino acids, which is the umami), the canonical Beijing tianmianjiang ratio (some traditional recipes use as little as 10% soybean and 90% wheat, producing a much sweeter and less funky sauce; the 40:60 bean:wheat ratio here is a middle path), the salt logic (17% of total weight is at the high end; allows safe room-temperature cure but produces a saltier final sauce than commercial versions, which use 12 to 14% and refrigerate during cure), the brand-name landscape (Liubiju and Wangzhihe are the canonical Beijing brands; Liubiju is sweeter and rounder, Wangzhihe is funkier and more aggressive), and the cross-application potential (tianmianjiang is the foundational sauce for jingjiang rousi, zhajiangmian, Peking duck, and Tianjin jianbing; once a cook commits to one batch, the use-rate is high).

  Realistic timeline: 8 weeks from start to first usable paste. The flavor improves notably with another 4 to 6 months of slow cure in the refrigerator but most home cooks will have used the batch before then. A typical 600 g home batch covers 4 to 6 months of weekly northern-Chinese cooking.

  Failure modes: visible non-target mold (orange, black, fuzzy gray) during the wheat-koji phase or the salt cure means contamination; discard. Aspergillus oryzae should appear as a fluffy pale-yellow-green colonization on the wheat; black, gray, or pink colors are wrong. Kahm yeast (white film on the brine surface) during the salt cure is harmless and skimmed off; other surface molds are not. Wet-bottom or sour-smelling cure means temperature was too high or salt was too low; both produce an unsafe batch that should be discarded. For most home cooks, buying a good commercial Liubiju or Wangzhihe tianmianjiang at any Chinese grocery is the right answer; this homemade version is a project for cooks with strong interest in northern-Chinese fundamentals and active fermentation projects already running.
