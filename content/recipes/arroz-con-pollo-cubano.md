before_you_start:
  - 'The dish is one-pan from start to finish but requires careful sequencing: sear the chicken first, build the sofrito in the rendered fat, then add the rice. Once the rice goes in, do not lift the lid until the cook is done. Lifting releases the steam that finishes the rice.'
  - 'Use Valencia rice (the canonical paella rice) or medium-grain bomba rice for the best texture; long-grain works in a pinch but produces a slightly different texture profile.'
  - 'Have all the sofrito and aromatics chopped before searing the chicken. The cooking moves quickly once the sear is done and you cannot stop to chop mid-cook.'

  name: 'The Cuban canon. Arroz con pollo (rice with chicken) is a pan-Caribbean and Latin American dish family with regional variants in Cuba, Puerto Rico, the Dominican Republic, Colombia, Peru, Spain (the Asturian ancestor), and the Philippines. The Cuban version stabilized in the 19th century with the Cuban sofrito base, the use of dry beer or sherry, and the saffron or annatto coloring. Documented across Cuban exile cookbooks and as a Sunday lunch standard in Cuban households.'
  note: 'The Cuban version is distinguished from the Puerto Rican (which uses recao-based sofrito and pigeon peas) and the Spanish original (which uses saffron and seafood as alternatives). steps:
  - text: 'Phase 1, season and sear the chicken. Rub the 1200 g chicken thighs with the 12 g salt, 3 g pepper, 2 g oregano, and 2 g cumin; let rest 15 minutes at room temperature. Heat the 45 g olive oil in a large heavy paella pan or Dutch oven (30 cm wide is ideal) over medium-high heat until shimmering. Add the chicken skin-side down; sear undisturbed 6 to 7 minutes until the skin is deeply golden and crisp. Flip; sear 3 minutes on the bone side. Transfer the chicken to a plate; reserve.'
    technique: 'maillard-sear'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Phase 2, bloom the annatto and build the sofrito. Add the 5 g annatto seeds to the chicken-fat-and-oil in the pan; cook over medium heat 60 to 90 seconds until the oil turns deep orange-red. Strain the oil through a fine-mesh sieve back into the pan, discarding the seeds (annatto seeds are fibrous and unpleasant in texture). Add the 250 g diced onion and 150 g diced bell pepper; sweat 8 to 10 minutes until soft and slightly golden. Add the 20 g garlic; cook 60 seconds.'
    technique: 'sweating-aromatics'
    time_min: 12
  - text: 'Bloom the tomato and spices. Add the 30 g tomato paste; cook 2 minutes, stirring constantly, until the paste darkens to a deep brick-red. Add the 200 g crushed tomatoes, 3 g cumin, 2 g oregano, and 2 bay leaves; cook 2 to 3 minutes until the sofrito is fragrant and the liquid has reduced slightly. The sofrito should read deeply sweet, slightly smoky from the annatto, and structurally complete.'
    technique: 'spice-tempering'
    time_min: 5
  - text: 'Phase 3, add the rice. Add the 400 g Valencia rice to the sofrito; stir to coat each grain in the oil and sofrito (this is the structural step that prevents the rice from going gummy). Cook 90 seconds, stirring, until the rice surface looks slightly translucent. Pour in the 600 g hot chicken stock and 350 g beer; add 6 g salt. Stir once to distribute, then do not stir again (stirring releases starch and turns the dish gummy).'
    time_min: 3
  - text: 'Braise the chicken and rice. Nestle the seared chicken thighs skin-side up into the rice (the chicken should be partially submerged; the skin stays above the liquid). Bring to a strong simmer over medium-high heat; reduce to low, cover with a tight-fitting lid, and cook 22 to 25 minutes undisturbed. Do not lift the lid; the steam is structural to the rice cook. The rice is done when the liquid is fully absorbed, the chicken reads 76°C / 170°F at the thickest part of the thigh, and the bottom of the pan has a thin layer of toasted rice (the structural Cuban socarrat).'
    time_min: 25
  - text: 'Phase 4, finish. Remove from heat. Scatter the 150 g frozen peas over the top of the dish; arrange the 100 g roasted red pepper strips and 60 g halved olives decoratively. Cover the pan with the lid (or with a clean kitchen towel); rest 10 minutes off heat. The residual heat thaws the peas, warms the peppers, and lets the rice finish absorbing the last moisture.'
    time_min: 10
  - text: 'Serve. Discard the bay leaves. Scatter the 8 g chopped cilantro over the top. Serve directly from the pan at the table, spooning chicken and rice onto each plate. The 60 g lime wedges go on the side; the lime squeeze at the table brightens the dish and is the structural finishing acid. Canonical Cuban pairings: a green salad with vinaigrette, fried sweet plantains, or a small bowl of black beans on the side.'
    time_min: 3

notes: |
  Arroz con pollo Cubano is the Sunday-lunch one-pan dish of the Cuban home and the structural entry point for any cook learning Cuban cuisine. The technique compresses sofrito, sear, and braise into one pan; the result is a dish where the rice has absorbed the flavors of the chicken, the sofrito, and the beer, while the chicken sits on top with crisp skin and tender meat. Master arroz con pollo and most of Cuban one-pan cookery (arroz con gandules, arroz con marisco, paella) follows naturally.

  The annatto-bloom is structural. Annatto seeds infused in hot oil release the structural orange-red color that defines arroz con pollo visually; without the bloom, the rice reads beige rather than the canonical sunset color. Strain the seeds out after blooming (they are fibrous and unpleasant to eat); the structural function is the oil, not the seeds themselves. Saffron and smoked paprika are acceptable substitutes; annatto is the canonical Cuban choice.

  The sear-first sequence is structural. The chicken is seared skin-side down to develop the Maillard crust and to render the fat; that fat then becomes the oil for the sofrito, layering the chicken flavor into the structural base. Skipping the sear (adding raw chicken directly to the rice and braising together) produces a structurally different dish that reads as boiled chicken and rice rather than as arroz con pollo. The sear is non-negotiable for canonical depth.

  The do-not-stir rule is the second structural feature. Once the rice goes into the pan and the liquid is added, do not stir again. Stirring releases the rice starch and produces a gummy risotto-like texture; the Cuban arroz con pollo is structurally a pilaf preparation where each grain stays distinct and absorbs the liquid by capillary action. The toasted bottom layer (socarrat) is a structural feature; serve directly from the pan and scrape the bottom for the best bites.

  The beer is the structural Cuban liquid. A pale lager (Cuban Cristal, Bucanero, or any standard American or Mexican lager) contributes malty body that water or stock alone cannot match; the alcohol cooks off in the first 5 minutes of the simmer, leaving the structural caramelized malt depth. Mexican beers (Modelo, Pacífico) work as natural substitutes; avoid dark beers (porter, stout) which dominate the dish, and avoid hoppy IPAs which read aggressive against the sofrito.

  The finish is structural. The peas, roasted red peppers, and olives are added at the end (not cooked through with the rice) because they contribute structural fresh-and-bright contrast to the long-cooked sofrito-and-rice base. Adding them earlier dulls the colors and softens the textures past the structural point. Scatter them on top, cover, and let the residual heat warm them; this is the canonical service.

  Storage. Refrigerated, 4 days. The rice tightens in the refrigerator and reads firmer reheated than fresh; this is normal. Reheat gently in a covered pan with 30 g water per portion over medium heat 5 minutes. The cooked dish does not freeze well; the rice texture degrades on thawing. Cook fresh whenever possible.
