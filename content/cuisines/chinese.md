      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">Chinese</h1>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">A four-axis pantry — soy, vinegar, aromatic, heat — combined in shifting ratios across regions. Every dish reads the cook's hand on those four levers.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="four-axes"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>The four axes</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p>Most of the canon resolves into four levers. Pull them in different ratios and you move from Cantonese to Sichuan to Shanghainese without changing the underlying grammar.</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Soy.</strong> Light soy sauce for salt and umami; dark soy sauce for color and a faint molasses sweetness. The two are not interchangeable. Most dishes use both, in different proportions.</li>
        <li><strong>Vinegar.</strong> Chinkiang (black, malted) for braises and dipping; rice vinegar for cold dishes and pickles. Acid is rarely loud; it sits under the soy.</li>
        <li><strong>Aromatic.</strong> Ginger, garlic, scallion as the base trio. Star anise, cinnamon, Sichuan peppercorn, white pepper as the spice register. Shaoxing wine deglazes and rounds.</li>
        <li><strong>Heat.</strong> Fresh chilies in the south; dried, fermented, and oil-bloomed in Sichuan and Hunan. Sichuan peppercorn brings numbing tingle (málà), a separate axis from chili pungency.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>Regional split, briefly: Cantonese pulls toward freshness and restraint (more light soy, less spice); Sichuan pulls toward málà and chili oil; Shanghainese leans dark soy and rock sugar (red-cooking); northern dishes lean wheat, vinegar, and raw garlic.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="pantry"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>Pantry</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p>The twelve ingredients that earn shelf space if you cook Chinese food more than occasionally.</p>
      <ul>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/light-soy-sauce.html">Light soy sauce</a>: the salt-and-umami workhorse. Most "soy sauce" in a recipe is this one.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/dark-soy-sauce.html">Dark soy sauce</a>: aged, thicker, used in small amounts for color. Not a substitute for light soy.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/chinkiang-vinegar.html">Chinkiang vinegar</a>: black rice vinegar with a malty depth. Carries braises and dumpling dips.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/shaoxing-wine.html">Shaoxing wine</a>: fermented rice wine for deglazing and aroma. Dry sherry is the closest sub.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/sichuan-peppercorns.html">Sichuan peppercorns</a>: citrusy, numbing. Toast and crush at use; pre-ground loses the volatile oils within weeks.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/star-anise.html">Star anise</a>: anchors red-cooked dishes and master stocks. One pod goes a long way.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/ginger.html">Ginger</a>: fresh, sliced or smashed. Old ginger for braises, young for stir-fries and salads.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/garlic.html">Garlic</a>: sliced, smashed, or grated depending on register. Raw garlic is its own ingredient.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/white-pepper.html">White pepper</a>: sharper and more floral than black; the default ground pepper in Chinese soups and stir-fries.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/toasted-sesame-oil.html">Toasted sesame oil</a>: finishing oil only. High heat destroys it.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/sesame-paste.html">Sesame paste</a>: Chinese sesame paste (toasted) is darker and nuttier than tahini. The base of dandan sauce.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/chili-oil.html">Chili oil</a>: store-bought is fine to start; eventually you will make your own with whole spices.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>Supporting cast that earns space once you settle in: <a href="../ingredients/rock-sugar.html">rock sugar</a> for braises, <a href="../ingredients/wood-ear-mushroom.html">wood ear mushroom</a> for texture in cold dishes, <a href="../ingredients/sui-mi-ya-cai.html">sui mi ya cai</a> for dandan and dry-fried beans, <a href="../ingredients/dried-osmanthus.html">dried osmanthus</a> for desserts.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="start"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>Where to start</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p><strong>Start with <a href="../recipes/chinese-tea-eggs.html">tea eggs</a>.</strong> The smallest possible vehicle that teaches the three moves you will use over and over: blooming whole aromatics in liquid, the contrast between light and dark soy, and steeping as a flavor-loading technique distinct from cooking. The recipe is technically a snack, but the brine is the same family of liquid you build for red-cooked pork or master-stock chicken, just at a different concentration. Get fluent here and the leap to braises is small.</p>
      <p><strong>Then <a href="../recipes/sichuan-dandan-mian.html">dandan mian</a>.</strong> Once tea eggs feel automatic, dandan introduces the sesame-paste-plus-hot-water emulsion, the rendered-pork-plus-ya-cai topping, and the chili oil register. It is also the first recipe where the four axes are simultaneously visible in one bowl: soy underneath, Chinkiang on top, Sichuan peppercorn in the oil, garlic raw at the bottom. Learning to balance those by taste rather than measurement is the next skill.</p>
      <p>For a cold-dish counterpoint, <a href="../recipes/smashed-cucumber-wood-ear-salad.html">smashed cucumber and wood ear salad</a> teaches the raw-garlic-plus-Chinkiang dressing that anchors most northern cold plates.</p>
