    <span class="section-anchor" id="flavor-logic"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>The flavor logic</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p>Italian cooking is a discipline of subtraction. Three or four ingredients on a plate, each one carrying a specific job: fat, salt-umami, acid, and starch. Most of the canon recurs from those four roles in different combinations.</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Fat.</strong> Olive oil in the south and center, butter in the north, cured pork (guanciale, pancetta) almost everywhere. Fat is the carrier: for pepper piperine, for cheese solids, for the volatile aromatics in garlic and herbs.</li>
        <li><strong>Salt-umami.</strong> Aged cheese (pecorino, parmigiano), anchovy, tomato paste reduced hard, cured pork. These are the savory anchors. A dish almost always has one; rarely more than two.</li>
        <li><strong>Acid.</strong> Lemon, wine, vinegar, the lactic tang of pecorino itself. Used to lift, never to dominate. If you taste the acid clearly as acid, you've gone too far.</li>
        <li><strong>Starch.</strong> Pasta water is the fourth axis: the suspended starch is what builds emulsions in pan-built sauces. Discarding all the pasta water is the most common mistake non-Italians make.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>The technique sits in the ratios. Cacio e pepe, carbonara, and amatriciana share a structure: hot pasta, fat, cheese, starchy water, vigorous tossing. Swap pecorino for guanciale-rendered fat plus pecorino and you have carbonara; add tomato and onion and you have amatriciana. The discipline is identical.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="pantry"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>Pantry</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p>The site's Italian footprint is small for now (one recipe), so the pantry below reflects what's already here, with a forward-looking note on what's coming.</p>
      <ul>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/pecorino-romano.html">Pecorino romano</a>: aged sheep's-milk, sharp and salty. The lactic tang is its own form of acid. Microplane at use; pre-grated is dust.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/butter.html">Unsalted butter</a>: northern fat. Brown it for nutty Maillard depth that approximates the savory floor of cured pork in vegetarian dishes.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/black-pepper.html">Black peppercorns</a>: whole, cracked at use. The piperine is fat-soluble; bloom in butter or oil before tossing.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/all-purpose-flour.html">All-purpose flour</a>: for fresh pasta, gnocchi, and coating cutlets. Tipo 00 is better for pasta if you can find it; AP works.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>Coming soon to the pantry as the catalog expands: extra-virgin olive oil (cooking grade and finishing grade; keep two bottles), guanciale, parmigiano reggiano, anchovies in oil, San Marzano tomatoes, dried pasta, garlic.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="start"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>Where to start</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p><strong><a href="../recipes/brown-butter-cacio-e-pepe.html">Brown butter cacio e pepe</a>.</strong> Three ingredients plus pasta. The entire dish is a single technique — pan emulsion — and it either holds or it breaks. There is nowhere to hide. If the pan is too hot the cheese seizes into a clump; if it's too cool the cheese never hydrates. The fix in both cases is more pasta water and harder tossing, and learning to feel that fix in your wrist is the move that unlocks every other pan-built Italian sauce: carbonara, amatriciana, alfredo, vongole.</p>
      <p>The brown butter version is a divergence from tradition (classic uses olive oil only), but the technique is identical and the Maillard products in the milk solids replace the savory anchor of pancetta, which makes this a useful vegetarian gateway to the carbonara family without compromising structure.</p>
    </div>
