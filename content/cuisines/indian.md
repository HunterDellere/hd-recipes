    <span class="section-anchor" id="flavor-logic"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>The flavor logic</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p>Indian cooking is, at its core, a system for managing volatile aromatics across time and heat. The same spice (cardamom, cumin, coriander) behaves as a different ingredient depending on whether it enters the pan whole in hot oil, ground into a curry paste, or added raw as a finishing note. Understanding this is more useful than memorizing any specific recipe.</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Whole spices in fat (tadka / tarka).</strong> The first move in most North Indian dishes. Whole seeds or pods dropped into hot ghee or oil: the fat-soluble aromatics bloom and transfer into the cooking medium before anything else goes in. The window is short. Cardamom and cinnamon extract in seconds; held too long they bitter.</li>
        <li><strong>Ground masala mid-cook.</strong> The spice paste or powder added after the aromatics soften. Needs direct contact with the pan fat before the liquids arrive, or the raw-flour note never cooks out. This is why the recipe tells you to cook the masala until the fat separates: that visual cue means the volatile top notes have driven off and the deeper compounds have had time to develop.</li>
        <li><strong>Dairy as buffer.</strong> Milk, cream, and yogurt moderate heat and carry spice compounds differently than water-based broths. They also prevent over-reduction: the proteins thicken and coat, protecting the sauce from going dry.</li>
        <li><strong>Acid as finish.</strong> Tamarind, amchur (dried mango powder), lime. Applied late, rarely reduced in. The goal is brightness at the end, not body built through cooking.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>A note on scope: the Indian catalog here is currently one drink. That is honest, not comprehensive. The drink teaches the spice-layering principle (whole spices bloomed in fat-rich milk, tea steeped off heat to avoid bitterness) in a forgiving format. The full savory repertoire (dal, biryani, curry) will follow as those dishes earn pages.</p>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="pantry"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>Pantry</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p>The chai recipe here draws on a subset of the Indian spice pantry. These are the ingredients the site currently uses.</p>
      <ul>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/assam-tea.html">Assam tea</a>: robust, malty, high-tannin loose-leaf black from northeast India. The default base for masala chai; steep off heat to avoid harsh bitterness.</li>
        <li><strong>Black cardamom.</strong> Smoky, camphor-adjacent, larger pod than green. Bloomed whole in fat; the seeds crack to release the aromatics.</li>
        <li><strong>Green cardamom.</strong> Floral, citrusy. Same family, entirely different register. Both appear in chai; neither substitutes for the other.</li>
        <li><strong>Cinnamon (cassia).</strong> The bulk spice in masala blends. Cassia is more assertive than Ceylon; either works but the ratio needs adjusting.</li>
        <li><strong>Whole cloves.</strong> Intense; one or two is enough. A third tips the blend medicinal.</li>
        <li><strong>Black peppercorns.</strong> Present in most masala blends for mild background heat and to extend the finish.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/ginger.html">Fresh ginger.</a> Added toward the end of the chai simmer; its heat is sharper and more volatile than the dry-spice register.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/whole-milk.html">Whole milk.</a> The carrier. Full fat is not optional: the fat phase dissolves fat-soluble spice aromatics and gives the drink body.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>Coming to the pantry as the catalog expands: ghee, fenugreek seeds, cumin, coriander, turmeric, mustard seeds, dried red chilies, curry leaves, tamarind, amchur.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="start"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>Where to start</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p><strong><a href="../recipes/house-blend-chai.html">House blend chai</a>.</strong> The teaching unit here is the whole-spice bloom: whole cardamom and cinnamon dropped into hot milk, extracted in fat before the tea goes in, then the tea steeped off heat to pull flavor without bitterness. Every Indian recipe that starts with "heat ghee and add whole spices" is running the same sequence in a different medium. Get fluent with the timing (the two-to-three minute bloom window, the off-heat steep) and that pattern becomes recognizable everywhere in the canon.</p>
      <p>The recipe is also an introduction to the masala principle: grinding your own blend means you control the ratios, and small adjustments (more black cardamom for smoke, more ginger for heat, less clove to soften) produce a meaningfully different drink. Most masala blends sold commercially are calibrated for inoffensiveness rather than character.</p>
