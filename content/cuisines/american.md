    <span class="section-anchor" id="flavor-logic"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>The flavor logic</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p>"American" is a wide tent, and a useful cuisine page won't try to cover all of it. The throughline across the recipes on this site is a deliberate counter-move to the default American grocery palate: bakes that pull back on sugar and lean into browned fat; savory dishes that punch acid and herbs forward instead of relying on dairy and salt alone.</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>The bake side.</strong> Brown butter, dark sugars, restrained leavening. The goal is depth, not sweetness — a sugar cookie that tastes of toasted dairy and caramel rather than just sugar; a chocolate cookie where the cocoa is loud and the sugar steps back. Sweetness is the carrier, not the headline.</li>
        <li><strong>The savory side.</strong> Lemon, mustard, vinegar, raw herbs as the brightening register; dry-brining and glazing as the structural moves. American holiday roasts live or die on whether the cook treats the protein like a project (dry brine, two-stage roast, late glaze) or a single-step task.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>What unifies them is technique discipline over ingredient flexing. Brown the butter properly. Dry-brine the bird overnight. Hit the green beans with garlic crunch instead of more salt. The ingredients are familiar; the moves are not optional.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="pantry"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>Pantry</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p>Split into two — the bake-side core and the savory-finish kit. Most American home kitchens already have a version of both; the question is whether each item is the right grade.</p>
      <p><strong>Bake core</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/butter.html">Unsalted butter</a> — brown it for nutty Maillard depth; control the salt yourself.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/all-purpose-flour.html">All-purpose flour</a> — the workhorse. Bleached vs unbleached matters less than weighing it.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/granulated-sugar.html">Granulated sugar</a>, <a href="../ingredients/light-brown-sugar.html">light brown sugar</a>, <a href="../ingredients/dark-brown-sugar.html">dark brown sugar</a> — three different molasses levels, three different jobs. Not interchangeable.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/baking-soda.html">Baking soda</a> and <a href="../ingredients/baking-powder.html">baking powder</a> — different chemistries; recipes that call for both are doing it deliberately.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/vanilla-extract.html">Vanilla extract</a> — real, not imitation. Imitation reads as plastic in a low-sugar bake where it can't hide.</li>
        <li><a href="../ingredients/eggs.html">Eggs</a>, <a href="../ingredients/kosher-salt.html">kosher salt</a>, <a href="../ingredients/heavy-cream.html">heavy cream</a>, <a href="../ingredients/dutch-process-cocoa.html">Dutch-process cocoa</a>, <a href="../ingredients/dark-chocolate.html">dark chocolate</a> for the deeper bakes.</li>
      </ul>
      <p><strong>Savory finish</strong></p>
      <p>A working savory pantry needs whole-grain and Dijon mustard, lemons (zest and juice both), apple cider vinegar, maple syrup (Grade A dark or Grade B), fresh herbs (thyme, sage, parsley, dill — by season), and good kosher salt for dry brining. These pages are coming as the catalog grows; for now the savory recipes call them out inline.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="start"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><h2>Where to start — two doors</h2></div>
    <div class="scholar">
      <p>The catalog has a savory side and a sweet side, and they teach different things. Pick based on what you cook more often.</p>
      <p><strong>Bake side: <a href="../recipes/brown-butter-sugar-cookies.html">brown butter sugar cookies</a>.</strong> The teaching unit is brown butter plus restrained sweet. You melt the butter past the foaming stage to deep amber, let it cool, and build a one-bowl dough around it. The result is a cookie with the structural depth of a holiday bake at weeknight effort. Get this right and the same brown-butter base unlocks chocolate chip, blondies, and the entire crackled-top family.</p>
      <p><strong>Savory side: <a href="../recipes/maple-mustard-glazed-turkey-breast.html">maple-mustard glazed turkey breast</a>.</strong> The teaching unit is dry-brining plus late glaze application. Salt goes on the bird the day before, the herb butter goes under the skin, the glaze goes on only in the last fifteen minutes (any earlier and the sugars burn before the meat is done). It is the structural template for any glazed protein — pork loin, salmon, chicken thighs — and it is the recipe that demonstrates why American holiday cooking, done right, is a project rather than an event.</p>
      <p>For a vegetable side that teaches the bright-finish register, <a href="../recipes/lemony-green-beans-with-garlic-crunch.html">lemony green beans with garlic crunch</a> shows what acid and texture do to a vegetable when you trust them to carry the dish.</p>
    </div>
