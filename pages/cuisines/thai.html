The flavor logic

Thai cooking is the canonical example of a cuisine that holds four flavor registers (sweet, salt, heat, sour) inside a single dish. Western traditions tend to separate them across a meal: a salad as the acid course, a main as the salt-and-fat course, a dessert as the sweet. Thai food refuses that split. A single bite of green papaya salad carries all four; a curry does too; even a glass of cha yen has sweet, salt (the milk's mineral edge), and the bitter-tannic length of long-steeped tea.

  • Sweet. Palm sugar, condensed milk, ripe fruit. Rarely cane sugar straight; the caramel notes of palm or the dairy depth of condensed milk pull their weight.
  • Salt. Fish sauce above all. A Thai pantry without fish sauce is not a Thai pantry.
  • Heat. Fresh bird's-eye chilies, dried chilies in pastes. The heat is sharp and quick, not slow-burning.
  • Sour. Lime, tamarind, sometimes green mango. Acid is loud and forward, the opposite of the Italian rule.

A note on what's missing: this site's Thai catalog is currently two recipes and both are drinks. That is honest, not aspirational. The flavor-base description above describes the cuisine; the recipes below describe what the catalog has earned. As savory dishes (curries, salads, noodles) get cooked enough times to write up, this page will expand.

Pantry

What's here now is built around the drinks. The savory pantry (fish sauce, palm sugar, lime, lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime leaves, bird's-eye chilies, tamarind, jasmine rice) is not yet on the site and will land as the recipes do.

  • Assam tea: robust, malty, high-tannin loose-leaf black. The tea base for Thai iced tea. Ceylon works as a sub; bagged English breakfast does not (it stales fast and tastes flat).
  • Sweetened condensed milk: the sweetener and the dairy in one move. Carries caramelized lactose notes that plain sugar plus milk can't replicate.
  • Evaporated milk: the float on top. Thicker mouthfeel and faintly toasted from the canning process; not interchangeable with whole milk or cream.
  • Star anise: one of the spice anchors in the tea decoction. A single pod is enough.
  • Cinnamon stick: the second spice anchor. Cassia or Ceylon both work; cassia is more assertive.
  • Vanilla bean paste: for the whipped-cream variant. Paste over extract here because the seeds carry visually and the alcohol bite of extract competes with the tea.

Where to start

Thai iced tea (no dye). The teaching unit is extraction without shortcuts: a spice decoction simmered separately, a gentle off-heat tea steep (long enough to pull tannin and color, short enough not to go bitter), condensed milk as both sweetener and body, evaporated milk as the float on top. There's no orange food coloring; the color comes from the steep, which means the steep has to be right. Get fluent here and the same extraction logic carries into chai, hojicha lattes, and any milk-tea-style drink built on whole spices and loose leaf.

The vanilla whip variant is the same base with a whipped-cream cap, useful once the no-dye version feels automatic and you want to push the dessert register.

For the first savory entry, green curry pork is the four-register balance in a bowl: store-bought paste augmented with fresh aromatics (lemongrass, ginger, shrimp paste) for depth, two cans of full-fat coconut milk as the fat buffer, and the eggplant-bamboo-shimeji combination to carry texture at different bite sizes. It teaches the augmentation principle (how to push a jar paste toward restaurant flavor with twenty minutes of prep) and the coconut milk management (keep it lipid-rich; diluting with stock kills the register).
