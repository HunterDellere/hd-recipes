Cuisine

French

Technique as the transferable unit. The French canon is less about ingredients than about a vocabulary of methods: emulsification, reduction, the roux. These travel across the full range of the cuisine.

The flavor logic

French cooking is organized around method, not ingredient. The same handful of techniques (emulsification, reduction, the maillard crust, the roux) recur across nearly every classic dish. The ingredient list changes by region and season; the underlying grammar stays the same.

  • Fat as carrier. Butter, crème fraîche, duck fat. French fat is rich and almost always present in the sauce. It dissolves flavors, extends texture, and builds mouthfeel that water-based reductions alone cannot achieve.
  • Acid as structure. Wine, mustard, vinegar, lemon. Acid does not feature loudly in French cooking; it lifts and sharpens, preventing richness from going flat. A beurre blanc lives on the balance between butter fat and wine acid.
  • Reduction as concentration. Most French sauces are built on reduced stock or wine. The goal is not to add more; it is to cook off water until what is left is dense, coherent, and self-supporting.
  • Mustard as emulsifier. Dijon appears in vinaigrettes, pan sauces, and braises not only for flavor but because the mucilage in the mustard seed holds oil and vinegar together. This is why French vinaigrette does not immediately split.

A note on scope: this catalog is currently two condiments. That is honest. The recipes below reflect what has been cooked carefully enough to write up — the broader French repertoire will follow as those dishes earn pages.

Pantry

The site's French footprint is condiment-first for now. These two earn shelf space in any kitchen, not just a French one.

  • Mustard seeds. Brown (hotter, more pungent) and yellow (milder, more aromatic) ground together in the Dijon tradition. The heat of mustard comes from the myrosinase reaction when the seed is broken in acid; no heat means no heat.
  • White wine. The soaking liquid for the seeds and the body of the grind. Anything dry and acidic works; a sharp cider vinegar finish pulls it tighter.
  • Neutral vinegar. Cider or white wine vinegar as the acid finish. Sharpens without competing.

Coming to the pantry as the catalog expands: crème fraîche, shallots, tarragon, unsalted butter, dry white wine, chicken stock, herbes de Provence.

Where to start

Dijon mustard. The case for making it yourself is taste, not difficulty. Fresh Dijon has a sharp, volatile heat from the myrosinase reaction that is fully present when made and largely gone from the jar. The technique (soak, blend, cook briefly to bind) is twenty minutes and teaches the emulsifier principle that will reappear every time you make a vinaigrette or a pan sauce.

Whole-grain mustard is the same method pulsed coarse instead of ground smooth. The intact seeds release heat in waves as they pop rather than all at once, giving the condiment a textural and temporal character that Dijon does not have. Once the Dijon method is fluent, the whole-grain version is a five-minute variation.
