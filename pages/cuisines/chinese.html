Chinese

A four-axis pantry — soy, vinegar, aromatic, heat — combined in shifting ratios across regions. Every dish reads the cook's hand on those four levers.

The four axes

Most of the canon resolves into four levers. Pull them in different ratios and you move from Cantonese to Sichuan to Shanghainese without changing the underlying grammar.

  • Soy. Light soy sauce for salt and umami; dark soy sauce for color and a faint molasses sweetness. The two are not interchangeable. Most dishes use both, in different proportions.
  • Vinegar. Chinkiang (black, malted) for braises and dipping; rice vinegar for cold dishes and pickles. Acid is rarely loud; it sits under the soy.
  • Aromatic. Ginger, garlic, scallion as the base trio. Star anise, cinnamon, Sichuan peppercorn, white pepper as the spice register. Shaoxing wine deglazes and rounds.
  • Heat. Fresh chilies in the south; dried, fermented, and oil-bloomed in Sichuan and Hunan. Sichuan peppercorn brings numbing tingle (málà), a separate axis from chili pungency.

Regional split, briefly: Cantonese pulls toward freshness and restraint (more light soy, less spice); Sichuan pulls toward málà and chili oil; Shanghainese leans dark soy and rock sugar (red-cooking); northern dishes lean wheat, vinegar, and raw garlic.

Pantry

The twelve ingredients that earn shelf space if you cook Chinese food more than occasionally.

  • Light soy sauce: the salt-and-umami workhorse. Most "soy sauce" in a recipe is this one.
  • Dark soy sauce: aged, thicker, used in small amounts for color. Not a substitute for light soy.
  • Chinkiang vinegar: black rice vinegar with a malty depth. Carries braises and dumpling dips.
  • Shaoxing wine: fermented rice wine for deglazing and aroma. Dry sherry is the closest sub.
  • Sichuan peppercorns: citrusy, numbing. Toast and crush at use; pre-ground loses the volatile oils within weeks.
  • Star anise: anchors red-cooked dishes and master stocks. One pod goes a long way.
  • Ginger: fresh, sliced or smashed. Old ginger for braises, young for stir-fries and salads.
  • Garlic: sliced, smashed, or grated depending on register. Raw garlic is its own ingredient.
  • White pepper: sharper and more floral than black; the default ground pepper in Chinese soups and stir-fries.
  • Toasted sesame oil: finishing oil only. High heat destroys it.
  • Sesame paste: Chinese sesame paste (toasted) is darker and nuttier than tahini. The base of dandan sauce.
  • Chili oil: store-bought is fine to start; eventually you will make your own with whole spices.

Supporting cast that earns space once you settle in: rock sugar for braises, wood ear mushroom for texture in cold dishes, sui mi ya cai for dandan and dry-fried beans, dried osmanthus for desserts.

Where to start

Start with tea eggs. The smallest possible vehicle that teaches the three moves you will use over and over: blooming whole aromatics in liquid, the contrast between light and dark soy, and steeping as a flavor-loading technique distinct from cooking. The recipe is technically a snack, but the brine is the same family of liquid you build for red-cooked pork or master-stock chicken, just at a different concentration. Get fluent here and the leap to braises is small.

Then dandan mian. Once tea eggs feel automatic, dandan introduces the sesame-paste-plus-hot-water emulsion, the rendered-pork-plus-ya-cai topping, and the chili oil register. It is also the first recipe where the four axes are simultaneously visible in one bowl: soy underneath, Chinkiang on top, Sichuan peppercorn in the oil, garlic raw at the bottom. Learning to balance those by taste rather than measurement is the next skill.

For a cold-dish counterpoint, smashed cucumber and wood ear salad teaches the raw-garlic-plus-Chinkiang dressing that anchors most northern cold plates.
