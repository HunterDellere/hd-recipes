Jamaican ackee and saltfish

The national dish of Jamaica: salt cod desalted in cold-water soaks, then flaked and sauteed with sweet onion, bell pepper, scotch bonnet, scallion, thyme, and tomato, with canned ackee (the buttery yellow arils of a tropical fruit) folded in at the last minute so they hold their shape. The dish reads buttery, lightly smoky, gently sweet, with a clean salt-cod chew.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The salt cod needs 12 to 24 hours of cold-water soaking with 2 to 3 water changes to remove enough salt to be edible. Plan the day before. Skipping or shortening this soak produces a brick-salty dish that no other ingredient can rescue.
  • Canned ackee is structural and is the only safe way to cook ackee outside the Caribbean. Look for Linstead Market, Grace, Ashbourne, or Tropical Sun brands at Caribbean groceries or Latin markets; the cans say "ackee in salted water." Fresh ackee picked unripe causes Jamaican vomiting sickness (hypoglycin A toxicity), which is potentially fatal; never substitute fresh.
  • Ackee is extremely delicate. Once added to the pan, fold gently with a wooden spoon and never stir aggressively; the arils crumble into mush in under a minute of vigorous mixing. The texture of intact buttery arils is the structural pleasure of the dish.

Phase 1, the salt cod (start day before)

  1. 250 g salt cod (bacalao), thick fillets if possible, on the bone or off, soaked 12 to 24 hours with 2 to 3 water changes

Phase 2, the sofrito

  1. 30 ml neutral oil or coconut oil
  2. 150 g yellow onion, medium dice, medium dice (~1 cm) — (1 medium onion)
  3. 120 g red bell pepper, medium dice, seeded, medium dice — (1 medium pepper; red is canonical, green works)
  4. 50 g scallions, sliced, thinly sliced, whites and greens — (4 scallions)
  5. 12 g garlic, minced — (3 cloves)
  6. 4 g fresh thyme — (6 to 8 sprigs, leaves stripped)
  7. 6 g scotch bonnet, finely minced (gloves on), seeds removed for medium heat, finely minced — (½ chili)
  8. 150 g roma tomato, seeded and diced, seeded, medium dice — (1 large tomato)
  9. 2 g black pepper, cracked

Phase 3, the ackee (folded in last)

  1. 540 g canned ackee (Linstead, Grace, or Ashbourne), drained gently in a colander; do not rinse or shake

Execution

  1. 1
    Day before: rinse the 250 g salt cod under cold running water for 30 seconds to wash off surface salt. Place in a wide bowl and cover with cold water by 5 cm. Refrigerate. Change the water at 4 hours, again at 8 hours, and once more before cooking; total soak 12 to 24 hours. After the final soak, taste a corner of the fish; it should read pleasantly salty (like firm seasoned fish), not aggressively salty. If still too salty, soak another 4 hours.1 d 0 h
  2. 2
    Drain the soaked cod and place in a saucepan. Cover with fresh cold water by 2 cm and bring to a bare simmer (do not boil hard; boiling toughens the fish). Simmer 8 minutes until the fish flakes easily when pressed with a fork. Drain. When cool enough to handle, remove any skin, dark stripe, or bones; flake the flesh into rough 2 cm pieces by hand. Reserve.
  3. 3
    Heat the 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil or coconut oil in a wide skillet over medium-high. Add the 150 g diced onion and 120 g diced bell pepper; saute 4 to 5 minutes until the onion is translucent and the pepper has softened with light golden edges. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Reduce heat to medium. Add the 50 g sliced scallions, 12 g / 3 cloves garlic, 4 g thyme leaves, and 6 g minced scotch bonnet. Cook 90 seconds until fragrant and the scallion greens have wilted. Blooming aromatics
  5. 5
    Add the 150 g diced tomato; cook 3 to 4 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the tomato has broken down into a thick pulp and the liquid has reduced. Add the 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper.
  6. 6
    Add the flaked saltfish; toss gently with the sofrito for 2 minutes until heated through and the fish has absorbed the aromatics. Do not add salt at this stage; the fish carries its own. Taste a small piece for salt; if needed, finish with a pinch at the end after the ackee is added (it usually does not).
  7. 7
    Add the drained ackee (about 320 g). Reduce heat to medium-low. Fold the ackee in with a wooden spoon, 3 or 4 gentle lifting motions, just enough to distribute. Do not stir or break the arils. Cover the pan and let warm through 2 minutes. The ackee should stay in intact, butter-yellow arils throughout the dish, with the saltfish-and-sofrito clinging to but not crushing them.
  8. 8
    Off heat, taste once more for seasoning (it almost never needs more salt). Serve hot, alongside fried festival, hard dough bread, or the canonical breakfast plate of boiled green banana, boiled yam, and fried plantain. The dish does not hold well; assemble the partners before the ackee goes into the pan.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion occasion→as the saltfish-only side dish (for partnering with boiled foods)

    Skip the ackee entirely. Increase the saltfish to 350 g (rehydrated) and the tomato to 200 g. Serve as a saltfish side over boiled green banana, yam, and dumpling; this is the daily-life Jamaican version when ackee is out of season or unavailable.

  • Protein protein pivot→as ackee with shrimp

    Replace the saltfish with 250 g raw peeled shrimp seasoned with 4 g salt. Add the shrimp at the end of step 4 (just before the tomato); cook 90 seconds per side until pink, then proceed. The dish reads sweeter and less salty than the cod version; common Caribbean variant.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • salt cod (bacalao) → salt pollock, or fresh cod brined 4 hours in 8% salt solution Salt pollock is a 1:1 substitute (often labeled "saltfish" in Caribbean groceries and is actually pollock these days). Brined fresh cod is a workaround if no salt cod is available; the texture reads softer and the salt depth is less complex.
  • canned ackee → NEVER fresh ackee. If unavailable, substitute scrambled eggs (4 eggs, soft-scrambled then folded in) The egg version is called "ackee and saltfish without the ackee" by Jamaicans and is an acknowledged improvisation; the texture is close enough that the dish still reads like itself.
  • scotch bonnet → habanero 1:1 substitute. Avoid jalapeno or serrano, the fruit notes are wrong.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → salt cod (bacalao) make it yourselfstub Home-curing fresh cod with kosher salt and a 5 day cold cure produces a fish identical to commercial salt cod, with full control over the salt level and the option to use a fresher fish than the dried imports.

Notes

Why two soaks plus a fresh-water simmer. The first soak (12 to 24 hours with water changes) reduces the salt cod from about 18% salt by weight to about 3 to 4%. The fresh-water simmer in step 2 cooks the fish and drops the salt another notch into the 1.5 to 2% range, which reads as well-seasoned rather than aggressively salty. A single 4-hour soak leaves the fish at 10%+ salt; the dish becomes uneatable.

The ackee texture rule. Canned ackee arils are about 60% water bound into a soft butter-textured cell structure. The cell walls fail at the touch of a spoon; over-stirring breaks every aril into yellow paste, which tastes the same but reads visually wrong. The dish is named "ackee" first for a reason; the intact arils are the point. Fold gently 3 or 4 times and stop.

Why no added salt anywhere. The salt cod brings the salt budget for the whole dish, even after the soaking. Adding salt during the sofrito stage produces an oversalted finished dish. Taste at the end before deciding to salt.

Pairings. The canonical breakfast plate in Jamaica is ackee and saltfish with boiled green banana, boiled yam, fried plantain, and a fried dumpling or festival (sweet fried cornmeal dumpling). The starchy partners are not optional; the dish is intensely seasoned and benefits from the bland starches to balance.

Storage. Best eaten immediately. Leftovers keep 1 day refrigerated and reheat acceptably in a covered pan over very low heat with a splash of water; the ackee texture degrades on day 2 (becomes more uniformly soft, less aril-distinct).

Sources
  • The Jamaican national dish — Ackee (Blighia sapida) is a West African fruit brought to Jamaica during the slave trade and naturalized as the national fruit and structural component of the national dish. The fruit is dangerous when unripe (it contains hypoglycin A, which causes Jamaican vomiting sickness), so home cooks outside Jamaica work almost exclusively from canned ackee, which is harvested ripe and cooked-canned. The dish pairs ackee with desalted bacalao (saltfish) for protein and umami; the structural method is desalt the fish, build a Jamaican sofrito, fold the ackee in last to preserve the buttery aril texture.
