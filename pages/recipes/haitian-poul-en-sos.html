Haitian poul en sos

Haitian chicken in tomato-pepper sauce: bone-in chicken pieces marinated in epis (the Haitian green seasoning paste of parsley, scallion, garlic, thyme, bell pepper, Scotch bonnet, lime), then dry-browned and braised in a tomato-paste-and-onion sauce until the meat is fall-tender and the sauce is glossy red-orange. The structural Haitian Sunday dinner.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The chicken needs at least a 4 hour marinade in epis for the green seasoning to penetrate. The canonical Haitian preparation is overnight; plan ahead. Same-day poul en sos is acceptable for a 4 hour minimum cold marinade.
  • Epis (also spelled epis vert) is the Haitian sofrito-equivalent: a blender paste of parsley, scallion, garlic, thyme, bell pepper, Scotch bonnet, and lime juice, made in batches and kept in the fridge for daily use. The recipe builds a small fresh batch here; a Haitian-grocery jar is acceptable.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the epis (green seasoning paste)

  1. 30 g flat-leaf parsley, leaves and tender stems, rough chopped — (1 large handful)
  2. 80 g scallions, rough chopped — (6 scallions)
  3. 25 g garlic cloves — (6 cloves)
  4. 80 g green bell pepper, rough chopped — (½ pepper)
  5. 4 g fresh thyme leaves
  6. 8 g Scotch bonnet (gloves on), seeds removed for medium heat, rough chopped — (1 small chili)
  7. 30 ml fresh lime juice
  8. 30 ml neutral oil (for the paste)

Phase 2, the chicken (marinate 4 to 24 hours)

  1. 1200 g bone-in skin-on chicken thighs and drumsticks, rinsed with 30 ml white vinegar then patted dry (Haitian standard pre-treatment)
  2. 12 g kosher salt
  3. 3 g black pepper, cracked

Phase 3, the cook

  1. 30 ml neutral oil (for the sear)
  2. 200 g yellow onion, thick slice, sliced into half-moons 5 mm thick — (1 large)
  3. 150 g red bell pepper, sliced, sliced into strips 1 cm wide — (1 medium)
  4. 1 × 6 oz / 170 g can tomato paste
  5. 400 ml water or chicken stock
  6. 12 g whole intact Scotch bonnet — (1 chili, whole and unpierced)
  7. 15 ml fresh lime juice (for the finish)

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the epis. Combine all of Phase 1 (30 g parsley, 80 g scallions, 25 g garlic, 80 g green pepper, 4 g thyme, 8 g Scotch bonnet, 30 ml lime juice, 30 ml oil) in a blender or food processor. Pulse until you have a coarse green paste, scraping down once or twice. The paste should look like a chunky pesto, not a smooth puree. Yields about 250 g; you will use about 120 g in the marinade and the rest keeps 2 weeks refrigerated for future use.
  2. 2
    Rub the 1200 g chicken with the 12 g / 3 tsp salt, 3 g / 1½ tsp pepper, and 120 g of the fresh epis. Massage into the skin and under any loose skin flaps. Refrigerate 4 to 24 hours.
  3. 3
    Day of: scrape most of the epis off the chicken back into the marinade bowl (the wet paste burns aggressively during the sear); reserve the scraped-off epis for the braise. Pat the chicken pieces dry with paper towels. Heat the 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide deep skillet or shallow Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Sear the chicken in 2 batches, skin-side down first, 5 to 6 minutes per side until deep gold. Transfer to a plate; reserve. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Reduce heat to medium. Add the 200 g sliced onion and 150 g sliced red bell pepper to the pan; cook 5 to 6 minutes in the rendered chicken fat until the onion is translucent and starting to brown at the edges and the pepper has softened.
  5. 5
    Add the reserved epis (from the scrape-off in step 3) and the 45 g / 3 tbsp tomato paste. Cook 2 minutes, stirring, until the tomato paste has darkened from bright red to brick red and the epis has begun to caramelize into the onion. The pan should smell aggressively of garlic, thyme, and Maillard-tomato. Blooming aromatics
  6. 6
    Add the 400 ml / 1⅔ cups water or stock; scrape the fond off the pan bottom. Bring to a hard simmer. Return the seared chicken to the pan, skin-side up, along with any accumulated juices. The liquid should come about two-thirds up the chicken. Float the whole 12 g Scotch bonnet on top.
  7. 7
    Reduce heat to medium-low, cover with the lid slightly ajar (10 percent evaporation), and braise 30 minutes. Check at the 20-minute mark; the sauce should be steadily simmering and reducing.
  8. 8
    Uncover; raise heat to medium. Reduce the sauce 5 to 8 minutes more until it has thickened to a glossy consistency that coats the back of a spoon and the chicken is fully tender (a fork inserted into a thigh meets no resistance). Fish out and discard the Scotch bonnet (carefully, do not pierce now). Off heat, drizzle in the 15 ml / 1 tbsp fresh lime juice and stir gently to integrate.
  9. 9
    Serve over diri djon djon, white rice, or rice and beans, with a side of fried sweet plantain or pikliz (Haitian fermented Scotch-bonnet cabbage condiment). Spoon the sauce generously over both the chicken and the rice.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein pivot→as poisson en sos (Haitian fish in sauce)

    Replace the 1200 g chicken with 4 whole red snapper or 4 thick fillets of firm white fish (snapper, grouper, sea bass). Reduce marinade time to 30 minutes (fish absorbs faster and can over-cure). Skip the long braise; sear the fish in step 3 for 3 minutes per side, remove, build the sauce as in steps 4 and 5, simmer the sauce 10 minutes, return the fish to warm through for 3 to 4 minutes. The result is the Haitian seafood-restaurant standard.

  • Occasion protein pivot→as poul fri (Haitian fried chicken, a related preparation)

    Marinate the chicken in epis as in step 2. Skip the braise entirely. Deep-fry the marinated chicken pieces at 175 °C / 350 °F for 12 to 14 minutes until golden and cooked through. Serve with pikliz and rice and beans. The Haitian street-food version, structurally a sibling rather than a sub-recipe.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • Scotch bonnet → habanero 1:1; the Haitian preference is Scotch bonnet for the fruit-floral aroma, habanero is acceptably close.
  • fresh thyme → dried thyme (1 g / ½ tsp) Fresh thyme is structural to Haitian epis; dried works in a pinch but the dish reads slightly less green.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → water or chicken stock make it yourself Real chicken stock deepens the sauce body; the dish reads more layered than with plain water.

Notes

Why epis. Epis is the Haitian equivalent of Cuban sofrito, Puerto Rican recaito, and Brazilian temperos verdes: a green seasoning paste made in advance, kept in the fridge, and used as the savory base for nearly every Haitian savory dish. The blend (parsley, scallion, garlic, thyme, bell pepper, Scotch bonnet, lime) is structural to Haitian cooking and gives every dish a recognizable green-herbaceous bass note that distinguishes Haitian from Cuban or Puerto Rican cooking.

Why scrape the epis off before searing. The wet green paste burns aggressively at sear temperatures (180 to 200 °C); leaving it on the chicken produces black bitter spots and prevents the Maillard crust from forming on the skin. Scraping most of it off and adding it back during the braise step (where the temperature is lower) gets the best of both: a crispy seared chicken plus a green-herbaceous sauce.

The whole Scotch bonnet rule. As in jerk and rice and peas, the Scotch bonnet rides whole on top during the braise. The skin releases fruit-floral capsaicin volatiles into the steam; the intact seed-and-placenta does not rupture. A pierced or chopped chili turns the dish dramatically hotter and pushes Haitian-mild into Jamaican-jerk territory.

Storage. Keeps 4 days refrigerated; improves overnight as the epis integrates into the sauce. Reheat over medium-low heat with a splash of water. Freezes well; portion into 500 g containers.

  • Haitian Sunday-dinner canon — Poul en sos (chicken in sauce) is the Haitian Sunday and weeknight chicken dish, anchored by epis (the green Haitian seasoning paste used as the base for nearly every Haitian savory dish) and finished with a tomato-and-onion braise.
