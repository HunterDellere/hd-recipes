Wedge salad with blue cheese dressing

The American steakhouse wedge: a cold quarter of iceberg lettuce, its dense water-crisp leaves the entire reason the dish works, blanketed in chunky buttermilk blue cheese dressing and built up in layers of crisp bacon, halved cherry tomatoes, more crumbled blue cheese, sliced chives, and a finish of flaky salt, black pepper, and a squeeze of lemon. The dish is an assembly, not a cook; its quality is set almost entirely by the temperature of the lettuce, the rendering of the bacon, and the funk of the cheese.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The dressing is the long-lead component. The chunky blue cheese dressing wants at least 2 hours refrigerated before service so the funk integrates, so make it earlier in the day or the night before (see the make-it-from-scratch link below). If you are buying dressing, this step disappears.
  • Chill the iceberg hard before you cut it. Iceberg is mostly water held in crisp parenchyma cells; cold makes those cells turgid and gives the signature glassy snap. Refrigerate the whole head, or the cut wedges on a tray, until ice-cold. A room-temperature wedge goes limp under the dressing within minutes.
  • Render the bacon first and let it cool and crisp on a rack while you build everything else. Bacon laid on hot from the pan steams the lettuce and wilts the wedge; it needs to be cool and shatter-crisp before it touches the salad.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

The bacon

  1. 115 g thick-cut bacon, about 4 slices

The wedges

  1. 600 g iceberg lettuce, 1 large head, well-chilled, outer leaves removed, cored, cut through the core into 4 wedges — (1 large head; the dense water-crisp leaves are non-negotiable, romaine and little gem will not hold the dressing the same way)

The dressing

  1. 240 g chunky blue cheese dressing

The garnish

  1. 150 g cherry tomatoes, halved (or quartered if large)
  2. 40 g blue cheese, well-chilled, crumbled, for finishing
  3. 8 g fresh chives, thinly sliced
  4. 1 each fresh lemon, cut into wedges, for squeezing at the table — (the finishing acid, applied at the plate)
  5. 2 g flaky salt (Maldon or any large-flake finishing salt)
  6. 1 g freshly cracked black pepper

Execution

  1. 1
    Render the bacon. Lay the 115 g / 4 slices thick-cut bacon in a cold skillet and bring it up over medium heat. Cooking from cold renders the fat slowly and evenly so the bacon crisps without scorching, about 10 to 12 minutes, turning once, until deep brown and rigid. Transfer to a wire rack (not paper towel, which traps steam and softens it) and let it cool fully. Once cold and crisp, chop or crumble into rough 1 cm pieces.
  2. 2
    Cut the wedges. Pull the ice-cold head of iceberg from the fridge. Remove any wilted outer leaves, then cut the 600 g head straight down through the core into 4 equal wedges, leaving the core intact on each so the leaves stay anchored in a wedge rather than falling apart into loose leaves. Pat the cut faces dry with a clean towel; surface water dilutes the dressing and makes it slide off. Set each wedge cut-side up on a chilled plate.
  3. 3
    Sauce the wedges. Spoon the 240 g / 1 cup chunky blue cheese dressing generously over each wedge, about 60 g per wedge, letting it pool into the layered gaps between the leaves and run down the cut faces. The dressing should drape the wedge, not fully coat it; you want bare crisp lettuce in some bites and heavy dressing in others.
  4. 4
    Build the layers. Scatter the bacon crumble, the 150 g / 1 cup halved cherry tomatoes, and the 40 g / ⅓ cup extra crumbled blue cheese over and around the dressed wedges, distributing evenly so every plate gets all three. Layering on top of the dressing rather than tossing keeps each component distinct, which is the structural logic of the wedge.
  5. 5
    Finish and serve immediately. Shower the 8 g / 2 tbsp sliced chives over the top, then crack the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper coarsely over each wedge and pinch the 2 g / ½ tsp flaky salt across the cut surfaces, where the macro-flakes hit the tongue first and read as bright contrast to the long savory dressing. Serve with the lemon wedges alongside; a squeeze of fresh lemon at the table is the finishing acid that cuts the bacon fat and the blue cheese richness in the same stroke. Eat at once, while the lettuce is still cold and snapping.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein, make it a main→add sliced steak or grilled chicken

    Lay 110 g / 4 oz per plate of sliced rested steak (medium-rare flank, skirt, or leftover seared chuck eye) or warm sliced grilled chicken across the dressed wedge before the garnish. The wedge becomes a composed entree salad; keep the lettuce ice-cold so the warm protein contrast lands.

  • Dietary vegetarian→smoked-almond crunch instead of bacon

    Replace the bacon with 40 g / ⅓ cup roughly chopped smoked almonds plus a few drops of smoked paprika oil over the dressing. You lose the rendered-fat richness but keep the smoke-and-crunch register the bacon provided; check that the dressing uses a microbial-rennet blue cheese if strict vegetarian.

  • Texture acid lift→pickled-onion wedge

    Scatter 30 g / ¼ cup quick-pickled red onion (sliced thin, steeped 15 minutes in red wine vinegar with a pinch of sugar and salt) over the garnish. The sharp pickle acid cuts the dressing harder than the lemon alone and adds a magenta visual pop; a common modern steakhouse variation.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • iceberg lettuce → romaine heart or little gem Both are crisp enough to hold up, but neither has iceberg's dense glassy water-crunch; the dish reads more like a Caesar-adjacent leaf salad than a true wedge. If using romaine, halve the hearts lengthwise and treat the cut half like a wedge.
  • thick-cut bacon → pancetta or guanciale, diced and rendered crisp Italian cured pork renders to a cleaner, less smoky crisp. Lovely, but it drops the smokiness that the American wedge leans on; add a pinch of smoked paprika to the dressing to compensate if you want the smoke back.
  • cherry tomatoes → vine tomatoes, diced and drained Use in-season vine or beefsteak tomato diced to 1 cm and drained on a towel for 5 minutes so the wedge does not get watery. Out of tomato season, cherry or grape tomatoes are reliably sweeter than mealy off-season slicers; prefer them.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → chunky blue cheese dressing make it yourself The dressing is the dish. A from-scratch buttermilk-and-real-blue-cheese version, folded in two stages so half melts and half stays chunky, is in a different category from bottled chunky blue cheese, which leans on stabilizers and reads gummy and flat. It is a ten-minute whisk plus a rest.
  • → thick-cut bacon make it yourselfstub Home-cured bacon lets you control the cure salt, the smoke, and the sugar, and it renders cleaner than commercial bacon pumped with water and phosphates. On a salad where the bacon is one of five components, the difference reads clearly.

Notes

The wedge is a temperature dish disguised as a salad. Three of its five components are about thermal contrast: ice-cold iceberg, warm-rendered then cooled crisp bacon, and a cold dressing. Get the lettuce genuinely cold (chill the head, work fast, plate on chilled plates) and the dish has a crispness and refreshment that a room-temperature wedge never reaches. This is the single most-skipped step and the single biggest quality lever.

Iceberg earns its place here, despite its reputation. The dense, tightly-packed leaves hold a high water content in turgid cells, which gives the glassy snap and the cold-water refreshment that the dish is built around. The leaves are also structurally stiff enough to hold a heavy chunky dressing in their layered gaps without collapsing, which delicate leaves cannot do. This is the one classic American salad where iceberg is the correct choice, not a budget compromise.

Why bacon from a cold pan and onto a rack. Starting bacon in a cold skillet renders the subcutaneous fat slowly and evenly, so the meat crisps before it scorches; dropping it into a hot pan sears the outside and leaves flabby unrendered fat. Cooling it on a wire rack rather than paper towel lets steam escape from all sides so it sets shatter-crisp. Bacon laid hot onto the lettuce wilts the wedge instantly; cool it fully first.

Acid is the line that holds the dish together. Between the rich dressing, the fatty bacon, and the salty blue cheese, the wedge is a wall of richness without intervention. The cherry tomatoes contribute one acid vector and the table lemon another; the dressing itself carries lemon and cultured buttermilk. Do not skip the lemon wedges at the plate, the squeeze is what keeps the third bite as appealing as the first.

Build, do not toss. The defining structure of a wedge is that the components stay layered and discrete rather than tossed into uniformity. Each forkful crosses a different ratio of cold lettuce, dressing, bacon, tomato, and cheese, and that variation across bites is the experience. Tossing it homogenizes the contrast and turns it into an ordinary chopped salad.
