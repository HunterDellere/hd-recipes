Bigos (Polish hunter's stew)

Sauerkraut and fresh cabbage cooked low and slow with multiple meats (pork shoulder, kielbasa, smoked bacon), dried porcini, dried plum, juniper, caraway, and bay, then improved by 3 to 5 days of reheating. The Polish national stew; structurally a kraut-and-meat braise that genuinely gets better with each day of rewarming, the kind of dish made on a Sunday and eaten through Thursday.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Bigos is a multi-day project, not because of active cook time (the cook is 3 hours) but because the dish genuinely improves with rewarming over 3 to 5 days. Plan to make a large batch on a Sunday and eat through the week. Day-1 bigos is good; day-3 bigos is canonical.
  • Source two distinct kinds of cabbage: refrigerated raw sauerkraut (not shelf-stable canned) for the lactic-fermented base, and fresh green cabbage for the textural and acid-balancing layer. The 50/50 blend is structural; pure-kraut bigos reads too sharp.
  • Soak the dried porcini and the dried prunes in warm water for 20 minutes before the cook starts; both go into the stew with their soaking liquids strained.
  • Use a heavy 6 to 8 quart Dutch oven; bigos volumes down dramatically over the cook but starts as a very full pot. A 5-quart pot will overflow.

Phase 1, the soaks and aromatics

  1. 25 g dried porcini mushrooms
  2. 300 ml warm water (for soaking the porcini)
  3. 100 g pitted dried prunes (or dried plums), roughly chopped after soaking
  4. 200 ml warm water (for soaking the prunes)

Phase 2, the meats

  1. 600 g pork shoulder (boneless), cut into 3 cm chunks, trimmed of excess external fat, cut into 3 cm chunks
  2. 350 g smoked Polish kielbasa (or any quality smoked pork sausage), sliced into 1.5 cm thick rounds
  3. 150 g smoked bacon (or smoked pork belly), cut into 1 cm pieces
  4. 6 g kosher salt (for seasoning the pork)
  5. 3 g black pepper, freshly ground

Phase 3, the cabbage and aromatics

  1. 600 g sauerkraut (refrigerated raw, not canned), drained from brine, lightly squeezed to remove excess liquid, do not rinse (the tang is structural), chopped roughly to 1 to 2 cm pieces
  2. 600 g fresh green cabbage, cored and shredded, outer leaves removed, cored, shredded into 1 cm ribbons — (half of a medium head; the fresh half balances the kraut acid)
  3. 250 g yellow onion, peeled and finely diced, finely diced — (1 large onion; cooks down into the base)
  4. 1 × 6 oz / 170 g can tomato paste
  5. 8 g sweet paprika (Hungarian sweet preferred)
  6. 4 g caraway seed, lightly crushed
  7. 3 g juniper berries, lightly crushed — (8 to 10 berries; the canonical aromatic for game and kraut preparations across Central Europe. Lightly crush with the side of a knife to release the resin)
  8. 3 each bay leaves — (3 leaves; for the long simmer)
  9. 2 g allspice berries (or ¼ tsp ground allspice) — (6 to 8 berries; the canonical Eastern European warm spice background)
  10. 250 ml dry red wine (or beef stock as substitute)

Execution

  1. 1
    Soak the porcini and the prunes. Place the 25 g / ¾ oz dried porcini in a heatproof bowl with the 300 ml / 1¼ cups warm water; place the 100 g / ½ cup dried prunes in a second bowl with the 200 ml / ¾ cup warm water. Let both soak 20 to 25 minutes. Lift out the porcini, squeeze gently, chop to 4 to 5 mm pieces, and strain the porcini liquid through a coffee filter or paper-towel-lined sieve to remove silt. Lift out the prunes, chop roughly, and reserve their soaking liquid (no need to filter, prunes do not throw silt).
  2. 2
    In a heavy 6 to 8 quart Dutch oven over medium heat, render the 150 g / 5 oz bacon for 8 to 10 minutes, stirring every 2 minutes, until crisp and the fat has rendered out (about 4 to 6 tbsp of bacon fat in the pot). The rendered fat is the cooking medium for everything that follows.
  3. 3
    Push the rendered bacon to one side. Pat the 600 g / 1¼ lb pork shoulder chunks dry, season with the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt and the 3 g / 1 tsp black pepper, and sear in the bacon fat in two batches, 3 minutes per side, until deeply browned on all surfaces. Transfer the seared pork to a plate, leaving the bacon and rendered fat in the pot. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Add the 250 g / 1 large diced yellow onion to the pot and cook 8 to 10 minutes, stirring every 2 minutes, until deep golden brown and soft. The slow caramelization is the structural base of the stew.
  5. 5
    Push the onions to one side. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp tomato paste to the empty side and fry 2 minutes, stirring constantly, until it darkens to brick red. Stir the onions back through with the 8 g / 1 tbsp sweet paprika and cook 30 seconds (paprika scorches fast, watch it).
  6. 6
    Pour in the 250 ml / 1 cup red wine and stir to deglaze, scraping up any browned bits from the bottom of the pot. Simmer 2 minutes until the alcohol smell burns off. Add the strained porcini soaking liquid (about 280 ml after filtering) and the prune soaking liquid (about 180 ml).
  7. 7
    Add the 600 g / 3 cups packed sauerkraut, the 600 g / half-head shredded fresh cabbage, the chopped porcini, the chopped prunes, the 4 g / 1½ tsp caraway, the 3 g / 8 to 10 crushed juniper berries, the 3 bay leaves, the 2 g / 6 to 8 allspice berries, the seared pork, and the 350 g / 12 oz sliced kielbasa. Stir thoroughly to combine; the cabbage will heap up well above the pot rim at this stage, which is correct.
  8. 8
    Bring to a simmer over medium heat, then drop to low and cover. Cook covered for 2 hours, stirring every 30 minutes to redistribute the cabbage and prevent the bottom from sticking. The cabbage volumes down dramatically over the first hour as it wilts and releases its water; what was a heaping pot becomes a half-pot of dark glossy stew. The pork shoulder breaks down into pull-apart tender pieces by the end of the 2 hours.
  9. 9
    Uncover and simmer for another 30 to 45 minutes, stirring every 10 minutes, to reduce any excess liquid and concentrate the stew. The finished bigos should be glossy and thick, with the cabbage soft but still showing distinct ribbons, the meat pull-apart tender, and a visible sheen of fat across the surface. Taste; bigos at this stage should read deeply spiced, faintly sweet from the prune, tangy from the kraut, smoky from the bacon and kielbasa. Adjust salt only at the very end; the smoked meats and the kraut both carry salt.
  10. 10
    Pull from heat. Rest covered for 15 minutes off heat. Discard the bay leaves (the juniper and allspice can stay; diners pick around them or eat them, both work). Serve with crusty rye bread for the day-1 meal. Refrigerate the rest in a covered container for at least 24 hours; the dish improves dramatically over 3 to 5 days of refrigerated rewarming. Reheat individual portions in a small saucepan over low heat for 10 minutes, or the whole pot in a 150 °C oven covered for 30 minutes.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→with venison or wild boar (the genuine hunter's version)

    Replace half the pork shoulder with 300 g cubed venison shoulder or wild boar. Both contribute deeper game flavor that pairs with the juniper and the dried fruit. Extend the covered braise to 2½ hours (venison and boar take longer to tenderize than pork). The original bigos was a true hunter's stew built on game; this version honors that lineage.

  • Occasion occasion→with dried mushroom-bread dumplings (kluski śląskie or knedle)

    Serve over Silesian dumplings (kluski śląskie, the small thumb-shaped potato dumplings with a dimple) or German-style bread dumplings (knedle). The dumplings absorb the rich bigos broth and turn the stew into a more substantial Sunday-dinner format. Boiled potatoes are the alternative starch.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dried prunes → dried apple, dried fig, or dried apricot All carry a similar sweet-tart structural role. Dried apple is the closest by texture; dried fig is the closest by sweetness; dried apricot reads slightly brighter. Quantity 1:1 by weight.
  • smoked Polish kielbasa → any quality smoked pork sausage (German bratwurst smoked, Czech klobasa, Mexican longaniza-style smoked) The smoke is structural; an unsmoked sausage reads recognizably different. Look for a kielbasa-style sausage with visible meat texture rather than a hot-dog-style emulsified sausage.
  • juniper berries → a splash of gin (the modern Western improvisation) Juniper is the structural aromatic of game-and-kraut cookery across Central Europe; the gin substitute carries the same juniper note. Add 2 tbsp gin with the wine in Step 6.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → sauerkraut (refrigerated raw, not canned) make it yourself Homemade kraut is genuinely better and reads more clearly through the long bigos cook; the lactic complexity that commercial kraut lacks integrates over the 3-day rewarm into the deep tangy backbone of the finished dish.

Notes

Why bigos improves over days. The cooked stew is a complex matrix of salt-pulled cabbage cells, slowly hydrolyzing meat collagen, oxidizing fat, integrating spices, and slowly diffusing aromatic compounds between the meat, the kraut, and the broth. Over 24 hours of refrigerated rest, the rough day-1 stew (where the elements still read individually) becomes a fully integrated dish on day 3 where every spoonful carries the full complexity. Many Polish kitchens never serve day-1 bigos; the dish is by tradition cooked on a Saturday for Tuesday eating. The freeze-thaw cycle works too; some cooks freeze portions immediately after cooking and report that thawed bigos reads as if it has rested 5 days.

Why the 50/50 cabbage blend. Pure-sauerkraut bigos reads aggressively sour and one-noted; pure-fresh-cabbage versions read flat and missing the lactic-acid depth. The 50/50 blend (by raw weight) produces the balanced acid-and-sweet cabbage character that defines proper bigos. Some regional versions skew further to one side or the other; the recipe default is the canonical center.

The three-meat structure. Pork shoulder (slow-cook, collagen for body), smoked kielbasa (spice and smoke and visible sausage chunks), smoked bacon (rendered fat as the cooking medium plus a third smoke layer). Each meat does a distinct job; reducing to one or two changes the dish recognizably. Game (venison, boar, hare) was the original meat in the hunter tradition; the pork-and-sausage version is the modern home-cooking standard.

Storage and freezing. Bigos holds 5 to 7 days refrigerated in a covered container; the dish improves the first 3 days, plateaus days 3 to 5, and starts to fade after day 5. Freezes beautifully for 3 months in deli containers; thaw overnight in the refrigerator and reheat covered over low heat. The fat layer that solidifies on top in the refrigerator is structural; do not skim, stir it back in during reheating.

Service. Bigos is traditionally served on its own in a wide shallow bowl with rye or sourdough bread on the side for sopping. Boiled potatoes are the more substantial accompaniment. Sour cream is not the canonical finish (unlike with many other Polish dishes); a small dollop is acceptable but optional.
