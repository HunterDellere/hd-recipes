Pakistani keema aloo (minced beef and potato curry)

Minced beef browned with onion to a deep brown base, bloomed with whole and ground spices, then braised with diced potato in a tomato-and-ginger-garlic gravy until the potatoes are tender and the keema clings to them. The weeknight workhorse of Pakistani home cooking: 45 minutes start-to-finish, deeply spiced, equally good with naan, roti, or basmati rice.

  2. 500 g starchy potatoes (russet or Yukon Gold), peeled and cut into 2 cm cubes — (2 medium; cubes hold their shape during the braise)
  3. 400 ml water

Phase 6, garnish

Execution

  1. 1
    Bloom the whole spices. Heat the 45 g ghee in a heavy 4 to 5 quart Dutch oven or wide pot over medium heat until shimmering. Add the 5 g / 2 tsp cumin seeds, 3 cracked cardamom pods, 3 g cinnamon stick, and 1 bay leaf. Bloom 30 to 45 seconds, until the cumin sizzles and darkens slightly and the kitchen fills with the toasted-spice aroma. Blooming aromatics
  2. 2
    Brown the onion. Add the 250 g diced onion. Stir-fry over medium heat for 10 to 12 minutes, stirring every 30 to 60 seconds, until the onion has gone from white to gold to deep mahogany-brown. The onion is the structural color and depth of the dish; pale onions produce a pale-and-flat keema. Maillard sear
  3. 3
    Add the 15 g grated ginger, 20 g minced garlic, and 10 g slit green chilies. Stir-fry 60 to 90 seconds; the rawness cooks off and the smell goes from sharp to warm.
  4. 4
    Brown the keema. Push the onion base to the sides; add the 700 g ground beef into the cleared center. Break it up with a wooden spoon and cook 6 to 8 minutes over medium-high heat, stirring frequently, until the beef has released its juices, the juices have evaporated, and the beef is starting to brown at the edges. Do not skip the browning step; the Maillard reaction on the beef is structural to the depth of the dish, and unbrowned keema reads as boiled meat in gravy.
  5. 5
    Add the ground spices. Reduce heat to medium-low. Add the 8 g / 1 tbsp ground coriander, 5 g / 2 tsp Kashmiri chili powder, 2 g / ½ tsp turmeric, half (2 g / 1 tsp) of the garam masala, and the 12 g / 1 tbsp salt. Stir-fry 60 to 90 seconds, splashing with 2 tbsp water if the spices threaten to scorch. The ghee should pool slightly red-orange at the edges (the visual marker of the bhuna). Red oil rendering
  6. 6
    Add the 300 g diced tomato. Cook 5 to 6 minutes over medium heat, stirring occasionally, until the tomato has fully broken down and the mixture looks pasty with visible ghee pooling around the edges.
  7. 7
    Braise. Add the 500 g cubed potatoes and the 400 ml / 1⅔ cups water. Stir to integrate. Bring to a simmer, cover, and cook 18 to 22 minutes over medium-low heat, stirring every 6 minutes to prevent the bottom from scorching, until the potatoes are tender to a fork-poke and the gravy has reduced to a thick clinging consistency that just coats the keema and potatoes.
  8. 8
    Finish. Uncover; if the gravy is still too loose, simmer uncovered another 3 to 5 minutes to reduce. Stir in the remaining 2 g / 1 tsp garam masala. Taste; adjust salt. The dish should be deeply spiced, slightly sweet from the browned onion, with the potatoes fully tender and the keema clinging.
  9. 9
    Plate and garnish. Transfer to a serving dish. Scatter the 20 g chopped cilantro and 10 g julienned ginger across the top. Serve with naan, roti, or basmati rice and the lemon wedges on the side. Each diner squeezes lemon over their portion; the acid lifts the dish.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→keema with lamb or mutton

    Substitute 700 g ground lamb for the beef. Lamb reads richer and slightly more aggressive; the spice levels stay the same. The dish becomes mutton keema, equally canonical in Pakistani cooking.

  • Occasion vegetable→keema matar (with peas) instead of aloo

    Replace the 500 g potato with 300 g frozen green peas added in the last 5 minutes of the braise. Reduce the braise water to 250 ml (peas do not need as much liquid). The dish is the canonical keema matar; a Pakistani winter favorite.

  • Occasion occasion→as keema paratha filling

    Cook the keema until the gravy has fully evaporated and the mixture is dry-ish (an extra 10 minutes uncovered). Cool completely. Use as the filling for stuffed parathas, following the aloo paratha rolling technique. The dish becomes keema paratha, a Pakistani Eid breakfast tradition.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • ground beef (80% lean) → ground lamb (700 g) See modifications. Lamb is the structural alternative; the dish reads richer and is equally canonical.
  • ghee → unsalted butter + a small amount of neutral oil (50/50) See ingredients/ghee. Acceptable but the dish loses some of the structural toasted-milk-solid aromatic that defines Pakistani cooking. The neutral oil keeps the smoke point manageable for the long brown-onion stage.

Notes

Why the onion goes deep brown. Pakistani gravies are structurally onion-based; the brown-onion stage is what gives the dish its color, depth, and inherent sweetness. Pale onions (cooked only to translucent) produce a watery, flat gravy; deep-brown onions (cooked to mahogany) produce the thick, glossy, deeply savory base that defines authentic Pakistani home cooking. The 10 to 12 minute brown is non-negotiable. Do not rush.

The bhuna stage and red-oil rendering. After the spices are added and the tomato has reduced, the canonical Pakistani visual marker is the ghee pooling separately from the masala paste at the edges of the pan, with a distinct red-orange color from the Kashmiri chili and turmeric blooming into the fat. This separation (called bhuna in Urdu) is the structural signal that the spices have properly cooked and integrated; if the ghee is still mixed homogeneously with the masala, the bhuna is incomplete and the dish will taste raw-spiced. Patience here pays off.

Two-stage garam masala. Adding half the garam masala during the bhuna and half at the end is the structural Pakistani technique: the first half integrates into the gravy and provides body, the second half provides the bright aromatic top-note that defines fresh-cooked Pakistani food. Pre-ground garam masala loses its volatile aromatics within months; the second-half addition compensates by providing fresh aroma at the moment of service.

The brown-color heat. Kashmiri chili powder is structurally a colorant first and a heat second; the chili variety produces a deep red color with only moderate heat. The 5 g in this recipe is what gives the dish its rich red-orange color without making it aggressively spicy. For more heat, add 1 g of cayenne or hotter Indian chili powder; do not increase the Kashmiri chili (it changes the color but adds little heat).

Storage. Refrigerated in a sealed container, 4 days; the dish improves significantly overnight as the spices integrate. Freezes well for 3 months; thaw overnight in the refrigerator and reheat gently. The potatoes can go slightly mushy on freezing; this is acceptable. Reheat with a splash of water if the gravy has thickened too much.
