Miso soup (miso-shiru)

Niban dashi (or fresh ichiban for foreground applications) brought to 80°C, miso whisked in off-heat through a small sieve to break up clumps, finished with rehydrated wakame, cubed silken tofu, and sliced scallion. The miso enters at the end and never sees a boil , that single rule separates good miso soup from flat miso soup.

Phase 2, finishing solids

  1. 200 g silken tofu
  2. 4 g dried wakame

Phase 3, miso

Phase 4, finish

  1. 15 g scallion, thinly sliced — (2 scallions, green parts only)

Execution

  1. 1
    Hydrate the wakame. Place the 4 g dried wakame in a small bowl and cover with cold water. Let stand 5 minutes; the flakes will swell to roughly 7 to 10 times their dried volume and turn vivid emerald. Drain through a small sieve and squeeze gently; do not press hard. Set aside.
  2. 2
    Heat the dashi. In a 2-quart saucepan, bring the 800 ml dashi to 80°C / 175°F (small bubbles forming at the edges, no rolling boil). If you are using freshly-strained ichiban dashi, it should already be near this temperature; if niban or refrigerated dashi, warm gradually over medium-low heat. Do not boil the dashi vigorously; the kombu and katsuobushi flavors are heat-sensitive even after extraction. Dashi (kombu and katsuobushi extraction)
  3. 3
    Add the tofu. Slide the 200 g cubed silken tofu gently into the dashi. The cubes should be just submerged; bring the pot back up to 80°C and hold for 1 minute to warm the tofu through. Do not boil , boiling fractures the silken curd into ragged shreds. The cubes will look slightly translucent at the edges when warmed.
  4. 4
    Add the wakame. Scatter the 4 g rehydrated wakame across the surface of the dashi. Hold for 30 seconds to warm through; do not stir aggressively, the wakame is delicate and excess agitation tears the leaves.
  5. 5
    Whisk in the miso off-heat. Pull the saucepan off the burner. Place a small fine-mesh sieve into the dashi and add the 60 g miso into the sieve. Press the miso through the sieve with the back of a small spoon or whisk while moving the sieve in slow circles through the dashi; the sieve breaks up clumps and ensures the miso dissolves evenly without grainy chunks. Alternatively, ladle 60 ml of hot dashi into a small bowl, whisk the miso into the bowl until smooth, then pour back into the pot. The miso should fully dissolve in 30 seconds; the dashi will turn from clear to cloudy-tan-brown. Do not return to a boil under any circumstances. Boiling drives off the volatile esters that miso adds and dulls the umami; this is the single rule that separates a working miso soup from a flat one.
  6. 6
    Serve immediately. Ladle into 4 small bowls (200 ml each). Sprinkle the sliced scallion greens on top of each bowl as a finishing aromatic. Serve as part of a washoku meal , the standard accompaniment is a bowl of plain Japanese rice and one or two banchan or okazu (a piece of grilled fish, a small salad, a quick-pickled cucumber). The miso soup goes from the kitchen to the table within 2 minutes; reheating it to a boil ruins the flavor, and a tepid bowl of miso soup is one of the saddest things in a Japanese kitchen.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion season→autumn (root vegetable miso soup)

    Add 80 g daikon (peeled and sliced into 3 mm half-moons) and 60 g satoimo (taro) or sweet potato (peeled and 1 cm dice) during phase 2, before the tofu. Simmer 8 to 10 minutes at 90°C until tender. The root vegetables ground the soup into a heartier autumn dish. Use red or awase miso instead of white , the deeper miso matches the heartier base.

  • Protein protein→pork miso soup (tonjiru)

    Brown 200 g sliced thin pork belly in a dry pot with 1 tsp toasted sesame oil before adding the dashi. Add 80 g daikon, 60 g carrot, 40 g gobō (burdock root, scraped and shaved with a knife), and 80 g shimeji or maitake mushrooms during phase 2. Simmer 12 to 15 minutes; finish with red miso (90 g, scaled up to match the richer base). This is the substantial winter version; eats as a meal rather than a side.

  • Protein season→spring (clam miso soup)

    Replace the tofu with 200 g asari clams (purged in salt water for 30 minutes). Add the clams to the dashi during phase 2, simmer until shells open (3 to 4 minutes), discard any unopened. The clams add a second umami nucleotide layer (succinic acid) on top of the kombu-katsuobushi base. Use white miso, slightly reduced to 50 g, to keep the clam flavor in the foreground.

  • Occasion occasion→instant miso soup (single-serving)

    For a single bowl on a busy morning: place 1 tbsp / 15 g miso in a teacup. Add 1 tsp dried wakame, 1 tbsp small-cubed silken tofu (or none), 1 tsp dried hanakatsuo, and 1 sliced scallion. Pour 200 ml just-off-the-boil water over (allow 10 seconds for the water to cool below boiling). Stir to dissolve the miso. The hanakatsuo serves as instant dashi, the wakame rehydrates in seconds. Faster than instant miso packets and substantially better; takes 2 minutes total.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • kombu-katsuobushi dashi → shiitake-kombu vegetarian dashi (kombu cold-soaked overnight, dried shiitake added during the kombu soak, brought to 60°C for 5 minutes, strained) For vegetarian or vegan miso soup. The guanylate-glutamate synergy is roughly equivalent to inosinate-glutamate in measured umami intensity. The character is mushroomier, less marine.
  • kombu-katsuobushi dashi → hondashi (instant dashi powder), 1 tsp per 800 ml hot water For weeknight speed. Workable but flatter; the hondashi-and-miso combination produces a saltier, less complex bowl than fresh dashi. Acceptable for everyday cooking; not for showcase miso soup.
  • awase miso → white miso (shiro) for milder, red miso (aka) for stronger See ingredients/white-miso and ingredients/red-miso. Awase is the middle-ground default; either single-style miso works depending on whether you want the soup more delicate or more assertive.
  • silken tofu → soft (medium-soft) tofu Slightly more substantial cube structure; holds the heat better. Some Japanese home cooks prefer it for tonjiru and heartier versions. Wrong for delicate ichiban-dashi miso soup where silken is the textural goal.

Notes

The order of operations matters. Tofu before wakame before miso, with miso always last and always off-heat. The sequence is not arbitrary: tofu needs 1 minute at 80°C to warm through and absorb dashi seasoning; wakame needs 30 seconds, no more, to warm without going slimy; miso needs zero seconds at heat , it goes in off-burner and gets dissolved with no further cooking. Front-loading the miso (whisking it in at the start, then adding the solids) is a common mistake that produces a flat, slightly bitter, dulled-aroma soup because the miso has been cooked through and the volatile esters and amino acid aromatics that define its character have been driven off.

Why a sieve. Miso is a paste that does not dissolve readily in water unless broken up. The fine-mesh sieve serves two functions: (1) it traps any insoluble husk fragments or unpasteurized koji clumps that some artisanal misos contain, producing a cleaner-looking soup; (2) it provides a mechanical break-up surface that disperses the paste evenly through the dashi without aggressive whisking. Whisking miso into hot dashi in a free pot tends to leave grainy chunks suspended; the sieve protocol is the textbook home solution.

Salt and miso brand variation. Miso varies substantially in salt by brand and style: light-fermented shiro misos sit at roughly 5 to 6% salt by weight, while aged akamiso goes to 10 to 14%. A 60 g dose of a 5% white miso contributes 3 g salt to 800 ml of soup; the same dose of 12% red miso contributes 7.2 g , more than double. The 60 g recommendation in this recipe is calibrated for awase miso (~8 to 10% salt). Adjust by 10 to 20 g either direction based on your specific brand and your salt tolerance; taste the soup before serving and add small increments of miso through the sieve if it tastes flat.

The 200 ml serving size. The Japanese home miso-soup bowl is small , 150 to 220 ml , and the soup is one of several small dishes in a washoku meal, not a main course. Western recipes often serve miso soup in 350 to 400 ml bowls, which dilutes the seasoning and shifts the dish away from its design. If you want a larger bowl, scale up the recipe; do not stretch a 4-bowl recipe across 2 oversized bowls.

Storage and reheating. Made-and-served-fresh is the only acceptable mode for serious miso soup. The miso should not be reheated after the first off-heat dissolve; reheating drives off the aromatics that justify the off-heat protocol in the first place. For a meal-prep pattern, store the dashi (with cooked solids) in the refrigerator for up to 2 days and add miso fresh each time you reheat , warm the dashi to 80°C, pull off heat, whisk the miso in, serve. Never store miso-soup-with-miso-already-added overnight; the next day's reheated bowl tastes like a miso soup that has had the soul cooked out of it, because it has.

The miso soup test. A common Japanese home-cook quality test: ladle a spoonful of finished miso soup, taste it. The first sensation should be the dashi , clean, faintly fishy-marine, gently sweet. The second sensation should be the miso , savory, slightly funky, salt-tinged. The third sensation is the aromatics from the scallion and wakame. If the miso hits first and dominates, the soup has too much miso or too thin a dashi base; reduce miso by 10 g and rebuild. If the dashi tastes weak and the miso supplies all the flavor, the dashi was underextracted; remake the dashi with more kombu and katsuobushi.
