Coq au vin

The Burgundian chicken-in-red-wine braise: bone-in chicken thighs and drumsticks marinated in red wine and aromatics overnight, then browned hard, the marinade reduced and added back, slow-braised 50 to 60 minutes until the meat falls from the bone, and finished with sautéed lardons, pearl onions, and cremini mushrooms tucked in at service. Structurally the chicken sibling of beef bourguignon: same Burgundian wine, same garnish, same lardon-based fat structure; different protein, different timing. The overnight marinade is the structural difference from bourguignon (chicken does not need to braise as long, so the wine flavor has to penetrate before the cook).

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The overnight wine marinade is structural and not optional. Chicken cooks much faster than beef chuck (50 to 60 minutes vs 3 hours), so the wine flavor must penetrate the meat before the cook. A same-day "quick coq au vin" with no marinade produces meat that is still slightly bland in the center and reads less like Burgundian chicken than like generic chicken in red wine sauce. Plan for: day 1 evening = season chicken, marinate in wine; day 2 = cook and serve.
  • Open the bottle of red Burgundy or pinot noir 1 hour before the cook begins; the wine benefits from aeration.
  • Brown the chicken in two batches with the skin-side down first; the rendered chicken fat is structural for the aromatic base. Crowding the pan turns the sear into a steam; the Maillard crust on the skin is the structural source of the savory depth.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the overnight wine marinade

  1. 1800 g bone-in skin-on chicken thighs and drumsticks (mixed), patted dry; do not remove skin (the rendered skin fat is structural)
  2. 16 g kosher salt (for the chicken)
  3. 750 ml red Burgundy or pinot noir (full bottle, dry, medium-body)
  4. 200 g yellow onion, sliced (for the marinade), thickly sliced (will be reserved for the cook) — (1 medium onion)
  5. 20 g garlic, smashed (for the marinade), smashed with the side of a knife, papery skin removed — (5 to 6 cloves)
  6. 1 each bouquet garni for the marinade (thyme, bay, parsley stems), 6 sprigs fresh thyme + 2 bay leaves + 10 parsley stems tied with kitchen twine — (1 bundle; will be transferred from the marinade to the cooking pot)

Phase 2, the cook

  1. 150 g slab bacon or pancetta, in 1 cm cubes, cut into 1 cm cubes (lardons)
  2. 30 g all-purpose flour
  3. 1 × 6 oz / 170 g can tomato paste
  4. 350 ml chicken stock (homemade or low-sodium)
  5. 2 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Phase 3, the garnish (sautéed separately, added at the end)

  1. 250 g pearl onions (frozen peeled)
  2. 350 g cremini mushrooms (or button), quartered, cleaned, halved or quartered depending on size
  3. 30 g unsalted butter (for the garnish sauté)
  4. 5 g granulated sugar (for the pearl onion glaze)

Phase 4, the finish

  1. 10 g flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped, finely chopped just before serving — (a small handful)

Execution

  1. 1
    Day 1, evening (the marinade): season the 1800 g / 4 lb chicken pieces with the 16 g / 1 tbsp salt and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper, rubbing into all surfaces. Transfer to a large bowl or zip-top bag. Add the 750 ml / 1 bottle red Burgundy, the 200 g / 1 onion (sliced), 20 g / 5 to 6 cloves smashed garlic, and the bouquet garni. Cover or seal; refrigerate 12 to 24 hours. The wine penetrates the meat and stains it deeply purple (this is normal and the color cooks out to mahogany brown).12 h
  2. 2
    Day 2 (the cook): remove the chicken from the marinade; pat each piece thoroughly dry with paper towels (any surface water prevents the sear, and the wine-soaked surface is wetter than usual). Strain the marinade through a fine-mesh sieve into a saucepan, reserving the solids (onion, garlic, bouquet garni) separately. Bring the strained wine to a hard boil in the saucepan and skim off the foamy grey protein scum that rises in the first 2 minutes (this is dissolved myoglobin from the chicken; skimming clarifies the sauce). Reduce the wine by about a third (8 to 10 minutes). Reserve.
  3. 3
    Preheat oven to 160°C / 325°F. Set a 6 to 7 L heavy-bottomed Dutch oven over medium heat. Add the 150 g / 5 oz cubed bacon to the cold pot; render slowly 8 to 10 minutes until the lardons are deeply golden and crispy. Remove with a slotted spoon to a paper-towel-lined plate; reserve. Pour off all but about 30 g of the rendered fat (reserve the rest for the garnish sauté). Lardon rendering
  4. 4
    Lightly dredge the dried chicken pieces in the 30 g / 3½ tbsp flour (shake off excess). Increase heat to medium-high. Brown the chicken in 2 batches, skin-side down first, 5 to 6 minutes per side until the skin is deeply golden brown. Crowding the pan turns the sear into a steam; one piece should never touch another. Transfer browned pieces to a plate. The fond at the bottom of the pot should be dark brown but not black. Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Reduce heat to medium. Add the reserved marinade-strained onion and garlic to the Dutch oven; sauté 5 to 6 minutes until the onion has softened and the bottom of the pot has begun to brown again. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp tomato paste; cook 2 minutes until the paste has darkened from bright red to brick red. Blooming aromatics
  6. 6
    Pour in the reduced wine marinade (from step 2), scraping the fond loose with a wooden spoon. Add the 350 ml / 1½ cups chicken stock. Bring to a hard simmer; reduce by about a third more (about 6 to 8 minutes). The liquid should now be deeply red-brown and beginning to thicken. Deglazing
  7. 7
    Add the browned chicken pieces (and any accumulated juices) back to the pot, skin-side up. Add the reserved bouquet garni from the marinade. The liquid should come about two-thirds up the side of the chicken pieces (not fully submerged; the exposed skin will stay slightly crisp during the oven braise). Bring to a tremor on the stovetop.
  8. 8
    Cover with the lid slightly ajar (10 percent evaporation) and transfer to the preheated 160°C / 325°F oven. Braise 50 to 60 minutes, checking every 20 minutes to confirm the temperature is a tremor (not a boil). The chicken is done when a fork inserted into a thigh meets no resistance and a thigh joint pulled gently with tongs separates easily; if there is still resistance at 60 minutes, give it another 10 to 15 minutes.
  9. 9
    While the chicken braises, sauté the garnish. In a separate wide skillet over medium-high heat, add 15 g / 1 tbsp of the reserved bacon fat plus 15 g / 1 tbsp of butter. Add the 250 g / 8 oz pearl onions; sauté 5 to 6 minutes until lightly browned. Add the 5 g / 1 tsp sugar and 30 ml / 2 tbsp water; reduce heat to medium-low. Cook 3 to 4 minutes more until the onions are glossy with a light brown glaze. Transfer to a bowl. In the same skillet, add the remaining 15 g / 1 tbsp butter and the 350 g / 12 oz quartered cremini mushrooms; cook 6 to 8 minutes (let them release their water and start to brown before stirring). Season with a pinch of salt. Maillard sear
  10. 10
    When the chicken is done, remove from the oven. Fish out the bouquet garni and discard. Transfer the chicken pieces to a warm platter; cover loosely with foil. Set the Dutch oven over medium-high heat on the stovetop; reduce the sauce 5 to 8 minutes until it has thickened to a glossy consistency that coats the back of a spoon (the sauce should pool around the chicken at service, not run thin). Taste; adjust salt (the long reduction concentrates the sauce and it often needs a final pinch of salt).
  11. 11
    Return the chicken to the Dutch oven. Add the sautéed pearl onions, sautéed mushrooms, and reserved crispy lardons. Stir gently to coat and warm through, 2 to 3 minutes. Top with the 10 g chopped parsley just before serving. Serve in shallow bowls over buttered egg noodles, mashed potatoes, or with crusty bread; the sauce should pool generously around the meat.
Safety
  • Bone-in dark-meat chicken braised to fully tender (internal 85 to 90°C / 185 to 195°F) rather than the USDA published-safe 165°F target for poultry Coq au vin is a braise; the chicken thighs and drumsticks cook well past the USDA published-safe instantaneous target of 165°F internal. The canonical braise endpoint is 85 to 90°C / 185 to 195°F, the temperature at which the collagen in the dark meat has fully converted to gelatin and the meat falls from the bone. The long simmer above 75°C provides generous pasteurization equivalency. The food-safety attention in a braise is on rapid cooling of leftovers (within 2 hours of cooking) and reheating to 75°C / 165°F throughout before serving, not on the internal temperature at first service. Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as coq au vin jaune (the Jura yellow-wine variant)

    Replace the red Burgundy with vin jaune from the Jura (the oxidatively-aged sherry-like white wine; 750 ml). Replace the cremini mushrooms with 350 g of yellow chanterelles or morels. Add 200 g of crème fraîche to the final reduced sauce off-heat. The result is structurally coq au vin jaune, the Jura regional variant that reads dramatically different from the Burgundian original: nutty, sherry-aromatic, white and creamy rather than dark and red.

  • Occasion protein pivot→as a same-day coq au vin (the no-marinade home shortcut)

    Skip the overnight marinade entirely. Salt the chicken and rest at room temperature 30 minutes. Proceed with the cook as written, adding the wine directly to the pot after the tomato paste in step 5 (instead of the reduced marinade). Increase the chicken stock to 500 ml to make up for the unreduced wine volume; reduce the cooking time slightly. The result is a faster home-friendly version that reads slightly less wine-integrated than the canonical overnight-marinade version; acceptable for weeknight cooking.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • bone-in skin-on chicken thighs and drumsticks (mixed) → a whole chicken cut into 8 pieces, with the breasts added later Mixed thighs and drumsticks is the canonical choice for the high-collagen dark meat. A whole chicken cut into 8 pieces (2 wings, 2 breasts, 2 thighs, 2 drumsticks) works if the breasts are added in the last 20 minutes of the braise only (the breasts overcook and dry out in a 60-minute braise). Do not use boneless skinless chicken breast at all; the dish reads dry and chalky and the rendered skin fat is missing.
  • red Burgundy or pinot noir (full bottle, dry, medium-body) → a medium-bodied non-Burgundian red (Côtes du Rhône, Beaujolais cru, Spanish Garnacha) Same selection rules as for beef bourguignon. Avoid heavy oaky cabernet sauvignon, syrah, or zinfandel.

Notes

The overnight wine marinade is the structural difference between coq au vin and a generic chicken-in-red-wine stew. Chicken cooks fast (50 to 60 minutes for bone-in dark meat in a 325°F oven), and the wine flavor cannot fully penetrate the meat during such a short braise. The 12 to 24 hour cold marinade does the penetration work the cook cannot: the wine flavors infuse into the meat overnight, and the resulting cooked chicken reads as wine-saturated all the way through. The marinade also dyes the chicken purple (this is normal and the color cooks to a deep mahogany brown).

The skim-the-marinade step is a small detail that produces a dramatically cleaner sauce. The wine has been in contact with the raw chicken for 12 to 24 hours and has dissolved a small amount of myoglobin (the iron-protein from chicken muscle); when the marinade is brought to a boil, this myoglobin coagulates and rises as a foamy grey scum. Skimming this off produces a clearer, cleaner-tasting sauce; skipping the skim produces a slightly muddy sauce with a faint metallic edge.

The pat-dry step is structural and often skipped. After pulling the chicken from the wine marinade, the surface is wetter than raw chicken usually is (the wine has soaked into the skin). Patting thoroughly dry with paper towels is essential for the Maillard sear; wet chicken skin does not brown, it steams. The dry surface develops the dark gold crust that gives the sauce its color.

The skin-side-down first rule is structural. The chicken thighs and drumsticks have a layer of fat under the skin; starting skin-side down renders this fat into the pot (providing additional aromatic depth) and crisps the skin to a golden brown that survives the braise. Starting flesh-side down forfeits the rendered fat and produces flabby skin in the finished dish.

Storage. Keeps 3 to 4 days refrigerated; reheats well on the stovetop over medium-low heat to a tremor. The dish improves overnight as the sauce and meat integrate further; many French home cooks make coq au vin the day before serving and reheat at service. Freezes well; portion into 500 g containers, freeze flat, keeps 3 months at -18°C / 0°F. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator, warm gently. Do not microwave; the sauce separates.

Sources
  • Burgundian canon (codified through Escoffier and Julia Child) — Coq au vin is structurally the chicken counterpart of beef bourguignon, rooted in the same Burgundian peasant tradition of slow-braising tough cuts in local red wine. Originally made with a tough old rooster (coq), which required overnight marination plus 3+ hours of braising; the modern adaptation uses bone-in chicken thighs and drumsticks (the dark meat with the highest collagen content), which need only an overnight marinade plus 50 to 60 minutes of braising. The defining elements: bone-in dark meat (boneless skinless chicken breast is structurally wrong, the dish reads dry and chalky), overnight red wine marinade with aromatics (the structural difference from bourguignon, where the long braise extracts wine flavor into the beef during the cook), red Burgundy or pinot noir, the same lardon-pearl-onion-mushroom garnish tradition as bourguignon, and a final sauce reduction to coat the chicken in a glossy wine glaze. Julia Child's 1961 version is the canonical English-language reference.
