Recipe

Chicken-zucchini meatballs with yogurt-dill

Ground chicken thigh mixed with salt-drained grated zucchini, grated onion, garlic, lemon zest, fresh dill, and a small panade of breadcrumbs hydrated in milk. The zucchini contributes moisture that keeps the lean chicken from drying out during the bake, and the panade traps that moisture in a starch matrix that releases gradually as the meatballs cook. Baked at 220 °C on parchment-lined sheet pans (no frying, no nonstick), served with a cold lemon-dill yogurt sauce that doubles as both dressing and dip. Mediterranean leaning, weeknight feasible, the meatballs hold beautifully for next-day lunches.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The zucchini must be salt-drained 20 minutes before mixing. Grate it, salt it (1% by weight), let it sit in a sieve, then wring it out in a clean kitchen towel. Skipping this step means watery meatballs that fall apart and steam in their own released moisture rather than browning. Plan for the 20-minute drain to overlap with the rest of the mise.
  • Set the oven to 220 °C with the rack in the upper third, and line two sheet pans with parchment paper before the meatballs are shaped. Sticky raw ground chicken on a bare metal pan tears when transferred; parchment prevents that and is the right tool over silicone mats (which trap moisture under the meatballs and inhibit browning).
  • The yogurt-dill sauce benefits from a 30-minute rest in the refrigerator before serving, the raw garlic mellows and the flavors integrate. Make it first so it can rest while the meatballs bake.
  • Pull the chicken from the refrigerator 15 minutes before mixing. Cold ground meat seizes when handled and the panade does not distribute evenly through the mix.

Execution

  1. 1
    Grate the 300 g zucchini on the large holes of a box grater into a sieve set over a bowl. Toss with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt (zucchini salt only). Let drain 20 minutes, occasionally pressing down with a flexible spatula to encourage the water release. The zucchini should release 80 to 120 g of green liquid during this rest.
  2. 2
    While the zucchini drains, make the yogurt-dill sauce. In a small bowl, whisk together the 250 g / 1 cup Greek yogurt, the 8 g / 3 tbsp chopped dill, the 15 g / 1 tbsp lemon juice, the 3 g / 1 clove microplaned garlic, the 10 g / 2 tsp olive oil, and the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt. Cover and refrigerate while the meatballs cook; the rest lets the garlic mellow.
  3. 3
    Heat the oven to 220 °C / 425 °F with the rack in the upper third. Line two sheet pans with parchment paper. The upper rack position puts the meatballs closer to the top heating element, which encourages surface browning without burning the bottoms.
  4. 4
    Make the panade. In a small bowl, combine the 35 g / ½ cup panko with the 60 g / ¼ cup whole milk. Stir and let sit 5 minutes; the panko absorbs all the milk and forms a wet paste. This is the moisture-trap that keeps the chicken juicy.
  5. 5
    Wring out the zucchini. Transfer the drained zucchini to the center of a clean kitchen towel, gather the corners, and twist hard over the sink to expel as much water as possible. You should produce another 40 to 60 g of liquid. Unfurl the towel; the zucchini should be a dense compact pile of bright-green shreds.
  6. 6
    In a large wide bowl, combine the 500 g ground chicken thigh, the wrung-out zucchini (about 180 g), the 60 g grated yellow onion, the 12 g / 3 cloves microplaned garlic, the 12 g chopped dill, the 10 g chopped parsley, the 3 g microplaned lemon zest, the 50 g / 1 lightly beaten egg, the milk-soaked panade, the 3 g / 1½ tsp ground cumin, the 2 g / 1 tsp ground coriander, the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt, and the 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper. Mix with damp hands or a sturdy wooden spoon for 60 to 90 seconds until fully incorporated and the mixture turns from loose-and-shaggy to slightly cohesive. Do not overwork; the mixture should still feel pliable, not pasted.
  7. 7
    Shape the meatballs. With damp hands, portion the mixture into 20 meatballs of about 40 g each (a slightly heaped tablespoon scoop produces the right size). Roll each between damp palms with light pressure, do not compress, and arrange on the parchment-lined pans with 3 cm of space between meatballs. Brush each meatball lightly with the 20 g / 1½ tbsp olive oil; the oil encourages surface browning during the bake.
  8. 8
    Bake the meatballs at 220 °C / 425 °F for 16 to 18 minutes, rotating the pans halfway through (top to bottom rack and front to back). The meatballs are done when they read 74 °C / 165 °F at the center on a probe thermometer and the surface is golden-brown with darker caramelized spots. Do not overcook; chicken at 80 °C / 175 °F is meaningfully drier.
  9. 9
    Rest the meatballs on the pan for 3 minutes; the carryover cooking finishes the interior and the surface juices reabsorb. Transfer to a wide platter, drizzle with a small spoonful of the yogurt-dill sauce, and scatter a final pinch of chopped fresh dill across the top. Serve with the remaining yogurt-dill sauce on the side for dipping, alongside warm pita, rice, or a chopped salad of cucumber, tomato, and red onion.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→with ground turkey, ground lamb, or a beef-pork blend

    Ground turkey thigh substitutes 1:1 and reads similarly. Ground lamb makes a more aggressively flavored Middle Eastern leaning meatball; add 2 g / 1 tsp dried mint to the mix and skip the cumin. A 70/30 beef-pork blend pushes the meatballs toward Italian polpette territory; swap the dill for basil and the cumin-coriander for dried oregano.

  • Occasion service form→meatball pita wrap

    After baking, stuff 3 to 4 meatballs into a warm pita with a generous schmear of the yogurt-dill, plus 30 g shredded romaine, 30 g cucumber rounds, 20 g pickled red onion, and 10 g crumbled feta. Roll tight and eat handheld. The format moves from dinner-plate to handheld lunch.

  • Occasion season→autumn version with grated butternut

    Replace 200 g of the grated zucchini with 200 g grated butternut squash (uncooked, peeled, grated on the large holes, no salt-drain needed because butternut is denser and lower in water). The meatballs read slightly sweeter and the color shifts to gold-flecked. Bake 2 minutes longer.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • ground chicken thigh → ground chicken breast, ground turkey thigh, or ground turkey breast Chicken thigh is the best choice for the highest fat content and resulting juiciness. Breast (chicken or turkey) substitutes 1:1 but the meatballs read significantly drier; compensate by adding an extra 30 g milk to the panade and 10 g olive oil to the mix.
  • panko breadcrumbs → fresh white breadcrumbs, dried breadcrumbs, or instant mashed potato flakes Fresh breadcrumbs torn from sourdough or country bread substitute well and reads slightly more rustic. Dried breadcrumbs absorb more milk per gram; use 25 g panade-bread instead of 35 g panko. Instant mashed potato flakes are the gluten-free option and work surprisingly well.
  • whole milk → buttermilk, kefir, or unsweetened plant milk Buttermilk and kefir add a slight tang that complements the dill. Plant milks work but the dairy-free meatball reads slightly less rich; oat milk is the closest match to whole milk in body.

Notes

Why salt-drain the zucchini. Untreated grated zucchini is roughly 95% water by weight. Mixed straight into ground chicken without draining, that water releases during the bake, turning the meatballs into chicken-and-vegetable patties swimming in green liquid that can never brown. Salting the grated zucchini with 1% salt for 20 minutes draws out 30 to 40% of that water through osmosis (the salt creates a hypertonic environment that pulls water from the cells), and wringing in a towel removes another 15 to 20%. What remains is concentrated zucchini flavor, hydrated protein structures, and just enough moisture to keep the chicken juicy without flooding it.

Why panade matters more than egg. Most home cooks add an egg to meatballs and call the binding done. The egg is a structural protein that holds the meatball together, yes, but the panade (milk-soaked starch) does the more important job: it traps moisture in a gel matrix that releases gradually during cooking. As the meatball heats, the proteins contract and squeeze liquid outward; the panade absorbs that liquid and holds it inside the meatball rather than letting it pool on the pan. Skip the panade and lean chicken meatballs lose 15 to 20% of their weight in the oven; with the panade, that drops to 8 to 10%.

Why 220 °C, not 180 °C. Lower-temperature baking is gentler but takes 25 to 30 minutes for the meatballs to reach internal doneness, and the extended time dries out the surface. Higher temperature (220 °C / 425 °F) cooks the meatballs through in 16 to 18 minutes, browns the surface during that window, and gets them out of the oven before the chicken proteins squeeze out too much water. The Maillard browning at the higher temperature also contributes savory complexity that the lower-heat bake misses.

The yogurt-dill is half the dish. The meatballs alone are competent but not striking; paired with a generous spoon of cold, lemony, garlicky yogurt sauce per bite, the dish reads as a proper Greek-Cypriot keftedes plate. The temperature contrast between the warm meatball and the cold sauce, the acid against the warm spice, the fresh dill against the cooked dill, these are the structural moves that elevate the dish. Skimping on the sauce is the biggest home-cook mistake. Make extra; it keeps 5 days refrigerated and is excellent on anything (eggs, roasted potatoes, salads, grain bowls).

Make-ahead and storage. Cooked meatballs hold 4 days refrigerated and reheat well in a 160 °C / 325 °F oven for 8 to 10 minutes (they tolerate reheating better than most ground chicken preparations because of the zucchini moisture). The raw mix can be shaped and refrigerated up to 24 hours before baking; the seasonings continue to penetrate. For freezing: bake fully, cool, freeze in a single layer on a sheet pan, then transfer to a freezer-safe container. Reheat from frozen in a 180 °C / 350 °F oven for 18 minutes. The yogurt sauce keeps 5 days refrigerated; do not freeze.
