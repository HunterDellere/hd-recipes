Recipe

Cast-iron chuck eye steaks with shallot pan sauce

Two 0.5 lb chuck eye steaks dry-brined overnight, hard-seared in a smoking cast-iron skillet, butter-basted with smashed garlic and thyme sprigs to a 54 °C medium-rare, then rested while the same pan builds a sauce from minced shallot, red wine, beef broth, dijon, and cold butter mounted in off-heat. The American steakhouse and French bistro overlap: ribeye-grade flavor from the chuck primal, a glossy mahogany pan sauce that takes 6 minutes of attention while the steaks rest, and a finish that ties marbling, fond, and acidity into one continuous bite.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Dry-brine the steaks 12 to 24 hours ahead, salt them on both sides at 0.9% of weight (4 g kosher salt for two 225 g steaks), set on a wire rack over a sheet tray, and refrigerate uncovered. The surface needs to be visibly dry to the touch before searing; salt without overnight rest crusts but does not penetrate, and the steak weeps water into the pan instead of building Maillard.
  • Pull the steaks out of the fridge 45 minutes before searing so the interior comes up toward 18 to 20°C. A cold-from-fridge interior overshoots the sear window (the surface goes black before the center hits 54°C), and the pan sauce window collapses chasing temperature.
  • The pan sauce is built in the same hot cast iron while the steaks rest 5 minutes. Stage the minced shallot, red wine, broth, dijon, and the cold cubed butter on the counter before the sear; once the steaks come out you have a 6 minute window to build, reduce, and mount, no time to start mincing.
  • Have the kitchen ventilation on high. A smoking cast iron at 230°C plus butter-basting fills the kitchen with smoke within 90 seconds; without the fan running you trigger smoke alarms and the cook becomes about the alarm rather than the steak.

Execution

  1. 1
    The night before. Pat both 225 g steaks dry on both sides with paper towels. Lay them on a wire rack set over a small sheet pan. Sprinkle 4 g / 1 tsp Diamond Crystal kosher salt evenly across both sides of both steaks; that is roughly 0.9% of the total steak weight, the canonical dry-brine ratio. The salt will draw beads of surface moisture in the first hour (visible as fine droplets on the meat), then re-absorb over the next 6 to 8 hours as the salinity gradient flips and the brine carries the salt back into the meat. Place the rack-and-pan setup uncovered in the refrigerator for 12 to 24 hours. Uncovered is the operative word; the cold dry fridge air pulls additional surface moisture and forms a tacky pellicle on the meat that sears dramatically faster than wet meat. By the morning the steak surface should look slightly matte-tacky and the flesh should feel firmer than raw. Dry brining12 h
  2. 2
    One hour before cooking. Pull the steaks out of the refrigerator and let them temper at room temperature for 45 to 60 minutes, still on the rack. A cold-from-the-fridge steak takes 60 to 90 seconds longer per side to reach interior temperature, which gives the surface time to overshoot into a charred-black rather than mahogany crust. Room-temperature meat (around 18 to 21 °C surface, the center still cool) gives the cleanest crust-to-interior gradient. While the steaks temper, do every cut for the pan sauce, the 50 g / 2 shallots fine-minced, the 12 g / 3 garlic cloves side-smashed with skins on, the 4 g / 4 thyme sprigs and the 1 g / ½ tsp stripped thyme leaves separated, the 30 g of cold cubed butter for basting and the 30 g of cold cubed butter for mounting in two small bowls in the refrigerator. The mise needs to be complete before the pan goes on the heat, the sear is 4 minutes total and there is no time to chop mid-cook.
  3. 3
    Preheat the pan and crack the pepper. Place a heavy 30 cm / 12-inch cast-iron skillet over high heat. Cast-iron preheats slowly; give it 4 to 5 full minutes with the burner on its highest setting. The cue for the right temperature: a drop of water flicked into the pan should immediately ball into a single rolling bead and dance across the surface (the Leidenfrost effect, indicating the pan is well above 175 °C / 350 °F), and the first faint wisps of dry heat-shimmer should be visible above the cooking surface. Crack the 2 g / 1 tsp coarse black pepper directly onto both sides of both steaks while the pan preheats; the pepper goes on now rather than during the dry-brine because pepper left on raw meat in the fridge for 12 hours oxidizes and reads stale by the time it hits the pan.
  4. 4
    Sear, first side. Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil to the smoking cast-iron pan and tilt to coat the surface; the oil should ripple and shimmer within 3 to 5 seconds and just begin to smoke at 8 to 10 seconds. The pan is ready. Lay the 2 steaks in the pan, pulling each one toward you so the oil sprays away (oil splash safety). They will sizzle violently and immediately, that is the cue that the pan is hot enough, the steaks should be loud the second they touch the surface. Press each steak flat to the pan with the back of a fish spatula for 5 to 10 seconds to ensure full contact. Then leave them undisturbed for 90 seconds. Resist the urge to lift and peek, the crust forms in a single uninterrupted contact with the iron, and lifting at 30 seconds breaks that and starts the clock over. Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Flip and sear, second side. After 90 seconds, lift the corner of one steak with the spatula; the crust should be a deep mahogany-bronze, with the cooked-flesh band climbing about a third of the way up the side of the steak. If the crust is still pale gray, give it another 20 to 30 seconds. When ready, flip both steaks to the second side. Lay them down in clean pan-territory (not the spot they were just searing on, which has lost surface temperature and accumulated rendered fat that will steam rather than sear). Sear undisturbed for another 90 seconds. Maillard sear
  6. 6
    Add aromatics and butter-baste to finish. Drop the 30 g / 2 tbsp cold cubed butter, the 12 g / 3 smashed garlic cloves (skins on), and the 4 g / 4 thyme sprigs into the pan around the steaks. The butter will foam and brown within 15 seconds; tilt the pan slightly so the foaming butter pools at one edge, and use a large spoon to scoop the herb-and-garlic-flavored butter onto the top surface of each steak repeatedly for 60 to 90 seconds. The baste does three things: it puts a thin film of milk-solid-browned butter onto the seared crust (the Maillard browning of the butter compounds the Maillard of the steak), it carries thymol and allicin (the volatile compounds in thyme and garlic) onto the surface, and it equalizes the steak temperature top-to-bottom so the second-side sear gets a head start before any flip. Check internal temperature in the thickest part with an instant-read thermometer; the target is 50 to 52 °C / 122 to 126 °F to come off the heat (the steak will climb 3 to 5 °C during the rest). For a 1-inch chuck eye starting from properly tempered meat, 60 to 90 seconds of butter-baste is the right window.
  7. 7
    Rest. Pull the steaks from the pan onto a small board or warmed plate. Tent loosely with foil, do not seal, sealed foil traps steam and softens the crust the dry-brine spent 12 hours building. Rest 5 to 7 minutes. The rest is functional, not cosmetic; the muscle fibers that contracted under the heat relax back and the internal moisture redistributes. A steak cut immediately bleeds its juice onto the cutting board; the same steak rested 5 minutes holds 30 to 40% more juice when sliced. Internal temperature should climb to 52 to 57 °C / 126 to 135 °F (medium-rare to medium-rare-plus) during the rest. Remove the spent 4 g / 4 thyme sprigs and 12 g / 3 garlic cloves from the pan with tongs; the garlic should be deeply golden and edible (squeeze the soft clove out of the skin and eat it as the cook's treat). Keep the rendered butter-fat in the pan, that is the sauce base.
  8. 8
    Pan sauce, the shallot sweat. Reduce the heat under the cast-iron pan to medium. The pan is still very hot from the sear; the residual butter-fat should be glossy and just below smoking. Add the 50 g / 2 shallots fine-minced and stir constantly with a wooden spoon for 60 to 90 seconds. The shallot should turn translucent and just begin to take on a pale golden color, no deeper. Color past blond is texture loss; the shallot turns from a soft aromatic into a crunchy bitter chip if it browns past gold.
  9. 9
    Deglaze. Pour in the 120 g / ½ cup red wine; it will boil aggressively the second it hits the pan. Use the back of the wooden spoon to scrape every brown spot of fond off the bottom of the pan (the dark crusty bits from the steak sear are pure glutamate-driven flavor; the wine is the solvent that lifts them into the sauce). Reduce the wine over medium-high heat until it has roughly halved in volume and the alcoholic harshness has cooked off; the visual cue is a glossy syrupy puddle of dark red liquid coating the bottom of the pan, about 60 to 90 seconds. The wine is reduced when you can drag the spoon across the pan and the liquid takes a moment to flow back together rather than rushing in immediately. Deglazing
  10. 10
    Add the broth and mustard. Pour in the 240 g / 1 cup beef broth and whisk in the 8 g / 1 tsp dijon mustard. If using the optional 5 g / 1 tsp Worcestershire, add it now. Bring to a hard simmer and let the liquid reduce to a sauce consistency: the volume should drop to roughly one-third of the original (a scant ½ cup), with bubbles becoming smaller and slower as the liquid concentrates, and the sauce should coat the back of a spoon with a visible drag-line when you run a fingertip through it (the classic nappant test). This reduction takes 3 to 5 minutes at a hard simmer. Taste the sauce, it should read strongly beefy, a touch sharp from the wine, with the mustard reading as background tang rather than mustard-front. Add a pinch of kosher salt only if needed; the seared fond and the reduced beef broth carry significant salt already.
  11. 11
    Mount with cold butter, off heat. Pull the pan completely off the burner. The pan retains enough residual heat to melt cold butter without breaking the emulsion; this is the move on which the sauce's gloss and body depend. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp cold cubed butter all at once and whisk continuously with a fork or a small whisk, moving fast, until the butter has fully integrated into the sauce and the surface has gone glossy-opaque rather than oil-slicked. This is monter au beurre, the French bistro butter-mount, and the cold-butter-into-warm-sauce direction is what produces a temporary emulsion of butterfat in reduced wine and broth. Hot butter into hot sauce produces a broken oily sauce; cold butter into off-heat sauce produces silk. Whisk in the 1 g / ½ tsp stripped thyme leaves at the end. Taste again, add a small splash of red wine vinegar (a teaspoon, off-pantry) if the sauce reads too rich and needs lift. Pan emulsion (mantecare)
  12. 12
    Slice and plate. Place each rested steak on a warmed plate. Slice across the grain (perpendicular to the visible muscle striation; the longissimus eye runs one direction and the smaller surrounding muscles may run another, follow the dominant grain through the eye) into 1 cm thick slices; the cross-grain slice shortens the muscle fibers to bite size and reads dramatically more tender than a with-grain slice. Spoon the shallot pan sauce generously over the slices, about 60 to 80 g of sauce per plate; the sauce should pool around the meat rather than fully coat it, so each forkful can take a different sauce-to-meat ratio. Scatter a pinch of the 2 g / ½ tsp flaky finishing salt over the slices, the macro-flake salt sits on top of the cut surface, hits the tongue first, and reads as a sharper bright contrast to the sauce's long umami. Serve immediately, ideally alongside the sheet-pan roasted shallots, cauliflower, and delicata squash from the companion recipe on this site, with the same sauce spilling over the vegetables.
Safety
  • Pulling chuck eye at 52 to 57 °C / 126 to 135 °F internal (below the USDA published-safe 145 °F for whole-muscle beef) The USDA published-safe target for whole-muscle beef is 145 °F (63 °C) followed by a 3-minute rest. The American and French steakhouse canon pulls medium-rare steaks at 52 to 57 °C (126 to 135 °F) followed by a 3- to 5-minute rest, and this is the doneness range at which chuck eye eats best. Whole-muscle beef from a reputable source carries a substantially lower pathogen risk than ground beef (the bacterial load lives on the surface, which the hard sear pasteurizes; the interior of a whole muscle from a healthy animal is essentially sterile). Cooks who want the published-safe number should pull at 145 °F, accepting drier, chalkier meat. Ground beef, mechanically tenderized cuts, and blade-tenderized steaks should always be cooked to 71 °C / 160 °F since the surface bacteria are mixed throughout. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein upgrade→ribeye, NY strip, or hanger steak

    Ribeye is the direct upgrade: same muscle (longissimus dorsi) without the chuck-side connective bands, costs 2 to 4× more per pound, behaves identically in the pan but tolerates a wider doneness window (52 to 60 °C is the sweet spot). NY strip works the same way; pull at 54 °C, the strip is a touch leaner. Hanger steak is a different muscle (the diaphragm pillar) with a much grainier texture and bigger beefy flavor; sear hot and fast (60 seconds per side for a 2 cm thick hanger), pull at 52 °C, slice thinner across the grain because the grain is much more pronounced.

  • Texture thicker cuts (1.5 to 2 inch chuck eye or ribeye)→reverse sear

    Preheat oven to 110 °C / 225 °F. Salt the steaks the night before and rest on a rack as usual. Place the racked steaks in the oven and roast slowly to 43 to 45 °C internal (a 1.5-inch steak takes 30 to 40 minutes; a 2-inch steak takes 50 to 65 minutes). Pull, rest 5 minutes, then sear in the smoking cast-iron pan with the butter baste for 45 to 60 seconds per side, just to build crust. The reverse sear is the right technique for any steak above 1 inch thick; the low oven gives the interior a long, even temperature climb, and the hard sear at the end builds crust without overshooting the interior.

  • Regional sauce direction→green peppercorn cream sauce (steak au poivre vert)

    Swap the wine reduction direction. After sweating the shallot, deglaze with 60 g / 2 oz brandy or cognac (flambé optional, for the dramatic burn-off; let it reduce by half regardless), then add 120 g / ½ cup heavy cream instead of beef broth and 15 g / 1 tbsp green peppercorns in brine (drained). Reduce to nappant, then whisk in 15 g / 1 tbsp cold butter off-heat. The sauce reads richer and more bistro-classic, with the green peppercorns carrying the heat structure that the dijon carries in the original.

  • Occasion compound butter finish (no pan sauce)→maître d'hôtel butter

    Skip phases 3 to 4 entirely. Mash together 60 g / 4 tbsp room-temperature unsalted butter, 4 g / 1 tbsp chopped fresh parsley, 4 g / 1 tsp minced shallot, 8 g / 1 tsp lemon juice, 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt, and a few twists of black pepper. Roll into a log in parchment paper and refrigerate 30 minutes to firm. Slice 1 cm coins of the cold butter and lay one directly onto each rested steak so it melts over the surface as you carry it to the table. Faster, lower-effort, and the better choice on a true weeknight when the pan sauce's 6 minutes is too much.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • chuck eye steaks → ribeye, NY strip, top sirloin, or flat iron steak Ribeye is the obvious upgrade; NY strip behaves identically with slightly less marbling; top sirloin and flat iron are leaner and pull at 52 °C rather than 54 °C. Skirt steak and flank steak work for the slice-thin-across-grain version but not for the plated-whole presentation.
  • dry red wine → dry white wine, port, or no-wine deglaze Dry white wine (Sauvignon Blanc, Chardonnay, or any unoaked white) makes a lighter sauce that reads more French-classic and less rustic; reduce the same way. Tawny port adds sweet richness and reads more steakhouse-American; use 90 g / 3 oz and reduce harder. Skipping wine entirely is acceptable; deglaze with 60 g / ¼ cup additional broth plus 8 g / 1 tsp red wine vinegar to provide the acid the sauce needs, the dish loses some complexity but stays alcohol-free and works for kids and pregnant cooks.
  • beef broth → chicken broth, vegetable broth, or homemade veal stock Chicken broth makes a noticeably lighter sauce that still works (the dijon and red wine carry most of the savory load); vegetable broth makes a sauce that reads more like a pan-jus than a steak sauce, fine but missing depth. Veal stock (Tetsu, the better online stocks) is the French restaurant gold standard and produces a sauce that eats like an upscale bistro dish; use it if you have it, the difference is meaningful.
  • fresh thyme → fresh rosemary (smaller amount), dried thyme, or fresh marjoram Fresh rosemary is much more aggressive than thyme; use one sprig instead of four, and pull it at the end of the baste rather than letting it sit in the butter. Dried thyme is fine in the sauce reduction (¼ tsp), less ideal in the butter baste (the dried leaves can scorch). Fresh marjoram or savory are 1:1 substitutes.
  • dijon mustard → whole-grain mustard or no mustard Whole-grain mustard reads texturally different (visible seed bits in the sauce) but contributes the same emulsion stabilizer and acid. Skipping the mustard entirely is acceptable; the sauce loses its tangy backbone but reads cleaner and more straight-red-wine; bump the red wine vinegar finish to a teaspoon.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → beef broth, low-sodium or unsalted homemade make it yourself Homemade beef broth is the entire reason a pan sauce reads bistro-level. A 6-hour roasted-bone broth carries the gelatin (collagen hydrolyzed under low heat) that gives the sauce its body and the mouth-coating gloss that the butter mount alone cannot produce. Store-bought "beef broth" in cartons is typically chicken broth with caramel color and natural flavors added; the body and depth difference is immediate and obvious. Make 2 L in a single afternoon, freeze in 1-cup portions, and any pan sauce night thereafter is 6 minutes start to finish.
  • → dijon mustard make it yourself Homemade dijon takes 5 minutes of grinding and a week of patience for the pungency to mellow into the sharp-but-balanced edge that defines a good dijon. The acid (verjuice, white wine, or wine vinegar) and the mustard variety (Brassica juncea brown seed for dijon, plus a smaller fraction of yellow) are both single-variable controls the cook can dial; commercial dijon (Maille, Edmond Fallot, Grey Poupon) is reliable but cannot match a fresh-ground house version on aromatic peak. The week of mellowing in the fridge is the structural step; under 5 days, the mustard reads aggressively horseradish-hot; past 3 weeks, the bite starts to fade.

Notes

Why this cut on cast-iron specifically. Chuck eye sits one rib forward of the strip loin and one rib back of the ribeye, carrying the same longissimus dorsi cross-section that defines those cuts. The marbling is roughly Select to low Choice grade, lower than a typical Choice ribeye but comparable on the eye itself. The cut runs 4 to 6 dollars per pound at most American supermarkets against 15 to 25 dollars per pound for ribeye, and the perceived flavor difference on a properly cooked sear is small enough that most home cooks would not pick the ribeye in a blind tasting. The trade-off is that chuck eye has a band or two of connective tissue at the edges of the eye, the silver-white sheets visible on the cross-section, which need the longer carryover into the 54 to 57 °C window to soften enough to eat tender. Cast iron is the right pan for the cut because the thermal mass holds temperature when two cold steaks hit the surface, and the rough seasoned-iron surface produces a more textural crust than the smoother carbon-steel pan that defines the French equivalent. Either works; cast iron is what the American steakhouse tradition built around.

The dry-brine is the night-before lift that distinguishes a good cast-iron steak from a great one. A salted-30-minutes-before steak is fine; the salt has only worked the surface and the meat reads salted-on-the-outside. A 12-to-24-hour dry-brine equilibrates the seasoning through the entire cross-section, fundamentally changing the eating profile. The uncovered fridge rest does the secondary structural work: dry surface sears hotter and faster than wet surface, and the pellicle that forms over 12 hours of cold dry air is the foundation of the deep mahogany crust. If you only have 30 minutes, salt and proceed, the dish still works, but the difference between 30-minute-salt and overnight-dry-brine is the difference between a home steak and a steakhouse steak.

Why the butter-baste matters and where it can be skipped. The 60 to 90 seconds of butter-basting at the end of the sear does three structural things: it browns butter on the pan surface (the milk solids in butter Maillard at the same temperature the meat does, producing nutty butterfat that compounds the meat''s own browning), it infuses the surface with thymol from the thyme and allicin from the garlic, and it equalizes the top-side temperature of the steak so the second side doesn''t need to re-sear from cold meat. The baste is a finish, not a foundation; if you''re cooking 4 steaks in two batches and the pan is overloaded, skip the baste and rely on the crust the dry sear built. The shallot pan sauce supplies the same butter-and-aromatic register the baste would have brought, so the dish is not poorer for the skip.

Pan-sauce structure and where it can break. The shallot sweat, the wine reduction, the broth reduction, and the cold-butter mount are four discrete moves in sequence. Each one fails for a different reason. The shallot sweat fails when the pan is too hot and the shallot browns past blond into bitter brown chip; reduce the heat to medium the moment you scrape out the steak fat, not after. The wine reduction fails when the wine is over-reduced past syrup into a sticky lacquer that won''t loosen when broth hits; if you overshoot, add 30 g of water before the broth to bring it back. The broth reduction fails when the heat is too low and the sauce never concentrates into nappant; medium-high heat with the bubbles audibly hard-rolling is the right level. The cold-butter mount fails when the pan is too hot and the butter melts cleanly into oil-on-water rather than emulsifying into the sauce; pulling the pan completely off the heat for the butter mount is non-negotiable, residual cast-iron heat is plenty.

Doneness targets and carryover. Pull the steaks at 50 to 52 °C internal in the thickest part. The carryover during the 5- to 7-minute rest will climb the temperature 3 to 5 °C into the 52 to 57 °C window, which is the sweet spot for chuck eye. Past 60 °C the eye dries chalky; under 50 °C the connective tissue at the edges reads chewy. The optimal-doneness window for chuck eye is narrower than the ribeye''s 52 to 60 °C window because the connective bands need temperature to soften. An instant-read thermometer is the right tool here; reading doneness from finger-press or visual cues alone produces too much variance on a cut this thin. The Thermapen ONE (or any sub-3-second instant-read at the same price point) earns its keep on every steak it touches.

Why no bone-in option. Bone-in cuts hold the bone-side meat at a lower temperature during the sear (the bone acts as a heat sink), producing uneven doneness across the steak. Chuck eye is typically sold boneless and the dish is calibrated to that. Bone-in ribeye works for a different dish (the long, slow oven-finish reverse sear or a true steakhouse broiler approach), not for a 4-minute cast-iron sear.

The American-French overlap. The American steakhouse tradition built this dish around the cast-iron pan, the dry-brine, the butter-baste, and the rest. The French bistro tradition built the same dish around a carbon-steel pan, an overnight salt, an off-heat baste with beurre noisette, and the same rest. The pan sauce, monter au beurre with shallot and red wine, is French in origin (the entire technique of mounting reduced wine sauces with cold butter off-heat is bistro classique) but has been absorbed wholesale into modern American steakhouse cookery. This recipe sits exactly at the overlap; an experienced French cook and an experienced American cook will both recognize the dish as their own canon.
