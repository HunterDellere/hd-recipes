Recipe

Miso-glazed black cod

Sablefish (black cod) cured two to three days in a Saikyo-style marinade of sweet white miso, sake, and mirin, then broiled until the sugars in the glaze lacquer into a blistered mahogany crust over flesh that flakes in glassy, fat-rich sheets. The marinade is doing two jobs at once: the miso proteases break down muscle protein into a silkier texture while the salt draws moisture and the sugars set up the eventual Maillard and caramelization on the broiler. This is the Nobu-canonical dish rebuilt from first principles, with the cure timed to the enzyme rather than to a clock, and the broil calibrated so the glaze blackens at the edges without the interior overcooking past the point where sablefish renders its remarkable fat.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a make-ahead dish. The fillets cure in the miso marinade for 2 to 3 days in the refrigerator before they ever see heat. Start the cure 48 to 72 hours before you plan to serve. A 24-hour cure works but reads as marinated rather than transformed; past 3 days the texture turns mealy as the proteases over-soften the flesh.
  • Pat the fillets bone-dry before they go into the marinade. Surface water dilutes the miso at the fish interface and slows the salt-and-enzyme penetration that does the curing.
  • On serving day, scrape (do not rinse) the marinade off the fish, then let the fillets sit at room temperature 20 minutes before broiling so the interior is not ice-cold when the surface starts to char. A fridge-cold fillet blackens on top before the center warms through.
  • Run the broiler on high with a rack positioned 10 to 15 cm / 4 to 6 inches below the element, and have the kitchen ventilation on. The residual miso sugars scorch and smoke fast; this is a stand-at-the-oven dish, not a set-and-walk-away one.

Execution

  1. 1
    Pre-salt the fish (15 minutes before the cure). Pat the 340 g / two 6 oz black cod fillets bone-dry with paper towels on both sides. Sprinkle the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt evenly over the flesh side of both fillets and let them sit on a wire rack for 15 minutes. The salt firms the surface proteins and pulls a film of moisture to the surface, which you will blot away before the marinade goes on. This light pre-salt keeps the long miso cure from leaving the flesh waterlogged and is what gives the finished fish its clean, dense flake rather than a soft, brine-soaked one. Dry brining
  2. 2
    Build the Saikyo marinade. In a bowl, whisk the 240 g / 1 cup sweet white miso, the 60 g / ¼ cup sake, the 60 g / ¼ cup mirin, and the 30 g / 2½ tbsp granulated sugar into a smooth, spreadable paste the consistency of thick yogurt. If you are using genuine Saikyo miso, cut the sugar to 15 g / 1 tbsp, it is already intensely sweet. The sake and mirin thin the dense miso so it contacts the fish evenly; the sugar and the glucose already present in the mirin are the fuel for the caramelized lacquer that forms later under the broiler.
  3. 3
    Cure the fish, 2 to 3 days. Blot the moisture off the salted fillets. Spread a 5 mm bed of the miso marinade across the bottom of a non-reactive dish or a deep tray, lay the fillets flesh-side down on it, then pack the remaining marinade over the top and sides so the fish is fully encased. Press a sheet of parchment directly onto the surface to seal out air, cover the dish with a fitted lid, and refrigerate 48 to 72 hours. During the cure the miso's koji-derived proteases (the same enzymes that ferment the soybeans) slowly break down the muscle proteins, which both tenderizes the flesh and deepens its savory glutamate load, while the salt and sugar draw water out and drive seasoning in. Two days is the floor for transformation; three days is the sweet spot for sablefish; past three days the proteases over-soften the flesh toward mealy.2 d 0 h
  4. 4
    On serving day, scrape and temper. Lift the fillets from the marinade and scrape the miso off with the back of a knife or your fingers, leaving only a thin residual film. Do not rinse under water; rinsing strips the seasoning you spent three days driving in and leaves the surface wet. A thin miso film is what caramelizes, but a thick clinging layer will scorch black and bitter before the fish cooks, so wipe more thoroughly than feels right. Set the scraped fillets on a plate and leave them at room temperature for 20 minutes so the interior is not fridge-cold going under the broiler.
  5. 5
    Preheat the broiler hard. Position an oven rack so the fish surface will sit 10 to 15 cm / 4 to 6 inches below the broiler element and set the broiler to high; give it a full 5 minutes to come up to temperature. Line a sheet pan with foil and brush it with the 5 g / 1 tsp neutral oil, or oil a wire rack set over the pan. The high, close heat is what blisters the miso sugars into a lacquer in the time it takes the thin sablefish fillet to just cook through; a cooler oven dries the fish before the glaze ever browns.
  6. 6
    Broil to a blistered lacquer. Lay the fillets skin-side down on the prepared pan. Slide under the broiler and cook 6 to 8 minutes without flipping, watching constantly from minute 4 on. The surface will go from gold to deep mahogany to charred-black at the high points; you want the deep mahogany with a scatter of true-black blisters, which is the same controlled-scorch you see on yakitori tare or crème brûlée, not an even brown. If the top is darkening faster than the fish is cooking, drop the rack one position. The fish is done when it flakes into glossy, fat-slicked sheets at the thickest point and an instant-read thermometer reads 52 to 55 °C / 126 to 131 °F; sablefish is so fatty it stays moist even at the high end of that range, but pulling on the lower side keeps the flake silkier. Maillard sear
  7. 7
    Rest, then plate and finish. Let the fillets rest 2 to 3 minutes off the heat; the carryover settles the flake and the surface sugars cool from molten to set. Transfer each fillet to a warmed plate, scatter the 15 g / 2 sliced scallion greens over the top, and set a lime wedge alongside. Squeeze the 15 g / ½ lime over the fish just before eating; the acid is non-negotiable here, it cuts through the sweet glaze and the heavy fat and resets the palate so the second bite hits as cleanly as the first. Serve with steamed short-grain rice and the optional 40 g pickled ginger or quick-pickled daikon, whose sharp acidity does the same palate-resetting work between bites.
Safety
  • sablefish at 52 to 55°C / 126 to 131°F internal (below the USDA published-safe 145°F for fish) The Saikyo-yaki tradition pulls sablefish at 52 to 55°C / 126 to 131°F so the fat-rich flake stays glossy and silken; past 60°C the fat renders out and the flesh turns dry and chalky. The published 145°F is the conservative line for the immunocompromised. Sablefish from a reputable counter, cured in salt-forward miso for 2 to 3 days and then hard-broiled until the surface chars, follows long-standing practice at the lower temperature, with the broiler pasteurizing the exterior where the bacterial load lives and the miso cure suppressing surface growth during the hold. Cooks who want the USDA-published-safe number should broil to 145°F / 63°C internal, accepting drier flake. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture cooking method→sear-then-roast (no broiler)

    If your broiler is weak or unpredictable, get the lacquer in a pan instead. Heat 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil in an oven-safe nonstick or carbon-steel skillet over medium-high, lay the scraped fillets flesh-side down, and sear 2 to 3 minutes until the miso film is deep brown, then flip to skin-side down and transfer the pan to a 200 °C / 400 °F oven for 5 to 7 minutes to finish. You trade the blistered char of the broiler for a more even caramelized crust, which is the more forgiving path.

  • Protein fish→salmon, sea bass, or another fatty fillet

    The Saikyo cure works on any fatty fish; lean white fish (Atlantic cod, halibut) dries out and is the wrong choice. King salmon takes the same 2 to 3 day cure and broils identically. Striped bass or sea bass cure faster, 24 to 36 hours, because the flesh is leaner and the proteases over-soften it sooner; pull the cure earlier and watch the broiler, the thinner flesh cooks through in 5 to 6 minutes.

  • Regional flavor direction→yuzu-miso

    Whisk 10 g / 2 tsp yuzu juice and the finely grated zest of half a yuzu into the marinade before curing, and finish with yuzu wedges instead of lime. The floral citrus reads more distinctly Japanese than lime and threads acid through the cure itself rather than only at the finish. Frozen yuzu juice is the practical source; fresh yuzu is seasonal and expensive.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • sweet white miso (Saikyo) → any shiro/white miso, plus the full 30 g sugar Genuine Saikyo miso is sweet and low-salt; a standard shiro miso is saltier, which is why the recipe adds sugar to bridge the gap. Do not substitute red (aka) miso here, its aggressive aged funk and higher salt overwhelm the delicate fish and the glaze reads harsh rather than sweet-savory.
  • mirin (hon-mirin) → 15 g / 1 tbsp extra sake plus 15 g / 1 tbsp sugar Real hon-mirin contributes both sweetness and the glucose that browns; faking it with sake and sugar gets you most of the way. Avoid mirin-fu chomiryo (the sweetened cooking-liquid substitute), it has the sugar but none of the fermented depth that hon-mirin brings to the cure.
  • sake → dry sherry or dry white wine Sake is closest in profile, but dry sherry is a reasonable stand-in for the cure; dry white wine works in a pinch though it reads slightly more acidic. All three just need to thin the miso and burn off, so the exact choice matters less here than it would in a finished sauce.
  • lime → yuzu, sudachi, or lemon Yuzu or sudachi are the authentic Japanese citrus and read more floral; lemon is brighter and more aggressive but works. The job is the same: a sharp finishing acid to cut the sweet glaze and the rich fat.

Notes

Why sablefish specifically. Black cod (Anoplopoma fimbria) is not a true cod; it is a deep-cold-water North Pacific fish whose flesh runs extraordinarily high in fat, comparable to a fatty salmon or a good piece of toro. That fat is the entire reason the dish works. The long miso cure and the hard broil would render a lean white fish like Atlantic cod or halibut dry and stringy, but sablefish holds moisture through both, and its fat carries the umami of the cure and bastes the flesh from within as it broils. If you cannot source it, a fatty king salmon is the right substitute; a lean fillet is the wrong one.

Why the cure is timed to the enzyme, not the clock. Saikyo miso is loaded with koji (Aspergillus oryzae) and its proteolytic enzymes. Over 2 to 3 days in the refrigerator, those proteases slowly hydrolyze the fish's muscle proteins into shorter peptides and free amino acids, which is simultaneously a tenderization (the flesh turns silkier and denser) and an umami amplification (free glutamate goes up). The salt and sugar in the marinade run an osmotic exchange alongside it, drawing water out and driving seasoning in. Under 48 hours the fish reads marinated rather than transformed; past 72 hours the proteases keep going and the flesh slides toward mealy. Three days is the calibrated sweet spot for a 2.5 cm sablefish fillet.

Why you scrape rather than rinse. The thin film of miso left on the fish is the precursor for the caramelized lacquer; rinsing it off under water both strips the seasoning you spent three days driving in and wets the surface, which then steams rather than browns. But a thick clinging layer of marinade is worse than none, because the high sugar load scorches to acrid black long before the fish is cooked. Scrape thoroughly, leave a sheen, no more.

Why the broiler runs hot and close. Sablefish fillets are thin and cook through fast. The marinade sugars need real radiant heat to caramelize and blister in that short window. A moderate oven gives you cooked fish with a pale, untransformed surface; the close broiler gives you the blistered mahogany lacquer that defines the dish, the same controlled scorch you want on yakitori tare or the top of a crème brûlée. The discipline is in watching it: the line between deep mahogany and burnt-bitter is about 45 seconds, so stand at the oven from minute 4 on.

The umami-and-acid architecture. The glutamate source is built into the dish at its foundation, the miso cure is one of the most concentrated natural glutamate deliveries in any cooking tradition, amplified further by the koji proteases liberating free amino acids over three days. That deep, sweet-savory richness is exactly why the finishing acid is non-negotiable: a squeeze of lime (or yuzu) at the table cuts the sweet glaze and the heavy fat and resets the palate. Skip it and the dish reads cloying by the third bite; with it, every forkful lands as clean as the first.

On the Nobu lineage. The dish most Western diners know as "miso black cod" is Nobu Matsuhisa's, but the technique is the centuries-old Kyoto saikyo-yaki tradition of curing fish in sweet white miso to preserve and refine it. Nobu's contribution was the specific pairing with sablefish and the global stage; the underlying method is older and broadly applicable to any fatty fish. Building it from first principles, as here, means timing the cure to the koji enzymes and the broil to the sugars rather than following a fixed recipe by rote.
