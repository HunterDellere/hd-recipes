Recipe

Avgolemono soup

The Greek chicken-rice-egg-lemon soup: bone-in chicken pieces simmered in water with aromatics to build the broth, the chicken pulled and shredded, short-grain rice cooked into the broth until tender, then finished by tempering a vigorously whisked egg-and-lemon mixture into the hot broth off-heat. Structurally a textbook tempered-egg emulsion: 3 eggs whisked with 100 ml of lemon juice form an acidic foam, which is brought up to broth temperature by drizzling in 250 ml of hot broth while whisking, then poured back into the pot off-heat. The eggs cook gently into a velvety pale yellow sauce that thickens the broth and binds the rice; the lemon brightens the dish to a clean tangy finish. The canonical Greek comfort soup, and the structural foundation for the broader avgolemono sauce family.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The egg-tempering finish is structural and time-sensitive. Have everything ready before starting the temper: 3 eggs cracked into a bowl with the lemon juice already whisked, the broth at a bare simmer (just barely steaming, no active bubbles), the pot pulled off heat the moment the temper begins. Tempering is the entire technique of this dish and the moment that separates a successful avgolemono from a broken curdled soup.
  • Use bone-in chicken pieces, not breast meat alone. The bones (especially the carcass and thigh bones) extract gelatin into the broth during the simmer; this gelatin is structural for the final body of the soup. Boneless skinless chicken breast produces a thin watery broth that does not finish well.

Phase 1, the broth

  1. 1200 g bone-in skin-on chicken (mixed pieces: 2 thighs, 2 drumsticks, 2 breast halves, or a small whole chicken cut up)
  2. 2500 ml water (cold)
  3. 200 g yellow onion, halved, halved through the root, skin on (the skin adds color and aromatics) — (1 medium onion)
  4. 100 g celery stalks, rough chop, rough chop, 4 cm pieces — (2 stalks)
  5. 1 each lemon peel (no pith), peeled from 1 lemon with a vegetable peeler in long strips, no white pith (the pith is bitter) — (zest of 1 lemon; the lemon used for this is the same lemon whose juice goes into the temper)
  6. 2 each bay leaf — (2 leaves)
  7. 3 g whole black peppercorns
  8. 8 g fine sea salt (for the broth)

Phase 2, the rice and finish

  1. 120 g short-grain or medium-grain rice (arborio, carolina, or Greek glasse) — does not work, the broth stays thin)
  2. 150 g large eggs (whole, room temperature) — (3 large whole eggs; room temperature; cold eggs straight from the refrigerator shock the broth during temper and risk uneven coagulation)
  3. 100 g fresh-squeezed lemon juice (from 2 to 3 lemons), juiced from 2 to 3 lemons (use the same lemons whose zest went into the broth); strained of seeds

Phase 3, garnish

  1. 10 g flat-leaf parsley or fresh dill, finely chopped, finely chopped just before serving
  2. 30 g extra-virgin Greek olive oil (for finishing)

Execution

  1. 1
    Place the 1200 g / 2½ lb chicken in a large stockpot. Add the 2500 ml / 10½ cups cold water; the chicken should be fully submerged with about 5 cm of water above it. Bring slowly to a simmer over medium heat (this takes 12 to 15 minutes). As the broth comes up to temperature, a foamy grey scum will rise to the surface (coagulated dissolved proteins and impurities); skim this off with a fine-mesh skimmer for the first 10 minutes after the broth reaches a simmer (about 3 to 4 skims). A well-skimmed broth is clear and clean-tasting; an unskimmed broth is cloudy and reads muddy.
  2. 2
    Add the 200 g / 1 onion (halved), 100 g / 2 stalks celery (rough chop), the lemon peel strips, 2 bay leaves, 3 g / ½ tsp whole peppercorns, and 8 g / 1¼ tsp salt. Reduce heat to medium-low; simmer at a bare tremor (no rolling bubbles) for 45 minutes, partly covered with the lid ajar. The chicken should be fully cooked through and the broth should be deeply golden and rich-smelling.
  3. 3
    Remove the chicken pieces with tongs to a plate; cover loosely with foil to keep warm. Strain the broth through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean pot, discarding the aromatics. You should have about 2 L / 8½ cups of clear chicken broth.
  4. 4
    When the chicken is cool enough to handle (about 10 minutes), remove the skin and pull the meat off the bones in large shreds (5 to 7 cm pieces). Discard the skin and bones (or freeze the bones for a second-pass deeper stock). You should have about 400 g / 14 oz of pulled chicken meat. Set aside.
  5. 5
    Return the strained broth to a simmer over medium heat. Add the 120 g / ⅔ cup rice; stir once to prevent the grains from sticking. Reduce heat to maintain a gentle simmer; cook 18 to 20 minutes (15 to 16 minutes for arborio, 20 minutes for medium-grain), stirring occasionally, until the rice is fully tender and has released its starch into the broth (the broth will visibly thicken and become slightly cloudy).
  6. 6
    Add the 400 g / 14 oz pulled chicken meat back to the pot; warm through 3 to 4 minutes until heated. Reduce heat to the absolute minimum (a bare tremor, no visible bubbles). Taste; the broth should be well-salted before the egg temper.
  7. 7
    The egg temper (the structural moment of the dish): in a large mixing bowl, crack the 150 g / 3 eggs (whole, room temperature). Whisk vigorously with a balloon whisk or fork for 60 seconds until the eggs are completely uniform, pale, and slightly foamy. Add the 100 g / 7 tbsp fresh-squeezed lemon juice; whisk to combine fully (the mixture will become slightly cloudy and lighter colored).
  8. 8
    Pull the pot of broth off the heat entirely. Place the pot on a kitchen towel on the counter (off any heat source). Now temper: with one hand whisking the egg-lemon mixture continuously, with the other hand ladle hot broth from the pot into the bowl, drizzling slowly in a thin steady stream while whisking. Start with about 250 ml / 1 cup of broth added over 30 to 40 seconds (this is the critical tempering volume; less than this and the eggs scramble when they hit the larger volume of broth). The egg-lemon mixture should warm and lighten visibly during the temper. Tempering eggs
  9. 9
    Pour the tempered egg-lemon-broth mixture back into the pot of broth in a slow steady stream, whisking the broth gently with the other hand to integrate evenly. The broth should turn a pale yellow color and thicken slightly to a velvety body. Do not return the pot to the heat. The residual heat of the broth (just below simmer) is enough to gently cook the eggs into the emulsion without scrambling them.
  10. 10
    Taste; adjust salt (often needs another 2 to 3 g / ½ tsp at this stage) and add black pepper or additional lemon juice if needed (the soup should be bright and tangy). Ladle into warm bowls, dividing the rice and chicken evenly. Top each bowl with a sprinkle of the 10 g / ⅓ cup chopped parsley or dill and a small drizzle of the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil. Serve immediately; avgolemono soup is at its peak texture and temperature within 5 minutes of plating, and tightens visibly as it cools.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein pivot→as an avgolemono lamb fricassee (with lamb shoulder and greens)

    Replace the chicken with 1200 g of bone-in lamb shoulder, cubed and browned in olive oil before adding water. Cook 2½ hours instead of 45 minutes until the lamb is fork-tender. Skip the rice; add 400 g of romaine lettuce or escarole, chopped, in the last 15 minutes of simmering. Finish with the egg-lemon temper as written. The result is arnaki fricassee, the canonical Greek Easter dish; the structural avgolemono technique applied to lamb and greens.

  • Occasion cuisine pivot→as avgolemono sauce (without the soup body, for dolmades and stuffed vegetables)

    After step 7 (the egg temper), instead of pouring back into a soup pot, use the tempered mixture as a sauce poured over hot stuffed grape leaves, stuffed cabbage rolls, or braised artichokes. Skip the rice and chicken. The sauce is the standalone avgolemono finishing sauce used across Greek cooking; it works as a stand-in for béchamel in any application that wants a lighter brighter finish.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • bone-in skin-on chicken (mixed pieces) → a whole chicken (1.5 to 1.8 kg) cut into 8 pieces Mixed bone-in pieces (thighs, drumsticks, breast halves) is the most practical choice; a whole chicken cut into 8 pieces is equally valid and structurally preferred (the carcass adds the most gelatin to the broth). Do not substitute boneless skinless breast or thigh meat alone; the dish reads thin and watery without the bone-derived gelatin.
  • short-grain or medium-grain rice (arborio, carolina, or Greek glasse) → medium-grain rice (calrose) or orzo pasta (kritharaki) Short or medium grain is the structural choice for starch release into the broth. Long-grain rice (basmati, jasmine) does not work; the broth stays thin and the rice grains stay distinct rather than releasing their starch. Orzo pasta (kritharaki in Greek) is the canonical pasta alternative; common in Greek home cooking and reads as a slightly more substantial soup with the pasta carrying the broth.

Notes

The off-heat egg temper is the structural moment of the dish and the single most common failure mode. The eggs and lemon are whisked together cold; this mixture is then warmed gradually by drizzling in 250 ml of hot broth while whisking (the temper); the warmed mixture is then poured back into the pot of hot broth that has been pulled off the heat. The pot must not be over heat when the tempered mixture goes back in; residual heat in the broth is enough to cook the eggs into the emulsion without scrambling them. Returning the pot to heat after the egg has gone in (or, worse, pouring the egg mixture into actively-boiling broth on the heat) causes the eggs to scramble on contact and the soup reads as broth with cooked-egg flecks instead of a velvety emulsion. The dish lives or dies on this technique.

The 250 ml tempering volume is structural and not arbitrary. The 3 eggs and 100 ml lemon juice form a cold acidic foam at about 20°C / 68°F; the broth is at about 85°C / 185°F. Drizzling in 250 ml of hot broth while whisking gradually warms the egg-lemon mixture to about 55°C / 130°F, which is below the egg-coagulation threshold (around 65°C for whole eggs). Pouring this warmed mixture into the larger volume of hot broth (around 1700 ml at this stage) raises the egg temperature gradually to about 75°C / 167°F, which is just at the cusp of coagulation but below scrambling. The eggs gently cook into the emulsion rather than seizing into solid pieces. Tempering with less broth (100 ml or so) does not warm the eggs enough; tempering with more broth (500 ml) over-cooks the eggs before they enter the larger pot.

The lemon juice quantity is structural. The 100 ml of fresh juice (the juice of 2 to 3 lemons) is the canonical Greek ratio for 1.7 L of soup; this produces a brightly tangy soup that reads clearly Greek. Less lemon (50 ml or so) produces a soup that reads as chicken-rice soup with a vague hint of citrus; more lemon (150 ml) produces a soup that reads aggressively acidic and the egg emulsion may break on the lemon-rich finish. Use the canonical 100 ml and adjust slightly to taste, but do not deviate dramatically.

Bottled lemon juice does not work in this dish. The dish is structurally a lemon-driven preparation, and the canonical Greek ratio of 100 ml of fresh juice is exposing the diner directly to the bright clean flavor of fresh-squeezed lemon. Bottled lemon juice (RealLemon, etc.) has been pasteurized and stabilized with preservatives; it reads as metallic, flat, and chemical in this volume. The dish is best skipped entirely if fresh lemons are unavailable.

Storage. Avgolemono soup does not store as well as most soups because the egg emulsion is somewhat fragile. It keeps 2 days refrigerated and can be reheated gently over low heat (do not bring back to a simmer; the eggs may continue to coagulate further), but the texture is noticeably less velvety on day 2 than fresh. The dish is best made fresh and eaten within 6 hours of cooking. The broth itself (before the rice and egg temper) keeps 5 days refrigerated; refresh by re-simmering and tempering fresh eggs into the warm broth at service.

Sources
  • Greek canon (the structural egg-and-lemon technique runs across Greek and Sephardic cookery) — Avgolemono (αυγολέμονο, literally "egg-lemon") is both a soup and a sauce technique in Greek cooking. The sauce is a tempered emulsion of beaten egg and lemon juice into hot broth, used as a finishing thickener for soups, stews (lamb fricassee, dolmades), and braised dishes (artichokes, peas). The soup version uses the technique as the finish for a chicken-rice broth, producing a velvety pale yellow soup with a bright acidic tang that reads as the quintessential Greek comfort dish. The structural elements: a real homemade chicken broth (built from bone-in chicken pieces simmered with onion, celery, lemon zest, bay; not store-bought stock), short-grain or medium-grain rice (arborio or carolina), 3 whole eggs (not separated; the whole eggs provide the right balance of yolk fat and white protein for the emulsion), and fresh-squeezed lemon juice (about 100 ml, the juice of 2 lemons). The technique is tempering: the eggs and lemon are whisked together, hot broth is drizzled into them while whisking to bring the temperature up gradually, then the warmed egg mixture is poured into the pot of hot broth off-heat. The off-heat finish is structural; adding the eggs to actively-boiling broth scrambles them on contact and the soup reads as broth with cooked-egg flecks instead of a velvety emulsion.
