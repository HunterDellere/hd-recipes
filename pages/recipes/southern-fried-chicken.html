Recipe

Southern fried chicken (buttermilk-brined, cast-iron-fried)

Bone-in chicken pieces brined overnight in buttermilk with kosher salt, hot sauce, and aromatics, then double-dredged in seasoned flour and shallow-fried in 2.5 cm of neutral oil in a cast-iron skillet at 165°C until the crust is a deep golden-brown and the meat reads 75°C in the thigh. The American Southern home tradition: shallow pan-fried in cast iron, not deep-fried in a fryer, with the rendered fat doing both crust development and gentle thermal cooking.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Brine the chicken in buttermilk overnight, 12 to 24 hours. Skipping the overnight brine produces a structurally lesser dish, the buttermilk lactic acid tenderizes the meat through gentle protein hydrolysis and the salt seasons through to the bone. A 1-hour quick brine is the floor; the overnight is the canonical.
  • Have a Dutch oven of 165°C / 330°F neutral oil ready before the first piece goes in. A digital thermometer clipped to the side of the pan is the right tool; the temperature WILL drop 15 to 25°C when cold chicken hits the fat, so the burner needs to be on medium-high to recover quickly. Frying at 145°C produces a soggy chicken with a pale crust; frying at 195°C burns the crust before the interior cooks.
  • Fry in 2 to 3 batches, not all at once. Overcrowding crashes the oil temperature, steam pockets form, and the crust goes soft. 3 to 4 pieces per batch in a 30 cm skillet is the right ratio.
  • Set a wire rack over a sheet pan in a 90°C / 200°F oven to hold the fried pieces while you cook subsequent batches. Resting on paper towels steams the bottom crust soft; the wire rack keeps the crust crisp on all sides while the chicken finishes carrying over.

Execution

  1. 1
    Overnight brine. Whisk together 1000 ml / 4 cups buttermilk, 30 g / 2½ tbsp kosher salt, 30 g / 2 tbsp hot sauce, 20 g / 6 cloves smashed garlic, and 4 g / 1 tsp cracked black peppercorns in a large bowl until the salt dissolves. Add the 1600 g chicken pieces; submerge under the brine, pressing down to fully cover. Cover and refrigerate 12 to 24 hours. The salt penetrates through to the bone, the buttermilk lactic acid hydrolyzes surface proteins (reading as more tender meat without going mushy), and the brine seasons the chicken throughout its mass. Dry brining12 h
  2. 2
    Drain the chicken. Lift the chicken pieces out of the brine; let excess buttermilk drip off but do not pat dry. The buttermilk coating IS the dredge adhesive; a dry surface produces a thin, patchy crust. Place the brined chicken on a wire rack while you mix the dredge.
  3. 3
    Mix the seasoned dredge. In a large wide bowl or pie plate, whisk together 300 g all-purpose flour, 60 g cornstarch, 12 g kosher salt, 6 g sweet paprika, 3 g smoked paprika, 6 g garlic powder, 6 g onion powder, 3 g cayenne, 4 g cracked black pepper, and 2 g dried thyme. The dredge should be uniformly tinted pale orange-pink from the paprikas and should smell aggressively seasoned (it will read milder after the fry since significant dredge falls off in the oil).
  4. 4
    Double dredge. Working one piece at a time, drop a buttermilk-wet chicken piece into the seasoned dredge. Toss to coat thickly, pressing the dredge into every crevice (under the skin, into the joint folds, around the bone ends). Lift out, shake off the loose dredge. Dip back into the buttermilk for 2 to 3 seconds, then back into the dredge for the second coat, pressing the dredge into the now-tacky buttermilk-wet flour layer. The double dredge produces a thicker, craggier crust with the small lumpy "knobs" that catch and crisp in the fry. Set on a wire rack. Repeat with all pieces; let the dredged chicken rest on the rack 15 minutes; the dredge hydrates from the buttermilk and bonds tightly to the chicken (skipping this rest makes the dredge slough off in the fry).
  5. 5
    Heat the oil. Pour 1200 g neutral oil into a heavy 30 cm / 12-inch cast-iron skillet to a depth of 2.5 cm / 1 inch. Clip a deep-fry or candy thermometer to the side of the pan so the tip is submerged in oil but not touching the bottom. Heat over medium-high until the oil reads 175°C / 350°F (the starting temperature; the oil drops to ~155°C / 310°F when cold chicken hits it, and you want to recover quickly to the 165°C / 330°F working temperature). Preheat the oven to 90°C / 200°F with a wire rack set over a sheet pan inside.
  6. 6
    Fry, batch 1 (dark meat). Carefully lay the 2 thighs and 2 drumsticks into the hot oil, skin-side down, pulling away from yourself to avoid the splatter. The oil should bubble aggressively and audibly the moment the chicken touches it; that is the cue the oil is hot enough. The temperature will drop; adjust the burner to maintain 160 to 170°C through the cook. Fry the first side undisturbed 6 to 8 minutes until the crust is deep golden-brown. Flip with tongs; fry second side 6 to 8 more minutes. Total cook for dark meat: 12 to 16 minutes. Check internal temperature in the thickest part of the thigh; the target is 75°C / 167°F.
  7. 7
    Transfer batch 1 to the oven. Lift the dark meat out of the oil with tongs; let any excess oil drip off back into the skillet for 3 seconds. Transfer to the wire rack in the 90°C oven. The oven holds the fried pieces hot and crisp while you cook the second batch; the wire rack lets the underside stay crispy rather than steaming on a paper towel.
  8. 8
    Fry, batch 2 (white meat). Bring the oil temperature back up to 175°C; this takes 1 to 2 minutes between batches. Lay the 4 half-breasts in the oil skin-side down. Fry 5 to 6 minutes per side (breasts cook faster than dark meat); pull when the internal temperature in the thickest part reads 71°C / 160°F. The breast will carry over to 73 to 74°C during the rest. Transfer to the wire rack in the oven with the dark meat.
  9. 9
    Rest and serve. Hold all the chicken on the wire rack in the warm oven for 5 to 8 minutes; the rest finishes the carryover cook and lets the juices redistribute through the meat. The crust should be a deep mahogany-gold, audibly crispy when tapped with a fork, and the meat juice should run clear (not pink) when the thickest part is pierced. Serve immediately on a platter with hot sauce and lemon wedges; the canonical Southern accompaniments are mashed potatoes with cream gravy, collard greens, and biscuits, but cold beer also works.
Safety
  • pulling chicken thighs at 75°C / 167°F and breasts at 71°C / 160°F USDA published-safe target is 74°C / 165°F across the bird. Thighs read structurally better at 75 to 80°C as the connective tissue softens; breasts are pulled at 71°C and carry over to 73 to 74°C during the rest, comfortably clearing the pasteurization equivalency table (150°F / 66°C held under 3 minutes achieves the same lethal log reduction; breasts at 71°C internal with the bone retaining heat for the 5-minute rest sit above this threshold for the entire rest period). Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→chicken wings (the Buffalo or fried-wing direction)

    Use 1500 g chicken wings split into flats and drumettes. Brine the same way 6 to 12 hours (wings need less brine time since they are smaller). Dredge the same way. Fry 8 to 10 minutes per batch at 175°C oil; the wings reach 75°C quickly and the crust crisps fast at the smaller surface-to-mass ratio. Toss in Buffalo sauce (60 g hot sauce whisked with 60 g melted unsalted butter) or serve as-is with hot sauce on the side.

  • Heat spice level→Nashville hot chicken

    Fry as written. Whisk together 60 g of the hot frying oil with 12 g cayenne, 8 g sweet paprika, 8 g light brown sugar, 4 g garlic powder, 4 g kosher salt. Brush the spiced oil onto the hot fried chicken immediately after it comes out of the skillet, coating every surface. Serve on white bread with sliced pickles. The dish is from Prince's Hot Chicken in Nashville (claimed 1930s); the post-fry hot oil dredge is the structural move.

  • Regional fat→lard-and-bacon-fat fry (the heritage Southern fat)

    Substitute rendered leaf lard plus 200 g bacon fat for the neutral oil (1000 g lard plus 200 g bacon fat). The chicken reads more deeply flavored, more porky, and recognizably distinct from the neutral-oil fry. This is how Edna Lewis and the heritage Southern cooks of the early 20th century fried; modern home cooks substitute peanut oil for convenience. Worth doing once.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • buttermilk → whole milk + lemon juice (acidified milk) Whisk 1000 ml whole milk with 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice or white vinegar; let stand 10 minutes until visibly curdled. The acidified milk replicates the lactic acid of buttermilk for tenderizing; the lactose content for browning is the same. Acceptable substitute; cultured buttermilk reads slightly more aromatic.
  • hot sauce (Crystal or Louisiana Gold) → Tabasco, Frank's RedHot, or any cayenne-based hot sauce Vinegar-and-aged-cayenne is the right profile. Sriracha is wrong (too sweet, garlic-front); habanero sauces are wrong direction (too fruity and too hot for a brine).
  • all-purpose flour + cornstarch (90/10 mix in the dredge) → all-purpose flour alone Acceptable; the crust reads slightly heavier and less crispy without the cornstarch fraction. The 80/20 flour-to-cornstarch ratio is the Korean fried chicken hack that improved American fried chicken in the 2010s; many Southern home cooks now use it.
  • peanut oil → vegetable oil, canola oil, refined lard, beef tallow Peanut oil is the canonical Southern fry oil (high smoke point ~230°C, mild flavor). Vegetable and canola work identically. Lard and tallow add deeper flavor but smoke at lower temperatures (about 190°C for lard), so calibrate the burner accordingly.

Notes

Shallow-fry, not deep-fry. Southern home fried chicken is shallow-fried in a cast-iron skillet with the oil reaching about halfway up the chicken pieces, then flipped to fry the second side. This is structurally different from deep-frying (full submersion) and produces a different crust: more textured, with visible "knobs" where the dredge crisped on the surface that broke above the oil line. Cast iron is the right pan because its thermal mass holds oil temperature steady when cold chicken hits the fat; thin pans crash the temperature and produce soggy crust.

The buttermilk brine does three things. (1) The salt penetrates through the meat over 12 to 24 hours, seasoning to the bone. (2) The lactic acid of cultured buttermilk hydrolyzes surface proteins through gentle proteolytic action, producing more tender meat without the mushy texture that strong acid (lemon, vinegar) brines cause. (3) The buttermilk coating on the chicken at the dredge step IS the adhesive that bonds the flour layer to the meat; without buttermilk on the surface, the dredge slides off in the fry. All three functions are why the buttermilk brine became canonical.

The double-dredge with rest. The two-layer dredge with a 15-minute hydration rest produces the thick, craggy crust that defines Southern fried chicken. The first dredge coats the buttermilk-wet chicken; the dip back into buttermilk re-wets the now-floury surface; the second dredge bonds to the tacky buttermilk-flour paste. The rest lets the dredge fully hydrate so it adheres rather than slumping off in the fry. Skipping the rest is the most common failure mode; the dredge falls off in the first 30 seconds of the fry and you end up with a thin, patchy crust.

Why cornstarch. The 80/20 all-purpose-to-cornstarch ratio is borrowed from Korean fried chicken technique (KFC, the cuisine, not the chain). Cornstarch granules gel differently than wheat starch when fried, producing a crispier, lighter, more shatteringly crispy crust. Modern Southern home cooks have widely adopted it; old-school recipes use all flour and read a touch heavier. Both are valid.

Oil temperature is the single biggest variable. Cold chicken hitting 175°C oil drops the temperature to about 155°C within 30 seconds. The recovery has to be quick; burner on medium-high, fry only 3 to 4 pieces at a time in a 30 cm skillet. Frying at 145°C produces soggy chicken with pale crust; frying at 195°C burns the crust before the meat cooks. Hold at 165°C (working temperature) by watching the thermometer constantly.

Why a 90°C oven hold. Resting fried chicken on paper towels steams the bottom crust soft within 2 minutes. The wire-rack-in-warm-oven approach keeps every surface dry and crispy while subsequent batches fry, and it lets the chicken carry over to safe internal temperature without going cold. Hold up to 30 minutes; longer than that and the crust begins to dry out from the oven heat.

Edna Lewis is the master. The Edna Lewis approach (Virginia, Freetown native, Café Nicholson chef) is the gold-standard reference: buttermilk brine, double dredge, lard fry, cast-iron skillet, rest on a wire rack, serve with country ham gravy. Her A Taste of Country Cooking is the canonical text. This recipe sits in her tradition with the modern cornstarch fraction in the dredge.

Day-2 fried chicken is the cold-plate revelation. Cold leftover fried chicken at the picnic, eaten with the fingers over a paper plate of potato salad and a glass of iced tea, is a Southern institution. The crust stays crispy in the fridge if stored on a wire rack uncovered. Refrigerate up to 3 days. Do not reheat in a microwave (steams the crust soft); reheat in a 175°C oven for 8 to 10 minutes on a wire rack if hot service is wanted.

Sources
  • American Southern home tradition (the cast-iron shallow-fry school) — Southern fried chicken descends from the West African technique of frying chicken in palm oil with a flour coating (the technique arrived with enslaved cooks); the Scottish settlers in Appalachia contributed the cast-iron pan and the lard fat. The dish was codified in American Southern home cooking by the late 19th century. Cast-iron shallow-fry (in 2.5 cm of fat) is the home-kitchen canonical method, distinct from the commercial deep-fry that defines chain-restaurant fried chicken.
