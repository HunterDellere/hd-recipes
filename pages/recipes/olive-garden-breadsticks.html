Recipe

Olive Garden breadsticks (the copycat)

Lightly enriched yeast dough with bread flour, butter, milk, and a small dose of sugar, shaped into 18 cm batons, proofed to pillow-soft, baked, then brushed with garlic butter and dusted with seasoned salt. The chain-restaurant breadstick reverse-engineered to the kitchen-correct version.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Total time is 2 hours 10 minutes, most of it passive proofing. Start at least 2.5 hours before service: a 60-minute bulk rise, a 30-minute shaped proof, and a 15-minute bake.
  • Warm the milk to exactly 35 C with a thermometer. Above 40 C kills the yeast, below 30 C the dough takes hours longer to rise.
  • Mix the garlic butter brush at the start of the bulk rise so the raw garlic has 60 minutes to mellow in warm butter. Cold-mixed garlic butter brushed on a hot breadstick reads sharp and harsh.

Phase 1, the dough

  1. 460 g bread flour
  2. 240 ml whole milk, warmed to 35 °C
  3. 7 g instant yeast
  4. 25 g granulated sugar
  5. 30 g unsalted butter, melted and cooled to lukewarm
  6. 8 g fine sea salt

Phase 2, the garlic butter brush

  1. 60 g unsalted butter, melted
  2. 6 g garlic, pressed or finely grated on a microplane — (2 cloves; raw garlic for sharper bite, pressed and rested 5 minutes to develop allicin before mixing into the butter)
  3. 3 g kosher salt
  4. 2 g garlic powder
  5. 1 g dried oregano
  6. 1 g dried parsley flakes

Execution

  1. 1
    Build the dough in a stand mixer or by hand. Combine the 240 ml warmed (35 °C) milk, 7 g instant yeast, and 25 g sugar in the bowl of a stand mixer; whisk briefly and let stand 5 minutes (the surface will foam slightly, confirming the yeast is alive, see ingredients/instant-yeast for the bloom-test reasoning). Add the 460 g bread flour, 30 g melted-cooled butter, and 8 g fine sea salt. Fit the dough hook; mix on low for 2 minutes until shaggy, then on medium for 8 minutes until the dough is smooth, elastic, and pulls cleanly from the sides of the bowl. By hand: combine all ingredients in a large bowl, turn out onto an unfloured surface, and knead 10 to 12 minutes until smooth and elastic. Enriched dough
  2. 2
    First rise (bulk fermentation). Transfer the dough to a lightly oiled bowl; cover with plastic wrap or a damp tea towel. Set in a warm spot (24 to 26 °C) and rise 60 to 90 minutes until roughly doubled in volume. The dough is correctly proofed when a finger-poke leaves a partial dent that springs back slowly. Underproofed: the dent fills immediately. Overproofed: the dent holds without springing back at all (shape and bake immediately at this point; the breadsticks will be slightly flatter but otherwise correct).
  3. 3
    Shape into 12 batons. Turn the dough out onto a clean unfloured surface (a wet surface helps prevent sticking; flour at this stage produces uneven shaping). Divide the dough into 12 equal portions of about 65 g each, use a kitchen scale; uneven portions produce uneven bake times. Roll each portion under your palm into a 18 cm baton, slightly thicker in the middle and tapering at the ends. The hand-rolling is structural, pin-rolling produces a flatter, less pillowy breadstick.
  4. 4
    Second rise (proof). Arrange the 12 batons on two parchment-lined sheet pans, 6 per pan, with at least 4 cm between each. Cover loosely with plastic wrap or a damp tea towel; rise at room temperature 30 to 40 minutes until visibly puffed but not fully doubled (overproofed breadsticks collapse on baking and produce a denser-than-intended crumb). The finger-poke test: a slow partial spring-back is correct.
  5. 5
    Bake. Position racks in the upper and lower thirds of the oven; preheat to 200 °C (400 °F) for at least 15 minutes before the breadsticks go in. Bake the breadsticks 12 to 15 minutes, swapping the pans top-to-bottom and rotating front-to-back at the 7-minute mark. The breadsticks are correctly baked when the tops are golden-brown, the bottoms are deep golden, and an internal-temperature probe reads 88 to 90 °C. Underbaking produces a gummy interior; overbaking produces a hard crust and dry crumb.
  6. 6
    Brush with the garlic butter while hot. While the breadsticks bake, prepare the brush: melt the 60 g butter in a small saucepan or microwave, then whisk in the 6 g pressed-and-rested garlic, 2 g garlic powder, 1 g dried oregano, and 1 g dried parsley flakes. The mixture should sit at room temperature 5 minutes before use to let the garlic flavor infuse the butter. As soon as the breadsticks come out of the oven, brush each one generously with the seasoned butter on all surfaces (top, sides, ends, the bottom is sufficient from the pan butter that pooled). Sprinkle the 3 g kosher salt over the tops while the butter is still wet so the salt grains stick.
  7. 7
    Serve hot. Olive Garden breadsticks are a hot-from-the-oven dish; the texture compromises noticeably within 10 minutes of cooling. Serve immediately in a bread basket lined with a cloth napkin (the napkin holds heat); pass alongside marinara sauce, alfredo sauce, or a bowl of soup. Cooled breadsticks reheat acceptably in a 175 °C oven for 4 minutes.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion occasion→cheesy garlic breadsticks

    Brush the breadsticks with the garlic butter as in the original; immediately top with 60 g shredded mozzarella plus 30 g grated parmigiano divided across the 12 breadsticks. Return to the oven for 2 minutes at 230 °C until the cheese melts and lightly browns. The dish becomes a different category (cheesy bread rather than breadstick); good companion to a tomato soup or a cup of bolognese.

  • Occasion flavor→sweet cinnamon-sugar breadsticks (dessert pivot)

    Skip the garlic butter; instead brush with 60 g melted butter and dust generously with a mixture of 50 g granulated sugar plus 5 g ground cinnamon plus a pinch of salt. The dish becomes a churro-adjacent dessert breadstick; serve warm with a small bowl of vanilla ice cream or a chocolate dipping sauce.

  • Occasion shape→rolls (the buns version)

    Divide the dough into 16 portions of about 50 g each instead of 12. Shape each into a tight ball; arrange in a 9x13 buttered baking dish with the balls touching. Proof and bake as in the original; brush with the same garlic butter on coming out of the oven. The dish becomes pull-apart dinner rolls, same dough, different presentation, suited to a holiday table.

  • Regional cuisine→olive oil and herbs (Mediterranean pivot)

    Replace the garlic butter brush with: 60 ml extra-virgin olive oil whisked with 2 cloves grated garlic, 2 g chopped fresh rosemary, 2 g flaky sea salt, and a quarter-teaspoon of dried oregano. The dish reads cleaner and more savory; pairs well with a Tuscan-style minestrone or a robust ragù.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • bread flour → all-purpose flour plus 30 g vital wheat gluten Mix the AP and vital wheat gluten thoroughly before combining with the wet ingredients. The breadsticks will read about 90 percent of the chew of the bread-flour version. Acceptable but the bread-flour upgrade is meaningful.
  • instant yeast → active dry yeast Use 9 g instead of 7 g; bloom in the warmed milk for 5 minutes before adding the rest of the ingredients. See ingredients/instant-yeast for the substitution math.
  • whole milk → water plus 30 g powdered milk Functional but the dish reads slightly less rich. The milk fat contributes to the soft crumb; the powdered milk approximates the milk solids without the fat.

Notes

Why bread flour. The chew of an Olive Garden breadstick is structural to the dish identity, a soft, pillowy interior with enough chew to be substantial. Bread flour at 12.5+ percent protein gives the gluten margin to hold the rise without collapsing under the relatively-high enrichment of butter and milk. AP flour produces a softer, less chewy result that reads as a dinner roll rather than a breadstick.

Why warm the milk. Yeast metabolizes fastest around 35 °C; cold milk straight from the refrigerator slows the bloom and lengthens the rise unnecessarily. The 35 °C temperature is hot to the touch but not uncomfortable to the wrist (similar to a hot bath). Above 40 °C the yeast cells start dying; verify the milk is correctly warm with a thermometer or by feel before adding the yeast.

The pressed-and-rested garlic in the butter. Crushing or pressing garlic and resting it 5 to 10 minutes before adding to the dish triggers the alliinase enzyme to convert the dormant alliin precursor into allicin, the structural sharp-garlic compound. Garlic added to butter immediately after pressing produces a softer, less defined garlic flavor; the rested garlic produces the sharp, pungent note that defines the chain breadstick. The same rest applies anywhere raw garlic is the flavor lever, bruschetta, vinaigrette, aioli.

The double-garlic technique. The chain breadstick uses both fresh garlic and dehydrated garlic powder in the brush. The reasoning is that the two forms cover different parts of the garlic flavor spectrum: fresh garlic provides sharpness and immediate bite; dehydrated garlic provides a slow-developing, almost-toasted background note that sits behind the fresh. Skipping either one produces a one-dimensional garlic flavor; using both produces the layered, distinctive Olive Garden profile.

Why brush hot. The breadsticks must come out of the oven within 30 seconds of brushing. The hot crust absorbs the seasoned butter into its outer 2 mm, integrating the garlic-and-salt flavor into the bread itself rather than sitting on top. Brushing cooled breadsticks produces a greasy surface with the flavor on top of the bread; brushing hot breadsticks produces a flavor-permeated breadstick that reads as a single integrated thing. The same applies to brioche, hot rolls, and any enriched bread that wants a finishing brush.

The freezer strategy. Shape the breadsticks fully, then freeze on the sheet pan; transfer to a freezer bag for up to 2 months. To bake from frozen: arrange on a parchment-lined sheet, cover with plastic wrap, and let proof at room temperature for 90 to 120 minutes (until visibly puffed). Bake as in the original; the breadsticks will read identical to fresh-shaped. The frozen-and-baked-direct workflow is the canonical home-kitchen way to keep Olive-Garden-style breadsticks on hand for any pasta night.

The marinara dipper. The chain pairs breadsticks with marinara as the canonical dip; the ragù alla bolognese in the same recipe collection of this site is too meaty for breadstick-dipping (the ragù wants pasta as a vehicle). A simple marinara, canned San Marzano tomatoes, garlic, olive oil, basil, salt, simmered 20 minutes and pureed smooth, is the right pairing here. Ratio: 250 ml marinara per 4 breadsticks for shared-table service.
