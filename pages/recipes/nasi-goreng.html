Recipe

Nasi goreng

Indonesian fried rice built on day-old jasmine rice, a bumbu of shallot, garlic, chili, and toasted terasi, and the structural defining ingredient: kecap manis, the thick palm-sugar-sweetened soy that lacquers each grain dark and caramel. Topped with a fried egg, fried shallots, sliced cucumber and tomato, and a wedge of lime.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Use day-old rice, refrigerated overnight uncovered. Freshly cooked rice is too wet; the grains stick together and the dish becomes a sticky mass. The cold-rest tightens the starch granules (retrogradation) and the grains stay separate in the wok. If you must use fresh rice, spread it on a sheet pan and cool 30 minutes in the fridge before frying.
  • Toast the terasi (3 g / ½ tsp wrapped in foil over a burner for 60 seconds per side) before pounding the bumbu. Raw terasi tastes harshly fishy in the finished dish.
  • Have everything chopped and within arm's reach of the wok before turning on the heat. Once the bumbu hits the pan, the dish is 6 minutes from done; stopping to dice an aromatic will burn the paste.
  • Cook the eggs in a separate pan in parallel, sunny-side-up or over-easy with crisp lacy edges. The egg is structural to the dish (the runny yolk dresses the rice on the plate); do not skip it.

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, bumbu spice paste

  1. 80 g shallot, peeled, rough chopped — (about 4 medium shallots)
  2. 25 g garlic, peeled — (about 6 cloves)
  3. 30 g fresh red chili (Holland or Fresno), stems pulled, seeds left for heat — (2 to 3 chilies; use bird-eye for more heat, halve the quantity)
  4. 3 g toasted terasi

Phase 2, the rice base

  1. 700 g jasmine rice, cooked and refrigerated overnight, broken up with wet fingers into individual grains
  2. 45 g kecap manis
  3. 15 g light soy sauce
  4. 2 g kosher salt
  5. 30 g neutral oil for the wok

Phase 3, protein

  1. 200 g shrimp, peeled and deveined, patted dry, halved if large optional
  2. 2 each eggs, for the bumbu, beaten lightly with a fork — (2 eggs scrambled into the rice; separate from the 4 fried-egg toppers below)

Phase 4, vegetables

  1. 40 g scallion, white and green parts, sliced 5 mm on a bias — (4 scallions)
  2. 80 g napa cabbage or Chinese cabbage, shredded into ribbons 5 mm wide optional

Phase 5, topping and garnish

  1. 4 each eggs, for the fried-egg topper — (1 per portion, fried sunny-side-up in a separate pan, lacy crisp edges, runny yolk)
  2. 20 g fried shallots (crispy)
  3. 100 g cucumber, sliced thin, sliced into 3 mm rounds — (about ½ English cucumber; the cooling foil to the spice-and-caramel rice)
  4. 100 g tomato, sliced thin, sliced into 3 mm rounds — (1 medium tomato)
  5. 1 each lime, cut into wedges, cut into 4 wedges — (squeezed over each plate at the table)
  6. 30 g sambal oelek (or sambal terasi)

Execution

  1. 1
    Pound or blend the 80 g shallot, 25 g garlic, 30 g chili, and 3 g toasted terasi to a coarse wet paste, about 2 minutes in a food processor or 4 minutes in a mortar. The texture should be slightly chunky, not smooth puree; you want some shallot bite remaining in the finished dish.
  2. 2
    Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide wok or 30 cm / 12 inch skillet over medium-high heat until shimmering. Add the bumbu paste; it will sputter. Fry, stirring constantly, until the raw shallot-chili smell shifts to a deeper aromatic and the paste darkens from bright orange-pink to deep brick-orange, about 3 minutes. Oil will visibly separate around the edges of the paste.
  3. 3
    If using shrimp: push the bumbu to one side of the pan, add the 200 g shrimp to the bare side, sear 60 seconds per side until just turning opaque. Toss with the bumbu and remove to a plate; the shrimp finishes cooking in the residual heat. If using chicken instead, dice fine and cook 3 minutes until opaque, then remove.
  4. 4
    Crack the 2 beaten eggs directly into the same pan with the remaining bumbu. Stir vigorously for 30 seconds to scramble; the eggs cook into soft curds coated in spice paste. Do not separate from the bumbu.
  5. 5
    Add the 700 g cold day-old rice, breaking up any remaining clumps with your hands or a wide spatula as you add it. Toss aggressively with two spatulas or wok tools for 90 seconds to coat every grain in the bumbu-egg mixture and to drive off surface moisture. The rice should look pale-orange and individually grained at this point.
  6. 6
    Drizzle the 45 g / 3 tbsp kecap manis and the 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy sauce directly over the rice. Toss for 2 to 3 minutes, scraping the bottom of the pan continuously; the kecap manis caramelizes onto the grains and the rice darkens to a deep mahogany. This is the structural color and flavor moment of the dish, the high sugar content of the kecap manis browns aggressively on direct heat. Add the 80 g shredded cabbage (if using) in the last 60 seconds to wilt just slightly.
  7. 7
    Return the cooked shrimp (or chicken) to the pan. Add the 40 g sliced scallion and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. Toss 30 seconds to combine and warm through. Taste, the dish should read sweet-savory-front (kecap manis), with the chili heat building after, scallion-and-shallot in the background. If flat, add 5 g / 1 tsp more kecap manis; if too sweet, 5 g / 1 tsp more light soy or fish sauce.
  8. 8
    While the rice finishes, fry the 4 topper eggs in a separate skillet. Heat 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil over medium-high until shimmering. Crack the eggs in one at a time, leaving space; cook 90 seconds without moving until the whites are set with crisp lacy edges and the yolk is still bright and runny. Salt the whites with a pinch as they cook.
  9. 9
    Plate. Mound a portion of nasi goreng on each plate, top with one fried egg (yolk facing up, ready to be broken into the rice), scatter a heavy pinch of fried shallots over the egg, arrange a few cucumber and tomato slices alongside, and place a lime wedge and a small mound of sambal on the side. Serve immediately. The diner breaks the egg yolk into the rice, squeezes the lime over, and adds sambal to taste.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→nasi goreng ayam (chicken)

    Substitute 250 g diced chicken thigh for the shrimp. Cook 3 to 4 minutes longer than shrimp; brown the chicken surfaces lightly before tossing with the bumbu. The chicken absorbs the bumbu flavor more deeply than shrimp does.

  • Protein protein→nasi goreng kambing (lamb, Jakarta street style)

    Substitute 300 g lamb shoulder, cut into 1 cm dice, for the shrimp. Pre-cook the lamb in a separate pan with a pinch of cumin and coriander for 5 minutes before adding to the bumbu. The lamb version is the late-night Jakarta street-vendor canonical, served from carts that specialize in the lamb-fat-richer take.

  • Regional cuisine→nasi goreng pattaya (Indonesian-Malaysian)

    Skip the topper egg. Instead, after plating the rice in a low mound, drape a thin omelet (2 eggs beaten with a pinch of salt, cooked into a flat crepe) over the entire mound, tucking the edges underneath like a blanket. Top with sambal and scallion. The Malaysian peninsula variant common in Singapore as well.

  • Occasion occasion→nasi goreng for breakfast

    Skip the shrimp and the cabbage. Halve the chili. Top with two fried eggs per portion instead of one. Serve with a side of acar (Indonesian quick pickle of cucumber and shallot in vinegar) instead of the raw cucumber and tomato. The breakfast version is lighter and more egg-forward; common across Indonesian households.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • jasmine rice → long-grain white rice (basmati or generic) Jasmine has the right balance of starchiness and grain separation for fried rice; basmati is slightly drier and produces a less-glossy finished dish; generic long-grain works but is one-dimensional. Avoid short-grain or sushi rice, too sticky.
  • kecap manis → dark soy sauce + palm sugar + molasses (3:2:1 by weight, whisked and warmed) Approximates viscosity and flavor. Real kecap manis has fermentation depth that quick blends miss; acceptable in a pinch.
  • shrimp → omit (vegetarian version) A vegetarian nasi goreng is legitimate; add diced firm tofu (200 g, pan-fried first) in place of the shrimp for protein, or simply omit and bulk the cabbage. Keep the bumbu egg for richness.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → fried shallots (crispy) make it yourself Store-bought crispy shallots are usually fried in palm oil and taste stale within weeks of opening. Freshly fried (5 minutes of active work) tastes dramatically sweeter and more aromatic, and the crisp lasts longer because the oil is fresher.
  • → sambal oelek (or sambal terasi) make it yourself Fresh-made sambal has bright chili character that store-bought jarred versions lose within months. Takes 15 minutes and keeps two weeks.

Notes

Why day-old rice. Freshly cooked rice carries roughly 65 to 70 percent water; the grains are soft, sticky, and clump when stirred in a hot pan. Refrigerating cooked rice for 8 to 24 hours triggers starch retrogradation, the amylose molecules recrystallize into a tighter structure that holds less water and resists clumping when reheated. The result is grains that stay separate in the wok, fry crisp on their exteriors, and absorb the kecap manis caramelization without going to a sticky paste. If you must use freshly cooked rice, spread it in a single layer on a sheet pan and cool 30 minutes in the fridge before frying; the partial retrogradation is enough.

Why the kecap manis matters more than the soy. The defining flavor and color of nasi goreng comes from kecap manis, the thick palm-sugar-sweetened Indonesian soy sauce. A nasi goreng made with regular soy sauce (light or dark) and no kecap manis is functionally a different dish, closer to Chinese fried rice; the high-sugar caramelization that produces the mahogany color and the sweet-savory-front flavor signature requires the palm sugar content. If you only have one Indonesian condiment in the cupboard, kecap manis is the one.

Why toast the terasi. Raw terasi (fermented shrimp paste) smells aggressively fishy and ammonia-tinged; the toast (60 seconds per side in foil over an open flame) transforms it into a deep, savory-funky umami that integrates seamlessly into the bumbu. Skipping the toast is the single most common reason home-cooked nasi goreng tastes raw and one-dimensional; the terasi never fully cooks out during the brief stir-fry phase.

Why the bumbu fry takes 3 minutes. The wet aromatic paste has to lose its raw edge before the rice goes in. Underdone bumbu produces a dish where the chili reads harsh, the shallot tastes oniony, and the terasi stays funky-raw. The 3-minute fry develops Maillard products on the shallot-garlic-chili matrix, blooms the spice-paste oils, and reduces the water content of the paste so the rice does not steam when it hits the pan.

The egg architecture. There are two distinct egg moments in this dish. The scrambled-into-the-bumbu egg (step 4) is structural; it coats the grains with a thin egg-and-bumbu film that gives the rice its glossy, slightly-rich mouthfeel. The fried topper egg (step 8) is finishing; the runny yolk breaks over the rice at the table and dresses each bite with a yolk-kecap-manis emulsion. Both eggs are non-negotiable for the canonical presentation; the scrambled egg cannot replace the topper, and the topper cannot replace the scrambled.

Why the cucumber and tomato. The raw vegetable garnish is the cooling foil to the spice-and-caramel weight of the rice. It is not decoration; eating a slice of cool cucumber between bites of nasi goreng is the canonical Indonesian eating rhythm, the way Vietnamese diners alternate pho bites with bites of herbs from the plate. Skipping the cucumber and tomato makes the dish feel heavier and one-noted.

Sources
  • Adapted from Javanese home-cook canon — Nasi goreng exists across the Indonesian archipelago in regional variants. The Javanese version (this one) is the most-recognized, built on kecap manis as the structural sweet-soy backbone. Sumatran nasi goreng leans spicier and uses more chili; Balinese nasi goreng often includes sambal matah on the side. The fried egg, fried shallots, cucumber, tomato, and lime garnish is the canonical national-level presentation, the version any Indonesian warung will serve.
