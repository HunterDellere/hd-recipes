Recipe

Salsa macha

The Veracruzan fried-chili-and-nut-and-seed oil sauce: dried chiles (a mix of morita, chile de árbol, and ancho or pasilla), garlic, peanuts, sesame seeds, and pepitas, fried briefly in warm neutral oil and ground to a coarse paste, with vinegar, salt, and a small amount of sugar for balance. Structurally an oil-suspended chili paste with crunchy textural ingredients, served as a condiment with everything from tacos and grilled meat to eggs, beans, soups, cheese, and roasted vegetables. The most cuisine-flexible Mexican salsa and the only one that travels naturally across non-Mexican applications.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The 25-minute cook is fast, but the salsa needs a 2-hour rest at room temperature before first use for the chili, nut, and oil flavors to integrate. Plan to make it the morning of (or the day before) you want to use it.
  • Oil temperature is the single most important variable. Use a probe thermometer to hold the oil at 130 to 150 °C; above that, the chilies and garlic scorch in seconds and the entire batch reads bitter. A sesame-seed test (one seed should dance, not burst) is the manual fallback.
  • Source dried morita, chile de árbol, and ancho or pasilla from a Mexican grocery or online. Generic crushed red pepper is not a substitute, the chili identity is the entire dish.
  • Have a clean dry glass jar (about 400 ml) ready before starting. The hot salsa goes in directly after grinding and stores refrigerated up to a month if the jar is contamination-free.

The fry oil

  1. 250 g neutral oil (grapeseed, sunflower, or canola)

The chilies

  1. 15 g dried morita chilies (smoked jalapeño), stems removed, seeds left in for heat (or partially removed for milder); torn into rough pieces — (about 4 to 5 morita chilies; smoked jalapeño, deep smoky flavor with medium heat; chipotle is an acceptable substitute though slightly larger and more aggressive)
  2. 8 g dried chile de árbol (or dried Thai chili), stems removed, seeds left in; left whole or torn — (about 10 to 12 chiles de árbol; bright clean heat without smoke; the structural heat source in salsa macha)
  3. 12 g dried ancho chili (or pasilla), stems and seeds removed; torn into rough pieces — (1 large ancho chili; mild, sweet, raisin-like depth that balances the heat from the árbol and the smoke from the morita)

The aromatics, nuts, and seeds

  1. 20 g garlic clove, peeled, thinly sliced (not minced; the sliced cloves fry more evenly without burning) — (4 to 5 medium cloves; the fried garlic is structurally a flavor pillar of salsa macha and should be visible in the finished paste)
  2. 50 g raw unsalted peanuts
  3. 20 g raw pepitas (pumpkin seeds)
  4. 20 g white sesame seeds

The balance

  1. 20 g apple cider vinegar
  2. 6 g fine sea salt
  3. 5 g light brown sugar (or piloncillo if available) — )

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the 250 g / 1 cup plus 2 tbsp neutral oil in a medium saucepan over medium-low heat until just shimmering and 130 to 150°C / 270 to 300°F (a sesame seed dropped in should sizzle gently and dance, not burst; if the oil is too hot the chilies and aromatics burn instantly and the salsa goes bitter). The oil temperature control is the single most important variable in salsa macha; warm oil produces deep clean flavor, hot oil produces burnt bitter sauce. Use a thermometer or test with a single sesame seed before adding the rest of the ingredients.
  2. 2
    Add the 20 g / 4 to 5 sliced garlic cloves to the warm oil; fry gently, stirring with a wooden spoon, for 90 seconds to 2 minutes until the garlic turns a light golden color and just begins to crisp at the edges. Pull the pan off the heat the moment the garlic reads pale gold; garlic continues cooking in the residual oil heat and goes from golden to burnt in 30 seconds. Burnt garlic ruins the salsa and there is no rescue; pull early.
  3. 3
    Return the pan to medium-low heat. Add the 50 g / ⅓ cup peanuts; fry 90 seconds, stirring constantly, until the peanuts are a shade darker and aromatic. Add the 20 g / 3 tbsp pepitas and 20 g / 3 tbsp sesame seeds; fry 60 seconds, stirring, until the sesame seeds turn light golden and start to pop and the pepitas puff slightly.
  4. 4
    Add the 15 g / 4 to 5 morita chilies, 8 g / 10 to 12 chiles de árbol, and 12 g / 1 large ancho chili (torn into pieces); fry only 30 to 45 seconds, stirring constantly, just until the chilies darken slightly and release a deep aromatic smoke. Do not over-fry the chilies; the dried chilies absorb the warm oil quickly and burn from the inside, and burnt chilies make the entire batch bitter. Pull the pan off the heat the moment the chilies smell aggressively aromatic; the residual oil heat continues the cook.
  5. 5
    Let the oil-and-ingredients mixture cool 10 minutes off the heat; the residual heat continues to extract flavor into the oil during the cool-down (a brief infusion step that is structural for depth). Pour the entire mixture into the bowl of a food processor (5 to 7 cup capacity); add the 6 g / 1 tsp salt and 5 g / 1 tsp brown sugar. Pulse 10 to 12 times in 1-second bursts to a coarse-medium grind; the texture should preserve visible bits of chili, garlic, peanut, pepita, and sesame (a chunky oil-suspended sauce, not a smooth puree). Add the 20 g / 4 tsp apple cider vinegar; pulse 3 to 4 more times to integrate.
  6. 6
    Taste and adjust: salsa macha should read deeply chili-smoky-fried with crunchy nut-and-seed texture, balanced acid from the vinegar, gentle sweetness from the brown sugar, and a long warm heat at the back; if flat, an extra 1 g / pinch of fine sea salt and 5 g / 1 tsp apple cider vinegar; if too acidic, more sugar. Transfer to a clean glass jar (about 400 ml capacity); the salsa is meant to have visible oil pooled on top with the chunky paste below. Let rest at room temperature 2 hours before first use for the flavors to integrate fully; refrigerate after the rest period. Use as a condiment for tacos, eggs (especially fried or scrambled), grilled meat (steak, chicken, pork, lamb), grilled or roasted vegetables, beans (canonical with stewed black beans and pinto beans), soups (a spoonful stirred into a bowl of bean or tomato soup), cheese (on quesadillas, grilled cheese, queso fresco, or just spooned over a wedge of feta), and avocado.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion flavor pivot→as a salsa macha with almonds and a brighter profile

    Replace the peanuts with 60 g raw almonds (slivered or whole, lightly chopped); add 5 g / 1 tbsp dried Mexican oregano with the chilies; add 15 g / 1 tbsp lime juice in addition to the apple cider vinegar (off-heat, with the vinegar). The result reads brighter, more aromatic, and slightly more Mediterranean-leaning; pairs especially well with grilled fish and shellfish, lighter applications where the deep smoky peanut version would dominate.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as a salsa macha-chili crisp hybrid (the modern globalized form)

    Add 10 g / 2 tbsp Sichuan peppercorns to the oil with the garlic (fry 30 seconds then strain out before adding the other aromatics, or leave in for the structural numbing character); add 15 g / 2 tbsp dried mushroom powder (porcini or shiitake) to the food processor with the salt for added umami; replace the apple cider vinegar with 15 g / 1 tbsp rice vinegar. The result reads as a salsa macha and Chinese chili crisp hybrid; the structural shared logic (fried chilies, garlic, aromatic seeds, in oil) means the fusion works cleanly. Pairs especially well with rice bowls, noodles, dumplings, and any application where chili crisp and salsa macha would both be valid.

  • Heat heat level→as a mild salsa macha (for cooks who prefer less heat)

    Remove all seeds from the chiles de árbol before frying; reduce the chiles de árbol to 4 g (instead of 8); double the ancho or pasilla to 24 g. The result reads deeply chili-flavored and structurally complete but without the aggressive heat backbone; a useful entry point for cooks new to dried-chili condiments and a brilliant background flavor where heat would dominate.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dried morita chilies (smoked jalapeño) → dried chipotle chilies (in adobo or dry) Moritas and chipotles are both smoked jalapeños; the difference is the smoke duration and the moisture level (moritas are smoked shorter and stay slightly moister; chipotles are smoked longer and dry). Chipotle is widely available in North American supermarkets (often in adobo sauce in cans; drain and pat dry before using) and produces a faithful smoky harissa with a slightly more aggressive smoke than morita.
  • raw unsalted peanuts → raw almonds, raw cashews, or a blend Peanuts are the canonical Veracruzan choice and the most widely used; almonds read brighter and more Mediterranean (see modifications); cashews read creamier and the resulting salsa is denser and less crunchy. All are acceptable; the peanut version is the reference point.
  • apple cider vinegar → white vinegar, rice vinegar, or lime juice Apple cider vinegar is the most common modern Mexican choice; white vinegar reads sharper and is canonical in some Veracruzan preparations; rice vinegar reads softer and is useful in the chili-crisp hybrid version (see modifications); lime juice (added off-heat) reads brighter and is canonical in some northern Mexican preparations. Use the same 20 g amount for any.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → raw unsalted peanuts make it yourself Home-roasted peanuts (raw peanuts, deep oven roast) read fundamentally fresher and more aromatic than supermarket roasted peanuts; for salsa macha where the peanut character is one of the structural flavor pillars, the difference is clearly tasted in the finished result.
  • → apple cider vinegar make it yourself Home-fermented apple cider vinegar (a long apple-and-water-and-mother fermentation) reads dramatically cleaner and more complex than industrial pasteurized vinegar; the unfiltered live vinegar brings a structural depth to any application where the vinegar is the dominant acid.

Notes

The oil temperature is the single most important variable in salsa macha and the most common point of failure for first-time cooks. The traditional Veracruzan technique uses warm oil (130 to 150°C / 270 to 300°F), not hot oil; the temperature is just enough to gently fry the aromatics and extract their flavor into the oil over a few minutes, without burning the chilies or the spices. Hot oil (180°C and above) burns the chilies and garlic in under 30 seconds, producing a bitter scorched flavor that no amount of vinegar or sugar can rescue; the entire batch has to be discarded. Use a thermometer for the first few times you make this; the visual cues (gentle shimmer, a sesame seed that dances rather than bursts) become reliable after experience.

The fry order matters. Garlic first (because it needs the longest fry time and is the most heat-sensitive); then peanuts (which need a medium fry to deepen the toast); then the smaller pepitas and sesame seeds (which fry quickly); finally the chilies (which need only 30 to 45 seconds because they are the smallest and absorb the oil heat fastest). The wrong order (chilies first, then peanuts) produces burnt chilies and undertoasted nuts; the canonical sequence builds each ingredient at its appropriate temperature.

The texture is structural. Salsa macha is not a smooth sauce and not a chunky chip-and-dip salsa; it sits between the two as a coarse oil-suspended paste with visible textural ingredients. Pulse the food processor in short bursts and stop while there is still visible structure in the chilies, peanuts, pepitas, and sesame; the texture is what distinguishes salsa macha from a generic chili paste. Some Veracruzan households use a mortar instead of a processor, which produces an even chunkier and more rustic texture; both are correct, with the processor being faster and the mortar being more traditional.

The acid-and-sugar balance is structural. Salsa macha without vinegar reads heavy and one-dimensional; salsa macha without sugar reads sharp and unrounded. The small amounts of both (20 g vinegar, 5 g sugar) are not optional; they balance the deep fried chili and nut flavors and give the salsa its characteristic depth and length. The piloncillo (raw cane sugar in cone form) is the canonical Mexican sugar choice and reads more caramel-molasses than light brown sugar; if you can find it, use it.

Versatility. Salsa macha is one of the most cuisine-flexible sauces in the global pantry, and the only Mexican salsa that travels naturally across non-Mexican applications. Use it as: a condiment for tacos of all kinds (carnitas, al pastor, barbacoa, fish, vegetable; a small spoonful on top of each taco brings smoky chili depth without watering down the filling); a topping for eggs (fried, scrambled, poached; a teaspoon on a fried egg over rice is one of the most successful breakfast pairings in modern Mexican cooking); a finish for grilled meat (steak, chicken, pork, lamb); a finish for grilled or roasted vegetables (sweet potato, cauliflower, broccoli, eggplant, carrots, Brussels sprouts; the smoky chili oil crusts on the surface beautifully); a topping for beans (the canonical pairing with stewed black beans and pinto beans, especially as a finish on a bowl of beans with rice); a stir-in for soups (a teaspoon stirred into a bowl of bean, tomato, vegetable, or even ramen brings smoky depth); a topping for cheese (queso fresco, feta, fresh mozzarella, ricotta; the chili oil over a wedge of cheese is a brilliant snack); a finish for avocado (over avocado toast, sliced avocado, or guacamole); a flavor base for marinades (mix 30 g salsa macha with 30 g oil and 15 g lime juice as a marinade for chicken or shrimp); a topping for pizza (in place of or in addition to chili oil); a sauce for grain bowls and rice bowls (a Mexican alternative to chili crisp); a topping for ramen and noodle bowls (the chili-crisp-hybrid version, see modifications); and as a finish for sandwiches, especially grilled cheese, breakfast sandwiches, and torta-style Mexican sandwiches.

Pairs across Mexican (canonical), Tex-Mex, broader Central American, modern American (where salsa macha has been deeply integrated into contemporary cooking through restaurants and commercial brands like Tia Lupita and El Yucateco), contemporary fusion (the salsa macha-chili crisp hybrid is the structural bridge between Mexican and Chinese pantry cookery), and modern European cooking (where chefs use salsa macha as the structural chili condiment on the modern Mediterranean and contemporary Italian table).

Storage. Keeps 3 to 4 weeks refrigerated in a sealed glass jar; the oil layer on top should be maintained (refresh with a tablespoon of neutral oil after a few scoops have pulled the level down). The salsa thickens significantly in the cold (the fried particles settle and the oil firms); stir before serving to redistribute. Do not freeze; the texture is acceptable on thawing but the freshness fades.

  • The Veracruzan canon (the eastern Mexican Gulf coast state, with roots in the Orizaba and Córdoba highlands and in the Mexican-Lebanese diaspora of the late 19th century, which is the structural reason salsa macha shares architectural DNA with the chili oils of the broader Levant). Documented in the modern stabilized form (dried chilies, garlic, nuts, seeds, fried in neutral oil, ground to a coarse paste, balanced with vinegar) across late 20th-century Mexican cookbooks; the dish has become an international phenomenon over the past decade through restaurants like Pujol (Mexico City) and through commercial brands. — Salsa macha is structurally distinct from the broader Mexican salsa tradition (raw or roasted-tomato-based pourable sauces) because it is oil-based rather than water-based; this gives it the cross-cuisine flexibility that the water-based salsas lack (an oil-based condiment travels onto applications where a water-based one would dilute). The traditional Veracruzan preparation is mortar-ground and oily; the modern food-processor preparation is faster and produces a slightly more uniform texture. Both are correct. The dish varies meaningfully by household: chili blend (morita-and-árbol is the most common modern reference; pure ancho or pasilla produces a milder smokier version), nut blend (peanut-only, almond-only, mixed; some versions use sunflower seeds), and acid (apple cider vinegar is the most common modern reference; some use white vinegar, others use lime juice off-heat).
