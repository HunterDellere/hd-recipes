Korean spicy cucumber salad (oi-muchim, 오이무침)

Sliced Korean or Persian cucumbers salted briefly, then tossed with gochugaru, garlic, fish sauce, rice vinegar, and toasted sesame oil. The fastest Korean banchan: 10 minutes from cucumber to bowl, eaten the same hour for peak crunch.

Mise en place by phase 2 phases ▾

Vegetables

  1. 500 g Korean or Persian cucumbers, sliced into 3 mm half-moons — (2 to 3 Korean cucumbers or 5 to 6 Persian; thin-walled varieties only)
  2. 6 g kosher salt (for the salt cure)

Dressing

  1. 8 g gochugaru (Korean chili flakes, coarse)
  2. 10 ml fish sauce
  3. 15 ml rice vinegar
  4. 4 g granulated sugar
  5. 10 g garlic, finely grated — (3 medium cloves; Korean cooking uses garlic in quantity)
  6. 10 ml toasted sesame oil
  7. 20 g scallion, thinly sliced — (2 scallions; whites and greens both)
  8. 4 g toasted sesame seeds

Execution

  1. 1
    Slice and salt-cure the cucumbers. Slice the 500 g cucumbers into 3 mm half-moons (rounds halved lengthwise first, then cut crosswise). Toss with 6 g / 1 tsp kosher salt in a colander set over a bowl. Let sit 15 minutes. Osmotic water leaves the cells, the cucumbers lose 10 to 15% of their weight, and what remains has dense flavor and stays crunchy under the dressing instead of weeping water into it. Dry brining
  2. 2
    Mix the dressing. In a small bowl, combine 8 g / 1 tbsp gochugaru, 10 ml / 2 tsp fish sauce, 15 ml / 1 tbsp rice vinegar, 4 g / 1 tsp sugar, and 10 g grated garlic. Whisk until the gochugaru hydrates into a loose paste and the sugar dissolves. Wait 2 minutes; the gochugaru blooms in the aqueous mixture and turns from flake-dry to brick-red and glossy.
  3. 3
    Press out remaining water. Lift the salted cucumbers in handfuls and gently squeeze over the colander to expel surface water. The cucumber half-moons should feel firm and translucent at the rim, not soggy. Do not rinse; the surface salt is part of the seasoning.
  4. 4
    Dress. Tip the squeezed cucumbers into a serving bowl. Pour the gochugaru dressing over and toss with chopsticks or a silicone spatula until every piece is coated red. Add 10 ml / 2 tsp toasted sesame oil last and toss again, the oil should suspend in a thin film over the aqueous dressing rather than mix in fully. Adding sesame oil before the aqueous dressing causes the oil to coat the cucumbers and block the seasoning from adhering.
  5. 5
    Finish. Scatter 20 g sliced scallion and 4 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame seeds over the top. Serve immediately or within 30 minutes. Taste; if flat, a few drops more fish sauce. If too sharp, a 1 g pinch more sugar.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion cucumber variety→with Kirby or American slicing cucumbers

    If only thick-skinned slicing cucumbers are available, peel them in stripes (alternating peeled and unpeeled bands), halve lengthwise, scrape out the seed core with a spoon, and slice into 4 mm half-moons. Salt-cure as written but extend to 20 minutes. The dish reads close to the Korean cucumber version; thicker, less crunchy. English cucumbers also work and need no peeling.

  • Heat heat level→milder for children or non-Korean palates

    Reduce gochugaru to 4 g (½ tbsp) and skip the garlic step or halve it. The dish reads sweet-and-savory rather than spicy and remains identifiably Korean. To return some color without heat, add a pinch of paprika.

  • Heat heat level→aggressively spicy version

    Increase gochugaru to 15 g (2 tbsp) and add 1 finely chopped Korean green chili (cheong-gochu) or 1 thinly sliced Thai chili. The dish becomes anju (drinking-food) territory.

  • Occasion serving format→as the cold side for grilled meat (KBBQ)

    Prepare as written but mound into individual small bowls (1 small bowl per diner) and serve alongside grilled bulgogi, samgyeopsal, or galbi. The acid and heat of the cucumbers cut through the rendered fat of the grilled meat. This is the standard banchan format at a Korean barbecue table.

  • Protein add fish→with dried anchovies (myeolchi)

    Toast 20 g small dried anchovies (myeolchi) in a dry pan over medium heat for 90 seconds until fragrant. Fold into the finished salad with the scallion. The anchovies add salinity and a deeper umami floor; the dish reads more like a stand-alone main rather than a banchan side. Serves 3 as a small main.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • gochugaru → Aleppo pepper or coarse Sichuan chili powder Both are imperfect substitutes. Aleppo pepper is the closest match in heat profile (mild to medium, rounded) but lacks the deep red color. Sichuan chili powder is similar in color but more aromatic. Korean cooking specifically; the rounded warm heat is the gochugaru signature. 1:1 by weight.
  • fish sauce → salted-shrimp paste (saeujeot, 새우젓) drained and minced, or light soy sauce Saeujeot is the canonical Korean alternative and is more traditional in some regional muchim recipes; use 1 tsp drained and minced for 2 tsp fish sauce. Light soy sauce works as a vegan substitute but the dish reads less complex.
  • rice vinegar → apple cider vinegar or distilled white vinegar reduced to 10 ml Rice vinegar is mild (4 to 5% acetic acid); apple cider is similar acid level and works 1:1. Distilled white vinegar is 5 to 7% acidity and needs reducing to about ⅔ the volume to avoid over-acidifying.
  • toasted sesame oil → no good substitute Untoasted sesame oil lacks the roasted-aromatic compounds (sotolon, sesamol breakdown products) that define the finishing note; any other neutral oil reads flat. Korean grocery cold-pressed sesame oil is the gold standard; Kadoya (Japanese-style) is the most widely available acceptable option.

Notes

Why the salt cure matters more than the dressing. Oi-muchim that goes into the bowl without the salt step is a different dish: the gochugaru, fish sauce, vinegar, and sugar are aqueous, and the moment they hit raw cucumber the cucumber begins to leak water into the dressing. Within 5 minutes the dish is a soup of pink water with limp cucumbers floating in it. The salt cure pulls 10 to 15% of the cucumber weight as osmotic water before dressing; the resulting cucumbers are denser, more flavorful, and stable in the dressing for the 30 to 60 minutes they will sit on the table. Skip the salt cure only if eating within 2 minutes of dressing.

Gochugaru bloom in the aqueous mix. The 2-minute wait after combining gochugaru with fish sauce, vinegar, and sugar is structural; gochugaru is dehydrated, and its color and flavor compounds need water to migrate into the surrounding liquid. Adding gochugaru directly to cucumbers without pre-blooming leaves dry flakes coating the cucumber surface, the color is paler, and the heat reads as a one-note burn rather than a rounded depth. The bloom step is also why this dressing is best mixed in a small bowl rather than tossed directly in the serving vessel.

Why oil goes last. Toasted sesame oil has a much higher viscosity than the aqueous components and will coat any surface it touches before the water-based seasonings can adhere. Adding sesame oil to the cucumbers first means the gochugaru-fish-sauce-vinegar mixture slides off the oil-coated cucumber surface. Add the aqueous dressing first, toss, then drizzle the sesame oil last; the oil suspends in a film over the dressed cucumbers and contributes its aromatic top note without blocking the structural seasoning.

Eating mechanics. Oi-muchim is one of the standard banchan in any Korean meal: alongside rice, soup, a protein dish, and 2 to 5 other banchan (kimchi, namul, japchae, jeon). It is not eaten as a salad in the Western sense (large bowl, fork, single course); each diner takes a few half-moons with chopsticks onto rice or onto a bite of grilled meat. The dish is also a staple at Korean barbecue restaurants as one of the side dishes that accompany the grilled meat. Eat within the hour; the dish loses crunch fast, and even with the salt cure, the cucumbers continue to soften over time.

Anti-pattern: pre-made oi-muchim from the banchan counter. Korean grocery banchan counters sell oi-muchim by the pound. The mass-market version is heavily soaked in dressing, often days old, and has lost all crunch; the cucumbers are limp and the dressing is dilute. Made at home, eaten within the hour, this is one of the most refreshing Korean dishes in the canon. The 10-minute active time is well spent.

  • Korean banchan tradition — Oi-muchim (오이무침, "seasoned cucumber") is the cucumber expression of the muchim family: vegetables seasoned raw with chili, garlic, sesame, and a liquid (vinegar, fish sauce, or soy).
