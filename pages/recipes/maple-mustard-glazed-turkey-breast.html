Maple and mustard glazed turkey breast

Bone-in turkey breast dry-brined overnight, roasted with herb butter under the skin, and lacquered with a maple-mustard glaze at the end.

Turkey + dry brine

  1. 2700 g bone-in turkey breast, skin-on
  2. 22 g kosher salt

  4. 1 g smoked paprika

Under-skin butter

  1. 60 g unsalted butter, softened
  2. 4 g fresh sage, finely minced
  3. 3 g fresh thyme leaves
  4. 2 each garlic, grated to a paste — (2 cloves)

Maple-mustard glaze

  1. 80 g pure maple syrup
  2. 30 g whole-grain mustard
  3. 15 g Dijon mustard
  4. 15 g apple cider vinegar
  5. 5 g soy sauce

Execution

  1. 1
    Up to 24 hours ahead (1 hour minimum), pat the 2700 g / 5–7 lb turkey breast bone-dry with paper towels. Mix 22 g / 1.5 tbsp kosher salt, 2 g / 1 tsp cracked black pepper, and 1 g / ½ tsp smoked paprika; rub all over, focusing on the meatier areas. Set on a rack in a sheet pan, uncovered, in the fridge. The salt seasons deep; the air dries the skin for crispness.1 d 0 h
  2. 2
    Heat oven to 425°F (220°C). Mash the 60 g / 4 tbsp softened butter with 4 g / 1 tbsp minced sage, 3 g / 1 tbsp thyme leaves, and 2 grated garlic cloves.
  3. 3
    Slide your fingers between skin and meat to loosen the skin without tearing it. Push the herb butter into that pocket and massage it across the breast.
  4. 4
    Set the turkey on a rack in a roasting pan. Roast at 425°F (220°C) for 20 minutes; this jumpstarts the Maillard on the skin.
  5. 5
    Drop oven to 325°F (165°C). Continue roasting.
  6. 6
    Whisk maple syrup, both mustards, vinegar, and soy in a small bowl while the turkey roasts.
  7. 7
    When the thickest part of the breast hits 150°F (65°C), usually 45–60 minutes total, brush liberally with the glaze. Return to the oven.
  8. 8
    Roast another 10–15 minutes until the breast hits 160°F (71°C) and the glaze is bubbly and sticky. If it's pale, broil 60 seconds, watching constantly.
  9. 9
    Pull from the oven. Rest at least 20 minutes; carryover takes it to a safe 165°F (74°C) and the fibers reabsorb the juice. Slice against the grain.
Safety
  • Pulling turkey breast at 160°F before glaze, finishing to 165°F via carryover after rest A 20-minute rest under foil drives carryover from 160°F into the published-safe 165°F window for poultry. Cutting before the rest leaves the breast both juicier-on-the-board and short of the kill target. Reference →

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • butter → olive oil or duck fat with the herbs Dairy-free version. Duck fat adds richness butter doesn't.
  • sage → rosemary or parsley Rosemary for piney intensity, parsley for a fresher, lighter profile.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → whole-grain mustard make it yourself Homemade keeps the seed integrity that delivers heat in waves rather than in a single front-loaded hit. Two-day mellow, then it holds for months.
  • → Dijon mustard make it yourself Cleaner heat than the jar, with the wine acidity dialed to the dish. The maple-mustard glaze tastes more like itself when the mustard isn't carrying brand-flat sweetness underneath.

Notes

Dry brine over wet brine: wet brines water down the meat. A dry brine pulls salt into the muscle through osmosis, denaturing the proteins so they hold onto their own juices when cooked. Bonus: it dries the surface, which is the only way to get crisp skin.

Butter under the skin, not on top, because turkey breast is lean — fat applied on top mostly drips off into the pan. Trapped against the meat, it self-bastes the breast as it renders.

The teaspoon of soy sauce in the glaze does not make the turkey taste Asian. It''s glutamates — the missing bass note that bridges the maple sweet and the mustard sharp. Without it, the glaze tastes one-dimensional. Apple cider vinegar plays the same role for sugar: maple is heavy on sucrose, and acid is what keeps the glaze from going cloying.

Resting is non-negotiable. At 160°F the muscle fibers are contracted and pressurized. Cut immediately and the juice ends up on the cutting board. Twenty minutes lets the fibers relax and reabsorb.
