Maple panna cotta

A barely-set cream panna cotta sweetened entirely with maple syrup, no cane sugar. Gelatin dosed low for the trembling wobble that dissolves on the tongue, the cream browned lightly with butter and steeped with vanilla and cinnamon to meet maple's caramel-woodsy register, finished with a thread of Grade A dark syrup and toasted pecans.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a make-ahead dessert: it needs at least 4 hours in the fridge to set, and it is better after 6 to 24 hours. Plan to make it the day before, or first thing in the morning for that night.
  • Bloom the gelatin in cold liquid before you heat anything. Powder sprinkled onto warm cream clumps into fish-eyes that never dissolve; it must swell in cold liquid first.
  • Gelatin must never boil. Sustained heat above about 80 C hydrolyzes it and permanently weakens the set, so the cream is warmed only until steaming, never simmered.
  • Use a real Grade A dark (formerly Grade B) maple syrup, not pancake syrup. Pancake syrup is corn syrup with flavoring and will read one-note and cloying; the whole flavor of this dessert is the maple.

Bloom

  1. 8 g powdered gelatin
  2. 60 ml cold whole milk

Cream base

  1. 25 g unsalted butter
  2. 360 ml heavy cream
  3. 120 ml whole milk
  4. 5 g vanilla bean paste
  5. 1 each cinnamon stick, whole, steeped and removed
  6. 1 g fine sea salt

Sweeten and finish

  1. 100 g maple syrup, Grade A dark
  2. 25 g maple syrup, for finishing
  3. 20 g pecans, toasted and roughly chopped
  4. 1 g flaky sea salt — (a few flakes per serving; against the maple)

Execution

  1. 1
    Bloom the gelatin. Pour the 60 ml / ¼ cup cold whole milk into a small bowl and sprinkle the 8 g / 2½ tsp powdered gelatin evenly over the surface (sprinkle, do not dump, so it hydrates without clumping). Leave it undisturbed 5 to 10 minutes; it will swell into a saturated, wrinkled sponge. This cold bloom is what lets the gelatin dissolve smoothly later.
  2. 2
    Brown the butter. In a small saucepan over medium-low, melt the 25 g / 2 tbsp butter and cook past the foam until the milk solids turn amber and smell of toasted hazelnut, about 3 minutes. This browned-butter note is what gives the maple custard a toasted depth that plain cream would not. Pull off the heat the moment it smells nutty, before it goes dark. Brown butter (beurre noisette)
  3. 3
    Steep the cream. To the browned butter add the 360 ml / 1½ cups heavy cream, the 120 ml / ½ cup whole milk, the 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste, the 1 cinnamon stick, and the 1 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt. Warm over medium-low, stirring, until it steams and small bubbles form at the edge but before it simmers, about 4 to 5 minutes. Do not boil. Pull off the heat, cover, and steep 10 minutes so the vanilla and cinnamon infuse.
  4. 4
    Dissolve the gelatin. Return the cream to low heat until just warm again (not simmering). Add the bloomed gelatin sponge and whisk gently until it fully dissolves and the mixture is completely smooth, about 1 minute; rub a little between two fingers to confirm no granules remain. Keep the heat low, the gelatin must not boil or the set weakens.
  5. 5
    Sweeten off the heat. Take the pan off the heat and whisk in the 100 g / ⅓ cup maple syrup until fully combined. Maple is more heat-stable than honey but still tastes fresher added off direct heat; whisking it into the warm cream keeps the caramel-woodsy note bright. Taste, it should read clearly of maple and vanilla with the browned butter behind it, gently sweet, salt in the background. Fish out the cinnamon stick and discard.
  6. 6
    Strain and fill. Pour the mixture through a fine-mesh sieve into a measuring jug with a spout (the sieve catches any undissolved gelatin or browned-butter solids and gives a flawless texture). Divide evenly among five 120 ml ramekins or serving glasses. If you plan to unmold, lightly oil the ramekins with a neutral oil first.
  7. 7
    Chill to set. Cool the filled molds on the counter 15 minutes (so the fridge does not have to fight residual heat), then refrigerate uncovered until skin-firm, about 1 hour, then cover and chill at least 4 hours total, ideally 6 to 24. The set is correct when a gentle tap makes the surface wobble like the classic panna cotta quiver but the custard holds its shape.
  8. 8
    Finish and serve. Serve in the glass, or unmold: dip each ramekin to the rim in hot water for 5 to 8 seconds, run a thin knife around the edge, invert onto a plate, and give a firm downward shake to release. Thread the 25 g / 1½ tbsp finishing maple syrup over each portion, scatter the 20 g / 2 tbsp toasted chopped pecans, and finish with a few flakes of sea salt. The raw syrup, the toasted nuts, and the salt turn a soft set into a plated dessert with contrast in every bite.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion flavor→bourbon-maple panna cotta

    Whisk 1 tbsp / 15 ml bourbon into the cream along with the maple syrup off the heat. The alcohol mostly stays (it is never boiled), so this reads as an adult dessert; the oak and vanilla of the bourbon amplify the maple. Do not exceed 2 tbsp or the alcohol can interfere with the set.

  • Occasion occasion→maple panna cotta with a maple-walnut brittle shard

    Boil 60 g maple syrup to 150 °C, stir in 40 g toasted walnuts, spread thin on parchment, cool, and shatter. Stand a shard in each set custard at serving. The hard-cracked maple against the soft set is the textural showpiece; make the brittle the same day so it does not go tacky.

  • Regional flavor→maple-chai panna cotta

    Steep the cream with a crushed chai spice bundle (2 cardamom pods, 2 cloves, a slice of fresh ginger, the cinnamon stick, a few black peppercorns) instead of cinnamon alone, then strain. The warm spice reads beautifully against maple; keep the ginger small so its protease does not touch the gelatin (steeping in hot cream denatures it, so this is safe).

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • maple syrup, Grade A dark → honey, 85 g / ¼ cup Also cane-free, with a floral flavor instead of caramel-woodsy. Honey is sweeter by weight, so use less, and skip the brown-butter step, which suits maple more than honey (this matches the ground-up honey panna cotta in this collection). See ingredients/honey.
  • powdered gelatin → gelatin sheets, 4 silver sheets (about 8 g) Soften the sheets in a bowl of cold water 5 minutes, wring them out, and whisk into the warm cream in place of the bloomed powder. See ingredients/gelatin.
  • pecans → toasted walnuts or hazelnuts Any toasted nut works for the crunch. Walnuts lean into the maple-walnut tradition; hazelnuts echo the browned butter.

Notes

Why brown the butter. Maple''s flavor lives in a caramel-and-woodsy register, and a brown-butter step gives the cream base a toasted, nutty depth in the same family, so the maple lands on a foundation that meets it rather than a flat sweet-cream backdrop. Two tablespoons is the amount that actually registers in this much cream, less than that and the note gets lost; browned, its milk-solid melanoidins carry a real toasted edge under the maple. The honey version of this dessert skips it, honey wants brightness, not toast.

Why Grade A dark, not pancake syrup. Real maple syrup is boiled-down tree sap and nothing else; its flavor comes from compounds formed during that boil. Grade A dark (the old Grade B) is boiled longer and tastes more strongly of maple than the pale, delicate early-season grades, which is what you want when maple is the entire point. Pancake syrup is corn syrup with artificial flavoring and caramel color; it reads one-dimensional and cloying and has none of maple''s complexity. This is the one place the ingredient cannot be economized.

Why this gelatin dose. Panna cotta lives on the edge of set. Here the 8 g of gelatin is about 1.2% of the total liquid weight (the dairy plus the maple syrup, a bit over 650 g), which is enough to unmold onto a plate with a firm shake while still giving the trembling wobble that dissolves the instant it hits the tongue. Maple carries a third of its weight as water, so it dilutes the network a little more than honey does; do not drop below 8 g if you intend to unmold. For a softer glass-served set, 6 g is fine.

Make-ahead and holding. This is a fridge dessert that only improves overnight as the flavors marry and the set firms. It holds covered for up to 3 days; after that the surface dries and picks up fridge odors. Add the toasted pecans only at serving so they stay crisp. Do not freeze, gelatin sets weep and turn grainy on thawing.
