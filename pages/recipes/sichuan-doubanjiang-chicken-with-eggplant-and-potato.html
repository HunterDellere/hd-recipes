Recipe

Sichuan doubanjiang chicken with eggplant and potato

Bone-in chicken thighs braised with Pixian doubanjiang, the fermented broad-bean and chili paste that is the soul of Sichuan cooking, bloomed in oil until its red lipids release before any liquid goes in. Eggplant drinks the spiced braise and turns silky; waxy potato holds its shape and tempers the heat. Toasted Sichuan peppercorn delivers the má tingle the dish is built around, and a finish of black vinegar and scallion lands it. Numbing, savory, gently sweet, and acid-lifted.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Optional but recommended brine: dissolve 30 g / 2 tbsp kosher salt in 1 L / 4 cups cold water and soak the thighs 30 to 45 minutes, then pat fully dry. Doubanjiang is intensely salty, so if you brine, add no further salt and taste before adjusting at the end.
  • Salt the eggplant 20 minutes ahead: toss the chunks with 6 g / 1 tsp kosher salt and rest in a colander, then blot dry. This collapses the spongy cell structure so the eggplant absorbs less oil and braises silky instead of greasy. Start this before you sear the chicken.
  • Toast and grind the Sichuan peppercorns just before cooking; their numbing aromatics (hydroxy-alpha-sanshool) fade fast once ground. Toast whole in a dry pan 60 seconds until fragrant, cool, then grind.
  • Steam jasmine rice to finish alongside the braise; this dish is assertive and needs the neutral rice to balance it.

Mise en place by phase 7 phases ▾

Phase 1, the eggplant prep

  1. 400 g eggplant, cut into 4 cm chunks, salted 20 min, blotted dry — (about 1 large globe or 3 Chinese eggplants; Chinese eggplant is less seedy and silkier if you can find it)
  2. 6 g kosher salt (for the eggplant)

Phase 2, the chicken

  1. 1000 g bone-in skin-on chicken thighs, patted very dry; salt only if not brined and even then lightly, the doubanjiang is salty — (about 6 thighs)
  2. 30 g neutral oil

Phase 3, the má and aromatics

  1. 4 g Sichuan peppercorns, toasted dry 60 sec, cooled, ground
  2. 25 g ginger, minced — (a 4 cm knob)
  3. 25 g garlic, minced — (about 6 cloves)
  4. 40 g scallion, whites minced, greens sliced thin and reserved — (about 4 scallions)

Phase 4, the braise base

  1. 45 g Pixian doubanjiang (fermented broad-bean chili paste), chopped if chunky
  2. 10 g fermented black beans (douchi), rinsed, lightly mashed optional
  3. 45 g Shaoxing wine
  4. 20 g light soy sauce
  5. 12 g granulated sugar
  6. 360 ml water or unsalted chicken stock

Phase 5, the vegetables

  1. 300 g waxy potato, peeled, cut into 3 cm chunks — (2 medium; holds shape and tempers the heat. The flexible slot)

Phase 6, finish

  1. 12 g Chinkiang black vinegar
  2. 5 g toasted sesame oil

Phase 6, serve

  1. 400 g steamed jasmine rice

Execution

  1. 1
    Confirm the 400 g eggplant has salted 20 minutes and blot it very dry with a towel; surface water makes it steam and turn greasy rather than silky. Toast the 4 g Sichuan peppercorns in a dry pan over medium 60 seconds until fragrant, cool, and grind. Set aside.
  2. 2
    Heat 15 g / 1 tbsp of the neutral oil in a wide heavy pot or Dutch oven over medium-high. Sear the 1000 g chicken thighs skin-side-down 5 to 6 minutes until deeply golden, flip 1 minute, then lift to a plate. Pour off the rendered fat and wipe the pot if it scorched. Maillard sear
  3. 3
    Add the remaining 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil and lower heat to medium. Add the 45 g / 3 tbsp doubanjiang and bloom it, stirring constantly, 2 to 3 minutes until the oil stains deep red and the paste smells toasty rather than raw. This is the single most important step; blooming the fermented paste in fat is what releases its color and depth. Do not let it scorch or it turns acrid.
  4. 4
    Add the 25 g minced ginger, 25 g minced garlic, the 40 g scallion whites, the 10 g mashed douchi (if using), and half the ground Sichuan peppercorn. Stir 1 minute until fragrant. Pour in the 45 g / 3 tbsp Shaoxing wine, scraping the base, and let it reduce 30 seconds.
  5. 5
    Stir in the 20 g / 4 tsp light soy, 12 g / 1 tbsp sugar, and 360 ml / 1½ cups water or stock. Return the chicken skin-side-up and tuck in the 300 g potato chunks. Bring to a simmer, cover, and reduce to low. Braise 20 minutes.
  6. 6
    Uncover and add the 400 g salted, blotted eggplant, pushing it into the liquid. Simmer uncovered 15 minutes; the eggplant collapses to silky and the potato turns tender, and the sauce reduces and thickens. Baste the chicken skin two or three times with the reducing liquid.
  7. 7
    Pull off the heat. Stir in the 12 g / 2½ tsp Chinkiang black vinegar, the 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, and the remaining ground Sichuan peppercorn. Taste: it should read numbing and savory first, then a sweet-sour balance, with a building chili warmth. If flat, more vinegar; if too sharp, a pinch of sugar; if you want more má, more ground peppercorn now while it is fresh.
  8. 8
    Spoon over hot jasmine rice, scatter the reserved scallion greens (from the 40 g scallions) across, and serve immediately. The rice is structural, it carries the heat and the má between bites.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture vegetable→mushroom-forward, no potato

    Drop the potato. Add 200 g torn oyster mushrooms and 150 g halved shimeji with the eggplant in the uncovered phase. The braise turns leaner and earthier, the mushrooms donate their own glutamate, and it cooks 5 minutes faster without the potato to soften. Reduce the water to 300 ml since there is less starch to absorb it.

  • Texture vegetable→kabocha squash

    Replace the potato with 300 g peeled kabocha in 3 cm chunks, added with the eggplant rather than the chicken since squash softens faster. The squash sweetness pushes the dish toward the sweeter end of fish-fragrant balance, so cut the added sugar to 6 g.

  • Heat heat→milder, family-style

    Cut the doubanjiang to 25 g and add 15 g hoisin to round it. Halve the Sichuan peppercorn. The dish reads gentler and slightly sweeter, closer to a home-style jia chang braise than a fierce restaurant version. Good for feeding people new to Sichuan heat.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • Pixian doubanjiang → any Sichuan doubanjiang Pixian (Pi County) is the benchmark for depth and red color, but any fermented broad-bean chili paste labeled doubanjiang works. Avoid Korean or Japanese bean pastes here; the fermentation and chili profile are different.
  • Sichuan peppercorns → a blend of red and green Sichuan peppercorn Green (qing huajiao) is more intensely numbing and citrusy; red is rounder. A 50/50 blend is excellent. There is no real substitute for the má sensation; black pepper and chili flakes give heat but not the tingle.
  • eggplant → Chinese eggplant Chinese (long, thin, lavender) eggplant is less seedy and goes silkier than globe eggplant. Use the same weight; no need to salt as long since it holds less water.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → water or unsalted chicken stock make it yourself A gelatin-rich homemade stock gives the braise body the doubanjiang and water alone cannot, and lets you control the salt against the very salty paste.

Notes

Why bloom the doubanjiang. Doubanjiang is a fermented paste whose color and depth live in fat-soluble compounds (the red comes from chili carotenoids and the fermentation pigments). Stirring it in raw with the liquid leaves the braise pale, harsh, and one-dimensional; frying it in oil first until the fat stains red and the smell turns toasty releases those compounds and cooks off the raw-ferment edge. This is the defining move of Sichuan braising and the step most Western adaptations rush or skip.

Why salt the eggplant. Raw eggplant is a sponge of open cells that soak up oil and braising liquid and turn greasy and waterlogged. A 20-minute salt draws out free water, collapses the cell walls through osmosis, and lets the flesh braise to a silky, custardy texture while absorbing flavor rather than fat. Blotting dry afterward is essential; surface water steams the eggplant instead of letting it braise.

Why the má is not heat. Sichuan peppercorn contains hydroxy-alpha-sanshool, which acts on touch and vibration receptors to produce a tingling, numbing sensation (má), entirely separate from the burning heat (là) of chili. The two together are the málà signature. Omitting the peppercorn because you want it less spicy is a category error; it does not add heat, it adds the tingle that makes the dish Sichuan. Grind it fresh; the aromatic numbing compounds are volatile and fade within an hour of grinding.

Why sweet and sour both. Fish-fragrant (yu xiang) balance is built on the interplay of salty (doubanjiang, soy), sweet (sugar), and sour (black vinegar), with the chili and má layered over the top. Drop the sugar and the dish reads harshly salty and sharp; drop the vinegar and it goes flat and heavy. Both legs go in late, the sugar dissolved into the braise and the vinegar off heat so its aromatics survive.
