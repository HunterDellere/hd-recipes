Homemade tomato paste (slow-roasted concentrate)

Whole canned tomatoes reduced over 6 to 8 hours of low-oven roasting into a thick, deep-rust-red concentrate; the homemade tomato paste that distinguishes from the metallic can. The structural umami amplifier for cioppino, pasta sauces, and braises, with a layered flavor profile that includes caramelized fruit sugars (from Maillard browning) and reduced glutamate concentration (from water removal). A weekend project; one batch fills a small jar that lasts months in the refrigerator under a layer of olive oil.

Mise en place by phase 1 phase ▾

Single phase

  1. 4 × 28 oz / 794 g can canned San Marzano whole peeled tomatoes (or Bianco DiNapoli, Pomi, any good-quality)
  2. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  3. 3 g fine sea salt

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat oven to 95 °C / 200 °F with a rack in the middle. Pour the 2400 g canned tomatoes into a large bowl; crush by hand into rough chunks (do not blend; the texture matters less in the paste than in a sauce but blending creates too many fine particles that dry into a gritty texture). Spread in a thin, even layer over 2 heavy half-sheet pans (the surface area to volume ratio matters; deeper layers take longer to reduce and brown unevenly). Drizzle the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil over the tomatoes and sprinkle with the 3 g / ½ tsp salt.
  2. 2
    Roast 6 to 8 hours, stirring with a spatula every 30 to 45 minutes for the first 4 hours and every 15 to 20 minutes for the last 2 hours. The paste reduces visibly through the cook: hour 1, the tomatoes are still wet and chunky; hour 3, the texture is loose-sauce; hour 5, thick sauce, beginning to coat the pan; hour 7, paste-thick, deep rust-red, glossy, sticky. The endpoint is when a heaped spoonful holds its shape on a plate and the surface has caramelized to a deep brick-red color. Scrape any dried-paste edges into the center as you stir; the edges dry first and need to be reincorporated.7 h
  3. 3
    Pack the finished paste into a clean 250 ml glass jar; press down to eliminate air pockets. Drizzle a thin layer of olive oil over the top (about 1 tbsp) to seal the surface against oxidation. Seal the jar tightly and refrigerate. The paste keeps 2 to 3 months refrigerated under the oil layer; once a portion is removed, top up the oil layer to keep the surface covered.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional flavor→with aromatic infusion (bay, garlic, oregano)

    Tuck 2 bay leaves, 4 garlic cloves (whole, peeled), and a small sprig of fresh oregano into the tomato layer at the start. Remove at the 4-hour mark before they over-concentrate. The paste reads slightly more complex and reads "Italian" in the sense of being seasoned; the basic version is unseasoned because it must work in many dishes without competing with their seasoning.

Notes

This is a stub. The full drafting should cover the chemistry of slow concentration vs fast concentration (slow oven preserves the fresh tomato flavor while concentrating the umami; stovetop fast reduction caramelizes at the edges and produces a darker, more burnt-sugar version), the difference between this slow-oven paste and the sun-dried conserva di pomodoro (the southern Italian sun-dried version develops more lactic-acid fermented notes from days of slow water removal at low temperature; the oven version is cleaner but less complex), and the use cases in cioppino, ragu alla Bolognese, puttanesca, and any dish where commercial tomato paste reads metallic.
