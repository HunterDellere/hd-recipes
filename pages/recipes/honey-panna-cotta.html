Honey panna cotta

A barely-set cream panna cotta sweetened entirely with honey, no cane sugar. Gelatin dosed low for the trembling wobble that dissolves on the tongue, the cream steeped with vanilla and a strip of lemon zest, and the honey stirred in off the heat so its aromatics survive. Finished with a thread of raw honey and flaky salt.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a make-ahead dessert: it needs at least 4 hours in the fridge to set, and it is better after 6 to 24 hours. Plan to make it the day before, or first thing in the morning for that night.
  • Bloom the gelatin in cold liquid before you heat anything. Powder sprinkled onto warm cream clumps into fish-eyes that never dissolve; it must swell in cold liquid first.
  • Gelatin must never boil. Sustained heat above about 80 C hydrolyzes it and permanently weakens the set, so the cream is warmed only until steaming, never simmered.
  • Decide how you will serve it. In a glass, no unmolding needed. To unmold onto a plate, lightly oil the ramekins first with a neutral oil so the set slips out cleanly.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Bloom

  1. 8 g powdered gelatin
  2. 60 ml cold whole milk

Cream base

  1. 360 ml heavy cream
  2. 120 ml whole milk
  3. 5 g vanilla bean paste
  4. 1 each lemon zest, peeled in 2 wide strips with a vegetable peeler, no pith — (the strips steep and are removed, they echo honey's natural acidity)
  5. 1 g fine sea salt

Sweeten and finish

  1. 85 g honey
  2. 20 g honey, for finishing
  3. 1 g flaky sea salt — (a few flakes per serving; the finishing crunch against the honey)

Execution

  1. 1
    Bloom the gelatin. Pour the 60 ml / ¼ cup cold whole milk into a small bowl and sprinkle the 8 g / 2½ tsp powdered gelatin evenly over the surface (sprinkle, do not dump, so it hydrates without clumping). Leave it undisturbed 5 to 10 minutes; it will swell into a saturated, wrinkled sponge. This cold bloom is what lets the gelatin dissolve smoothly later.
  2. 2
    Steep the cream. In a small saucepan, combine the 360 ml / 1½ cups heavy cream, the 120 ml / ½ cup whole milk, the 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste, the 2 strips of lemon zest, and the 1 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt. Warm over medium-low, stirring, until it steams and small bubbles form at the edge but before it simmers, about 4 to 5 minutes. Do not boil. Pull off the heat, cover, and let steep 10 minutes so the vanilla and lemon infuse.
  3. 3
    Dissolve the gelatin. Return the cream to low heat until just warm again (not simmering). Add the bloomed gelatin sponge and whisk gently until it fully dissolves and the mixture is completely smooth, about 1 minute; rub a little between two fingers to confirm no granules remain. Keep the heat low, the gelatin must not boil or the set weakens.
  4. 4
    Sweeten off the heat. Take the pan off the heat and whisk in the 85 g / ¼ cup honey until fully dissolved. Adding the honey off the heat is deliberate: honey's floral aromatics are volatile and boiling drives them off, so the flavor stays brightest when it goes in warm rather than hot. Taste, it should read clearly of honey and vanilla, gently sweet, with the salt in the background. Fish out the two lemon strips and discard.
  5. 5
    Strain and fill. Pour the mixture through a fine-mesh sieve into a measuring jug with a spout (the sieve catches any undissolved gelatin or vanilla fiber and gives a flawless texture). Divide evenly among five 120 ml ramekins or serving glasses. If you plan to unmold, lightly oil the ramekins with a neutral oil first.
  6. 6
    Chill to set. Cool the filled molds on the counter 15 minutes (so the fridge does not have to fight residual heat), then refrigerate uncovered until skin-firm, about 1 hour, then cover and chill at least 4 hours total, ideally 6 to 24. The set is correct when a gentle tap makes the surface wobble like the classic panna cotta quiver but the custard holds its shape.
  7. 7
    Finish and serve. Serve in the glass, or unmold: dip each ramekin to the rim in hot water for 5 to 8 seconds, run a thin knife around the edge, invert onto a plate, and give a firm downward shake to release. Thread the 20 g / 1 tbsp finishing honey over each portion and scatter a few flakes of sea salt. The raw honey on top carries aromatics the cooked honey inside has softened, and the salt flakes give a crunch and a savory counterpoint that keeps it from cloying.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture flavor→buttermilk panna cotta (tangier)

    Replace the 120 ml whole milk with 120 ml cultured buttermilk, added off the heat with the honey (buttermilk curdles if boiled). The lactic tang plays against the honey the way crème fraîche does, and the set reads lighter and more refreshing. Keep the gelatin dose the same.

  • Occasion occasion→honey panna cotta with roasted stone fruit

    Halve and pit 4 apricots or 2 peaches, toss with a spoon of honey, and roast at 200 °C for 15 to 20 minutes until collapsing and syrupy. Spoon warm over the cold set at serving. The warm-against-cold contrast and the caramelized fruit turn it into a plated dessert course.

  • Regional flavor→honey-lavender or honey-thyme

    Steep 1 tsp dried culinary lavender, or 3 sprigs of fresh thyme, in the cream along with the vanilla and lemon, then strain out. Pair with a matching honey (lavender honey, thyme honey) to double the aromatic. A little lavender goes far; over-steeping reads soapy.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • honey → maple syrup, 100 g / ⅓ cup Also cane-free, with a caramel-forward flavor instead of floral. Maple is a little less sweet than honey by weight, so use more (this matches the ground-up maple panna cotta in this collection). See ingredients/maple-syrup.
  • powdered gelatin → gelatin sheets, 4 silver sheets (about 8 g) Soften the sheets in a bowl of cold water 5 minutes, wring them out, and whisk into the warm cream in place of the bloomed powder. See ingredients/gelatin.
  • vanilla bean paste → vanilla extract, 1 tsp / 5 ml, plus the scraped seeds of ¼ pod if you have one Extract alone works but you lose the visible seed flecks that read as quality in a pale set custard. See ingredients/vanilla-extract.

Notes

Why honey goes in off the heat. Honey carries volatile floral and fruity aroma compounds inherited from the flowers the bees foraged, and those are exactly what a good honey is prized for. Heat drives them off: boil honey and you are left with a generically sweet syrup that has lost most of what made that particular honey worth using. So the honey is whisked in after the pan comes off the heat, warm enough to dissolve but not hot enough to strip the aroma. It also means the specific honey you choose is the whole flavor of the dessert, pick one you actually like the taste of.

Why this gelatin dose. Panna cotta lives on the edge of set. Here the 8 g of gelatin is about 1.2% of the total liquid weight (the dairy plus the honey, a bit over 650 g), which is enough to unmold onto a plate with a firm shake while still giving the trembling wobble that dissolves the instant it hits the tongue. If you are only ever serving it in the glass and want it even softer, drop to 6 g; do not push much past 8 g or you trade the melt for a rubbery, sliceable set that reads like a supermarket dessert.

Why lemon zest with honey. Honey carries a faint natural acidity (mostly gluconic acid), and a strip of lemon zest steeped in the cream echoes and lifts it without turning the custard sour. The zest oil is aromatic, not sharp; it frames the honey the way a squeeze of lemon frames a drizzle of honey on toast. Do not substitute juice, which would water the cream and risk curdling.

Make-ahead and holding. This is a fridge dessert that only improves overnight as the flavors marry and the set firms to its final texture. It holds covered for up to 3 days; after that the surface starts to dry and the custard picks up fridge odors. Do not freeze, gelatin sets weep and turn grainy on thawing.
