Umeboshi (salt-pickled and sun-dried plums)

Sour-salty preserved plums made by lacto-fermenting unripe Japanese ume in coarse salt for several weeks, then sun-drying for three consecutive hot days, optionally re-pickling with red shiso for the canonical pink color. The umami-and-acid hammer of Japanese pickles. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 4 items ▾
  1. 1000 g unripe ume plums (yellow-blushing, just before full ripeness)
  2. 200 g coarse sea salt (akashio or comparable) — (20% by weight of plums; the high-salt traditional ratio. Lower-salt 10 to 13% versions need refrigeration.)
  3. 100 g fresh red shiso leaves (akajiso), washed and dried optional — (optional but canonical; provides the pink color and a herbaceous-floral note)
  4. 30 ml shōchū or vodka, for sterilizing the crock

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: rinse the unripe ume in cold water, remove any stems with a toothpick. Soak overnight in cold water to remove residual bitterness from underripe plums.12 h
  2. 2
    Stub: dry the plums thoroughly, toss with the 200 g salt in a sterilized non-reactive crock or large jar. Weight with a plate-and-clean-stone (~1 kg) to compress the plums and force them to release liquid (umezu, plum vinegar). Within 3 to 5 days the plums will be submerged in their own brine; keep them weighted for 3 to 4 weeks at cool room temperature.21 d 0 h
  3. 3
    Stub: optionally bruise red shiso leaves with salt to release pigment, add to the crock at week 2 to color the plums pink-red. Continue to ferment.
  4. 4
    Stub: in midsummer (the hottest, driest 3-day stretch you can get), drain the plums and lay them on bamboo or wire racks in direct sun for 3 consecutive days, turning at midday. Bring inside at night. The plums shrivel, darken, and develop the characteristic umeboshi texture.3 d 0 h
  5. 5
    Stub: optionally return the dried plums to a small portion of the umezu or to fresh shiso-colored brine for storage. Pack into sterilized jars; lasts 2+ years sealed at room temperature, decades for traditional 20%-salt batches.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the lacto-fermentation chemistry (Lactobacillus and Pediococcus species converting plum sugars to lactic acid in the salt-tolerant brine, the citric and malic acid contribution from the plums), the role of the sun-drying step (concentrates flavor, kills surface molds, develops the leathery texture), the historical anti-microbial purpose (umeboshi in onigiri were the standard travel preservation strategy from the Heian period through World War II), the salt-percentage spectrum (20% traditional shelf-stable, 10 to 13% modern refrigerated low-salt, below 8% at risk of mold), the umezu (plum vinegar) byproduct as a salad-dressing acid, the shiso-coloring chemistry (anthocyanins from the red shiso are pigment-acid stable in the umezu), the buying spectrum (commercial umeboshi range from cheap dyed-with-food-coloring versions to artisanal 5-year-aged premium plums at $40+ per pound), and the eating contexts (one plum on hot rice; pitted and minced into salad dressings and marinades; in a glass of hot water as a folk remedy for hangovers).
