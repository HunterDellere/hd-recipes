Shaved brussels sprout salad with pecorino and hazelnut

Raw brussels sprouts shaved paper-thin on a mandoline, dressed with a sharp lemon-dijon vinaigrette that lightly tenderizes the shreds, and tossed with microplaned pecorino, toasted hazelnuts, and shaved raw shallot. Raw sprouts are crisp and faintly peppery rather than sulfurous, hold a dressing for hours without wilting, and read as a bright, dense autumn-winter slaw with the salt of the cheese and the toasted fat of the nuts.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Toast the hazelnuts first and let them cool. Toasting is what turns raw hazelnut from grassy to deeply nutty, and warm nuts steam and go limp in the salad; toast, cool, then crush.
  • Shave the sprouts as thin as you safely can. A mandoline or the slicing disc of a food processor gives the paper-thin shreds that take the dressing and eat tender; hand-slicing works but aim for as fine as your knife skills allow. Use the mandoline guard, sprouts are small and slip.
  • This salad is better made 20 to 30 minutes ahead. Unlike leafy salads, raw shaved sprouts improve as the acid lightly tenderizes them; dress and let it sit while you finish the rest of the meal.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, toast the hazelnuts

  1. 60 g hazelnuts, raw

Phase 2, the vinaigrette

  1. 1 each lemon, zested and juiced (about 30 g / 2 tbsp juice) — (1 lemon; the bright acid that tenderizes the shreds)
  2. 8 g dijon mustard
  3. 60 g extra-virgin olive oil
  4. 5 g honey
  5. 3 g kosher salt
  6. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Phase 3, the salad

  1. 450 g brussels sprouts, trimmed, shaved paper-thin on a mandoline
  2. 30 g shallot, shaved paper-thin — (1 small shallot; raw, for a sharp allium bite)
  3. 40 g pecorino romano, half microplaned fine, half shaved into petals with a peeler

Execution

  1. 1
    Toast the 60 g / ½ cup hazelnuts in a dry skillet over medium heat, shaking often, for 6 to 8 minutes until fragrant and the skins darken and loosen. Tip them onto a towel, rub off the loose skins, and let them cool. Crush them coarse with the flat of a knife or a few pulses; you want rubble, not powder. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  2. 2
    Make the vinaigrette. In the bottom of a large salad bowl, whisk the zest and 30 g / 2 tbsp juice of the lemon, the 8 g / 1 tsp dijon, the 5 g / 1 tsp honey, the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper until the salt dissolves. Stream in the 60 g / ¼ cup olive oil while whisking so the dijon emulsifies it into a smooth, glossy dressing. Vinaigrette emulsion
  3. 3
    Shave the vegetables. Shave the 450 g / 1 lb trimmed sprouts paper-thin on a mandoline (use the guard) straight into the bowl, fluffing the shreds apart as you go. Shave the small shallot (30 g) just as thin and add it. Add the microplaned half of the 40 g pecorino now so it dissolves into the dressing and seasons the whole bowl.
  4. 4
    Dress and rest. Toss the sprouts, the 30 g shallot, and the dressing thoroughly with your hands so every shred is coated, massaging gently for 30 seconds. Let the salad sit 20 to 30 minutes at room temperature; the acid lightly tenderizes the raw shreds and the flavors marry. Raw sprouts will not wilt to mush the way lettuce would; they only improve.
  5. 5
    Finish and serve. Just before serving, taste and adjust, more lemon for brightness, more salt if flat, a thread more oil if sharp. Toss in most of the 60 g / ½ cup crushed hazelnuts and the shaved pecorino petals, then scatter the rest over the top so the nuts stay crisp and the cheese petals show. Serve at room temperature.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion add fruit and sweetness→apple, pear, or dried cranberry

    Add a tart apple or firm pear, julienned or shaved thin, and a handful of dried cranberries or sour cherries. The crisp sweet fruit against the peppery raw sprout and salty pecorino is the autumn-holiday version of the salad; toss the apple in with the dressing so the acid keeps it from browning.

  • Protein make it a main→add a grain and a protein

    Fold in 200 g / 1 cup cooked farro or wheat berries and top with shredded roast chicken or crisp pancetta. The shaved sprouts become a hearty grain bowl that holds for lunch the next day, since the raw sprouts do not wilt; dress only what you will eat if keeping leftovers crisp matters.

  • Regional cheese and nut direction→parmigiano and toasted almond, or pecorino and walnut

    Swap pecorino for parmigiano (milder, nuttier, less salty, so add a pinch more salt) and hazelnuts for toasted sliced almonds or walnuts. Walnuts lean the salad more bitter-rich; almonds keep it lighter. Toast whichever nut you choose, raw nuts read flat against the sharp dressing.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • hazelnuts → toasted walnuts, pecans, almonds, or pepitas Any toasted nut or seed brings the crunch and toasted fat. Walnuts and pecans are richer, almonds lighter, pepitas make it nut-free. Toast whatever you use; the toasting is what makes it work.
  • pecorino romano → parmigiano reggiano or grana padano Parmigiano is milder and less salty (cow's milk), so bump the salt slightly; grana padano is a cheaper near-equal. Pecorino's sharp sheep's-milk salt is the most assertive choice and the best foil for the raw sprout.
  • lemon → red wine vinegar or sherry vinegar Either vinegar swaps in for the lemon juice (keep a little zest if you have a lemon) for a more savory, less citrusy dressing. Sherry vinegar leans nutty and pairs especially well with the hazelnut.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → dijon mustard make it yourself In a raw vinaigrette where nothing is cooked, the mustard's freshness shows directly. Homemade dijon, ground and mellowed a week in the fridge, has a sharper, cleaner pungency than the commercial bottle and emulsifies the dressing just as well.

Notes

Why raw sprouts work where you would expect them not to. The reflex is that brussels sprouts must be cooked, but raw and shaved thin they are a different ingredient: crisp, dense, mildly peppery, and only faintly brassica. The reason they read clean rather than sulfurous is that shaving produces the isothiocyanate compounds (the pleasant mustardy notes) without ever reaching the wet-heat stage where those compounds degrade into the rotten-cabbage sulfur smell. There is no overcooking to fear because there is no cooking at all.

Why this salad holds where a leafy one collapses. Lettuce dressed ahead wilts to slime within minutes because its thin cells flood and rupture under the salt and acid. The dense, waxy leaves of a shaved sprout resist that; the acid only lightly tenderizes the shreds and brightens them, so the salad is genuinely better made 20 to 30 minutes ahead and holds for hours, even overnight. That makes it a rare dress-ahead salad and a strong make-ahead side for a crowded meal.

Toast the nuts, salt the dressing. Two small disciplines carry the dish. Raw hazelnuts read grassy and flat; toasted, they turn deeply nutty and their loosened skins rub away, and that toasted fat is half the salad''s appeal, so toast and cool them before they go in. And the salt belongs in the dressing, dissolved into the lemon and dijon, not sprinkled on the sprouts, so it seasons evenly; microplaning half the pecorino into the dressing does the same, melting the cheese''s salt and glutamate through the whole bowl rather than leaving it in stray flecks.

Two cuts of cheese, on purpose. Half the pecorino is microplaned to a fine fluff that dissolves into the dressing and seasons everything; the other half is shaved into broad petals with a peeler and scattered at the end so there are visible, distinct salty-sharp bites of cheese against the shreds. The same logic applies to the nuts: most tossed in, some scattered on top to stay crisp and visible. Texture and seasoning both want the split.
