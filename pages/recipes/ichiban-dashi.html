Ichiban dashi (first-extraction Japanese dashi)

Kombu cold-soaked in water, lifted just before boiling; the water then warmed to just under boiling, katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes) sprinkled on top, steeped 3 to 4 minutes off heat, and strained. The foundational broth of Japanese cuisine: 5 minutes of active extraction from two ingredients, no boiling, no pressing. The structural broth under miso soup, suimono, simmered dishes, and most of washoku (traditional Japanese cooking).

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The kombu cold-soak takes 30 minutes minimum, or overnight for the deepest extraction. Plan accordingly. The cold-water start is structural; hot water extracts bitter alginate compounds that ruin the dashi.
  • Do not boil the water with the kombu in it. The lift point is structural; the kombu must come out just before the water reaches a boil (about 80 to 85°C / 175 to 185°F, small bubbles forming at the bottom but no rolling boil). Continued heating with the kombu in the pot releases the bitter compounds.

Mise en place by phase 2 phases ▾

Phase 1, the kombu cold-soak

  1. 10 g kombu (dried kelp) — (one 10 × 10 cm square; Rishiri, Hidaka, or Ma-kombu all work, with subtle character differences. Do not rinse the kombu; the white powder on the surface is mannitol and glutamate residue, structural flavor)
  2. 1000 ml cold filtered water

Phase 2, the katsuobushi steep

  1. 20 g katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes) — (2 large handfuls; thin flakes (hanakatsuo) are the standard, thicker kezuribushi produce a more robust dashi suitable for richer applications)

Execution

  1. 1
    Cold-soak the kombu. Place the 10 g kombu in a 2-quart saucepan; pour the 1000 ml cold water over it. Let stand at room temperature for 30 minutes (or refrigerated overnight, 8 to 12 hours, for the deepest extraction). The kombu will rehydrate, soften, and slowly release glutamate into the cold water during this rest. The water should take on a faint amber color and a subtle savory smell by the end of the soak.
  2. 2
    Heat the kombu water. Place the saucepan over medium heat. Bring slowly to just under boiling, about 8 to 10 minutes; the water should reach approximately 80 to 85°C / 175 to 185°F, with small bubbles forming at the pot bottom but no rolling boil. Watch the water carefully; the lift point is structural.
  3. 3
    Lift the kombu. Just before the water boils (when you see strings of small bubbles rising and the water is steaming actively but not yet rolling), use chopsticks or tongs to lift the kombu out of the water. Reserve the kombu on a plate. Continuing to heat the water with the kombu in it releases alginate compounds that produce a slimy, bitter dashi; the lift point is non-negotiable. The reserved kombu can be used for niban dashi (the second extraction), sliced into ribbons for vegetable simmers, or chopped finely for furikake.
  4. 4
    Bring to just below boiling for the katsuobushi steep. Increase heat slightly; bring the water to about 95°C / 200°F (a faint simmer, with consistent small bubbles rising). Sprinkle the 20 g katsuobushi evenly across the surface of the water. The flakes will absorb water within 30 to 60 seconds and sink to the bottom of the pot. Pull the pot off heat immediately as soon as the flakes are mostly submerged.
  5. 5
    Steep off heat. Let the dashi stand off heat for 3 to 4 minutes. The katsuobushi releases inosinate (the umami nucleotide that synergizes with the kombu glutamate to produce dashi's characteristic deep savory taste) during this brief steep. A longer steep (5 to 8 minutes) produces a stronger but slightly more bitter dashi; the ichiban style targets the 3 to 4 minute window for the cleanest extraction. Suimono (clear soup) and refined kaiseki applications get the 3 minute steep; everyday miso soup gets the 4 minute steep.
  6. 6
    Strain. Set a fine-mesh sieve lined with a single layer of cheesecloth (or a clean unscented paper towel for an everyday substitute) over a clean pot or large measuring cup. Pour the dashi through the sieve. Do not press the katsuobushi against the sieve to squeeze out the last of the liquid; pressing extracts bitter and astringent compounds and turns clear dashi cloudy. Let the katsuobushi drain naturally; the small amount of dashi left in the flakes is structural, not worth saving. You should have about 800 ml of clear amber dashi.
  7. 7
    Use immediately or refrigerate. For peak flavor, use the dashi within 4 hours of making. Refrigerated in a sealed container, dashi keeps 3 days; it loses subtle aromatics over time but the structural umami stays intact. For longer storage, freeze in ice-cube trays (each cube is about 30 ml, convenient for individual portions); transfer cubes to a zip-top freezer bag and use within 2 months. Frozen dashi is dramatically better than commercial powder for any application where the dashi is a primary flavor.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion occasion→niban dashi (second extraction, the economical everyday dashi)

    Place the reserved kombu and katsuobushi from the ichiban dashi into a fresh 1000 ml of cold water. Bring to a simmer over medium heat; simmer 10 to 15 minutes (longer simmer is acceptable for the second extraction because there is less bitter compound left). Add an additional 5 g of fresh katsuobushi at the end; steep 2 minutes off heat. Strain. The result is niban dashi, weaker than ichiban but structurally sufficient for everyday miso soup, simmered vegetables, and udon noodle broth. Many Japanese households make ichiban dashi in the morning for the breakfast suimono and niban dashi later in the day for the everyday cooking.

  • Occasion occasion→awase dashi (mixed dashi, with both fish and kelp)

    This recipe is technically already awase dashi (kombu plus katsuobushi); the term is used to distinguish it from kombu-only or katsuo-only dashi. No changes needed; the recipe IS the awase dashi.

  • Dietary dietary→vegetarian (kombu-shiitake dashi, shojin style)

    See the separate recipe at recipes/kombu-shiitake-vegetarian-dashi. Structurally distinct: uses dried shiitake in place of katsuobushi, the shiitake guanylate replacing the bonito inosinate as the umami nucleotide. Required for Buddhist temple cooking (shojin ryori) and vegetarian/vegan Japanese preparations.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • kombu (dried kelp) → wakame (smaller dried seaweed, use 8 g for 10 g kombu) Wakame produces a lighter, less complex dashi; acceptable substitute when kombu is unavailable but the dish reads structurally different. Soak wakame for only 10 minutes (it rehydrates much faster than kombu).
  • katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes) → dried anchovies (niboshi) or dried mackerel flakes (sababushi) Niboshi produce iriko dashi, a structurally different broth common in Western Japan; rich and slightly fishier than katsuo dashi. Soak niboshi in cold water 30 minutes, then simmer briefly. Sababushi produces a more aggressive dashi suitable for richer applications. Both are valid Japanese broth alternatives.
  • cold filtered water → spring water or tap water Filtered or spring water is structurally better (less chlorine, less mineral interference); tap water is acceptable in cities with clean water supplies. The difference is subtle but perceptible in suimono (clear soup) where the dashi is the primary flavor.

Notes

Why two ingredients produce such a deep broth. The kombu releases glutamate (free L-glutamic acid) into the cold soak, which delivers the base umami sensation on the human tongue (the 5th taste, identified by Kikunae Ikeda at Tokyo Imperial University in 1908 specifically through kombu research). The katsuobushi releases inosinate (5''-IMP, inosine monophosphate) into the warm steep, which is a different umami molecule. When glutamate and inosinate are present together at the right ratio, they produce a synergistic umami sensation 8 times stronger than either alone (a 1979 study by Yamaguchi quantified this synergy). This umami synergy is the structural reason why dashi can produce such a deep savory broth from two ingredients in 50 minutes; the combination is mathematically and biochemically optimized.

The kombu cultivar differences. Rishiri kombu (from northern Hokkaido) produces a clear, refined dashi favored for kaiseki and suimono. Hidaka kombu (from southern Hokkaido) produces a slightly more vegetal, everyday dashi favored for home cooking. Ma-kombu (from southern Hokkaido) produces a sweet, rich dashi favored for more complex preparations. Naga kombu (long kombu, from various Hokkaido coasts) produces a more aggressively flavored dashi suitable for stronger preparations like Osaka-style udon broth. Most US-market kombu is unidentified by cultivar; any quality Japanese kombu produces good dashi.

Why pressing the katsuobushi is wrong. The katsuobushi flakes hold residual dashi after the strain; pressing them against the sieve to extract this residual liquid extracts bitter and astringent compounds (mostly tannins and oxidation products) that turn the clear dashi cloudy and ruin the clean profile. The clean strain technique is structural; let the flakes drain naturally and accept that a small amount of dashi stays in the flakes (about 30 ml out of 800 ml). The strained katsuobushi can be re-used in niban dashi or for okaka furikake.

Ichiban dashi vs niban dashi by application. Ichiban dashi (the first extraction, this recipe) is the structurally refined version, reserved for suimono (clear soup), kaiseki cooking, chawanmushi (savory egg custard), and any preparation where the dashi flavor is dominant. Niban dashi (the second extraction, see modifications) is the everyday version, used for miso soup (where the miso paste covers the dashi anyway), simmered vegetables, udon broth, and tempura dipping sauce. Many Japanese households use ichiban for special-occasion suimono and niban for everyday cooking; the two-tier system is structural to Japanese kitchen economy.

The umami baseline. Ichiban dashi tastes savory, marine, slightly sweet, with a clean finish and no fishy aftertaste. If your dashi tastes fishy or bitter, the issue is either: the kombu was boiled with the water in the pot (releases bitter alginates), the katsuobushi was steeped too long (releases bitter compounds), the katsuobushi was pressed (releases bitter compounds), or the water was tap with high chlorine. The fix for each is direct: lift the kombu before boiling, steep the katsuobushi only 3 to 4 minutes, do not press, use filtered water. With these four rules respected, every batch produces clean canonical dashi.

Storage. Refrigerated in a sealed container, 3 days at peak quality; the dashi slowly loses subtle aromatics over time but retains the structural umami. Frozen in ice-cube trays then bagged, 2 months; the frozen dashi is structurally indistinguishable from fresh after thawing. Do not store at room temperature for more than 2 hours; the protein-rich broth grows bacteria readily.

Sources
  • Japanese washoku tradition (the foundational stock of traditional Japanese cuisine) — Ichiban dashi (one-bancha dashi, the "first" extraction) is the structural foundation of washoku, formalized in the Heian period (794 to 1185) as part of the Buddhist temple cooking tradition that codified Japanese flavor architecture. The two-ingredient minimalism (kombu and katsuobushi) is intentional; the glutamate from the kombu and the inosinate from the katsuobushi produce umami synergy that no other broth tradition matches with such simple inputs. The recipe technique has been essentially unchanged for 1000 years.
