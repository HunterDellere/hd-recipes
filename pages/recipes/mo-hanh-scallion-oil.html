Mo hanh (Vietnamese scallion oil)

The Southern Vietnamese pour-over scallion oil that finishes com tam (broken rice), grilled pork, and grilled chicken. Thinly sliced scallion bloomed under hot neutral oil with a pinch of fine sea salt and sugar, the thermal flash quenching the allinase enzyme that drives raw onion bite and fixing the green into a bright, glossy spoonable oil. Distinct from the Cantonese ginger-scallion paste: no ginger, oil pulled cooler so the scallion stays slack and emerald rather than fried, sugar in the seasoning to read Southern-sweet. The condiment that turns a plate of plain rice and grilled meat into com tam suon nuong.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Slice all the scallion before the oil goes on the heat. The bloom is a single fast pour: once the oil is at temperature you have seconds to pour, and there is no time to slice mid-pour. Have the sliced scallion seasoned and waiting in a heatproof bowl.
  • Use a heatproof bowl, metal or thick ceramic. The oil reaches roughly 160 to 170 °C / 320 to 340 °F at the pour; a thin glass bowl can crack. A small heavy saucepan to heat the oil and a heatproof bowl to receive it is the whole rig.
  • This is a finishing condiment, not a standalone dish. Plan it as the last thing made before plating grilled meat or com tam, or make it ahead and refrigerate; it keeps about a week and the flavor peaks at days 1 to 3.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Aromatic

  1. 100 g scallions, whole stalks, thinly sliced crosswise into 2 to 3 mm rings, whites and greens both — (8 to 10 stalks; unlike the Cantonese ginger-scallion oil this uses the green tops too, they carry most of the color)
  2. 3 g fine sea salt
  3. 4 g granulated sugar

Hot oil

  1. 120 ml neutral oil (refined avocado, rice bran, or canola)

Finish

  1. 5 g fish sauce optional
  2. 5 g lime juice, fresh-squeezed

Execution

  1. 1
    Slice 100 g / 8 to 10 stalks scallion crosswise into thin 2 to 3 mm rings, whites and greens together, and put them in a heatproof metal or thick-ceramic bowl. Slicing rather than mincing is the texture target: mo hanh reads as distinct slack rings suspended in oil, not the dense paste of the Cantonese version. The greens carry most of the color, so keep them in.
  2. 2
    Stir 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt and 4 g / 1 tsp granulated sugar through the sliced scallion until evenly coated. The salt and sugar season the scallion from the surface inward and pull a little water to the cut edges, which the oil will flash off at the pour. The sugar is the Southern signature; it rounds the raw allium and the salt so the finished oil reads balanced rather than sharp.
  3. 3
    Heat 120 ml / ½ cup neutral oil in a small heavy saucepan over medium-high until it shimmers and just begins to wisp, about 160 to 170 °C / 320 to 340 °F. This is cooler than the Cantonese ginger-scallion pour: the goal is to blanch the scallion and fix the green, not to deep-fry it to gold. The cue without a thermometer is a single scallion ring dropped in sizzling steadily within a second or two, not violently, and not browning. Pull off heat the moment it reaches that point.
  4. 4
    Pour the hot oil over the seasoned scallion in one steady stream. The oil will sizzle and foam for 10 to 15 seconds as the surface water flashes off; this brief contact is what quenches the allinase enzyme that drives raw onion pungency, softening the bite, and fixes the chlorophyll green so it stays emerald instead of dulling to khaki. Stir once or twice with a heatproof spoon to submerge every ring. The scallion should go slack and bright green within seconds, not brown; if any rings darken past green, the oil was too hot, which is the one common failure here. Hot-oil finish (smoking-oil bloom over raw aromatics)
  5. 5
    Let the oil cool 2 to 3 minutes to warm, then stir in the optional 5 g / 1 tsp fish sauce and the 5 g / 1 tsp fresh lime juice. Both go in off the heat: fish sauce added to smoking oil scorches and turns acrid, and lime added hot flashes off its bright top notes. The fish sauce is the umami anchor and the lime is the finishing acid; together they take mo hanh from a plain scallion fat to a seasoned condiment that needs nothing else on the plate.
  6. 6
    Use warm, spooned generously over com tam (broken rice) and grilled meat, or transfer to a clean glass jar (scallion and oil together) and refrigerate. Stir before each use; the scallion settles and the oil clarifies on top. Pull from the fridge 10 minutes before serving so the oil regains its pourable consistency.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional aromatic profile→mo hanh with fried shallot

    Fry 30 g / ¼ cup thinly sliced shallot in the 120 g / ½ cup oil over medium heat until pale gold and just crisp, 4 to 5 minutes, then strain the shallot out and use that now-shallot-infused oil for the scallion bloom; scatter the crisp shallot over the finished plate. The base oil carries a deeper allium-Maillard note and the crisp shallot adds the textural crunch that com tam plates often want.

  • Heat heat→spicy mo hanh

    Add 6 g / 1 tbsp coarse Vietnamese or Korean chili flakes to the seasoned scallion before the oil pour. The chili blooms in the same hot-oil flash, staining the oil red-orange and adding a clean background heat without changing the scallion-forward character. Good when the grilled meat itself is unspiced.

  • Occasion application→mo hanh for banh mi and rice paper

    Slice the scallion finer, to 1 to 2 mm, and reduce the oil to 90 g / 6 tbsp so the condiment reads more concentrated and spreadable. Brush it inside a banh mi alongside the pate and pickles, or stir a spoonful into the dipping nuoc cham for fresh rice-paper rolls. The finer cut keeps it from overwhelming the lighter assemblies.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • neutral oil (refined avocado, rice bran, canola) → refined peanut oil or grapeseed oil Any high-smoke-point neutral oil works; refined peanut adds a faint nuttiness that suits grilled pork. Avoid olive oil and unrefined sesame oil; both smoke before the bloom temperature and fight the clean scallion profile.
  • fish sauce → light soy sauce or extra fine sea salt For a vegan oil, swap the fish sauce for 5 g / 1 tsp light soy sauce (keeps a glutamate-driven savory note) or simply add another pinch of fine sea salt. The dish loses some of the funk-forward depth that defines the Southern Vietnamese version but stays balanced.
  • lime juice → calamansi juice or rice vinegar Calamansi is the more regionally authentic acid where you can get it, sweeter and more orange-perfumed than lime. Rice vinegar works as a non-citrus stand-in; use the same 5 g / 1 tsp and add it warm rather than hot.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → fish sauce make it yourself Mo hanh and nuoc cham are the two condiments that finish almost every com tam and grilled-meat plate, and they share the fish-sauce-lime-sugar axis. A jar of homemade nuoc cham on the table next to the scallion oil is the canonical Southern Vietnamese pairing; making the dipping sauce yourself lets you tune the salty-sour-sweet balance to match the oil.

Notes

How mo hanh differs from Cantonese ginger-scallion oil. Both pour hot oil over raw scallion, but they are different preparations aimed at different dishes. The Cantonese ginger-scallion oil minces ginger and scallion into a dense paste and pours oil at 175 to 190 °C to deep-fry the aromatics in place, building a thick savory paste for white-cooked chicken. Mo hanh has no ginger, slices the scallion into rings rather than mincing it, pours the oil cooler (160 to 170 °C) so the scallion blanches and stays slack and emerald rather than frying to gold, and carries a pinch of sugar for the Southern-sweet seasoning balance. The result is a thinner, brighter, pourable oil whose job is to gloss broken rice and grilled meat, not to anchor a plate as a paste.

The allinase quench is the technique. Cut scallion releases allinase, the enzyme that converts the cell's sulfur precursors into the volatile thiosulfinates responsible for raw onion bite. At room temperature that reaction runs for minutes and the scallion stays harsh; the brief contact with hot oil denatures the enzyme within seconds, arresting the reaction and locking the flavor at a softened point. The same flash fixes the chlorophyll, setting the bright green before the heat can degrade it to dull khaki. Pour too cool and the scallion stays raw and oniony; pour too hot and it browns past green into a bitter fried note. The 160 to 170 °C window is the target.

Why the oil goes cooler than the ginger-scallion version. The defining visual of a good mo hanh is slack, bright green scallion rings suspended in clear oil, not browned bits. Because there is no dense ginger to protect and no paste to build, the scallion is fully exposed to the oil and browns fast; pulling the oil at 160 to 170 °C rather than at smoking gives the scallion the brief blanch it needs without tipping into fried. If the rings darken at the pour, the oil was too hot, drop it 10 to 15 °C next time.

How to use it. Spoon mo hanh over com tam (broken rice) with grilled pork chop (suon nuong) or shredded pork skin; over any grilled meat off the charcoal; over a plate of plain jasmine rice with a fried egg; stirred into a bowl of rice noodles with grilled meat (bun thit nuong); brushed inside a banh mi. It is a finishing oil, never a cooking oil; the bloom is the only heat it sees.

Storage. Refrigerate in a sealed glass jar with the scallion and oil together. It keeps about a week refrigerated; the flavor peaks at days 1 to 3 and the green slowly dulls after that. Never introduce water (a wet spoon, a condensation-beaded lid) into the jar; water is what turns any aromatic oil rancid and cloudy. Pull from the fridge 10 minutes before serving so the oil loosens from its fridge-set thickness.
