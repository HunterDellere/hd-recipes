Recipe

Seared cremini and white mushrooms with garlic and thyme

Cremini or white button mushrooms dry-seared in a hot uncrowded pan until their water boils off and the cut faces brown deep, then finished with butter, smashed garlic, thyme, and a splash of soy and lemon. The whole cook is one discipline: drive the water out before adding fat, so the mushrooms brown and turn meaty instead of stewing pale and rubbery. The soy adds glutamate that compounds the mushrooms own savory depth, and the lemon keeps the butter from reading flat.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Do not crowd the pan. Mushrooms shed a lot of water, and a crowded pan traps it so they steam the whole time and never brown. Use your widest skillet and work in two batches if 450 g will not fit in a single layer; the brown crust is the entire point and crowding kills it.
  • Clean the mushrooms dry, not wet. They are sponges; soaking them adds water you then have to cook off. Brush them or wipe with a barely-damp towel just before cooking.

Mise en place by phase 2 phases ▾

Phase 1, the dry sear

  1. 450 g cremini or white button mushrooms, wiped clean, trimmed at the stem base, halved (quartered if large) so each piece has a flat cut face
  2. 10 g neutral oil (high smoke point)

Phase 2, the finish

  1. 30 g unsalted butter, cold, cubed
  2. 12 g garlic cloves, smashed, side-smashed with the flat of a knife, skins removed — (3 cloves; smashed so they perfume the butter without burning)
  3. 3 g fresh thyme, leaves stripped, or 3 sprigs left whole and pulled at the end — (3 sprigs)
  4. 10 g light soy sauce
  5. 2 g kosher salt
  6. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked
  7. 0.25 each lemon, juiced
  8. 8 g flat-leaf parsley, chopped optional — (a small handful; the fresh green finish)

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the pan dry and hot. Place your widest heavy skillet over high heat for 2 to 3 minutes until genuinely hot. Add the 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil, just a film, and swirl.
  2. 2
    Dry-sear, water first. Add the 450 g / 1 lb halved mushrooms cut-face down in a single uncrowded layer. They will squeak and then, within a minute, flood the pan with their own water. Do not season, do not add fat, do not stir much yet, let the water boil off hard over high heat, 3 to 5 minutes. The pan goes from sizzling to a wet simmer back to sizzling as the liquid evaporates; that second sizzle is your cue the water is gone and browning can start. Maillard sear
  3. 3
    Brown deep. Once the pan is dry again, leave the mushrooms cut-face down 2 to 3 minutes to build a deep brown crust, then toss and brown a second face for another 2 minutes. They should shrink to about half their raw volume, firm to a meaty bite, and color to a rich brown. This is the payoff of the dry sear; rush it and they stay pale and rubbery. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Add butter and aromatics. Drop the 30 g / 2 tbsp cold cubed butter, the 12 g / 3 smashed garlic cloves, and the 3 g / 3 sprigs thyme into the pan. The butter will foam and brown within 20 seconds; toss the mushrooms in it and let the garlic and thyme perfume the foaming butter for 60 to 90 seconds. Tilt the pan and spoon the herb butter over the mushrooms.
  5. 5
    Season late and deglaze with soy. Add the 10 g / 2 tsp light soy sauce, the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper, and toss; the soy will sizzle and glaze the mushrooms with a savory sheen. The salt goes in now, not earlier, because salt added during the dry sear draws water out and floods the pan. Cook 30 seconds until the soy reduces to a glaze.
  6. 6
    Finish with acid and serve. Pull off the heat, squeeze over the 1 tsp lemon juice, and toss. Pull the thyme sprigs if you left them whole. Taste: it should read deeply savory and meaty, with the lemon lifting the butter and the soy reading as depth rather than as soy. Scatter the optional 8 g parsley and serve hot, next to a steak or a piece of fish, or piled over toast or soft polenta as a plate.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture mixed mushroom version→cremini plus shiitake, oyster, or maitake

    Replace a third of the cremini with stemmed shiitake, torn oyster mushrooms, or pulled maitake. The wild mushrooms shed less water and brown faster, so add them to the pan a minute after the cremini have started releasing their liquid. The dish gains a more complex, layered woodland flavor; treat the rest of the recipe identically.

  • Texture cream and sauce→mushrooms in a quick pan cream

    After the soy and before the lemon, pour in 120 g / ½ cup heavy cream and simmer 2 to 3 minutes until it reduces to a clinging sauce. The dish becomes a rich mushroom ragout for spooning over toast, pasta, chicken, or steak; finish with the lemon and a little extra thyme. The acid keeps the cream from reading heavy.

  • Occasion to make it a plate→mushroom toast with a soft egg

    Pile the finished mushrooms on a thick slice of toasted, garlic-rubbed sourdough, top with a fried or soft-poached egg, and shower with parsley and flaky salt. The runny yolk binds the mushrooms and the toast soaks up the buttery soy-lemon pan juices. A vegetarian main in five extra minutes.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • cremini or white button mushrooms → portobello (diced), shiitake, oyster, or maitake Portobello is the same species fully mature, meatier; dice it to cremini size. Shiitake, oyster, and maitake all sear well and shed less water, so they brown faster; reduce the dry-sear water phase. See the mixed-mushroom modification.
  • light soy sauce → Worcestershire sauce, or a splash of dry sherry plus a pinch of salt A few drops of Worcestershire give the same glutamate depth with a tangier edge; dry sherry deglazes in the more classic French direction (use 15 g and let it cook off). Either keeps the savory backbone.
  • fresh thyme → fresh rosemary (less), tarragon, or dried thyme Rosemary is more aggressive, use one small sprig; tarragon pushes the dish French-anise and is excellent with mushrooms; dried thyme works in the butter (¼ tsp) but can scorch, so add it with the soy rather than the butter.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → light soy sauce make it yourselfstub A house light soy contributes a fresher, cleaner glutamate hit than bottled, and here the soy is doing precise work, a small amount deepening the mushrooms own umami without announcing itself. A single long-ferment batch seasons dozens of dishes and the difference shows on a finish this restrained.

Notes

The water-then-brown sequence is the whole recipe. A mushroom is roughly 90% water, and in a hot pan that water comes out fast, floods the surface, and drops the temperature below boiling so the mushrooms simmer in their own liquid, pale, soft, and squeaky. Only once that water has fully evaporated does the pan climb back hot enough to brown the now-dry surface and firm the texture to a meaty chew. So the order is non-negotiable: dry sear first over high heat in an uncrowded pan, wait out the flood-and-evaporate, and add fat and salt only after the second sizzle tells you the water is gone. Butter or salt added early just prolongs the steaming and you never get the crust.

Why salt goes in late. Salt draws water out of the mushrooms osmotically. Added at the start, it pulls even more liquid into the pan and lengthens the steaming phase; added after the mushrooms have browned, it seasons without flooding. This is the opposite of how you salt most vegetables, and getting it wrong is the second most common reason home-cooked mushrooms come out gray and limp (crowding is the first).

Why soy in a French dish. Mushrooms carry free glutamate plus the ribonucleotides that multiply perceived umami; soy sauce is almost pure glutamate. A small splash compounds the mushrooms own savoriness so the dish tastes deeper and meatier than butter and garlic alone would deliver, and at 2 teaspoons across a pound it reads as savory depth, not as soy. It is the same trick that a spoon of fish sauce or a Parmesan rind does in a tomato sauce, an umami amplifier hiding in the background.

Why the lemon at the end. Browned mushrooms in butter are rich and round and can read flat and one-note. A squeeze of lemon at the very end, off the heat, sharpens everything: it cuts the butter, brightens the earthy mushroom flavor, and gives the dish a top-note that keeps it from sitting heavy. Add it last so the acid stays bright rather than cooking dull.
