Recipe

Cioppino (San Francisco Italian-American seafood stew)

The North Beach Italian-American seafood stew built around a tomato-fennel-saffron broth fortified with seafood stock and dry white wine, loaded with Dungeness crab (in the shell), halibut chunks, head-on shrimp, littleneck clams, and mussels, finished with basil, parsley, and crushed red pepper, served with toasted sourdough rubbed with raw garlic. The clams and mussels are staged into the broth so all bivalves open within a single 60-second window. A whole-table dish: cracked shells, broth-soaked bread, hands and bibs.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Purge the clams and scrub the mussels at least 30 minutes ahead. Clams need a 30 minute saltwater purge (3% brine) to release sand; mussels need scrubbing and debearding. Sandy bivalves ruin the broth and the dish in a single pot, there is no recovery.
  • Order Dungeness crab cleaned and cracked from the fishmonger if possible, or do it yourself before the cook begins. Cracking a whole crab mid-stew is the wrong time, the meat tears, the shells splinter into the broth, and the timing on the other seafood collapses.
  • Stage every seafood addition by minute in the recipe (the bivalves open within a single 60 second window only if added in the right sequence). Pull crab, halibut, shrimp, clams, and mussels onto separate plates with the addition order labeled; mid-stew is the wrong time to be picking through a fridge drawer.
  • Toast the sourdough in the last 10 minutes of the broth simmer. The bread is structural carrier for the broth and the toast must be hot and crackly when the bowls go to the table; pre-toasted bread held warm goes soft, and untoasted bread sponges all the broth into one slice.

  5. 2 g fennel seed, toasted and crushed, dry-toasted in a small skillet 60 seconds until fragrant, then crushed in a mortar or under a knife blade
  6. 2 g red pepper flakes (crushed red pepper)
  7. 2 g dried oregano (Mediterranean)
  8. 4 g fine sea salt

Phase 2, broth build

  1. 1 × 6 oz / 170 g can tomato paste
  2. 350 ml dry white wine (Pinot Grigio, Sauvignon Blanc, or unoaked Italian white)
  3. 2 × 28 oz / 794 g can canned San Marzano whole peeled tomatoes, crushed by hand, turned out into a bowl, crushed by hand into rough 2 to 3 cm chunks (do not blend, the broth wants tomato pieces not puree)
  4. 1000 ml seafood stock (homemade roasted-shell version, see recipe) or 1:1 fish stock and clam juice — plus low-sodium chicken stock at 1:1 is the canonical Italian-American shortcut)
  5. 0.3 g saffron threads, crumbled between the fingers; steeped in 30 ml hot water 10 minutes before adding — (a generous pinch, about 30 threads; the Provencal-Genovese signature, do not skip)
  6. 8 g anchovy fillets in oil, mashed to paste with the side of a knife — (2 fillets; structural umami fortifier, the broth reads less identifiable as anchovy than as deep seafood depth)
  7. 2 each bay leaves
  8. 4 each fresh thyme — (4 sprigs)
  9. 1 each lemon peel, wide strips (no pith), peel pared off in wide strips with a vegetable peeler, white pith scraped away with the back of a knife — (peel from 1 lemon; the volatile citrus oils bloom in the broth, structural with the saffron)

Phase 3, seafood (added in stages)

  1. 900 g Dungeness crab, live or pre-cooked, cleaned and quartered, if live: cooked in a separate pot of boiling salted water 12 minutes, then cleaned (carapace off, gills out, body quartered into 4 legged pieces, claws cracked with the back of a heavy knife); if pre-cooked, cleaned and quartered the same way
  2. 500 g halibut fillet, skinless, cut into 3 cm chunks; pat dry with paper towels — (or any firm white fish: cod, lingcod, black sea bass, Pacific rockfish; see substitutions)
  3. 400 g head-on shrimp (or shell-on tail-on jumbo shrimp), shells left on for flavor and presentation; deveined through the back with a paring knife if needed — (8 to 12 large head-on shrimp; the heads carry concentrated flavor that fortifies the broth, shells stay on for the visual)
  4. 700 g littleneck clams (or Manila clams), purged in salt water, soaked in cold water with 35 g kosher salt per 1 L water for 30 to 60 minutes to purge grit; lifted out of the soak water (not poured); rinsed once more under cold water — (about 20 to 25 littlenecks or 40 to 50 Manilas; both species work, see ingredient page)
  5. 500 g mussels, debearded, beards pulled off (grip the protruding fiber tuft between thumb and a paper towel, pull sharply toward the hinge end), rinsed; any cracked or gaping-unresponsive mussels discarded — (about 25 to 30 mussels; rope-grown blue mussels are the cleanest option)

  4. 1 each fresh lemon, cut into wedges for the table — (8 wedges; the finishing acid that lifts the broth from rich-tomato to balanced-seafood)
  5. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil (finishing pour)

Execution

  1. 1
    Prepare the Dungeness crab. If using a live crab: bring a large pot of heavily salted water (35 g salt per 1 L) to a rolling boil; chill the live 900 g crab on ice 20 to 30 minutes to slow metabolism; lower the crab head-first into the boiling water and cook 12 minutes; lift out into an ice bath 5 minutes to stop cooking. If using pre-cooked: skip this step. Either way, clean the crab: lift off the carapace (top shell), pull out and discard the gray gills (the "dead man's fingers" lining the body cavity) and the small pointed apron on the underside; rinse the body cavity briefly under cold water but reserve any orange-cream "crab butter" (hepatopancreas) in the body cavity, as this is broth gold. Quarter the body into 4 leg-bearing sections; crack each leg and claw with the back of a heavy knife (so eaters can extract meat at the table) but leave the meat in the shell.
  2. 2
    Build the fennel-aromatic base. In a wide 6 to 8 quart Dutch oven over medium heat, warm the 45 g / 3 tbsp olive oil. Add the 250 g onion, 250 g fennel bulb dice, 4 g salt, and 2 g / 1 tsp toasted fennel seed. Sweat 10 to 12 minutes, stirring occasionally, until both vegetables turn translucent and start to take on a faint golden-edge color (the goal is softened and sweet, not browned; the broth wants the vegetables silky and dissolved into the background rather than caramelized). Blooming aromatics
  3. 3
    Add the 30 g / 6 to 8 minced garlic cloves, the 2 g / ½ to ¾ tsp red pepper flakes, the 2 g / 1 tsp dried oregano, and the 8 g / 2 anchovy fillets (mashed to paste). Stir and cook 90 seconds, until the garlic smells fragrant and the anchovy has dissolved. Push the aromatics to the edges of the pot, drop the 60 g / ¼ cup tomato paste into the cleared center, and let it sizzle in the oil for 60 to 90 seconds without stirring, until the paste turns from bright red to a deeper rust-red and a faint sweet-roasted aroma rises (the tomato-paste-bloom step is the structural umami amplifier; underdone tomato paste reads metallic). Blooming aromatics
  4. 4
    Pour in the 350 ml / 1½ cups dry white wine; scrape up any fond. Increase heat to medium-high and reduce the wine by half (about 175 ml), 3 to 4 minutes; the alcohol burns off and the acid concentrates. The pot should smell of wine, garlic, and tomato. Deglazing
  5. 5
    Add the 800 g / 1 can crushed San Marzano tomatoes (hand-crushed, not pureed); the 1000 ml / 4 cups seafood stock; the 0.3 g saffron (pre-steeped in 30 ml hot water; pour in both saffron and the steeping water); the 2 bay leaves; the 4 thyme sprigs; the wide-strip lemon peel from 1 lemon. Bring to a gentle simmer; reduce heat to maintain a bare tremor of a simmer (about 90 °C / 195 °F). Cook uncovered 25 to 30 minutes, until the broth has deepened to a brick-red color and lost the raw-tomato note; taste and adjust salt (typically another 2 to 4 g / ½ to 1 tsp). The broth is the structural element of the dish; everything else is texture and protein.
  6. 6
    Stage in the Dungeness crab. Lower the cleaned, quartered 900 g crab pieces (including any reserved crab butter from the body cavity) into the simmering broth. The shells release additional crab flavor into the broth and warm through; the meat does not need additional cooking if the crab was pre-cooked. Cover and simmer 4 minutes. If the crab is large or cold from the refrigerator, 6 minutes is fine.
  7. 7
    Stage in the firm white fish and the slower-opening clams. Add the 500 g halibut chunks (patted dry, 3 cm pieces) and the 700 g purged littleneck or Manila clams to the broth. Tuck the clams into the broth (shells will spread the broth as they open), distribute the halibut chunks evenly. Cover and cook 3 minutes; the clams take 5 to 7 minutes total to open and the halibut takes 4 to 6 minutes by 3 cm thickness, so the clams need the head start. Staged bivalve steam
  8. 8
    Stage in the faster-opening mussels and the shrimp. Add the 500 g debearded mussels and the 400 g head-on shrimp. Tuck the mussels among the clams; arrange the shrimp on top so the heat hits them last. Cover and cook 2 to 3 more minutes, until the bulk of the clams and mussels are open and the shrimp turn opaque-pink. Pull off the heat immediately; the residual heat finishes the last few open-shells and the dish stops before anything overcooks. Discard any bivalves that have not opened after a full minute past the rest of the batch. Staged bivalve steam
  9. 9
    While the seafood cooks, make the garlic toast. Heat a heavy skillet or grill pan over medium-high heat. Brush both sides of the 400 g / 6 to 8 sourdough slices with the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil. Toast 2 to 3 minutes per side until deeply golden and crisp on the surface, slightly chewy at the center. Immediately rub one side of each hot toast with the cut face of the 8 g / 2 halved garlic cloves; the toast acts as a microplane on the garlic, depositing a tiny aromatic film. Pile the toasts on a board next to the bowls. Maillard sear
  10. 10
    Finish and serve. Stir in the 10 g basil chiffonade, the 15 g chopped parsley, and the 5 g chopped fennel fronds; taste and adjust salt and acid (a splash of lemon juice from a wedge if the broth reads heavy on the tomato; a pinch more salt if the broth reads thin). Ladle the broth and seafood into wide shallow bowls; arrange a crab section, a few clams and mussels in their shells, a halibut chunk, and a couple of shrimp in each bowl. Drizzle the bowls with the 30 g / 2 tbsp finishing olive oil. Serve with lemon wedges on the side and the garlic toast on the board. Each diner pulls bread, cracks crab and shells with their hands, dunks bread into the broth, and squeezes lemon into the bowl as desired. The dish is intentionally messy; provide bibs or aprons and a large empty bowl on the table for discarded shells. Pan emulsion (mantecare)
Safety
  • Bivalves should snap shut when tapped sharply pre-cook; discard any that stay open or are broken. After cooking, discard any bivalves that did not open after a full minute past the rest of the batch. Bivalves are filter-feeders and harbor whatever bacteria, viruses, and biotoxins (Vibrio, hepatitis A, norovirus, paralytic shellfish poisoning toxins) were in the water they filtered. A bivalve already gaping pre-cook is dead, and dead bivalves are a food safety hazard because of bacterial decomposition in the gut. A bivalve that fails to open during cooking has not heated through enough to be safe and may have been dead going into the pot; discard rather than force open. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein seafood mix→with rockfish and lingcod instead of halibut (Pacific Coast canonical)

    Replace the 500 g halibut with an equal weight of Pacific rockfish or lingcod, cut into 3 cm chunks. The structural role is identical (firm white fish that holds its shape in broth); the dish reads more authentically Pacific Coast Italian-American (rockfish was the original fishermen-cooked fish), and the broth flavor profile is slightly more delicate.

  • Protein crab unavailability→with extra shrimp and lump crab meat folded in at the end

    Skip the live or whole Dungeness; double the shrimp to 800 g (head-on if possible) and fold in 200 g of refrigerated lump crab meat (not pasteurized) at the end with the herbs. The dish loses the cracked-shell drama and the structural crab-shell flavor in the broth; in compensation, add 250 ml extra clam juice or seafood stock at the start of Phase 2 to fortify the broth flavor.

  • Regional regional→as cacciucco alla Livornese (Tuscan ancestor)

    Cacciucco is the Tuscan ancestor dish to cioppino; the differences are: red wine instead of white (use a dry medium-bodied Italian red like Chianti); no saffron (Tuscan version is tomato-and-red-wine forward without the Provencal saffron influence); the seafood mix is more rustic (octopus, squid, mussels, mantis shrimp, rockfish), no Dungeness; the broth is thicker, more reduced; and the dish is served over toasted bread that has been rubbed with garlic and placed in the bowl before the broth is ladled (a much more direct bread-in-broth presentation). Adjust accordingly.

  • Occasion time-shortcut→with bottled clam juice broth shortcut

    Skip the homemade seafood stock; use 500 ml bottled clam juice (Bar Harbor or Snow's, the canonical Italian-American shortcut) plus 500 ml low-sodium chicken broth in place of the 1000 ml seafood stock. The dish is 85 percent of the homemade-stock version and is the standard Italian-American restaurant approach; the only real loss is the slight depth from the roasted-shell stock.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • Dungeness crab → blue crab (whole, cleaned, quartered) or snow crab clusters See dungeness-crab ingredient page for substitution details. Blue crab is the East Coast equivalent; count 2 to 3 small blue crabs per Dungeness. Snow crab clusters are an acceptable substitute for the leg-meat-focused presentation but skip the body-meat dimension.
  • halibut → cod, lingcod, black sea bass, Pacific rockfish, or monkfish See halibut ingredient page. Cod is the most-available substitute; rockfish and lingcod are the Pacific Coast canonical choices; monkfish gives a lobster-like texture.
  • head-on shrimp → shell-on tail-on jumbo shrimp The heads add concentrated flavor and visual drama; tail-on jumbo shell-on shrimp work well for the texture and shell-on flavor, with less broth fortification.
  • littleneck or Manila clams → cockles, more mussels, or canned chopped clams in the broth Cockles cook in roughly the same window as Manila clams. Doubling the mussels and adding 250 ml bottled clam juice into the broth is the canonical shortcut for clam-unavailable cooks.
  • mussels → more clams or omit Mussels can be omitted from cioppino without structural damage to the dish; the clams cover the bivalve role. Increase clams by 200 g to keep the total bivalve mass.
  • San Marzano canned tomatoes → any good-quality canned whole peeled tomatoes (Bianco DiNapoli, Pomi, store brand whole-peeled) San Marzano DOP is canonical but expensive; any high-quality whole-peeled tomato works. Avoid pre-crushed or pre-pureed canned tomato (the texture is too smooth and the dish reads as a tomato soup rather than a stew).
  • saffron → no good substitute; reduce dish ambition or use ¼ tsp paprika as a color-only sub Saffron is structural in cioppino; without it the dish reads as a generic Italian-American seafood-tomato stew. The flavor compound (safranal) and color compound (crocin) have no good substitute; if saffron is unavailable, use ¼ tsp paprika as a color-only sub and the dish becomes "tomato seafood stew" rather than cioppino specifically.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → tomato paste make it yourselfstub Reducing whole canned tomatoes to a deep red-brown paste over 6 to 8 hours of slow oven-drying produces a concentrated tomato paste with substantially more aromatic complexity than the can. A weekend project; one batch fills a small jar for months.
  • → sourdough bread make it yourself A naturally-leavened country loaf carries the slight tang and open-crumb structure that San Francisco sourdough is famous for; the bread is the structural carb that defines cioppino service. A two-day project once the starter is established.

Notes

Origin story (and why the saffron is non-negotiable). Cioppino is a dish that traveled from Liguria to San Francisco in the late 1800s with Genovese fishermen who settled in North Beach and worked the Wharf (Fisherman''s Wharf). The Ligurian ancestor dish, ciuppin, was a rougher fish stew (broth, onions, garlic, tomato, white wine, a mixed catch of rockfish, no saffron). The San Francisco version added the saffron (a Provencal-Genovese cross-pollination, since the same fishermen traveled the Mediterranean Riviera between Liguria and Marseille, and saffron was a regional currency along that coast), the Dungeness crab in the shell (the Pacific signature), and the bread-on-the-side service (rather than broth-strained-over-bread, the Italian rustic preparation). The dish stabilized at North Beach restaurants in the 1900s to 1920s and became the canonical Italian-American Pacific Coast seafood plate. Removing saffron from cioppino removes the cross-pollination dimension that distinguishes the dish from generic East Coast Italian-American zuppa di pesce; the dish becomes structurally cacciucco (Tuscan) rather than cioppino (Ligurian-Provencal-San Franciscan). The 0.3 g of saffron in this recipe is a generous pinch; it should color the broth a noticeably deeper red-orange and add a faintly mineral, honey-leather backnote.

The bivalve timing is the technique. The hardest operational task in cioppino is getting the clams and mussels to open within the same window without overcooking the seafood that goes in first. Clams take 5 to 7 minutes from a cold start in hot broth; mussels take 3 to 5 minutes; halibut chunks take 4 to 6 minutes by 3 cm thickness; shrimp take 2 to 3 minutes. The staging in this recipe (crab first for 4 minutes, clams plus halibut next for 3 minutes, mussels plus shrimp last for 2 to 3 minutes) produces a synchronized finish where the slowest bivalves open in the same 60-second window as the fastest, and no protein has overcooked. See the staged-bivalve-steam technique page for the underlying mechanism. The single most common failure mode in home cioppino is adding everything at once, which gives rubbery mussels (overcooked) and locked-clam shells (undercooked).

The umami architecture. The broth carries three structural umami sources: the anchovy paste in Phase 1 (10 g / 2 fillets, glutamate-rich, dissolves invisibly into the background), the tomato paste bloomed in fat at the start of Phase 2 (60 g, the umami-and-color amplifier), and the cooked-out San Marzano tomatoes through the 30-minute simmer (free glutamate from ripe peeled tomato). Layered on top is the seafood-stock contribution (homemade roasted-shell stock or the clam-juice-plus-chicken-broth shortcut), which adds 5''-nucleotides (inosinate from fish, guanylate from shellfish) that combine with the glutamate to produce the umami synergy effect (glutamate plus 5''-nucleotides creates a 7 to 30-fold flavor intensification, the same mechanism behind dashi and Italian Parmesan-and-tomato sauces). The finishing acid is the lemon at the table; the broth''s structural acid comes from the wine reduction in Phase 2. Salt-acid-fat-umami are all present in deliberate layers; cioppino served without lemon wedges at the table is operationally incomplete.

Sourcing the seafood. The dish is forgiving of substitutions across the protein lineup but the structural mix matters: at least one crustacean (crab, lobster, or shrimp), at least one firm white fish (halibut, cod, rockfish, lingcod, sea bass), and at least one type of bivalve (clams or mussels, ideally both). H Mart, 99 Ranch, Whole Foods, and any well-stocked seafood counter will carry the full set. Live Dungeness is sold November through May on the West Coast; pre-cooked frozen Dungeness is available year-round at Costco, H Mart, and Asian seafood markets. Halibut is best frozen-at-sea Pacific (Costco, Whole Foods); the supermarket "fresh" version is often a 2 to 4 day-old thawed product. Head-on shrimp are at Asian seafood counters and high-end Italian markets; tail-on jumbo shell-on is the supermarket compromise. Littleneck and Manila clams are sold live at any Italian, Portuguese, or Asian fishmonger; mussels are typically rope-grown blue mussels (Whole Foods, Trader Joe''s carries them in mesh bags).

The bread is structural, not garnish. San Francisco sourdough is the canonical bread for cioppino (the regional pairing; the city is famous for both), and the bread serves three functions: a vehicle for soaking up the broth (the broth is the best part, and the bread reaches it after the eater has worked through the shells), a textural counterpoint to the soft seafood and silky broth (the toasted crust is the crunch element), and a structural carb that turns the stew from "main dish, two pounds of seafood, still hungry afterward" into a complete meal. Use a 2-day-old sourdough loaf if possible (open crumb, slight tang, sturdy crust); fresh sourdough works but the dry-out bath in the broth is less effective. Rub the hot toast with raw garlic immediately after toasting; the rough toast surface acts as a microplane on the garlic.

Service notes. Cioppino is a whole-table dish. The eater works with their hands: cracks the crab leg shells (already cracked at the table by the cook, but additional cracking happens), pulls out the white meat, pulls the meat from the mussels and clams (using one shell as a lever and pincer), peels the shrimp from the head (sucking the head juices if inclined, the canonical American Italian way), spoons broth into bread, and squeezes lemon as the dish reads heavier. Provide bibs or aprons, a large empty bowl in the center for discarded shells, finger bowls or wet napkins for the inevitable sticky fingers, plenty of bread, and the lemon wedges at the table. The leftover broth is excellent the next day over pasta or with grilled bread; the seafood does not store well as leftovers.

Sources
  • Adapted from the Genovese-immigrant seafood-stew canon of the late-nineteenth-century North Beach district of San Francisco, where Ligurian fishermen working the Wharf adapted the Genoa-region ciuppin (rough fish stew with rockfish, onion, garlic, tomato, and wine) to the Pacific seafood available off the California coast. The dish stabilized in the early twentieth century around the combination of Dungeness crab, local rockfish or halibut, prawns, clams, and mussels, with the tomato-fennel-saffron-wine broth that distinguishes it from East Coast Italian-American cacciucco or Iberian zarzuela. — Cioppino is structurally an Italian-American invention even though the ancestor dish (ciuppin from Liguria, scuppin in some dialects) was a thinner, more rustic Italian fish broth strained over toasted bread. The San Francisco version added Dungeness crab in the shell (the regional Pacific signature), white wine instead of red, saffron (the Provencal-Genovese cross-pollination that traveled along the Mediterranean Riviera), fennel bulb as a sweet aromatic, and the move from strained-broth-over-toast to whole-stew-in-bowl with bread on the side. The dish is a celebration plate at North Beach restaurants like Tadich Grill, Sam's, and the Original Joe's, and it is also a home dish for Pacific Coast Italian-American families when the Dungeness season opens in November. The structure is wide: the broth recipe and the bivalve-timing technique are the constants; the specific seafood mix flexes with what the seafood counter offers on the day. The version here is the canonical mix (Dungeness, halibut, shrimp, clams, mussels) but the dish is forgiving of substitutions across the protein lineup.
