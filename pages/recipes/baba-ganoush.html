Recipe

Baba ganoush

The Levantine smoked-eggplant dip: whole eggplants charred directly over a gas flame (or under a broiler, or on a grill) until the skin is fully blackened and the flesh has collapsed to a soft smoky pulp, then drained, mashed with tahini, lemon, garlic, and salt, and crowned with olive oil, pomegranate seeds, mint, and parsley. The structural defining feature is the smoke: oven-roasted eggplant produces a fine dip but a different and inferior one. The fire-char carries the entire identity of the dish, which is why every serious Levantine kitchen owns or improvises a way to char eggplant directly over flame.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • A gas burner, a charcoal grill, or a broiler at maximum is required. Oven-roasted eggplant is a fallback (see substitutions) but the dish is structurally a smoked-eggplant dip, not a roasted-eggplant dip. If you have no way to apply direct flame, you can still make the recipe and it will be good; it will not be the dish.
  • Drain the charred eggplant flesh in a colander for 15 minutes before mashing. The drain removes excess water that would dilute the dip and produce a runny texture; the drained flesh mashes into a firm, spreadable consistency.
  • Globe eggplants are the canonical choice; choose medium-sized firm ones (about 400 g each), heavy for their size, with tight glossy skin. Soft or hollow-feeling eggplants are old, bitter, and seedy.

Phase 1, the char

  1. 1200 g globe eggplants, pricked all over with a fork at least 6 times each (to vent steam during the char and prevent explosions) — (2 large or 3 medium eggplants; the structural canvas; medium are easier to handle and char more evenly)

Phase 2, the dip base

  1. 80 g tahini (well-stirred, smooth, single-ingredient Lebanese or Palestinian) — , and is generous compared to many recipes)
  2. 30 g fresh lemon juice
  3. 8 g garlic clove, peeled, finely grated on a microplane or pounded to a paste with a pinch of salt — (2 medium cloves; raw garlic; the structural sharp counterpoint to the smoke)
  4. 5 g fine sea salt
  5. 1 g ground cumin

Phase 3, the finish

  1. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil, for finishing
  2. 30 g pomegranate seeds (in season) or sumac
  3. 5 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
  4. 3 g fresh mint leaves, thinly sliced

Execution

  1. 1
    Char the eggplants: set the 1200 g pricked eggplants directly over a high gas flame (or on a hot charcoal grill, or under a broiler at maximum). Turn with tongs every 4 to 5 minutes so all sides char evenly. Total time is 20 to 25 minutes; the eggplants are done when the skins are fully blackened and crackled all over, the eggplants have collapsed visibly (they look deflated and sag when picked up), and the flesh inside is completely soft when pierced with a knife (no resistance). The char should smell deeply of woodsmoke; this is the structural flavor of the dish.
  2. 2
    Transfer the charred eggplants to a colander set over a bowl; let cool 10 minutes (they are too hot to handle right away and need to release their first wave of steam). When cool enough to handle, slit each eggplant lengthwise and use a spoon to scrape the soft smoky flesh into the colander; discard the blackened skin and the stem. A few flecks of black skin in the flesh are acceptable and contribute to the rustic appearance; do not obsess about removing every speck.
  3. 3
    Let the flesh drain in the colander 15 minutes; press gently with a spoon every 5 minutes to encourage water release. The drained flesh should weigh about 600 g; the drained-off water (about 100 g) is bitter eggplant juice and is discarded.
  4. 4
    Transfer the drained eggplant flesh to a bowl. Mash with a fork (not a food processor; the food processor produces a smooth purée that reads more like an industrial dip; the rustic fork-mash texture is structural for baba ganoush). Mash for 90 seconds until the flesh is broken down into a coarse pulp but still has some visible texture.
  5. 5
    Add the 80 g / ⅓ cup tahini, 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, 8 g / 2 garlic cloves (grated), 5 g / 1 tsp fine sea salt, and 1 g / ½ tsp cumin. Stir vigorously with a fork or wooden spoon for 60 seconds; the mixture will transform from a loose pulp into a creamy spreadable dip as the tahini binds. Taste and adjust: the dip should read smoky-forward, with tahini body, bright lemon, gentle garlic, and clean salt. Add more salt or lemon in small increments (1 g salt, 5 g lemon at a time) if needed.
  6. 6
    Let stand 15 minutes at room temperature for the flavors to integrate; the smoke deepens, the garlic mellows slightly, and the tahini binds further. Taste again and adjust if needed.
  7. 7
    Plate: spoon the dip onto a wide shallow plate. Using the back of the spoon, create a few sweeping ridges and a shallow well in the center. Pour the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil across the surface so it pools in the ridges and the well. Scatter the 30 g pomegranate seeds (or sprinkle the sumac), the 5 g chopped parsley, and the 3 g sliced mint. Serve at room temperature with warm pita, crudités, or warm flatbread. Best on the day made.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional mutabbal (with yogurt)→as Palestinian mutabbal

    Add 60 g of thick whole-milk Greek yogurt (or labneh) to the mash with the tahini in step 5. The dish reads creamier, slightly tangier, and slightly less smoke-forward; this is the canonical Palestinian mutabbal. Both Lebanese baba ganoush (dairy-free) and Palestinian mutabbal (with yogurt) are correct; choose by tradition or preference.

  • Regional with charred eggplant + walnut (muhammara-adjacent)→as a smoked eggplant and walnut dip

    Add 60 g toasted walnuts and 1 tsp pomegranate molasses to the bowl with the eggplant flesh; reduce the tahini to 40 g. The dish becomes structurally adjacent to muhammara (the Syrian red-pepper-walnut dip) with the smoked eggplant taking the structural place of the red pepper. A modern crossover dish.

  • Occasion with a generous spice crown→with a baharat-and-pomegranate finish

    In addition to the standard finish, sprinkle 1 tsp of baharat (a Levantine spice blend: cumin, allspice, cinnamon, black pepper, cardamom, cloves) over the dip and scatter 30 g of toasted pine nuts. The spice crown reads richer and more substantial; appropriate when baba ganoush is served as a more central dish (with grilled meats) rather than as a starter.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • globe eggplants → eggplant roasted in a 250°C / 480°F oven 45 to 60 minutes (no fire) When fire is not available, oven-roasted eggplant is a fallback; the dish reads cleaner and less smoky but is not the canonical baba ganoush. To partially compensate, add a small pinch (under ½ tsp) of smoked paprika or 1 drop of liquid smoke to the dip. Acceptable for kitchens without flame but structurally different.
  • tahini (well-stirred, smooth) → a 50/50 blend of tahini and Greek yogurt A lighter, tangier variant common in modern home cooking; the yogurt adds creaminess. The dish becomes adjacent to mutabbal. Acceptable but structurally a different dish.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → tahini (well-stirred, smooth, single-ingredient Lebanese or Palestinian) make it yourself Home-toasted-and-ground sesame tahini reads dramatically cleaner than industrial tahini; in baba ganoush where the tahini is the structural binding fat, the difference matters.

Notes

Why fire is structural. The Maillard browning and the smoke compounds produced when eggplant skin is charred directly over flame are the defining flavors of baba ganoush. Three things happen during the open-flame char that do not happen in the oven: (1) the skin burns to char (a thin layer of carbonized cellulose) that contributes structural smoke flavor when the flesh is scraped from underneath; (2) the high direct heat causes rapid evaporation and concentration of the eggplant flesh, producing a more savory and concentrated final texture; (3) the wood-smoke or gas-flame combustion products penetrate the flesh through the steam vents created by the pricking, infusing the dip with the structural smoky character. Oven-roasted eggplant produces a perfectly nice dish, but it is structurally a smoked-eggplant-adjacent dip rather than baba ganoush; the smoke is what the dish is named for.

Why the drain is structural. Charred eggplant flesh holds a surprising amount of water; mashed into the dip undrained, the water dilutes the tahini, the dip never reaches the structural creamy consistency, and the smoke flavor gets stretched too thin. The 15-minute drain removes about 15 to 20% of the flesh weight as bitter eggplant water, concentrates the smoky flavor, and produces the structural firm-spreadable dip texture. Skip this step and the dip reads watery and pallid.

Why fork-mash, not food processor. The rustic fork-mashed texture is structural for baba ganoush; the slight visible chunks of eggplant flesh are the textural signature that distinguishes the dish from a smooth purée dip like hummus. Food-processed baba ganoush reads as a generic eggplant spread, like something from a supermarket deli case; the fork mash reads as the home-kitchen dish it has always been. The five extra minutes of hand work are worth it for the structural texture difference.

Versatility. Baba ganoush serves as: a mezze starter (the canonical role) with warm pita, a sandwich spread (especially good in falafel wraps), a base under grilled vegetables or grilled meat, a dip for crudités (raw cucumber, carrot, fennel, bell pepper), thinned with water as a sauce for grain bowls, or folded into pasta as a smoky vegetable sauce (try with farfalle and feta).

Pairs across Lebanese (canonical baba ganoush), Palestinian (mutabbal, the close cousin with yogurt), Syrian, Jordanian, Israeli, Egyptian, Turkish (where the dish is called patlıcan ezmesi, with similar but distinct preparation), Greek (melitzanosalata, an Aegean cousin), and broader Mediterranean cookery. The dish is one of the most globally recognized Levantine starters.

Storage. Best on the day made; the smoke flavor degrades within 24 hours and the dip darkens slightly on the surface. Keeps 3 days refrigerated in a sealed container; bring to room temperature 30 minutes before serving and stir to re-distribute the oil. The fresh herbs and the pomegranate seeds should be added fresh at each service. Does not freeze well; the texture turns grainy.

Sources
  • The Levantine canon. Baba ganoush (in Lebanon and Syria; called mutabbal in Palestine and Israel; both names refer to similar but slightly different dishes, with mutabbal often containing yogurt for additional richness) is documented across the eastern Mediterranean as the canonical smoked-eggplant tahini dip. — Two structural splits: (1) the smoke source. Wood or charcoal grill produces the deepest smoke; gas burner is the home standard and produces excellent smoke; broiler produces less smoke but the most consistent char. (2) The dairy. Lebanese baba ganoush is dairy-free (tahini, lemon, garlic, salt). Palestinian mutabbal often includes a spoon of plain yogurt or labneh for additional creaminess. The version here is the dairy-free Lebanese form; the dairy variant is in modifications.
