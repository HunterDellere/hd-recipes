Recipe

Saba shioyaki

Salt-grilled mackerel: saba fillets cross-hatch scored, salted at the furi-jio rate and rested 25 minutes to draw surface water and firm the flesh through protein denaturation, sake-rinsed, patted dry, then cooked skin-side-up under a roaring broiler until the subcutaneous fat renders and the skin blisters to lacquered crackling and the flesh sets fully through. The teishoku and izakaya canon: an oily blue fish managed entirely by salt and dry radiant heat, plated with cool grated daikon oroshi, a soy pour, and a wedge of citrus.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Start the Japanese short-grain rice FIRST: 20-minute soak, 12-minute cook, 10-minute rest. The grill itself takes under 10 minutes, so the rice is the long pole and should be timed to finish as the fish comes off the heat.
  • Salt the fillets and rest them 25 minutes before grilling. This furi-jio cure is not optional seasoning: it draws surface water that would otherwise steam under the broiler and firms the flesh through partial protein denaturation, so the skin can blister rather than poach.
  • Grate the daikon oroshi while the fish cures and press the water off in a sieve. Watery oroshi floods the plate and dilutes the soy; the radish should be cold and barely moist at serving.

Phase 1, fish prep and cure

Phase 2, daikon oroshi

Phase 3, to serve

  3. 10 g scallion, green tops only, sliced into the thinnest rings — (1 stalk; for the final scatter)
  4. 2 g toasted sesame oil optional

Accompaniment

  1. 150 g Japanese short-grain rice (uncooked)

Execution

  1. 1
    Start the rice. Rinse the 150 g / ¾ cup Japanese short-grain rice in cold water until the water runs nearly clear, soak 20 minutes, then cook (rice cooker or 1:1.2 rice-to-water by volume, covered, low, 12 minutes) and let it rest covered 10 minutes off heat. Time it to finish as the fish comes off the broiler.
  2. 2
    Prep the fillets. Run a fingernail across the 350 g / 2 saba fillets from tail to head to feel for pin-bones and pull each with tweezers in a clean draw. Pat both sides thoroughly dry. The skin must be intact and silvery; a tear lets the fillet buckle under the heat.
  3. 3
    Score the skin. Lay each fillet skin-up and, with a sharp paring knife held at a low angle, cut a shallow cross-hatch: parallel lines about 5 mm apart, then perpendicular, just through the skin and barely into the surface fat, never into the muscle blocks. The score lets the subcutaneous fat render out and blister rather than sit trapped under the skin, and it opens a path for the 6 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt to penetrate evenly in the next step. Fish skin scoring
  4. 4
    Apply the furi-jio cure. Set the scored fillets on a wire rack over a sheet pan. Sprinkle the 6 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt from a height of 20 to 30 cm so it lands evenly over both sides of both fillets (the high sprinkle is the furi-jio technique, it scatters the crystals in a fine even rain rather than clumps), working a little into the score lines. Rest, uncovered, in the refrigerator for 25 minutes. Within 10 minutes clear liquid beads on the surface as the salt draws water out by osmosis; by 25 minutes the surface reads tacky and the flesh has firmed visibly. Under-salted saba grills bland against its own richness; this rate seasons the eaten skin as well as the flesh. Dry brining
  5. 5
    While the fish cures, make the oroshi. Peel and grate the 200 g / 8 cm daikon section on the finest tooth of an oroshigane or a microplane, moving in a circular motion to keep the texture snowy and loose rather than stringy; grate the optional 5 g / 1 cm ginger alongside it. Pile the grated daikon into a fine sieve, let it drain 5 minutes, then press lightly with the back of a spoon to shed excess liquid. It should be moist but not wet. Keep it cold until plating.
  6. 6
    Set up the broiler. Move an oven rack so the fish will sit about 10 cm / 4 inches below the element and run the broiler on high for a full 5 minutes to saturate it with heat; a cold broiler poaches the fillet instead of blistering it. Line a sheet pan or broiler tray with foil and set a wire rack on it so radiant heat reaches the skin from above and air circulates below.
  7. 7
    Rinse and dry. Brush both sides of the cured fillets with the 15 g / 1 tbsp sake to lift residual fishy notes, then pat thoroughly dry once more (the cure keeps drawing water, and wet skin steams rather than crackles). Lay the fillets skin-side-up on the rack.
  8. 8
    Grill skin-side-up, no flip. Slide the tray under the broiler and grill 5 to 7 minutes without turning. The dry radiant heat works top-down: the rendering subcutaneous fat bastes the skin, the cross-hatch edges curl and char, and the skin blisters to a deep mahogany, lacquered crackling. Watch from minute 4; broilers vary and the thin skin goes from bronzed to scorched fast. The fish is done when the thickest part flakes cleanly under a fork and the flesh is opaque and just-set throughout (around 60°C internal, fully cooked, unlike a pan-seared medium-rare saba). If the fillets are thick and the skin colors before the center sets, drop the rack one level and give it another minute.
  9. 9
    Plate and serve. On each of two warmed plates, mound half the drained 200 g daikon oroshi as a loose hill and lay a fillet skin-side-up beside it so the crackling stays clear of liquid. Pour the 20 g / 4 tsp light soy sauce over the oroshi, not the skin (soy on the skin softens the crackling instantly); add the optional few drops of 2 g / ½ tsp toasted sesame oil to the oroshi. Scatter the 10 g / 1 stalk thin scallion rings over the fish and set a 25 g lemon wedge on each plate. The diner squeezes the citrus over the fish, pulls each bite through the soy-dressed daikon, and eats with the steamed rice; the rice, the radish, and the acid are the structural counters to the rich saba, not garnish.
Safety
  • Histamine (scombroid) risk on mackerel and other scombroid species Mackerel, tuna, mahi-mahi, sardines, and bluefish accumulate heat-stable histamine when held above 40°F after harvest. Histamine is not destroyed by cooking, freezing, curing, or salting, so unlike a pathogen there is no temperature that fixes a fish that has already spoiled. Source mackerel that has been held cold from harvest (vacuum-packed frozen fillets are often safer than fresh in this regard), and discard any fish with ammonia, off-smells, or a fishy or metallic taste in the raw flesh. This dish cooks the saba fully through, which addresses pathogens but does nothing for histamine; freshness at purchase is the only control. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional heat source→charcoal or gas-grilled (sumibi-yaki)

    After the 25-minute cure, grill over medium-high charcoal (binchotan if you have it) or a gas grill instead of the broiler: skin-side-down first for 3 to 4 minutes to set and crisp the skin, then flip and cook flesh-side-down 2 to 3 minutes until set through. Charcoal adds the smoke note that defines izakaya saba; the broiler is the indoor approximation. A grill basket makes the flip safe without tearing the skin.

  • Regional seasoning→mirin-boshi (sweet-salt dried finish)

    After salting, brush the fillets with a 2:1 mirin-to-soy glaze and air-dry on a rack in the refrigerator for 2 to 4 hours before grilling. The surface dehydrates slightly and the sugars in the mirin lacquer and caramelize under the broiler, giving a sweeter, glossier, more concentrated fish closer to a himono dried-fish preparation. Grill at one rack level lower to keep the sugars from scorching.

  • Protein protein→with Spanish mackerel (sawara), aji, or sardines

    Swap the saba for 350 g of Spanish mackerel, horse mackerel (aji), or butterflied sardines. The salt-grill technique is identical; thinner fillets (aji, sardines) need only 3 to 4 minutes under the broiler and a 15-minute cure. Sawara reads milder and a touch more delicate; sardines char fast and want close watching.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • mackerel fillets (saba) → Spanish mackerel (sawara), Atlantic mackerel, or butterflied sardines Spanish and Atlantic mackerel are nearly interchangeable with Pacific saba under the broiler; cure rate and grill time hold. Sardines work at small scale with a shorter cure and faster grill. Salmon is wrong here, the lipid chemistry is different and the dish loses its character.
  • daikon (oroshi) → Korean mu, watermelon radish, or large red radishes Korean mu is structurally close, slightly denser and milder; watermelon radish brings color but less of the isothiocyanate bite; red radishes work in a pinch but the color reads pink. For cutting the rich grilled saba, sharper is better, so grate from the tail end of the daikon if the upper end is too mild.
  • lemon → sudachi, yuzu, kabosu, or lime Sudachi is the canonical citrus for grilled saba (small, green, intensely aromatic, sharper and more floral than lemon); kabosu and yuzu are close. Lime is a passable everyday stand-in; lemon is the default. The acid is non-negotiable on grilled oily fish, the brand of citrus is flexible.
  • light soy sauce → ponzu, or tamari for gluten-free A citrus-soy ponzu turns the pour into the acid and the umami at once and is arguably the better choice; see the homemade alternatives below. Tamari is the gluten-free soy swap with the same glutamate load. A dab of grated daikon with a few drops of soy (daikon-oroshi-joyu) is the most traditional minimal version.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → light soy sauce make it yourselfstub A from-scratch shoyu controls the salt level and the glutamate depth far better than a generic bottle, and the difference is stark in a dish where the soy is poured raw over the fish rather than cooked into a sauce.
  • → ponzu (in place of the soy pour) make it yourself Yuzu or a fresh citrus blend steeped 24 hours with kombu and katsuobushi in soy and mirin makes a single pour that supplies both the acid and the umami the grilled saba needs, with aromatic top notes no bottle holds.

Notes

Why salt-grilling, and how it differs from the pan-sear. Shioyaki and the pan-seared saba solve the same problem (a 12 to 18% fat blue fish that turns greasy and fishy when mishandled) with opposite thermal strategies. The pan-sear drives intense conductive heat into the skin from a smoking metal surface and pulls the fish at a medium-rare 56°C so the lipid stays creamy; shioyaki uses gentler, dry radiant heat from above and cooks the fillet all the way through to a flaking, set texture. The salt-grilled version is the everyday teishoku and izakaya standard precisely because it is forgiving: there is no flip to tear the skin, no smoking pan, and the fully-cooked flesh is more familiar to most palates than medium-rare oily fish. The two share a backbone, the cross-hatch score and the salt pretreatment, but the doneness target and the heat source are the divergence.

The furi-jio cure. Furi-jio means "sprinkled salt," and the height of the sprinkle is the technique: salt thrown from 20 to 30 cm up lands as a fine even rain across the whole fillet instead of clumping where your fingers release it. The salt does three jobs. It draws surface water out by osmosis so the skin can blister under the broiler instead of steaming in its own moisture; it firms the surface flesh through partial denaturation of the muscle proteins, which keeps the fillet from falling apart on the rack; and it seasons the skin, which on shioyaki is eaten, not discarded. The 1.7% rate runs slightly higher than a pan-sear cure because the eaten skin needs seasoning and because the longer dry-heat cook can leach a little surface salt. Twenty-five minutes is the window: shorter under-firms, much longer drifts toward the heavily-salted register of a himono dried fish.

The skin is the dish. The single defining feature of good shioyaki is the skin, rendered, blistered, and shattering-crisp, and the broiler builds it from above. Radiant heat hits the silver skin directly while the cross-hatch score lets the dense subcutaneous fat render out and baste the surface as it crisps. Skin-side-up with no flip is correct: the fat renders downward through the flesh and the top-down heat lacquers the skin in a single pass, no turning to tear it. Keep the soy and the citrus off the skin at the table; the moment liquid touches the crackling it goes soft. The diner pulls each bite of fish through the soy-dressed daikon at the side of the plate, so the skin stays crisp to the last piece.

The daikon oroshi is the enzymatic counter. Raw daikon carries glucosinolates that, once the cell walls are ruptured by grating, the radish's own myrosinase enzyme converts into isothiocyanates, the same pungent compounds behind horseradish, wasabi, and mustard. On the palate they cut the perception of fat through a sensory route that overlaps with how acid cuts richness, and they fire the TRPA1 receptors that read as a clean sinus tingle. Grating in a circular motion ("en wo egakuyō ni," as if drawing a circle) ruptures cells laterally for a fluffier, more pungent snow; grating straight up and down gives stringier, milder oroshi. The 5-minute drain matters: too wet and it floods the plate, too long a drain (15-plus minutes) volatilizes the isothiocyanates and leaves a sweet, underpowered radish. The optional grated ginger stacks a second sharp aromatic on the same job.

Umami and acid. The glutamate comes from the soy pour (or, better, a ponzu that folds kombu and katsuobushi glutamate-inosinate synergy into the same spoonful) plus the natural inosinate in the saba flesh itself; the finishing acid is the citrus wedge, sudachi or yuzu by tradition, lemon by availability. Grilled oily fish is heavy and one-note without the acid; squeeze it over the fish at the table so the volatile citrus oils hit warm skin and bloom.

Sourcing and the histamine point. Buy saba on freshness above all: clear shiny eyes, bright iridescent silver-blue skin, tight scales, no gummy slime, a clean ocean smell with no ammonia. Vacuum-packed frozen fillets, processed and frozen within hours of harvest, are frequently a better choice than fresh fillets that have sat in the case, and for the scombroid-histamine risk specifically (see the safety note) cold-from-harvest handling is the only real control, since salting, grilling, and freezing all leave histamine intact. Discard any fish that smells off in the raw flesh rather than cooking through it.

  • Adapted from the saba shioyaki / washoku grilled-fish canon — Shioyaki (literally salt-grilling) is the most elemental treatment in the washoku fish repertoire and the standard grilled-fish course of the teishoku set meal and the izakaya. Where the pan-sear pulls saba at a medium-rare 56°C for a creamy lipid texture, shioyaki cooks the fillet fully through under dry radiant heat: the goal is rendered subcutaneous fat, blistered crackling skin, and flaking, set flesh that still reads moist because the salt cure and the fast high heat conspire to hold water in the muscle.
