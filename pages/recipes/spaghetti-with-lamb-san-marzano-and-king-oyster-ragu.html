Spaghetti with lamb, San Marzano, and king oyster ragu

A 50-minute ragu built on ground lamb browned hard in its own rendered fat, king oyster mushrooms cut two ways for texture (seared coins and pulled strands), and a San Marzano base cut with red wine and rosemary. The gaminess of the lamb is framed, not masked, by warm spice and a finishing lift of lemon and mint.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Start the pasta water FIRST. A 6-quart pot takes about 8 minutes to reach a rolling boil, and the spaghetti drops during the sauce simmer. If the water is not boiling when the sauce is ready, the timing collapses and you lose the pan-emulsion window.
  • Reserve a full 500 ml / 2 cups of starchy pasta water before draining, in a heatproof measuring cup. The emulsion and any rescue of a tightened sauce need it; pasta drained without a reserve cannot rebuild a broken sauce.
  • Cut the king oyster mushrooms two ways before you start: slice the fat lower stems crosswise into 1.5 cm coins and tear the caps and thinner stems lengthwise into strands. The two cuts do different jobs, seared coins give meaty bite, pulled strands carry sauce, and you cannot stop to do this once the lamb is browning.
  • Lamb browns faster and throws more fat than beef because its fat is soft and low-melting. Work quickly at the browning stage and be ready to spoon off excess fat; a lamb ragu can turn greasy if all the rendered fat stays in.

Execution

  1. 1
    Set the 3000 ml / 12 cups water to boil in a 6-quart pot with the 35 g / 3 tbsp kosher salt. Taste it once boiling: it must read aggressively saline, like the sea. This water seasons the pasta from the inside, and under-salted pasta water cannot be corrected later in the pan.
  2. 2
    While the water heats, prepare your mise. Cut the 300 g / 10 oz king oyster mushrooms two ways: slice the fat lower stems crosswise into 1.5 cm coins and score a shallow crosshatch into each cut face, then tear the caps and thinner stems lengthwise into strands. Dice the 170 g / 1 onion small (~3 mm), mince the 18 g / 5 cloves garlic and the 2 g / 1 tbsp rosemary fine, mince the 8 g / 2 anchovy fillets to a paste, crush the 400 g / one can San Marzano tomatoes by hand into rough 1 cm shreds with their juices, grate the 60 g / ¾ cup pecorino, cube the 30 g / 2 tbsp butter cold, and zest the lemon.
  3. 3
    Heat a wide 12-inch stainless or carbon-steel skillet (not non-stick, you need fond) over high heat until a drop of water skitters. Add the 15 ml / 1 tbsp olive oil, then the king oyster coins and strands in a single layer. Dry-sear undisturbed for 2 minutes; because the king oyster is dense, it takes longer than a thin oyster mushroom to color. Toss once, add 3 g / about 1 tsp of the kosher salt to season the mushroom directly, then sear another 2 minutes until the coins carry a deep-gold crust and the strands have shrunk and browned at the edges. Transfer to a bowl. Searing the mushroom hard before the meat builds a second Maillard layer and keeps the mushroom meaty rather than letting it stew soft in the sauce; salting it now, rather than leaving it to lean on the sauce for seasoning, keeps it from reading flat against the salted lamb. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Return the pan to medium-high. Add the 450 g / 1 lb ground lamb in a flat, thin layer and press it down with the back of a wooden spoon. Leave it undisturbed for 2 minutes; lamb renders its soft fat fast, so it will be swimming in its own fat quickly and browning almost immediately. Once the underside is deep brown, break the meat into small irregular crumbles (leave size variation, do not work it to a paste) and add the remaining 5 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt. Cook, stirring occasionally, until the lamb is granular and deeply browned with a brown fond on the pan bottom, about 4 more minutes. If there is a pool of rendered fat deeper than a thin film, spoon off all but about 2 tbsp; lamb fat carries the flavor but too much of it makes the finished sauce greasy.
  5. 5
    Push the lamb to one side and add the 170 g / 1 diced onion to the cleared space with the rendered lamb fat. Sweat 3 to 4 minutes over medium heat until translucent and soft, stirring occasionally; do not brown the onion, you want its sweetness, not bitterness. Add the 18 g / 5 cloves garlic, the 2 g / 1 tbsp rosemary, and the 8 g / 2 anchovy fillets. Stir for 60 seconds until the anchovy dissolves completely into the fat and the pan smells intensely savory and herbal, not fishy. Blooming aromatics
  6. 6
    Stir the lamb back through the aromatic base. Push everything aside to clear a hot spot and add the 45 g / 3 tbsp tomato paste, the 1 g / ½ tsp cumin, the 0.5 g / ¼ tsp cinnamon, and the 1 g / ½ tsp crushed red pepper directly to the bare pan. Let them sit, stirring once or twice, for 90 seconds: the paste darkens from bright red to deep brick and the spices bloom in the hot fat, which is what turns raw, dusty spice into rounded aroma (the chili blooms the same way, its capsaicin is fat-soluble). Stir the bloomed paste and spice through the meat.
  7. 7
    Deglaze with the 180 ml / ¾ cup dry red wine, scraping the fond off the pan bottom with the wooden spoon. Reduce until almost all the liquid has cooked off and the pan goes glossy rather than soupy, 2 to 3 minutes. The wine pulls the soluble browned fond into the sauce and its acidity cuts the lamb's richness; this is where much of the dish-level depth comes from. Deglazing
  8. 8
    Add the hand-crushed 400 g / one can San Marzano tomatoes with their juices, the 2 bay leaves, the pecorino rind if you have one, and the seared king oyster mushrooms with any liquid they have released. Stir together, bring to a simmer, then drop the heat to a gentle bubble. Simmer uncovered for 12 to 15 minutes, stirring every few minutes, until the sauce coats the spoon and a little fat pools red-orange at the edge. Taste: it should read deeply savory, gently sweet, and warmly spiced, with the lamb and mushroom clearly layered. Add salt only if needed; the anchovy, the pecorino to come, and the salted pasta water are all contributing.
  9. 9
    When the sauce has simmered about 8 minutes, drop the 400 g / 14 oz spaghetti into the boiling salted water. Cook 1 minute shy of the package al dente time; it finishes in the sauce. Before draining, reserve a full 500 ml / 2 cups of starchy pasta water in a heatproof measuring cup.
  10. 10
    Using tongs, lift the pasta straight from the pot into the simmering sauce (do not drain in a colander; you want the surface water it carries). Add 120 ml / ½ cup reserved pasta water and toss vigorously over medium heat for 60 to 90 seconds. The starch emulsifies with the rendered lamb fat and bloomed tomato to bind the sauce to every strand. Pull the bay leaves and the pecorino rind. Drop the heat to low. Pan emulsion (mantecare)
  11. 11
    Pull the pan off the heat. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp cold cubed butter and toss until it melts and the sauce turns glossy. Add the 60 g / ¾ cup grated pecorino in three handfuls, tossing constantly between each. If the sauce tightens, splash in more pasta water 30 ml / 2 tbsp at a time. The sauce should coat each strand glossily with no pooled liquid; tilt the pan and the pasta should slide as one mass, not slosh.
  12. 12
    The Adjustment: taste. If flat, add cracked pepper and a pinch (1 g / ¼ tsp) more kosher salt. If the sauce reads heavy or the lamb fat sits flat on the palate, stir in 5 ml / 1 tsp red wine vinegar off the heat, the lemon zest is aromatic but carries no titratable acid, so the vinegar is the lever when the dish needs sharpening rather than perfuming. Lamb reads rich, so the finish is built to cut it: scatter the zest of 1 lemon and the 6 g / small handful of torn mint over the top and toss through just before serving (the lemon oil and mint lift the whole dish and frame the gaminess, do not substitute lemon juice, which waters the sauce and reads sour). Divide among four warm bowls, finish each with a little more pecorino and a turn of black pepper. Eat within 4 minutes; the pasta keeps drinking the sauce, and a stalled bowl is meaningfully worse than a fresh one.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→a milder, broader-appeal lamb ragu

    Use 225 g ground lamb and 225 g ground beef chuck instead of all lamb. This roughly halves the gaminess while keeping lamb's fat character present. Brown the two together; the beef browns a touch slower, so give the pan an extra minute before crumbling.

  • Regional cuisine→an eastern-Mediterranean lamb ragu

    Lean the spice frame east: double the cumin to 1 tsp, add ½ tsp ground coriander and a pinch of Aleppo pepper with the cinnamon, and finish with a spoon of thick yogurt swirled into each bowl instead of the butter mantecatura. Serve over the spaghetti or over orzo. The dish moves from Italian toward a giouvetsi-adjacent register.

  • Dietary vegetable-forward→a mostly-mushroom ragu with lamb as accent

    Invert the ratio: 200 g ground lamb, 500 g king oyster mushrooms cut two ways and dry-seared in two batches so they brown rather than steam. The pulled strands keep the dish reading meaty while the lamb becomes a seasoning rather than the bulk.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • king oyster mushroom → a mix of common oyster mushroom and cremini Tear the oysters along the grain and quarter the cremini. You lose the scallop-firm coin, but the pulled-strand body survives. Sear both hard and expect more shrinkage.
  • pecorino romano → parmigiano reggiano Less sharp and less overtly sheepy, so the echo with the lamb softens. Bump the black pepper and add a pinch more salt to compensate for pecorino's missing bite.
  • dry red wine → dry white wine plus 1 tsp / 5 ml red wine vinegar, or unsalted stock plus the vinegar For a non-alcoholic or white-only kitchen. The vinegar restores the acidity that cuts the lamb; without some acid at the deglaze the sauce reads flat and heavy.

Notes

Why lamb changes the technique. Lamb fat is softer and lower-melting than beef fat, so ground lamb renders early and browns fast, and it renders *a lot*. The two consequences are that you barely need added oil for the meat, and that you should spoon off the excess rendered fat before it makes the sauce greasy. A cook running this on beef instincts will both add unnecessary oil and leave too much lamb fat in the pan.

Why the king oyster is cut two ways. The dense stem of the king oyster is the meatiest structure in the cultivated-mushroom set, and it earns two cuts. Coins scored and seared give a springy, scallop-like bite that survives the simmer; strands torn along the grain read like pulled meat and carry sauce. One cut alone flattens the texture; the pair is the point.

The spice frame, and why it is a whisper. Cinnamon and cumin with lamb and tomato is a real Mediterranean move (from Greek pastitsio and moussaka to eastern-Mediterranean lamb sauces), not a novelty. Kept to a whisper it frames the lamb''s natural sweetness and gaminess rather than turning the dish into a curry. The lemon zest and mint at the end are the counterweight, the acid and the cooling aromatic that keep a rich sauce from cloying.
