Shoyu ramen from scratch

A Tokyo-style shoyu ramen built from a chicken-pork hybrid broth simmered 6 to 8 hours, a soy-sauce tare layered with kombu and katsuobushi for glutamate depth, fresh wheat noodles cooked to the snap, and three classic toppings (chashu pork belly, ajitama marinated egg, blanched scallion). A weekend project where each component is built in parallel; the assembly takes 4 minutes once everything is staged.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Ramen is a four-component dish (broth, tare, noodles, toppings) built in parallel over a weekend day. Read the entire recipe before starting. The plan: start the broth in the morning (8 hours), make the tare while it simmers (10 minutes), braise the chashu in the broth at the 4 hour mark (3 hours in the broth), marinate the ajitama eggs the night before (overnight). Noodles cook in 90 seconds at the very end.
  • Make the chashu and ajitama 1 to 2 days ahead if possible. Both improve in the marinade overnight, and splitting the work across days makes the day-of much less stressful. Chashu keeps 5 days refrigerated and freezes 1 month; ajitama keeps 5 days refrigerated.
  • Source fresh ramen noodles, not dried instant. Sun Noodle (Hawaiian-American producer) ships nationally and is the most accessible high-quality fresh ramen noodle in the US. Japanese groceries (Mitsuwa, Marukai, Nijiya) carry fresh ramen noodles in the refrigerator. Dried "ramen-style" noodles are not a substitute (no kansui, wrong texture).
  • Build the assembly station before the noodles go in the boiling water. The noodles cook in 90 seconds and the bowls must be hot, the tare measured, the toppings sliced and laid out, and the broth at 90°C all simultaneously. Once the noodles drain, you have 30 seconds to plate and serve.

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, broth (8 hours total)

  1. 1500 g chicken backs and feet (with carcasses if available), rinsed in cold water; chicken feet contribute the gelatin that gives the broth body
  2. 1000 g pork bones (neck bones, back bones, or trotter), rinsed in cold water; blanched 5 minutes in boiling water then drained and rinsed (removes scum and impurities)
  3. 4000 ml water (cold)
  4. 200 g yellow onion, halved skin-on — (1 large; the skin adds golden color to the broth)
  5. 40 g fresh ginger, sliced thick — (3 inch knob, sliced into 5 mm coins)
  6. 30 g scallions (the green tops, save the white parts for garnish) — (green parts of 3 scallions)
  7. 10 g dried shiitake mushroom — (4 to 6 caps; for the glutamate background, distinct from the katsuobushi in the tare)

Phase 2, chashu pork belly (braised in the broth at the 4-hour mark)

  1. 700 g pork belly, skin off, rolled and tied, rolled into a tight cylinder and tied with kitchen twine at 2 cm intervals; or use a flat slab if you prefer slab chashu
  2. 120 g soy sauce (Japanese koikuchi shoyu, Kikkoman or Yamasa)
  3. 60 g mirin
  4. 60 g sake
  5. 20 g sugar (granulated)

Phase 3, shoyu tare (10 minutes, makes more than needed)

  1. 200 g soy sauce (Japanese koikuchi shoyu)
  2. 60 g mirin
  3. 60 g sake
  4. 10 g kombu (dried kelp) — (1 small piece, about 5 × 8 cm; the glutamate amplifier)
  5. 10 g katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes)
  6. 10 g sugar (granulated)

Phase 4, ajitama (marinated soft-boiled egg, made the night before)

  1. 4 each large eggs, refrigerator-cold — (refrigerator-cold for clean peeling)
  2. 80 g soy sauce (Japanese koikuchi shoyu)
  3. 40 g mirin
  4. 80 g water
  5. 10 g sugar (granulated)

Phase 5, noodles and assembly

  1. 500 g fresh ramen noodles (Sun Noodle medium-thin, or from a Japanese grocer), kept refrigerated until use; do not pre-cook; do not soak
  2. 30 g scallions (white and pale-green parts), thin-sliced, 2 mm bias slices — (white parts of 3 scallions reserved from broth)
  3. 4 each nori sheets, cut into 4 × 6 cm rectangles — (1 sheet quartered, or 4 pre-cut rectangles)
  4. 60 g menma (fermented bamboo shoots) optional — (optional traditional topping; store-bought from a Japanese grocer)

Execution

  1. 1
    Night before: marinate the ajitama eggs. Bring a saucepan of water to a hard boil. Lower the 4 refrigerator-cold eggs into the water on a spoon (cold-to-hot for the membrane to release cleanly from the shell). Boil exactly 6 minutes 30 seconds for a slightly runny yolk and fully set white. Transfer immediately to an ice bath; chill 5 minutes. Peel under running water. Whisk together the 80 g soy sauce, 40 g mirin, 80 g water, and 10 g sugar in a small container. Add the peeled eggs; submerge with a piece of parchment cut to fit (eggs float; the parchment keeps them under). Refrigerate 8 to 12 hours. Past 12 hours the white over-salts and goes leathery; pull at 12 hours max.
  2. 2
    Morning: start the broth. Place the 1000 g pork bones in a large pot, cover with cold water, bring to a hard boil, boil 5 minutes (the bones release a gray scum that rises to the surface). Drain in a colander; rinse the bones under cold water to wash off any residual scum. In the same pot (rinsed), combine the blanched pork bones, 1500 g chicken backs and feet, 4000 ml cold water, halved 200 g yellow onion (skin on), 40 g sliced ginger, 30 g scallion greens, and 10 g dried shiitake. Bring to a hard boil, then immediately reduce to the lowest possible simmer where the surface barely trembles (a hard simmer or boil emulsifies fat and clouds the broth; this is critical). Skim the gray scum that rises in the first 30 minutes with a fine-mesh skimmer. Simmer covered with the lid offset 6 to 8 hours. The broth should reduce slightly and turn pale gold; it should NOT turn milky-white (that is tonkotsu, a different style requiring hard-boiling).8 h
  3. 3
    Make the shoyu tare. While the broth simmers, combine the 200 g soy sauce, 60 g mirin, 60 g sake, and 10 g sugar in a small saucepan over medium heat. Bring to a simmer; cook 2 minutes to burn off the alcohol. Pull off heat. Add the 10 g kombu and 10 g katsuobushi to the hot liquid. Let steep 30 minutes; the kombu releases glutamate, the katsuobushi releases inosinate, and the umami synergy multiplies in the soy base. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a glass jar. Refrigerate up to 3 weeks; this is more tare than 4 bowls of ramen need, so the surplus is for the next session.
  4. 4
    Braise the chashu in the broth (at the 4-hour mark of the broth simmer). Tie the 700 g pork belly into a tight cylinder with kitchen twine at 2 cm intervals. In a separate small heavy pot or saucepan, combine 120 g soy sauce, 60 g mirin, 60 g sake, 20 g sugar, and 600 g of the simmering broth (ladle from the main pot). Submerge the rolled pork belly. Simmer covered very gently (90°C, just under a simmer) for 2.5 to 3 hours. The pork should be fork-tender but still hold its shape; over-braising into shred-tender produces chashu that falls apart when sliced. Pull the chashu from the braise liquid; let cool to room temperature, then refrigerate 4 hours or overnight (firm chashu slices into clean thin coins; warm chashu shreds). Strain and reserve the braise liquid; it can fortify the broth or season subsequent bowls.
  5. 5
    Finish the broth. After 6 to 8 hours total simmering, strain the broth through a fine-mesh sieve lined with a single layer of cheesecloth into a clean pot. Discard the spent bones, vegetables, and aromatics. You should have about 2500 to 2800 ml of pale gold broth; if reduced too far, top up with hot water to 2500 ml. Taste; the broth should read deeply meaty and slightly gelatinous (it will gel when cold). Do not salt the broth; the tare does all the seasoning in the bowl. Bring back to 90°C just before assembly.
  6. 6
    Pre-assembly: stage everything. Slice the cold chashu into 4 mm thick coins (cleaner slices when fully cold). Cut the marinated eggs in half lengthwise with a damp knife; the runny yolk releases cleanly with a wet blade. Slice the 30 g scallion whites and pale greens into 2 mm bias slices. Cut the nori into 4 rectangles. Warm 4 large ramen bowls (deep, wide bowls 18 to 20 cm diameter) with a kettle of hot water; pour out the water just before assembly. Set out the 60 g menma if using.
  7. 7
    Cook the noodles. Bring a large pot of unsalted water to a hard rolling boil (ramen noodle water is NOT salted; the kansui in the noodles provides the seasoning, and salt in the water can over-season). Drop the 500 g fresh ramen noodles into the boiling water; cook 90 seconds for medium-thin Sun Noodle (check the package; some noodles want 75 to 120 seconds). Stir continuously to prevent clumping. Drain immediately in a colander; do NOT rinse (the surface starch is the seasoning carrier).
  8. 8
    Assemble the bowls. Working fast (the next 30 seconds): pour out the bowl-warming water. Spoon 30 to 40 g of shoyu tare into each warm bowl (start at 30 g and dial up by household preference; the tare is the only seasoning). Ladle 500 to 600 ml of 90°C broth into each bowl, swirling to dissolve the tare. Divide the cooked noodles evenly across the 4 bowls, using chopsticks to fold them into a neat mound that breaks the broth surface. Lay 2 to 3 chashu coins overlapping on one side of each bowl. Lay one ajitama egg half on the opposite side. Mound the sliced scallion in the center. Stand one nori rectangle upright against the side of each bowl. Add the menma if using. Serve immediately; ramen waits for no one and the noodles soften within 3 minutes in the broth.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→shio ramen (salt-only tare, clearer and lighter)

    Replace the shoyu tare with a shio tare: 100 g sea salt, 200 g water, 30 g sake, 30 g mirin, 10 g kombu, 10 g katsuobushi. Simmer gently 5 minutes, steep 30 minutes off heat, strain. Use 25 to 30 g shio tare per bowl. The dish reads pale gold and much cleaner; this is the Hakodate / Hokkaido style.

  • Regional cuisine→miso ramen (Sapporo style)

    Replace the shoyu tare with a miso tare: 200 g red miso (akamiso), 100 g sake, 60 g mirin, 30 g grated garlic, 15 g grated ginger, 30 g toasted sesame oil. Whisk into a paste. Use 40 to 50 g miso tare per bowl. The dish reads richer, slightly sweet, and is canonically topped with butter, corn, and bean sprouts. The broth can be the same hybrid; many Sapporo shops use a tonkotsu-leaning broth.

  • Regional cuisine→tonkotsu ramen (creamy pork bone)

    Skip the chicken bones; use 3 kg pork bones (femur, knuckle, neck). Boil hard (NOT simmer) for 12 to 18 hours, adding hot water to maintain liquid level. The hard boil emulsifies the rendered fat and collagen into the water, producing the white, creamy tonkotsu broth. The same shoyu tare works (Hakata tonkotsu shoyu); shio tare gives a Kyushu-purist tonkotsu. The dish reads dramatically richer than this shoyu base.

  • Occasion occasion→tsukemen (dipping noodles)

    Make the broth thicker (reduce it by 30%) and the tare proportionally stronger; serve the noodles separately, chilled and rinsed (the only context where ramen noodles are rinsed), with the concentrated broth in a small bowl on the side. The diner dips each chopstickful of noodles into the broth, eats, and finishes by adding hot water (sup-wari) to the leftover broth to drink as a soup. A summer ramen format.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • fresh ramen noodles → frozen ramen noodles, home-made from scratch Frozen ramen noodles (also from Sun Noodle, sold frozen at Asian groceries) work identically. Home-made with kansui is a separate weekend project (see homemade_alternatives). Dried "ramen-style" noodles or instant noodles are not substitutes.
  • pork belly chashu → pork shoulder chashu, chicken chashu Pork shoulder produces a leaner, more fibrous chashu (the cook calls it kakuni-style); chicken thigh rolled and braised the same way is a lighter alternative. Pork belly is canonical.
  • kombu → no kombu Skipping the kombu reduces the umami depth of the tare noticeably. Kombu is widely available at Japanese groceries and Whole Foods; the substitution is "do without," not "replace with."
  • mirin (hon-mirin) → mirin-fu chōmiryō (sweetened cooking liquid) Acceptable substitute but the depth is lower; hon-mirin (real fermented mirin, alcoholic) versus mirin-fu (sweetened sake substitute) is a meaningful flavor difference. Try to find Eden or Mitoku hon-mirin.

Notes

Ramen is four dishes. The structural unit of ramen is (broth) + (tare) + (noodles) + (toppings). Each component is made independently and then assembled at the moment of service. The broth provides body and depth; the tare provides salt and the flavor profile that names the bowl (shoyu, shio, miso, tonkotsu); the noodles provide texture; the toppings provide variation and visual structure. Mastering ramen at home is mastering the orchestration of four parallel cooks, not mastering one recipe.

The broth-versus-tare distinction is critical. The broth itself is essentially unseasoned (no salt added); all the seasoning comes from the tare. This is opposite to most Western soup traditions where the stock is salted as it builds. The reason is calibration: a single broth can serve shoyu, shio, miso, and tonkotsu bowls by changing the tare, and the cook can dial the salt level by adjusting the tare quantity per bowl rather than re-salting after the broth is done. Many home cooks make the mistake of salting the broth and then adding tare on top, producing an over-salted bowl.

The hybrid chicken-pork broth is the Tokyo approach. Pure chicken broth makes a clean but thin bowl (Tokyo shio-style); pure pork bone broth in hard boil makes tonkotsu; the 60/40 chicken-pork hybrid simmered (not boiled) is the canonical Tokyo shoyu base. The chicken provides gelatin and a clean meaty backbone; the pork adds depth and sweetness. Chicken feet are the structural addition; they provide more gelatin per gram than any other chicken part and give the broth its body.

Do not boil; simmer. The single most important technique distinction is the simmer-versus-boil call. Tonkotsu ramen is made by hard-boiling pork bones for 12 to 18 hours to emulsify the fat into the water (the milky-white appearance). Shoyu, shio, and miso ramen are made by gentle simmering at 85 to 90°C so the broth stays clear. Boiling a shoyu broth produces a cloudy, greasy broth that no amount of tare can rescue. The lid offset on the pot, the burner on its lowest possible setting, and patience are the entire technique.

The umami synergy: kombu (glutamate) and katsuobushi (inosinate) together produce 7 to 8 times the umami punch of either alone. This is the structural reason the tare contains both, and the structural reason Japanese stock-making puts both in the kombu-katsuobushi dashi. The synergy is the most thoroughly documented umami interaction in food science; Kikunae Ikeda discovered glutamate via kombu in 1908, and the inosinate-katsuobushi multiplier was characterized in the 1950s.

Why the broth is not salted. The tare is the entire salt vehicle. A bowl of ramen uses 30 to 50 g of tare in 500 to 600 ml of broth, which is 5 to 8% tare by volume. The tare itself is approximately 15% salt (soy sauce is ~16% NaCl by weight), so the finished bowl is about 0.75 to 1.2% salt, which lands in the canonical seasoning range. Salting the broth on top of this overshoots into inedibly salty territory.

The assembly is the entire dish. The 30-second window from drained noodles to served bowl is the moment where ramen is made. The noodles soften in the broth from the instant they hit; serving more than 3 minutes after the noodles drain produces noodles in lukewarm broth. This is why every component must be staged before the noodles go in the water, every bowl must be pre-warmed, and the broth must be at 90°C (not boiling, which would cook the egg yolks gray on top).

Weekend project, ahead-prep friendly. The broth (8 hours), tare (10 minutes), chashu (3 hours, fits inside the broth window), and ajitama (10 minutes plus overnight marinade) can all be done a day or two ahead. Day-of: pull the chashu and ajitama from the fridge an hour before, slice everything, reheat the broth to 90°C, cook the noodles, assemble. With the components prepped, the day-of work is 30 minutes. This is the only practical way to make ramen at home regularly.

Ramen is best fresh and impossible to reheat. The noodles do not survive refrigeration in broth (they bloat and turn mush). Leftover broth, tare, chashu, and eggs can all be saved separately and reassembled the next day with fresh-cooked noodles for a second-day bowl that is as good as the first. Never refrigerate assembled ramen.
