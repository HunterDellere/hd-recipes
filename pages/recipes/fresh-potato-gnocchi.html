Fresh potato gnocchi

Hand-rolled potato dumplings: baked russets riced fine, bound with the minimum egg and flour the dough will tolerate, formed into pillows, and cooked just past floating. The technique is built around moisture management; everything else follows. Stub.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the potato base

  1. 1000 g russet potatoes
  2. 6 g kosher salt (for the potato seasoning)

Phase 2, the binding

  1. 1 each large egg yolk (the minimum-binding choice)
  2. 200 g "00" flour or all-purpose flour
  3. 30 g parmigiano reggiano, finely grated

Phase 3, the cook

  1. 25 g kosher salt (for the boiling water)
  2. 4000 ml water

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: bake the 1000 g russets whole at 200 °C for 60 to 75 minutes until a knife slides through with no resistance. Baking (rather than boiling) is structural, boiling waterlogs the potato and the dumplings absorb water on cooking and turn gummy. The bake-vs-boil decision is the single largest variable in gnocchi quality.
  2. 2
    Stub: while the potatoes are still hot (this matters; cold potato builds gluten differently), peel and pass through a ricer or food mill onto a clean work surface. Spread thin, sprinkle with the 6 g salt, and let cool for 5 minutes. The ricer produces the fluffy, no-gluten texture; a food processor or mashing collapses the starch and produces a gummy dough that will not work.
  3. 3
    Stub: form a well in the riced potato. Add the 18 g egg yolk and 30 g grated parmigiano if using. Sprinkle the 200 g flour over the surface in two or three stages, working the dough gently with a bench scraper to fold flour in. Stop adding flour the moment the dough holds together when squeezed; resist the home-cook instinct to add more flour. The dough should feel slightly tacky but not sticky.
  4. 4
    Stub: divide the dough into 6 portions. Roll each portion into a 2 cm rope on a lightly floured surface. Cut the rope into 2 cm pillows with a sharp knife or bench scraper. Optionally roll each pillow over a gnocchi paddle or the back of a fork tine to create the ridges that will hold sauce. Place formed gnocchi on a flour-dusted parchment-lined sheet in a single layer; do not stack.
  5. 5
    Stub: bring the 4 L water and 25 g salt to a rolling boil. Drop the gnocchi in batches of 30 to 40 (do not crowd; the water temperature drops with each addition and crowded gnocchi stick to each other). Cook 90 seconds past the moment they float to the surface; lift out with a spider strainer; transfer directly to the awaiting sauce. Do not drain in a colander, the residual cooking water carries starch that helps the sauce cling.
  6. 6
    Stub: serve immediately. Gnocchi do not hold; even 5 minutes of sitting in a warm sauce produces a softer, less distinct dumpling. Plate and eat fast.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the potato chemistry (russet vs Yukon Gold vs new potato, moisture, starch composition, dumpling texture); the bake-vs-boil reasoning (waterlogging, starch gelation order, the irreversible textural shift); the temperature-of-the-potato variable (hot riced potato vs cooled, gluten development differs); the minimum-flour principle (every gram of flour past the binding minimum produces a tougher, denser, more flour-tasting dumpling, the dough should feel underflour-ed); the ricer-vs-food-mill choice (food mill gives a slightly coarser texture, both work, food processor and masher both wreck the dough); the gnocchi-paddle ridges (sauce adhesion vs smooth surface, debated, paddle preferred for cream-and-cheese sauces, smooth fine for browned-butter applications); the freezing strategy (freeze formed gnocchi on the sheet pan, transfer to bags, cook directly from frozen, adds 30 seconds to the cook time and the gnocchi hold up better than refrigerated-and-cooked); the sauce pairings (browned butter and sage, pomodoro, pesto, gorgonzola cream, and the gnocchi alla bava and gnocchi al ragù regional preparations); the gnocchi alla romana semolina contrast (different dish entirely, made with semolina paste cut into rounds and baked).
