Homemade bacon

Pork belly cured for 7 days in salt, pink curing salt #1 (sodium nitrite), sugar, and aromatics, then cold-smoked over hardwood for 12 to 24 hours. The home-cured product is markedly more complex than supermarket bacon, with the cure profile tuned to the cook's preference and the smoke level chosen by the cook. Weekend project; 10 days elapsed time, 4 hours active. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 9 items ▾

Produce 2

  1. 8 g Garlic
  2. 250 g hardwood for smoking (hickory, apple, maple, or cherry)

Meat & seafood 1

  1. 2000 g pork belly, skin off

Spices & dried herbs 3

  1. 3 each Bay leaf (tej patta, तेज पत्ता)
  2. 6 g Black peppercorns
  3. 5 g pink curing salt #1 (sodium nitrite 6.25 percent, salt 93.75 percent)

Pantry 3

  1. 20 g Dark brown sugar
  2. 30 g Granulated sugar
  3. 50 g Kosher salt

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: combine the kosher salt, pink curing salt #1, both sugars, cracked black pepper, crumbled bay, and microplaned garlic in a small bowl. Rub the mixture evenly across all surfaces of the pork belly; press the cure into the meat. Place the belly in a 2-gallon zip bag or a tightly-sealed plastic container, refrigerate at 2 to 4 °C. Flip the belly every 24 hours for 7 days; liquid will accumulate in the bag and the belly will firm up dramatically as the cure penetrates.
  2. 2
    Stub: after 7 days, remove the belly from the cure liquid, rinse under cold water for 30 seconds to remove surface salt and aromatics, pat dry with paper towels. Place the belly on a wire rack over a sheet pan, refrigerate uncovered for 12 to 24 hours; this dries the surface and produces a sticky pellicle that the smoke adheres to. The belly is now ready for smoking; this stage is called "cured pork belly" and is technically edible (cook to 71 °C internal before eating) but not yet bacon.1 d 0 h
  3. 3
    Stub: cold-smoke the belly. Set up a smoker for cold smoke (under 30 °C) using a smoke tube, a Maze pellet generator, or a fire-isolated cold-smoke chamber. Generate hickory or apple smoke continuously for 12 to 24 hours; the longer smoke produces deeper smoke penetration. The belly should not cook; it should pick up smoke aromatics and dry slightly. For hot-smoked bacon, smoke at 90 to 100 °C until the belly reaches 65 to 68 °C internal (about 3 to 4 hours); the bacon is technically cooked-through but still raw-textured for slicing and pan-rendering.18 h
  4. 4
    Stub: cool the smoked belly fully to refrigerator temperature (4 °C), wrap tightly in plastic, and refrigerate overnight before slicing; the cold belly slices cleanly into 2 to 4 mm strips on a deli slicer or a sharp chef knife. Sliced bacon keeps 2 weeks refrigerated; freeze portions for longer storage. Cook from raw (cold-pan render as for any bacon) before eating; cold-smoked bacon is cured-and-smoked but not cooked-through, hot-smoked bacon is cooked-through but still benefits from pan-rendering for crisp texture.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the equilibrium-cure chemistry (why 2.5 percent salt + 0.25 percent pink #1 by weight is the safe-and-tasty target), the role of sodium nitrite (color fixing via nitrosylmyoglobin, antimicrobial against C. botulinum spores, characteristic cured flavor), the pellicle formation chemistry (proteins denaturing on the dry surface and forming the tacky layer that smoke adheres to), the difference between cold-smoke (under 30 °C, slow penetration, raw-textured product that needs cooking) and hot-smoke (90 to 100 °C, fully-cooked product), wood selection (hickory aggressive, apple mellow, maple sweet, cherry mild-sweet, oak neutral — all hardwoods, never softwoods which contain resinous sap that tastes acrid), maple bacon technique (replace the granulated sugar with maple syrup, brushed on after the cure rinse and before the dry), peppered bacon technique (heavy black pepper crust applied after the rinse and before the smoke), and the safety boundaries (do not skip the pink curing salt for any long-cure pork; the nitrite is the safety margin against botulism; salt-only cures are safe for short cures like pork chops but not for bacon).

The home cure approach gives the cook complete control: salt level tuned to preference (2.5 percent is the canonical, some cooks prefer 2.2 to 2.3 percent for milder bacon), sugar level tuned to preference (1.5 to 3 percent total, depending on whether the bacon is intended for sweet breakfast applications or savory cooking), smoke depth tuned to preference (12 hours for moderate smoke, 24 hours for aggressive). The economics: pork belly runs $6 to $10 per pound at supermarkets and lower at Asian markets and Costco; the finished cost of homemade bacon is about half of artisan dry-cured supermarket bacon and produces 1.6 kg of finished bacon from 2 kg of pork belly (the cure draws out moisture). For a cook who uses 200 g of bacon per week, a homemade batch lasts a month and a half.
