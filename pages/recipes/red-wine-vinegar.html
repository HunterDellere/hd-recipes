Red wine vinegar

Live mother of vinegar fed leftover red wine in a wide jar — produces vinegar with floral and tannic complexity that pasteurized commercial product lacks. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 3 items ▾
  1. 100 g live mother of vinegar (active acetobacter culture)
  2. 1000 g red wine (dry, unoaked or lightly oaked, 11 to 13.5 percent alcohol) — (roughly 1.3 bottles; leftover or discount wine works fine — quality of the wine carries through to the vinegar)
  3. 250 g water (filtered, non-chlorinated)

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: combine the 1000 g red wine and 250 g water in a wide-mouth glass or ceramic jar (the wide surface area is critical; acetobacter is aerobic and needs oxygen contact). The wine-water mix should target 6 to 8 percent alcohol; higher kills the bacteria, lower stalls the cure.
  2. 2
    Stub: float the 100 g live mother of vinegar gently on the surface (do not submerge — the mother is a biofilm and needs surface oxygen). Cover the jar opening with a tight-weave cloth (cheesecloth or a coffee filter) secured with a rubber band; this excludes fruit flies while allowing oxygen exchange.
  3. 3
    Stub: hold at 21 to 27 °C (room temperature; warmer accelerates the cure, cooler stalls it) in a dark cupboard for 60 to 90 days. The mother grows visibly across the surface as a translucent jelly layer; the wine slowly converts. After 30 days, taste-test from beneath the mother layer with a clean straw or pipette — when the liquid reads fully vinegary with no residual wine character, the cure is complete.
  4. 4
    Stub: bottle the mature vinegar (leaving the mother in the jar plus 100 g of vinegar to start the next batch). Filter through cheesecloth if a clean visual is wanted. Optionally pasteurize by heating to 70 °C for 15 minutes to halt residual acetobacter activity (preserves the flavor longer; live vinegar continues to slowly evolve in the bottle).

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the acetobacter chemistry (oxidation of ethanol to acetic acid via two-step enzymatic conversion through acetaldehyde); the alcohol-tolerance window (acetobacter dies above 12 percent alcohol, stalls below 5 percent); the wine choice (high-tannin reds like Cabernet, Syrah, Merlot give complex vinegars; low-tannin reds like Pinot Noir give brighter, simpler vinegars); the mother-sourcing options (Wine-and-Beer-Making Supplies USA, Boone's Farm Vinegar Mothers, or a gift from a vinegar-making friend); failure modes (fruit-fly contamination if cloth cover is loose, kahm yeast contamination if alcohol drops too low, no acetobacter activity if temperature drops below 18 °C).

Vinegar production is a standalone fermentation hobby with its own equipment culture (vinegar crocks, oak barrels for aging) and timeline. A simple kitchen setup gives you better-than-commercial vinegar; serious enthusiasts age in oak barrels for 1 to 5 years and produce vinegars rivaling commercial Forvm or Volpaia.
