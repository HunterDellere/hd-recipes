Red wine vinegar

Live mother of vinegar fed leftover red wine in a wide jar — acetobacter oxidizes the ethanol to acetic acid over 60 to 90 days. Yields a vinegar with floral and tannic complexity that pasteurized commercial product lacks.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a 60 to 90 day fermentation, not a recipe you cook today. Active hands-on time is 30 minutes total spread across two months; the work is the patience and the monthly taste check, not the technique.
  • Source a live mother of vinegar before sourcing the wine. Options: gift from a home vinegar-maker, the cloudy sediment from an unpasteurized bottle of Bragg or similar (slow start but works), or a wine-making supply shop (fastest, most reliable). Without a live mother the wine will turn to volatile acidity and spoilage, not vinegar.
  • Pick a stable spot at 21 to 27 °C, dark and undisturbed, for two months. A pantry shelf or cupboard works; a windowsill does not. Temperature swings stall the culture.
  • Use a wide-mouth glass or ceramic jar (2 L). Tall narrow jars run at half the speed because acetobacter only converts at the air-liquid surface. Cover with cheesecloth or coffee filter, never a solid lid, the culture is aerobic and suffocates under a seal.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 3 items ▾

Pantry 2

  1. 100 g live mother of vinegar (active acetobacter culture)
  2. 1000 g red wine (dry, unoaked or lightly oaked, 11 to 13.5 percent alcohol)

Other 1

  1. 250 g water (filtered, non-chlorinated)

Execution

  1. 1
    Combine the 1000 g red wine and 250 g filtered water in a wide-mouth glass or ceramic jar (2 L / 2 quart capacity is appropriate). The wide surface area is critical; acetobacter is an obligate aerobe and converts ethanol to acetic acid only at the air-liquid interface. A tall narrow jar with a small opening will run twice as slowly as a wide low jar with the same liquid volume. The wine-water mix targets 6 to 8 percent alcohol, which is the optimal feed concentration for acetobacter; higher than 12 percent kills the culture, lower than 5 percent stalls the conversion.
  2. 2
    Float the 100 g live mother of vinegar gently on the surface of the wine-water mix. Do not submerge it; the mother is a biofilm of acetobacter encased in a cellulose matrix and depends on surface contact with the air for oxygen. If the mother sinks (which sometimes happens with older or thicker mothers), lift it gently with a clean spoon and re-float; if it persistently sinks, leave it submerged and a new surface mother will form on the liquid surface within 1 to 2 weeks.
  3. 3
    Cover the jar opening with a tight-weave cloth (cheesecloth folded twice, a coffee filter, or a clean kitchen towel) secured with a rubber band. The cover excludes fruit flies (which are an active threat throughout the cure and will lay eggs on any uncovered vinegar) while allowing the oxygen exchange the acetobacter requires. A solid lid suffocates the culture and stalls the cure.
  4. 4
    Hold at 21 to 27 °C (room temperature; warmer accelerates the cure within the window, cooler stalls it) in a dark cupboard. Light is not strictly harmful but degrades some of the volatile aromatics over a multi-month cure. The mother will grow visibly across the surface as a translucent jelly layer over the first 2 to 3 weeks; the wine slowly converts as the acetobacter oxidizes ethanol to acetaldehyde and then acetaldehyde to acetic acid (a two-step enzymatic conversion).
  5. 5
    After 30 days, taste-test from beneath the mother layer with a clean glass straw or pipette (do not disturb the surface mother more than necessary; lift the mother edge gently and dip in to draw a sample). Early-stage vinegar reads sweet-and-alcoholic with a faint vinegary edge; mid-stage reads balanced wine-and-vinegar; the cure is complete when the liquid reads fully vinegary with no residual wine character and no remaining alcohol burn at the back of the throat. The full conversion takes 60 to 90 days at the recommended temperature; slower at the cool end of the window, faster at the warm end.
  6. 6
    When the vinegar reads fully converted, decant for bottling: lift the mother to a clean plate, ladle out the mature vinegar (leaving 100 g of vinegar in the jar plus the mother to start the next batch), filter through fine cheesecloth or a coffee filter if a clear visual is wanted (some sediment is normal and not harmful). Bottle in clean glass jars or vinegar bottles with tight lids; store at room temperature.
  7. 7
    Optional pasteurization: heat the bottled vinegar to 70 °C / 160 °F for 15 minutes, then cool. Pasteurization halts residual acetobacter activity and stabilizes the flavor for long-term storage; live (unpasteurized) vinegar continues to slowly evolve in the bottle and may eventually develop new mothers at the surface (harmless but visually unsettling). Most home producers skip pasteurization; serious enthusiasts pasteurize bottles intended for gifting or aging.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional wine→high-tannin reds for complex vinegar

    Use Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah, Merlot, or Sangiovese as the feed wine. The tannins and the more aggressive aromatic compounds carry through the conversion and produce vinegars with deep, complex finishes suitable for finishing salads and braises. The trade is a slightly slower cure (the higher alcohol fights the culture for the first week).

  • Regional wine→low-tannin reds for bright, simple vinegar

    Use Pinot Noir, Beaujolais, or Gamay as the feed wine. The lower tannins and lighter aromatic load produce a brighter, more straightforward vinegar suitable for delicate vinaigrettes and finishing roasted vegetables. The cure runs slightly faster (50 to 70 days) because of the lower alcohol burden.

  • Regional wine→white wine vinegar

    Substitute dry white wine (Sauvignon Blanc, Pinot Grigio, Vermentino) at the same volume. The conversion runs identically; the result is a clear, brighter vinegar suited to fish vinaigrettes, hollandaise, and lighter applications. Aged Chardonnay produces a more complex white vinegar; oaked whites can produce a vanilla-and-toast finish that is either prized or polarizing depending on the cook.

  • Regional aging→oak-aged vinegar (toward Forvm or Volpaia commercial style)

    After the bottle ferment is complete, decant into a small toasted oak barrel (5 to 20 L is the home-scale range, sourced from a wine-making supplier) and age for 1 to 5 years. The oak contributes vanilla, toasted-bread, and tannic complexity; over time the vinegar concentrates as some water evaporates through the wood. This is the production approach behind commercial Forvm Cabernet Sauvignon vinegar and Volpaia Tuscan vinegar; serious home enthusiasts replicate it for personal use.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • live mother of vinegar (active acetobacter culture) → unfiltered raw apple cider vinegar (Bragg or similar) as starter, no mother needed Substitute 250 g unfiltered raw apple cider vinegar (which contains live acetobacter) for the 100 g mother. Add to the wine-water mix and proceed; a new mother will form on the surface over the first 2 to 4 weeks. The cure runs slightly slower (90 to 120 days) because the starter culture is less concentrated.
  • red wine (11 to 13.5% alcohol) → red wine over 13.5% diluted with extra water High-alcohol reds (Zinfandel, Amarone, some Napa Cabernets) above 14% require additional water to bring the alcohol concentration into the acetobacter-tolerable window. Use 350 g water per 1000 g wine for a 14.5% feed wine; the resulting vinegar is slightly less concentrated.
  • red wine → rose wine or fortified wine Rose wines convert by the same method and produce a pink-amber rose wine vinegar suitable for delicate vinaigrettes. Fortified wines (sherry, port, madeira) are above the alcohol-tolerance window; dilute heavily (2:1 water-to-fortified-wine) and expect a 120 to 180 day cure. Sherry vinegar is conventionally produced from sherry diluted in this way and aged in oak.

Notes

The acetobacter chemistry. Acetobacter aceti (and several closely related species in the genus Acetobacter) is an obligate aerobic bacterium that oxidizes ethanol in two enzymatic steps. The first step uses alcohol dehydrogenase to convert ethanol to acetaldehyde; the second uses aldehyde dehydrogenase to convert acetaldehyde to acetic acid. Both reactions consume oxygen, which is why the air-liquid interface is so critical to the cure. The bacterium itself lives as a biofilm in the cellulose-matrix mother; the mother grows by binding the acetobacter cells together with extracellular cellulose fibrils, producing the distinctive translucent jelly layer.

The alcohol-tolerance window. Acetobacter activity peaks at 5 to 8 percent ethanol; activity declines sharply above 10 percent and the culture dies off above 12 percent. Below 4 percent the culture stalls because the rate of ethanol-substrate availability falls below the rate at which the cells can sustain themselves. This is why the 1000 g wine plus 250 g water dilution is canonical: it brings 12.5% ABV wine down to roughly 8% in the feed, which is the optimal acetobacter feed concentration.

Wine choice and flavor inheritance. The aromatic complexity, tannin, and underlying sweetness of the feed wine carry through the conversion to a substantial degree. Cheap wine produces cheap vinegar; quality wine produces complex vinegar. The conventional rule is that the vinegar will inherit roughly 60 to 70 percent of the wine character, with the acetic acid contribution overlaid on top. A $5 wine will produce a $5 vinegar; a $25 wine will produce a $20 vinegar after the cure overhead. For most home producers, leftover wine from the previous night's dinner (which is too oxidized to drink but still has full flavor) is the working source; supermarket house wines and discount Trader Joe's-tier wines work fine for everyday vinegar.

Mother sourcing. The mother of vinegar is the seed culture and the starting point. Sources: (1) a previous batch (the canonical perpetuation method, like a sourdough starter); (2) a friend or local fermenter who keeps a mother (vinegar mothers gift well and are essentially indestructible if kept in vinegar); (3) home-brewing-and-wine-making supply shops (Wine and Beer Making Supplies USA, Beverage Factory, More Wine, Williams Brewing) sell live mothers for $10 to $20 plus shipping; (4) generation from raw unfiltered apple cider vinegar (Bragg), which contains live acetobacter and will form a new mother in the wine-water mix over 2 to 4 weeks (slowest method but the most accessible).

Failure modes. Fruit-fly infestation: the cloth cover was loose, fell off, or the rubber band slipped — the larvae must be discarded and the batch may need to be discarded depending on contamination level. Kahm yeast (a white film distinct from the mother): forms when the alcohol drops too low or the cure stalls in cool conditions; skim and discard the kahm and add a small dose of fresh wine to bump the alcohol back up. No acetobacter activity for 4 weeks: temperature is too low (below 18 °C) or the mother was not viable; warm up the cure to room temperature and add a fresh starter (raw apple cider vinegar or a fresh mother).

Vinegar production as a hobby. Home vinegar making is a standalone fermentation hobby with its own equipment culture (vinegar crocks with spigots, oak barrels for aging, refractometers for measuring acidity and alcohol). A simple wide-mouth jar gives you better-than-commercial vinegar; serious enthusiasts age in oak barrels for 1 to 5 years and produce vinegars that compete with commercial premium vinegars (Forvm, Volpaia, La Posta dei Velieri) at a fraction of the price. The cost of entry is low (one jar, one mother, leftover wine); the time investment is minimal (15 minutes every 60 to 90 days).

Use. Homemade red wine vinegar goes into vinaigrettes (the canonical 3:1 oil-to-vinegar ratio), French and Italian braises (deglazing pan sauces), pickled red onions, and any application where commercial red wine vinegar is called for. The home product is more aromatically complex than commercial vinegar and reads less harshly acidic; recipes calibrated for commercial vinegar may need a slightly larger volume (10 to 15 percent more) of homemade to deliver the same brightness on the palate.
