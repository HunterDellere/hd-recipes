Ube halaya no-bake cheesecake bars

Toasted coconut graham crust under a layer of cream cheese, mascarpone, ube halaya, and gelatin-stabilized whipped cream. The gelatin sets the filling firm enough to slice cleanly; halaya provides nearly all the sweetness, so no added sugar in the filling itself.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Pull the cream cheese and mascarpone to room temperature 60 minutes before starting. Cold dairy will not blend smoothly with halaya and leaves lumps that no amount of beating fixes; the filling needs to be uniform-violet before the gelatin and whipped cream go in or the slices read patchy when cut.
  • The bars need at least 6 hours of fridge set before slicing, ideally overnight. Gelatin reaches full strength at around 4 hours but the filling is still soft at the edges; full 6 to 8 hours gives clean knife cuts. Plan to make the bars the day before serving.
  • Line the 20 x 20 cm pan with parchment with 5 cm overhang on two opposite sides. Without the overhang you cannot lift the set bars out of the pan in one piece, and trying to cut them in-pan with the crust against metal produces broken corners and crumbled bottoms.

Execution

  1. 1
    Line a 20 x 20 cm / 8 x 8 in metal baking pan with parchment, leaving 5 cm / 2 in overhang on two opposite sides; this is the handle that lifts the set bars out for clean slicing. Toast 30 g / 5 tbsp desiccated coconut for the crust plus 8 g / 4 tsp for the finish together in a small dry stainless skillet over medium-low heat, shaking constantly, until the shreds turn uniform pale golden and smell roasted-nutty, about 90 seconds. Tip immediately onto a cold plate. Reserve 8 g / 4 tsp for the finish; the rest goes into the crust.
  2. 2
    In a food processor, pulse 180 g / 12 graham sheets to a uniform fine crumb (about 20 one-second pulses). Add the toasted crust coconut (22 g / 4 tbsp), 25 g / 2 tbsp packed light brown sugar, and 1.5 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt; pulse 3 more times to combine. Pour in 90 g / 6 tbsp melted unsalted butter and pulse 8 to 10 times until the mixture clumps when pressed between your fingers. Tip into the parchment-lined pan and press firmly into an even layer with the flat bottom of a measuring cup; corners and edges matter as much as the center. Refrigerate 15 minutes while you build the filling.
  3. 3
    Sprinkle 7 g / 1 packet powdered gelatin evenly over the surface of 45 ml / 3 tbsp cold water in a small heatproof cup. Let stand 5 minutes; the granules absorb water and swell into a translucent jelly. This is the bloom step and it is non-negotiable: gelatin added directly to a warm liquid clumps into rubbery beads. Once bloomed, microwave the cup in 10-second bursts (usually 1 burst is enough) until fully liquefied and clear, no granular texture. Set aside; do not let it cool past 35 °C / 95 °F or it will set in the cup before you can add it.
  4. 4
    In a stand mixer with the paddle (or a hand mixer in a wide bowl), beat 340 g / 12 oz room-temperature cream cheese and 110 g / scant ½ cup mascarpone on medium for 2 minutes until completely smooth and lump-free. Scrape down. Add 230 g / scant 1 cup ube halaya, 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste, and 1.5 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt; mix on low until uniformly violet, about 60 seconds. Scrape down again. Pour the warm liquid gelatin into the violet mixture in a slow stream while the mixer runs on low; mix 30 seconds to fully distribute. The filling should still be soft and pourable at this point.
  5. 5
    In a separate cold bowl, whip 180 g / ¾ cup very cold heavy cream on medium-high for 45 to 60 seconds to soft peaks. The cream should hold a peak that slumps over at the tip, not stand up straight; stiffer cream resists folding into the dense halaya base and you get streaks of plain whipped cream in the violet filling. Add the soft-whipped cream to the violet base in three additions, folding with a rubber spatula and stopping when no white streaks remain, 12 to 15 strokes total. Do not over-fold; you want the lift the cream contributes.
  6. 6
    Pull the chilled crust from the fridge. Scoop the violet filling on top and spread evenly with a small offset spatula, going all the way to the corners. Smooth the top. Warm the 40 g / 2½ tbsp swirl halaya 10 seconds in the microwave until pourable. Drop the halaya onto the surface in 7 to 8 random small dollops; drag the tip of a chopstick or skewer through the dollops in a figure-8 pattern, 4 to 5 times across the pan, to make the canonical violet-on-violet swirl. Shower the reserved 8 g / 4 tsp toasted desiccated coconut over the swirl.
  7. 7
    Cover loosely with plastic wrap (do not let the wrap touch the swirled surface) and refrigerate at least 6 hours, ideally overnight. The gelatin sets fully at around 4 hours but the filling stays soft at the edges until 6+ hours; cutting too early gives you broken corners and smeared slices. Once set, lift the slab out of the pan using the parchment overhang. Wipe a long knife with a clean wet cloth, dry it, and cut a 3 x 3 grid (9 squares). Wipe-and-dry the blade between every cut; halaya is sticky and a wet blade smears the cut edges. Serve cold; the gelatin softens noticeably above 18 °C / 65 °F and the bars lose their clean slice.6 h 5 min

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion occasion→individual ube halaya cheesecake jars

    Skip the parchment-lined pan. Divide the toasted coconut graham crust evenly between 8 small jars (180 to 240 ml / 6 to 8 oz capacity, wide-mouth Weck or canning jars work well), pressing firmly into the bottom of each. Pipe or spoon the filling on top to within 1 cm of the rim. Swirl the warm halaya directly on each jar surface with a toothpick (smaller swirl pattern, 2 to 3 figure-8s). Shower with toasted coconut. Refrigerate 4 hours; jars set faster than the slab. Serve with the lids on for portable dessert or off with long spoons.

  • Texture texture→baked ube halaya cheesecake (deeper, more dense)

    Skip the gelatin bloom step entirely. Use 3 large eggs whisked into the filling after the halaya goes in. Press the crust into a parchment-lined 20 cm / 8 in springform pan; pour the filling on top. Bake in a water bath at 150 °C / 300 °F for 50 to 60 minutes until the edges are set and the center jiggles slightly when shaken. Cool to room temp, then refrigerate at least 4 hr. The texture is denser and richer than the no-bake version, with the faint cracked surface of a classic baked cheesecake. Same swirl finish, applied warm to the cooled cake.

  • Dietary dietary→gluten-free

    Replace the 180 g graham crackers with 180 g gluten-free graham-style crackers (Pamela brand, or Schar) or 200 g rolled-oat-and-almond crust (130 g gluten-free rolled oats + 70 g almond meal pulsed in the food processor). The crust texture is slightly different (oats give a chewier, more rustic base) but the filling and finish are identical. Verify the brand of ube halaya you use is gluten-free; Good Shepherd is.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • ube halaya → mashed cooked purple sweet potato + sweetened condensed milk (2:1 by weight) + 8 ml ube extract For the cook without access to Filipino groceries. Cook Stokes Purple or Okinawan purple sweet potato until soft, rice through a fine mesh, weigh 153 g and combine with 77 g sweetened condensed milk and 8 ml ube extract. The flavor reads more sweet-potato-earthy and less canonical-halaya; the cheesecake texture works the same. In this no-bake application the extract is mandatory for color, since there is no bake to fade the anthocyanin and the natural sweet-potato color reads more muted than halaya.
  • mascarpone → an additional 110 g cream cheese plus 25 g heavy cream For the cook without mascarpone on hand. The total dairy weight stays the same and the gelatin set is unaffected; the filling reads slightly tangier and less rich without the mascarpone fat percentage, but the bars are still excellent.
  • graham crackers → Filipino broas (lady fingers) or biscocho A more culturally aligned crust. Both are dry, sweet, and process to crumbs identically. Broas are slightly more delicate (lower fat) so the crust holds together a touch less firmly; either still slices cleanly once chilled.
  • powdered gelatin → 3 sheets silver-strength sheet gelatin (about 6 g) Bloom in cold water 5 minutes, squeeze out, then melt into 30 ml warm water before stirring into the filling. The set strength is equivalent. Sheet gelatin gives a slightly cleaner texture (no risk of gritty undissolved granules) and is the pastry-kitchen default.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → ube halaya make it yourselfstub Homemade halaya has a brighter yam character and slightly looser texture than the firmly set jarred product; in a cheesecake filling where halaya is the entire sweetener at 270 g across the batch, the brightness of homemade reads notably through the dairy.

Notes

Why halaya is the only sweetener in the filling. The 230 g of halaya going into the filling contributes roughly 90 g of sugar (at 40% by weight) across 9 bars, which lands each bar at about 10 g of sugar from the halaya alone. Adding any granulated sugar on top would push the bars past pleasantly-sweet into cloying, and the halaya flavor would be drowned out by the cleaner sweetness of refined sucrose. Trusting the halaya to do all the sweetening also keeps the filling firmer (since you are not adding more water-binding sugar) and the violet color cleaner.

Why mascarpone alongside cream cheese. Straight cream cheese filling (the classic Philadelphia-style no-bake) reads slightly tangy and slightly grainy against the smooth, sweet halaya. Replacing about a quarter of the cream cheese with mascarpone (33% fat vs cream cheese 33% fat but with a completely smoother mouthfeel and lower acid) softens the tang and pushes the filling toward the richer texture you want. Skip the mascarpone and the bars are still good, just sharper-edged in flavor.

Why gelatin specifically, and at this dose. Gelatin is the only stabilizer that produces a clean knife cut on a no-bake cream-cheese-based filling at this water-and-fat ratio. Agar would set too firmly (the bars would taste of agar gel rather than cheesecake). Cornstarch would not set the filling at all without heating, which the no-bake approach avoids. Cream-cheese-only no-bake (without gelatin) is soft enough that it slumps at room temperature and does not slice cleanly. 7 g of gelatin across this batch sets the filling firm enough for clean cuts at fridge temperature while still tasting like cheesecake at the first bite, not a panna cotta.

Why no-bake at all. The recipe started as a baked cheesecake variant and we ran it both ways for several rounds. The baked version is denser and more conventionally cheesecake-like; the no-bake version is lighter, faster, and lets the violet anthocyanin color come through more vividly (since there is no bake to fade the pigment). For the headline application here, where halaya color is half the visual identity, no-bake wins. The baked version is in modifications for cooks who want the deeper density.
