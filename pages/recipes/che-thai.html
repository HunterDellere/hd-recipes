Chè Thái (Vietnamese-Thai mixed fruit and jelly dessert)

Cold sweet soup of jackfruit, longan, lychee, basil seeds, and red ruby water chestnuts in sweetened coconut cream. Vietnamese in origin despite the name, built on tinned tropical fruits and the textural contrast of jelly, crunch, and pop.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Chill the coconut sauce at least 30 minutes before assembly. The dish is served over crushed ice and warm or room-temperature sauce melts the ice immediately, diluting the dessert to flavorless coconut water within 2 minutes.
  • Hydrate the basil seeds in COLD water only, 15 minutes. They bloom into the translucent gel beads with the black dot inside that define the texture; hot water turns them mucilaginous and slimy and there is no recovery.
  • Drain the canned tropical fruits and RESERVE 60 ml / ¼ cup of the syrup for the coconut sauce. Discarding the syrup leaves the sauce one-note sweet; the syrup carries the fruit aromatics that tie the whole bowl together.
  • Crush the ice just before assembly. Pre-crushed ice in the freezer fuses back into a solid block; the dish wants loose shaved ice that the warm coconut sauce melts into a chilled slush.

Red ruby water chestnuts

  1. 200 g fresh water chestnuts, peeled, diced to 8 mm / ⅓ inch cubes — (about 8 medium)
  2. 1000 ml water for blanching

  4. 30 ml fresh red dragon fruit juice or beet juice

Basil seeds

  1. 15 g sweet basil seeds (hat é) or chia seeds
  2. 120 ml cold water

Tropical fruit

  1. 150 g canned jackfruit in syrup, drained, sliced
  2. 150 g canned longan in syrup, drained
  3. 150 g canned lychee in syrup, drained, halved

Coconut sauce

  1. 2 × 13.5 oz / 400 ml can full-fat coconut milk
  2. 80 g sweetened condensed coconut milk
  3. 1 g fine sea salt — (small pinch)
  4. 60 ml reserved fruit syrup

Service

  1. to taste crushed ice

Execution

  1. 1
    Red rubies. Toss the diced 200 g / 8 mm cubes of water chestnut with the 30 ml / 2 tbsp dragon fruit or beet juice in a bowl. Let stand 5 minutes, the surface dyes pink but the white center remains for visual contrast. Drain off any excess liquid. Dredge the pink-stained pieces in the 40 g / ⅓ cup tapioca starch, tossing until each piece is fully coated and the starch sticks to the damp surface. Shake off loose excess in a sieve. The starch coating is what gives the ruby effect: clear-pink jelly skin over an opaque white core when cooked.
  2. 2
    Bring the 1000 ml / 4 cups water to a rolling boil. Drop the coated water chestnuts in. They sink, then rise as the tapioca coating gelatinizes, about 3 to 4 minutes after the water returns to a boil. Boil 1 minute past surfacing. Drain immediately into a sieve and rinse under cold running water for 15 seconds to halt cooking and wash off surface starch. Set aside in cold water until use; they hold 4 hours this way.
  3. 3
    Basil seeds. In a small glass, stir the 15 g / 1 tbsp basil seeds into the 120 ml / ½ cup cold water. Let stand 15 minutes; the seeds hydrate into translucent gel beads with a black dot inside (the seed). Do not heat, basil seeds bloom in cold water and turn mucilaginous in hot. If using chia, identical method.
  4. 4
    Coconut sauce. In a measuring jug whisk together the 480 ml coconut milk, the 80 g / ¼ cup condensed coconut milk, the 1 g pinch salt, and the 60 ml / ¼ cup reserved fruit syrup. Taste, should read clearly sweet but not cloying. Adjust with more condensed milk if flat, more salt if too sweet. Refrigerate.
  5. 5
    Assembly. In each of 4 tall glasses or shallow bowls, layer 3 tbsp of the water chestnut rubies, then 2 tbsp each of the jackfruit, longan, and lychee. Spoon 2 tbsp of the hydrated basil seeds over the fruit. Add a generous handful of crushed ice (60 to 80 g per glass).
  6. 6
    Pour 150 ml / ⅔ cup of the chilled coconut sauce over each glass. Serve immediately with a long spoon. The eater stirs the dessert together; the layering is presentation, not preservation.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion fruit selection→with rambutan and mangosteen

    Replace the longan with canned rambutan and add 100 g / ⅓ cup canned mangosteen pieces. The Saigon street-stall version often runs 5 to 7 tropical fruits; the three-fruit base is a sensible home build but expand whenever the markets cooperate.

  • Texture serving format→as a smoothie (sinh tố chè Thái)

    Combine all components except the basil seeds in a blender with the coconut sauce and a heaping cup of ice. Pulse to a thick slush, texture should be thicker than a milkshake but pourable. Top with the basil seeds. This is the cafe iteration.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • sweet basil seeds (hat é) → chia seeds, identical method and quantity Slightly different gel, chia is a bit slipperier, basil seeds a bit firmer. Visually similar; functionally interchangeable.
  • red dragon fruit juice → a few drops red food coloring + water Less special but acceptable. Real dragon fruit juice has the visual depth of natural pigment that food coloring lacks; some shops use grenadine for the same effect.
  • fresh water chestnuts → canned water chestnuts, drained and patted dry Texturally inferior, canned water chestnut has lost most of its crisp snap and absorbed metallic water. If only canned available, use jicama instead.
  • fresh water chestnuts → jicama, peeled and diced Jicama is the standard substitute in the US where fresh water chestnuts are scarce. Texturally close, slightly sweeter.

Notes

The dish is built on textural contrast, not flavor complexity. The coconut sauce is the unifier; the water chestnut rubies are the chewy-crunchy backbone; the basil seeds are the popping bead; the fruits are the soft sweet centers. Skip any one and the balance flattens.

Canned tropical fruits in this dish are the correct ingredient, not a compromise. Fresh jackfruit and lychee in the West are inconsistent and often underripe; the canned syrups (which are then re-used in the sauce) carry a tropical-fruit-cocktail aroma that is structurally part of chè Thái. This is the Southeast Asian dessert that pairs well with shelf-stable ingredients; lean into the convenience.

Adding the ice last and pouring the cold sauce over is non-negotiable. Add sauce before ice and the ice dilutes the sauce as it melts; add ice after pouring and the temperature drops gradually as it should, with the sauce thickening to a slush on the spoon by the bottom of the glass.
