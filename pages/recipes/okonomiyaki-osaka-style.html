Okonomiyaki (Osaka-style cabbage pancake)

A thick savory pancake of finely chopped cabbage, scallion, beni shoga, and pork belly slices, bound with a dashi-rich flour batter built around grated nagaimo yam (the texture secret). Pan-fried on both sides to a deep golden crust around a tender steamed-cabbage interior, then loaded with okonomi sauce, Kewpie mayo, dancing bonito flakes, and aonori. The defining Osaka street food and home-cooking convention.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Source nagaimo (Japanese mountain yam, also called yamaimo). The grated nagaimo is the textural secret of proper okonomiyaki: its mucilaginous starch lightens the batter so the interior reads fluffy-tender rather than dense-doughy. Find at Japanese, Korean, or some Chinese groceries (long brown cylinder, white interior). Substitute 1:1 with peeled grated daikon plus a pinch of cornstarch if absolutely necessary, but the dish reads structurally different without nagaimo.
  • Buy Otafuku brand okonomiyaki sauce, Kewpie mayonnaise, katsuobushi bonito flakes, beni shoga (red pickled ginger), and aonori (powdered green seaweed) at a Japanese grocery. These are the canonical toppings and substitutes do not produce the same dish. All are pantry items that last months.
  • Cook one pancake at a time in a 24 cm nonstick or well-seasoned cast-iron skillet. Trying to flip a 30 cm pancake at home is the most common failure; a smaller pancake flips cleanly with a wide spatula.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, batter base

  1. 150 g all-purpose flour
  2. 180 g dashi (kombu-katsuobushi, see homemade_alternatives)
  3. 100 g nagaimo (Japanese mountain yam), peeled and grated on a fine grater, peeled, then grated on the smallest holes of a box grater into a mucilaginous puree (it will look like clear gooey starch; this is correct)
  4. 2 each large eggs
  5. 4 g kosher salt
  6. 8 g soy sauce (Japanese koikuchi shoyu)
  7. 4 g sugar (granulated)

Phase 2, mix-ins

  1. 500 g green cabbage, very finely chopped (3 to 4 mm pieces), core removed, leaves stacked and shredded fine; the small dice is structural so the cabbage incorporates into the batter rather than sitting on top — (~½ medium head; the volume looks excessive but it cooks down dramatically)
  2. 60 g scallions, thin-sliced (white and green parts) — (4 to 5 scallions)
  3. 30 g beni shoga (red pickled ginger), drained and chopped
  4. 8 g katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes), for the batter (separate from the topping) — (small handful; adds umami depth to the batter)

Phase 3, pork and cook

  1. 200 g pork belly, thinly sliced (about 2 mm), shabu-shabu thin-sliced pork belly from a Japanese grocery is ideal; otherwise freeze a slab for 90 minutes and slice as thin as possible by hand
  2. 40 g neutral oil for the pan

Phase 4, toppings

  1. 80 g okonomi sauce (Otafuku brand)
  2. 40 g Kewpie mayonnaise
  3. 10 g katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes), for topping — (generous handful; the flakes dance in the heat rising from the pancake; the canonical visual finish)
  4. 3 g aonori (powdered green seaweed)
  5. 15 g beni shoga (for topping the cooked pancake) — (small pile per portion)

Execution

  1. 1
    Mix the batter base. In a large bowl, whisk together the 150 g all-purpose flour and 180 g cold dashi until smooth, no lumps. Whisk in the 100 g grated nagaimo (the mixture will go gluey and viscous from the yam mucilage; this is correct). Whisk in the 2 eggs, 4 g salt, 8 g soy sauce, and 4 g sugar until uniform. The batter should be the consistency of slightly thick pancake batter; if it is too thick, splash in more dashi 30 g at a time.
  2. 2
    Fold in the cabbage and mix-ins. Add the 500 g finely chopped cabbage, 60 g sliced scallions, 30 g chopped beni shoga, and 8 g katsuobushi flakes to the batter bowl. With a wide spoon or your hands, fold everything together until each cabbage shred is coated with batter. The mixture should look like a cabbage-heavy slaw barely held together with sticky batter; if it looks soupy, the batter-to-cabbage ratio is wrong (need more cabbage). Rest the mixed batter 5 minutes; the cabbage gives up a small amount of water that the batter absorbs, producing a more cohesive pancake.
  3. 3
    Heat the pan. Heat 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil in a 24 cm nonstick or well-seasoned cast-iron skillet over medium heat until shimmering. The temperature is calibrated to medium, not high; high heat burns the outside before the cabbage cooks through.
  4. 4
    Form pancake 1. Scoop about ¼ of the batter mixture (a generous 350 g) into the hot pan. Spread gently with a spatula into a thick disc about 2.5 cm tall and 20 cm across, leaving a 2 cm gap to the pan edge for flipping room. Do NOT press down hard; the pancake should remain lofty. Lay 4 to 5 thin slices of pork belly (about 50 g) over the top of the disc in a single layer.
  5. 5
    Cook side 1. Cook undisturbed 4 to 5 minutes. The bottom should turn deep golden-brown and the edges should set; lift gently with a spatula at minute 4 to check color. The interior cabbage is still raw at this stage; the steam under the pancake is cooking it.
  6. 6
    Flip. Drizzle a little oil around the pancake edges. Slide a wide spatula (a 15 cm wide turner is ideal; use two spatulas if you have two) under the pancake. In one confident motion, flip the pancake over so the pork-belly side is now down. If the pancake breaks slightly, push it back together with the spatula; it will re-seal as the second side cooks. The pork belly will sizzle and start rendering its fat into the pan; this is the structural moment when the pork-fat-meets-batter interface develops.
  7. 7
    Cook side 2 (the pork-belly side). Cook undisturbed 4 to 5 minutes. The pork belly will brown and crisp at its edges; the pancake will continue to steam from the inside, cooking the cabbage tender. Press gently in the center with the spatula at minute 4 to check; if the spatula meets springy resistance and the center feels firm, the pancake is done.
  8. 8
    Flip back and finish. Flip the pancake one more time so the original side is up. Cook 60 seconds to re-crisp. Slide onto a plate, pork-belly-side up; the canonical Osaka presentation is pork-belly-up so the dressings layer over the meat.
  9. 9
    Top and serve pancake 1. Drizzle 20 g okonomi sauce over the surface in a zig-zag pattern (use a squeeze bottle if you have one). Drizzle 10 g Kewpie mayo in a perpendicular zig-zag over the sauce. Scatter a generous handful of katsuobushi flakes over the top; the flakes will visibly dance and curl from the steam rising off the hot pancake (the "dancing bonito" is the canonical visual element). Sprinkle 1 g aonori in a stripe across the center. Top with a small pile of beni shoga off to one side. Cut into 6 to 8 wedges with the side of a spatula; the diners pull wedges directly off the plate with chopsticks.
  10. 10
    Cook pancakes 2, 3, and 4 the same way. Wipe out the skillet between pancakes; add fresh 10 g oil per pancake. Total cook time is about 25 minutes across 4 pancakes; serve each one fresh and hot rather than holding them.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→Hiroshima-style (layered, with yakisoba noodles)

    Skip the batter-cabbage mix. Pour a thin batter pancake (just batter, eggs, dashi) onto the griddle. Pile 200 g chopped cabbage on top. Add 50 g pork belly slices over the cabbage. Pour a small ladle of batter over the top to bind. Flip the entire stack with two spatulas. In a separate area of the griddle, fry 80 g pre-cooked yakisoba noodles with okonomi sauce. Slide the cabbage stack onto the noodles. Fry a thin egg layer separately; slide onto the top of the stack. This is the Hiroshima city tradition, more complex and griddle-dependent; requires a flat-top.

  • Protein protein→seafood okonomiyaki (squid, shrimp, oyster)

    Skip the pork belly. Substitute 200 g total of mixed seafood: 100 g chopped raw squid, 100 g chopped raw shrimp. Mix INTO the batter rather than laying on top. The dish reads cleaner and more coastal; common in the Setouchi region.

  • Regional protein→kimchi okonomiyaki

    Add 100 g chopped well-fermented sour kimchi to the batter (replacing 100 g of the cabbage). Top with pork belly as usual. The dish reads as a Korean-Japanese fusion; common in modern restaurants in both Osaka and Tokyo.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • nagaimo (Japanese mountain yam) → peeled grated daikon + cornstarch (1:1 by weight + 1 tsp cornstarch per 100 g) Nagaimo is structural; the substitute approximates the textural effect but the dish reads less light. Find nagaimo at Japanese, Korean, or Chinese groceries; it keeps 2 weeks refrigerated.
  • pork belly, thinly sliced → thick-cut bacon, pancetta slices Bacon reads smokier and saltier; pancetta reads cleaner and more European. Both work; pork belly is the canonical Japanese choice.
  • dashi → water + 1 tsp instant dashi powder (hondashi), or chicken stock Instant hondashi (Ajinomoto brand) makes a perfectly acceptable shortcut dashi; the dish loses about 10% of its structural umami complexity. Chicken stock works but reads as the wrong direction (too meaty for the cabbage-and-egg dish).
  • Kewpie mayonnaise → Japanese-style mayo from any brand, or American mayo + rice vinegar (1 tbsp per cup) Kewpie is the canonical Japanese mayo, made with egg yolks only (no whites) and rice vinegar instead of distilled. Mixing American mayo with a splash of rice vinegar gets close enough.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → dashi (kombu-katsuobushi, see homemade_alternatives) make it yourself Homemade ichiban dashi takes 15 minutes and is dramatically more aromatic than instant hondashi. The cold-soaked kombu and the off-heat katsuobushi infusion produce a clean, complex stock that elevates the batter.
  • → okonomi sauce (Otafuku brand) make it yourselfstub Otafuku is the canonical bottled sauce; the homemade version (Worcestershire, ketchup, oyster sauce, mirin, sugar, dates) takes 10 minutes and gives control over sweetness and tang.
  • → Kewpie mayonnaise make it yourself Homemade mayo with rice vinegar and only egg yolks produces a richer, tangier mayo than store-bought; the Kewpie profile is achievable at home in 5 minutes with a stick blender.

Notes

Nagaimo is the textural secret. The single ingredient that distinguishes restaurant-quality okonomiyaki from home attempts is grated nagaimo (Japanese mountain yam). The yam''s mucilaginous starch (similar to okra slime) lightens the flour batter so the interior cooks fluffy-tender rather than dense-doughy. Most Western recipes omit nagaimo because it is hard to find; the resulting pancakes are recognizably heavier and less appealing. The hunt for nagaimo at a Japanese grocery is worth the trip.

The cabbage ratio is the structural call. Okonomiyaki is roughly 70% cabbage by weight in the finished pancake; the batter is just the binder. New cooks frequently use too much batter and not enough cabbage, producing a flat dense pancake that reads as a savory pancake with cabbage in it rather than as cabbage-with-batter. The 500 g cabbage to 150 g flour ratio in this recipe is the canonical proportion; trust the volume.

Why pork belly on top, not in the batter. Laying the thin pork belly slices on the surface of the pancake (and flipping so the pork hits the pan directly in side 2) produces a structurally specific result: the pork fat renders directly into the pan and caramelizes the contact surface to a deep golden crust, while the pork meat sits in direct contact with the cabbage interior, infusing it with pork flavor as it steams. Mixing pork into the batter produces a less dimensional dish.

The dancing bonito is the visual centerpiece. Katsuobushi flakes scattered on the hot pancake immediately after topping with sauce and mayo will curl, twist, and visually "dance" from the steam rising off the surface. This is the canonical Japanese serving moment and is worth pausing to watch. The dancing slows as the pancake cools; serve immediately so the diner sees it.

Okonomi means "how you like." The name "okonomiyaki" combines "okonomi" (how you like, your preference) with "yaki" (grilled). The Osaka tradition is highly customizable: the canonical pork-and-cabbage is the home default, but cooks add seafood, mochi, cheese, kimchi, corn, mountain vegetables, and dozens of other variations to taste. The dish is meant to flex with the cook''s preference; this recipe gives the canonical baseline.

Osaka versus Hiroshima, the actual distinction. The two cities have a friendly rivalry over their okonomiyaki traditions. Osaka mixes everything into one batter and pan-fries; Hiroshima layers the components separately on a griddle, including yakisoba noodles as a structural layer. Both are valid; the Osaka style translates to home pans, while the Hiroshima style requires a flat griddle. Most okonomiyaki served in Tokyo and abroad is Osaka-style.

Day-2 okonomiyaki is acceptable but not as good. The pancake reheats in a dry pan over medium-low for 4 to 5 minutes per side; the cabbage softens further and the texture goes a touch denser. The toppings (sauce, mayo, bonito, aonori) should be re-applied fresh after reheating since they degrade on the chill. Refrigerate up to 2 days.

Eating mechanics. The pancake is cut into 6 to 8 wedges directly on the serving plate (the side of the spatula does the work; no need for a knife). Diners pull wedges with chopsticks from a communal plate, or each diner gets their own pancake. Some Osaka diners eat directly off the hot griddle at teppanyaki restaurants; the canonical home presentation is on a plate at the table.

Sources
  • Osaka okonomiyaki tradition (the home and street-food canonical form) — Okonomiyaki (お好み焼き, "grilled how you like") has two distinct schools. Osaka-style mixes all ingredients into the batter and pan-fries as a single thick pancake; Hiroshima-style builds the layers (batter, cabbage, pork, noodles, egg) stacked separately on the griddle. The Osaka form is more common in home cooking and outside Japan; this recipe is the canonical Osaka direction. The dish became popular post-WWII as a way to stretch limited rice with cabbage and flour; teppanyaki-style restaurants featuring tableside okonomiyaki griddles are common throughout Kansai (Osaka, Kobe, Kyoto).
