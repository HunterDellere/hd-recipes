Lomo saltado

The Chinese-Peruvian (chifa) stir-fry that defines the Lima Saturday lunch: beef sirloin seared hot in a smoking wok, tossed with red onion, tomato, ají amarillo, soy sauce, and red wine vinegar, then plated over a bed of crisp French fries with white rice on the side. A structural fusion of Cantonese wok technique and Peruvian pantry, both elements visible in every bite.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Lomo saltado is structurally a three-component plate (fries, rice, stir-fry) that all need to be hot at service. Start the rice first (20 minutes), the fries second (15 minutes), and the stir-fry last (5 to 7 minutes). Everything hits the plate at the same moment.
  • Use a carbon-steel wok or a heavy stainless or cast-iron skillet. A non-stick pan cannot reach the high temperature needed for wok hei (the smoky char that defines Chinese-Peruvian chifa technique).
  • Slice the beef across the grain into thin strips (5 mm thick, 4 cm long). Marinate 15 minutes in soy sauce, vinegar, and cornstarch for the structural velvet-and-flavor base.
  • Cut the onion and tomato into wedges (not dice). The structural lomo saltado has identifiable wedge-cut vegetables, not minced or sliced thin.

Phase 1, marinate the beef

  1. 500 g beef sirloin (or flank, tri-tip, hanger steak), sliced across the grain into strips 5 mm thick, 4 cm long, 1.5 cm wide
  2. 30 g light soy sauce
  3. 15 g red wine vinegar
  4. 8 g cornstarch
  5. 2 g ground cumin
  6. 2 g black pepper, freshly ground

Phase 2, the fries (or rice)

  1. 600 g russet potatoes, peeled, cut into 1 cm thick fries (8 cm long) — (2 large potatoes; the structural chifa starch; oven-baked, deep-fried, or air-fried as the cook prefers)
  2. 60 g neutral oil (for frying)
  3. 4 g fine sea salt (for the fries)

Phase 3, the stir-fry sauce

  1. 30 g light soy sauce (for the sauce)
  2. 15 g oyster sauce
  3. 30 g red wine vinegar (for the sauce)
  4. 15 g ají amarillo paste
  5. 60 g beef stock (or water)

Phase 4, the stir-fry

  1. 30 g neutral oil (for the wok)
  2. 12 g garlic cloves, peeled, finely minced — (3 cloves)
  3. 200 g red onion, cut into 2 cm wedges (separate the layers) — (1 medium onion; the structural chifa wedge-cut vegetable)
  4. 200 g Roma tomatoes (firm, not overripe), cored, cut into 2 cm wedges (8 wedges per tomato) — (2 medium tomatoes; the structural chifa wedge-cut tomato; use firm tomatoes that hold their shape in the wok)
  5. 6 g fresh ají amarillo (or substitute serrano), stemmed, seeded, cut into thin strips — (1 ají amarillo or 1 serrano; the structural fresh chili layered on the paste)
  6. 60 g scallions, cut diagonally into 3 cm lengths — (4 scallions; the structural chifa finishing aromatic)
  7. 8 g fresh cilantro, chopped

Phase 5, the rice

  1. 600 g long-grain white rice, cooked

Execution

  1. 1
    Phase 1, marinate the beef. In a medium bowl, combine the 500 g sliced beef, 30 g light soy sauce, 15 g red wine vinegar, 8 g cornstarch, 2 g cumin, and 2 g black pepper. Mix with your hands to coat each piece; let rest 15 minutes at room temperature. The structural velvet step coats the beef in cornstarch to protect against the high-heat sear; the soy and vinegar penetrate the surface and add structural flavor. Velveting
  2. 2
    Start the rice. Cook 200 g jasmine or long-grain white rice in 400 g water with 1 g salt: rinse the rice 3 times until the water runs clear; bring to a boil; reduce heat to lowest setting; cover; cook 18 minutes. Rest 10 minutes off heat covered. Fluff with a fork before serving. The rice should be ready at the same moment the stir-fry hits the plate.
  3. 3
    Phase 2, make the fries (in parallel with the rice). For oven-baked: preheat oven to 220°C / 425°F. Toss 600 g cut fries with 30 g neutral oil and 2 g salt; spread on a parchment-lined sheet pan in a single layer. Bake 25 to 30 minutes, flipping halfway, until deeply golden and crisp. For deep-fried (canonical): heat 1 L neutral oil to 170°C / 340°F; fry the cut fries 4 minutes (the first soft fry); drain. Increase oil to 190°C / 375°F; fry the parcooked fries 3 minutes more until deeply golden and crisp; drain on paper towels; salt with 4 g fine sea salt immediately.
  4. 4
    Phase 3, mix the stir-fry sauce. In a small bowl, combine the 30 g light soy sauce, 15 g oyster sauce, 30 g red wine vinegar, 15 g ají amarillo paste, and 60 g beef stock. Stir to combine; set near the wok.
  5. 5
    Phase 4, heat the wok and sear the beef. Heat a carbon-steel wok or heavy stainless skillet over the highest possible heat 3 minutes until the surface is smoking. Add 15 g of the 30 g neutral oil; swirl to coat the wok. Add the marinated 500 g beef in a single layer (do not crowd; sear in 2 batches if the wok is small); sear undisturbed 60 seconds. Toss and stir-fry 30 to 45 seconds until the beef is browned on the outside and still pink at the center; transfer to a plate. The total cook is structural; longer than 90 seconds at high heat overcooks the beef.
  6. 6
    Phase 5, stir-fry the aromatics. Return the wok to high heat; add the remaining 15 g neutral oil. Add the 12 g minced garlic; stir-fry 15 seconds until fragrant. Add the 200 g red onion wedges and 6 g fresh ají amarillo strips; stir-fry 60 to 90 seconds until the onion starts to soften but still has structural crunch.
  7. 7
    Combine and finish. Return the seared beef and any juices to the wok; add the 200 g tomato wedges. Pour the stir-fry sauce around the edges of the wok (the sauce caramelizes against the hot wok surface, the structural wok hei flavor). Toss everything together 60 to 90 seconds until the tomatoes just start to soften but still hold their wedge shape, the sauce coats the beef and vegetables in a glossy lacquer, and the entire stir-fry reads deeply aromatic. Add the 60 g scallion pieces; toss 15 seconds more.
  8. 8
    Plate and serve. For each plate, mound about 150 g cooked rice on one side. Cover the other half of the plate with about 150 g hot fries. Spoon the lomo saltado over the fries (canonical) so the sauce soaks into the top of the fries. Scatter the 8 g chopped cilantro over each plate. Serve immediately; the structural eating motion is to take a forkful of beef-and-tomato-and-fries together, alternating with rice.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as tallarín saltado (the noodle version)

    Replace the rice and fries with 400 g cooked spaghetti or fresh Chinese egg noodles (cooked just to al dente, drained, tossed with 15 g neutral oil to prevent sticking). After the final toss of beef, vegetables, and sauce, add the cooked noodles to the wok; toss to coat. Plate as a noodle dish; finish with cilantro. The result is tallarín saltado, the structural chifa noodle version that pairs the same flavor architecture with the structurally Italian-Chinese-Peruvian noodle base. Canonical alternative chifa preparation.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as arroz chaufa (Peruvian-style fried rice)

    Cook 300 g long-grain rice the day before; refrigerate. Skip the marinated beef sear; dice 300 g cooked roast chicken or smoked ham instead. Stir-fry 100 g diced onion and 100 g diced green bell pepper in 30 g neutral oil; add 2 beaten eggs and scramble briefly; add the cold rice and break up the clumps; add the chicken or ham and 30 g light soy sauce and 15 g sesame oil; toss to combine. Garnish with scallions. The result is arroz chaufa, the structural Peruvian-Chinese fried rice; canonical chifa weeknight dish.

  • Protein protein→as pollo saltado (chicken version)

    Replace the beef with 500 g boneless skinless chicken breast or thigh, sliced the same way and marinated the same way. The cook time in the wok is slightly longer (90 seconds to 2 minutes total to cook through). The pollo saltado version reads lighter than the beef canon and is the canonical alternative protein in chifa restaurants. Vegetable saltado (with mushrooms, tofu, and additional vegetables) is the vegetarian version using the same architecture.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • beef sirloin (or flank, tri-tip, hanger steak) → flank steak or skirt steak Both are canonical alternatives; sirloin is the standard Peruvian cut but flank and skirt produce excellent lomo saltado. Slice all of them across the grain at 5 mm. Avoid lean cuts (tenderloin); the dish needs the structural beef fat for the wok hei character.
  • ají amarillo paste → a blend of 1 part habanero paste plus 2 parts roasted yellow bell pepper puree Ají amarillo is the structural Peruvian chili character; the substitute approximates the heat and color without the structural floral note. Acceptable when ají amarillo is unavailable.
  • oyster sauce → mushroom oyster sauce (vegetarian) or hoisin sauce Oyster sauce is canonical chifa; the vegetarian mushroom oyster sauce works mechanically with the same depth. Hoisin sauce reads sweeter and is less canonical but acceptable.

Notes

Lomo saltado is the structural chifa dish of Peru and one of the great cross-cultural fusion preparations in any cuisine. The technique (high-heat wok sear, soy-and-vinegar sauce, served over a starch) is structurally Cantonese; the ingredients (beef, ají amarillo, French fries, white rice) are structurally Peruvian and European. The resulting dish is structurally chifa, neither Chinese nor Peruvian in isolation; it represents the 19th-century Cantonese immigration to Peru that created one of the most successful immigrant cuisines in South America.

The wok hei is structural. Wok hei (Cantonese for breath of the wok, the smoky-char flavor that comes from food searing against a smoking-hot wok surface) is the structural Cantonese technique that defines lomo saltado as a chifa dish. Without wok hei, the dish reads as good beef-and-tomato stir-fry; with wok hei, it reads as canonical chifa. Achieve wok hei through three structural conditions: a carbon-steel wok or heavy stainless or cast-iron skillet (non-stick cannot reach the temperature), the highest possible burner heat (a wok burner if you have one), and small batches that do not crowd the wok (crowding drops the surface temperature and produces steam rather than sear).

The fries-on-the-plate are structural. French fries (papas fritas) are the canonical chifa starch in lomo saltado and serve a structural role: the hot fries absorb the soy-vinegar sauce from the stir-fry while staying crisp at the edges, producing the canonical chifa flavor density that defines the dish. Some Peruvian households cook the fries directly into the stir-fry in the final 60 seconds; this works but loses the structural crispness. The plate-and-serve approach (stir-fry over fries) is the more common modern preparation.

The white rice is the second structural starch and is non-negotiable. Lomo saltado is canonically served with both rice and fries; the diner eats them together with the stir-fry, producing the structural carb-heavy chifa plate that defines Peruvian Saturday lunch. Skipping the rice and serving only fries (or vice versa) produces a structurally incomplete dish; the canonical Peruvian eats both.

The wedge cut is structural. Onion and tomato in lomo saltado are cut into 2 cm wedges (not diced, not sliced thin) because the structural eating experience depends on identifiable vegetable pieces with their own integrity. The wok hei sear chars the outside of the wedges while the interior stays tender-but-still-structured; this is the structural visual and textural signature of the dish. Diced or sliced vegetables produce a structurally different stir-fry that reads as generic Chinese rather than as canonical chifa.

The sauce should be glossy and minimal. Lomo saltado is not a saucy stir-fry; the canonical version has just enough sauce to lacquer the beef and vegetables, with a small amount pooling at the bottom of the plate to season the fries and rice. A wet over-sauced lomo saltado reads structurally wrong; the structural chifa sauce is concentrated and glossy, not loose.

Versatility. The chifa flavor architecture (soy, vinegar, ají amarillo, oyster sauce, garlic, onion, tomato) transfers to chicken (pollo saltado), tofu (saltado vegetariano), shrimp (camarón saltado), and noodles (tallarín saltado). The stir-fry technique transfers to virtually any protein-and-vegetable combination; the canonical Peruvian chifa kitchen runs through dozens of saltado variations on the same structural template.

Storage. Lomo saltado is structurally a freshly-finished dish and is at its best within 10 minutes of cooking. Refrigerated leftovers (the beef and vegetables, not the fries) keep 2 days; reheat in a hot skillet 2 minutes to refresh. The fries do not store; eat fresh. Cook the dish fresh whenever possible.

Sources
  • The Peruvian chifa canon. Lomo saltado developed in the late 19th century in Lima Chinatown (the largest Chinatown in South America), when Cantonese immigrant cooks adapted their wok-stir-fry technique to Peruvian ingredients (beef instead of pork, tomato, ají amarillo, French fries). The dish stabilized in the early 20th century as the canonical chifa preparation and is now one of the most-cooked dishes in Peru, served at home, in chifa restaurants, and in fine-dining establishments. — The chifa tradition (from Cantonese 食饭 chī fàn, eat rice) is the structural Chinese-Peruvian cuisine that emerged from 19th-century Chinese contract labor on Peruvian sugar plantations. Lomo saltado, arroz chaufa (Peruvian-style fried rice), and tallarín saltado (the noodle version of lomo saltado) are the three structural chifa dishes. The French fries on the plate are structurally chifa: they bridge Peruvian and European traditions in the same dish that bridges Peruvian and Chinese.
