Shrimp pulao (jhinga pulao)

Coastal-Indian basmati pilaf cooked with marinated shrimp, ginger-garlic, whole spices, and a coconut-milk-and-stock cooking liquid. Less elaborate than biryani (no parboil-and-layer), more aromatic than plain rice. The shrimp-and-spice flavors infuse into each grain through absorption-method cooking.

Execution

  1. 1
    Marinate the shrimp. In a bowl, combine the 500 g shrimp with the 60 g yogurt, 30 g ginger-garlic paste, 4 g Kashmiri chili powder, 1 g turmeric, 4 g salt, and 8 ml lemon juice. Toss to coat. Set aside 20 minutes at room temperature (no longer; shrimp toughens in acidic marinade past 30 minutes).
  2. 2
    Rinse and soak the rice. Place the 400 g basmati in a bowl and rinse under cold water 3 to 4 times until the water runs almost clear (this removes surface starch that would make the pulao gummy). Cover with fresh cold water and soak 20 to 30 minutes; soaking lets the grains pre-hydrate and produces longer, more separate grains in the finished pulao. Drain just before cooking.
  3. 3
    Bloom the whole spices. Heat the 45 g ghee in a heavy 4-quart Dutch oven or wide pot with a tight lid over medium heat until shimmering. Add the 4 cracked cardamom pods, 4 cloves, 5 g cinnamon stick, 2 bay leaves, 4 g / 1½ tsp cumin seeds, and 1 star anise pod. Bloom 30 to 45 seconds; the cumin will sizzle and darken slightly, the kitchen will fill with the warm-spice aroma. Blooming aromatics
  4. 4
    Brown the onion. Add the 200 g sliced onion and 8 g slit green chilies to the spiced ghee. Stir-fry over medium heat for 8 to 10 minutes until the onion is deep golden-brown at the edges and softened throughout. Pulao onion does not need to go fully mahogany like nihari onion; deep gold with brown edges is correct.
  5. 5
    Sear the marinated shrimp. Add the marinated shrimp (with all the marinade) to the pot. Stir-fry 2 to 3 minutes; the shrimp will turn from grey-translucent to pink-opaque and the yogurt-marinade will reduce to a thick coat. Pull the shrimp out with a slotted spoon (do not over-cook them now; they will warm through again in the steaming step). Set aside on a plate. Maillard sear
  6. 6
    Add the rice and toast. Add the drained rice to the pot. Stir-fry 2 minutes over medium heat; the grains will absorb the spice-flavored ghee and turn from translucent to faintly opaque. This brief toast in flavored fat is the structural pulao step (distinguishing pulao from boiled rice with sauce).
  7. 7
    Add liquid and bring to a boil. Pour in the 200 ml coconut milk, 500 ml water, and 6 g salt. Stir gently once to distribute. Bring to a strong simmer over medium-high heat (the surface should bubble actively but not violently boil). Taste the liquid; it should be aggressively seasoned (the rice will absorb the seasoning).
  8. 8
    Steam-cook the pulao. Once the liquid is at a strong simmer, return the cooked shrimp to the pot and gently nestle them on top of the rice (do not stir them in). Reduce heat to low, cover with a tight lid (if your lid is loose, place a clean kitchen towel between pot and lid to seal). Cook 12 minutes undisturbed. Pull off heat. Let rest, still covered, 10 minutes (the residual heat finishes the rice and lets the grains relax to their final texture).
  9. 9
    Fluff and serve. Remove the lid. Add the 15 g cilantro and 8 g mint leaves to the surface. Using a fork (not a spoon — a spoon mashes the grains), gently fluff the rice from the bottom of the pot upward, lifting the rice and shrimp together; this brings the spices distributed through the bottom up through the dish without breaking the grains. Transfer to a wide serving dish. Top with the 30 g fried onions. Serve immediately with the lemon wedges and a side of raita.

Equipment

heavy 4-quart Dutch oven or wide pot with tight lid

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→chicken pulao

    Substitute 500 g boneless chicken thighs cut into 3 cm pieces. Marinate the same way (yogurt + spices) for 30 minutes. Brown the chicken in step 5 for 5 to 6 minutes until lightly browned (chicken can take more aggressive cooking than shrimp). Same procedure throughout. The dish becomes the standard South Asian chicken pulao.

  • Occasion cooking liquid→biryani-style with parcooked rice

    Skip the absorption-method rice cooking. Boil the rice 70% of the way (5 to 6 minutes in heavily salted water), drain. Cook the shrimp masala (steps 1-5) until the shrimp masala is rich and thick. Layer parcooked rice over the shrimp masala in a heavy pot, drizzle with saffron-milk (8 threads steeped in 60 ml warm milk) and ghee, cover tightly, dum-cook on very low heat 20 minutes. The dish becomes shrimp biryani, more complex, more layered, and structurally a different dish.

  • Occasion serving format→with sealed dough lid (dum pulao)

    After step 7 (liquid added), seal the lid with a roti dough (50 g atta + water rolled into a snake and pressed around the rim). The dough seals the lid hermetically, intensifying the steam-cook. Cook at low heat 18 minutes. The dough is broken at the table, visually dramatic. The dish becomes the more elaborate dum pulao.

  • Heat heat level→mild for kids

    Omit green chilies. Reduce Kashmiri chili powder to 2 g (¼ tbsp). The dish becomes a fragrant aromatic pulao with no heat; suitable for diners who want the spice-and-shrimp-and-coconut character without chili.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • basmati rice → no good substitute See ingredients/basmati-rice. The long-grain aromatic structure is essential to pulao. Other long-grain rice (jasmine, Carolina) produces wrong-textured pulao.
  • full-fat coconut milk → whole milk + 1 tsp coconut oil The coconut milk is the coastal-Indian signature. Whole milk + a small amount of coconut oil approximates without coconut character entirely. The dish reads as a non-coastal pulao but works.
  • fresh shrimp → frozen shrimp, fully thawed and patted dry Frozen is excellent and often fresher than supermarket "fresh." Defrost overnight in the fridge.

  • → chicken stock make it yourself Real gelatin-rich chicken stock makes the pulao body richer than plain water; the difference is real in absorption-cooked rice dishes.
  • → coconut milk make it yourselfstub Fresh coconut milk made from grated mature coconut is dramatically richer and more aromatic than canned. A weekend project for the dedicated cook.

Notes

Aged basmati matters. The best pulao uses aged basmati rice (1 to 2 years aged from harvest). Aged basmati cooks up with longer, more separate grains and a more pronounced aromatic profile than freshly-harvested basmati. Indian groceries label "aged" or "premium aged" on premium-tier bags; the cost premium is worth it for special occasions. Standard supermarket basmati works well; just rinse and soak as written.

Why absorption method, not boil-and-drain. Pulao is structurally absorption-cooked: a precise liquid-to-rice ratio (1.75:1 for soaked basmati, 2:1 for unsoaked) is added once and the rice absorbs all the liquid during steaming. The all-the-flavor-in-one-pot principle of pulao depends on absorption method; boiling and draining (the biryani parcook step) lets too much flavor drain off with the cooking water. The technique is the difference.

Liquid-to-rice ratio. The exact ratio depends on the rice age, the soak length, the pot type (Dutch oven vs thin saucepan), and the heat behavior. The 1.75:1 ratio (700 ml liquid for 400 g rice when the rice is soaked 20 to 30 minutes) is the textbook Indian pulao standard and works in most home kitchens. If your pulao comes out slightly soupy, reduce next time by 50 ml; if it comes out slightly dry, add 50 ml. Cooks calibrate to their specific equipment.

Eating mechanics. Pulao is a complete meal-on-a-plate when served with raita (yogurt + chopped cucumber or boondi + spices) and a small side of pickle (lime or mango achar) or a salad of sliced tomatoes-onions-cucumbers with lemon. The temperature contrast of cool raita and hot pulao is part of the eating experience. Reheats well, the day-after pulao often tastes better as the spices integrate further. Refrigerate up to 3 days.

Sources
  • Coastal Indian / Mughlai-influenced tradition — Pulao (पुलाव, also pilaf, polo, pilav) is a rice-cooked-in-flavored-liquid technique that traces back to ancient Persian polo and migrated through Central Asia and Mughal India into the modern subcontinental kitchen. Shrimp pulao is a coastal-Indian variant (Goa, Mangalore, Kerala) that uses local seafood in place of the meat that defines northern pulaos and biryanis.
