Lahpet (fermented tea leaves)

Burmese pickled tea: tender green tea leaf steamed soft, pounded, and lacto-fermented under pressure and oil for weeks until its raw astringency mellows into a soft, sour, faintly bitter green mass. This is the from-scratch base of lahpet thoke, and one of the few foods on earth built from eaten, not brewed, tea.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a 3-week project, minimum. Active work is about 40 minutes spread over two sessions; the rest is the jar sitting at cool room temperature lacto-fermenting. Start it at least 21 days before you want to make lahpet thoke, and know that 4 to 6 weeks tastes better than 3.
  • This is an anaerobic lacto-ferment, the leaf must stay submerged under brine and oil the entire time, with no leaf exposed to air. Exposed leaf molds. You will weight the leaf down; check it every few days and push any floaters back under.
  • Use the best green tea leaf you can get. Fresh or frozen tender green tea leaf (Asian-grocery or online) is ideal; good loose-leaf sencha, rehydrated, is the practical proxy. Avoid teabag dust and heavily flavored teas, they ferment muddy.
  • Salt is the safety control. Do not reduce it. The 2.5% brine concentration is what selects for lactic-acid bacteria and suppresses spoilage organisms; under-salting invites the wrong microbes.

Mise en place by phase 2 phases ▾

Phase 1, the leaf

  1. 100 g tender green tea leaves (fresh or frozen), or good loose-leaf sencha, rinsed if fresh; measured dry if using sencha — (dry sencha roughly triples in weight once rehydrated; see step 1)
  2. 1000 ml water, for steaming and the soak

Phase 2, the brine and pack

  1. 8 g kosher salt
  2. 6 g garlic, peeled, crushed flat optional — (2 cloves; optional traditional aromatic)
  3. 15 g lime juice, fresh
  4. 30 g neutral oil, to seal the surface

Execution

  1. 1
    Soften the leaf. If using fresh or frozen tender green tea leaf, rinse it and steam it over simmering water for 3 to 5 minutes until limp and dark, the way you would blanch a green. If using dry sencha, pour just-boiled water over the 100 g / about 1¼ cups loose leaf to cover, let it sit 10 minutes to rehydrate fully, then drain hard. Either way you want soft, pliable, fully hydrated leaf with no raw crunch left, and you want it well drained, surface water dilutes the brine.
  2. 2
    Rinse out the bitterness. Tip the softened leaf into a bowl of cool water, swish, and drain; repeat twice more with fresh water. Raw green tea leaf is aggressively astringent and tannic, and these rinses pull out the harshest catechins so the ferment starts from a cleaner base. Squeeze the rinsed leaf firmly in your hands or in a clean towel to wring out as much water as you can, the drier the leaf, the more concentrated and stable the ferment.
  3. 3
    Pound and salt. Transfer the squeezed leaf to a mortar, or chop it coarsely on a board, and pound or press it into a rough, cohesive paste, this bruises the cells and releases the juices the lactic bacteria will feed on. Work in the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt, the crushed 6 g / 2 cloves garlic if using, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp lime juice, kneading until the leaf feels slick and wet and the salt has fully dissolved into the released liquid. The mixture should be glossy and just barely weeping brine when squeezed.
  4. 4
    Pack airtight under pressure. Press the salted leaf hard into a small, scrupulously clean glass jar, tamping down with a spoon or muddler to drive out air pockets, until the leaf is compacted and its own brine rises to just cover the surface. The leaf MUST sit below the liquid line. If it does not weep enough brine to submerge itself, top with a teaspoon or two of 2.5% salt water (2.5 g salt per 100 ml). Lay a small water-filled zip bag or a clean weight directly on the surface to hold the leaf down.
  5. 5
    Cap with oil and seal. Pour the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil over the surface so it floats as a thin film, a second anaerobic barrier on top of the brine. Close the jar loosely (fermentation produces gas that must escape; a tight seal can build pressure) or use a fermentation lid. Label it with the date.
  6. 6
    Ferment cool and slow. Leave the jar at cool room temperature (18 to 22°C / 64 to 72°F) out of direct light for at least 3 weeks, ideally 4 to 6. For the first week, check daily: push any floating leaf back under the brine, and if you see a thin white film (kahm yeast, harmless) skim it; fuzzy or colored mold means air got in and the batch is lost. Over the weeks the leaf darkens, softens further, and turns from harsh-astringent to mellow, sour, and savory as the pH drops below 4.21 d 0 h
  7. 7
    Taste and store. From week 3 on, taste a pinch: it should read pleasantly sour and faintly bitter, soft, almost olive-like, with the raw tea harshness gone. When you like it, it is done. Keep the jar refrigerated, always with the leaf submerged under its brine and oil, where it holds for several months and only deepens. Drain and lightly squeeze a portion before dressing it into lahpet thoke.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion speed→a quick no-ferment tea-leaf base

    For a same-day salad without the wait, skip fermentation entirely: steam and triple-rinse the leaf as above, then dress it with extra lime, salt, and a little oil to mimic the sourness. It is brighter and lacks the funk-depth of true lahpet, but it makes a credible quick tea-leaf salad. Use it as the base for the no-ferment lahpet thoke variant.

  • Regional oil-cured Mandalay style→a richer, oilier finished lahpet

    After fermentation, drain the leaf and warm it gently with an extra 30 g / 2 tbsp oil and a little fried garlic until glossy, then cool. This oil-massaged style is closer to the rich, dark lahpet sold pre-dressed in Burmese shops and keeps even longer under its oil cap.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • tender green tea leaves → good loose-leaf sencha, rehydrated The practical proxy outside Myanmar. Sencha is already steamed, so it skips straight to the rehydrate-and-ferment stage. Avoid teabag dust and flavored teas.
  • kosher salt → any non-iodized salt, by weight Match the weight, not the volume (salts differ in crystal size). Iodized salt and anti-caking agents can inhibit the ferment; use pickling, kosher, or fine sea salt.

Notes

Why ferment tea at all. Across nearly all of the world tea is brewed and the leaf discarded, but in Myanmar and parts of the Shan hills the tender leaf is also a food: lacto-fermented into lahpet and eaten. Fermentation does to tea what it does to cabbage or olives, lactic-acid bacteria convert the leaf's sugars to acid, dropping the pH, preserving the leaf, and transforming a harsh, tannic, undrinkable-raw green into something soft, sour, savory, and faintly bitter. The harsh catechins that make raw green tea astringent mellow over the weeks; what is left is closer to a pickled green or a mild olive than to a cup of tea.

Why salt and submersion are non-negotiable. This is a true anaerobic lacto-ferment, identical in safety logic to sauerkraut or kimchi. The 2.5% salt concentration selects for salt-tolerant lactic-acid bacteria and suppresses spoilage microbes; keeping every scrap of leaf under brine and oil keeps oxygen-loving molds and yeasts out. Do not cut the salt and do not let leaf poke above the liquid. A white film on the surface is harmless kahm yeast and can be skimmed; fuzzy, green, black, or pink growth means the batch breathed and must be discarded.

Why rinse before fermenting. Raw green tea leaf carries a punishing tannin load, far more than you would ever brew into a cup, because you are about to eat the whole leaf rather than a dilute infusion. The triple cold-water rinse strips the most aggressive catechins up front so the finished lahpet reads pleasantly bitter rather than mouth-puckering. Skip it and even a well-fermented batch stays harsh.

The from-scratch caveat. Genuine lahpet starts from a specific fermentation-grade leaf, picked young for pickling and pressed for months in bamboo or pits, and it is not the same plant material sold as drinking tea. This home version, built from steamed drinking-grade green leaf and a faster jar ferment, is a faithful approximation rather than an exact reproduction; it gets you most of the way to the real thing with what is actually buyable outside Myanmar. If you can source true fermentation leaf, use it, and ferment longer.
