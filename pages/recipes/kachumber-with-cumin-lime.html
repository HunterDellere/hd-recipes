Kachumber with cumin and lime

North Indian chopped raw salad: even fine dice of cucumber, tomato, and red onion, dressed with toasted cumin bloomed in lime, chaat masala, green chili, and mint. Built as a cooling foil to rich curries; the seasoning is sour, smoky from cumin, and salt-forward, not sweet.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a side, not a main; build it to land at the same time as the curry, dal, or biryani it accompanies. Start kachumber within 25 minutes of plating; the dish loses crunch and reads watery past the 20-minute mark after the vegetables hit the dressing.
  • Toast and crack the cumin FIRST, then build the dressing on top of the cumin in a wide bowl. The cracked cumin needs 3 to 5 minutes of contact with the lime acid to bloom its terpenoid aromatics; that window runs in parallel with the vegetable dice work.
  • Cut every vegetable to even 6 to 8 mm dice (red onion one notch finer at 4 to 5 mm). The salad is structurally a composed bite; uneven dice produces a stochastic salad that reads as patches of tomato and patches of cucumber rather than an integrated whole.
  • Salt goes into the dressing, never directly on the vegetables. Salted dice draws cellular water and slumps into mush within 5 minutes; the salt-in-dressing approach keeps the dice audible to the spoon for the full eating window.

Phase 1, toasted cumin

  1. 4 g cumin seeds, whole

Phase 2, dressing

  1. 30 g lime juice, fresh
  2. 4 g fine sea salt
  3. 3 g chaat masala
  4. 6 g green chili (Indian or Thai bird), seeded if heat-sensitive, minced fine, 1 to 2 mm — (1 to 2 chilies; the heat is fresh-capsaicin, not chili-powder, and reads brighter)
  5. 1 g black pepper, freshly ground

Phase 3, vegetables

  1. 300 g Persian or English cucumber, unpeeled, seeded if using English (scrape with a teaspoon), cut to even 6 to 8 mm dice — (2 to 3 Persian, or 1 English; the dice size is the recipe ; uneven dice produces an uneven salad)
  2. 250 g firm ripe tomato (Roma or vine), cored, seeded (scoop the gel pockets out with a teaspoon), flesh diced 6 to 8 mm to match cucumber — (2 medium tomatoes; do not use cherry or heirloom ; the high-water gel destroys the salad. Roma is the canonical kachumber tomato.)
  3. 100 g red onion, peeled, halved, diced 4 to 5 mm (one notch finer than cucumber and tomato so the onion does not dominate the bite) — (1 small red onion; soaked in cold water 5 minutes if the onion is aggressive)

Phase 4, herbs

  1. 15 g cilantro, leaves and tender stems, rough chopped
  2. 8 g fresh mint leaves, leaves only, larger leaves torn

Execution

  1. 1
    Dry-toast the cumin. Set a small dry skillet (cast iron or stainless, no oil) over medium heat. Add the 4 g / 2 tsp whole cumin seeds and toast 60 to 90 seconds, shaking constantly, until the seeds darken from tan to medium-brown, smell aggressively toasted-cumin, and a few seeds begin to pop. Pull off heat the moment the aroma peaks; cumin scorches into bitterness within 5 to 10 seconds past peak. Slide the seeds onto a plate to stop the carryover and cool 1 minute. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  2. 2
    Crack (do not powder) the toasted cumin. Tip the cooled seeds into a mortar and pound 5 to 10 strokes with the pestle, just enough to crack each seed in half and split the volatile-oil-bearing ridges. Or pulse twice in a spice grinder. The target is cracked-not-ground: cracked seeds release the toasted-cumin aromatic into the dressing while still contributing a discrete bursting-aroma textural note in each bite, a fully ground powder dissolves the texture and dulls the top notes within minutes.
  3. 3
    Build the dressing in the bottom of a wide mixing bowl. Combine the 30 g / 2 tbsp lime juice, the 4 g / 1 tsp fine sea salt, the 3 g / 1 tsp chaat masala, the 6 g / 1 to 2 minced green chilies, the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper, and the cracked cumin from step 2. Whisk briefly with a fork. Let the dressing stand 3 to 5 minutes. The cracked cumin needs that minute of contact with the cool acidic liquid to bloom ; the lime acid and lime water together extract cumin aldehydes and terpenoids that pure dry seeds will not release on the vegetables alone. Chaat masala (which contains kala namak, the sulfurous black salt, plus amchur, the sour mango powder) similarly hydrates and integrates rather than sitting on top as a dry sprinkle. Blooming aromatics
  4. 4
    Cut the vegetables to even dice while the dressing rests. Cucumber: 6 to 8 mm dice, 300 g / 2 to 3 Persian. Tomato: core, halve, scoop the gel pockets out with a teaspoon (the gel is mostly water and pectin, leaving only the flesh keeps the salad firm rather than weeping), then dice the flesh 6 to 8 mm to match the cucumber, 250 g / 2 medium. Red onion: 100 g / 1 small, peeled and diced 4 to 5 mm, finer than the other two so its punch reads as background rather than foreground. The geometric uniformity is the point; kachumber is engineered as a single composed bite, every spoonful delivering cucumber + tomato + onion in roughly equal mass.
  5. 5
    Optional onion soak. If the 100 g / 1 small diced red onion smells aggressive when cut (the allyl-propyl disulfide level in old or warm-storage onions can be unpleasantly raw), tip the diced onion into a small bowl of cold water for 5 minutes, then drain and pat dry on a paper towel. The cold water leaches a portion of the volatile sulfur compounds without destroying the textural crunch. Skip this step with a fresh-and-mild onion ; over-soaked onion goes flavorless.
  6. 6
    Add the diced cucumber, tomato, and red onion to the bowl with the dressing. Add the 15 g / ½ cup chopped cilantro and the 8 g / ¼ cup torn mint. Fold gently with a large spoon, just enough to coat every dice surface ; 4 to 6 turns of the spoon, no more. Aggressive tossing crushes the tomato gel-free flesh and bruises the herbs; kachumber is finished by folding, not tossing.
  7. 7
    Taste a spoonful that contains all three vegetables. The dressing should read sharply sour from the lime, sulfurous-saline from the chaat masala (the kala namak hits as a faint sulfur-egg note in the throat), warmly smoky from the toasted cumin, and capsaicin-hot from the green chili. If flat, add a 1 g / pinch of salt and a 5 g / 1 tsp splash of lime; if heat is missing, more minced chili (do not add chili powder, which is a different flavor profile and reads dusty in a raw salad). Serve within 15 minutes of mixing. Kachumber is engineered for the immediate ; once tomato cell water and onion cell water leach into the dressing past the 20-minute mark, the salt concentration drops and the dish reads watery and dilute. The leftover-fridge version of kachumber is a different, lesser dish.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→kachumbari (East African Indian diaspora)

    Drop the cumin seeds, drop the chaat masala, drop the mint. Use lemon juice instead of lime, double the cilantro, and add a pinch of dried red chili flakes for heat instead of fresh green chili. The dish becomes the kachumbari that goes alongside Kenyan and Tanzanian nyama choma ; same chopped-vegetable backbone, simpler salt-and-citrus seasoning that reflects the Gujarati merchant communities who carried the dish from western India to East Africa in the 19th century.

  • Regional cuisine→cachumber (Persian-Pakistani fattoush adjacent)

    Add 100 g / 1 cup torn fresh herbs (parsley, dill, more mint) and a 30 g / 2 tbsp drizzle of pomegranate molasses to the dressing. Skip the chaat masala. The dish drifts toward the Mughlai-Persian raw-vegetable salads served with kebab platters in Pakistan and Iran; the pomegranate molasses replaces the chaat masala as the secondary acid.

  • Protein protein→kachumber raita (yogurt-bound)

    Fold the finished kachumber into 250 g / 1 cup whisked Greek yogurt thinned with 30 g / 2 tbsp water and an additional 2 g / ½ tsp salt. The dish becomes a chunky raita ; the kachumber dressing acts as the seasoning vehicle for the yogurt, and the cooling effect doubles. Serve alongside biryani or as a dipping companion to roti.

  • Occasion season→winter kachumber (radish and mooli)

    Replace half the cucumber (150 g) with 100 g / 1 small daikon (mooli), peeled and diced 6 mm, plus 50 g / 1 small bunch red radishes, sliced 2 mm. The dish gains a cleaner peppery-mineral note from the radish and stays seasonal in the months when cucumber is hothouse and dull.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • chaat masala → a pinch of black salt (kala namak) plus 2 g / ½ tsp amchur (dried mango powder) plus an extra 1 g / pinch toasted cumin These are the three structural components of chaat masala; the spice blend also typically contains ginger powder, ajwain, asafoetida, and ground chili, but the sulfur-sour-cumin axis carries the dish. The substitute reads close to chaat masala but slightly less complex.
  • green chili → serrano (1 small) or jalapeño (1 medium, seeded) Indian green chilies (hari mirch) are typically smaller and more aggressively hot than jalapeño, closer to a serrano in heat (Scoville 30,000 to 50,000). Thai bird chili is also acceptable and has a similar heat profile. Avoid jalapeño that has been refrigerated for weeks; the capsaicin reads muddier than fresh.
  • lime → lemon juice (use the same volume) Lemon shifts the acid profile from lime-citral aromatic to lemon-citral aromatic; the dish reads slightly less aggressively sour and a little more floral. Acceptable; many North Indian households actually use lemon over lime, regional preference is split.
  • tomato → firm peach or green mango (cut to the same dice) In summer mango season, North Indian cooks sometimes substitute unripe green mango for tomato; the salad becomes more acid-forward and loses the tomato glutamate. Different dish but recognizably from the same family. Skip if the substitution is just a peach-out-of-season hack.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → chaat masala make it yourselfstub Chaat masala is a toast-and-grind blend of cumin, coriander, kala namak, amchur, ajwain, ginger powder, and chili ; the homemade version, made in 15 minutes from whole spices toasted and ground fresh, has a vivid sulfur-sour top note that store-bought tins (which lose the volatile aromatics within 6 months of grinding) cannot match.

Notes

The thesis is geometric uniformity plus a bloomed acid-forward dressing. Kachumber is not a "chopped salad" in the loose Western sense; the discipline of even 6 to 8 mm dice on the cucumber and tomato (with red onion one notch finer at 4 to 5 mm) is what makes the spoon deliver a composed multi-vegetable bite every single time, rather than a stochastic mouthful of tomato-then-cucumber-then-onion in arbitrary ratios. Indian home cooks talk about kachumber as a salad whose entire character lives in the cut; the seasoning is simple enough that the texture has nowhere to hide.

Why toast and crack the cumin rather than use ground cumin from the jar. Toasted whole cumin and ground cumin are functionally different ingredients (see content/ingredients/cumin-seeds.md). Toasting volatilizes and concentrates the cuminaldehyde and other terpenoid aromatics that define cumin, and cracking (rather than grinding) the toasted seeds preserves a discrete bursting-aroma textural note in each bite while still letting the dressing extract the cumin oil into the lime acid. Pre-ground store-cumin in a kachumber dressing reads dusty and one-dimensional; the blooming step does not work because the volatile aromatics have already escaped the powder.

Why chaat masala specifically. Chaat masala carries kala namak (Himalayan black salt, mineral and sulfurous from naturally occurring iron sulfides and hydrogen sulfide), amchur (dried unripe mango powder, citric and malic acid forward), and additional toasted cumin and ground coriander. The kala namak alone is the structural identity ; without its faint sulfur-egg note, the dish is a vegetable salad with cumin and lime, recognizably good but not recognizably kachumber. A pinch of pure kala namak plus amchur plus extra cumin (the substitution above) recovers the axis.

The umami-and-acid axis. The umami source is the seeded tomato flesh itself (free glutamate from ripening), bolstered by the sulfurous chaat masala. The finishing acid is lime-plus-amchur, layered with the toasted-cumin's faint bitter top note as a counterweight. There is no oil in classical kachumber; the dressing is dry-spice-and-lime, not vinaigrette, and adding olive oil would push the dish toward fattoush or panzanella territory and destroy the kachumber identity.

Why no oil. Indian raw-vegetable salads (kachumber, kachumbari, koshimbir, the various chopped salads served alongside thali) are oil-free by tradition because they share the table with curries, dals, and rice already cooked in ghee or oil; the salad's job is to provide the cooling, fat-free, acid-forward palate reset that the oily centerpieces need. Adding oil to a kachumber undermines the salad's structural function on the plate, which is why even the East African kachumbari descendants, where the dish is sometimes served alone, hold the line on no oil.

Salt timing. The salt goes into the dressing, not on the diced vegetables. Salting the cucumber and tomato dice directly draws cellular water out via osmosis within 3 to 5 minutes, slumping the dice into a wet mush by the time the salad reaches the table. Letting the salt dissolve into the lime first, and folding the vegetables in only at serving, keeps the dice firm and the salad audible to the spoon for the 15-minute eating window. The same logic governs the rule of mixing kachumber within minutes of serving rather than holding it in the fridge.

How kachumber is served. Kachumber is a side, not a main. The canonical North Indian thali pairs it with biryani, dal makhani, raita, papad, and a pickle (achar); the kachumber is the cooling raw element in a meal otherwise dense with fat and slow-cooked spice. Pakistani Punjabi tables do the same with nihari, haleem, or chapli kebab. The dish does not stand alone as a Western "side salad"; it is engineered as a counterweight to a richer main, and its proportions (small portion, aggressive seasoning) reflect that role.
