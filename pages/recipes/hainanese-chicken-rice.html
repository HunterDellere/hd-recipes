Hainanese chicken rice

Singaporean-Malaysian national dish. A whole chicken poached in aromatic stock at sub-simmer temperatures for tender, gelatinous skin and silky-rare meat near the bone, served at room temperature with rice cooked in chicken fat and stock, a sharp ginger-scallion oil, a fermented chili-garlic-lime sauce, and a small bowl of the poaching broth on the side. Defined by restraint and technique; almost no spice load, all on the structure.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Use a whole chicken, not parts. The dish depends on the whole-bird poach to extract collagen from the carcass and flavor the broth that then cooks the rice. Parts produce a thinner broth, a less-flavored rice, and the canonical presentation (chopped whole chicken on a platter) is structurally impossible.
  • The ice bath after the poach is non-negotiable. The shock-chill firms the gelatin in the skin and stops the cook precisely; without it the chicken keeps cooking on residual heat and the textural signature (silky, almost translucent skin with a thin layer of clear gel beneath) is lost.
  • Render the chicken fat (from the cavity fat and the skin trim) before starting the rice. The rice is cooked in chicken fat plus toasted garlic, not vegetable oil; the fat is what gives the rice its characteristic richness and color.
  • Make the ginger-scallion sauce and the chili sauce before the chicken finishes. Both want 30 minutes for flavors to integrate; the chili sauce particularly benefits from a 1-hour rest.

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, chicken poach

  1. 1800 g whole chicken, free-range if possible, patted dry, cavity fat reserved separately for the rice (about 30 g)
  2. 3500 ml water
  3. 60 g ginger, sliced into thick coins, bruised with the back of a knife — (about an 8 cm / 3 inch knob)
  4. 60 g scallion, whole, bruised, knotted — (6 to 7 scallions)
  5. 3 each pandan leaves, knotted — (optional; the canonical Hainanese addition for floral-vanilla-grassy aromatic depth in both broth and rice)
  6. 15 g kosher salt
  7. 2000 ml ice bath water

Phase 2, chicken rice

  1. 450 g jasmine rice, rinsed until water runs clear
  2. 30 g reserved chicken fat (from the cavity)
  3. 20 g garlic, minced fine — (about 5 cloves)
  4. 15 g ginger, minced fine — (2 cm / ¾ inch knob)
  5. 600 ml reserved chicken poaching broth
  6. 1 each pandan leaf, knottedoptional — (reserved from the poach; floral aromatic in the rice)
  7. 3 g kosher salt

Phase 3, ginger-scallion sauce

  1. 40 g ginger, fine-grated on a microplane
  2. 50 g scallion, white and pale green parts only, sliced 2 mm thin — (5 to 6 scallions)
  3. 3 g fine sea salt
  4. 90 g neutral oil (peanut or sunflower)

Phase 4, chili sauce

  1. 60 g fresh red Holland or Fresno chili, stems pulled, rough chopped, seeds in for heat — (about 4 to 5 chilies; or use bird-eye and halve for a sharper Singaporean-canonical heat)
  2. 20 g garlic — (5 cloves)
  3. 15 g fresh ginger — (2 cm / ¾ inch knob)
  4. 30 g lime juice, fresh
  5. 15 g rice vinegar
  6. 8 g granulated sugar
  7. 2 g fine sea salt
  8. 30 g reserved chicken poaching broth

Phase 5, serving

  1. 200 g cucumber, sliced, sliced into 3 mm rounds — (1 English cucumber; the cooling foil, plated under the chopped chicken)
  2. 20 g cilantro sprigs
  3. 30 g dark soy sauce

Execution

  1. 1
    Set up the poach. Place the 1800 g whole chicken in a stockpot just large enough to hold it. Add the 3500 ml / 14 cups water, 60 g bruised ginger, 60 g bruised scallion, 3 knotted pandan leaves (reserve one for the rice in step 5), and 15 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt. The chicken should be fully submerged with 3 to 5 cm clearance; add water if needed.
  2. 2
    Bring the pot slowly to 85°C / 185°F over medium-low heat, about 20 minutes. The bubble activity should be a barely-perceptible tremor at the surface; never a rolling boil. Once at temperature, reduce heat to the lowest setting to hold the water between 80°C and 85°C (use an instant-read thermometer in the water, not against the chicken). Cover loosely (lid cracked) and hold for 35 to 40 minutes. The chicken is done when the deepest part of the thigh reads 70°C / 158°F (insert thermometer between leg and breast). Total active poach time at temperature: about 35 to 40 minutes for a 1800 g bird.
  3. 3
    Lift the chicken from the broth with two large spoons or by the cavity scallions; transfer immediately to the prepared ice bath. Submerge fully; the chicken will firm up and the skin will tighten over 10 minutes. This is the structural step that produces the canonical gelatinous, almost-translucent skin texture; without it the cook continues on residual heat and the chicken texture is lost. Reserve the poaching broth, keep at a low simmer to maintain warmth and concentration.
  4. 4
    While the chicken cools, render the chicken fat. Place the 30 g reserved cavity fat in a small saucepan over medium-low heat. As the fat melts (about 5 minutes), it releases clear golden chicken fat (schmaltz) and leaves crisp brown solids. Strain the fat into a clean container; you should have about 2 tbsp. Reserve for the rice.
  5. 5
    Build the rice. Heat the 30 g rendered chicken fat in a heavy 3-quart saucepan over medium-low heat. Add the 20 g minced garlic and 15 g minced ginger; sweat 3 to 4 minutes until fragrant and just turning pale gold, do not brown. Add the 450 g rinsed jasmine rice; toast in the fat 90 seconds, stirring constantly, until the grains turn from translucent-soaked to opaque-coated.
  6. 6
    Add 600 ml / 2½ cups of the warm reserved chicken broth (skim the surface fat layer back into the rice pot for extra richness), the reserved 1 knotted pandan leaf, and 3 g / ½ tsp kosher salt to the rice. Bring to a vigorous simmer over medium-high heat; once boiling, reduce heat to lowest setting, cover tightly, and cook 15 minutes without lifting the lid. Pull from heat and rest, still covered, another 10 minutes. Fluff with a fork before serving.
  7. 7
    Build the ginger-scallion sauce. Combine the 40 g grated ginger, 50 g sliced scallion, and 3 g / ½ tsp salt in a heatproof bowl. Heat the 90 g / 6 tbsp neutral oil in a small saucepan to 175°C / 350°F (about 80 seconds over medium-high; the oil should shimmer and a piece of scallion dropped in sizzles aggressively). Pour the hot oil directly over the ginger-scallion-salt; it will sizzle hard and bloom the aromatics. Stir to combine. The sauce is ready immediately but improves in 30 minutes as the salt draws moisture from the scallion and integrates with the bloomed oil.
  8. 8
    Build the chili sauce. Blend the 60 g chili, 20 g garlic, 15 g ginger, 30 g / 2 tbsp lime juice, 15 g / 1 tbsp rice vinegar, 8 g / 2 tsp sugar, 2 g / scant ½ tsp salt, and 30 g / 2 tbsp warm chicken broth to a smooth bright-red sauce in a blender or with an immersion blender. Texture target is hot-sauce smooth, not chunky. Rest 30 minutes for flavors to integrate; the chili heat blooms over the first hour at room temperature.
  9. 9
    Pull the chicken from the ice bath after at least 30 minutes; pat dry with paper towels. For canonical presentation, brush the entire skin with a thin coat of sesame oil for gloss (optional; about 1 tsp). Chop the chicken Cantonese-style: split down the breastbone with a cleaver, separate legs, then chop each leg quarter and each breast half crosswise into 2 cm wide strips through the bone (about 5 to 6 strips per breast half, 4 to 5 per leg). Reassemble into the chicken silhouette on a platter, atop sliced cucumber rounds, and scatter cilantro sprigs over.
  10. 10
    Plate. Each diner gets a mound of chicken rice on the plate, a portion of chopped chicken alongside, a small ramekin each of ginger-scallion sauce, chili sauce, and dark soy sauce, and a small bowl of the warm chicken broth on the side (garnished with extra scallion). Eat with chopsticks and spoon. The canonical bite is: a small piece of chicken dipped first into the ginger-scallion oil, then into the chili sauce, eaten with a forkful of fragrant rice and a spoon of broth between.
Safety
  • Pulling chicken at 71°C / 160°F internal (just past the carryover threshold to a final 165°F) Pulled at 160°F internal, then rested for 3 minutes covered, the meat carries to roughly 165°F (USDA published-safe instantaneous target). Resting under foil drives the carryover; pulling at 165°F internal directly produces dry, chalky chicken. Vulnerable diners (pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, very old) should be served chicken cooked to 165°F internal with no carryover credit. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→Malaysian-style soy chicken (substitute the poach with a soy-braise)

    Skip the white-poach. Instead braise the whole chicken in 1500 ml chicken stock plus 200 g dark soy sauce, 100 g light soy, 80 g rock sugar, 5 star anise, 1 cinnamon stick, 6 g five-spice, and the aromatics from the white-poach. Bring to a bare simmer, hold 30 minutes, flip, hold 20 more. The chicken comes out mahogany-glazed instead of pale; serve the same way over chicken rice with the same three sauces. This is the version sold alongside Hainanese chicken at most Malaysian hawker stalls.

  • Regional cuisine→roast chicken rice (Singaporean alt)

    Substitute a roasted chicken for the poached. Roast a 1800 g chicken at 220°C / 425°F for 50 to 60 minutes until 75°C / 165°F at the thigh and golden-crisp skin. Use store-bought chicken stock for the rice in place of the missing poaching liquid. The roast version is the third canonical Singaporean preparation (alongside white poach and soy braise); chop the same way and serve with the same sauces.

  • Occasion occasion→broth-only chicken rice for cold days

    Serve the chicken hot off the poach (skip the ice bath). The skin will be looser and the meat hotter; the dish loses its room-temperature canonical character but gains warmth, useful in cold weather. Bump the chili sauce up by 50 percent to compensate for the dulled palate effect of hot food.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole chicken (Cornish cross supermarket bird) → free-range kampong or heritage chicken The firmer-fleshed free-range bird is the canonical Hainanese chicken; supermarket Cornish cross runs softer and the textural signature is slightly off. If only Cornish cross is available, drop the poach temperature to 78°C / 172°F for a slower, gentler cook that better preserves what texture the soft-fleshed bird has.
  • jasmine rice → basmati rice Functional substitute; the dish reads slightly drier and more aromatic-Indian-leaning. Cooks identically in the chicken-fat-and-broth method.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → sambal oelek (or sambal belacan), or any default chili paste make it yourself For diners who want a side of pure chili paste in addition to the lime-garlic chili sauce, fresh-made sambal oelek is dramatically brighter than jarred. Keeps two weeks.

Notes

Why sub-simmer poach. The textural identity of Hainanese chicken is silky, almost translucent skin with a thin layer of clear gelatin beneath, and meat that reads juicy and slightly translucent at the bone. This texture only happens at sub-simmer temperatures (78 to 85°C / 172 to 185°F), where the collagen in the skin partially gelatinizes without the muscle fibers seizing into tight, dry strands. A rolling boil (100°C / 212°F) overshoots fast, the skin tears and the meat goes stringy. The 35-to-40-minute hold at temperature for an 1800 g bird brings the deepest meat to 70°C / 158°F (just-cooked safe) while keeping the skin perfectly intact.

Why the ice bath. The skin contains collagen that turns to gelatin during the poach; without an immediate cold shock, the residual heat in the bird continues to break down the gelatin and the skin softens into the canonical loose-and-greasy supermarket-rotisserie texture. The 30-minute submerge in ice water firms the gelatin into the characteristic clear gel layer just beneath the skin, and the skin itself tightens to a slightly translucent, almost lacquered surface. Skipping the ice bath is the single most common reason home-cooked Hainanese chicken misses the textural mark.

Why rice cooked in chicken fat and broth. The rice is half the dish. Plain jasmine rice cooked in water with the chicken on the side is a different dish entirely; the rice has to carry the chicken-fat richness and the aromatic broth depth, otherwise the whole composition lacks center. The garlic-and-ginger toast in chicken fat at the start (the pilaf method) coats each grain in flavored oil before the broth absorption; this is what produces the slightly-glossy, individual-grained, deeply-fragrant rice that defines the dish.

Why three sauces. Each sauce hits a different part of the palate. The ginger-scallion sauce is the umami-aromatic backbone (works with the chicken and the rice). The chili sauce is the bright acid-heat (works with the chicken specifically). The dark soy sauce is the deep salt-front (drizzled over the chicken for a saltier bite). Diners build each bite from these three condiments; one or two of the three is not the canonical eating experience. The three sauces are not optional; they are the structural seasoning of the dish, which is otherwise almost unsalted.

Why broth on the side. The poaching broth, lightly seasoned and full of dissolved chicken collagen, is the palate-cleansing element between bites of the rich chicken-and-rice. Each spoonful resets the mouth, the broth is light enough to refresh and just-flavored-enough to feel like part of the dish. A bowl of chicken rice without the broth feels heavy and one-noted; with the broth it is balanced and complete.

Why served at room temperature. The chicken is served at the temperature it comes to after the ice bath (cooled to about 15 to 18°C / 60 to 65°F). The rice is served hot; the broth is served hot; the chicken is intentionally cool. The temperature contrast is structural to the eating experience, and the cool chicken better preserves the gelatinous skin texture that the dish is built around. Serving the chicken hot (microwaved, or pulled directly from the poach without the ice bath) defeats the entire textural premise.

Storage. The poaching broth holds 4 days in the fridge and freezes 3 months; this is some of the best home-cook chicken stock you will ever make. The chopped chicken holds 2 days; reheat by submerging in warm (not hot) broth for 2 minutes. The rice is best fresh; reheats in a covered pan with a splash of broth.

Sources
  • Adapted from Singaporean and Malaysian hawker tradition (Wenchang chicken / Kainan chicken) — The dish traces to Hainan Island in southern China, brought to Singapore and Malaysia by 19th- and 20th-century Hainanese migrants who adapted it to local palates and ingredient availability. The Singaporean version (Tian Tian, Boon Tong Kee, Maxwell hawker stalls) is the canonical export version; the Malaysian version is similar with regional accent differences. The chicken is served at room temperature (not hot), the rice is served hot, and the three sauces (ginger-scallion, chili, dark soy) are non-negotiable structural elements.
