Okaka (soy-and-sesame bonito flake mix)

Katsuobushi tossed with soy sauce, mirin, toasted sesame oil, and a small pinch of sugar; left to marinate 10 minutes until the flakes absorb the seasoning into a damp-savory clump. The two-minute onigiri filling, rice topping, and pantry staple. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 6 items ▾
  1. 30 g katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes, hanakatsuo)
  2. 15 ml light soy sauce
  3. 8 ml mirin (hon-mirin)
  4. 3 ml toasted sesame oil optional
  5. 2 g granulated sugar
  6. 6 g toasted white sesame seeds optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: in a small bowl, whisk together the 15 ml soy sauce, 8 ml mirin, 2 g sugar, and 3 ml toasted sesame oil until the sugar dissolves.
  2. 2
    Stub: add the 30 g katsuobushi flakes to the bowl. Toss with chopsticks or a fork until the flakes are evenly coated and have absorbed the liquid; the texture should shift from dry-and-papery to damp-and-clumping. Add the 6 g sesame seeds if using; toss to combine.
  3. 3
    Stub: let stand 10 minutes for the flakes to fully absorb the seasoning. Use immediately as an onigiri filling (1 tbsp per onigiri) or rice topping (1 to 2 tsp per bowl), or transfer to a small airtight container and refrigerate up to 5 days.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the chemistry (the soy-mirin-sugar liquid is hygroscopic enough to be absorbed by the dried katsuobushi, restoring some of the moisture lost during the original drying-and-fermentation process and creating an integrated savory paste-like clump), the role in Japanese home cooking (every Japanese home pantry has a quick-okaka technique because the basic katsuobushi-soy-sugar combination is the starting point for hundreds of variations), the variation spectrum (with grated daikon for a brighter version; with finely chopped umeboshi for sour-salty contrast; with shaved bonito instead of flakes for a more textured product; with chili oil for a spicy hybrid), the canonical eating contexts (the most common onigiri filling in Japanese convenience stores after umeboshi and salmon; folded into rice for ochazuke; layered between rice and grilled fish in bento boxes; mixed with butter for an unconventional but delicious toast topping), the storage limits (5 days refrigerated; the moisture content makes it more perishable than dry katsuobushi flakes), and the relation to furikake (okaka is wetter and clumpier; furikake is drier and more sprinkleable; the same ingredients can be cooked further to bridge between the two).
