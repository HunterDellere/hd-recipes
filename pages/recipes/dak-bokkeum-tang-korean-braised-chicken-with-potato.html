Dak-bokkeum-tang (Korean braised chicken with potato)

The Korean spicy braised chicken stew, bone-in thighs simmered with waxy potato and carrot in a brick-red sauce built from gochugaru and gochujang, deepened with a spoon of doenjang for funk and a grated Asian pear for enzymatic tenderness and a sweet backnote. Garlic-heavy in the Korean way, the sauce reduces and clings; the potato thickens it as it softens. A finish of toasted sesame oil and perilla lands the dish. Sweet-hot, savory, and deeply fermented.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Optional but recommended: a quick parboil of the chicken. Drop the thighs into boiling water 2 minutes, then drain and rinse. This Korean technique (called called for in many home versions of dak-bokkeum-tang) removes surface scum and excess fat so the finished braise is clean rather than greasy. It is separate from a brine; do not do both, the parboil already seasons the surface lightly.
  • If you would rather brine than parboil: dissolve 30 g / 2 tbsp kosher salt in 1 L / 4 cups cold water, soak the thighs 30 to 45 minutes, pat dry, and skip the parboil. Both improve the meat; the parboil makes a cleaner sauce, the brine makes juicier meat. With either, taste before adding the optional finishing salt, since the gochujang and doenjang are salty.
  • Make the sauce base (the yangnyeom) before you start cooking so it is ready to add; grate the Asian pear last so it does not brown.
  • Steam Korean short-grain rice to finish with the braise; this dish is eaten with rice and banchan, not over noodles.

Phase 1, the chicken

  1. 1000 g bone-in skin-on chicken thighs, parboiled 2 min and rinsed, or brined and dried (see before_you_start) — (about 6 thighs; cut crosswise through the bone into 2 pieces if you want the classic bite-size Korean cut, or leave whole)

  6. 15 g ginger, grated — (a 3 cm knob)

Execution

  1. 1
    If you have not already, parboil the 1000 g chicken: drop the thighs into a pot of boiling water for 2 minutes, then drain and rinse off the gray scum under cool water. This is the step that keeps the finished sauce clean rather than greasy. Skip it if you brined instead.
  2. 2
    Mix the yangnyeom. In a bowl whisk the 24 g / 3 tbsp gochugaru, 45 g / 3 tbsp gochujang, 15 g / 1 tbsp doenjang, 30 g / 2 tbsp light soy, 30 g minced garlic, 15 g grated ginger, 80 g grated Asian pear, and 10 g / 2½ tsp sugar into a loose red paste. This is the seasoning core of the dish.
  3. 3
    Heat the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a wide heavy pot over medium. Add the yangnyeom and bloom it, stirring constantly, 2 minutes, until the gochugaru darkens slightly and the paste smells toasty rather than raw. Blooming the chili in oil deepens the color and rounds the raw edge off the gochujang and doenjang. Do not scorch it.
  4. 4
    Add the parboiled chicken and turn to coat in the paste, 1 minute. Pour in the 500 ml / 2 cups water or stock and scrape the base smooth. Add the 350 g potato, 120 g carrot, and 150 g onion wedges. Bring to a simmer, cover, and reduce to medium-low. Braise 25 minutes, until the potato is nearly tender and the chicken reads 75°C / 165°F at the bone.
  5. 5
    Uncover and raise the heat to medium-high. Simmer hard 10 to 12 minutes to reduce the sauce by roughly half, until it turns glossy, brick-red, and clings to the chicken and potato. Stir gently now and then so the bottom does not catch; the potato edges will fray and thicken the sauce. Add the 50 g scallion batons in the last 3 minutes so they soften but keep some bite.
  6. 6
    Pull off the heat. Stir the 8 g / 1½ tsp toasted sesame oil through the sauce. Taste: it should read sweet-hot up front, deeply savory and fermented underneath, with the garlic forward. If flat, a splash more soy; if too sharp or hot, a pinch more sugar; it rarely needs salt with this much gochujang and doenjang.
  7. 7
    Spoon into shallow bowls, scatter the 5 g toasted sesame seeds and the perilla ribbons across (if using), and serve with steamed short-grain rice and whatever banchan you have. The sauce is meant to be spooned over the rice between bites of chicken.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture vegetable→kabocha or sweet potato

    Swap the waxy potato for 350 g peeled kabocha or sweet potato in 4 cm chunks. Both are sweeter, so cut the added sugar to 5 g, and add them 8 minutes into the covered braise rather than the start so they do not collapse. The sweetness plays beautifully against the gochugaru heat.

  • Regional vegetable→with rice cakes (tteok), dak-galbi lean

    In the last 8 minutes add 200 g cylindrical or sliced rice cakes (tteok), pre-soaked if dried, and a handful of shredded cabbage. The tteok soak up the sauce and turn chewy, pushing the dish toward dak-galbi territory. Add 100 ml more water since the tteok drink liquid.

  • Heat heat→milder, kid-friendly

    Cut the gochugaru to 12 g and the gochujang to 30 g, and add 15 g more sugar or a tablespoon of rice syrup. The sauce keeps its color and funk but loses most of the burn. Korean families routinely make a milder pot for children.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • Asian pear → Bosc pear or a few tablespoons of grated onion plus a pinch of sugar Asian pear has the enzymes and clean sweetness; a ripe Bosc is the closest stand-in. Grated onion alone gives the enzymatic tenderizing without the perfume. Use the same weight.
  • doenjang (fermented soybean paste) → red (aka) miso Doenjang is funkier and earthier than miso, the way aged blue cheese differs from young brie. Red miso is the closest substitute; use the same weight and accept a slightly milder, less funky result.
  • gochugaru (Korean red chili flakes) → there is no good substitute Gochugaru is fruity, brick-red, and only moderately hot. Cayenne or generic red pepper flakes will overheat and underflavor the dish badly. If you must, use Aleppo pepper, which is closest in fruitiness, and expect a different result.
  • granulated sugar → rice syrup (jocheong) or honey Rice syrup is the traditional sweetener and adds gloss; honey works too. Use about 15 g of either in place of the 10 g sugar, since they are less sweet by weight.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → gochujang (fermented red chili paste) make it yourselfstub A long-fermented homemade gochujang has a depth and balance of funk, sweetness, and slow heat that mass-market tubs flatten; it changes the entire backbone of the braise.
  • → water or unsalted chicken stock make it yourself A gelatin-rich homemade stock bodies the braise and lets you control the salt against the gochujang and doenjang, both of which are salty.

Notes

Why parboil the chicken. Dropping the thighs in boiling water for 2 minutes coagulates and releases the surface proteins and blood that would otherwise scum into the braise, and renders off a little of the subcutaneous fat. The result is a cleaner, brighter-tasting sauce rather than a greasy one. It is a widespread Korean home technique for braised chicken and is the single biggest lever on how clean the finished pot tastes. Brining instead gives juicier meat but a slightly richer sauce; pick one.

Why both gochugaru and gochujang. They do different jobs. Gochugaru (the dried flakes) provides the brick-red color and a clean, fruity, direct heat. Gochujang (the fermented paste) provides body, a slow-building sweetness, and the deep fermented funk that gochugaru alone cannot. The ratio between them is the heat-and-depth dial: more gochugaru for a brighter, hotter sauce, more gochujang for a sweeter, deeper, milder one.

Why doenjang in a chili braise. A single spoon of doenjang, the Korean fermented soybean paste, adds a savory, earthy funk underneath the chili that you notice mostly by its absence. It is less sweet and more aggressively fermented than Japanese miso, and it pulls the whole sauce toward savory depth rather than letting the gochujang sweetness dominate. This is a common home refinement that separates a flat sweet-spicy sauce from a layered one.

Why the grated pear. Asian pear contains proteolytic enzymes that gently tenderize the chicken, and it adds a clean, floral sweetness without the heaviness of more sugar. Grated fine with its juice, it dissolves into the sauce invisibly. It is the same logic Korean cooks use in bulgogi and galbi marinades; here it does double duty as tenderizer and sweetener.

Why the potato thickens the sauce. Waxy potato holds its shape through most of the braise, then its edges begin to fray and shed starch during the hard uncovered reduction, thickening the sauce so it clings to the chicken without any added starch. This is why the dish is a braise that becomes a clinging stew rather than a soup. A floury baking potato would dissolve too much and turn the sauce gluey; a waxy potato gives just enough.

Why the sesame oil goes in last. Toasted sesame oil is a finishing oil; its aroma is volatile and it turns bitter if cooked. Stirring it in off the heat at the end perfumes the sauce without scorching it. The same is true across Korean cooking, where toasted sesame oil and seeds are almost always finishers, not cooking fats.
