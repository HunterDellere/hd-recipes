Maple-cider vinaigrette

Apple cider vinegar, dark maple syrup, whole-grain mustard, microplaned shallot, salt, and olive oil whisked into a tight emulsion. The autumn American dressing for grain salads, roasted-vegetable plates, and chicken-and-greens lunches. Doubles or triples cleanly and keeps a week refrigerated.

Mise en place by phase 2 phases ▾

Acid phase

  1. 45 g apple cider vinegar, unfiltered
  2. 22 g maple syrup, Grade A dark / robust
  3. 18 g whole-grain mustard
  4. 22 g shallot, microplaned, peeled, microplaned to a paste — (1 medium shallot; the raw mucilage adds emulsifier body and a faint allium edge)
  5. 3 g kosher salt
  6. 0.5 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Oil phase

  1. 120 g extra-virgin olive oil

Execution

  1. 1
    In a small bowl or a wide-mouth jar, combine the 45 g / 3 tbsp apple cider vinegar, the 22 g / 1½ tbsp maple syrup, the 18 g / 1 tbsp whole-grain mustard, the 22 g / 1 medium microplaned shallot, the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt, and the 0.5 g / ¼ tsp cracked black pepper. Whisk vigorously for 15 to 20 seconds; the salt fully dissolves, the mustard distributes evenly, and the shallot mucilage starts to thicken the mixture. Salt always goes into the acid phase first, never into the oil phase; salt does not dissolve in oil and will read as gritty hot spots if added at the end. Vinaigrette emulsion
  2. 2
    While whisking constantly with one hand, stream in the 120 g / ½ cup + 1 tsp olive oil with the other in a thin steady drizzle (5 to 10 seconds for the full pour, not faster). The slow oil addition gives the mustard mucilage time to coat each newly-introduced droplet before the next one arrives; the vigorous whisking shears the oil into smaller droplets that stay suspended longer. The vinaigrette tightens visibly as the emulsion forms; by the time the last of the oil is in, the dressing should be opaque-amber and ribbon off the whisk. Vinaigrette emulsion
  3. 3
    Whisk for an additional 20 to 30 seconds after the last of the oil; the dressing should reach a tight emulsion, opaque amber, with the consistency of cold heavy cream. Taste; the dressing should read sweet-acid-mustard in that order, fully salted; if it tastes flat, add another 1 g / ¼ tsp kosher salt; if it tastes harsh-acidic, a tiny pinch more maple; if it tastes too sweet, a splash more vinegar. The balance is dish-dependent (sharper for raw greens, sweeter for roasted vegetables); calibrate to the salad it will dress.
  4. 4
    Use immediately or transfer to a sealed jar and refrigerate. The vinaigrette holds tight emulsion for 3 to 4 hours at room temperature; refrigerated, the emulsion separates over 24 to 48 hours and the dressing needs a vigorous reshake before each use. The flavor improves over the first 24 hours as the shallot mucilage continues to integrate; refrigerated, the dressing keeps a full week. If the emulsion breaks badly during storage (visible oil layer that does not reincorporate with shaking), transfer to a small bowl, add another teaspoon of mustard plus a tablespoon of warm water, and whisk into the broken dressing in dribbles; the emulsion rebuilds. Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→french-leaning with sherry vinegar and dijon

    Replace the apple cider vinegar with 45 g / 3 tbsp sherry vinegar, replace the whole-grain mustard with 18 g / 1 tbsp smooth dijon, replace the maple syrup with 15 g / 1 tbsp honey. The dressing reads more European-savory and pairs better with arugula, radicchio, and frisée than with grain bowls.

  • Texture fat→with hazelnut or walnut oil

    Replace 30 g / 2 tbsp of the olive oil with 30 g / 2 tbsp toasted hazelnut or walnut oil (added at the end of the whisking, off heat, since these oils oxidize quickly). The dressing develops a pronounced toasted-nut character and reads particularly well on salads with the same nut.

  • Heat acidity→sharper for salty cheeses and rich proteins

    Pull the acid-to-oil ratio to 1:2 (use 90 g / ⅓ cup + 1 tsp olive oil instead of 120 g). The dressing reads aggressively bright; better against blue cheese, salty pork, or rich braised proteins than against subtle grains.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • apple cider vinegar → sherry vinegar or white wine vinegar (with 20% volume reduction) Both are sharper than apple cider vinegar; reduce the volume to 36 g / 2½ tbsp to compensate. The dressing loses the autumn-coded apple-fruit character.
  • maple syrup, Grade A dark → honey or brown sugar dissolved in 1:1 warm water Honey is slightly more floral and less caramel; brown sugar dissolved in water provides the sweetness and a hint of molasses but lacks the maple-specific aromatic compounds. Both work; the dressing reads as a different sweetener.
  • whole-grain mustard → smooth dijon mustard Smooth dijon emulsifies more aggressively (more available mucilage) and the dressing reads slightly creamier. The textural pop of the whole grains is gone; the dressing is more uniformly smooth.
  • shallot, microplaned → finely minced red onion, or omit Red onion provides the allium note but less mucilage; the emulsion is slightly less stable. Omitting the shallot is acceptable; the dressing emulsifies fine on the mustard alone, but loses some body and aromatic depth.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → whole-grain mustard make it yourself Cracked yellow and brown mustard seeds soaked in white wine vinegar for 48 hours produce a fresher, more aromatic whole-grain mustard than commercial. The 5 minutes of soaking plus 5 minutes of pulse-blending is the entire technique.
  • → apple cider vinegar, unfiltered make it yourself Apple scraps fermented at room temperature for 4 to 6 weeks produce a live unpasteurized cider vinegar with notably more complex apple character than commercial bottles. Two batches a year covers a household supply.

Notes

Why microplane the shallot rather than mince. Microplaned shallot releases mucilage (the same polysaccharide compound that gives okra its slime, present in much smaller quantity) that contributes to emulsion stability; minced shallot floats as discrete pieces that read as crunchy garnish rather than dissolved seasoning. The microplane also breaks more cell walls, releasing more allium aromatic compounds, so the shallot reads more pronounced in the dressing. The microplane is the right tool; a sharp knife mince to extreme fineness is acceptable but slower.

Why dark maple syrup specifically. Grade A Amber Light reads as generic sweet against the apple cider vinegar and the dressing loses its autumn coding; the maple aromatic compounds (vanillin, syringaldehyde, hydroxymethylfurfural) are concentrated in the darker grades and these are what carry the flavor through the dressing. Grade A Dark / Robust is the correct register; Very Dark / Strong works if you can find it but is less common in supermarkets. Pancake syrup (high-fructose corn syrup with maple flavoring) is not a substitute and produces a dressing that reads cloyingly sweet without depth.

The 1:3 ratio is the floor of the acid load. Counting the maple syrup in the acid phase (the maple is a wet ingredient and behaves like the acid for ratio purposes), the total acid-phase mass is 110 g (vinegar + maple + mustard + shallot + salt), and the oil phase is 120 g. This is approximately 1:1 by mass but the effective acid-to-oil sensory ratio is closer to 1:3 because the maple and mustard contribute mass without contributing acid. The dressing reads bright but not sharp at this balance; pulling the oil to 90 g produces a sharper dressing for raw greens, pulling to 150 g produces a softer dressing for roasted vegetables.

The dressing improves over 24 hours. Fresh-mixed vinaigrette is correct but slightly raw-tasting; the shallot needs time to mellow into the acid, the mustard needs time to fully hydrate, and the maple needs time to integrate. A dressing made the morning before a dinner party is meaningfully better than one made just before serving. Beyond 7 days refrigerated, the shallot starts to dominate (the raw allium compounds intensify over time) and the dressing reads aggressive; make a fresh batch.

Pairings beyond the canonical grain salad. The dressing works on: warm roasted root vegetables (carrots, parsnips, beets), seared chicken thigh over greens, grilled pork tenderloin with apples, roasted brussels sprouts and bacon, butternut squash soup as a swirl-finish, sliced ripe pears with blue cheese on a salad plate, lentils and farro for cold lunch bowls. The autumn coding is strongest; spring and summer salads (asparagus, peas, tomatoes) read better with a cleaner vinaigrette built on white wine vinegar and lemon.
