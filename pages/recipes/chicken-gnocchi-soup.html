Chicken gnocchi soup (Olive Garden copycat)

A cream-finished chicken-and-vegetable soup with potato gnocchi as the structural starch and baby spinach added off-heat for color and texture. The chain-restaurant comfort soup reverse-engineered with real chicken stock, a proper roux, and gnocchi cooked to just-floating in the broth.

  1. 500 g boneless skinless chicken thigh, cubed (2 cm)

Execution

  1. 1
    Build the soffritto. Heat a 6-quart Dutch oven or heavy-bottomed soup pot over medium heat. Add the 60 g butter and 15 ml olive oil; when the butter foams, add the 200 g diced onion, 100 g diced carrot, and 100 g diced celery. Sweat 8 to 10 minutes, stirring every 90 seconds, until the vegetables are completely soft and the onions are translucent, but not browned. The goal is sweetness from low-heat caramelization, not Maillard depth; this is a cream soup and aggressive browning fights the cream finish. Add the 12 g pressed garlic and 2 g thyme leaves; cook 60 seconds until fragrant. Blooming aromatics
  2. 2
    Build the roux. Sprinkle the 40 g flour over the soffritto. Stir constantly with a wooden spoon for 2 to 3 minutes; the flour will absorb the fat and form a paste, then loosen as the starch cooks out. The roux is correctly cooked at the blonde stage, pale gold, smelling like toasted bread, which takes about 3 minutes. Pushing further toward a brown roux pulls the soup toward gumbo territory; stopping short produces a soup that tastes raw-flour starchy.
  3. 3
    Whisk in the chicken stock. Add the 1500 ml chicken stock in 4 to 5 stages, whisking vigorously between each addition to prevent the roux from clumping. The first 250 ml will form a thick paste; subsequent additions will progressively thin the soup to broth consistency. After all the stock is incorporated, add the 8 g salt, 0.3 g nutmeg, and a generous twist of black pepper. Bring to a low simmer over medium heat.
  4. 4
    Simmer with the chicken. Add the 500 g cubed chicken thigh. Reduce heat to maintain a bare simmer (about 90 °C, surface should tremor not boil, boiling toughens the chicken proteins and curdles the dairy when added). Simmer 12 to 15 minutes until the chicken is fully cooked through (internal temperature 75 °C) and the soup has thickened slightly from the chicken-released gelatin. Skim any surface foam during the simmer.
  5. 5
    Add the milk, cream, and gnocchi. Pour in the 250 ml whole milk and 250 ml heavy cream; stir to combine. The dairy added at this stage (after the chicken cooks but before final reduction) integrates fully without curdling, the temperature is below the dairy break point, the acid from the soffritto and broth has been buffered by the chicken protein, and the cream stabilizes the milk against thermal shock. Add the 500 g gnocchi directly to the simmering soup; stir gently to submerge them. Cook 3 to 4 minutes until the gnocchi float to the surface and have softened to a tender bite, fresh gnocchi take 3 minutes, shelf-stable take 4 to 5. Do not over-cook the gnocchi; they continue softening in the residual heat after serving and overcooked gnocchi disintegrate into the broth.
  6. 6
    Finish with spinach off-heat. Pull the pot off the heat. Add the 150 g baby spinach in two large handfuls, stirring after each addition. The residual heat wilts the spinach in 90 seconds; the off-heat addition preserves the bright green color and prevents the spinach from going olive-drab. Taste; adjust salt (more is usually needed at this stage as the cream and gnocchi have absorbed seasoning) and grind another half-teaspoon of black pepper.
  7. 7
    Serve. Ladle into wide bowls. Top each bowl with a small pinch of the 30 g grated parmigiano (about 5 g per bowl) and a final twist of black pepper. Pass the remaining parmigiano at the table for guest-grating. Serve immediately with a basket of warm Olive Garden breadsticks (the canonical Italian-American chain pairing, see recipes/olive-garden-breadsticks).

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→rotisserie chicken (the weeknight shortcut)

    Skip the cubed-thigh-and-simmer step. After whisking in the stock, simmer the soup base 5 minutes for the flavors to meld, then add 500 g shredded rotisserie chicken at the same stage as the cream and gnocchi. Total cook time drops from 50 to 30 minutes. Acceptable for a weeknight; the dish reads slightly less integrated because the chicken has not been simmered into the broth.

  • Dietary protein→vegetarian (with white beans)

    Replace the chicken with 1 can (425 g) cannellini or great northern beans, drained and rinsed. Replace the chicken stock with vegetable stock or mushroom stock. Add a parmigiano rind to the simmer for glutamate depth. The dish becomes a different category (Italian bean-and-gnocchi soup) but uses the same technique and produces a similarly satisfying cream-finished soup.

  • Regional flavor→Tuscan kale and white bean (a regional Italian pivot)

    Replace the spinach with 200 g lacinato kale (stems removed, chopped into ribbons); add at the gnocchi stage rather than off-heat (kale needs 4 to 5 minutes of cooking to soften). Add 1 can drained cannellini beans. Replace the cream with 100 ml additional whole milk. The dish becomes a creamy Tuscan-style minestrone-adjacent soup; lighter, more vegetable-forward, less restaurant-comfort.

  • Occasion occasion→Spring vegetable variant (peas, asparagus, lemon)

    Add 150 g frozen peas and 200 g asparagus tips (cut into 3 cm lengths) at the cream-and-gnocchi stage. Skip the spinach. Finish with the zest of one lemon and a tablespoon of lemon juice off-heat. The dish becomes a spring-themed cream soup; brighter, fresher, less heavy than the original.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • chicken thigh → chicken breast Cube smaller (1.5 cm rather than 2 cm) and reduce simmer time to 8 minutes; pull at internal temperature 65 °C. The soup reads cleaner and lighter; loses some of the depth that thighs contribute. See ingredients/chicken-thigh for the full discussion.
  • fresh gnocchi → shelf-stable vacuum-pack gnocchi (DeLallo, De Cecco, Olivieri) The everyday convenience choice. The shelf-stable product is denser and chewier than fresh; cook 4 to 5 minutes rather than 3. Acceptable for weeknight cooking; the fresh-from-scratch upgrade is the structural improvement.
  • heavy cream → half-and-half or whole milk plus 30 g additional flour Lower-fat alternatives that approximate the cream finish. Half-and-half is functional with a slight loss of body; the milk-and-flour route requires increasing the roux flour to 70 g total to compensate for the lower fat structure. Avoid skim or 2% milk; the dish reads thin and watery.
  • baby spinach → frozen chopped spinach (squeezed dry) Use 100 g frozen spinach, thawed and squeezed completely dry of liquid. Add at the gnocchi stage rather than off-heat. The texture is softer and the color slightly duller; functional for a weeknight version.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → chicken stock make it yourself Cream soups taste meaningfully different with real homemade stock, the gelatin and glutamate compound the body and depth in ways that boxed stock cannot match. The freezer stash earns its place here.
  • → fresh or shelf-stable potato gnocchi make it yourselfstub Hand-rolled potato gnocchi from baked russets and a minimum-flour dough are dramatically more delicate than commercial. A weekend project that elevates this soup from chain-copycat to genuine restaurant-grade.

Notes

Why the dairy goes in late. Adding milk and cream at the start of a soup that contains acid (from soffritto, garlic, tomato, none of which this soup has, but the principle holds) or that boils heavily produces curdled dairy and a broken texture. Adding dairy at the late stage, after the soup has reduced and the protein has been cooked, integrates the dairy without breaking. The temperature rule: dairy added below 85 °C and held below 95 °C will not curdle. The technique rule: stir constantly during dairy addition and reduce the heat immediately after.

Why nutmeg in cream soups. A small dose of fresh-grated nutmeg (around 0.3 g, an eighth of a teaspoon) is the structural finishing aromatic for cream-finished soups across European tradition, French béchamel, Italian zuppa di gnocchi, Greek avgolemono adjacent. The compound responsible (myristicin and elemicin) is fat-soluble and reads through cream and butter clearly while disappearing in water-based broths. Skip the nutmeg and the soup reads thinner; double the dose and the soup reads as eggnog. The amount matters and is small.

Why thigh, not breast. The cubed thigh holds together through a 13-minute simmer in a way that breast does not. Breast at 13 minutes in a simmer is fully overcooked (internal temperature past 75 °C, the meat dry and chalky). Thigh at the same time is still juicy because the dark meat tolerates a wider doneness window and the higher fat content keeps the cubes moist. Recipes that specify "chicken breast" for this soup either reduce the simmer to 8 minutes (acceptable but trickier to time around the gnocchi), use rotisserie chicken (the everyday workaround), or accept a drier result.

The gnocchi-in-broth doneness rule. Gnocchi cooked in soup follow the same float-test rule as gnocchi cooked in plain water: drop them in, cook 90 seconds past the moment they float to the surface, then check texture. In soup, the broth is more viscous than water and the heat transfer is slower, so the cook time runs slightly longer than in water, 3 to 4 minutes for fresh, 4 to 5 for shelf-stable. Overcooking is the failure mode; the gnocchi disintegrate into the broth and become a starchy thickening agent rather than a discrete dumpling. Pull the pot off the heat the moment the gnocchi are at the right texture; residual heat continues cooking them at the table.

The chain-restaurant fingerprint. Three details define the chain version against home-cook attempts: the cream-and-milk ratio (50/50, not heavy-cream-only), the nutmeg (always present, often unidentifiable), and the gnocchi-just-shy-of-overdone texture (fresh gnocchi cooked to floating, not the firmer al-dente of homemade-from-scratch). Hitting these three details produces a soup that tastes genuinely chain-equivalent; missing any one produces a soup that reads as good but distinctly different from the Olive Garden version.

The freezer question. This soup does not freeze well as-is. The dairy and gnocchi both suffer freeze-thaw damage, the dairy breaks and the gnocchi turn gummy. The workaround: freeze the soup base (everything through the chicken simmer step, before adding dairy and gnocchi) for up to 2 months. Thaw, bring to a simmer, then add the dairy and freshly-cooked gnocchi at the table. The base-freeze approach gives the convenience of a freezer-stash soup without the texture compromise.
