Recipe

Whole-roasted striped bass with salsa verde

A scored whole striped bass stuffed with lemon, smashed garlic, fennel fronds, thyme, and parsley, rubbed with olive oil and salt, and roasted at 225°C until the skin crisps and the flesh just sets at 55°C. A sharp green salsa verde of parsley, capers, anchovy, garlic, and lemon is spooned over at the table: the anchovy and capers carry the umami, the lemon and red wine vinegar the finishing acid. One fish, best meat-to-bone ratio, feeds four off a single sheet pan.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Preheat the oven fully to 225°C / 440°F with a rack in the upper third BEFORE the fish goes near it. A whole roast lives or dies on the oven being at full temperature at the moment the fish enters: a cold or still-heating oven steams the skin instead of crisping it. Give it a full 20 minutes to come up, and trust the preheat indicator only after that.
  • Temper the fish at room temperature for 20 to 30 minutes before roasting. A fridge-cold fish needs longer in the oven, which overcooks the thin belly before the thick spine sets; a tempered fish roasts evenly and crisps faster. Pat it bone-dry again right before it goes in, since tempering draws surface moisture.
  • Make the salsa verde while the fish roasts, not before. It needs only 10 minutes, and it is brightest within the hour: the chopped parsley dulls and the garlic sharpens if it sits too long. Have the board and knife staged so you can build it during the 25 minute roast and spoon it over the moment the fish lands on the platter.

Phase 1, the fish and cavity

  1. 1400 g whole striped bass, scaled and gutted, gills removed, rinsed inside and out, blood line scraped from the spine cavity with a small spoon, patted bone-dry with paper towels, three diagonal slashes 1.5 cm deep cut through the skin into the flesh on each side from gill to tail
  2. 100 g lemon, half sliced into thin rounds for the cavity; half reserved as wedges for the table
  3. 20 g garlic, smashed with the flat of a knife, skins left on, for the cavity — (5 cloves, smashed)
  4. 80 g fennel bulb with fronds, fronds reserved for the cavity; bulb cut into 5 mm planks for the bed under the fish
  5. 6 g fresh thyme, whole sprigs, for the cavity — (6 sprigs)
  6. 8 g flat-leaf parsley, a small bunch of stems and leaves, for the cavity — (8 sprigs; the leafy tops also feed the salsa below)
  7. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  8. 9 g kosher salt
  9. 1 g black pepper, freshly ground

Phase 2, salsa verde

  1. 40 g flat-leaf parsley, leaves, leaves picked, finely chopped just before mixing
  2. 16 g anchovy fillets in oil, finely minced to a paste — (4 fillets; the umami anchor of the sauce)
  3. 20 g capers, drained, rinsed if salt-packed, roughly chopped
  4. 4 g garlic, grated to a paste on a microplane — (1 small clove)
  5. 30 g lemon, juice and zest, zested fine, then juiced — (juice and zest of ½ lemon)
  6. 10 g red wine vinegar
  7. 20 g shallot, minced fine — (½ small shallot)
  8. 90 g extra-virgin olive oil
  9. 25 g niçoise olives, pitted, pitted, roughly chopped — (~15 olives; optional, for a brinier sauce)

Execution

  1. 1
    Pull the 1400 g / 1 whole striped bass from the fridge 20 to 30 minutes before roasting to temper, and set the oven to 225°C / 440°F with a rack in the upper third. Verify the fishmonger scaled and gutted the fish and removed the gills. Rinse the cavity, and scrape any dark blood line along the inside of the spine with the back of a small spoon; the blood line carries the strongest fishy flavor and is the main source of "fishy fish" complaints. Pat the fish completely dry inside and out, since a wet skin steams instead of crisping.
  2. 2
    With a sharp knife, cut three diagonal slashes 1.5 cm deep into the flesh on each side of the fish, from the gill plate to the tail. The slashes do three jobs: they let the heat reach the spine evenly so the thick back cooks in step with the thin belly, they open channels for the salt and oil, and they give a visual doneness cue (the flesh in the slashes opaques first). Rub the 9 g / 2¼ tsp kosher salt over the skin on both sides, into the slashes, and inside the cavity. Grind the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper over the skin.
  3. 3
    Stuff the cavity. Lay in the ~50 g / sliced lemon rounds (half the 100 g lemon), the 20 g / 5 smashed garlic cloves, the ~20 g fennel fronds (reserved from the 80 g fennel bulb), the 6 g / 6 thyme sprigs, and the 8 g / small bunch parsley. Pack it loosely; the aromatics perfume the flesh from the inside and steam the cavity as the fish roasts. Rub the 30 g / 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil over the skin on both sides and work a little into the slashes so the skin renders and browns rather than drying out.
  4. 4
    Build the bed. Scatter the ~60 g / fennel planks (the bulb from the 80 g fennel) across the center of a rimmed sheet pan or shallow roasting tray, and lay the fish on top. The fennel raises the fish off the pan so hot air circulates under the skin and the bottom crisps rather than sticking and steaming in its own juices, and the planks roast into a sweet aromatic base that you serve alongside. Pat the top skin dry one more time; this is the last chance before the oven and it matters most for crisp.
  5. 5
    Roast the fish in the upper third of the fully preheated 225°C / 440°F oven, undisturbed, for 22 to 28 minutes depending on size. The ~1.4 kg fish here runs about 25 minutes. The skin should tighten, blister, and brown, and the flesh in the slashes should turn fully opaque. Begin checking at 22 minutes by inserting an instant-read thermometer into the thickest part of the back near the spine: pull the fish at 55°C / 131°F, where the large flake has just set and stays moist. The flesh should lift cleanly from the bone with gentle pressure; if it still has a translucent core, give it 2 to 3 minutes more. Past 60°C the flake dries and separates, so err toward the early pull and let carryover finish it.
  6. 6
    While the fish roasts, build the salsa verde. On a board, finely chop the 40 g / large bunch parsley leaves and scrape into a bowl. Mince the 16 g / 4 anchovy fillets to a paste and add them; the anchovy is the umami anchor, dissolving into the oil and carrying glutamate through the whole sauce. Add the 20 g / 2 tbsp chopped capers, the 4 g / 1 small clove garlic grated to a paste, and the 20 g / ½ shallot minced fine.
  7. 7
    Finish the salsa verde with acid and oil. Whisk in the 30 g / juice and zest of ½ lemon, the 10 g / 2 tsp red wine vinegar, and the 90 g / 6 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil until the sauce is loose and spoonable; fold in the 25 g / chopped niçoise olives if using. Taste: it should be sharp, salty, and herbaceous, with the acid forward enough to cut the roasted skin. The capers and anchovy supply the salt, so add no more until you have tasted; if it reads flat, a few more drops of red wine vinegar or lemon lift it. Hold at room temperature, not the fridge, so the oil stays loose.
  8. 8
    Rest the fish 3 to 5 minutes on the pan; the flake firms slightly and carryover brings the spine to about 57°C. Transfer the whole fish to a warm platter using two spatulas under opposite ends, and arrange the roasted fennel planks alongside. To serve, run a knife down the spine and lift the top fillet off the bones in two pieces, then lift the whole skeleton clear from the tail to free the bottom fillet. Spoon the salsa verde generously over the flesh and pass the rest at the table, with the reserved ~50 g / lemon wedges for squeezing.
Safety
  • Pulling striped bass at 55°C / 131°F internal (below the USDA published-safe 145°F for fish) The Mediterranean whole-roasted fish canon pulls the fish at 52 to 57°C / 126 to 135°F so the large flake just sets and stays moist; past 60°C the flesh dries and separates. The published 145°F is the conservative line for the immunocompromised. Whole fish from a reputable counter, sourced fresh and roasted hot until the skin renders, follows long-standing practice at the lower temperature, with the hot oven surface pasteurizing the exterior where the bacterial load lives. Cooks who want the USDA-published-safe number should roast to 145°F / 63°C internal, accepting drier, more separated flake. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture crispier, faster skin→broiler finish

    Roast as written but pull the fish 3°C early, at 52°C, then move the pan to the top rack under a high broiler for 90 seconds to 2 minutes, watching constantly, until the skin blisters and crisps hard. The brief broiler blast crisps the skin past what a 225°C roast alone achieves without overcooking the interior, since the carryover from the broiler lands the spine right at 55°C. Best when the fish is on the larger end and the skin is reluctant to crisp at roasting temperature.

  • Regional tomato and olive sheet-pan version→roasted with cherry tomatoes

    Scatter 250 g / 1½ cups cherry tomatoes and the 25 g niçoise olives across the fennel bed before the fish goes on, and add a splash of white wine to the pan. The tomatoes burst and reduce into a quick pan sauce as the fish roasts, and the dish becomes a one-pan supper that needs only bread. The salsa verde becomes optional rather than central, since the roasted tomato and olive supply their own acid and brine.

  • Protein two-fillet weeknight version→crispy-skin pan-seared fillets

    Skip the whole fish and use 2 skin-on striped bass fillets (~250 g each). Score the skin, salt it, and sear skin-side-down in a hot pan with a press for 4 to 5 minutes until the skin crisps and the flesh turns opaque from below, then flip for 60 seconds and pull at 55°C. The same salsa verde finishes it. The dish loses the share-plate drama and the high meat-to-bone economy, but it cooks in 10 minutes for a smaller table.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole striped bass → whole branzino, black sea bass, or red snapper Branzino is the closest whole-roast analog at a smaller plate size; reduce the roast time by 5 to 8 minutes for a thinner fish and check temperature early. Black sea bass matches the meaty flake and crisp skin almost exactly. Snapper roasts well and crisps hard. All four take the salsa verde identically.
  • red wine vinegar → white wine vinegar or sherry vinegar White wine vinegar is slightly brighter and milder; sherry vinegar is deeper and nuttier. Both work in the salsa verde; with white wine vinegar add a few extra drops since it reads gentler than red wine vinegar.
  • niçoise olives → kalamata or taggiasca olives, or omit Kalamata is brinier and more assertive; taggiasca is closer to niçoise in size and mild fruitiness. The olives are optional richness in the salsa, so omitting them leaves a cleaner, sharper sauce that is equally correct.
  • fennel bulb → thinly sliced onion or leek For the roasting bed and the cavity fronds, a sliced onion or split leek raises the fish off the pan and aromatizes it similarly. You lose the anise note that pairs so naturally with fish, so add a pinch of fennel seed to the cavity to recover it.

Notes

Why whole, and why this fish. The whole roast is the most efficient way to cook a fish for a table: the bone, skin, and head insulate the flesh and baste it with rendering fat, the cavity aromatics season from the inside, and you get the best meat-to-bone ratio of any preparation. Striped bass is the ideal candidate because it carries a high proportion of edible flesh, a moderate fat that stays moist through the roast, a large meaty flake that holds together when you lift the fillets off the bone, and a firm skin that crisps cleanly at 225°C. A single ~1.4 kg fish off one sheet pan feeds four. See the striped bass ingredient page for the wild-versus-farmed distinction and freshness cues at the counter.

The two technical moves. First, dryness and heat for the skin: the fish must be patted bone-dry and the oven fully preheated, because surface water boiling off at the start steams the skin soft instead of letting it render and crisp. Second, temperature for the flesh: pull at 55°C and rest, because striped bass flake just sets across the 52 to 57°C window and begins to dry past 60°C. The slashes are what reconcile the two, letting heat reach the thick spine in step with the thin belly so the whole fish lands at temperature together. An instant-read thermometer in the spine is worth more than any timer here.

The umami and acid engineering. A roasted whole fish is mild and rich, and it needs an external counterpoint with both glutamate and bright acid. The salsa verde supplies both. The anchovy and capers are the umami anchor: anchovy is dense in free glutamate and the capers add a salty-funky brine, and together they give the sauce a savory depth that a plain herb oil lacks. The lemon and red wine vinegar are the finishing acid, sharp enough to cut the richness of the crisp skin and brighten the mild flesh. Build the salsa fresh while the fish roasts, since chopped parsley dulls and raw garlic sharpens within the hour, and keep it at room temperature so the oil stays loose and spoonable.

Sourcing and serving. Whole farmed striped bass at H Mart and most Asian seafood counters is usually the best value in the case, priced for volume at the 700 g to 1.6 kg plate size; scale this recipe's salt and aromatics roughly with the fish weight. Look for clear bulging eyes, bright red gills, tight scales, and a clean ocean smell, and cook it the day you buy it. To serve, lift the top fillet off the bones in two pieces, then lift the skeleton clear from the tail to free the bottom fillet; the cheeks behind the eyes are a small prized lobe of dense flesh worth digging out. Roasted fennel, a crisp salad, and bread to mop the salsa verde are all the sides it needs.
