Recipe

Mediterranean grain bowl with tahini-yogurt

Farro cooked al dente in salted water, mixed roasted vegetables (zucchini, red pepper, red onion) hard-roasted at 230 °C until the cut faces brown, crisped chickpeas dried hard before they hit the oil, a cold tahini-yogurt sauce thinned with lemon juice and ice water, and a heavy finish of parsley, dill, and lemon zest. The bowl is built around the contrast between the warm caramelized vegetables, the cool acidic dressing, and the chewy grain that picks up both. Batch-cookable: every component holds 4 days refrigerated, the bowl assembles cold or warm in 5 minutes.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Get the oven to 230 °C with a heavy sheet pan inside before you do anything else. The pan needs 15 minutes preheat for the vegetables to sizzle on contact, and the chickpeas need a screaming-hot pan to crisp rather than steam.
  • Start the farro in salted water as soon as you turn the oven on. Pearled farro takes 20 to 25 minutes, whole farro takes 35 to 45; both run in parallel with the vegetable roast and the sauce assembly.
  • Drain and dry the chickpeas thoroughly. Spread on a kitchen towel and pat the tops dry with a second towel. Wet chickpeas steam instead of crisp; this is the single most common failure point.
  • The tahini-yogurt sauce can be made up to 4 days ahead. The raw garlic mellows over the first 24 hours and the sauce reads more rounded by day two.

Execution

  1. 1
    Set the oven to 230 °C / 450 °F with a heavy half-sheet pan inside on the middle rack. The pan needs 15 minutes of preheat. While the oven heats, bring 1500 ml / 6 cups of water to a boil with the 12 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt for the farro.
  2. 2
    Add the 200 g / 1 cup pearled farro to the boiling salted water. Cook at a steady simmer, uncovered, for 20 to 25 minutes until the grains are tender but still have a discernible bite at the center. Drain in a sieve and spread on a sheet pan to stop the carryover cooking; this prevents the grains from continuing to soften into mush.
  3. 3
    Cut the 350 g / 2 medium zucchini into 2 cm half-moons, the 250 g / 2 medium red peppers into 2 cm squares, and the 200 g / 1 large red onion into 8 root-attached wedges. In a wide bowl, toss the cut vegetables with the 35 g / 2½ tbsp olive oil, the 9 g / 2¼ tsp kosher salt, and the 2 g / 1 tsp dried oregano. Every surface should glisten.
  4. 4
    Drain and pat the chickpeas dry. Spread the 250 g / 1 cup chickpeas on a kitchen towel, top with a second towel, and press gently to absorb every drop of surface moisture. In a small bowl, toss the dried chickpeas with the 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil, the 2 g / 1 tsp ground cumin, the 2 g / 1 tsp smoked paprika, and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt.
  5. 5
    Pull the preheated sheet pan out of the oven. Tip the dressed 800 g vegetables onto one half of the pan in a single layer with space between pieces; tip the seasoned 250 g chickpeas onto the other half. The pan should sizzle audibly on contact. Roast 20 minutes without disturbing. High-heat dry roasting
  6. 6
    After 20 minutes, toss the vegetables and chickpeas separately with a spatula, scraping any browned bits from the pan. Return for another 10 to 12 minutes until the vegetables show deep caramelization on the cut faces and the chickpeas are crisp on the surface (they should rattle slightly when shaken on the pan). Pull and let rest on the pan for 5 minutes. High-heat dry roasting
  7. 7
    Build the tahini-yogurt sauce while the vegetables roast. In a medium bowl, whisk the 200 g / ¾ cup Greek yogurt, the 60 g / ¼ cup tahini, the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, the 4 g / 1 clove microplaned garlic, and the 3 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt until smooth. The sauce will be thick. Stream in the 30 g / 2 tbsp ice water in 1 tbsp additions, whisking between each, until the sauce ribbons off the whisk and settles slowly back into the surface. Taste; it should read tahini-led, lemon-bright, fully salted. Tahini emulsion
  8. 8
    Assemble. Divide the cooked farro across four wide shallow bowls (about 100 g / ½ cup per bowl). Top each with one-quarter of the roasted vegetables, one-quarter of the crispy chickpeas, and a generous spoonful (about 70 g / ¼ cup) of the tahini-yogurt sauce. Scatter the 20 g chopped parsley, the 10 g chopped dill, the 2 g microplaned lemon zest, and the 80 g crumbled feta across the bowls. Serve immediately, warm or at room temperature.
  9. 9
    For batch storage, hold every component separately in covered glass containers in the refrigerator. The farro keeps 4 days, the vegetables 3 days, the chickpeas 3 days (they soften over time but still eat well), the sauce 4 days. Assemble cold from the fridge or warm individual portions in a 160 °C / 325 °F oven for 8 minutes before adding the cold sauce and herbs.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture grain→quinoa, barley, or brown rice

    Quinoa cooks in 15 minutes absorption-style (200 g grain + 360 g water + 4 g salt, simmer covered 15 min, rest 5 min, fluff). Barley behaves like farro at 30 to 35 minutes pasta-style. Brown rice takes 40 minutes absorption-style. All substitute 1:1 by weight. Quinoa is the protein-densest swap.

  • Protein protein→with grilled chicken or salmon

    Add 400 g grilled chicken thigh (seasoned with 1.5% salt, grilled 4 minutes per side over medium-high heat, rested 5 minutes, sliced against the grain) or 400 g salmon (roasted alongside the vegetables for the final 10 minutes of their cook). The bowl becomes a full dinner rather than a vegetable-forward lunch.

  • Occasion season→autumn version with butternut and Brussels sprouts

    Replace the zucchini with 350 g butternut squash cut into 2 cm cubes and the red pepper with 250 g halved Brussels sprouts. Same temperature, same timing. The bowl shifts from summer-bright into autumn-savory; add a drizzle of pomegranate molasses at plating for a sweet-tart counter to the heavier vegetables.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • pearled farro → spelt berries, freekeh, or wheat berries All cook pasta-style at 25 to 40 minutes depending on the grain. Freekeh has a smoky cracked-wheat character that reads more Levantine. Wheat berries are chewier and need 50 minutes.
  • Greek yogurt → labneh or thick whole-milk yogurt strained 2 hours through cheesecloth Labneh is the Levantine analogue and gives a richer, tangier sauce. Regular yogurt strained briefly substitutes structurally. Low-fat yogurt is not a real substitute; the sauce will read watery.
  • dried oregano → fresh oregano (use 6 g / 2 tbsp chopped), za'atar, or dried thyme Fresh oregano added to the cooked vegetables off heat reads brighter but less concentrated. Za'atar adds sumac and sesame notes that lean more strongly Levantine.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → tahini, single-origin make it yourself Toasting hulled sesame seeds lightly and grinding them with neutral oil produces a fresher, more aromatic tahini than commercial bottles. Worth the 20-minute process if you go through tahini quickly.
  • → whole-milk Greek yogurt make it yourselfstub Homemade yogurt strained through cheesecloth gives a thicker, tangier result than commercial Greek yogurt. The straining stage is what differentiates Greek from regular yogurt, and homemade lets you control how thick.

Notes

Why hard-roast the vegetables instead of soft-roast. The grain-bowl format dies when every component reads neutral. A 200 °C / 390 °F oven cooks the vegetables tender but leaves them flat in flavor; the bowl reads as steamed vegetables on a bed of grain. At 230 °C / 450 °F on a preheated sheet pan, the vegetable surfaces hit Maillard temperature within 5 minutes of pan contact and develop the caramelized character that anchors the bowl. Same vegetables, same time investment, structurally different result.

Why dry the chickpeas hard. Surface moisture is the single point of failure on crispy chickpeas. Wet chickpeas spend the first 10 minutes of the roast boiling off water, and by the time the surface is dry the interior has overcooked into a paste. Dry chickpeas hit the hot pan and start dehydrating-and-browning immediately. The kitchen-towel-pat is not optional; it is the technique.

The sauce calibration. Tahini alone is thick and bitter without salt and acid. Yogurt alone reads as plain dairy. The combination at roughly 75% yogurt / 25% tahini by weight produces a sauce that reads creamy-and-tangy with the structural sesame depth that pulls the Mediterranean register together. The lemon juice and the microplaned raw garlic are non-negotiable; the acid finishes the protein hydration in the tahini and the garlic compounds the savory.

Why batch-cook this format. Every component reheats or eats cold without quality loss, and the assembled bowl reads as composed-on-purpose rather than reheated-leftovers because the cold sauce and fresh herbs go on at serving time. The format is calibrated for the work-from-home or office-lunch use case where the cook wants 4 lunches from one 60-minute Sunday session. Hold all components in glass (not plastic) to avoid the off-flavors that build up in plastic containers over multiple days.
