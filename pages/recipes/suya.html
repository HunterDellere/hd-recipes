Suya (Nigerian peanut-spiced grilled beef skewers)

Thin-sliced beef rubbed heavily with yaji (ground roasted peanut, chili, ginger, onion powder, paprika) and grilled hot and fast over coals until the spice rub blisters and the meat is crusted-charred outside, still tender inside. The Nigerian street-food signature of the Hausa north, eaten with raw sliced onion, tomato, and additional yaji on the side.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The 30 to 60 minute rub-rest is structural; the dry yaji needs to bind to the surface moisture of the meat and the peanut oil needs to penetrate slightly. Plan for the rest. Longer (up to 4 hours) is fine and produces deeper flavor.
  • Slice the beef while partially frozen (30 to 45 minutes in the freezer makes the cut dramatically easier). The slices need to be 4 to 5 mm thick for the cook to work; thicker pieces undercook or the spice burns waiting for the interior.
  • Make the yaji from scratch; commercial suya spice exists in some West African groceries but the freshly ground roasted peanut is dramatically better. The peanut grind is the structural element.
  • Cook over high direct heat: live coals (the canonical method), or a hot gas grill, or under a hot broiler 10 cm from the element. The yaji rub blisters and chars at high heat; medium heat produces dusty grey skewers.
  • Soak wooden skewers in water for 30 minutes if using; metal skewers are sturdier and reusable.

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the yaji. In a dry skillet over medium-low heat, toast the 100 g / ¾ cup raw peanuts for 8 to 10 minutes, shaking the pan every 30 to 45 seconds, until the peanuts are deep golden brown and smell distinctly nutty. Transfer to a plate to cool for 5 minutes; the residual heat keeps cooking them and they go bitter if left in the hot pan. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  2. 2
    Grind the cooled peanuts in a spice grinder or food processor in 5 to 10 second pulses until the texture is somewhere between coarse cornmeal and fine sand. Do not over-grind or the peanuts release oil and form peanut butter; the yaji needs a dry powder consistency. If it does start to clump, that is fine, the meat sticking helps the rub adhere.
  3. 3
    In a small bowl, combine the ground peanuts with the 15 g / 2 tbsp sweet paprika, the 6 g / 2 tsp cayenne, the 6 g / 2 tsp ground ginger, the 8 g / 1 tbsp onion powder, the 5 g / 1½ tsp garlic powder, the 1 g / ¼ tsp ground cloves, the 10 g / 2½ tsp kosher salt, and the 4 g / 1 tsp Maggi powder if using. Mix thoroughly with a fork. The yaji should be a uniform terracotta-orange powder with a strong peanut-and-chili aroma. Reserve about one-third of the yaji (roughly 50 g) in a small bowl for the table as dipping spice; the rest is the rub.
  4. 4
    Prepare the beef. Pull the partially frozen 700 g / 1½ lb beef from the freezer and slice against the grain into 4 to 5 mm thick strips, 8 to 10 cm long. The partially frozen meat slices cleanly; fully thawed meat tears under a knife.
  5. 5
    In a large bowl, toss the beef strips with the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil until every piece is lightly coated. Add the rub portion of the yaji (about 100 g) and toss again until every strip is heavily coated. Use your hands; the rub adheres better when worked in. Let the rubbed meat rest at room temperature for 30 minutes (or refrigerated for up to 4 hours). The salt in the rub draws surface moisture out, which dissolves the rub and forms a thin spice paste on the meat that grills into a deep crust.
  6. 6
    Thread the beef strips lengthwise onto skewers, 3 to 4 strips per skewer, spreading them out slightly so the air can circulate around each piece. Press any spice rub that fell off during threading back onto the skewered meat.
  7. 7
    Heat a charcoal grill, gas grill, or broiler to high. For the broiler, set the rack 10 cm from the element; for a grill, the coals should be glowing red with no flame and the grates hot enough that water flicked on them flashes off immediately. Brush the grates with oil.
  8. 8
    Cook the skewers over direct high heat for 2 to 3 minutes per side, flipping once or twice. The yaji rub blisters and develops a deep crusty char; the meat inside cooks to medium (slightly pink) in this short window. Pull from heat as soon as the rub has the desired char and the meat shows the first beads of moisture on the surface (overcooking past this point dries the thin strips quickly). Maillard sear
  9. 9
    Serve immediately. Arrange the skewers on a platter with the 150 g / 1 onion worth of thin red onion rings, the 150 g / 2 tomatoes worth of tomato rounds, the 30 g / 4 lime wedges, and the small bowl of reserved yaji as dipping spice. Diners squeeze lime over the meat, dip pieces into extra yaji as desired, and eat the onion and tomato alongside as cooling counterpoint to the spice heat.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→chicken suya (sose, or chicken yaji)

    Replace the beef with 700 g boneless skinless chicken thighs cut into 4 to 5 mm strips. Same yaji rub, same rest, same hot-grill cook for 2 to 3 minutes per side. The chicken cooks fast at this thickness; pull as soon as the rub has charred and the meat is opaque throughout.

  • Protein protein→fish suya (fish yaji)

    Replace the beef with 700 g firm white fish (tilapia, snapper, or sea bass), cut into 3 cm chunks rather than thin strips. The yaji rub goes on identically. Skewer and grill 90 seconds per side over very high heat; fish at this thickness cooks fast. The fish version is common along the West African coast.

  • Occasion occasion→suya kebabs in a fluffy bread wrap

    Wrap the grilled suya skewers in warm flatbread (Nigerian agege bread or pita or naan) with the raw onion, tomato, and a smear of harissa or sweet pepper sauce. The wrap is a common street-food presentation and turns the skewers into a portable sandwich.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • raw peanuts (for the yaji rub) → dry-roasted unsalted peanuts (skip the toast in Step 1), or kuli-kuli powder (ground peanut cake from West African groceries) Dry-roasted peanuts are an honest shortcut; the fresh toast and grind reads marginally better but the difference is small. Kuli-kuli powder is the canonical Nigerian version of pre-ground peanut and is the closest commercial product.
  • beef sirloin or chuck → flank steak (slice against the grain), or skirt steak, or beef bottom round Flank and skirt steak are the closest substitutes by texture; bottom round is leaner and slightly drier. All work with the thin-slice and quick-cook method.
  • Hungarian sweet paprika → generic sweet paprika (slightly less fruity), or smoked Spanish pimentón (shifts the profile toward smoky-Spanish) Hungarian sweet is the closest to the canonical Nigerian dried red pepper register. Smoked pimentón reads recognizably different and slightly off for suya.

Notes

Why ground peanut is structural. The peanut grind is what makes yaji yaji rather than a generic dry-spice rub. The ground peanut contributes fat (which carries the chili and spice flavors), texture (the slightly sandy grind that adheres to the meat), and a recognizable nutty register that reads through every bite. Other West African and African-diaspora grilled meats use dry rubs but the peanut is the Nigerian Hausa signature.

Why the meat is sliced thin. The thin-slice method (4 to 5 mm) maximizes the surface area for the spice rub, gives the high direct heat time to char the rub crust before the meat overcooks, and produces a chewy-tender texture that is the canonical suya bite. Thicker cuts produce a different dish; either the rub burns waiting for the interior or the interior is raw when the rub is right.

Why high heat. The yaji rub depends on hot direct heat to blister and char into a crust; medium heat dehydrates the rub into a dusty grey coating. Live coals, a screaming-hot gas grill, or a broiler at 10 cm distance are the three working setups. Anything cooler and the dish does not work.

Why the resting yaji on the plate. The reserved one-third of the yaji on the table as dipping spice is canonical; diners adjust their own heat and intensity by dipping pieces into extra yaji as they eat. The cooling raw onion and tomato are the counterpoint; the lime is the finishing acid.

Make-ahead. The yaji itself keeps 2 to 3 months in an airtight jar at room temperature; the peanut goes faintly stale after that but the dry spices stay fresh. Make a double batch and keep it on hand. The meat is best cooked fresh; rubbed beef holds 24 hours refrigerated before grilling but reads better at 30 minutes to 4 hours.
