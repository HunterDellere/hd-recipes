Brown sugar milk tea with boba (Tiger Sugar style)

Hot brown-sugar boba painted up the inside of a glass into the signature tiger-stripe pattern, then layered with cold milk and a strong tea concentrate. No commercial syrup, the syrup is built from the pearl cook.

20 mintotal

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The pearls drive the schedule, not the tea. Cook your tapioca pearls FIRST (recipe homemade-tapioca-pearls-black), they need 25 minutes of boiling plus a 30-minute off-heat rest, then 15 minutes finishing in brown sugar syrup. If using store-bought quick-cook pearls, follow the package and plan around their service window.
  • Pearls hold for 4 to 6 hours at room temperature in their syrup. Do not refrigerate cooked pearls, they harden within 2 hours and there is no recovery. Cook today, drink today.
  • Brew tea concentrate and let it cool while the pearls finish, pouring hot tea over the chilled milk muddies the layering and breaks the dairy emulsion. Aim for tea at room temperature and milk straight from the fridge.

Execution

  1. 1
    Brew tea concentrate first. Pour the 240 ml / 1 cup boiling water over the 8 g / 1½ tbsp CTC Assam tea in a small jug. Steep 5 minutes, longer than usual because the tea will be diluted heavily by milk and ice. Strain. The concentrate should look almost black. Cool to room temperature; tea added hot to milk turns the layering muddy.
  2. 2
    Prep the glasses. Use two tall, clear glasses (about 400 ml / 14 oz each), straight-sided or slightly tapered, never narrow. The tiger-stripe effect depends on a wide visible surface. Chill the glasses in the freezer for 5 minutes while you work; cold glass keeps the syrup viscous against the wall.
  3. 3
    Paint the stripes. Pour about 40 ml / 2½ tbsp of the warm brown sugar syrup into each chilled glass. Tilt and rotate the glass so the syrup runs in irregular streaks down the inside wall, leaving the characteristic tiger-stripe pattern. The syrup must be warm and fluid to flow, if it has cooled into a thick paste, microwave 10 seconds. The cold glass holds the syrup in place once it touches the wall.
  4. 4
    Add boba. Spoon 100 g / ½ cup warm cooked pearls into the bottom of each glass. The pearls should be warm from cooking, not refrigerator-cold. If your pearls have been sitting in syrup at room temperature for an hour or two that is correct.
  5. 5
    Layer milk. Slowly pour 180 ml / ¾ cup cold whole milk down the inside of each glass, pour against the wall, not into the center, to keep the brown stripes from dissolving. Add 4 to 5 ice cubes per glass on top of the milk.
  6. 6
    Add tea. Pour 120 ml / ½ cup of the cooled tea concentrate into each glass, again down the inside wall. The tea floats on top of the milk for a moment, creating a three-layer effect: dark stripes, white milk middle, amber tea top. After a few seconds the layers begin to merge from the bottom up; the visual is the first 30 seconds of service.
  7. 7
    Serve immediately with a wide boba straw. The eater stirs everything together at the table, the layering is presentation, not preservation. The proper drink experience is the muscovado-tea-milk blend with chewy pearls coming up the straw in clusters.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion sweetness→extra-strong brown sugar (50%, the chain default)

    Double the syrup painted on the inside of the glass to 80 ml per glass. This is closer to the Tiger Sugar / Xing Fu Tang chain sweetness level. Many home cooks find this overwhelming; restaurant boba aims for 100 percent sugar by default, which is genuinely too sweet.

  • Occasion temperature→hot brown sugar milk tea

    Use warm milk instead of cold, omit the ice, and serve in a heatproof glass or mug. Brew the tea concentrate fresh and keep it warm. The dark stripes still paint up the glass but the visual contrast is gentler, the dish reads more like a cozy winter drink.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • cooked black tapioca pearls → cooked quick-cook supermarket tapioca pearls Quick-cook pearls (the 5-minute kind) have a softer, less chewy bite. Finish them in a brown sugar syrup of 200 g muscovado + 240 ml water reduced 5 minutes to approximate the texture; still inferior but workable.
  • CTC Assam tea → orthodox Assam or English Breakfast Increase to 10 g / 2 tbsp and steep 7 minutes. Whole-leaf teas extract more slowly than CTC.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → cooked black tapioca pearls in brown sugar syrup make it yourself Homemade pearls are fresher, less starchy, and the brown sugar syrup they cook in is dramatically better than the watery muscovado knockoff sold in jars.

Notes

This drink lives or dies on pearl freshness. Boba is a 4-to-6-hour ingredient; everything after that is a compromise. If you cannot make the pearls within that window of the drink, this is not the right drink to make today.

The tiger stripes are functional, not just decorative. The viscous brown sugar syrup clinging to the inside wall provides a concentrated dose of muscovado in every sip, as the straw passes through the syrup-coated wall it picks up extra sweetness. A flat-bottomed-syrup version (just syrup pooled at the bottom) loses this and the drink reads less rich.

Cold milk into warm pearls is the right temperature flow. Hot milk to cold pearls causes the milk-tea emulsion to thicken too much and clings to the pearls instead of flowing around them; the chewy-cold-milk contrast is the bubble tea pleasure. Plan the temperatures: pearls warm, milk cold, tea room-temperature.
