Recipe

Sautéed beet greens with anchovy, garlic, and chili

A bunch of beet greens, stems and leaves separated, cooked in two stages in a wide skillet of olive oil that has first dissolved 3 anchovy fillets and bloomed sliced garlic and red pepper flakes. Stems first for 2 minutes to take the fibrous edge off, leaves on top with their rinse water still clinging so they steam-wilt under a brief lid, then uncovered to drive off moisture. Finished off heat with lemon zest, lemon juice, and a splash of red wine vinegar to bridge the iron-mineral leaves to the bright top end. Built as the green-vegetable counter to the cast-iron chuck eye with shallot pan sauce; the dishes share the skillet rotation and the anchovy stands in for the steak fond on the umami axis.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, prep the greens

  1. 250 g beet greens (the leaves and stems off 2 to 3 medium beets), rinsed in two changes of cold water (sand collects in the leaf folds), spun or shaken until barely damp; stems cut from the leaves at the leaf base, stems chopped into 1 cm pieces, leaves coarsely torn or chopped into 4 to 5 cm strips, kept in two separate piles — (1 large bunch; the typical leafy tops trimmed off 2 to 3 medium beets, weighed after the woody crown is removed)

Phase 2, oil and aromatics

  1. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  2. 9 g oil-cured anchovy fillets, lifted from the tin or jar, oil shaken off, roughly chopped — (3 fillets; the umami builder, will dissolve entirely into the oil and read only as savory depth)
  3. 16 g garlic cloves, sliced thin, peeled and sliced 1 to 2 mm thick along the long axis (sliced rather than minced so the cook can pull them at pale gold rather than over-bloom) — (4 medium cloves; sliced lets you watch the color, minced cooks too fast and goes bitter brown before the leaves are in)
  4. 1 g red pepper flakes
  5. 1 g kosher salt

Phase 3, finish

  1. 2 g fresh lemon zest, zested with a microplane just before use, no white pith
  2. 8 g fresh lemon juice, squeezed at the moment of use
  3. 3 g red wine vinegar
  4. 8 g extra-virgin olive oil, for finishing
  5. 1 g flaky salt (Maldon, Jacobsen, or any large-flake finishing salt) optional — (pinch; the macro-flake hits the tongue first and reads as a sharp top note over the long savory of the anchovy and oil)

Execution

  1. 1
    Wash and stage the greens. Lift the 250 g beet greens into a sink or large bowl of cold water, agitate, lift out (sand sinks); change the water and repeat until the rinse runs clean. Spin or shake the leaves until barely damp, the residual moisture clinging to the leaves is the steam liquor for Phase 5 and should not be towel-dried away. Cut the stems from the leaves at the leaf base. Chop the stems into 1 cm pieces. Coarsely tear or chop the leaves into 4 to 5 cm strips. Hold the stems and leaves in two separate piles; the staging is the entire reason the dish reads as cooked rather than wilted-then-overcooked.
  2. 2
    Heat the oil and dissolve the anchovy. Place a wide skillet (28 to 30 cm / 11 to 12 inches, stainless or carbon steel; cast iron also fine if you have it warm from the steak rest) over medium-low heat. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil and the 9 g / 3 chopped anchovy fillets. Cook, mashing the anchovy against the bottom of the pan with a wooden spoon, for 60 to 90 seconds. The fillets should fully dissolve, no visible fillet shreds remaining, only a uniform pale-bronze oil. Dissolved anchovy reads as deep savory depth; visible chunks read as fishy hot spots, the texture call is the entire flavor call. Blooming aromatics
  3. 3
    Bloom the garlic and chili. Add the 16 g / 4 sliced garlic and the 1 g / ½ tsp red pepper flakes to the anchovy oil. Stir continuously for 45 to 60 seconds. The garlic should turn from raw white to pale gold (not deeper); pull back to lower heat the moment any slice starts to color past gold, browned garlic is bitter and the dish has nowhere to hide it. The red pepper flakes will release their fruit-and-capsaicin volatiles into the oil within the first 30 seconds; the cue is a sharp peppery aroma rising off the pan. Blooming aromatics
  4. 4
    Cook the stems first. Raise the heat to medium-high. Add the chopped stems and the 1 g / ¼ tsp kosher salt. Toss to coat in the aromatic oil. Cook, stirring once or twice, for 2 minutes. The stems should turn from raw-bright magenta to a slightly softened cooked-magenta and lose the snap-resistance of raw stem (a piece bent against the spoon should yield rather than break crisp). The 2-minute window is the calibration: under-cooked stems read fibrous and chewy in a leaf that wilted to nothing; over-cooked stems collapse into stringy mush.
  5. 5
    Pile in the leaves and lid for the steam-wilt. Add the leaves to the pan in handfuls (the volume looks impossibly large, this is normal, the leaves drop to a quarter of their raw volume in 60 seconds). Toss once with tongs to begin draping the leaves over the hot stems and oil. Cover with a tight-fitting lid for 60 to 90 seconds. The residual rinse water clinging to the leaves becomes the steam medium under the lid; the leaves wilt from the top down without scorching from the bottom. Lift the lid; the leaves should be uniformly collapsed and a deep cooked green, with a thin layer of liquor in the bottom of the pan.
  6. 6
    Uncover and finish. Remove the lid and toss the greens vigorously with the tongs over medium-high heat for 30 to 45 seconds. The exposed liquor evaporates and the leaves take on a thin gloss of the anchovy oil. Cut the heat. Stir in the 2 g / 1 tsp lemon zest, 8 g / 1½ tsp lemon juice, and 3 g / ½ tsp red wine vinegar off the heat (lemon zest hit by direct pan heat goes flat-bitter; off-heat preserves the limonene perfume). Toss once to distribute. Taste and adjust: if the greens read flat, more lemon juice; if they read mineral-aggressive, the optional pinch of flaky salt and a second drizzle of olive oil; if they read too rich, more vinegar.
  7. 7
    Plate. Mound the greens on a warmed plate alongside the cast-iron chuck eye and its shallot pan sauce, or onto a serving dish for family-style. Drizzle the 8 g / 2 tsp finishing olive oil over the top. Scatter the optional pinch of flaky salt. Serve immediately; the greens hold their texture for 10 minutes off the heat but the vivid cooked-green color shifts toward olive past that window.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine direction→Cantonese stir-fry with garlic, ginger, and oyster sauce

    Skip the anchovy, lemon, and red wine vinegar. In a smoking wok, heat 20 g / 4 tsp neutral oil; add 12 g / 3 sliced garlic and 6 g / 1 tbsp grated ginger, toss 15 seconds. Stems in for 90 seconds at high heat (the wok hei depends on never letting the pan drop below smoke). Leaves on top, toss 60 seconds, then add 15 g / 1 tbsp oyster sauce, 8 g / 2 tsp Shaoxing wine, and 3 g / 1 tsp light soy sauce. Toss 30 seconds, finish with 3 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil off heat. The dish reads as a Cantonese leafy greens stir-fry; pairs better with a rice-based meal than with the steak.

  • Regional depth and sweetness→agrodolce with pine nuts and currants (Sicilian direction)

    After the leaves are wilted and uncovered, push them to one side of the pan. Add 20 g / 2 tbsp pine nuts and toast in the empty pan space for 60 seconds until pale gold; add 15 g / 1 tbsp currants (or raisins) and 5 g / 1 tsp honey, stir 30 seconds to coat. Toss everything together with the lemon and vinegar. The pine nut crunch and currant sweetness balance the bitter-mineral leaves; this is the Sicilian register that turns the dish into a course of its own rather than a side.

  • Dietary umami builder→parmesan finish (vegetarian)

    Skip the anchovy. Bloom the garlic and chili in the bare olive oil; add an extra pinch of kosher salt to compensate for the lost anchovy salt. Cook through to plate-up unchanged. At the table, grate 25 g / ¼ cup parmigiano reggiano over each plate; the cheese supplies the dissolved-glutamate depth the anchovy would have. Reads vegetarian rather than pescatarian, the dairy a different umami register but a structurally equivalent one.

  • Regional texture→long-braised lathera with tomato and onion (Greek direction)

    Skip the high-heat sauté. Sweat 100 g / 1 small diced yellow onion in the olive oil over medium-low for 8 minutes; add 6 g / 2 sliced garlic, cook 60 seconds. Add 200 g / 1 small can of crushed tomato, 60 g / ¼ cup water, and the chopped stems; simmer 10 minutes. Add the leaves, cover, simmer 20 to 25 minutes, until the greens are fully collapsed and silky. Skip the lemon and vinegar; finish with a glug of finishing olive oil. The dish is now a Greek lathera, served warm or room temperature, eaten with bread to mop the oil-tomato liquor.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • beet greens → Swiss chard, mature spinach, or lacinato kale Swiss chard is the closest substitute by chemistry (same species); use the same weight and add 60 to 90 seconds to the stem cook for thicker chard ribs. Mature spinach wilts faster and reads sweeter, missing the mineral edge; reduce the salt slightly and shorten the leaf wilt to 30 seconds. Lacinato kale needs a 60-second blanch in salted water before it enters the pan, otherwise the leaves stay leathery; once blanched, sauté time matches the recipe.
  • oil-cured anchovy fillets → colatura di alici, white miso, or a dash of fish sauce Colatura (the Italian fermented anchovy liquid) brings the same glutamate at lower volume; use 5 g / 1 tsp added at the end of the bloom rather than dissolved. White miso (15 g / 1 tbsp) added with the lemon at the end is the vegetarian umami stand-in, more sweet than salty. A 5 g / 1 tsp dash of fish sauce works in a pinch but reads more south Asian than Mediterranean and shifts the dish toward a different cuisine.
  • red wine vinegar → sherry vinegar, balsamic vinegar, or extra lemon juice Sherry vinegar is the better swap for cuisine fidelity (Italian and Spanish kitchens both use it). Aged balsamic adds sweetness and depth; use 2 g / ½ tsp rather than 3 g and skip the lemon zest to keep the dish from reading too dark-acidic. Extra lemon juice (a second teaspoon) is the simplest swap when no other vinegar is on hand; the dish loses its second acid axis but stays balanced.
  • red pepper flakes → Calabrian chili paste, Aleppo pepper, or no heat Calabrian chili paste (5 g / 1 tsp) reads brighter and fruitier than dry flakes, melts into the oil rather than floating. Aleppo pepper (2 g / 1 tsp) is mild and slow-warming, fine for cooks who want background heat rather than bite. Skipping the heat entirely is acceptable; the dish reads more directly Italian-canonical without it but loses the second top note over the lemon.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → oil-cured anchovy fillets make it yourself Salt-curing your own anchovies (or the closely related sardine) takes 30 days of nearly zero active work and produces a fillet with cleaner cured-fish flavor and less olive oil interference than the commercial midrange tin. The cost difference is meaningful when you go through anchovies regularly across Caesar dressings, pasta sauces, and dishes like this; the flavor difference is meaningful for whole-fillet plate applications.
  • → red wine vinegar make it yourself A jar of half-finished red wine plus a mother of vinegar produces a custom red wine vinegar over 4 to 8 weeks of countertop fermentation. The acid is sharper and more wine-character-forward than commercial vinegars (which are typically blends of cheaper bulk wine), and matching the vinegar variety to the steak's pan sauce wine variety is a small but real bridge between the side and the main.

Notes

Why this dish for the cast-iron chuck eye. The steak runs rich, with three layers of butterfat (the butter-baste, the rendered marbling, the cold-butter-mounted pan sauce) and the long sweet umami of the reduced wine and shallot. A side has to read as the structural counter to that, not as a parallel layer of richness. Beet greens deliver the bitter-mineral leaf and the iron-vegetable depth; the lemon and red wine vinegar deliver the second and third acid axes that the steak''s sauce only touches once (the wine reduction). The two plates eat as a single dish: a forkful of steak, a fork of greens, the sauce spilling between them, and the lemon-vinegar lift cuts through the butterfat coating on the palate so the next bite of steak lands clean rather than building toward palate fatigue.

Why anchovy and not parmesan as the umami builder. Either works on the chemistry; both supply free glutamate at the levels needed to register as savory depth without reading as the source ingredient. The anchovy choice is structural to the dish-with-steak pairing: anchovy melts into the cooking oil and reads as a continuous savory base under the leaves, parallel to how the seared fond reads as the savory base under the steak''s sauce. Parmesan finishing the plate adds a layer of dairy that, on top of the steak''s butter-mounted sauce, becomes redundant. The vegetarian modification is parmesan; for the steak pairing, anchovy is the better call.

Why the stem-and-leaf staging matters. Beet green stems are the same plant as the leaves but a different texture entirely. Cooked together for the same duration the leaves over-wilt to mush before the stems read as cooked, or the stems stay fibrous in a leaf that has already collapsed. The 2 minutes of stem-only cooking under the aromatic oil is the calibration that lets the leaves enter the pan when the stems are exactly tender, and the 60-to-90-second steam-wilt under the lid finishes the leaves without taking the stems past the cooked-tender point. Skipping the staging produces a side that reads as either uncooked-stems-with-overcooked-leaves or as a uniform stewy collapse, neither of which sits cleanly next to the steak.

Why the rinse water matters as steam liquor. The wet greens going into the covered hot pan generate a quick burst of steam that wilts the leaves uniformly from the top down; a fully dried bunch of greens hits the pan and either scorches on the bottom layer before the top wilts (uncovered) or produces no steam to wilt with (covered). The instruction to spin or shake the greens to "barely damp" rather than dry is structural, not casual. The leaves should look like they were just rinsed and shaken once, water beading on the surface and dripping when the leaves are held up.

Substitution notes for the steak pairing specifically. The Cantonese stir-fry modification eats well as a standalone side with rice but does not pair with the cast-iron chuck eye; the soy and sesame sit at a different umami register than the wine-fond pan sauce and the two dishes compete rather than support each other. The Sicilian agrodolce direction (with pine nuts and currants) pairs well but turns the side into a more substantial course; halve the steak portion if you serve it that way. The Greek lathera modification pairs well with a roast lamb or a slow-braised dish but is too soft and tomato-forward for the high-heat steak. For the chuck eye specifically, this baseline recipe (anchovy, garlic, chili, lemon, vinegar) is the right call.

Time-and-pan logistics with the steak. The steak''s rest is 5 to 7 minutes; the pan sauce takes another 6 minutes after that. The greens cook in 7 minutes from oil-in-pan to plate. Run them in a second skillet (a 28 cm stainless or carbon steel) starting the moment the steaks come off the heat for the rest, finishing at the same moment the pan sauce hits the cold-butter mount. If you only have one pan, run the greens first while the steaks finish their dry-brine temper (the 45 to 60 minutes at room temperature), hold them warm in the oven at 80 °C with the lemon and vinegar already added, and run the steak and the pan sauce after. The greens reheat poorly; cook them last in a parallel pan if at all possible.
