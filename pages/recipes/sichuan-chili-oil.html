Sichuan chili oil

Aromatics infused into hot oil at three precise temperatures, then poured over coarse chili flakes. The crackle is the technique. The result has structure store-bought never does — three layers of red, with sediment that earns its bowl.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The active cook is 35 minutes, but the oil needs a 24-hour rest at room temperature before first use so the chili flakes fully infuse and the aromatic compounds integrate. Make it the day before you need it.
  • Use a probe thermometer for the three-temperature pour. Hitting 220 °C, 180 °C, then 150 °C precisely is the entire technique; eyeballing the smoke produces either burnt chili (above 230 °C) or under-extracted color (below 200 °C). The wrong temperature is the difference between Chongqing chili oil and chili-tinted oil.
  • Source both coarse and fine chili flakes (èrjīngtiáo or Korean gochugaru for the coarse; fine Sichuan or chili powder for the fine). The two-grind split is structural: coarse provides texture and color, fine provides the deep red base and quick heat. A single grade produces a one-dimensional oil.
  • Have a heat-proof jar or bowl (500 ml ceramic or glass) ready and stable on the counter. The oil pour at 220 °C is violent and immediate; a jar tipped over mid-pour is a kitchen emergency.

Execution

  1. 1
    Combine 60 g / ½ cup coarse chili flakes, 30 g / ¼ cup fine chili flakes, 12 g / 2 tbsp toasted sesame seeds, and 4 g / ½ tsp kosher salt in a wide heatproof bowl (metal or thick ceramic; the oil will hit 180°C and you don't want it to crack). Set near the stove with a spider or sieve at hand. The pour happens fast.
  2. 2
    In a heavy saucepan, combine 500 ml / 2 cups neutral oil with 4 star anise pods, 1 cassia or cinnamon stick, 8 g / 1 tbsp Sichuan peppercorns, 3 bay leaves, 30 g / 2-inch knob sliced fresh ginger, 4 smacked scallions, and 1 sliced shallot if using. Place over medium-low heat.
  3. 3
    Bring the oil up slowly. The aromatics should sizzle gently around the edges but never fry hard; you're extracting volatile aromatics, not browning them. This takes 10–12 minutes. If anything turns dark brown, the heat is too high; pull off, let it cool 30 seconds, return on lower heat.
  4. 4
    When the 4 scallions are limp and amber-edged and the oil smells deeply spiced (around 130°C / 265°F if you're using a thermometer), strain the aromatics out through a sieve into a heatproof measuring cup. Discard the solids; they've given everything they had. Blooming aromatics
  5. 5
    Return the strained oil to the saucepan, raise the heat to medium, and bring it to ~190°C (375°F). The visual cue: a wooden chopstick dipped in immediately produces vigorous bubbles around it. Pull off heat.
  6. 6
    Let the oil cool for 60–90 seconds, just until the surface stops shimmering aggressively. Target temperature: ~175°C (350°F). Too hot and you scorch the chili flakes black; too cool and you don't bloom the capsaicin and red pigments.
  7. 7
    The pour: in three stages, ladle one-third of the hot oil over the chili-flake bowl. The mixture will foam and crackle violently; this is correct. Stir with a wooden spoon between each pour. The first pour does the deep extraction; the second sets the color; the third (cooler) preserves the surface flakes from burning.
  8. 8
    Once all the oil is in, stir thoroughly. The bowl should look like a vivid red slurry with crackling settling to a low fizz. Let it cool to room temperature, uncovered, on the counter, 60 to 90 minutes. Do not refrigerate while warm; condensation introduces water and water shortens shelf life.
  9. 9
    Transfer everything (oil + sediments) to a clean glass jar. Cover. Let it rest at room temperature for at least 24 hours before using. The oil deepens and the sediment settles into three visible bands: dark red flakes at the bottom, mid-red infused oil in the middle, light red oil-only at the top.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • coarse Sichuan chili flakes (èrjīngtiáo) gochugaru

    The Korean equivalent: same coarse cut, slightly less smoky, more vegetal sweetness. Easier to find in the West.

  • cassia bark cinnamon stick

    Cassia is sweeter and less sharp than true Ceylon cinnamon, which is what most American "cinnamon" actually is. Either works; cassia is more authentic.

  • rice bran or peanut oil avocado oil

    Higher smoke point (270°C), neutral flavor; works fine for this. Avoid olive oil; the flavor profile fights the spices.

Notes

The three temperatures do three different jobs: the slow infusion (130°C) extracts fat-soluble aromatics (the volatile oils in star anise, cassia, Sichuan peppercorn) without breaking them down. The high pour (175°C) hydrolyzes the chili pigments into the oil and triggers the Maillard reaction on the flake surfaces, giving you the deep red rather than the flat orange of a single-temperature oil. Skip the staging and you get either weak, flat oil or burnt, bitter oil.

Why two grinds of chili: the fine flakes/powder dissolve into the oil and color it deep red instantly. The coarse flakes hold their shape, give you the sediment to spoon out for noodles and dumpling sauce, and provide a slow-release bite as they continue to infuse over the next week. One grind only gives you one register of heat.

Why no garlic: garlic goes acrid above 140°C and bitter below 110°C. The temperature window for clean garlic infusion is narrow, and chili oil's three-temperature staging doesn''t respect it. If you want garlic, fry it separately in cooler oil (110°C) and add it to the bowl just before the first hot pour.

Why salt at the end with the chili flakes, not in the oil: salt is hygroscopic; it pulls moisture and competes with the oil for the chili surfaces. It belongs in the dry mix where it can season the flakes directly as the hot oil hits.

The three-band visual is the test: a properly made chili oil settles into clearly visible layers within 24 hours. Top oil for stir-fries and scallion-oil noodles where you don't want sediment; middle oil for vinaigrettes; bottom sediment-rich scoops for dan dan, mapo tofu, dumpling dips.

Storage: room temperature, sealed, dark cupboard. 3 months easily. The oil deepens and rounds in the first 2 weeks; after a month, the heat plateaus and only flavors slowly. Refrigeration is unnecessary and clouds the oil. Do not introduce water (wet spoon, condensation lid); that's what spoils chili oil.
