Korean pajeon dipping sauce (cho-ganjang, 초간장)

Soy sauce, rice vinegar, gochugaru, scallion, sesame seeds, and a touch of sugar. The acid-soy condiment that cuts the fried-batter richness of pajeon, haemul pajeon, kimchi-jeon, and most jeon. Built in 3 minutes; should rest 10 minutes for the gochugaru to bloom into the soy and the vinegar to integrate.

Base

  1. 45 ml light soy sauce
  2. 22 ml rice vinegar
  3. 3 g granulated sugar

Aromatic

  1. 2 g gochugaru (Korean chili flakes, coarse)
  2. 8 g scallion, green tops only, thinly sliced — (1 stalk green top; for fresh allium lift)
  3. 3 g garlic, finely grated optional — (½ small clove; optional but customary, adds raw pungency)
  4. 2 g toasted sesame seeds
  5. 2 ml toasted sesame oil

Execution

  1. 1
    In a small bowl, whisk together the 45 ml / 3 tbsp light soy sauce, 22 ml / 1½ tbsp rice vinegar, and 3 g / ¾ tsp granulated sugar until the sugar fully dissolves (15 seconds of vigorous whisking).
  2. 2
    Add the 2 g / ¾ tsp gochugaru, 8 g / 1 stalk thinly sliced scallion green, the 3 g / ½ clove finely grated garlic if using, 2 g / ½ tsp toasted sesame seeds, and 2 ml / ½ tsp toasted sesame oil. Stir gently to combine; do not whisk hard, the goal is to suspend the solids in the liquid, not to emulsify.
  3. 3
    Let the sauce rest at room temperature for 10 minutes before serving. The gochugaru hydrates and blooms color and gentle heat into the soy; the alliums soften and integrate; the vinegar acid rounds out. Past 30 minutes the scallion starts to lose color; the sauce is best within an hour of mixing. Blooming aromatics

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Dietary sweetness→sugar-free version

    Omit the sugar entirely. The sauce reads slightly sharper and more acid-forward; appropriate for richer fillings (kimchi-jeon, beef jeon) where the sugar is not needed.

  • Heat heat→mild version, no chili

    Omit the gochugaru. The sauce reads cleaner and more soy-forward; the canonical mandu (Korean dumpling) dipping sauce uses this version.

  • Occasion depth→with dashi

    Replace 15 ml of the soy sauce with 15 ml of dashi (kombu-only is fine for vegan, kombu-katsuobushi for omnivore). The sauce gains umami depth and softens slightly; particularly good with pajeon containing seafood, where the dashi echoes the marine flavor of the protein.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • light soy sauce → Japanese koikuchi shoyu or any standard soy sauce Korean ganjang and Japanese koikuchi are nearly interchangeable here. Avoid dark soy sauce (too sweet, too molasses-forward, wrong register).
  • rice vinegar → apple cider vinegar at 80% the amount Apple cider vinegar is slightly stronger and fruitier; reduce to 18 ml. White wine vinegar also works at the same ratio.
  • gochugaru → Aleppo pepper or mild Korean gochujang (1 tsp) Aleppo pepper has similar coarse texture and gentle heat; gochujang adds body and umami at the cost of clarity. The flake form is the canonical choice.

Notes

The umami source is the soy sauce (light soy contributes free glutamate and the fermented amino acid backbone), with secondary glutamate from the gochugaru. The finishing acid is the rice vinegar, which is non-negotiable; the sauce reads heavy and dimensional without it. The sesame oil at 2 ml is a quarter of what most cookbook recipes call for; more than this and the sesame dominates the soy-vinegar register and the sauce loses its cutting clarity.

The 10-minute rest is functional, not decorative. Gochugaru flakes are coarse and dry; immediate use leaves them gritty and the heat unevenly distributed. 10 minutes in the soy is enough for the flakes to absorb liquid, soften to a pliable texture, and bloom red color into the sauce; this is the same blooming mechanism that defines proper kimchi pastes and gochujang dressings. The 30-minute upper limit is the scallion: green tops oxidize and lose their bright herbal lift past 30 minutes in acid, going dull-green and soft. Mix the sauce 10 minutes before serving, use within the hour.

Scaling. The 80 ml yield is sized for one large 28 to 30 cm haemul pajeon serving 4. Scale linearly; the 3:1.5:1 ratio of soy to vinegar to (gochugaru + aromatics) holds across all batch sizes. For a large dinner with multiple jeon, multiply by the number of pancakes; the sauce keeps for the duration of one meal (no overnight hold without quality loss).

Sources
  • Korean banchan tradition — Cho-ganjang ("vinegared soy") is the canonical jeon dipping sauce, a one-bowl quick condiment built fresh and used within the hour. The same grammar produces dipping sauces for mandu, jjim, and other fried items; the gochugaru and sesame components are pajeon-specific refinements.
