Polish żurek

The Polish sour rye soup: rye fermented for 4 to 5 days with cold water and garlic to produce zakwas (a tangy lacto-fermented rye liquid), then cooked into a soup with smoked Polish sausage (kiełbasa biała or wiejska), bacon, onion, mushrooms, marjoram, garlic, and bay. Served with a hard-boiled egg halved on top and accompanied by chunks of dark rye bread. The structural Polish Easter Sunday breakfast and Lent-ending soup.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The zakwas (rye sour starter) needs 4 to 5 days of room-temperature fermentation. Plan a week ahead. The starter is straightforward (rye flour, garlic, cold water, room temperature) but cannot be rushed; the lacto-fermentation produces the structural tang that defines żurek. Bottled żurek starter is sold at Polish groceries (Krakus or Smak brand) if you cannot wait the week.
  • The biała kiełbasa (white sausage) is the Easter canonical preparation; it must be poached gently (not boiled, which bursts the casings) for 25 minutes before being added to the soup. Outside Easter, smoked wiejska kielbasa (the everyday Polish smoked sausage) can be sliced and added directly without pre-poaching. Both versions are structurally correct żurek.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the zakwas (rye sour starter, 4 to 5 days ahead)

  1. 100 g rye flour (medium or dark rye, not pumpernickel)
  2. 800 ml cold water (filtered, non-chlorinated)
  3. 15 g garlic cloves, smashed — (4 cloves)
  4. 1 each bay leaf

Phase 2, the soup base

  1. 120 g thick-cut bacon, diced, diced into 1 cm cubes
  2. 200 g yellow onion, fine dice — (1 large)
  3. 200 g cremini or button mushrooms, sliced, sliced 5 mm thick — (about 8 mushrooms; the structural umami contributor)
  4. 15 g garlic, minced — (4 cloves)
  5. 1200 ml beef or chicken stock (homemade preferred)
  6. 2 each bay leaves
  7. 5 g dried marjoram
  8. 2 g black pepper, cracked

Phase 3, the sausage

  1. 400 g biała kiełbasa (Polish white sausage, fresh and uncooked), whole links; will be poached separately before slicing into the soup — (2 links; structural Easter version; substitute wiejska smoked kielbasa, sliced and added directly without pre-poach)

Phase 4, the finish

  1. 80 g sour cream, tempered with hot soup before adding (see step)
  2. 3 each hard-boiled eggs, 3 large eggs, hard-boiled, peeled, halved lengthwise — (½ egg per serving; the structural Easter symbol on top of each bowl)
  3. 10 g fresh marjoram or parsley (for garnish) — (small handful)

Execution

  1. 1
    4 to 5 days ahead, make the zakwas. In a clean 1 L glass jar, combine the 100 g rye flour, 800 ml cold filtered water, 15 g smashed garlic, and 1 bay leaf. Stir vigorously until no flour lumps remain. Cover with a piece of cheesecloth or a loose lid (the ferment needs air exchange). Let stand at room temperature (20 to 22 °C / 68 to 72 °F) for 4 to 5 days. Stir once daily. The mixture will separate into a milky liquid on top and a paste at the bottom; this is normal. By day 4 or 5 the zakwas should smell aggressively sour-tangy, fizzy, like sourdough starter; taste a drop, it should pucker the mouth.5 d 0 h
  2. 2
    Day of soup: stir the zakwas vigorously to recombine the liquid and paste. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a measuring cup, pressing the rye paste to extract liquid; discard the rye solids and the garlic and bay. You should have about 600 ml of strained zakwas; reserve.
  3. 3
    Pre-cook the biała kiełbasa (Easter version only). Place the 400 g fresh white sausage links in a wide saucepan; cover with cold water. Bring to 75 to 80 °C / 165 to 175 °F (a bare simmer, single bubbles rising lazily); poach 25 minutes. Lift out with tongs; cool slightly. Slice into 1.5 cm rounds. Reserve. Skip this step if using pre-smoked wiejska kielbasa.
  4. 4
    In a large pot or Dutch oven over medium-high heat, render the 120 g diced bacon for 4 to 5 minutes until crispy with rendered fat. Add the 200 g diced onion; sauté 4 to 5 minutes until softened. Add the 200 g sliced mushrooms; cook 6 minutes until the mushrooms have released their water and started to brown. Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Add the 15 g / 4 cloves minced garlic and 5 g / 1 tbsp marjoram; cook 60 seconds until fragrant. Blooming aromatics
  6. 6
    Pour in the 1200 ml / 5 cups stock, the 600 ml zakwas, the 2 bay leaves, and the 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper. Bring to a steady simmer; reduce heat to medium-low. Simmer 15 minutes for the flavors to integrate.
  7. 7
    Add the sliced sausage (the poached biała kiełbasa, or the wiejska smoked kielbasa); simmer 5 more minutes to warm through.
  8. 8
    Temper the sour cream. Ladle about 200 ml of the hot soup into a small bowl with the 80 g sour cream; whisk smooth to bring the cream up to soup temperature (adding cold sour cream directly to hot soup causes it to curdle into white specks). Pour the tempered cream back into the soup; stir gently to integrate. Do not let the soup boil after adding the cream; it can still curdle if cranked.
  9. 9
    Taste; adjust salt (the bacon and sausage contribute most of the salt; rarely needs more). Fish out the bay leaves.
  10. 10
    Ladle the soup into wide bowls. Top each bowl with ½ a hard-boiled egg (cut-side up) and a sprinkle of fresh marjoram or parsley. Serve with thick slices of dark rye bread on the side for sopping. The egg on top is the structural Easter signal; many Polish homes serve the soup in a hollowed-out round bread loaf as the bowl, with the soup ladled inside.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as Polish barszcz biały (white borscht, the structural sister soup)

    Replace the zakwas with the equivalent volume of żur (a finer-grain wheat sour starter; same fermentation method, wheat flour instead of rye). Add 200 g diced cooked potato to the soup along with the sausage; this gives barszcz biały its potato-and-sour profile. Skip the mushrooms. The dish is the structural Polish white borscht; sister to żurek, distinct soup.

  • Regional occasion→as żurek staropolski (the old-Polish version with more aromatics)

    Add 30 g grated horseradish (fresh, not jarred) to the soup at step 6. Add 10 g fresh dill at the finish in place of the marjoram. The "old Polish" version reads more aggressively aromatic and adds a horseradish bite that some Polish households consider essential.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • homemade zakwas → bottled żurek starter (Krakus or Smak brand, sold at Polish groceries; about 500 ml per bottle, use the whole bottle) The bottled product is acceptable and used by most Polish-American home cooks; the flavor is slightly less complex than homemade but functionally fine.
  • biała kiełbasa (Polish white sausage) → smoked Polish wiejska kielbasa, or any smoked Polish sausage Smoked wiejska is the everyday Polish substitute and adds smoke to the soup. Bratwurst is the wrong flavor profile (too mild, no smoke).
  • rye flour (medium or dark rye) → whole-wheat flour (acceptable but the ferment reads less Polish) Rye is structural for żurek; the rye sourdough flavor is the canonical Polish profile. Whole wheat starter produces a soup that reads more like a sour wheat soup than like żurek.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → beef or chicken stock make it yourself Real homemade stock is dramatically better than commercial; the soup body reads meaningfully more layered.

Notes

Why the zakwas. The structural defining element of żurek is the rye sour starter; without it the soup is just a smoky bacon-and-sausage broth. The 4 to 5 day room-temperature ferment of rye flour with garlic and cold water cultivates wild lactobacillus (native to the rye grain) that produces lactic acid, slight CO2, and the complex sour-tangy flavor profile that is structurally Polish. Polish homes traditionally have a jar of zakwas in the fridge year-round, refreshed with new flour and water as it depletes.

Why marjoram. Marjoram (Origanum majorana, sweeter and milder than oregano) is the structural Polish soup herb; it appears in żurek, in flaki (tripe soup), in mushroom soups, and in many Polish meat dishes. The flavor is gentler and more floral than oregano; substituting oregano produces a soup that reads off (more Italian than Polish).

Why temper the sour cream. Polish soups are finished with sour cream for body and to round the acidity. Adding cold sour cream directly to hot soup curdles it into white specks; tempering (whisking some hot soup into the cream first to bring its temperature up gradually) prevents the curdle. This is the universal rule for cream-finished European soups.

The Easter egg on top. The halved hard-boiled egg is the structural Easter symbol (egg = rebirth, resurrection) and the structural finish for żurek served on Easter Sunday morning. The egg is also genuinely delicious in the soup (richness on top of sour-smoky depth). Year-round żurek often skips the egg; Easter żurek never does.

Pairings. Canonical with dark rye bread (the seedy Polish razowy chleb), or in a bowl made from a hollowed-out round loaf of bread (a Polish presentation flourish at Easter). A glass of cold vodka (Żubrówka or Belvedere) on the side for the holiday version.

Storage. Keeps 4 days refrigerated; the soup tastes better on day 2 as the flavors integrate. Reheat gently over low heat (do not boil after the cream is added). Freezes well; store without the sour cream (add fresh tempered cream to each reheated portion).

Sources
  • Polish Easter Sunday and Lent-ending canon — Żurek (full name "żur") is the structural Polish sour rye soup, served traditionally on Easter Sunday morning (the meal that breaks the 40-day Lent fast) and as a year-round Polish home dish. The defining elements are: zakwas, the lacto-fermented rye liquid (rye flour, garlic, cold water, 4 to 5 days fermentation); smoked Polish sausage (biała kielbasa, the unsmoked white sausage, is the Easter canonical; wiejska or other smoked kielbasa is the year-round substitute); bacon and onion for the structural soup base; marjoram (the structural Polish soup herb); a halved hard-boiled egg per bowl; and dark rye bread on the side. The soup reads tangy, smoky, slightly creamy, comforting.
