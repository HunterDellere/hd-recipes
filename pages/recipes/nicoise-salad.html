Salade niçoise

Oil-poached tuna, blanched haricots verts, jammy egg, salt-cured tomato, and nicoise olives over an anchovy-dijon vinaigrette. Composed, not tossed; every component salted on its own terms.

Execution

  1. 1
    Pull the 400 g / 14 oz tuna loin from the fridge 30 minutes before cooking and rub it with the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt. The salt initiates surface cure while the loin warms toward 18 °C; an ice-cold loin entering 60 °C oil drops the bath temperature past the target window. Dry brining
  2. 2
    In a small saucepan that fits the loin snugly, combine the 500 g / 2 cups extra-virgin olive oil, the 2 bay leaves, the 4 g lemon zest strips, and the 2 smashed garlic cloves. Bring the oil to 60 °C / 140 °F over low heat, monitoring with an instant-read thermometer. The aromatics infuse during the climb.
  3. 3
    Lower the tuna into the oil. Hold the bath at 60 °C / 140 °F for 12 minutes for rare-medium centre (still translucent, lightly flaking). The protein denatures at this temperature without driving off the oil-soluble glutamates that read as savory depth at room temperature. Lift the loin onto a rack, reserve the oil for cooking another day. Oil poaching
  4. 4
    While the tuna poaches, set a small saucepan of water to a hard boil. Lower the 4 fridge-cold eggs in with a slotted spoon, set a timer for 6 minutes 30 seconds. The fixed water mass and the cold-egg start make the timer reproducible; deviating by 30 seconds shifts you from jammy to set yolk. Jammy egg
  5. 5
    When the timer hits, transfer the eggs straight into an ice bath. Hold 5 minutes; the cold shock arrests carryover and contracts the white away from the shell, which is what makes peeling clean. Ice bath shock
  6. 6
    Bring the seasoned 2 L water with the 20 g / 2 tbsp kosher salt to a gentle boil. Add the 400 g / 14 oz waxy potatoes whole; reduce to a tremor. Cook 14 to 18 minutes until a paring knife meets the centre with no resistance. Cooking gently (not at a hard boil) keeps the skins intact and the cells from rupturing into chalky mashed-potato territory.
  7. 7
    Drain the potatoes and let them dry on the rack 5 minutes; cut larger ones into halves while the surface starch is still warm and tacky, which lets a spoonful of the vinaigrette grip later.
  8. 8
    In a wide pot, bring the 3 L water with the 30 g / 3 tbsp kosher salt to a hard rolling boil. Drop the 250 g / 9 oz haricots verts; cook 90 seconds for crisp-tender centre. Brief contact with vigorously boiling salted water sets the chlorophyll without giving the cell walls time to break down.
  9. 9
    Lift the 250 g / 9 oz haricots verts directly into the same ice bath the eggs vacated. Hold 2 minutes, then drain on a towel. The shock locks the chlorophyll green; without it, residual heat shifts the beans toward gray-olive within 10 minutes. Ice bath shock
  10. 10
    Toss the 400 g / 14 oz tomato wedges with the 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt in a colander. Let them drain 15 minutes. The salt pulls cellular water out and concentrates glutamate; without this step the tomato weeps watery juice into the salad and dilutes the vinaigrette.
  11. 11
    Build the vinaigrette in a small bowl. Whisk the 12 g / 4 minced anchovy fillets, 15 g / 1 tbsp dijon mustard, 25 g grated shallot, 30 g / 2 tbsp red wine vinegar, and 10 g / 2 tsp lemon juice until the anchovy fully dissolves into the acid. Stream in the 90 g / 6 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil while whisking; the mustard provides the surface-active agents that hold the temporary emulsion. Crack in the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. Vinaigrette emulsion
  12. 12
    Slice the rested tuna across the grain into 1 cm / ⅜-inch slabs. Peel the eggs and halve them lengthwise — the yolk should bead at the cut, not run.
  13. 13
    Compose, do not toss. On a wide platter, lay the warm potatoes, the haricots verts, and the drained tomato wedges in distinct piles. Tuck the tuna slabs and halved eggs alongside. Scatter the 80 g nicoise olives, the 15 g / 1 tbsp capers, and the 10 g basil. Spoon the vinaigrette over each pile so the dressing reads on each component, not pooled at the bottom of the platter.
  14. 14
    Taste at the table. The dish wants to land room-temperature, salty from anchovy and capers, acidic from vinegar and lemon, with the tuna and yolk providing the only soft notes against the snap of the beans and the give of the potatoes. If a single component reads bland, salt that component directly with a 1 g / pinch of fine sea salt rather than adding more vinaigrette.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→jarred oil-packed tuna in olive oil (high-quality Spanish ventresca or Italian tonno)

    Skip Phase 1 entirely. Drain 250 g / 9 oz of jarred ventresca, break into chunks at the platter. Loses the warm-rare centre but gains the buttery cured-belly fat profile that some cooks prefer. Use Ortiz, Conservas Cambados, or Tonnino brands; cheap supermarket oil-pack is not the same product.

  • Regional cuisine→salade niçoise puriste (no cooked vegetables)

    Drop Phases 3, 4, and 7 potatoes/beans. Replace with raw spring vegetables: shaved fennel, raw artichoke heart, radish, broad beans pinched from their pods. This is the Comité de la Cuisine Niçoise definition; the cooked-vegetable version is a 20th-century French-restaurant adaptation.

  • Texture protein→seared tuna in place of oil-poached

    Skip Phase 1. Pat the salted loin dry, sear 45 seconds per face in a 250 °C cast-iron pan with neutral oil, rest 4 minutes, slice. Faster than oil poaching but loses the gentle-curve protein denaturation; the centre is raw rather than uniformly translucent. The Maillard crust adds savory depth that the oil-poached version does not have.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • haricots verts → standard green beans, blanched 2 min instead of 90 sec Thicker walls take longer to set the chlorophyll. Texturally less elegant but still right.
  • red wine vinegar → sherry vinegar Slightly nutty, more complex; a half-step away from canon but valid in Provence.
  • shallot → red onion, finely diced and rinsed under cold water Rinsing strips the most aggressive sulfur compounds so the onion does not dominate the dressing.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → dijon mustard make it yourself Scratch dijon is sharper, more aromatic, and lets you control the heat curve; commodity dijon is calibrated to be inoffensive across a year on the shelf.
  • → oil-cured anchovy fillets make it yourself Brining and oil-curing your own anchovies (when fresh whole anchovies are available) yields a softer texture and a less aggressive cure than commercial tins.
  • → salt-packed capers make it yourself Salt-packing your own capers (when fresh nasturtium buds or true capparis spinosa flower buds are available) gives crisper bite than the brined-jar version.
  • → red wine vinegar make it yourself Live mother of vinegar in a jar of leftover red wine produces vinegar with floral and tannic complexity that pasteurized commercial vinegar lacks.

Notes

The structural argument: niçoise is a salting curriculum disguised as a salad. The tuna gets a dry brine, the potatoes get a heavily salted boil, the haricots verts get a heavily salted blanch, the tomatoes get a 15-minute salt cure, and the vinaigrette carries anchovy-and-caper salt. Each component arrives at the platter pre-seasoned to its appropriate level for its own texture; the vinaigrette is finishing acid, not seasoning rescue.

Why oil-poach the tuna at 60 °C: myosin denatures around 50 °C and actin around 66 °C; staying between drives a uniform translucent-pink centre with intact moisture. Higher and the loin reads chalky and dry; lower and the cure is perceptibly raw. Oil is the heat-transfer medium because it is gentler than water and because the oil-soluble flavor compounds infuse rather than diffuse out.

No cream, no mayonnaise, no mustard-cream emulsion. The dressing is a temporary vinaigrette held by the dijon's mucilage and the anchovy's surface-active proteins; it will break in the bowl after 20 minutes, which is correct. Spoon over individual components rather than tossing.

Plate room-temperature, not chilled. Cold tomato is bland; cold tuna is leathery; cold potato is starchy. Composed at 18 to 20 °C, every component reads at its volatility peak.
