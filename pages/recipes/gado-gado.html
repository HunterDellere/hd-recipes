Gado-gado (Indonesian salad with peanut sauce)

A composed salad of blanched vegetables (long bean, cabbage, bean sprouts, potato, carrot), hard-boiled egg, fried tempeh and tofu, and steamed lontong rice cake, all bound at the table by a thick peanut sauce built from roasted peanuts, palm sugar, tamarind, kecap manis, lime, and red chili. The structural Indonesian everyday composed-salad dish; literally "mix-mix," referring to the diner mixing the components together with the sauce at the table.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Lontong (steamed rice cake) takes 2 hours unattended; start it first or buy pre-made from an Indonesian grocery. The recipe below uses a quick rice option (steamed jasmine rice molded and cooled) as the accessible substitute; lontong is structurally better but adds significant time.
  • Toast the peanuts before grinding them for the sauce. Pre-roasted peanuts from a jar are acceptable but the dish reads significantly more aromatic with freshly dry-roasted raw peanuts (8 to 10 minutes in a 175°C / 350°F oven until golden and intensely aromatic).

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, the peanut sauce

  1. 200 g raw peanuts (skin off, unsalted)
  2. 30 g neutral oil (for frying the shallot-garlic-chili paste)
  3. 60 g shallots, peeled — (3 medium shallots; the structural Indonesian aromatic base)
  4. 15 g garlic — (4 cloves)
  5. 10 g red chili (Thai bird or Fresno), seeded for less heat — (2 chilies; adjust by preference, Indonesian gado-gado is traditionally mildly spicy not aggressively hot)
  6. 40 g palm sugar (gula jawa), grated, or dark brown sugar
  7. 15 g tamarind concentrate (or 30 g tamarind pulp soaked in 60 ml hot water and strained)
  8. 30 g kecap manis (sweet Indonesian soy sauce)
  9. 2 each kaffir lime leaves (or 1 tbsp lime zest) — (2 leaves, finely shredded after removing the central rib)
  10. 30 ml fresh lime juice
  11. 4 g fine sea salt
  12. 250 ml hot water (for thinning the sauce to spoonable consistency)

Phase 2, the protein add-ons

  1. 200 g firm tofu, cut into 2 cm cubes — (½ block; fried until golden)
  2. 200 g tempeh, cut into 1 cm thick slabs — (½ block; fried until golden and crisp at the edges; the canonical Indonesian fermented soybean protein, structurally important)
  3. 200 ml neutral oil (for frying tofu and tempeh)
  4. 4 each large eggs — (4 large; hard-boiled and halved)

Phase 3, the vegetables (blanched separately, each to the right point)

  1. 200 g long beans or green beans, cut into 5 cm pieces
  2. 200 g napa cabbage, cut into 5 cm chunks
  3. 150 g mung bean sprouts
  4. 250 g waxy potatoes, peeled and cut into 2 cm cubes — (2 medium; boiled 12 minutes until fork-tender)
  5. 150 g carrots, cut into matchsticks — (2 medium; blanched 90 seconds)
  6. 200 g cucumber, sliced into rounds — (1 medium; raw, the only un-blanched vegetable)

Phase 4, the rice cake (lontong or quick substitute)

  1. 200 g jasmine rice (for quick lontong substitute), or 200 g pre-made lontong

Phase 5, the garnish

  1. 40 g krupuk (Indonesian shrimp or rice crackers, store-bought) — (small handful; the structural crunch element)
  2. 20 g fried shallots (store-bought or homemade)

Execution

  1. 1
    Toast the 200 g raw peanuts. Spread on a sheet pan; bake at 175°C / 350°F for 10 to 12 minutes, shaking the pan once at the halfway mark, until pale golden and intensely aromatic. Cool fully (about 10 minutes).
  2. 2
    Build the peanut sauce. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide skillet over medium heat. In a small food processor or mortar, pound the 60 g shallots, 15 g garlic, and 10 g red chili into a smooth paste. Add the paste to the hot oil; stir-fry 4 to 5 minutes until the moisture has cooked off, the paste has darkened slightly, and the smell has gone from raw to deeply aromatic. Blooming aromatics
  3. 3
    Grind the toasted peanuts. Transfer the cooled peanuts to the food processor; pulse 8 to 12 times into a coarse-ground texture (like coarse breadcrumbs; not yet peanut butter). Add the ground peanuts to the skillet with the bloomed paste. Add the 40 g palm sugar, 15 g tamarind, 30 g kecap manis, 2 finely shredded kaffir lime leaves, and 4 g fine sea salt. Stir to integrate.
  4. 4
    Loosen the sauce. Gradually add the 250 ml / 1 cup hot water, stirring constantly, until the sauce reaches a thick spoonable consistency, like loose peanut butter (the cook should be able to ribbon the sauce over the salad without it pooling flat). Simmer 3 to 4 minutes; the sauce will thicken further as the peanuts hydrate. Add the 30 ml / 2 tbsp lime juice off heat. Taste; adjust salt and sugar. The sauce should be sweet-savory-spicy-tangy in balance; if flat, add more lime or tamarind; if too sweet, add more salt or tamarind; if too thick, add more hot water. Reserve covered.
  5. 5
    Hard-boil the 4 eggs. Cover with cold water in a small saucepan; bring to a simmer; cook 9 minutes; transfer to ice water for 5 minutes; peel; halve lengthwise. Reserve.
  6. 6
    Cook the rice (or use pre-made lontong). Rinse the 200 g jasmine rice in cold water until the water runs clear. Combine with 280 ml water and a pinch of salt in a small saucepan; bring to a simmer; reduce to lowest heat; cover; cook 15 minutes. Off heat, let stand covered 10 more minutes. Pack into a small loaf pan or container while warm; press firmly with the back of a spoon to compact; refrigerate 20 minutes to set. Cut into 2 cm cubes.
  7. 7
    Fry the tofu and tempeh. Heat the 200 ml / ¾ cup neutral oil in a deep skillet to 175°C / 350°F. Fry the 200 g tofu cubes in 2 batches for 3 to 4 minutes each, turning once, until golden on all sides. Drain on paper towels. Fry the 200 g tempeh slabs in the same oil for 3 minutes per side until golden and crisp at the edges. Drain on paper towels; cut tempeh into 2 cm cubes.
  8. 8
    Blanch the vegetables. Bring a large pot of salted water to a boil. Blanch each vegetable separately and time them precisely: 200 g long beans for 3 minutes (still snappy), 200 g napa cabbage for 1 minute (just-wilted), 150 g bean sprouts for 30 seconds (just-warm), 150 g carrot matchsticks for 90 seconds (al dente). Lift each batch out with a spider or slotted spoon; shock briefly in ice water; drain. Boil the 250 g potato cubes separately for 12 minutes until fork-tender; do not shock the potatoes (warm potato absorbs the sauce better than cold).
  9. 9
    Assemble the salad. On a large platter or 4 individual plates, arrange the components in distinct sections: blanched long beans, blanched cabbage, bean sprouts, boiled potato, carrot matchsticks, raw cucumber rounds, fried tofu, fried tempeh, halved hard-boiled eggs, and lontong (or rice) cubes. The composed-salad presentation is structural; the diner sees each component before mixing.
  10. 10
    Serve. Spoon a generous quantity of warm peanut sauce over the entire platter (about 80 ml per portion; pass extra sauce on the side). Scatter the 40 g krupuk crackers and 20 g fried shallots over the top. Each diner mixes their own portion with chopsticks or a fork at the table; the "gado-gado" name literally means "mix-mix," and the mixing is part of the eating experience. The components combine into the structural composed-salad bite: cool vegetable, hot fried protein, soft starchy rice cake, all coated in the rich peanut sauce.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion vegetable expansion→with seasonal additions (blanched water spinach, chayote, jicama)

    Add any of: 100 g blanched water spinach (kangkung, the canonical Indonesian green) for 60 seconds; 150 g chayote, peeled and matchstick-cut, blanched 2 minutes; 150 g jicama, raw matchstick-cut. The dish absorbs vegetable variations easily; gado-gado is structurally a vegetable showcase.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→karedok (Sundanese raw-vegetable cousin)

    Skip all the blanching; use the same vegetables raw, including long beans (very thinly sliced) and napa cabbage (very thinly shredded). Use a lighter, less-cooked peanut sauce. The dish becomes karedok, the Sundanese raw-vegetable cousin of gado-gado, structurally distinct in its texture.

  • Protein protein→with shrimp (non-traditional but common in restaurants)

    Add 150 g cooked peeled shrimp to the platter. Less traditional but a common Indonesian restaurant variation. The shrimp pair well with the peanut sauce.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • palm sugar (gula jawa) → dark brown sugar + a pinch of molasses (or muscovado sugar) Palm sugar has a distinctive caramel-tobacco depth from the palm-sap reduction process; dark brown sugar with a splash of molasses is the accessible approximation. The dish reads recognizably similar; muscovado is even closer in flavor.
  • kecap manis → soy sauce + palm sugar (50 g soy + 30 g sugar, simmered 2 minutes to dissolve) Kecap manis is the structural Indonesian sweet-savory soy; the soy-sugar mix is the accessible substitute. The dish reads slightly less complex but functional.
  • tamarind concentrate → lime juice + a pinch of brown sugar Tamarind has a molasses-and-dried-fruit depth that lime alone cannot replicate; the brown sugar approximates the molasses notes. Use 30 ml lime juice + 5 g brown sugar in place of 15 g tamarind. The dish reads brighter and less complex.
  • tempeh → extra fried tofu (200 g additional firm tofu, fried same as the other tofu) Tempeh is the structural Indonesian fermented soybean and is significantly different from tofu in flavor and texture; substituting more tofu loses the dish defining nutty fermented note but is acceptable when tempeh is unavailable.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → fried shallots (store-bought or homemade) make it yourself Homemade crispy fried shallots are dramatically more aromatic than bagged supermarket versions; the rendered oil from frying can be saved for other dishes.

Notes

The peanut sauce is the dish. Gado-gado is structurally a vehicle for the peanut sauce; the vegetable selection and proteins are infinitely variable, but the sauce is non-negotiable. The four-flavor target for the sauce is sweet (palm sugar), savory (kecap manis, shallot-garlic base), spicy (red chili), and tangy (tamarind + lime); all four must register on the first taste, with the peanut as the carrier. A sauce that tastes only of peanut butter is missing the structural Indonesian character; a sauce that tastes too sweet is over-corrected toward Thai satay sauce.

Why the vegetables are blanched separately. Each vegetable in gado-gado has a different optimal cook time: bean sprouts need 30 seconds (a structural Indonesian crunch), cabbage needs 1 minute (just-wilted), long beans need 3 minutes (still snappy), carrots need 90 seconds (al dente). Cooking them together produces some overcooked and some undercooked; cooking each separately gives every vegetable its correct texture. The technique is a structural Indonesian approach that distinguishes gado-gado from less-considered versions.

Tempeh is structural. Tempeh (fermented soybean cake, a distinctly Indonesian invention) is what gives gado-gado its nutty fermented backbone. Tofu provides creamy soybean protein; tempeh provides the more aggressive fermented-and-meaty soybean character. Both are present in canonical gado-gado; substituting more tofu for tempeh loses the dish-defining note. Tempeh is widely available in US supermarkets (Lightlife, Westsoy, Tofurky are reliable brands).

Composed-salad geometry. The presentation of gado-gado is structural: each component is arranged in its own distinct section on the platter or plate, so the diner sees the variety before mixing. A pre-mixed gado-gado is a structurally different dish (more like a thrown-together stir-fry salad); the visual presentation of distinct sections is part of the eating experience. The mixing is done by the diner at the table, and the "gado-gado" name literally means "mix-mix," referring to this final step.

Storage. The components store well separately for 2 days refrigerated; assemble fresh just before serving. The peanut sauce thickens dramatically on chilling; reheat gently with a splash of hot water to loosen. Blanched vegetables lose their snap on refrigeration; the dish reads significantly fresher when blanched the same day. Fried tofu and tempeh keep 2 days refrigerated and reheat well in a 175°C / 350°F oven for 5 minutes.

  • Indonesian Javanese tradition (Jakarta and West Java) — Gado-gado (literally "mix-mix" in Indonesian) is the canonical Indonesian composed salad, with strongest ties to Jakarta and West Java but found across the Indonesian archipelago.
