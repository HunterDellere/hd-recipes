Haitian griot

The Haitian benchmark pork dish: shoulder marinated in epis (the green seasoning paste of garlic, scallion, parsley, thyme, and scotch bonnet), citrus-braised until tender, then fried to a dark crisp finish. Served with pikliz (the fiery cabbage relish), fried plantains, and rice and beans.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The pork marinates 12 hours minimum in epis; this is structural for the green-herbal character that defines Haitian cooking. Plan the day before.
  • The two-stage cook (braise then fry) is the structural Haitian technique. Do not skip either stage; the braise tenderizes, the fry develops the crisp surface that defines griot.
  • Pikliz (the fiery pickled cabbage relish) is the canonical accompaniment and is structural to the dish, not optional. Make it at least 24 hours ahead so the flavors mature; recipe linked in homemade alternatives.
  • Wear nitrile gloves when chopping scotch bonnets. The capsaicin sticks to skin oils and migrates to eyes hours later.

Execution

  1. 1
    Phase 1, make the epis marinade. Combine the 8 g scotch bonnet, 30 g garlic, 80 g scallions, 30 g parsley, 6 g thyme leaves, 100 g bell pepper, 90 g lime juice, 60 g sour orange juice, 30 g olive oil, 1 g ground cloves, 12 g salt, and 3 g black pepper in a blender or food processor. Blend 90 seconds to 2 minutes until smooth and aromatic, scraping down sides as needed. The epis should read deeply green, bright, and aggressively herbal. The yield is roughly 380 g epis.
  2. 2
    Phase 2, marinate the pork. Place the 1500 g pork cubes in a large bowl or zip-top bag. Pour over about 280 g of the epis (reserve 100 g of epis; it will be added to the braise later); mix to coat each cube evenly. Refrigerate 12 hours minimum, ideally 24 hours. The long marinade is the structural step that defines Haitian cooking; do not skip.12 h
  3. 3
    Phase 3, braise the pork. Transfer the marinated pork with all the marinade into a large Dutch oven or wide heavy pot. Add the 500 g water, 2 bay leaves, and the reserved 100 g epis; stir to combine. Bring to a strong simmer over medium-high heat; reduce to medium-low, partially cover, and braise 60 to 75 minutes until the pork is tender (a fork inserted in a cube should meet little resistance) and most of the liquid has reduced to a thick aromatic glaze coating the meat. Stir occasionally to prevent sticking. The pork should still hold its cubed shape; do not let it fall apart.
  4. 4
    Drain and rest. Lift the braised pork cubes out of the Dutch oven with a slotted spoon onto a plate. Discard any remaining braising liquid (or reserve for a future dish; the epis-flavored broth makes a brilliant cooking liquid for rice or beans). Let the pork cubes air-dry 10 minutes; this is structural for the crisp fry to follow (wet meat does not crisp).
  5. 5
    Phase 4, fry the pork. Heat the 500 g neutral oil in a heavy deep skillet or wide Dutch oven (3 cm of oil) over medium-high heat to 180°C / 360°F (test with a small piece of pork; it should sizzle aggressively on contact). Fry the pork cubes in batches (do not crowd; crowding drops the oil temperature and produces steamed rather than fried pork) 5 to 7 minutes per batch until the surface is deeply crisp and mahogany-dark. Transfer to a paper-towel-lined plate; salt lightly. Repeat with the remaining pork; the fry should produce 3 batches.
  6. 6
    Phase 5, assemble and serve. Pile the crisp griot onto a serving platter; spoon a generous portion of 200 g pikliz over the top or arrange in a small bowl alongside. Serve with the 60 g lime wedges, fried sweet plantains, and rice and beans on the side. The diner eats the griot in chunks, pairing each bite with pikliz for the structural heat-and-acid contrast that defines the Haitian flavor balance.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion cuisine pivot→as oven-roasted griot (the modern crisp version)

    After the braise, spread the pork cubes on a wire rack set in a sheet pan. Broil under high heat 4 to 6 minutes per side until deeply crisp and dark. The oven version skips the deep-fry mess and produces a structurally similar crisp surface with less hands-on attention; it reads slightly less rich (the fry adds structural fat) but is a brilliant weeknight version.

  • Protein cuisine pivot→as griot di poul (Haitian fried chicken)

    Replace the pork shoulder with 1500 g bone-in chicken thighs and drumsticks. Reduce the braise to 30 minutes (chicken cooks faster than pork). Proceed with the fry as written; the chicken version reads structurally similar with a lighter character. Canonical Haitian alternative; pairs equally well with pikliz.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • scotch bonnet chilies → habanero (same weight) Same species, comparable heat, slightly different flavor profile. Habanero reads more citrus-floral, scotch bonnet more tropical-fruity. Use the same weight.
  • sour orange juice (or 50/50 lime and orange juice) → fresh sour orange juice from a Latin American grocery Fresh sour orange (naranja agria, Citrus aurantium) is canonical and reads structurally Caribbean. The 50/50 lime-orange substitute approximates the flavor balance. Available at Latin American groceries; bottled sour orange juice (Goya brand) is a shelf-stable alternative.
  • pork shoulder (Boston butt) → pork loin or pork tenderloin Loin and tenderloin are leaner; the dish reads slightly drier and less rich. The shoulder is canonical for griot specifically because the fat self-bastes the meat during the braise and provides the structural crisp during the fry. Use shoulder when possible.

Notes

Haitian griot is the structural celebration dish of Haiti and one of the great two-stage meat preparations of the African-Caribbean diaspora. The technique (marinate, braise, then fry to crisp) is structurally Haitian and developed from West African slow-cook traditions combined with French colonial frying methods; the resulting dish carries both the depth of a long-cooked stew and the surface character of a deeply fried protein. Master griot and the technique transfers to chicken, goat, and any other slow-cook-then-crisp meat preparation.

The epis is the structural Haitian flavor foundation. Epis (literally spice in Haitian Creole) is the green seasoning paste that underlies most Haitian cooking, similar in function to Caribbean sofrito but structurally distinct (epis is herbier, more aggressively garlic-and-parsley-driven, and almost always includes scotch bonnet). Make epis in a large batch (double or triple the recipe) and freeze in 100 g portions; it is the structural starting point for Haitian rice, beans, soups, and braises. The 12-to-24-hour marinade in epis is the structural step that distinguishes Haitian-cooked meat from generic braised meat.

The two-stage cook is structural. Braising tenderizes the pork through collagen-to-gelatin conversion; frying develops the structural crisp surface that defines griot. Either stage alone produces a different dish: braised-only is structurally pork stew; fried-only (without the braise) is structurally tough fried pork. The braise-then-fry sequence is the canonical Haitian technique and is what produces the structural griot texture (tender inside, crisp outside).

The fry temperature matters. 180°C / 360°F is the structural window; lower temperatures produce greasy pork without proper crisping, higher temperatures burn the surface before the interior reheats from the braise. Use a thermometer; if no thermometer, test with a small piece of pork (should sizzle aggressively on contact, develop crisp surface in 5 to 7 minutes, not turn dark too quickly).

The pikliz pairing is structural. Pikliz (the fiery pickled cabbage-carrot-scotch-bonnet relish) is non-negotiable for canonical griot service. The structural Haitian flavor balance depends on the acid-and-heat-and-crunch of the pikliz cutting the rich-fried-fat of the griot; without pikliz, the dish reads as good fried pork but not as canonical griot. Make the pikliz at least 24 hours ahead; the canonical pikliz matures over several days and is structurally complete at the 48-hour mark.

Versatility. The epis-marinate-and-braise technique transfers to chicken (griot di poul), goat (griot di kabrit), and beef (griot di bef). The fry-to-crisp finish works on any of these proteins. The pikliz pairing carries; serve any griot variant with pikliz and the structural Haitian flavor balance holds. Beyond griot, epis is the structural starting point for Haitian rice (riz djon-djon, with black mushrooms), Haitian red beans (sos pwa), and Haitian soup (soup joumou, the squash soup served on Haitian Independence Day).

The canonical Haitian plate. Griot is served with pikliz, riz collé aux pois (rice cooked with red beans), bannann peze (fried green plantains pounded flat then re-fried), and avocado wedges. This is the structural Sunday or celebration plate in Haiti; the four components together produce a complete meal with structural acid, heat, starch, fat, and fresh elements. A glass of Barbancourt rum or Prestige beer (the Haitian lager) is the canonical drink pairing.

Storage. Marinated raw pork keeps 48 hours refrigerated in epis. Cooked griot keeps 4 days refrigerated; freezes 2 months. Reheat in a 200°C / 400°F oven 10 to 12 minutes uncovered to re-crisp the surface; do not microwave (loses the structural crisp). Pikliz keeps 1 month refrigerated and reads better at 1 week than at 1 day.
