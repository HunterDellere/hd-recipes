Recipe

Ciabatta

A high-hydration (82%) Italian slipper loaf built on an overnight biga preferment, developed by no-knead stretch-and-fold in the tub rather than kneading, gently divided and shaped with floured hands so the gas stays in, and baked hot with steam to a thin shattering crust over a translucent open crumb of giant irregular holes. The biga does the flavor work: 12 to 16 hours of slow fermentation generates the organic acids and free glutamates that give a lean four-ingredient dough its depth, while the very wet final dough and minimal handling are what produce the wide-open alveoli that define the loaf.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a two-day bake. Mix the biga the night before, 12 to 16 hours ahead, and leave it at cool room temperature (18 to 21 °C) to ferment. The biga is the entire flavor engine of the loaf; there is no shortcut that replaces the overnight ferment.
  • The final dough is 82% hydration and will look and feel like a thick batter, not a kneadable dough. This is correct. Do not be tempted to add flour to firm it up; the wetness is what produces the open crumb. You develop it with wet hands and a bowl scraper, not by kneading on a counter.
  • Total elapsed time is roughly 19 hours, but hands-on time is about 45 minutes spread across the biga mix, four stretch-and-folds at 30-minute intervals, divide-and-shape, and the bake. Plan the final dough for a morning so the four folds and the proof land across the same day.
  • Have a baking steel or stone and a steam strategy ready before you shape: the loaves bake at 245 °C with steam for the first 12 minutes, and a cold or steam-less oven flattens the crust the whole method was built to create.

Execution

  1. 1
    The night before, mix the biga. In a medium bowl, stir the 0.4 g / scant ⅛ tsp instant yeast into the 120 g / ½ cup cool water until dispersed, then add the 200 g / 1⅔ cups bread flour. Mix with a fork or your fingers just until no dry flour remains; the biga is a stiff, shaggy, tacky mass, not a smooth dough, and it should not be kneaded. Cover the bowl with a damp towel or a fitted lid and leave at cool room temperature (18 to 21 °C) for 12 to 16 hours. By morning the biga should have roughly doubled, be webbed with bubbles, domed and just beginning to recede at the center, and smell sharply yeasty with a clean sour edge. That aroma is the point: the long ferment is generating acetic and lactic acid plus free amino acids and glutamates, the flavor depth a four-ingredient lean dough cannot get any other way.
  2. 2
    Mix the final dough. Add the 290 g / 1¼ cups room-temperature water to the bowl of fermented biga and squeeze the biga through your fingers to break it into the water until the mixture is a loose, lumpy slurry; this disperses the mature preferment evenly so it does not stay as dense pockets. Add the 300 g / 2½ cups bread flour and the 3 g / 1 tsp instant yeast and mix with a stiff spatula or your hand until a wet, sticky, shaggy dough with no dry flour remains, about 1 minute. Do not add the 11 g / 2 tsp fine sea salt yet. Cover and rest 20 minutes (an autolyse): the flour fully hydrates and the gluten begins to align on its own, so less mechanical work is needed afterward.
  3. 3
    Add the salt and do the first fold. Sprinkle the 11 g / 2 tsp fine sea salt over the rested dough and add a splash (about 15 g / 1 tbsp) of the water still clinging to the bowl, or a teaspoon of fresh water, to help it dissolve. With a wet hand, reach under one side of the dough, stretch it up until it resists, and fold it over the top; rotate the bowl a quarter turn and repeat, four folds total, until the salt is worked in and the mass tightens slightly. The dough is 82% hydration and behaves like a thick batter that holds a fold for a second before relaxing; this is correct, do not add flour. Lightly oil a straight-sided tub or bowl with a little of the 10 g / 2 tsp extra-virgin olive oil, transfer the dough in, cover, and start a timer for 30 minutes.
  4. 4
    Stretch-and-fold, three more sets at 30-minute intervals. With a wet or lightly oiled hand, grab the underside of the dough at one edge, stretch it up and over to the opposite side, rotate the tub 90 degrees, and repeat until you have folded all four sides (this is one set). Cover and rest 30 minutes, then repeat. You will do four sets in total over the first 90 minutes after adding salt (the salt-incorporation fold counted as set one, so three more here). With each set the dough transforms: by the third it stops tearing and starts stretching into a sheet, going from slack and soupy to smooth, jiggly, and elastic, with visible bubbles forming under the surface. This folding is how you build gluten in a dough too wet to knead; each fold stacks and aligns the gluten sheets and traps the fermentation gas.
  5. 5
    Bulk ferment until billowy. After the final fold, cover the tub and let the dough rise undisturbed at room temperature (22 to 24 °C) until it has roughly doubled and is domed, jiggly, and full of bubbles you can see through the side of the tub, about 1 to 2 hours depending on room temperature. The dough is ready when a gentle poke springs back slowly and leaves a slight dent, and the surface is taut with gas. Underproofed dough bakes dense; overproofed dough collapses on the peel and bakes flat, so watch the volume and feel, not the clock.
  6. 6
    Turn out and divide, handling as little as possible. Dust your work surface generously with about half the 40 g / ⅓ cup semolina or bread flour, and dust the top of the dough in the tub. Gently coax the dough out onto the bench with a bowl scraper, letting it fall as a rough rectangle; do not punch it down or degas it, the gas in the dough is the open crumb you are protecting. Flour the top, then with a bench knife or a sharp dough scraper cut the slab cleanly straight down (do not saw, which drags and degasses) into two equal rectangles, roughly 12 by 9 cm each. Flour the cut edges.
  7. 7
    Shape into slippers by stretching, not rounding. Slide both hands, well floured, under one rectangle and gently lift and stretch it lengthwise into a slipper about 25 cm long, then set it seam-side down on a generously floured peel, couche, or parchment-lined inverted tray. Tuck the long edges under just enough to give a loose oblong; never round it tight or you crush the alveoli. Repeat with the second piece, spacing them well apart. Dust the tops with flour, cover with a damp towel, and proof 30 to 45 minutes, until visibly puffy and jiggly but still slightly slack; ciabatta is proofed soft, on the young side, so it springs hard in the oven.
  8. 8
    Preheat for steam while the loaves proof. At least 45 minutes before baking, set a baking steel or stone on a middle rack, place a cast-iron skillet or a metal roasting pan on the rack below it, and preheat the oven to 245 °C / 475 °F. The empty pan will become the steam generator; the steel holds the high stable heat that drives oven spring. Boil a kettle of water near the end of the proof so you have steam-ready water at hand.
  9. 9
    Load with steam and bake. Working quickly, slide the proofed loaves (still on their parchment, or off the floured peel) directly onto the hot steel, keeping them well spaced. Immediately pour about 240 g / 1 cup of just-boiled water into the preheated skillet below and close the oven door at once to trap the burst of steam. The steam keeps the crust soft and elastic for the first minutes so the loaves can expand fully (oven spring) before the crust sets; it is what gives ciabatta its thin, blistered, shattering crust. Bake 12 minutes with steam.
  10. 10
    Vent and finish dry. After 12 minutes, open the oven, carefully remove the steam pan (or leave it; it is dry by now), and crack the oven door for a few seconds to release residual steam. Bake another 10 to 14 minutes, until the crust is deep golden brown with darker blistered patches and the loaves sound hollow when tapped on the bottom; an instant-read thermometer in the center should read 96 to 99 °C / 205 to 210 °F. A pale loaf is underbaked and gummy inside, so push the color further than feels comfortable; ciabatta wants a genuinely brown crust.
  11. 11
    Cool completely before cutting. Transfer the loaves to a wire rack and cool at least 45 minutes, ideally 1 hour, before slicing. The crumb is still setting as it cools; the starch retrogrades and the interior finishes from gummy to tender. Cutting a hot ciabatta drags the knife through pasty, unset crumb and collapses the open holes you worked the whole method to build. Listen for the crust crackling and singing as it cools, the sign of a properly hydrated, well-baked loaf.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion no baking steel or stone→preheated heavy baking sheet

    Flip a heavy rimmed baking sheet upside down and preheat it like a stone for 45 minutes at 245 °C. Slide the parchment-borne loaves onto the hot inverted sheet and steam the same way. The crust is marginally less crisp than off a steel, but the high preheated thermal mass still drives the oven spring the open crumb needs.

  • Regional rosemary and olive ciabatta→aromatic loaf

    During the second stretch-and-fold set, laminate in 8 g / 2 tbsp chopped fresh rosemary and 60 g / about 12 pitted oil-cured olives, halved: stretch the dough into a sheet, scatter the additions across it, fold it up, and continue folding as usual so they distribute without degassing. Reduce the fine sea salt to 9 g / 1¾ tsp to account for the salt the olives carry.

  • Occasion overnight cold proof (better flavor, split bake-day)→fridge-retarded final proof

    After dividing and shaping in step 6 and 7, cover the shaped loaves and refrigerate 8 to 14 hours instead of proofing at room temperature. Bake straight from the fridge, adding 3 to 4 minutes to the bake time. The cold retard deepens flavor (more acid and glutamate development) and lets you mix one day and bake the next morning.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • bread flour → Italian tipo 00 flour, or all-purpose flour Tipo 00 (W300 or higher, "panettone" or "manitoba" strength) is the authentic Italian choice and gives a slightly more tender, extensible crumb; use it at the same weight. All-purpose flour (11 to 12% protein) works but the lower gluten makes the 82% dough harder to handle and the crumb a touch tighter; if using all-purpose, drop the water by 20 g / 4 tsp to 78% hydration.
  • fine sea salt → kosher salt Fine sea salt dissolves evenly into the wet dough, which is why it is specified here. If using Diamond Crystal kosher salt, increase to 15 g / 1 tbsp by volume since the flakes are less dense; weigh it to 11 g and the result is identical.
  • semolina, for dusting → bread flour, rice flour, or a 50/50 mix Semolina gives the best non-stick release and a pleasant gritty texture on the base. Fine bread flour works but is grabbier on a very wet dough; rice flour is the most non-stick of all and is the safest dusting bed if your dough is sticking to the peel.

Notes

Why the biga, and why so little yeast in it. Ciabatta is a lean dough: flour, water, yeast, salt, with no enriching fat, sugar, or dairy to carry flavor. All of the depth has to come from fermentation. The biga, a stiff (60% hydration) preferment fermented 12 to 16 hours with a deliberately tiny yeast dose, is the engine. Over that long slow ferment the yeast and the flour's native lactic-acid bacteria generate organic acids (lactic and acetic) and break flour proteins down into free amino acids and glutamates. That is the dish's umami-and-acid axis, built by time rather than added as an ingredient: the glutamates read as savory depth and the acids as the clean sour tang that makes good bread taste like more than baked starch. A loaf mixed straight (all the yeast at once, no preferment) bakes faster but tastes flat and one-dimensional by comparison. The tiny yeast dose in the biga, a scant eighth-teaspoon, is intentional; more yeast would exhaust the dough's sugars and blow the biga out in four hours before the flavor compounds develop.

Why 82% hydration, and why you cannot knead it. The open, irregular, translucent-walled crumb that defines ciabatta is a direct consequence of very high hydration. More water means a slacker gluten network that can stretch around large gas bubbles without tearing, and it means more steam generated inside the loaf during the bake, which inflates the alveoli further. At 82% the dough is too wet to knead on a counter in any conventional sense; it would just smear. Gluten development instead comes from the autolyse (the flour hydrating and self-organizing during the 20-minute rest) plus the four stretch-and-fold sets, which stack and align the gluten sheets while trapping gas. Each fold visibly strengthens the dough; by the fourth set the slurry has become a jiggly, elastic, bubble-filled mass. Reference the stretch-and-fold technique page for the mechanism in detail.

Why minimal handling at every stage. Every time you handle a proofed high-hydration dough you risk degassing it, collapsing the very holes you are trying to keep. This is why the method is so deliberate about gentleness: do not punch down after bulk, do not round the loaves tight, cut rather than tear, stretch rather than roll, and shape with floured hands so nothing sticks and pulls. The slipper shape is not just aesthetic; it is the shape you get when you handle the dough as little as possible, cutting a slab into rectangles and stretching them long. Treat the gas in the dough as the finished product and protect it.

Steam and oven spring. The first 10 to 12 minutes of the bake are the only window in which the loaf can expand. A blast of steam at load keeps the crust soft and elastic so the loaf can spring fully before the surface dries and sets; it also gelatinizes the surface starch, which is what produces the thin, glossy, blistered, shattering crust. Without steam the crust sets in the first two minutes and clamps down on the rise, giving a smaller, paler, thicker-crusted loaf. After the spring window, venting the steam and finishing the bake dry is what crisps the crust and drives the crumb to a fully set, non-gummy interior. The internal temperature target (96 to 99 °C) is the reliable doneness cue; color and the hollow tap confirm it.

Reading the crumb. A great ciabatta cut shows a wildly irregular open crumb: large, glossy, thin-walled holes of very different sizes, with the cell walls translucent rather than tight and white. A tight, even, fine crumb means the dough was either underhydrated, overhandled (degassed during shaping), or underproofed. Big holes concentrated only along the top with dense crumb below ("flying crust") usually means the loaf was underproofed and sprang too violently, or shaped too tight. The fix is almost always more gentleness in handling and watching the proof by feel rather than the clock.

Sources
  • Adapted from the modern Italian ciabatta tradition (Arnaldo Cavallari's 1982 Veneto formula and the high-hydration biga method codified across Italian and American artisan-bread practice). — Ciabatta is a recent invention, developed in 1982 as an Italian answer to the French baguette. The defining features are deliberate: a stiff biga preferment for flavor and extensibility, a final dough wet enough (80%+ baker's hydration) that it cannot be kneaded by hand in the conventional sense, gluten development by folding rather than kneading, and the barest minimum of shaping so the fermentation gas survives into the open crumb. The slipper shape (ciabatta means slipper) comes from cutting the slack dough into rectangles and stretching them gently, never rounding or degassing.
