Recipe

Green goddess dressing

The San Francisco original rebuilt as a herb-saturated emulsion: tarragon, chives, parsley, and basil blended into a base of mayonnaise, sour cream, and buttermilk, with anchovy and lemon supplying the savory-acid backbone. Everything goes into a blender and runs until the herb cell walls rupture and stain the dressing a true chlorophyll green rather than a white cream with green specks.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a blender dressing, not a whisk-and-stir dressing. The whole point is to rupture the herb cells so the chlorophyll and volatile oils bleed into the dairy and stain it green; hand-chopping leaves you with a white base wearing green confetti. A small blender jar, a tall cup with an immersion blender, or a food processor all work. A blender that is too large for the volume will not catch the liquid on the blades, so scale up or use the narrowest jar you own.
  • Rest before serving for the best result. Blend it, then give it at least 60 minutes refrigerated so the raw garlic mellows from sharp to round and the herb oils integrate into the dairy. Fresh-blended it tastes raw and slightly harsh from the garlic; one hour later it reads cohesive. It is markedly better still on day two.
  • Pick the herbs clean of thick stems before measuring. Tarragon and parsley stems are fibrous enough to leave hard flecks the blender will not break down; basil stem is bitter. Leaves and the thin upper stems only.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Emulsion base

  1. 150 g mayonnaise (full-fat, real-egg)
  2. 100 g sour cream (full-fat)
  3. 60 ml buttermilk (full-fat, well-shaken)

Herbs

  1. 8 g fresh tarragon leaves, leaves stripped, thick stems discarded
  2. 12 g fresh chives, cut into 2 cm lengths
  3. 20 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, leaves and thin upper stems only
  4. 8 g fresh basil leaves, leaves only

Savory and acid

  1. 10 g oil-packed anchovy fillets, drained and patted dry — (3 to 4 fillets; the glutamate source, blended to invisibility so it reads as savory depth, not fish)
  2. 3 g garlic clove, peeled — (1 small clove; the blender purees it finer than a grater could)
  3. 20 ml fresh lemon juice
  4. 5 ml white wine vinegar
  5. 3 g fine sea salt
  6. 1 g freshly cracked black pepper

Execution

  1. 1
    Combine the base and the savory-acid additions in the blender first, before the herbs: the 150 g / ⅔ cup mayonnaise, the 100 g / scant ½ cup sour cream, the 60 ml / ¼ cup buttermilk, the 10 g / 3 to 4 anchovy fillets, the 3 g / 1 small garlic clove, the 20 ml / 4 tsp lemon juice, the 5 ml / 1 tsp white wine vinegar, the 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. Blend 20 to 30 seconds until the anchovy and garlic have disappeared into a smooth pale base. Starting with the liquid base on the blades means the herbs that go in next have something to circulate through; herbs blended dry just pack against the jar wall.
  2. 2
    Add all the herbs at once: the 8 g / ¼ cup tarragon, the 12 g / 3 tbsp chives, the 20 g / ¾ cup parsley, and the 8 g / ¼ cup basil. Blend on high for 45 to 60 seconds, stopping once to scrape the jar down with a wooden or bamboo spatula and push any clinging leaves back toward the blades. Run it longer than feels necessary. The dressing shifts from white-with-flecks to a uniform pale green at about 20 seconds, then deepens to a true chlorophyll green by 60 seconds as the shear ruptures the herb cell walls and releases the pigment and volatile oils into the dairy. That color shift is the doneness cue.
  3. 3
    Check the consistency. It should pour in a thick ribbon off the spatula, coating it with a clear drag-line. If it is too thick to pour (mayonnaise brands vary in stiffness), thin with buttermilk 1 tbsp at a time and pulse to combine; if it broke thin or looks loose, blend in 1 tbsp more mayonnaise to retighten the emulsion. For a thick dip rather than a pourable dressing, leave it as is or hold back half the buttermilk.
  4. 4
    Taste from a clean spoon and tune. The anchovy should register only as savory depth, never as fish; if it reads flat and one-note, that depth is missing, so add a quarter of a fillet and re-blend. If it tastes rich and heavy, it needs acid: add lemon juice ½ tsp at a time until the dressing tastes bright on the front of the tongue. Adjust salt by a pinch at a time. The herbs should taste green and layered, with the tarragon anise reading clearly through the parsley and basil; if the tarragon is buried, add a few more leaves and pulse.
  5. 5
    Transfer to a glass jar and seal with its fitted lid. Refrigerate at least 60 minutes before serving, ideally overnight. The rest mellows the raw garlic from sharp to round and lets the herb oils diffuse fully into the dairy; fresh-blended it tastes raw, and one hour transforms it. It holds 3 to 4 days refrigerated. The green dulls slightly toward olive past day two as the chlorophyll oxidizes, harmless and still good, but brightest on days one and two.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture texture→thick green goddess dip

    Drop the buttermilk entirely and use 130 g / ½ cup full-fat Greek yogurt in place of the sour cream. The dressing sets to a spoonable dip thick enough to mound on a board, the right body for crudités, wedges of little gem, or chilled shrimp. The yogurt also pushes the tang sharper, so taste and pull back the lemon by half.

  • Dietary dietary→dairy-free and vegan

    Swap the mayonnaise for a vegan aquafaba-based mayo, replace the sour cream and buttermilk with 160 g unsweetened cashew cream loosened with 15 ml lemon juice and 30 ml water, and replace the anchovy with 5 g white miso plus a 2 cm strip of toasted nori blended in for the same glutamate-and-sea savor. The herb and acid structure carries the dish; the umami swap is the only non-obvious move.

  • Occasion use→green goddess sauce for grilled fish or chicken

    Blend in an extra 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil and cut the buttermilk to 40 ml so the sauce is looser and glossier. Spoon it under or over grilled salmon, halibut, or chicken thighs rather than tossing it with greens. The added oil makes it cling to hot protein the way a thinner salad dressing would slide off.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • fresh tarragon → fresh dill or extra basil with a pinch of fennel seed Tarragon is the defining herb, so a true substitute is hard. Dill takes the dressing toward a ranch-adjacent profile but keeps it herbal and fresh. Basil plus a few crushed fennel seeds approximates the anise note tarragon brings. Dried tarragon is not worth using here; it loses the volatile anethole that matters.
  • oil-packed anchovy fillets → white miso or Worcestershire sauce 5 g white miso or 5 g Worcestershire delivers the same glutamate depth for cooks avoiding anchovy. Both blend in invisibly. Worcestershire actually contains anchovy, so it is not a fully fish-free swap; miso is the vegetarian-clean choice.
  • buttermilk → whole milk plus 1 tsp lemon juice, rested 10 minutes Acidified milk replicates the lactic-acid chemistry buttermilk supplies for thinning and tang; the live-culture difference is irrelevant in an uncooked dressing. The result reads a touch milder. Kefir or thinned plain yogurt also work.
  • sour cream → full-fat Greek yogurt or crème fraîche Greek yogurt is tangier and thicker (add 1 tbsp more buttermilk to compensate); crème fraîche is richer and less sharp. Both keep the cultured-dairy backbone that distinguishes this from a straight mayonnaise dressing.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → mayonnaise (full-fat, real-egg) make it yourself A from-scratch egg-yolk emulsion makes a cleaner, less industrial base than commercial mayo, and lets you build the dressing on a neutral oil you control rather than the soybean oil and stabilizers in a jar. The lecithin chemistry is identical; the flavor floor is higher.

Notes

Why this is not ranch. The two dressings share an architecture (mayonnaise, cultured dairy, fresh herbs, garlic, acid) and are easy to confuse, but the herb profile splits them cleanly. Ranch is built on dill, chives, and parsley with no anchovy; green goddess is built on tarragon and anchovy, and the tarragon anise plus the anchovy glutamate are exactly the two notes ranch lacks. Drop the tarragon and the anchovy from this recipe and you have made a slightly green ranch. They are the dish.

Why the blender, not the whisk. Chlorophyll and the herbs' volatile oils live inside intact plant cells. A knife chops the leaves into visible pieces but leaves most cells unbroken, so a hand-stirred version reads as a white base flecked with green and tastes mild because most of the aromatic load is still locked up. High-shear blending ruptures the cell walls, bleeds the pigment into the continuous dairy phase, and stains the whole dressing green. The color is the proxy for flavor extraction; a pale flecked dressing is an under-extracted one.

The umami and acid structure. The anchovy is the glutamate source and is non-negotiable for depth; blended to invisibility, it does not read as fish, it reads as the savory floor that makes the herbs taste like more than salad. Lemon juice plus white wine vinegar are the finishing acids, layered on purpose: lemon for round citrus brightness, vinegar for a sharper top note that stands in for the original's tarragon vinegar. A green goddess without a clear acid edge tastes muddy and rich; taste for brightness on the front of the tongue and add lemon until it lands there.

Herb ratios and the tarragon line. The parsley is the bulk green that sets the color and a neutral grassy floor; the tarragon, chives, and basil are the character. Tarragon is the one to protect: it is the herb the 1920s original was built on and the one supermarket bottles drop because it is expensive and seasonal. If you can only get three of the four herbs, keep tarragon and parsley and lose the basil before you lose the tarragon. Past about 50 g of total herb per cup of base the dressing starts to taste vegetal and bitter rather than bright, so resist the urge to keep adding.

Storage and color. Refrigerated in a sealed jar it holds 3 to 4 days. The chlorophyll slowly oxidizes and the green shifts toward olive after day two, which is cosmetic, not spoilage; the flavor holds well into day three before the herbs lose their edge. Press a round of parchment directly onto the surface under the lid to slow the oxidation if you want it to stay vivid for a board or a photographed plate.

Sources
  • Adapted from the Palace Hotel of San Francisco, where the dressing was created in the early 1920s, named for the William Archer play The Green Goddess. — The 1920s original was an anchovy-and-tarragon mayonnaise thinned with tarragon vinegar. The modern American form lightens the mayonnaise body with cultured dairy (sour cream and buttermilk) and pushes the herb load far past the original so the dressing reads green from chlorophyll, not from chopped flecks. Tarragon is the defining herb and the one most often dropped in supermarket versions, which is why those taste like flavored ranch.
