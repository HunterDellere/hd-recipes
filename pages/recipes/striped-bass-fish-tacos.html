Striped bass fish tacos with lime cabbage slaw and lime crema

Striped bass fillet cut into strips, rubbed with a chili-lime-cumin dry rub, and grilled hot until the surface chars and the large flake just sets, then flaked into warm corn tortillas. A crunchy lime cabbage slaw, a tangy lime crema of sour cream and mayonnaise, avocado, cilantro, and pickled jalapeño build the Baja taco in temperature and texture layers. The crema and the seared fish carry the umami; lime is the finishing acid in the slaw, in the crema, and squeezed at the table.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Make the slaw and the lime crema first and chill both. The slaw needs 20 to 30 minutes salted so the cabbage weeps and turns pliable-crunchy rather than raw-stiff, and the crema only improves as the lime and garlic bloom into the dairy. Both hold an hour in the fridge while you grill.
  • Get the grill fully hot before the fish goes near it. A grill that is not ripping hot will stick to the fish and tear the flake instead of searing a release crust; give a gas grill 10 to 15 minutes on high or a charcoal bed until the coals are ashed over and you cannot hold a hand above the grate for more than 2 seconds. Oil the grate just before the fish lands.
  • Warm the corn tortillas last, right before serving, and keep them wrapped in a towel. A cold or dry tortilla cracks when folded; a warm steamed one stays supple and is the difference between a taco that holds and one that falls apart in your hand.

Phase 1, lime cabbage slaw

  1. 300 g green cabbage, cored and shaved into thin ribbons on a mandoline or with a knife
  2. 50 g red onion, shaved into thin half-moons — (¼ medium onion)
  3. 15 g cilantro, leaves and tender stems, roughly chopped
  4. 30 g lime, juice, juiced
  5. 15 g neutral oil
  6. 3 g kosher salt

Phase 2, lime crema

  1. 120 g sour cream
  2. 40 g mayonnaise
  3. 30 g lime, zest and juice, zested fine, then juiced
  4. 3 g garlic, grated to a paste on a microplane — (1 small clove)
  5. 2 g kosher salt

Phase 3, the fish and rub

  1. 560 g striped bass fillet, skinless, patted bone-dry, cut lengthwise into strips 3 cm wide
  2. 4 g ground cumin
  3. 4 g smoked paprika
  4. 5 g lime, zest, zested fine — (zest of 1 lime)
  5. 5 g kosher salt
  6. 1 g black pepper, freshly ground
  7. 15 g neutral oil

Phase 4, build and garnish

  1. 320 g corn tortillas, warmed and wrapped in a towel — (8 tortillas, 6-inch; doubled up they hold the wet fillings better)
  2. 150 g avocado, sliced thin just before serving — (1 ripe avocado)
  3. 40 g pickled jalapeño, sliced into rings
  4. 10 g cilantro, leaves, picked
  5. 60 g lime, cut into wedges, cut into wedges — (2 limes in wedges, for the table)

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the slaw first. In a large bowl, toss the 300 g / shaved green cabbage and the 50 g / ¼ red onion with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt and let it sit 20 to 30 minutes. The salt draws water out of the cell walls so the cabbage softens into a pliable crunch instead of staying raw and stiff. After it weeps, squeeze out the excess liquid by the handful, then toss with the 30 g / juice of 1 lime, the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil, and the 15 g / ½ cup chopped cilantro. Taste and refrigerate; it should be tart and crunchy.
  2. 2
    Make the lime crema. Whisk the 120 g / ½ cup sour cream with the 40 g / 2½ tbsp mayonnaise until smooth and loose, then stir in the 30 g / zest and juice of 1 lime, the 3 g / 1 grated garlic clove, and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. The mayonnaise thins the sour cream to a drizzle and adds an egg-yolk savory depth that plain sour cream lacks. Refrigerate so the lime and garlic bloom into the dairy while you grill.
  3. 3
    Heat the grill to high and clean the grate. Give a gas grill 10 to 15 minutes on high, or build a charcoal bed and let it ash over until you cannot hold a hand above the grate for more than 2 seconds. A ripping-hot, clean grate is what sears a release crust on the fish; a cool or dirty grate grabs the flesh and tears it. Oil the grate with a folded paper towel dipped in a film of oil, held in tongs, just before the fish goes on.
  4. 4
    Rub the fish. Pat the 560 g / striped bass strips bone-dry, since surface water steams and blocks the char. In a small bowl, combine the 4 g / 2 tsp ground cumin, the 4 g / 2 tsp smoked paprika, the 5 g / zest of 1 lime, the 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. Coat the strips with the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil so the rub adheres, then dust the rub evenly over all sides and press it on.
  5. 5
    Grill the fish. Lay the 560 g / striped bass strips on the hot oiled grate perpendicular to the bars and leave them undisturbed for 3 to 4 minutes, until a char crust forms and the strips release cleanly without sticking. Flip once with a thin spatula and grill 2 to 3 minutes more, until the flesh turns fully opaque and flakes at a gentle press. Cook the fish through to opaque-and-flaky; striped bass sets into clean large flakes the moment it loses translucency, so pull it the instant it flakes rather than holding it over the heat and drying the flake out.
  6. 6
    Warm the tortillas. Pass the 320 g / 8 corn tortillas directly over a grill flame or a dry skillet for 15 to 20 seconds a side until supple and lightly charred at the edges, then stack them in a clean towel to steam and stay soft. Warm tortillas fold without cracking; cold ones split and spill the fillings. Slice the 150 g / 1 avocado thin now, just before building, so it does not brown.
  7. 7
    Build the tacos. Flake the grilled fish into large pieces with a fork. To each warm tortilla (double them up for the wet fillings), add a handful of the lime cabbage slaw, then a portion of flaked fish, a few slices of the 150 g / avocado, a drizzle of the lime crema, a few rings of the 40 g / pickled jalapeño, and a scatter of the 10 g / picked cilantro leaves. Serve immediately with the 60 g / lime wedges for squeezing at the table, the third and final dose of acid that brightens the whole taco.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture no grill, stovetop version→pan-seared in cast iron

    Skip the grill and sear the rubbed strips in a screaming-hot cast-iron skillet with 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil, 3 to 4 minutes a side, until a crust forms and the flake just sets. You lose the live-fire char aroma but gain an even, hard-seared crust and full pan contact, which actually crisps the rub more aggressively than the grate does. Best in winter or for an apartment kitchen.

  • Heat chipotle heat→smoky chipotle crema

    Mash 10 g / 1 chipotle in adobo with a teaspoon of its sauce into the crema along with the lime and garlic. The crema turns smoky-hot and dusky pink, and the chipotle deepens the umami while adding a slow-building heat that plays against the cool dairy. Drop the pickled jalapeño if you do not want a second heat source.

  • Regional cotija and a creamier finish→crumbled cheese on top

    Crumble 40 g / ⅓ cup cotija over the built tacos for a salty, milky-dry finish, or fold queso fresco into the slaw for a creamier crunch. The salty cheese adds another glutamate layer and a textural contrast to the soft fish; dial the salt in the rub back to 4 g / 1 tsp so the taco does not over-salt.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • striped bass fillet → black sea bass, red snapper, mahi-mahi, or halibut Any firm, meaty white fish with a large flake holds together on the grate. Black sea bass and snapper are near-identical in behavior; mahi-mahi and halibut are firmer still and grill beautifully. Avoid thin, soft-flaked fish like tilapia or sole, which fall apart through the grate bars.
  • green cabbage → red cabbage or a green-and-red mix Red cabbage is sturdier and more peppery and turns the slaw a vivid magenta once the lime hits it; a mix gives the best color and crunch. Napa cabbage works but is softer and weeps faster, so cut the salting time to 10 minutes.
  • pickled jalapeño → fresh serrano, fresh jalapeño, or a few dashes of hot sauce Fresh serrano or jalapeño bring a greener, sharper heat without the vinegar tang; add a squeeze of extra lime to recover the acid the pickling brine supplied. A vinegar-based hot sauce at the table is the simplest swap of all.
  • corn tortillas → flour tortillas Flour tortillas are more pliable and less prone to cracking, though they trade away the toasted corn flavor that defines a Baja taco and add gluten. Warm them the same way. Corn is the canonical and gluten-free choice.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → corn tortillas make it yourself Fresh masa tortillas pressed and griddled to order are softer, more pliable, and carry a toasted-corn aroma that vacuum-packed supermarket tortillas lose; they hold the wet taco fillings far better.
  • → mayonnaise make it yourself A made-to-order emulsion of fresh egg yolk and neutral oil is cleaner and more savory than jarred, and it loosens the lime crema with a richer egg-yolk depth.

Notes

Why grilled, not battered. The canonical Baja fish taco is beer-battered and deep-fried, but a grilled version is lighter, faster, and lets the fish itself carry the dish rather than the crust. Grilling demands a fish firm enough to survive the grate, which is exactly where striped bass earns its place: the large, well-defined muscle blocks separated by short collagen sheets break into clean flakes and hold together over live fire where a soft-flaked fish would shred and drop through the bars. See the striped bass ingredient page for the wild-versus-farmed distinction and the flake structure that makes it the workhorse here.

The char-crust release. The single technique that makes or breaks grilled fish is the release. A ripping-hot, clean, oiled grate sears a crust on the fish surface in the first 3 to 4 minutes that contracts and lifts the flesh off the metal; the strips will tell you when they are ready by releasing without resistance. Move them early and they tear and stick. The dry rub helps: the cumin and smoked paprika provide the surface sugars and proteins that brown into the char, and the oil coat carries the rub and conducts the heat for an even sear. Pat the fish bone-dry first, because surface water steams off and stalls the crust before it can form.

The umami and acid engineering. A grilled white fish is lean and mild, so the taco engineers its savory depth and its brightness into the cold components. The crema is the umami anchor: sour cream brings a lactic tang and the mayonnaise adds a savory egg-yolk glutamate depth, and the seared rub crust on the fish itself contributes the browned-protein savor. Lime is the finishing acid, and it is dosed three separate times: in the slaw, where it both seasons and lightly pickles the cabbage; in the crema, where it cuts the dairy fat; and in the wedges at the table, where a final squeeze brightens the assembled taco. Each dose hits a different layer, which is why a single squeeze at the end is not enough.

The build is a temperature and texture stack. The taco works because it layers contrasts: hot grilled fish, cold crunchy slaw, cool fatty crema, soft avocado, and the sharp vinegar heat of the pickled jalapeño, all in a warm pliable tortilla. Build each taco the moment before eating, since the slaw weeps into the tortilla and the avocado browns if it sits. Double up the tortillas for the wet fillings so the taco holds together in the hand, and serve them as they come off the build rather than plating a batch to go cold.
