Toasted glutinous-rice powder (khao khua)

Raw glutinous rice dry-toasted in a hot skillet to amber, cooled, and ground coarse. The structural element of larb, nam tok, and many Lao dipping sauces; the powder hydrates in the dish dressing and thickens it to a clinging slurry. Aromatic compounds (popcorn pyrazines, toasted-bread furans) develop in the toast and fade quickly once ground; freshly toasted khao khua is dramatically better than commercial pre-ground versions.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The toasted rice must cool to room temperature (5 to 10 minutes) before grinding. Hot rice in a mortar or spice grinder produces a gummy paste rather than a coarse powder; tip the rice onto a cool plate the moment it comes off the heat and let it sit.
  • Doubling or tripling the batch costs no extra time. The powder keeps 2 weeks at full aromatic top in a sealed jar and is the structural finish for larb, nam tok, and Vietnamese thinh applications; a 50 g batch covers about 4 larb dishes.

Mise en Place

Servings

  1. 80 g raw glutinous rice (sticky rice, sweet rice)

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat a heavy dry skillet (cast iron or stainless, no oil) over medium-low heat for 2 minutes. The pan should be hot enough that a single test grain of rice tossed in begins to color within 30 seconds; if it scorches immediately, the heat is too high; if it stays chalk-white after 60 seconds, the heat is too low.
  2. 2
    Add the 80 g / ½ cup raw glutinous rice in a single layer. Toast 6 to 8 minutes total, shaking the pan or stirring with a heatproof spatula every 20 to 30 seconds. The constant motion is non-negotiable, dry-toasting works by direct pan-to-grain conduction without fat as an intermediary; an unmoved grain scorches in 10 to 15 seconds while neighbors stay raw. The color cue is uniform amber, not patchy white-and-brown; the smell cue is popcorn-and-toasted-bread aroma carrying out of the pan. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  3. 3
    Pull at first uniform amber. The window between amber-popcorn and brown-bitter is narrow, 30 seconds late and the rice turns into a dish-ruining acrid powder. Tip the hot rice immediately onto a cool plate or sheet pan to arrest carryover; rice left in the hot skillet continues to color for 60 seconds and crosses the line.
  4. 4
    Cool to room temperature, 5 to 10 minutes. Transfer to a stone mortar or spice grinder. Pound or pulse to a coarse powder, target texture is between coarse sea salt and fine sand; never a fine flour. Each grain should be broken into 4 to 8 fragments, with some smaller dust mixed through. The coarse grind is structural; in larb dressing the powder hydrates to a slurry while keeping a faint grainy textural signature in each bite, which is part of the dish identity. A fine flour absorbs dressing the same way but reads as a thickened sauce rather than as larb.
  5. 5
    Transfer to an airtight glass jar. Use within 2 weeks for full aromatic top; usable for 6 weeks before the toasted aromatics fade meaningfully. Beyond that, the powder still functions as a starch thickener but loses the aromatic-popcorn signature that distinguishes fresh khao khua from commercial. Standard application: 15 g / 2 tbsp per 500 g of meat in larb or nam tok.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→thinh (Vietnamese rice powder)

    Same technique applied to glutinous rice; toast slightly darker (an extra 60 to 90 seconds, into deep amber rather than medium amber) and grind to slightly finer texture (closer to coarse sand than to coarse salt). Used in nem chua (fermented pork rolls), bi cuon (pork-skin rolls), and bun cha thit nuong. The darker toast and finer grind shift the powder toward a more nutty-deep aromatic profile.

  • Regional cuisine→with kaffir lime leaf and lemongrass (Lao spice-rice variant)

    Add 2 dried kaffir lime leaves and a 5 cm / 2 inch piece of dried lemongrass to the skillet for the last 60 seconds of toasting. Cool, grind together. The rice powder picks up a citrus-aromatic top that suits Lao dipping sauces (jeow) and northern Thai larb variants. Not standard for Isaan-style larb but a recognized regional pivot.

  • Occasion flavor→with toasted dried galangal

    Add 5 g / 1 tbsp dried galangal slices to the skillet for the last 90 seconds; cool and grind together. Adds a piney-citrus note that suits gaeng om (Lao herbal soup) and certain northern Thai larb variants.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • glutinous rice → jasmine rice Long-grain jasmine works in a pinch and yields a workable khao khua, but the slightly-sticky mouthfeel that pure-amylopectin starch contributes is missing. Acceptable substitute; not equivalent. Reduce toast time by 60 seconds; jasmine browns slightly faster than glutinous.

Notes

Why glutinous rice and not jasmine. Glutinous rice is essentially pure amylopectin (the highly branched starch); jasmine has 18% amylose (the linear starch). When the toasted powder hydrates in the dressing of larb or nam tok, the amylopectin starch from glutinous rice forms a slightly cohesive slurry that binds dressing to chopped meat with a faintly sticky mouthfeel. Jasmine rice powder hydrates the same way but produces a thinner, less-cohesive slurry; the dish reads as a wetter salad rather than a dressed-and-bound one. The difference is subtle in side-by-side tastings but consistent.

Why dry-toast, not oven-roast. The technique calls specifically for a stovetop dry skillet because the small batch (80 g) needs constant motion and the cook needs to read color and smell in real time. An oven roast at 175 °C / 350 °F technically produces the same chemistry but the cook cannot intervene fast enough at the amber-to-burnt transition; oven khao khua tends to come out either undertoasted or overtoasted. The 6 to 8 minute stovetop toast is the high-control move.

The aromatic top fades fast. The Maillard products developed in toasting are partly volatile; once ground, the surface area exposed to air increases by orders of magnitude and the aromatic top dissipates within days to weeks. A jar of khao khua ground 2 weeks ago is still functional as a starch thickener but reads aromatically flat against fresh-ground; the popcorn-and-toasted-bread top is mostly gone. The Lao home practice is to toast and grind in small batches every 1 to 2 weeks of cooking; commercial khao khua sold in bags at Asian markets is days to months from grinding and reads dull. The 10-minute home toast is structurally not skippable.

Yield and use. 80 g raw glutinous rice yields 50 to 55 g of khao khua after grinding (some loss to skillet residue and grinder dust). At 15 g per 500 g of meat in larb, this is enough for 3 to 4 batches of larb gai, larb moo, or nam tok. Scale up to 200 g raw rice if you cook larb regularly; the same skillet handles up to 250 g of rice in a single layer.

Pan choice and burn risk. A heavy cast iron or stainless skillet is correct; thin aluminum overheats locally and produces patchy scorch even with constant motion. The risk is uniformly distributed: at the right pan temperature with constant motion, every grain colors uniformly; at too-high pan temperature, individual hot spots scorch grains while neighbors stay pale; at too-low pan temperature, the whole batch ends up underdeveloped. Adjust heat by feel: if grains are taking longer than 8 minutes to color, raise heat one notch; if grains are coloring within 4 minutes, lower heat one notch. The first batch in a given pan-burner combination calibrates the rest.
