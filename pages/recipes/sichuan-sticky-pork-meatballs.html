Sichuan sticky pork meatballs in doubanjiang glaze

Bouncy pork meatballs built on salt-solubilized myosin, seared hard for fond, then lacquered in a glaze of fermented broad-bean paste, rock sugar, and soy reduced to a glossy cling. The doubanjiang carries fermented-chili heat and deep glutamate while Chinkiang vinegar off the heat snaps the sweetness back into focus.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Rest the bound mince 15 minutes in the fridge before rolling. Working the pork to a tacky myosin network and then resting it is what gives the meatball its snap; rolling a slack, just-mixed mince produces a softer, looser ball that sheds fat into the glaze.
  • Build the glaze and the meatballs in the same pan in sequence, so start rice or any partner dish first. Once the doubanjiang blooms it moves fast, and the meatballs finish cooking in the reducing glaze.

Execution

  1. 1
    Combine the 565 g / 1.25 lb ground pork with the 4.5 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt and the 1.5 g / ¼ tsp baking soda in a bowl. The salt extracts myosin from the muscle fibers and the baking soda raises pH, which keeps the proteins from gripping into a dry knot on cooking. Add the 12 g / 1 tbsp grated ginger, the 20 g / 3 tbsp minced scallion whites, the 1 g / ½ tsp white pepper, the 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy sauce, the 15 g / 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine, the 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, the 50 g / 1 egg, and the 9 g / 1 tbsp cornstarch. Myosin extraction
  2. 2
    Stir hard in a single direction for 2 to 3 minutes until the mass turns visibly tacky and pulls against the spoon. That drag is the soluble myosin cross-linking into the network that gives a Chinese meatball its bounce. Rest 15 minutes in the fridge to firm. Myosin extraction
  3. 3
    Roll into 16 balls of about 40 g each, wetting your palms to keep them from cracking.
  4. 4
    Heat a heavy skillet over medium-high with the 14 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil to shimmering, around 190°C / 375°F. Lay the meatballs in without crowding and leave them undisturbed for 2 minutes per face. Moving them early tears the forming crust and forfeits the Maillard browning that anchors the dish's savory depth. Roll to brown three or four sides, about 6 minutes total. They will not be cooked through; the glaze finishes them. Transfer to a plate. Pour off all but a thin film of fat, leaving the browned fond. Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Lower heat to medium. Into the same pan add the 24 g / 1.5 tbsp doubanjiang with the 15 g / 3 cloves minced garlic and the 8 g / 2 tsp minced ginger. Bloom in the fat for 60 to 90 seconds until the oil stains red and turns nutty. Watch it: the fermented beans scorch fast and go acrid past that point. Blooming aromatics
  6. 6
    Deglaze with the 30 g / 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine, scraping the fond up, then add the 120 g / ½ cup chicken stock, the 23 g / 1.5 tbsp light soy sauce, the 7 g / 1 tsp dark soy sauce, and the 40 g / 2 tbsp crushed rock sugar. Simmer to dissolve the sugar. Deglazing
  7. 7
    Return the meatballs and any plate juices. Simmer at a steady bubble, 88 to 93°C / 190 to 200°F, turning to coat, until the meatballs read 71°C / 160°F internal and the glaze reduces to a syrup that sheets off a spoon, roughly 5 to 7 minutes. The dissolved sugar raises the boiling point and thickens the liquid into a lacquer that clings rather than runs.
  8. 8
    Pull from the heat. Finish with the 10 g / 2 tsp Chinkiang vinegar and the few drops of toasted sesame oil off the flame: boiling the vinegar would drive off its volatile acetic and malty aromatics and leave the dish flat-sweet. Scatter the 14 g / 2 tbsp scallion greens.
  9. 9
    The Adjustment. If the glaze reads flat and one-dimensionally sweet, add Chinkiang vinegar in ½ tsp increments before more soy; the acid is what cuts the sugar, and that is the first thing missing. If it tastes sweet but hollow, a scant ½ tsp more doubanjiang stirred in at the end pushes the fermented-glutamate floor up. If the glaze breaks into a greasy slick, you reduced too hard and too far; splash in 1 tbsp stock and swirl off-heat to re-emulsify. If the meatballs read rubbery and tight, you took them past 74°C / 165°F and squeezed out moisture; next time pull at 70°C / 158°F and let carryover finish.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→Cantonese honey-soy register

    Drop the doubanjiang entirely; build the glaze from oyster sauce (36 g / 2 tbsp), light soy (15 g / 1 tbsp), and honey (42 g / 2 tbsp) bloomed with the garlic and ginger, and lean on maltose (20 g / 1 tbsp) for lacquer cling. Heat comes only from a sliced fresh red chili if you want it. The result is a milder, glossier glaze.

  • Texture finish→oven-finished for a crustier shell (sheet-pan version)

    Sear in phase 2, then roast the meatballs at 218°C / 425°F for 12 minutes while you reduce the phase 3 glaze to a thick syrup on the stove, and toss the roasted meatballs in it at the end rather than simmering them through.

  • Dietary diet→mushroom-tofu meatball (vegan)

    Replace the pork with a mince of pressed firm tofu (280 g / 10 oz) and finely chopped rehydrated shiitake (45 g dry / 1.5 oz) for the lost glutamate and chew, bound with cornstarch (27 g / 3 tbsp) and a flax egg (7 g / 1 tbsp ground flax in 3 tbsp water); swap the chicken stock for shiitake soaking liquid and confirm the doubanjiang brand is anchovy-free.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • Shaoxing wine → dry sherry Close aromatic match; avoid "cooking wine" with added salt, which throws the seasoning.
  • rock sugar → honey or maltose Maltose gives the stickiest lacquer and most cling; honey browns faster, so watch the reduction.
  • Chinkiang black vinegar → aged balsamic Similar malty-sweet acidity, slightly fruitier and less smoky; use the same volume.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → chicken stock make it yourself A gelatin-rich homemade stock gives the glaze body the carton version cannot, and it carries no added salt to fight the soy and doubanjiang already seasoning the sauce.

Notes

The bounce in a Chinese pork meatball comes from myosin extraction, not from breadcrumbs. Salt is an ionic agent: it dissolves the myosin out of the muscle fibers, and the baking soda's higher pH keeps those proteins from over-tightening. Working the mince in one direction shears the freed myosin into aligned strands, and when those strands hit heat they denature and cross-link into a continuous elastic gel that traps fat and water. The result is the springy, snappy texture (the "Q" of the bite) that a loose, breadcrumb-bound Western meatball cannot produce.

Build the glaze separately rather than pouring liquid over the meatballs in the pan: the doubanjiang has to bloom in fat to stain the oil red and turn nutty, and that only happens against the bare hot pan, not in a wet braise. Once it has bloomed, the deglaze and the sugar build the lacquer, and the meatballs finish cooking in it.
