Shaoxing wine (绍兴酒)

Glutinous rice and wheat fermented with qu starter for 6 to 12 months, producing the amber rice wine that anchors most Chinese cooking. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 3 items ▾

Pantry 2

  1. 2000 g Glutinous rice (sticky rice, sweet rice)
  2. 30 g qu starter (Chinese rice-wine starter; jiu qu)

Other 1

  1. 3000 ml filtered water

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: soak 2000 g / 4.4 lb glutinous rice in cold water for 8 hours; drain. Steam over rapidly boiling water for 60 minutes (in a bamboo steamer with a damp cheesecloth liner, or in a metal steamer with similar humidity protection). The rice should be fully translucent and fully tender, with no white starch core remaining. Spread on a clean tray to cool to body temperature (30 to 35 °C / 86 to 95 °F); rice that is too hot kills the starter, rice that is too cool ferments slowly.9 h
  2. 2
    Stub: when the rice is at body temperature, sprinkle 30 g / 2 tbsp qu starter evenly across the surface and toss gently with clean hands to distribute. Pack loosely into a clean fermentation crock or food-grade plastic bucket. Make a well in the center of the rice (this fills with the resulting wine over the next several days). Cover with a fitted lid that allows gas escape (a fermentation airlock or a loose-fitting lid).
  3. 3
    Stub: ferment at 25 to 30 °C / 77 to 86 °F for 5 to 7 days; the qu mold breaks down the rice starch into glucose, the yeast in the qu starter ferments the glucose into ethanol, and the well in the center of the rice fills with sweet rice wine. After 5 to 7 days, add the 3000 ml / 12 cups filtered water and stir to break up the rice mash; the resulting cloudy liquid is the moromi for the secondary ferment.6 d 0 h
  4. 4
    Stub: continue fermentation at 18 to 22 °C / 65 to 72 °F (cooler than the primary ferment) for 4 to 8 weeks. The yeast continues to ferment the residual sugars and the alcohol level rises to 14 to 16 percent. After the secondary ferment, strain the moromi through cheesecloth, transfer the clear liquid to clean glass bottles, and age in a cool dark place for 6 to 12 months. The aging develops complex aromatic compounds through slow oxidation and esterification; the finished wine is amber, smells distinctly winey-fruity, and tastes dry-savory with a faint sweetness.115 d 6 h

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the qu (麯) chemistry (the starter is a complex mixed culture of Rhizopus mold, Aspergillus mold, and various yeast species; the molds break down rice starch into glucose via amylase enzymes, and the yeasts ferment the glucose into ethanol; the simultaneous saccharification and fermentation is the technical defining feature of Chinese rice wine production), the qu sourcing (Chinese groceries sell dried qu balls or qu powder under various names: jiu qu, jiu yao, koji-equivalents; the home-brewing community has sources for high-quality jiu qu suitable for unattended ferments), the difference from Japanese sake (sake uses pure Aspergillus oryzae koji on rice, then a separate yeast culture; the simpler enzyme system produces a cleaner, less-complex wine; Chinese rice wine''s mixed culture produces more aromatic complexity), the difference from supermarket "Shaoxing cooking wine" (most US-supermarket Shaoxing cooking wine is dilute rice wine adulterated with salt to make it ineligible for ABC alcohol regulation; the salt makes it unsuitable for many cooking applications; real Shaoxing wine sold at Asian markets without salt added is the right comparison), the regional Shaoxing styles (yuan hong jiu, the most basic; jia fan jiu, with extra rice for sweetness; shan niang jiu, aged longer; hua diao jiu, aged 5 to 20 years and the apex of Shaoxing aging), the sweetness levels (gan, dry; ban gan, semi-dry; ban tian, semi-sweet; tian, sweet; the cooking-grade Shaoxing is typically gan or ban gan), the use cases beyond cooking (drinking warm in winter, serving with rich braised dishes, the canonical accompaniment to Shanghainese xiaolongbao), the aging chemistry (slow oxidation and esterification produce complex aromatic compounds; properly-aged Shaoxing develops a dried-cherry-and-walnut profile that home production can approximate but the multi-decade aged commercial product is hard to match), the failure modes (vinegar souring if oxygen exposure is too high during the ferment; off-flavors from temperature swings during aging; insufficient alcohol if the qu is weak or expired; the safety bar is lower than for soy sauce because the alcohol content prevents pathogenic bacteria), the storage (the finished bottled wine keeps 2 to 3 years at room temperature; refrigerate after opening, use within 6 months for cooking applications, longer for drinking).
