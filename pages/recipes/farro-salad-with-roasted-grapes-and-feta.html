Farro salad with roasted grapes and feta

Chewy semi-pearled farro tossed warm with hot-roasted red grapes (whose sugars concentrate and skins burst jammy at 220 °C), crumbled sheep's-milk feta, toasted walnuts, and a sherry vinaigrette cut sharp to balance the grape sweetness. A composed Mediterranean grain salad built on one controlled contrast: blistered, almost-savory roasted fruit against salty brined cheese, with the warm grain absorbing the rendered grape syrup and sherry acid into every bite.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Two tracks run in parallel and finish together: the farro (25 to 30 min simmer) and the grapes (25 to 30 min roast at 220 °C). Start the oven preheat AND the farro pot at the same moment, before any other prep. Toast the walnuts in the oven during its preheat so they are done before the grapes go in.
  • Use semi-pearled farro, not whole-grain. Whole farro needs an overnight soak and a 50-plus-minute simmer; if that is all you have, soak it the night before or the timing collapses.
  • Dress and toss the farro while it is still warm, not cold. Warm grain absorbs the sherry vinaigrette and the rendered grape syrup; refrigerator-cold grain sheds the dressing and eats gummy. Build the vinaigrette while the grain simmers so it is ready the moment the farro drains.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, farro

  1. 200 g semi-pearled farro, rinsed in a fine sieve until the water runs clear
  2. 1500 ml water
  3. 12 g kosher salt
  4. 1 each bay leaf — (1 leaf; perfumes the cooking water, pulled before tossing)

Phase 2, roasted grapes

  1. 500 g seedless red grapes, pulled off the stems, rinsed, patted fully dry
  2. 15 g extra-virgin olive oil
  3. 2 g fine sea salt
  4. 3 g fresh thyme sprigs — (3 sprigs; roasted whole with the grapes, leaves stripped and folded in after)

Phase 3, sherry vinaigrette

  1. 30 g sherry vinegar
  2. 25 g shallot, fine-minced, minced to 1 to 2 mm and steeped in the vinegar for 10 min before the oil goes in — (1 small shallot; the acid soak mellows its raw bite and seasons the dressing)
  3. 6 g dijon mustard
  4. 7 g honey
  5. 60 g extra-virgin olive oil
  6. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Phase 4, assembly

  1. 60 g walnuts, toasted, cooled, roughly chopped
  2. 120 g feta, sheep's-milk PDO, broken into rough 1 cm crumbles by hand, not crumbled fine
  3. 15 g flat-leaf parsley, leaves picked, roughly chopped
  4. 8 g fresh mint, leaves torn

Execution

  1. 1
    Start both tracks at once. Set the oven to 220 °C / 425 °F. Bring the 1500 ml / 6 cups water to a boil in a medium pot with the 12 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt and the 1 bay leaf. While the oven preheats, spread the 60 g / ½ cup walnuts on a small dry tray and toast in the heating oven for 6 to 8 minutes until fragrant and one shade darker, then pull and cool; toasting hydrolyzes the surface tannins and develops nutty pyrazines, raw walnuts read astringent and flat here. Set the cooled nuts aside to chop later.
  2. 2
    Cook the farro. When the water boils, add the rinsed 200 g / 1 cup farro and cook uncovered at a steady simmer, pasta-style in the abundant water, for 25 to 30 minutes until the grains are tender with a distinct chew at the center (al dente, not blown out). Drain in a sieve, discard the bay leaf, and let the farro steam-dry in the sieve for 2 minutes so the surface is not waterlogged. Tip into a wide bowl; keep it warm.
  3. 3
    Roast the grapes. While the farro simmers, toss the 500 g / 3½ cups dried red grapes with the 15 g / 1 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil and the 2 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt on a rimmed sheet pan in a single layer, tucking the 3 g / 3 thyme sprigs among them. Roast at 220 °C / 425 °F for 25 to 30 minutes until the skins blister and split, the grapes slump, and dark syrup pools and caramelizes at the pan edges. Drying the grapes first is the difference between roasting and steaming; surface water has to flash off before the skins can blister. Scrape the grapes and every bit of the rendered pan syrup into a small bowl; that syrup is concentrated grape acid and sugar, the flavor core of the dish. Strip the thyme leaves from the spent sprigs back over the grapes and discard the stems. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Build the vinaigrette. In a small bowl, combine the 30 g / 2 tbsp sherry vinegar with the 25 g / 1 shallot fine-minced and a pinch of the 2 g fine sea salt; let the shallot steep in the acid for 10 minutes to mellow its raw alliin bite and season the vinegar. Whisk in the 6 g / 1 tsp dijon mustard and the 7 g / 1 tsp honey until smooth, then stream in the 60 g / ¼ cup extra-virgin olive oil while whisking hard to emulsify. Whisk in the 1 g / ½ tsp cracked black pepper. The ratio is a deliberate 2:1 oil-to-vinegar, sharper than a standard 3:1, because the roasted grapes bring real sweetness and a soft vinaigrette would let the salad slide flat. Taste; it should read aggressively bright on its own. Pan emulsion (mantecare)
  5. 5
    Dress the warm farro. While the farro is still warm, pour about two-thirds of the sherry vinaigrette over it and toss; warm grain drinks the dressing into the starch, which cold grain cannot do. Add the roasted grapes along with all the rendered grape syrup from the bowl and fold gently so the grapes stay mostly intact, streaking the farro with dark syrup. The warm grain and warm syrup marry here; taste and add kosher salt only if the feta to come will not cover it.
  6. 6
    Finish and plate. Let the dressed farro cool 5 minutes off the heat so the residual warmth does not melt the cheese to paste. Fold in the toasted 60 g / ½ cup chopped walnuts, the 15 g / ½ cup chopped parsley, and the torn 8 g / ¼ cup mint. Scatter the 120 g / 1 cup hand-crumbled feta over the top and fold once, gently, leaving visible white crumbles rather than smearing the cheese through. Drizzle the reserved one-third of the vinaigrette over the assembled salad. Taste a forkful that includes a grape, a feta crumble, and a nut; it should land sweet, salty, sharp, and crunchy in one bite. Adjust with a final crack of black pepper or a few drops more sherry vinegar if the sweetness is winning. Serve warm or at room temperature.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture grain→pearled barley, freekeh, or wheat berries

    Swap farro one-for-one by weight, adjusting the simmer time: pearled barley 25 to 30 min, cracked freekeh 20 min (and it brings a smoky note that reads beautifully against the roasted grapes), whole wheat berries 50 to 60 min with an overnight soak. Cook all of them pasta-style in salted water and dress warm exactly as written. Freekeh is the upgrade here for its built-in smoke.

  • Regional roasted fruit→roasted figs or roasted red plums

    In late summer, halved Black Mission figs or quartered red plums roast the same way at 220 °C for 18 to 22 min (less time, they hold more water and collapse faster than grapes). Both bring a deeper jam and more acid; cut the honey in the vinaigrette to a scant ½ tsp since plums in particular roast sweeter. The dish moves from autumn toward high summer.

  • Protein protein, to make it a full main→add roasted chickpeas or torn roast chicken

    For a vegetarian main, roast 240 g / 1½ cups drained chickpeas (patted dry, tossed in 1 tbsp olive oil and ½ tsp smoked paprika) on a second pan alongside the grapes for 25 min until crisp, and fold them in with the walnuts. For a meat version, fold in 250 g / 2 cups torn roast or rotisserie chicken with the herbs. Either pushes the salad from side to centerpiece without touching the core balance.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • sherry vinegar → red wine vinegar or aged balsamic (less) Red wine vinegar is the closest accessible swap, slightly less nutty and round; use the same 2 tbsp. Aged balsamic works but is sweeter and darker, so cut it to 4 tsp and drop the honey entirely, or the dressing tips sweet against the already-sweet grapes.
  • feta, sheep's-milk PDO → ricotta salata, French feta, or aged goat cheese Ricotta salata is firmer and milder, a good choice if you want the salt without the funk; French feta is creamier and less tangy than Greek; aged goat cheese (a dry crottin) brings more barnyard funk and pairs hard with the roasted grapes. Avoid cow's-milk "feta-style" blocks, they read rubbery and bland.
  • walnuts → pecans, hazelnuts, or pistachios Pecans are sweeter and softer; hazelnuts (toasted and skinned) read more aromatic and pair classically with grapes; pistachios bring color and a resinous note. Toast all of them the same way to drive off raw astringency.
  • fresh mint → fresh basil or omit Torn basil is an excellent swap and leans the salad more Italian; a handful of arugula folded in at the end works too for a peppery bite. Omitting the mint is fine, the parsley still carries the green lift.

Notes

Why roast the grapes hard. A grape warmed gently in the oven is just a hot grape: still mostly raw sugar and water, with the skin intact. The transformation that makes this dish happen above 200 °C, where surface water flashes off, the skin loses turgor and blisters, and the interior sugars concentrate and begin to caramelize at the pan contact points. A fully roasted grape reads almost savory: jammy, faintly bittered at the skin, with its acidity sharpened by water loss. That is the ingredient the salad is built around, not raw fruit. The single most common failure is crowding the pan or skipping the pat-dry step, both of which trap moisture and steam the grapes pale instead of roasting them dark. One layer, dried surfaces, full heat.

Why dress the grain warm. Cooked grain starch is far more absorbent while warm; the gelatinized starch surface takes up dressing and the rendered grape syrup into the grain rather than letting it pool at the bottom of the bowl. Farro dressed cold from the refrigerator sheds the vinaigrette and eats gummy and flat. This is also why the salad is genuinely make-ahead in a useful way: dress the warm farro with the grapes and two-thirds of the vinaigrette, refrigerate, and the grain will have drunk the flavors overnight; bring it back to room temperature and add the cheese, nuts, herbs, and reserved dressing just before serving so those stay crisp and bright.

Why the vinaigrette runs sharp. The default vinaigrette ratio is 3:1 oil to acid, tuned for raw bitter greens that need the oil to soften their bite. This salad is the opposite problem: the roasted grapes and the touch of honey bring real sweetness, so a standard soft vinaigrette would let the whole bowl slide toward flat-sweet. The 2:1 ratio here is deliberate, the acid has to push back against the sugar for the salad to read balanced rather than dessert-adjacent. Taste the dressing on its own and trust that it should seem too sharp in isolation; against the grapes it lands correct.

Umami and acid, accounted for. The glutamate load comes from the brined sheep''s-milk feta, which is salt-and-protein-dense and reads as savory depth against the fruit. The finishing acid is the sherry vinegar in the dressing, reinforced by the concentrated acid of the roasted grape syrup itself. Both are structural, not optional; a version with under-salted cheese and a soft vinaigrette tastes like sweet wet grain.

The Mediterranean register. This sits in the composed-grain-salad tradition that runs across Greece, southern Italy, and the Levant: a chewy whole grain, a sweet roasted or dried fruit, a salty brined cheese, toasted nuts, fresh herbs, olive oil and a bright vinegar. The grape-and-feta pairing is squarely Greek and southern-Italian in spirit. Keep the olive oil generous and good, use real PDO feta rather than a cow''s-milk imitation, and let the herbs go in raw at the end rather than cooking out their volatiles.
