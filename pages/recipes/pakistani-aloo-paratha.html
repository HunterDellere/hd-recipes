Recipe

Pakistani aloo paratha (potato-stuffed flatbread)

Whole-wheat atta dough stuffed with a spiced mashed-potato filling, rolled into a thin disc, cooked on a dry tava with brushed ghee until brown-spotted on both sides. The canonical Punjabi-Pakistani breakfast: a flatbread sturdy enough to be a meal on its own, served with plain yogurt, mango pickle, and chai.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The dough needs at least 20 minutes of rest after kneading; the gluten relaxes and the dough becomes elastic enough to roll thinly without tearing. Skipping the rest produces dough that springs back when rolled and tears around the filling.
  • Boil and rice the potatoes BEFORE starting the dough. The potatoes need to cool to room temperature before being mixed with the spices and stuffed; warm potatoes release steam that softens the dough and makes stuffing impossible.

  4. 15 g fresh cilantro, finely chopped
  5. 5 g ground coriander
  6. 3 g ground cumin
  7. 3 g chaat masala
  8. 3 g Kashmiri chili powder
  9. 6 g kosher salt

Phase 3, cooking and finishing

  1. 50 g atta flour (for dusting the work surface)
  2. 60 g ghee (for brushing the parathas as they cook)

Phase 4, service

  1. 200 g plain whole-milk yogurt
  2. 60 g mango pickle (achar) or lime pickle

Execution

  1. 1
    Boil the potatoes. Peel and quarter the 500 g potatoes; boil in salted water 15 to 18 minutes until a knife slides through with no resistance. Drain; spread on a tray to cool to room temperature (about 20 minutes; do not skip this, warm potatoes release steam that wrecks the dough seal).
  2. 2
    Mix the dough. In a wide bowl, combine the 300 g atta flour and 4 g fine sea salt. Add the 15 g melted ghee; rub into the flour with fingertips until the mixture looks like coarse sand. Add the 180 ml warm water gradually, mixing with one hand until a shaggy dough forms. Knead 6 to 8 minutes by hand (or 4 minutes in a stand mixer with the dough hook) until smooth, elastic, and slightly tacky but not sticky. Cover with a damp cloth; rest 20 minutes at room temperature.
  3. 3
    Prepare the filling. Rice the cooled potatoes through a potato ricer (or mash thoroughly with a fork; do not use a food processor, the potatoes go gummy from over-processing). Add the 8 g grated ginger, 5 g minced green chili, 15 g chopped cilantro, 5 g ground coriander, 3 g ground cumin, 3 g chaat masala, 3 g Kashmiri chili powder, and 6 g kosher salt. Mix gently with a fork until uniform. Divide into 6 equal portions (about 90 g each); roll each into a ball.
  4. 4
    Stuff the parathas. After the dough rest, divide the dough into 6 equal portions (about 80 g each); roll each into a ball. Dust the work surface lightly with atta. Roll one dough ball into a 12 cm / 5 in disc with a rolling pin. Place a potato filling ball in the center. Bring the edges of the dough up over the filling, pleating as you go, and pinch firmly at the top to seal completely (the dough should fully envelop the filling, with no exposed filling at the top). Flatten gently with your palm into a stuffed disc about 10 cm / 4 in diameter.
  5. 5
    Roll the stuffed disc. Dust both sides lightly with atta. Roll gently and evenly from the center outward, rotating the paratha a quarter turn after each roll, into an 18 to 20 cm / 7 to 8 in disc about 3 mm / ⅛ in thick. Roll firmly enough to thin the dough but not so hard that the filling breaks through (some small breaks are inevitable and acceptable; major breaks mean the filling-to-dough ratio is off and the next paratha needs slightly less filling).
  6. 6
    Cook the first side. Heat a heavy cast-iron tava, skillet, or comal over medium-high heat (the surface should be hot enough that a drop of water dances and evaporates in 2 seconds). Transfer the rolled paratha to the dry surface. Cook 60 to 90 seconds, until the bottom shows small brown spots and the top surface looks dry and matte (the dough is no longer wet-looking).
  7. 7
    Flip and ghee the first side. Flip the paratha. Brush the now-up side (the previously-cooked side) with about 1 tsp of melted ghee. Cook the second side 60 to 90 seconds until similarly brown-spotted.
  8. 8
    Second flip and ghee the second side. Flip again. Brush the now-up side with another 1 tsp of ghee. Cook 30 to 45 seconds, pressing gently with a spatula in 2 to 3 spots to encourage the layers to puff (some of the steam from the cooking potato filling will inflate the paratha slightly; this is the visual signal that the layers are cooked through). Transfer to a plate; cover with a clean kitchen towel to keep warm.
  9. 9
    Repeat for the remaining 5 parathas (steps 4 through 8). Stack the finished parathas under the towel; they stay warm and pliable. Serve immediately with the 200 g plain yogurt, 60 g mango or lime pickle, and a glass of masala chai. Each diner tears off bites with their fingers, scooping yogurt and pickle.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein filling→gobi (cauliflower) paratha

    Replace the potato with 500 g finely grated cauliflower, salted with 6 g salt and rested 15 minutes, then squeezed dry through a clean towel (the cauliflower releases significant water that must be removed or the dough will not seal). Use the same spice mix. Roll and cook as written. The dish reads similarly textured but with the distinct sulfurous-vegetal note of cauliflower.

  • Protein filling→mooli (daikon radish) paratha

    Replace the potato with 500 g finely grated daikon, salted with 6 g salt and rested 20 minutes, then squeezed dry through a clean towel. Use the same spice mix plus 1 tsp of ajwain seeds (carom seeds) for the canonical mooli paratha aromatic. The dish is the Punjabi winter favorite, slightly more pungent than aloo paratha.

  • Protein cuisine pivot→keema paratha (Pakistani minced-meat stuffed)

    Replace the potato filling with 500 g spiced cooked keema (minced beef or lamb sautéed with onion, ginger-garlic, and garam masala until dry). Squeeze the keema in a fine sieve to drain residual fat before stuffing. The dish becomes a structurally distinct meat-stuffed paratha and is a Pakistani Eid breakfast tradition.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • atta flour → a 50/50 blend of whole wheat flour and all-purpose flour Atta is the structural correct flour, finer and slightly higher in gluten than American whole wheat. The blend approximates the structure; the dish reads recognizably correct.
  • ghee → unsalted butter (or refined coconut oil for vegan) Butter is acceptable but the dish loses some of the structural toasted-milk-solid aromatic that defines paratha. Coconut oil reads vegan but adds a distinct coconut note that competes with the Pakistani spice profile.
  • chaat masala → amchoor (dried mango powder) + a pinch of black salt Chaat masala is the canonical spice blend; amchoor and black salt are its two structural carriers. The dish reads slightly flatter but recognizable.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → chaat masala make it yourselfstub Homemade chaat masala is dramatically more aromatic than supermarket pre-ground; the volatile aromatics (especially the black salt sulfur compounds and the amchoor citric notes) oxidize within months once ground.

Notes

Why riced potatoes and not mashed. A potato ricer (or a thorough fork mash) produces a uniform, slightly fluffy texture without overworking the potato. A food processor or hand blender produces gummy, sticky potato that releases too much starch and creates a paratha filling that smears against the dough rather than sitting cleanly inside. The texture target is fluffy crumbs that hold together when pressed but break apart easily; gummy potato makes the paratha greasy.

The seal is structural. The pinched-and-sealed top of the stuffed disc is what keeps the filling contained during rolling. If the seal breaks during rolling, the filling spills out and the paratha becomes a folded chapati rather than a stuffed one. The technique is conservative: roll the initial 12 cm disc thick enough that the gathered-and-pinched edges have material to work with, and pinch firmly enough that the seal will not pop open under rolling pressure. Some small ruptures are inevitable; tolerate a few small brown spots where filling shows through. Major ruptures mean the filling-to-dough ratio is off; reduce filling by 10 g per ball for the next round.

Ghee timing. The two-stage ghee application (first side, then second side after the first flip) gives the paratha its characteristic layered crisp texture. Brushing ghee on the dough before the first cook causes the paratha to fry rather than puff; brushing only after the first cook lets the dough set its initial structure on the dry tava, then the ghee crisps the layers in the second cook. The technique is structural; skipping the ghee or applying it at the wrong time produces a softer, less complex paratha that reads more like a stuffed chapati.

Pakistani breakfast context. Aloo paratha is the canonical Pakistani Punjabi breakfast, served at home and at dhabas (roadside truck-stop restaurants) across the country. The standard service is two parathas per adult diner, with a small bowl of plain yogurt for cooling and a small spoonful of intensely flavored mango pickle for contrast, accompanied by hot strong masala chai. The whole meal is structural: the paratha is dense and filling, the yogurt cools and lubricates, the pickle provides the acid-and-spice contrast, the chai cuts through the fat. Eaten alone without these accompaniments, the paratha reads heavier; the Pakistani breakfast plate is a designed system.

Storage. Cooked parathas keep 24 hours wrapped in foil at room temperature, or 5 days refrigerated, or 3 months frozen (interleaved with parchment in a zip-top bag). Reheat directly on a dry hot tava for 30 seconds per side, or in a 175°C / 350°F oven for 5 minutes, or in a dry skillet over medium heat. Do not microwave; the paratha goes from crisp to rubbery instantly. Frozen parathas can be cooked directly from frozen on a hot tava (1 minute per side); no thawing required.
