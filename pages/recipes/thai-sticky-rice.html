Thai sticky rice (khao niao)

Glutinous rice soaked, then steamed in a bamboo basket over simmering water until each grain is translucent, chewy, and pinch-able. The table rice of Laos and northeast Thailand; the eating implement for larb, nam tok, and som tum. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 3 items ▾
  1. 400 g raw glutinous rice (sticky rice)
  2. 1500 ml cold water (for soaking)
  3. 1000 ml water (for steaming)

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: rinse the 400 g raw glutinous rice in a bowl of cold water, agitating with the fingers, until the water runs from cloudy to mostly clear, 3 to 4 changes. Cover with 1500 ml / 6 cups cold water and soak 4 to 8 hours (or overnight). The soak hydrates the dense glutinous-rice starch through the grain; an unsoaked grain steams unevenly with a hard chalky center.
  2. 2
    Stub: drain the soaked rice, rinse once more, and shake dry. Line a bamboo steamer basket (a Thai/Lao huat) with cheesecloth or pre-dampened muslin, mound the rice in the center in a single dome about 5 cm / 2 inches thick. Set the basket over a pot or a thai sticky-rice steamer pot of simmering water (the rice should sit above, not touching, the water surface).
  3. 3
    Stub: steam, covered, 20 to 25 minutes, until the grains are uniformly translucent and chewy-soft when pinched between fingers. Halfway through (10 to 12 minutes), flip the rice mound by lifting the cloth and inverting the rice block back onto the cloth; the bottom-now-top finishes cooking on the previously-up surface.
  4. 4
    Stub: tip the cooked rice into a covered bamboo serving basket (the canonical Lao/Isaan presentation), or onto a plate covered with a damp cloth. The rice stays warm and pinch-able for 30 to 60 minutes; serve while warm. Eat by hand, pinching small balls of rice and pressing them into larb or som tum to pick up dressing-and-meat.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the chemistry of why glutinous rice must be steamed rather than boiled (pure-amylopectin starch goes gluey under boiling water but holds discrete-grain cohesion under steam); the role of the 4-hour soak (gradual water penetration through the dense endosperm); pot-and-basket geometry (water level should not touch the bottom of the basket; the steamer should be sealed but not pressure-locked); the salt question (sticky rice is canonically unsalted, salt undermines the cohesion); the storage and reheat protocol (cooked sticky rice goes hard in the fridge but reheats perfectly with a 5-minute re-steam); the pad thai sticky rice cousin (mango sticky rice with coconut milk and palm sugar uses the same base technique); the rice-cooker shortcut (acceptable, but produces a wetter looser grain that does not pinch into balls as cleanly).
