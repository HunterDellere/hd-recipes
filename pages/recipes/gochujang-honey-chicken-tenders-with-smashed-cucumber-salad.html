Recipe

Gochujang-honey chicken tenders with smashed cucumber salad

Chicken tenderloins quick-brined in soy, ginger, and garlic, then dusted in cornstarch for a thin Maillard crust and seared in a smoking carbon-steel pan. Off-heat, the same pan builds a glaze of gochujang, honey, rice vinegar, and toasted sesame oil that lacquers the tenders in 30 seconds. Served with smashed Persian cucumbers salt-drained then tossed in rice vinegar, gochugaru, garlic, and sesame, the cool acid-and-crunch foil to the warm sweet-spicy glaze. Lean, fast (under 30 minutes), and structurally healthier than fried tenders: no breading, no deep-fry, the carbs come from a single tablespoon of honey rather than a sugar-loaded bottled sauce.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Pat the chicken tenderloins fully dry with paper towels before salting. Surface moisture dissolves the salt rather than letting it dry-brine, and wet protein steams in the pan instead of building Maillard browning.
  • Start the smashed cucumber 10 minutes before the chicken hits the pan. The salt-drain takes 10 minutes minimum to draw the surface water that would otherwise dilute the dressing; the dressing itself benefits from a 5-minute rest so the raw garlic mellows. Do the cucumber first, then mise the chicken.
  • Have the glaze pre-mixed in a small bowl before the chicken touches the pan. The off-heat glaze toss is a 30-second move; once the chicken comes off the heat you cannot stop to whisk gochujang into vinegar.

Execution

  1. 1
    Start the cucumbers. Lay the 400 g cucumbers on a cutting board and smash each one along its length with the broad side of a heavy knife or a rolling pin until the skin fractures and the cucumber splits open. Tear them apart by hand into rough 3 cm pieces, irregular shapes are correct. Transfer to a colander or sieve set over a bowl, scatter with the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt, toss to coat, and let drain at room temperature for 10 to 15 minutes while you mise the rest. The cucumber will release 30 to 50 g of water; that water is what you do not want in the salad.
  2. 2
    Mix the cucumber dressing. In a serving bowl, whisk together the 20 g / 4 tsp rice vinegar, the 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, the 4 g / 1 small clove microplane-grated garlic, the 4 g / 2 tsp gochugaru, and the 4 g / 1 tsp granulated sugar until the sugar dissolves. Let stand at room temperature 5 minutes so the raw garlic mellows into the acid.
  3. 3
    Dry-brine and dust the chicken. Pat the 600 g chicken tenderloins fully dry with paper towels. Lay them flat in a shallow bowl or rimmed plate. Toss with the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt, the 6 g / 1 tbsp grated ginger, and the 10 g / 2 tsp light soy sauce. Massage briefly so every tender is evenly coated. Let stand at room temperature for 5 to 8 minutes; the ginger protease starts breaking down the surface proteins while the salt draws a thin layer of moisture into the meat. Then scatter the 20 g / 3 tbsp cornstarch over the tenders and toss to coat each one in a thin even film. The surface should look matte and slightly tacky, not floury-white. If pockets of dry cornstarch persist, drizzle a teaspoon of water in and toss again. Velveting
  4. 4
    Mix the glaze. In a small bowl, whisk together the 45 g / 3 tbsp gochujang, the 30 g / 1½ tbsp honey, the 15 g / 1 tbsp rice vinegar, the 10 g / 2 tsp light soy sauce, the 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, and the 8 g / 2 cloves of grated garlic until smooth and pourable. The texture should be the consistency of ketchup; if it is paste-stiff, thin with a teaspoon of water.
  5. 5
    Finish the cucumber salad. Tip the salt-drained cucumbers into the bowl of dressing (do not rinse the cucumbers, the residual salt seasons the salad). Toss to coat. Scatter the 3 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds over the top. Set aside at room temperature, the salad eats best in the 5 to 30 minute window after dressing, while the cucumbers are still crisp and the gochugaru is hydrating into the dressing.
  6. 6
    Heat the pan. Place a 30 cm / 12-inch carbon-steel or nonstick skillet over medium-high heat for 2 to 3 minutes until the pan is hot enough that a drop of water beads and dances (Leidenfrost effect, indicating about 175 °C / 350 °F surface temperature). Add the 20 g / 1½ tbsp neutral oil and tilt to coat the surface; the oil should ripple and shimmer within 5 seconds.
  7. 7
    Sear the chicken. Lay the dusted tenders in the pan in a single layer, leaving 1 cm of space between each one (work in two batches if needed, crowding produces steam and the starch crust never browns). They should sizzle the second they touch the pan, an audible loud sizzle is the cue that the temperature is right. Leave them undisturbed for 3 minutes; resist the urge to lift and peek. The first side builds the starch-and-protein Maillard crust in one uninterrupted contact with the hot iron, and lifting at 90 seconds breaks the contact and restarts the clock. Maillard sear
  8. 8
    Flip and finish the sear. After 3 minutes, lift the corner of one tender with tongs; the underside should be deep golden-brown with the starch crust looking lacquered, not pale. If it is still tan, give it another 30 to 45 seconds. Flip each tender to the second side. Sear undisturbed another 2 to 3 minutes; the second side wants less time because the interior is mostly cooked through from the first sear. The target internal temperature for the chicken at this point is 70 to 72 °C / 158 to 162 °F. Pull a thicker tender from the pan, slice it in half across the grain; the interior should look just-set opaque white with no translucent pink at the center, no visible raw seam. Maillard sear
  9. 9
    Off-heat glaze toss. Pull the pan completely off the burner. Wait 15 seconds for the pan to drop below 140 °C (the glaze sugars scorch hard above 160 °C; the sugar-and-fermented-paste combination tips from glossy to burnt-bitter in 20 seconds at full heat). Pour the glaze over the tenders. The residual pan heat will bring the glaze to a sputtering reduction in 20 to 30 seconds, the sauce will thicken visibly as the water boils off and the paste gels. Use tongs to roll each tender through the glaze until lacquered on every surface, then transfer to a serving platter. Blooming aromatics
  10. 10
    Plate. Arrange the glazed tenders on the platter or over rice. Spoon any remaining glaze from the pan over the top. Scatter with the 30 g / 3 thin-sliced scallions and the 3 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds. The cucumber salad goes alongside in a separate bowl, the cool acid is the structural foil to the warm sweet-spicy glaze. Serve immediately with the optional lime wedges for cooks who want a sharper finish.
Safety
  • Pulling chicken tenders at 70 to 72 °C / 158 to 162 °F internal (below the USDA published-safe 165 °F for poultry) The USDA published-safe instantaneous target is 165 °F (74 °C). The USDA-FSIS pasteurization table publishes the equivalent holds: 160 °F (71 °C) held 14 seconds achieves the same 7-log Salmonella reduction. Tenders pulled at 70 to 72 °C off the pan continue to climb to 75 to 78 °C during the 30 to 60 seconds of off-heat glaze toss and the 1 to 2 minutes between plating and eating, spending well over 14 seconds above 160 °F. The carryover plus the residual pan contact clears pasteurization comfortably. Cooks who prefer the published-safe number should pull at 165 °F (74 °C), accepting noticeably drier meat. Ground chicken, mechanically tenderized cuts, and pre-marinated commercial chicken should always be cooked to the instantaneous 165 °F since the surface bacteria may be distributed through the mass. Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion cooking method→air-fryer

    After the cornstarch dust, spray or brush the tenders lightly with neutral oil and air-fry at 200 °C / 400 °F for 8 to 10 minutes, flipping once at 5 minutes. Transfer to a wide bowl, pour the glaze directly over the hot tenders, and toss to coat. The air-fryer gives a crisper exterior at the cost of less Maillard depth than a hard pan-sear; the trade is hands-off cooking, useful when you are also building rice and the salad simultaneously.

  • Dietary protein→tofu (extra-firm, pressed)

    Substitute 600 g of pressed extra-firm tofu, cut into 2 cm thick batons. Skip the ginger brine; toss the tofu directly in the cornstarch with 6 g salt and 4 g of grated ginger mixed in. Pan-sear identically; the tofu needs 4 minutes per side rather than 3 for the cornstarch crust to develop. The dish reads cleanly vegan and the gochujang glaze sticks to the cornstarch crust as well or better than it does to chicken.

  • Heat heat dial→mild family-friendly

    Drop the gochujang to 20 g, drop the gochugaru in the cucumber salad to 1 g, and add 15 g of additional honey and 5 g of additional rice vinegar to the glaze. The dish reads sweet-savory with a gentle warm tail rather than a heat-forward bite, suitable for cooks feeding children or heat-sensitive diners.

  • Dietary rice-pairing direction→lettuce-wrap bowl (low-carb)

    Skip the rice. Serve the tenders sliced across the grain into 2 cm pieces in butter-lettuce or romaine leaves, with the cucumber salad spooned alongside, plus a sprinkle of toasted rice powder for crunch. The plate reads like Korean ssam (lettuce-wrap meat) with a Korean-American glaze register; the lettuce contributes water and crunch that the rice would have provided as starch.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • chicken tenderloins → boneless skinless chicken breast, cut into 2 cm thick strips against the grain Cut a 600 g boneless skinless breast into 2 cm thick strips on a slight bias against the grain (the muscle fibers run lengthwise; the bias cut shortens them). The cooking time is the same; the strips eat slightly firmer than true tenders because the breast meat is denser than the pectoralis minor.
  • gochugaru → red pepper flakes (use half the quantity) or aleppo pepper (use the same quantity) Red pepper flakes (Italian style) are sharper and hotter; halve the amount and accept a brighter, less rounded heat. Aleppo pepper is closer to gochugaru in flavor profile (mild, fruity, warmly hot) and substitutes 1:1. The smashed cucumber is a different dish with cayenne or paprika; do not substitute powdered chilis into the salad, the heat distribution turns muddy.
  • rice vinegar → apple cider vinegar or white wine vinegar Both work in the glaze and the salad. Apple cider vinegar reads a touch fruitier and the glaze takes on a slight apple note that is acceptable but recognizably different. White wine vinegar is more neutral and closer to rice vinegar in profile. Avoid distilled white vinegar, the sharpness is wrong for both applications.
  • honey → maple syrup or light brown sugar Maple syrup substitutes 1:1 by weight; the glaze reads slightly less floral and a touch more caramelly. Brown sugar substitutes at the same weight; whisk it into the rice vinegar first so it dissolves before the gochujang goes in. Both work; honey is the canonical sweetener for yangnyeom-style Korean glazes.
  • Persian cucumbers → English cucumber or Kirby cucumbers English cucumber is the next-best swap: thin skin, low seed development, mild flavor. Smash and tear identically. Kirby (the small pickling cucumber) is also excellent and a touch more bitter; rinse once after the salt drain if the bitterness reads forward. Avoid standard slicing cucumbers from the supermarket, the thick waxy skin and developed seed cavity do not deliver the right texture.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → gochujang make it yourselfstub A homemade gochujang aged 3 to 6 months reads dramatically more complex than commercial brands: the meju (fermented soybean meal) and the glutinous rice ferment integrate in a way the temperature-controlled commercial brands cannot match. The 30-minute hands-on time spread over a 6-month ferment produces a paste that elevates this dish (and every other gochujang dish) from sweet-spicy-good to sweet-spicy-with-depth. Not a weeknight project, but a worthy weekend one.

Notes

Why the cornstarch dust over flour, breadcrumbs, or bare protein. Cornstarch is pure amylose-and-amylopectin starch with no protein; the granules gelatinize and brown in 60 to 90 seconds of hot pan contact, producing a thin glassy crust that locks the chicken interior moist while the glaze adheres to a rough surface rather than smooth wet protein. Flour produces a similar crust but reads doughier (the gluten develops as the flour hydrates), and the crust competes with the glaze for surface attention. Breadcrumbs (panko) produce a thicker, crunchier crust that is structurally different, more chicken-katsu than yangnyeom; reserve it for a different dish. Bare protein with no dusting produces an unsticky glaze surface, the gochujang slides off rather than lacquering.

Why ginger in the brine and not garlic. Ginger contains zingibain, a cysteine protease that gently hydrolyzes muscle proteins (the same enzymatic class as bromelain in pineapple and papain in papaya, but slower and more controllable). A 5-to-10-minute ginger exposure on chicken breast or tender produces a noticeably more tender bite without crossing into mushy over-tenderized territory; longer exposures (30 minutes or more) can over-soften the surface. Garlic contributes no protease activity to the brine and is wasted on raw protein contact at this duration; reserve it for the glaze where its allicin volatiles cook off briefly into the warm pan heat.

Why off-heat glaze and not pan-reduction. The classic Korean fried chicken glaze is built in the pan: gochujang plus aromatics simmered down before the chicken hits the pan. That works for deep-fried chicken (the fry crust is already hard set, the wet glaze does not soften it). For seared tender, the off-heat toss preserves the cornstarch crust integrity; if the chicken sits in simmering glaze for more than 30 seconds, the crust softens and the dish loses the contrast between lacquered surface and crisp interior. The 30-second off-heat toss is the right window: long enough for the glaze to reduce slightly from the chicken juices that release into the pan, short enough that the crust stays intact.

The smashed cucumber as structural foil, not garnish. The Sichuan and northern Chinese pai huang gua technique (smashing the cucumber to fracture rather than slicing) was built around exactly this problem: a rich savory protein needs an acid-and-water counterpoint that holds its texture against the heat and fat of the main dish. The fractured surface exposes 40 to 50 percent more cell wall than a clean knife cut, which means the salt-and-vinegar dressing penetrates faster and the cucumber stays crisp longer in the dressed state. A sliced cucumber salad goes limp in 10 minutes; a smashed cucumber salad stays crisp for 30. Time the cucumber so it hits the table 5 to 15 minutes after dressing for the peak texture window.

Doneness on chicken tenders. The pectoralis minor (the tender) is the smallest, leanest muscle on the bird and the easiest to overcook into chalky dry meat. The USDA-recommended internal temperature of 74 °C (165 °F) is the safe-without-question target, but tenders pulled at 70 °C and held for 90 seconds (which they will be, the carryover heat in the glaze toss plus the residual pan contact does the rest) are equally safe by the time-and-temperature equivalence rule. Pulling at 70 °C gives a noticeably moister, more tender eat. An instant-read thermometer is the right tool; visual cues alone produce too much variance on a cut this thin.

The pairing with rice and the carb dial. Steamed short-grain rice is the canonical Korean pairing and the structural starch that the glaze is calibrated against (the rice absorbs the surplus glaze that pools on the plate). For a lower-carb plate, skip the rice and serve the tenders in romaine or butter lettuce wraps (see modifications); the lettuce supplies the water-and-crunch that the rice would have supplied as starch, and the plate eats lighter. The cucumber salad is the structural side either way; do not skip it, the dish lives on the warm-cool, sweet-acid contrast it provides.

Storage and reheating. The glazed tenders hold in the refrigerator 3 days; the cucumber salad holds 1 day before the cucumbers go limp. Reheat the tenders in a 175 °C / 350 °F oven on a wire rack for 6 to 8 minutes (the wire rack prevents the bottom of the glaze from steaming into a sticky film); the microwave works but softens the cornstarch crust into something less interesting. Do not freeze the glazed tenders, the glaze breaks on thaw and the cornstarch crust goes mealy.
