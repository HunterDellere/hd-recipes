Recipe

Cantonese steamed whole flounder with ginger and scallion

A whole flounder ginger-cured, steamed flat over hard vapor for just 6 to 8 minutes, the pan liquid poured off and replaced with a hot soy-sesame seasoning, scattered with raw ginger-scallion julienne, and finished with smoking neutral oil poured over the aromatics. The flatfish register of the Cantonese steaming canon: the thin flat body cooks faster and reads more delicate than a round branzino, which is exactly why Hong Kong kitchens reach for sole and flounder when the point is refinement. Protein integrity preserved; aromatics deployed in three temperature registers.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Start jasmine rice FIRST as a parallel track. The flounder steams only 6 to 8 minutes and plates within 60 seconds of coming out; the rice (12 minute simmer plus 10 minute rest) must be finished by the moment the smoking oil hits the aromatics or the dish goes cold waiting.
  • Set up a steamer that fits the whole flat flounder on a heatproof plate. A wok with a bamboo steamer or a deep skillet with a wire rack both work; a flat fish needs a wide plate more than a deep one. Verify the empty plate clears the steamer with 2 cm around it before the fish goes near it.
  • Stage the three aromatic registers BEFORE the fish hits the steam: raw ginger-scallion julienne in a bowl by the plate, soy-sesame seasoning warmed in a small pot, neutral oil ready in a small saucepan to be pushed to smoking the moment the steamer comes off heat. The oil-pour finish is a 5 second move; fumbling with the julienne mid-pour means cold oil and no aromatic bloom.
  • The steam window is short and the fish is thin, so watch it. A flounder overcooks faster than a round fish; start checking at 6 minutes and pull it the instant the flesh at the deepest score is opaque and lifts from the bone.

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, fish prep and cure

  1. 650 g whole flounder (or sole or fluke), gutted and scaled, gills removed, rinsed inside and out, blood line scraped from the spine cavity with a small spoon, patted dry inside and out, three shallow diagonal slashes 5 mm deep cut into each side from gill to tail through the skin
  2. 25 g fresh ginger, peeled, 15 g sliced into thin coins for the cure and steaming bed; 10 g reserved for fine julienne in Phase 3 — (1½-inch knob total)
  3. 20 g scallion, whole, 2 stalks halved crosswise and bruised with the flat of a knife, for the cure and steaming bed; remaining stalks reserved in Phase 3 — (2 stalks for cure; 4 more for finish)
  4. 15 g Shaoxing wine
  5. 4 g kosher salt

Phase 2, soy seasoning sauce

  1. 30 g light soy sauce
  2. 30 g water, hot from the kettle
  3. 4 g rock sugar, crushed (or granulated sugar)
  4. 4 g toasted sesame oil
  5. 0.5 g white pepper, freshly ground

Phase 3, finish (raw aromatics)

  1. 10 g fresh ginger, fine julienne, reserved from Phase 1, sliced into thin coins, stacked, sliced into matchstick julienne 3 cm long
  2. 30 g scallion, white and pale-green parts, cut into 5 cm batons, julienned lengthwise into very thin slivers, dropped into ice water for 10 minutes to curl — (4 stalks; the dark green tops can go into the steaming bed)
  3. 8 g fresh cilantro, leaves and tender stems, roughly torn

Phase 4, hot-oil bloom

  1. 30 g neutral oil (peanut, rice bran, or canola)

Phase 5, serve

  1. 300 g steamed jasmine rice

Execution

  1. 1
    Examine and clean the 650 g / 1 whole flounder. The fishmonger should have gutted, scaled, and removed the gills; verify all three. Rinse the cavity under cold running water and scrape any dark blood line along the inside of the spine with the back of a small spoon (the blood line carries the strongest fishy flavor and is the main source of "fishy fish" complaints). Pat the fish completely dry inside and out. Wet fish steams unevenly and dilutes the seasoning sauce on the plate; dry is non-negotiable.
  2. 2
    Score and cure. With a sharp paring knife, cut three shallow diagonal slashes 5 mm deep into the flesh on each side of the flounder, from the gill plate to the tail. Keep them shallow, the flesh is thin and deep cuts on a flatfish tear it apart. Rub the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt over both sides and inside the cavity, working it into the slashes, then splash the 15 g / 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine over the same surfaces. Stuff the cavity loosely with the 15 g / sliced ginger coins and the 20 g / 2 bruised scallion stalks. Let the fish cure about 10 minutes while the steamer comes up; the brief cure firms the surface, draws out a little surface water, and lets the wine esters perfume the fish.
  3. 3
    Set up the steamer. Bring 3 cm of water to a hard rolling boil over high heat in a wok with a bamboo steamer, or in a deep skillet with a fitted rack and lid. The steaming temperature is the hard rolling boil of water, full vapor flux at 100°C, not a gentle simmer. A bamboo steamer is preferred because the wood absorbs surface condensation and prevents drips of cool water onto the fish; a metal lid wrapped in a clean kitchen towel does the same. Vapor steaming (high-vapor wet-heat cookery)
  4. 4
    Plate the fish for steaming. Choose a wide heatproof rimmed plate that fits inside the steamer with at least 2 cm of clearance all around. Lay 2 to 3 chopsticks or two scallion-top batons across the plate as a low rack; the gap under the fish lets vapor circulate and keeps the bottom skin from going gummy in pooled liquid. Lay the cured flounder flat across the chopsticks and discard any liquid that pooled during the cure (it is fishy and not part of the dish).
  5. 5
    Steam at full heat, 6 to 8 minutes. When the water is at a hard rolling boil, set the plate in the steamer, cover, and steam at full heat. Time by thickness: 6 minutes for a flounder 1.5 cm thick at the spine, 8 minutes for one 2 cm thick. This is the whole reason to steam a flatfish, it cooks in roughly two-thirds the time of a round fish. Start checking at 6 minutes by lifting the lid (carefully, vapor rises fast) and inserting a chopstick into the deepest slash near the spine; the flesh should be opaque and lift cleanly from the bone. If a translucent core remains, replace the lid for 60 seconds at a time. Pull the fish the instant it is opaque, a flounder crosses from silky to chalky faster than any round fish and is the one most punished by overcooking. Vapor steaming (high-vapor wet-heat cookery)
  6. 6
    Build the soy seasoning sauce while the fish steams. In a small saucepan combine the 30 g / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, the 30 g / 2 tbsp hot water, the 4 g / ¾ tsp crushed rock sugar, and the 0.5 g / ¼ tsp white pepper. Warm over low heat until the sugar dissolves (1 to 2 minutes), then pull off heat and stir in the 4 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil off-heat to preserve its volatile toast notes. The sauce should taste pronouncedly salty-sweet with a faint sesame-pepper depth; it seasons the fish from the outside.
  7. 7
    Prepare the raw aromatics for finishing. Cut the reserved 10 g ginger into the finest julienne possible (3 cm matchsticks; the finer the julienne, the better the hot-oil bloom). Cut the 30 g / 4 stalks scallion whites and pale greens into 5 cm batons and julienne lengthwise into very thin slivers, then drop them into a bowl of ice water; within 5 to 10 minutes they curl into elegant ribbons. Pick and tear the 8 g / ¼ cup cilantro. Have the aromatic stage loaded and ready before the fish comes out.
  8. 8
    Pour off the steaming liquid. When the flounder is done, pull the steamer off the heat and uncover. Transfer the plate to a heatproof surface (a spatula in each hand under opposite ends works). Tilt the plate and pour off all the liquid that has pooled; this liquid is full of fishy proteins released during steaming and is the biggest contributor to a dish that tastes "off." Discard it, and discard the bruised aromatics from the cavity, their job is done.
  9. 9
    Season and dress the fish. Pour the warm soy seasoning sauce around (not directly over) the fish so it pools and flows under the slashes, seasoning the flesh from the bottom and the cut surfaces. Lift the curled 30 g scallion slivers out of the ice water, shake them dry, and lay them in a strip down the spine of the fish. Scatter the 10 g ginger julienne over the scallions in the same tight center strip. Scatter the cilantro loosely over the platter as a final green note.
  10. 10
    The smoking-oil bloom. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a small saucepan over medium-high until just smoking, about 200°C / 390°F; a wooden chopstick dipped in should bubble vigorously within a second and the first wisp of pale smoke should rise. In one steady pour from 5 to 10 cm up, pour the smoking oil directly over the strip of ginger and scallion on top of the fish. The aromatics will sizzle violently for 5 to 10 seconds, flashing their volatile sulfur and allium compounds into the air and blooming the raw julienne into the cooked-but-textured finish that defines the dish. Cool oil produces a sad raw-scallion-on-fish plate; smoking oil produces the canonical bloom. Serve immediately with steamed jasmine rice, each diner taking a piece of fish and spooning a little of the seasoned oil-and-soy over the rice. Hot-oil finish (smoking-oil bloom over raw aromatics)

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein fish species→with whole branzino, sea bass, or snapper (round fish)

    Replace the flat flounder with a 700 g whole round fish. The thicker profile (3 cm at the spine) raises the steam time to 10 to 12 minutes and the slashes go deeper (1 cm). The dish reads meatier and less delicate; the round-fish version is the branzino default covered in the companion Cantonese steamed whole fish recipe on this site.

  • Regional aromatic profile→with fermented black beans

    Add 8 g / 1 tbsp rinsed and roughly chopped fermented black beans (douchi) to the seasoning sauce, bloomed first with 8 g / 1 tbsp minced garlic in 8 g / 2 tsp neutral oil for 30 seconds before stirring into the soy. The dish becomes steamed fish with black bean sauce (chi zhi zheng yu), funkier and deeper. The hot-oil finish step is preserved.

  • Protein cut→with thick fish fillets instead of whole fish

    Replace the whole flounder with 2 thicker fillets of sea bass, black cod, or halibut (each 200 to 250 g, ~3 cm thick). Skip the cavity step and cure the fillets 5 minutes with the same salt and Shaoxing rub. Steam 7 to 9 minutes by thickness. Weeknight-friendly and works without whole fish, though the visual drama and the cavity-aromatic register are gone.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole flounder (or sole or fluke) → whole branzino, rockfish, or tilapia Any thin flatfish (sole, fluke, plaice) is a direct swap and steams in the same 6 to 8 minutes. Branzino and rockfish are round-fish substitutes and need 10 to 12 minutes (see modifications). Whole tilapia is the budget option, structurally fine but milder in flavor.
  • Shaoxing wine → dry sherry The closest Western substitute by flavor profile; functionally identical for a brief cure. Dry sake works in a more Japanese register but is less ideal here. Avoid Western cooking wine (too sweet, too salty).
  • rock sugar → granulated sugar Granulated sugar dissolves faster but lacks the gentle clean sweetness of rock sugar; the seasoning sauce reads slightly more aggressive. Acceptable; rock sugar is canonical but not critical here.
  • light soy sauce → no good substitute The seasoning sauce is structurally light-soy-based; dark soy darkens the dish into the wrong color and register (red-cooked rather than white-steamed). Use light soy or skip the dish; tamari can sub if gluten-free is required, with a small salt reduction.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → light soy sauce make it yourselfstub Soybean and wheat fermented for 6 to 18 months produce a notably more aromatic and complex soy than mass-produced commercial. The work is multi-month but mostly passive; one batch covers a year of cooking.
  • → Shaoxing wine make it yourselfstub Glutinous rice and wheat fermented with qu starter for 6 to 12 months produce a true Shaoxing-style wine with the depth that supermarket "Shaoxing cooking wine" (often diluted and salted) lacks. A weekend project for a long-term ferment.

Notes

Why a flatfish steams best. A flounder is the ideal shape for the Cantonese steam. It lies perfectly flat on the plate so the whole fish sits in the vapor evenly, and it is thin enough that the spine reaches doneness in 6 to 8 minutes rather than the 10 to 12 a round branzino needs. The flesh is the leanest and finest-flaking of the common steaming fish, so the result reads as the most refined whole-fish plate in the Cantonese repertoire; this is why Hong Kong restaurants steam sole and flounder when the goal is delicacy rather than the meatier eat of a snapper or grouper. The trade-off is a narrower doneness window: with almost no fat to buffer it, a flounder goes from silky to chalky in under a minute past done, so the fish is watched and pulled the instant the flesh at the deepest slash turns opaque.

Why the hot-oil finish matters. The pour of smoking oil over the raw ginger-scallion julienne is the technique that separates this dish from steamed fish with raw aromatics on top. The 200°C oil flash-cooks the surface of the aromatics in 3 to 5 seconds, releasing their volatile compounds (allyl sulfides from the scallion, gingerols and shogaols from the ginger) into the air and depositing cooked-but-textured aromatics back onto the fish. It also crisps the cilantro edges and pulls the flavors down into the flesh through the slashes. Cool oil produces a sad raw-scallion-on-fish dish; smoking-hot oil produces the aromatic bloom that defines the dish. The 30 seconds of heating the oil are the most important 30 seconds in the recipe.

The water management is the subtle technique. The flounder releases 20 to 40 g of liquid during the steam, a mix of cell water and lid condensation, and this liquid is fishy, full of denatured proteins, and dilutes any seasoning that follows. Pouring it off completely before the seasoning sauce goes on is the difference between a plate that tastes clean and one that tastes fishy. Discard it whenever there is any doubt about the fish''s freshness.

The seasoning logic. The umami source is the light soy sauce in the seasoning (glutamate plus trace nucleotides) plus the natural glutamate the fish releases during the steam; the balancing sensations come from the rock sugar and white pepper rather than an explicit acid. Cantonese steamed fish is one of the few dishes with no acid finish in the canonical form; the soy is the seasoning anchor and the dish is plated immediately so the fresh-aromatic top notes carry. A squeeze of lime at the table is a fine non-canonical home addition.

Sourcing the fish. Whole flounder, sole, and fluke are sold on ice at H Mart, 99 Ranch, and most Asian seafood markets, and seasonally at Western counters (a US "sole" fillet is usually a flounder species). Look for clear (not cloudy) eyes, bright red gills, tight scales, no gummy slime, and a clean ocean smell. Choose a fish 600 to 700 g that lies flat on a single steaming plate; ask the counter to gut, scale, and remove the gills and verify all three at home. Steam the day of purchase; whole fish keeps less well than fillets because the gut cavity is exposed.
