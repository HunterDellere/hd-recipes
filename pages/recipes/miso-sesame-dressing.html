Miso-sesame dressing

White miso, toasted sesame paste, rice vinegar, mirin, and grated ginger whisked into a pourable emulsion. The sesame paste supplies both the roasted-nutty lead and the emulsifier; the miso and a touch of light soy carry the glutamate load; rice vinegar and a second hit of fresh ginger keep the whole thing bright rather than heavy. A two-minute no-cook sauce that dresses raw salads, steamed vegetables, cold noodles, and seared protein.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This dressing keeps 7 days refrigerated in a sealed jar and the flavor improves after a few hours as the raw garlic and ginger mellow into the miso. Make it ahead when you can.
  • Sesame paste separates in the jar, the oil rises to the top. Stir the jar vigorously to reincorporate the oil before you measure, or you will pull a thin, oil-heavy spoonful that throws off the ratio and the emulsion.
  • Build the emulsion in order: paste and miso first, then liquids in small increments. Adding all the liquid at once seizes the paste into clumps. This is the one rule that decides whether the dressing comes out glossy or grainy.

  3. 6 g toasted white sesame seeds, lightly crushed in a mortar to release aroma, or left whole for texture

Phase 4, adjust

  1. 1 g fine sea salt

Execution

  1. 1
    Stir the sesame paste jar first. The oil separates and rises; pulling an unstirred spoonful gives you a thin, oil-heavy measure that will not emulsify. Stir the jar until the oil is fully reincorporated and the paste is homogeneous, then measure 60 g / ¼ cup into a medium bowl.
  2. 2
    Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp white miso to the sesame paste and whisk the two into a single thick, smooth paste. Working the miso in before any liquid means both emulsifiers (the sesame oil-and-protein and the miso soy peptides) are evenly distributed before they have to hold water. The mixture will be stiff, like loose nut butter; that is correct. Pan emulsion (mantecare)
  3. 3
    Begin the liquids. Whisk in the 30 g / 2 tbsp rice vinegar in two additions; the paste will seize and stiffen the instant the first acid hits, then relax as you whisk. Do not panic and dump more liquid in to loosen it; keep whisking and it pulls together into a glossy, thick emulsion. Adding the acid this early, while the paste is still concentrated, builds the most stable base.
  4. 4
    Whisk in the remaining liquids one at a time, fully integrating each before the next: the 20 g / 1 tbsp mirin, then the 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy sauce, then the 10 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame oil, then the 12 g / 2 tsp honey. The dressing will thin and gloss with each addition. Adding the toasted sesame oil here, off any heat, preserves its volatile aroma; cooking it would scorch and turn it bitter.
  5. 5
    Adjust consistency with the 30 g / 2 tbsp cool water (or cold dashi), added in two additions, whisking between. Stop when the dressing coats the back of a spoon and slides off in a slow, even sheet; for steamed vegetables you want it just-pourable, for a salad coating slightly thicker. Hold back the last splash if it is already loose enough.
  6. 6
    Whisk in the aromatics: the 8 g / 1 tbsp microplaned ginger with its juice and the 3 g / 1 small clove grated garlic. The microplane pulp dissolves into the emulsion rather than sitting as chunks; the ginger juice is where most of the brightening gingerol lives, so grate it directly over the bowl to catch it. Stir in the 6 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame seeds last for texture.
  7. 7
    Taste and adjust, the seasoning curve. The dressing should land balanced across four axes: nutty (sesame paste), savory-salty (miso, soy), sweet (mirin, honey), and bright-acid (rice vinegar, ginger). If it reads flat, add the 1 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt or a few more drops of soy. If it reads heavy or dull, a teaspoon more rice vinegar lifts it. If the miso salt is too forward, a touch more honey rounds it. Transfer to a sealed jar; it keeps 7 days refrigerated and the garlic and ginger mellow into the miso over the first few hours.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture use case→steamed-vegetable coating (broccoli, green beans, asparagus, eggplant)

    Thin with the full 30 g / 2 tbsp water plus another 15 g / 1 tbsp so the dressing pools and clings to hot, just-steamed vegetables rather than sitting in a thick blob. Spoon over the vegetables while they are still warm; the residual heat loosens the emulsion further and carries the aroma. This is the goma-dare register, looser and more sauce-like.

  • Occasion use case→cold noodle sauce (soba, somen, ramen, or wheat noodles)

    Keep the dressing on the thicker side (hold back the last 15 g / 1 tbsp water) and toss with chilled, well-drained noodles. Add 15 g / 1 tbsp chili oil and a pinch of gochugaru for a dan-dan-leaning cold noodle bowl, or keep it clean and top with cucumber ribbons and scallion. The thicker emulsion clings to the strands instead of pooling at the bottom of the bowl.

  • Heat heat→spicy miso-sesame

    Whisk in 8 to 15 g / 1 to 2 tsp chili crisp or doubanjiang at the liquid stage, treating it as part of the liquid budget. The fermented-bean funk of doubanjiang stacks on the miso glutamate; chili crisp adds toasted-aromatic heat that reads with the sesame. Pull the honey down by half if the chili crisp is sweetened.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • toasted sesame paste (Chinese zhima jiang) → tahini plus 1 tsp toasted sesame oil per 3 tbsp tahini Tahini is made from raw or lightly toasted seeds, so it reads paler, looser, and sweeter, missing the roasted depth zhima jiang leads with. The extra toasted sesame oil compensates. Whisk the oil fully into the tahini before adding miso. This is the right move when toasted sesame paste is unavailable; the dressing is still good, just lighter on the toast.
  • light soy sauce → tamari Tamari is wheat-free, so swapping it makes the dressing gluten-free (the miso must also be a rice-only koji miso, not a barley one). Tamari reads slightly thicker and more intensely savory; pull the quantity to 12 g / 2 tsp to compensate.
  • mirin (hon-mirin) → rice vinegar plus a pinch more honey, or dry sake plus honey No mirin: add 5 g / 1 tsp extra honey to the dressing and 5 g / 1 tsp extra rice vinegar to keep the sweet-acid balance. Or use 20 g / 1 tbsp dry sake plus 6 g / 1 tsp honey for a closer flavor, though the alcohol stays raw in a no-cook dressing so use sparingly.
  • honey → maple syrup or granulated sugar dissolved in the vinegar Maple syrup is a clean 1:1 swap and keeps the dressing vegan. Granulated sugar works but dissolve it into the warmed rice vinegar first so it does not stay gritty in the cold emulsion.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → toasted sesame paste (Chinese zhima jiang), jar stirred to reincorporate the oil make it yourself A jar of fresh-ground toasted sesame paste is dramatically more aromatic than a long-shelf-life commercial jar, where the surface oil oxidizes toward rancidity before you finish it. Toasting your own seeds to the exact edge of bitterness and grinding them with a little neutral oil takes fifteen minutes and gives a paste that reads roasted and alive rather than flat and stale.

Notes

Why sesame paste is the emulsifier, not just the flavor. The dressing holds together because the ground sesame seeds carry both oil and protein. The protein fragments and the fine particulate sit at the oil-water interface and stabilize droplets of the toasted sesame oil and the released seed oil within the water-based vinegar-soy-mirin phase. This is the same mechanism that lets tahini bind a Levantine sauce or lets mustard powder hold a vinaigrette. White miso adds a second stabilizer: its soy peptides and the fine particulate of the fermented paste reinforce the interface. Build the emulsion paste-first and the dressing stays glossy and unbroken for a week.

Why the seize-then-relax is correct. When the first acid hits a concentrated sesame-miso paste, the paste stiffens and looks like it is breaking. It is not. The paste is hydrating, and the proteins are reorganizing around the new water. Keep whisking and it pulls into a glossy emulsion. The instinct to flood it with more liquid to loosen it is exactly wrong; it produces a thin, separated dressing instead of a thick stable one. Add liquid in increments, fully integrating each, and the emulsion builds itself.

Why no boiling, no heat anywhere. White miso loses its volatile koji-fermentation aromatics when boiled, and toasted sesame oil scorches into bitterness over heat. Both are added cold, off any flame, which is why this is a true no-cook sauce. The only transformation is mechanical (the emulsion) and enzymatic (the raw garlic and ginger mellowing into the miso over time in the fridge).

The umami-and-acid architecture. The savory load is stacked: free glutamates from the white miso, more from the light soy sauce, and the roasted-protein depth of the sesame paste. The finishing acid is doubled too: rice vinegar for the clean sour lift and fresh microplaned ginger whose gingerol reads as a bright, almost peppery brightness against the rich sesame. That double-acid is what keeps a sesame-heavy dressing from going dull and cloying. For a deeper savory base, swap the plain water thinner for cold dashi (kombu-and-katsuobushi or a vegetarian shiitake-kombu dashi); the glutamate-on-glutamate stack reads richer without adding salt.

What it dresses. Raw salads (shredded cabbage, romaine, mixed leaves, a chopped salad with cucumber and carrot), steamed or blanched vegetables (broccoli, green beans, asparagus, spinach, eggplant) where it sits in the goma-ae tradition, cold noodles, grain bowls, and as a sauce spooned over seared or cold poached protein. Thicker for coating, thinner for pooling; the consistency adjustment in the final step is what tunes it to the application.
