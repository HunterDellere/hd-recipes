Ube vanilla chocolate marble cake

A single butter-and-sour-cream loaf batter split three ways: a vanilla base, a violet ube-halaya batter, and a dark cocoa batter, then spooned in alternating dollops and dragged once with a knife so the three bake into distinct marbled veins rather than a blended muddy purple-brown. The ube reads sweet and faintly yam-vanilla, the cocoa reads bitter against it, and the plain vanilla is the neutral bridge that keeps the two from competing.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Bring the butter, eggs, and sour cream to room temperature (about 30 minutes out). Cold butter will not cream to the aerated state the cake rises on, and cold eggs or sour cream can seize the creamed butter into curdled lumps that bake gummy.
  • Have three bowls ready before you start splitting the batter. The base batter is divided into vanilla, ube, and cocoa portions, and you want to flavor each and start layering before the leavener loses its first lift, so stage the bowls, the sifted cocoa, and the halaya in advance.
  • Line the loaf pan with a parchment sling (a strip up the long sides) and butter the short ends. The cake bakes nearly an hour and the sour-cream crumb is tender; the sling lifts it out cleanly without tearing the marbled top.

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the oven to 175 °C / 350 °F with a rack in the lower third. Line a 23 x 13 cm / 9 x 5-inch loaf pan with a parchment sling up the long sides and butter the exposed short ends. Whisk the 250 g / 2 cups all-purpose flour, 6 g / 1½ tsp baking powder, and 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt in a bowl and set aside. Stir the 120 g / ½ cup sour cream and 60 g / ¼ cup whole milk together in a measuring cup.
  2. 2
    Cream the 170 g / ¾ cup softened butter and 230 g / 1 cup + 2 tbsp granulated sugar with the paddle on medium for 3 to 4 minutes until pale and visibly fluffy, scraping down twice. Add the 3 room-temperature eggs one at a time, beating 20 seconds after each until emulsified, then beat in the 8 g / 1½ tsp vanilla bean paste. If the mixture looks curdled, the eggs were too cold; keep beating and it will come together.
  3. 3
    Add the flour mixture in three additions alternating with the sour-cream mixture in two, beginning and ending with flour, mixing on low just until each addition disappears. Stop the moment the last streak of flour goes; overmixing builds gluten and toughens the crumb. This is the base batter.
  4. 4
    Split the base batter. Scoop about a third of it (roughly 320 g) into a second bowl and fold in the 100 g / scant ½ cup ube halaya and the optional 4 ml / ¾ tsp ube extract until uniform violet. Scoop another third into a third bowl and fold in the 18 g / 3 tbsp sifted dutch-process cocoa loosened with the 20 g / 4 tsp warm milk until uniform dark brown. Leave the final third plain vanilla. You now have three batters of roughly equal volume and the same consistency.
  5. 5
    Layer for marble. Drop the batters into the pan in alternating heaping spoonfuls, scattering vanilla, ube, and cocoa dollops across each layer so no two same-color blobs stack directly, until all three are used and the pan is evenly full. Resist any urge to smooth or stir as you go; the dollops are the marble.
  6. 6
    Swirl exactly once. Push a thin table knife or skewer down to the bottom of the pan and draw it through the batter in a single slow figure-eight running the length of the loaf, then lift out. One pass is the whole technique: it drags the three colors into folded veins while keeping them distinct. A second or third pass blends them toward a muddy uniform purple-brown, so stop after one. Tap the pan once on the counter to settle.
  7. 7
    Bake 55 to 65 minutes, until a skewer inserted into the center comes out with a few moist crumbs but no wet batter, and the top springs back to a light press. If the top browns before the center sets (around the 40-minute mark), tent loosely with foil. Cool in the pan 15 minutes, then lift out by the sling onto a rack and cool completely before slicing; a warm cut drags the tender crumb and smears the marble.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion shape→bundt cake

    Scale the recipe up by half (use 255 g butter, 345 g sugar, 4 to 5 eggs, 375 g flour, and proportional everything else) and bake in a well-greased 10-cup bundt pan at 175 °C for 50 to 60 minutes. Layer and swirl the same way. The bundt's fluting shows the marble veins on every slice and the larger surface bakes a slightly crustier exterior.

  • Occasion glaze→split ube-and-chocolate drizzle

    Make two quick glazes: whisk 60 g powdered sugar with 10 g ube halaya and a splash of milk for a violet drizzle, and 60 g powdered sugar with 8 g sifted cocoa and milk for a chocolate one. Spoon both over the cooled loaf in alternating stripes so the glaze echoes the marble inside. Let set 20 minutes before slicing.

  • Texture flavor depth→brown butter base

    Brown the 170 g butter to amber milk solids first, then chill it back to a soft-solid state before creaming (about 40 minutes in the fridge, stirring once). The nutty Maillard notes deepen both the vanilla and the ube veins; reference the brown-butter technique for the doneness cue. Add 2 tsp of water to the creamed mixture to replace the moisture lost in browning.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • ube halaya → mashed cooked purple sweet potato + sweetened condensed milk (2:1 by weight) + 5 ml ube extract For the cook without Filipino groceries: cook and rice Stokes Purple or Okinawan purple sweet potato, weigh 67 g, and combine with 33 g sweetened condensed milk and 5 ml ube extract. The vein reads more sweet-potato-earthy than canonical halaya, and the extract is mandatory here for color since the natural pigment fades brown-violet in the bake.
  • sour cream → full-fat plain yogurt or buttermilk Greek yogurt is a near one-for-one (same acidity and fat); thin buttermilk works but is looser, so cut the added milk by half. Both keep the crumb tender and the acidity that holds the ube violet.
  • dutch-process cocoa → natural cocoa powder Natural cocoa is more acidic and lighter brown; it works but the vein reads redder and a touch sharper. Dutch-process gives the darker, smoother chocolate contrast that plays best against the violet. No leavening adjustment needed at this small quantity.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → ube halaya make it yourselfstub Homemade halaya carries a brighter fresh-yam character and less of the cooked-down condensed-milk caramel that jarred versions show, which reads through clearly in the ube vein where the halaya is the whole flavor of that third of the cake.

Notes

Why three batters from one base. Marbling reads best when the swirled batters differ only in color and flavor, not in density or rise, so they bake at the same rate and hold their veins crisp instead of one sinking past another. Splitting a single finished base batter into vanilla, ube, and cocoa portions guarantees that. The only adjustments are additive: halaya and extract into one, cocoa loosened with a little warm milk into another (the milk compensates for the cocoa''s drying starch so all three stay the same pourable thickness). Equal volume and equal consistency are what make the marble clean.

Why ube wants the sour cream, chemically. Ube''s violet is anthocyanin, and anthocyanin reads most vividly violet in the slightly acidic pH 5 to 7 range, shifting dull and grey toward neutral-to-alkaline. A plain butter cake batter raised with baking powder trends slightly alkaline, which is exactly where the ube color goes muddy. The sour cream (pH around 4.5) pulls the batter back into the acidic window that keeps the pigment violet, and does double duty tenderizing the crumb. This is why the ube vein in a sour-cream cake stays brighter than in a straight buttermilk-free pound cake.

The single-swirl rule. The instinct is to keep dragging the knife until the cake "looks marbled," but every pass blends the three colors further toward a uniform purple-brown. One slow figure-eight through the layered dollops is enough: it folds the colors into distinct veins while leaving large zones of each still pure. If you want a more dramatic marble, add more dollops and more layers, not more swirling. Think of it as folding, not stirring.

Reading doneness on a marbled loaf. The cocoa vein bakes a touch faster and darker than the ube and vanilla, so judge by the center skewer (moist crumbs, no wet batter) and the spring-back, not by the surface color, which is unevenly dark by design. A tall loaf pan holds the heat long; if the top is setting dark before the center cooks through, tent with foil at the 40-minute mark and keep baking. Cool it completely before the first slice, the sour-cream crumb is tender and a warm knife smears the marble you worked to keep distinct.
