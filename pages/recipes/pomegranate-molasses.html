Pomegranate molasses

Pomegranate juice reduced 4:1 over 30 to 45 minutes to a deep-burgundy, sour-sweet syrup. The Levantine and Persian sour anchor — fattoush, fesenjan, muhammara. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 3 items ▾
  1. 1000 g pure pomegranate juice (POM Wonderful or freshly pressed)
  2. 50 g granulated sugar (optional, for a sweeter molasses) optional
  3. 30 g lemon juice, fresh optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: combine 1000 g / 4 cups pomegranate juice, the 50 g / ¼ cup sugar (if using), and the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice (if using) in a wide non-reactive saucepan (stainless steel or enamel; reactive aluminum or unsealed copper will discolor and pick up off-flavors from the acid). The wide surface area accelerates evaporation; a tall narrow pot will reduce twice as slowly.
  2. 2
    Stub: bring to a strong simmer over medium-high heat, then reduce to medium. Cook 30 to 45 minutes uncovered, stirring occasionally to prevent scorching at the edges, until the volume has reduced to ~250 ml / 1 cup and the liquid coats the back of a spoon — when you draw a finger across the back of a coated spoon, the line stays clear for 2 seconds before the molasses flows back.
  3. 3
    Stub: remove from heat and let cool to room temperature; the molasses will continue to thicken as it cools. Transfer to a clean glass jar, cap tightly, refrigerate. Use within 2 to 3 months.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the reduction chemistry (sugar concentration goes from ~14% in fresh juice to ~50% in the finished molasses; the syrup state depends on supersaturation, not gelation, which is why pomegranate molasses thickens by reduction rather than by pectin or thickener), the pomegranate-juice quality question (POM Wonderful is acceptable; fresh-pressed from whole pomegranates is better, but the 8 to 12 fresh fruits required to produce 1 liter of juice make this an expensive variant), the optional sugar-and-lemon math (the 50 g sugar bumps sweetness without changing acid balance much; 30 g lemon juice brightens the cooked-down register; both are optional and recipes vary).

Failure modes: scorched bottom (the sugar caramelizes and contributes a bitter burnt note; reduce on lower heat next time); too thin at the end of cooking (continue reducing; the molasses thickens further on cooling, but undercooked molasses runs at 1.20 g/ml density rather than 1.30 and reads thin in dressings); crystallization in the bottle on storage (normal; warm the bottle in a hot-water bath for 5 minutes and shake to redissolve).

Use: in fattoush dressings (1 tbsp / 20 g), in muhammara (the Syrian roasted-red-pepper-and-walnut dip; molasses is a defining ingredient), as a marinade base for grilled chicken thighs and lamb shoulder, brushed onto roasted carrots and beets in the last 5 minutes of roasting, drizzled over labneh or yogurt with za'atar and olive oil. Replaces grenadine in any cocktail context where a real pomegranate flavor is wanted.
