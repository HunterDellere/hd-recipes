Pomegranate molasses

Pomegranate juice reduced 4:1 over 30 to 45 minutes to a deep-burgundy, sour-sweet syrup. The Levantine and Persian sour anchor that defines fattoush dressings, fesenjan, and muhammara.

Mise en Place

Servings

Shopping list 3 items ▾

Produce 2

  1. 30 g Lemon optional
  2. 1000 g pure pomegranate juice (POM Wonderful or freshly pressed)

Pantry 1

  1. 50 g Granulated sugar optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Combine the 1000 g / 4 cups pomegranate juice, the 50 g / ¼ cup sugar (if using), and the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice (if using) in a wide non-reactive saucepan: stainless steel or enamel-coated cast iron is ideal. Avoid reactive aluminum or unsealed copper, which will discolor with the acid and pick up off-flavors. The pan width matters: a 25 to 28 cm / 10 to 11 inch wide pan reduces twice as fast as a 20 cm / 8 inch tall narrow pot of the same liquid volume because surface evaporation rate scales with surface area, not pot capacity.
  2. 2
    Bring to a strong simmer over medium-high heat (active bubbles across the surface, not a rolling boil), then reduce to medium so the simmer is steady but controlled. Cook 30 to 45 minutes uncovered, stirring with a heat-proof spatula every 5 to 7 minutes to prevent scorching at the edges. The volume drops gradually; track it by eye against the side of the pan or by lifting the spatula and watching how the liquid sheets off (early-stage sheets thin and fast, mid-stage sheets thicker and slower, finished molasses coats the spatula and stays).
  3. 3
    The cure is complete when the volume has reduced to roughly 250 ml / 1 cup (one quarter of the starting volume) and the liquid coats the back of the spatula. The definitive test: draw a finger across the back of a coated spoon; the line should stay clear for 2 to 3 seconds before the molasses flows back over it. Color shifts from bright pomegranate red to deep ruby-burgundy. The aroma concentrates from fresh-juice tartness to a complex sour-sweet roasted-fruit register.
  4. 4
    Remove from heat and let cool to room temperature in the pan; the molasses thickens further as it cools because the supersaturated sugar concentrates as the residual water cools. The finished molasses at room temperature pours like cold honey, slightly slower; if it pours like fresh juice, it is undercooked and needs another 5 to 10 minutes of reduction. If it pours like cold molasses (very slow, in ribbons), it is overcooked and risks crystallization on storage; thin with 30 to 60 ml of water, stir, and bring back to a simmer for 1 minute to redissolve.
  5. 5
    Transfer the cooled molasses to a clean glass jar; cap tightly. Refrigerate; the molasses holds 2 to 3 months refrigerated, longer (up to 6 months) at high acid concentration. The Adjustment: if the molasses tastes harsh or one-note sour, the lemon juice was too much for your batch of pomegranate juice; balance with a small drizzle of honey before use. If it tastes flat or candy-sweet, the lemon juice was insufficient or omitted; bump with a small splash (5 to 10 g / 1 to 2 tsp) of fresh lemon juice when using in dressings.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional concentration→thicker, syrupy molasses (Persian rob-e-anar style)

    Reduce further: continue cooking past the 250 ml / 1 cup point down to 200 ml / ¾ cup or even 150 ml / ½ cup. The Persian rob-e-anar tradition produces a near-paste molasses thicker than American supermarket bottles; serves as a glaze for grilled lamb and chicken, or as a base for fesenjan walnut-pomegranate stew. Add 30 g additional water to thin during use.

  • Regional sweetness→unsweetened, sour-only molasses (the Lebanese and Syrian default)

    Omit both the sugar and the lemon juice; reduce only the pomegranate juice. The result is the most assertive sour-pomegranate flavor, suitable for vinaigrettes that pair with rich foods and as a marinade base for grilled meats. The unsweetened version reduces slightly faster (35 to 40 minutes rather than 40 to 45) because there is less residual sugar to slow evaporation at the supersaturated stage.

  • Regional cuisine→fresh-fruit pomegranate molasses (whole-fruit method)

    Press 8 to 12 fresh pomegranates by hand or with a citrus juicer to yield 1000 g of fresh juice; strain through a fine-mesh sieve to remove seed and membrane pieces. Reduce by the standard method. The result is more aromatically complex than supermarket-juice molasses and sits closer to the high-end commercial product (Cortas, Al Wadi, Mymouné). Significantly more expensive — fresh pomegranates run $1.50 to $3 each, and 8 to 12 fruits are needed per liter of juice.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • pure pomegranate juice (POM Wonderful) → pomegranate juice from concentrate or refrigerated 100% pomegranate juice Refrigerated 100% pomegranate juice (Trader Joe's, Whole Foods house brand) works identically. Pomegranate juice cocktails (Pom Tea, Pom Lemonade) do NOT work; the added sugars and dilution change the reduction math. Read the label and avoid anything that is not 100 percent pomegranate.
  • pomegranate juice → sour cherry juice, mulberry juice, or grape juice Sour cherry molasses is the Iranian rob-e-albalu and reduces by the same method. Mulberry molasses is the Lebanese rob-el-toot. Grape molasses (pekmez or dibs) is the Turkish/Middle Eastern grape syrup. Each is a distinct ingredient with its own use cases; the reduction technique is identical.

Notes

The reduction chemistry. Pomegranate juice contains roughly 14 percent sugar (a mix of glucose, fructose, and sucrose), 1 to 2 percent organic acids (citric and malic acid), and 80 percent water. Reducing 4:1 concentrates the sugar to roughly 50 percent and the acid to roughly 5 percent; the resulting syrup is supersaturated in both, which is the technical state that makes it thick and dramatically more flavorful per gram. The thickening is by supersaturation, not by gelation or pectin extraction; pomegranate juice has very little pectin (unlike apple or quince juices), so the thickening curve is gradual rather than the sudden set of a jam or jelly. This is why the recipe is timed by visual cues (spatula coating, finger-line test) rather than by temperature thresholds.

Pomegranate juice quality. POM Wonderful (the dominant US brand) is acceptable; the bottled product is 100 percent pomegranate juice, pasteurized but not concentrated-from-frozen, and produces a reliable molasses. Other 100 percent pomegranate juices (refrigerated brands, Whole Foods house brand, Trader Joe's organic) work identically. Fresh-pressed juice from whole pomegranates is markedly better — more aromatic, more complex, with a fresh-fruit character that bottled juice loses to pasteurization. The trade is cost: 8 to 12 fresh pomegranates produce 1 liter of juice and run $12 to $30 per liter at retail prices, which is dramatically more than the $5 to $7 per liter for bottled juice. For most home use, bottled juice is the working choice.

The optional sugar-and-lemon math. The 50 g sugar bumps sweetness without changing the acid balance much; pomegranate juice is naturally tart enough that the sugar reads as balancing rather than sweetening. Some traditions (most modern Lebanese, most modern Persian) include the sugar; some (more austere Levantine, traditional Syrian) omit it. The 30 g lemon juice brightens the cooked-down register; reduction concentrates the citric and malic acid in the pomegranate juice, which can read flat or one-note after 45 minutes of evaporation, and the fresh lemon juice adds back the bright top-note acid the reduction has dulled. Both are optional. Recipes from different regions and households vary on inclusion.

Failure modes. Scorched bottom (sugar caramelizing and contributing a bitter burnt note): reduce on lower heat next time and stir more frequently; small dark flecks on the pan bottom mean some scorching occurred but the molasses is usually still usable; large burnt patches or a smell of burnt sugar mean discard. Too thin at the end of cooking: continue reducing in 5-minute increments until the spatula-coat-and-line test passes; the molasses will thicken further on cooling, but undercooked molasses runs at 1.20 g/ml density rather than 1.30 and reads thin in dressings. Too thick or crystallized in the bottle on storage: warm the bottle in a hot-water bath for 5 minutes and shake to redissolve; if crystallization persists, thin with 30 g water and gentle reheat.

Use. Pomegranate molasses is the foundation acid in fattoush dressings (1 tbsp / 20 g whisked with olive oil, lemon, and sumac), in muhammara (the Syrian roasted-red-pepper-and-walnut dip; molasses is a defining ingredient), as a marinade base for grilled chicken thighs and lamb shoulder (tenderizing acid plus caramelizing sugar plus aromatic complexity), brushed onto roasted carrots and beets in the last 5 minutes of roasting (creates a mahogany glaze with a sour-sweet edge), drizzled over labneh or yogurt with za'atar and olive oil (a dramatic contrast of sour and creamy), and as a base for the Persian fesenjan walnut-pomegranate stew. It also replaces grenadine in any cocktail context where a real pomegranate flavor is wanted (a tequila sour with pomegranate molasses reads dramatically better than the same drink with grenadine).

Storage. Refrigerated in a glass jar with a tight lid: 2 to 3 months. Frozen in ice cube trays then transferred to a sealed bag: 6 to 12 months (defrost individual cubes at room temperature for 30 minutes before use). Room temperature (in a cool dark cabinet): 4 to 6 weeks for a freshly made batch; the high acid and low water activity make ambient storage safer than for most homemade syrups, but refrigeration is the safer default.
