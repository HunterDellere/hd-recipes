Chicken and ginger congee

The healing-food version. Bone-in chicken thighs simmered into the rice, a heavy ginger load to clean the palate, finished with sesame oil, scallion, and chili oil at the table. The Cantonese kitchen calls this comfort food when someone is sick.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a 90 minute pot: a 15 minute rice rest after the oil-and-salt massage, then 60 to 75 minutes of bare tremor simmer with the chicken. Active hands-on is about 25 minutes; the rest is passive but the pot needs a stir every 10 to 15 minutes to keep grains off the bottom.
  • The oil-and-salt rice massage and 15 minute rest is structural, not optional. Without it the polished grains swell intact instead of fragmenting into the cloudy starch suspension that defines congee.
  • Pre-prep the triple-stage ginger before the simmer goes on: 25 g smacked coins for the pot, 15 g microplaned for juice at the finish, 10 g fine julienne raw for the bowl. Mid-simmer is the wrong time to be peeling and slicing a ginger knob.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Rice prep

  1. 200 g jasmine rice
  2. 7 g neutral oil (rice bran, peanut, or canola)
  3. 3 g kosher salt

Chicken base

  1. 700 g bone-in skin-on chicken thighs
  2. 8 g kosher salt
  3. 15 g Shaoxing wine

Aromatics

  1. 60 g fresh ginger, 20 g sliced into 3 mm coins, 20 g grated and pressed for juice, 20 g cut into thin matchsticks — (4-inch knob; the triple-stage ginger load)
  2. 2200 ml water
  3. 4 each scallions, whites smacked for the pot, greens sliced thin for the bowl — (4 stalks)
  4. 8 g dried scallop (conpoy) optional — (2-3 small scallops; optional but the umami floor)

Finish

  1. 20 g light soy sauce
  2. 8 g toasted sesame oil
  3. 1 g white pepper, freshly ground
  4. 30 g chili oil with sediments, optional optional
  5. 10 g rice vinegar, for the table acid
  6. 15 g fresh cilantro leaves optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Dry-brine the chicken: pat 700 g / 1.5 lb chicken thighs dry, rub all over (under and over the skin) with 8 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt and 15 g / 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine. Rest uncovered on a rack at room temperature 20 minutes while the rice prep happens. The cure firms the meat and the alcohol takes off any chicken-blood notes. Dry brining
  2. 2
    Rinse 200 g / 1 cup jasmine rice three times until the water runs from milky to faintly hazy. Drain five minutes. Massage with 7 g / ½ tbsp neutral oil and 3 g / ½ tsp kosher salt for 30 seconds. Rest fifteen minutes.
  3. 3
    In a heavy 5-quart pot, combine 2200 ml / 9 cups cold water, the brined chicken thighs, the 20 g / smacked ginger coins, the white parts of the 4 scallions, and the 8 g / 2 tbsp optional dried scallop. Bring to a hard boil over high heat.
  4. 4
    Skim the gray scum that rises in the first 4-5 minutes with a fine spider; this is coagulated blood proteins from the chicken bones. Once the surface skims clean, add the rinsed and oiled rice. Bring back to a boil.
  5. 5
    Drop the heat to a steady tremor (small bubbles, never a rolling boil). Crack the lid. Simmer 35 minutes, stirring every ten minutes to scrape the bottom.
  6. 6
    At the 35-minute mark, fish out the chicken thighs with tongs. Pull the bone, discard the skin (or chop it fine and stir back in if you want the rendered-fat richness), shred the meat with two forks into bite-sized strands. Return the meat to the pot and continue simmering. The bones can go back in for another 20 minutes of broth-deepening if you have a sieve handy at the end.
  7. 7
    Continue simmering 20-25 minutes uncovered. The rice should be fully fragmented and the body the consistency of single cream. Fish out the bones (if returned), the smacked 20 g / 1-inch ginger coins, the scallop if used (chop it fine and stir back in), and the scallion whites.
  8. 8
    Just before serving, squeeze the 20 g / grated ginger through cheesecloth or a fine sieve into the pot, capturing the bright juice and discarding the spent fiber. The ginger juice carries the volatile aromatics that long simmer drives off; this is what restores the bright ginger nose to the finished bowl. Stir in 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy sauce and ¼ tsp white pepper. Taste; adjust salt or soy.
  9. 9
    Build each bowl: ladle congee, top with a small fistful of the 20 g / ginger matchsticks, the green parts of 4 scallions, optional 15 g / ½ cup cilantro leaves, a few drops (~2 g / ½ tsp) toasted sesame oil, a final dust of 1 g / ½ tsp white pepper, optional ½-1 tbsp / 7-15 g chili oil with sediments, and 2-3 drops of 10 g / 2 tsp rice vinegar at the rim of the bowl. Stir once and eat immediately.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion protein→whole bird (Cantonese full-pot juk)

    Substitute one whole 1.5 kg / 3.3 lb chicken for the thighs; simmer 60 minutes before pulling. Pull the bird out whole, debone over a tray, return all the meat. The full bird produces a richer broth and is the canonical preparation for a special occasion or a full-table breakfast.

  • Occasion protein→turkey (Thanksgiving leftovers)

    Replace the raw chicken with 700 g / 1.5 lb shredded leftover turkey thigh meat plus the carcass bones. Simmer the bones with the rice for 60 minutes for stock, fish out, then add the shredded meat in the last 15 minutes to warm through. The dish is a lifesaver for the day after Thanksgiving.

  • Regional aromatics→ginger-and-Chinese-yam (huai shan) for traditional medicinal version

    Add 50 g / scant ½ cup peeled and chunked dried Chinese yam (or fresh) to the simmer alongside the chicken. The yam softens to a faintly sweet, starchy element that thickens the broth further. The combination is a classic warming-tonic preparation in southern Chinese herbal kitchens.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • bone-in skin-on chicken thighs → whole chicken legs (drumstick + thigh) More bone, more collagen, similar simmer time. Pull and shred the same way.
  • bone-in chicken thighs (lean preference) → boneless skinless thighs plus a 200 g chicken carcass or wing tips for stock The carcass or wings provide the bone-derived gelatin the broth depends on. Pull boneless thighs at 30 minutes; they cook faster.
  • Shaoxing wine → dry sherry Closer to Shaoxing than dry white wine. Skip if neither is on hand; the cure works on salt alone.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → chili oil with sediments make it yourself The sediment-rich homemade version layers in toasted chili and sesame texture against the smooth porridge; bottled chili oil is heat without body.
  • → ginger-scallion oil (alternate finish) make it yourself A spoonful of fresh ginger-scallion oil over the bowl turns the basic finish into something more aromatic; the recipe is fifteen minutes and keeps for two weeks.
  • → crispy fried shallots (alternate topping) make it yourself Adds the crunch the dish otherwise lacks. The shallot oil that comes free is also a great drizzle.

Notes

Why the triple-stage ginger: each form delivers a different fraction of the rhizome. Smacked coins in the long simmer give back the heat-stable warm gingerone notes that build the broth's depth. Pressed juice added off-heat at the end carries the volatile gingerol that drives off in the first ten minutes of any simmer; this is the bright top note the bowl needs. Raw matchsticks scattered on the bowl provide a sharp peppery bite that contrasts with the soft porridge. Each step does work the others cannot.

Why this is the healing version: the high ginger load is functionally medicinal in the southern Chinese tradition (warming, anti-nausea, palate-cleansing), the broth is gelatin-rich and easy to digest, and the seasoning curve runs gentle. This is the bowl Cantonese parents make when a child has the flu, and the bowl western traditions reach for in the form of chicken soup. The mechanism is similar; the technique is more refined.

The dry-brine on the chicken is twenty minutes minimum, but you can push it overnight in the refrigerator for deeper salt penetration and a firmer cooked texture. Longer cures call for a lower salt load (5 g / ¾ tsp instead of 8 g / 1 tbsp) since the cure is more efficient.

Storage: three days refrigerated; the chicken shreds dry out faster than the rice does. Reheat with a splash of water and stir gently. Add the bowl-stage ginger juice fresh on each reheat; the existing ginger note will have flattened by then.
