British Victoria sponge

The structural English afternoon-tea cake: a 4-4-4-4 equal-weight sponge (butter, sugar, eggs, self-raising flour) baked in two 20 cm tins, sandwiched with raspberry jam and lightly whipped cream, and dusted with caster sugar. The simplest possible cake in technique and the most demanding in execution: every variable (butter temperature, egg incorporation, oven heat) directly affects the crumb.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Butter, eggs, milk, and bowl must all be at room temperature (about 20 °C / 68 °F). Cold butter does not cream; cold eggs split the batter; cold milk seizes the butter. Pull everything out of the fridge 60 minutes before starting; this is non-negotiable for a smooth emulsified batter.
  • Self-raising flour (not self-rising; the British spelling) is structural to the canonical cake. UK self-raising flour contains a specific ratio of baking powder (~12 g per kg of flour). U.S. self-rising flour contains baking powder AND salt and a softer wheat; substitute the U.S. version and reduce added salt to 0. Or make your own UK-style: 225 g all-purpose flour + 9 g baking powder, sifted twice.
  • A standard British Victoria sponge uses two 20 cm sandwich tins (loose-bottomed cake tins about 4 cm deep). U.S. 8 inch cake tins are the closest substitute. Tins must be greased and lined with parchment circles on the bottom; ungreased sponges weld to the tin and tear out the crust on unmoulding.

Mise en place by phase 2 phases ▾

Phase 1, the sponge (4-4-4-4 equal weights)

  1. 225 g unsalted butter, soft and pliable at room temperature, cubed, soft enough to dent with finger pressure but not melted
  2. 225 g caster sugar (superfine sugar)
  3. 4 each large eggs, at room temperature, cracked into a measuring cup, lightly beaten
  4. 225 g self-raising flour (UK), sifted, sifted twice (the second sift is structural for a light crumb)
  5. 2 g fine sea salt
  6. 5 ml vanilla bean paste
  7. 30 ml whole milk (at room temperature)

Phase 2, the filling and finish

  1. 150 g raspberry jam (good quality, with seeds for the canonical version)
  2. 200 ml heavy cream, cold
  3. 15 g icing sugar (for the cream)
  4. 2 ml vanilla bean paste (for the cream)
  5. 15 g caster sugar (for the dust)

Execution

  1. 1
    Preheat oven to 180 °C / 350 °F (160 °C fan / 325 °F fan) with the rack in the center. Grease two 20 cm round sandwich tins with a thin film of butter and line the bottom of each with a 20 cm circle of parchment. Have all ingredients at room temperature.
  2. 2
    Cream the butter and sugar. In the bowl of a stand mixer (paddle attachment) or a wide bowl with a hand mixer, beat the 225 g soft butter alone for 60 seconds at medium speed until it has loosened and lightened. Add the 225 g caster sugar in a steady stream; beat at medium-high for 4 to 5 minutes until the mixture has lightened to pale ivory and tripled in volume. The mixture should hold a soft peak and look airy; this is the structural creaming stage and the cake's lift comes from the air whipped in here.
  3. 3
    Add the eggs and vanilla. With the mixer on medium-low, add the 225 g beaten eggs about 30 g at a time (a tablespoon at a time), waiting until each addition is fully incorporated before adding more. The mixture should stay smooth and emulsified; if it starts to look curdled or split (small specks of butter visible), add 1 tbsp of the sifted flour and beat to bring it back together. Total egg addition takes 4 to 5 minutes. Add the 5 ml vanilla bean paste with the last egg addition.
  4. 4
    Fold in the flour. Sift the 225 g self-raising flour and 2 g salt directly over the batter (the second sift is structural). With the mixer on low, mix just until no streaks of flour remain, about 20 seconds. Do not over-mix; gluten development at this stage produces a tough cake. Drizzle in the 30 ml / 2 tbsp room-temperature milk; fold gently by hand with a rubber spatula 4 or 5 times to reach a soft dropping consistency. The batter should drop from a raised spoon in a slow ribbon, not pour like pancake batter or sit on the spoon like dough.
  5. 5
    Divide the batter evenly between the two lined tins (a kitchen scale helps; about 510 g per tin). Smooth the surfaces gently with the back of a spoon; do not press down. Place both tins on the center rack with at least 5 cm of space between them.
  6. 6
    Bake for 22 to 25 minutes, until the cakes are golden on top, springy when lightly pressed in the center (the indentation should bounce back), and a wooden skewer inserted in the center comes out clean. Do not open the oven before the 18 minute mark; cold air during the rise causes the cakes to sink. The tops should rise slightly above the rim of the tin and the edges should pull away slightly.
  7. 7
    Cool the cakes in the tins on a wire rack for 10 minutes. Run a thin knife around the inside of each tin; invert onto the wire rack; peel off the parchment. Re-invert so the cakes cool top-up. Cool fully to room temperature (about 30 minutes); warm cake melts the cream filling and produces a soggy assembly.
  8. 8
    Whip the cream. In a cold bowl, whip the 200 ml cold heavy cream with 15 g icing sugar and 2 ml vanilla to medium-stiff peaks (the cream should hold its shape on the whisk and look glossy, not buttery; stop whipping the moment it holds, over-whip and you have butter). Refrigerate until assembly.
  9. 9
    Assemble. Place one cake bottom-side up on a serving plate (the flat former-bottom is now the structural top for the filling). Spread the 150 g raspberry jam evenly to within 5 mm of the edge. Spoon the whipped cream onto the jam in dollops; spread gently to cover. Top with the second cake top-side up (so the cake reads "smooth top, pretty crust").
  10. 10
    Dust the top of the cake generously with the 15 g caster sugar through a fine-mesh sieve. The canonical Victorian finish is caster sugar (not icing sugar / powdered sugar); the slight crystal texture is structural and reads correctly British. Slice with a serrated knife. Serve with a pot of Earl Grey or English breakfast tea.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion occasion→as a jam-only Victoria sponge (the historical canonical version)

    Skip the whipped cream layer entirely. Sandwich the two cakes with the raspberry jam alone. This is the historical Victorian-era version and reads slightly more traditional. The cream is a modern home addition that the Great British Bake Off has popularized; either is correct.

  • Occasion flavor→as a coffee-walnut Victoria sponge variant

    Add 30 g instant espresso powder dissolved in 30 ml hot water to the batter at the vanilla stage. Fold in 80 g rough-chopped walnuts at the end of the flour stage. Replace the raspberry jam with a coffee buttercream (60 g soft butter, 120 g icing sugar, 5 g instant espresso in 15 ml hot water, beaten smooth). The cake becomes the British coffee-walnut variant of Victoria sponge; canonical at British church bake sales.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • self-raising flour (UK) → all-purpose flour + baking powder (225 g + 9 g) The structural substitute; sift the flour and baking powder together twice. U.S. self-rising flour also works but contains added salt; reduce the added salt to 0.
  • caster sugar (superfine sugar) → granulated sugar processed 30 seconds in a food processor Caster sugar dissolves into butter faster and produces a finer crumb. Granulated produces a slightly coarser-textured cake but is acceptable.
  • raspberry jam → strawberry jam, or homemade raspberry compote Strawberry is the modern American substitute; the British canonical is raspberry. Avoid grape, blueberry, or any non-red-berry jam; the color and flavor are wrong.

Notes

Why room temperature. The creaming method depends on the butter being soft and pliable enough to absorb air when beaten (a process called creaming), and on the eggs being warm enough to emulsify smoothly into the butter without splitting the mixture. Cold butter does not cream (it stays in lumps); cold eggs cause the butter to seize into hard fat specks; cold milk does the same. Every variable being at the same room temperature is the structural requirement for a smooth emulsified batter that bakes into a fine even crumb.

Why split sponge plus filling. The cake is baked as two thin sponges and sandwiched, rather than as one tall cake split horizontally, because the two-tin format produces flatter sponges with more uniform crust-to-crumb ratio, no sinking center, and predictable bake times. The Victorian baker did this for thermodynamic reasons (smaller tins bake more evenly in a wood-fired oven); the modern baker does it for the same reasons in a convection oven.

Why caster sugar dust, not icing sugar. The British canonical finish is caster sugar (granulated sugar that has been ground to a fine but still crystalline texture, between granulated and powdered). It dusts the top with a slight crystal sparkle and stays distinct on the surface. Icing sugar (powdered sugar) dissolves into the surface within minutes and disappears; not what the Victorian cake wants.

The 4-4-4-4 ratio. The equal-weight ratio (butter, sugar, eggs, flour) is the structural creaming-method ratio that defines the cake: 4 oz / 113 g of each in the historical recipe, scaled up to 8 oz / 225 g for two 20 cm tins. The eggs are the structural moisture and lift; the butter is the structural fat and tenderness; the sugar is the structural sweetness and aeration partner; the flour is the structural body. Adjusting any one of these breaks the cake into a different cake.

Storage. Best eaten same day. Keeps 2 days under a cake dome; the cream filling pulls the cake into the soft-storage zone after day 2 and the sponge dries out. Freeze the un-filled sponges (wrapped in beeswax or parchment) for up to 1 month; thaw at room temperature 2 hours and fill fresh.

Sources
  • British afternoon-tea canon (mid-19th century) — The Victoria sponge (or Victoria sandwich) was the cake Queen Victoria reputedly took with her afternoon tea after the death of Prince Albert; named for her, the cake became the structural test of British home baking and the centerpiece of every village fete and church bake sale. The defining 4-4-4-4 ratio (4 oz / 113 g each of butter, sugar, eggs, self-raising flour) is the structural creaming-method sponge and the simplest cake in the canon. The filling is canonically raspberry jam alone; the modern home version (and the one judged at the Great British Bake Off) adds a layer of lightly whipped cream between the jam and the top sponge.
