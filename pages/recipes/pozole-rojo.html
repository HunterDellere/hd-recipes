Pozole rojo

The red pozole of central and western Mexico: nixtamalized hominy and pork shoulder simmered in a deeply flavored broth stained crimson by guajillo and ancho chilies, finished tableside with shredded cabbage, radish, oregano, lime, and crumbled chicharrón. A festival dish and a Saturday-morning hangover cure, served from a clay pot into wide bowls.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Total cook is 3.5 hours; the pork shoulder needs 2 hours to render tender and the chili broth needs another hour of simmer to integrate. Plan to start mid-afternoon for a Saturday-night dinner, or make the entire pot the day before. Pozole tastes meaningfully better at the 24-hour mark.
  • Canned hominy is the standard everyday shortcut and produces a good pozole; dried hominy (soaked overnight, simmered 4 to 6 hours) is the canonical preparation and produces a meaningfully better dish if you have the time.
  • Stage the garnish bowls before serving: shredded cabbage, sliced radish, dried oregano, lime wedges, sliced white onion, crumbled chicharrón, and dried árbol chili flakes go in separate small bowls. Pozole is structurally a build-your-own dish; tableside assembly is the point.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the pork

  1. 1500 g pork shoulder (Boston butt), bone-in if available, cut into 6 cm chunks, bone reserved if present
  2. 200 g white onion, halved, left in halves with skin on for the simmer — (1 medium onion)
  3. 40 g garlic head, cut crosswise in half — (1 whole head; the structural aromatic foundation)
  4. 2 each bay leaves
  5. 4 g dried Mexican oregano
  6. 18 g kosher salt (for the broth)
  7. 3500 ml water

Phase 2, the chili broth

  1. 40 g dried guajillo chilies, stems and seeds removed, toasted 30 seconds in dry skillet — (about 6 to 7 guajillos; the structural bright-fruity red base)
  2. 20 g dried ancho chilies, stems and seeds removed, toasted 30 seconds — (about 3 anchos; adds raisin-sweet depth and deepens the color)
  3. 3 g dried árbol chilies (for heat), stems removed, seeds left in — (about 4 árbol chilies; structural heat; double for spicier pozole)
  4. 2 g whole cumin seeds
  5. 80 g white onion (for the chili paste), roughly chopped — (½ medium onion)
  6. 12 g garlic cloves, peeled — (3 cloves)
  7. 30 g neutral oil

Phase 3, the hominy

  1. 750 g white hominy, drained

Phase 4, the table garnish

  1. 200 g green cabbage, finely shredded (about 3 mm wide) — (about ⅓ small head; the structural crunch and freshness)
  2. 100 g radishes, thinly sliced — (about 8 medium radishes)
  3. 60 g white onion (for garnish), finely diced — (about ⅓ medium onion)
  4. 90 g limes, cut into wedges — (3 limes, 12 wedges; the structural finishing acid)
  5. 4 g dried Mexican oregano (for garnish)
  6. 60 g crumbled chicharrón (pork crackling)
  7. 8 each tostada shells or fried corn tortillas — (1 per serving; the structural carb alongside pozole; the diner breaks the tostada and dunks)

Execution

  1. 1
    Phase 1, simmer the pork. Combine the 1500 g pork shoulder chunks, 200 g halved onion, 40 g halved garlic head, 2 bay leaves, 2 g dried oregano, 18 g kosher salt, and 3500 ml water in a large heavy pot (8 L works well). Bring to a boil over high heat, skim off the gray scum that rises in the first 10 minutes (this is structural for a clean broth). Reduce to a bare simmer (small bubbles breaking the surface, not a rolling boil; boiling produces a cloudy broth), partially cover, and cook 90 to 120 minutes until the pork shreds easily with two forks. Strain the broth; reserve 2.5 L of broth. Pull the pork from the bones if bone-in; discard the spent aromatics. Shred the pork into bite-size pieces; reserve.
  2. 2
    Phase 2, make the chili broth. While the pork simmers, toast the 40 g guajillo and 20 g ancho chilies (stems and seeds removed) in a dry skillet over medium-low heat 30 seconds per side until fragrant; transfer to a bowl. Toast the 3 g árbol chilies separately for 20 seconds; add to the bowl. Cover all chilies with 500 ml of the hot pork broth (or boiling water); soak 20 minutes until pliable. Toast the 2 g cumin seeds in the same skillet 30 seconds until fragrant. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  3. 3
    Blend the chili paste. Transfer the soaked chilies plus 250 g of their soaking liquid to a blender; add the 80 g chopped onion, 12 g garlic cloves, and 2 g toasted cumin. Blend 2 minutes until completely smooth. Pass through a fine-mesh sieve into a bowl, pressing with a spatula (this removes the chili skins and seeds; the strain is structural for a smooth pozole broth). The yield is roughly 600 g chili paste.
  4. 4
    Fry the chili paste. Heat the 30 g neutral oil in the same large pot over medium-high heat until shimmering. Pour in the 600 g chili paste all at once; it will sizzle aggressively. Reduce to medium; fry 6 to 8 minutes, stirring constantly with a wooden spoon, until the paste darkens to a deep brick-red and the oil separates at the edges. The salsa-fry is structural; it develops Maillard depth and concentrates the chili flavor.
  5. 5
    Combine. Add the 2.5 L reserved pork broth to the fried chili paste; whisk to combine. Add the shredded pork and the 750 g drained hominy. Bring to a bare simmer, reduce heat to low, and cook uncovered 45 to 60 minutes for the flavors to integrate and the hominy to absorb the broth flavor. Taste and adjust salt; pozole rojo should read deeply pork-and-chili-flavored with a bright tomato-and-fruit chili note at the front and a long warm finish. If flat, more salt (start with 3 g increments); if too thick, more water; if too thin, simmer another 20 minutes uncovered.
  6. 6
    Stage the garnish. Set out small bowls with the 200 g shredded cabbage, 100 g sliced radish, 60 g diced raw onion, 90 g lime wedges, 4 g dried oregano (the garnish portion), 60 g crumbled chicharrón, and the 8 tostada shells. Each diner builds the bowl: ladle pozole into a wide soup bowl, top with a small handful of cabbage, sliced radish, raw onion, a generous pinch of oregano, a squeeze of lime, a sprinkle of chicharrón. Eat with a tostada on the side, broken and dunked. The build-your-own assembly is the structural point of the dish; do not garnish in the kitchen.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→with chicken (pozole de pollo)

    Replace the pork shoulder with 1500 g bone-in chicken thighs and 500 g chicken backs or wings. Simmer 60 to 75 minutes (chicken cooks faster than pork shoulder). Shred the meat off the bones; discard skin and bones. Proceed with the chili broth and hominy as written. The dish reads lighter than the pork version and is the canonical Sunday version in many Mexican households; pairs especially well with a tomatillo salsa on the side.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as pozole verde (the green Guerrero version)

    Replace the dried red chili paste with a green chili paste: blend 300 g tomatillos (charred), 1 poblano chili (roasted, peeled), 2 jalapeños, 80 g raw pepitas (toasted), 1 small bunch cilantro, 1 small bunch epazote (or substitute parsley), and 6 garlic cloves with 250 ml broth. Strain and fry as for the red version. The result is pozole verde, a structurally distinct and slightly lighter green-stained pozole. Serve with the same garnish set.

  • Occasion as a make-ahead party dish→the canonical Mexican party version, made 1 to 2 days ahead

    Make the entire pozole 1 to 2 days ahead; refrigerate covered. The flavors integrate noticeably overnight and the dish reads better at 24 hours than at the moment of cooking. Reheat gently over medium heat 30 minutes before serving (do not boil; boiling toughens the pork). Set out the garnishes fresh on the day of serving; pre-cut garnishes hold up to 12 hours refrigerated in airtight containers. This is the structural Mexican approach for Independence Day, Christmas Eve, and family gatherings.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • white hominy, drained → home-cooked dried hominy (250 g dry, soaked overnight, simmered 4 to 6 hours) Dried hominy is the canonical Mexican preparation and produces a meaningfully better pozole with firmer kernels and a more flavorful broth. The canned shortcut is acceptable; the dried version is the next-level improvement when time permits.
  • pork shoulder (Boston butt) → pork shoulder bone-in plus 250 g pork neck bones, pork hocks, or pig trotters The traditional Guerrero pozole uses pork head and trotters for the structural gelatin in the broth; pork neck bones or hocks added to the shoulder simmer provide the same gelatin without the visual challenge of trotters at the table. Strain out the bone-cuts after the simmer and discard or reserve the meat for another use.
  • dried árbol chilies → dried chipotle morita (for smoke) or fresh serrano (for cleaner heat) Árbol is the canonical clean-heat chili in pozole rojo. Chipotle morita adds smoke; serrano (added with the broth simmer) adds a brighter cleaner heat. All work; the choice depends on the desired heat profile.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → tostada shells or fried corn tortillas make it yourself Homemade corn tortillas pressed from fresh masa and pan-fried until crisp produce a tostada with structural integrity and corn flavor that supermarket tostadas cannot match. For pozole specifically, where the tostada is broken and dunked into the broth, the difference is clearly tasted in every bite.
  • → white hominy, drained make it yourselfstub Home-cooked dried hominy from nixtamalized corn produces a kernel with firmer texture, a more flavorful interior, and a structural broth contribution that canned hominy lacks. The 4-to-6 hour simmer is the rate-limiting step; consider making a large batch and freezing in portions.

Notes

Pozole rojo is one of the structural festival dishes of Mexico and one of the easiest of the long Mexican projects (the components are conceptually straightforward, the technique tolerates inattention through the simmer phase, and the dish forgives most adjustments). The two structural skills are the chili-toast-and-strain (which produces a smooth deeply-flavored broth from raw dried chilies) and the salsa-fry (which transforms the chili paste from a raw vegetal puree into a structurally complete sauce base). Master these two techniques and pozole rojo, mole, enchiladas, and most of the deep-red Mexican sauce tradition are within reach.

The pork-broth-then-chili-broth sequence is structural to the dish. The pork shoulder simmers first in plain water with aromatics; the resulting broth carries pork depth that the chili paste then layers onto. Skipping the long simmer (browning the pork in oil first, for example, then proceeding to the chili paste) produces a structurally different dish that reads more like a chili stew than a true pozole; the gentle long simmer is what produces the broth character that defines the dish.

The hominy texture is the second structural feature. Properly cooked hominy is tender through but holds a slight chew at the kernel center; the kernel should yield to a spoon but not collapse. Canned hominy is pre-cooked and softer than the canonical version; do not overcook it in the final simmer or the kernels turn mushy. Dried hominy (soaked overnight, simmered 4 to 6 hours separately, drained, added at the same point as canned would be) produces the structurally superior texture and is worth the time for festival-grade pozole.

The garnish is the dish, not a decoration. The shredded cabbage adds structural crunch; the sliced radish adds peppery freshness; the lime is the structural finishing acid; the oregano adds a floral-camphoraceous high note; the diced raw onion adds bite; the chicharrón adds salty-rich crunch. Each diner builds the bowl to taste, and the structural completeness of pozole comes from the layered tableside assembly. A pozole served pre-garnished from the kitchen loses the structural interactive character that defines the dish.

The tostada is structural. The diner breaks the tostada into the broth, dunks the broken pieces, and eats them as the broth-soaked carbohydrate alongside the meat-and-hominy bowl. Without the tostada, the dish reads as a soup; with it, the structural completeness as a full meal becomes apparent. Sliced fresh corn tortillas or fried tortilla strips work as substitutes; a piece of crusty bread does not.

Storage. Refrigerated, 5 days; freeze in 500 g portions for 3 months. The pozole tastes better at 24 hours than at the moment of cooking; many Mexican households make it the day before any festival or family lunch. Reheat gently over medium heat; do not boil, which toughens the pork.

Sources
  • The Mexican central and western canon (especially Jalisco, Guerrero, Michoacán). Documented across pre-Columbian Mesoamerican religious tradition (pozole was historically a ritual dish made with maize and meat); the modern pork-based pozole rojo stabilized in the colonial era after the Spanish introduction of pork. The dish is served at Independence Day, weddings, baptisms, and Saturday family lunches across central and western Mexico. — Three regional schools: pozole rojo (this version, with guajillo and ancho, Jalisco and Guerrero), pozole verde (with pumpkin seeds, tomatillo, and roasted chilies, Guerrero), and pozole blanco (no chili broth, served with the chilies and aromatics on the side, Michoacán). Pork is the dominant protein modern; chicken pozole is an acceptable substitute. The traditional Guerrero version uses pork head and trotters for the structural gelatin; pork shoulder is the modern home-cook shortcut.
