Miso-dashi chicken nimono with kabocha and shimeji

A Japanese nimono, a gently simmered dish built on dashi rather than browning. Bone-in chicken thighs and wedges of kabocha squash simmer in a kombu-katsuobushi dashi seasoned with sake, mirin, and a late addition of miso whisked in off the boil so its aromatics and live enzymes survive. Shimeji donate guanylate to the dashi glutamate; the squash thickens the broth as its starch leaches out. Restrained, savory-sweet, and clean, the opposite of a heavy braise.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Optional but recommended brine: dissolve 30 g / 2 tbsp kosher salt in 1 L / 4 cups cold water and soak the thighs 30 to 45 minutes, then pat dry. Because the seasoning here is gentle (dashi and miso, not a salt-heavy braise), a short brine meaningfully deepens the chicken without making the finished simmer salty. If you brine, hold back the optional finishing salt and taste first.
  • Make the dashi first, or cold-steep the kombu in the water for several hours ahead for a rounder extraction. Dashi is the entire backbone of this dish; there is no browning to fall back on if the stock is weak.
  • Never boil the kombu or hold the dashi above a bare simmer once the katsuobushi is in; above roughly 60 to 65°C kombu turns slimy and bitter and katsuobushi goes sour and fishy. Restraint is the technique.
  • Steam Japanese short-grain rice to finish with the nimono; wash it until the water runs clear, then rest it after cooking.

Mise en place by phase 6 phases ▾

Phase 1, the dashi

  1. 10 g kombu (dried kelp), wiped with a damp cloth, not rinsed; one 10 cm square — (the white bloom on the surface is glutamate, do not wash it off)
  2. 15 g katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes)
  3. 800 ml water

Phase 2, the chicken

  1. 900 g bone-in skin-on chicken thighs, patted dry; if not brined, salt lightly with 4 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt — (about 5 to 6 thighs. Boneless skin-on thighs are common in Japanese home cooking and work well here)

Phase 3, the simmer

  1. 60 g sake
  2. 45 g mirin
  3. 20 g light soy sauce
  4. 6 g granulated sugar
  5. 15 g ginger, sliced thin — (a 3 cm knob; a clean aromatic lift)

Phase 4, the vegetables

  1. 400 g kabocha squash, seeded, cut into 4 cm wedges, skin left on — (about a quarter of a kabocha; the skin is edible and holds the wedges together. The flexible slot. No ingredient page yet, so it renders as plain text)
  2. 150 g shimeji mushrooms, base trimmed, pulled into clusters — (1 to 2 packs; donate guanylate to the dashi)

Phase 5, finish

  1. 50 g white miso (shiro miso)
  2. 5 g rice vinegar
  3. 20 g scallion, sliced thin on the bias — (2 scallions, for the table)

Phase 5, serve

  1. 360 g steamed Japanese short-grain rice

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the dashi. Put the 10 g kombu in a pot with the 800 ml / 3⅓ cups water and warm slowly over low heat, 10 minutes, pulling it just before the first simmer bubbles break; never let it boil. Lift out the kombu (reserve it; see notes). Bring the liquid to a brief boil, kill the heat, and rain in the 15 g katsuobushi. Let it steep 2 minutes off heat as the flakes sink, then strain through a fine sieve. You have about 750 ml dashi. Do not press the flakes; squeezing extracts bitterness.
  2. 2
    In a wide pot, combine the strained dashi, 60 g / ¼ cup sake, 45 g / 3 tbsp mirin, 6 g / 1½ tsp sugar, and the 15 g sliced ginger. Bring to a gentle simmer over medium and let it bubble 2 minutes to cook off the raw sake alcohol. Note the seasoning order: sugar now, soy later, miso last, following sa-shi-su-se-so so the sugar penetrates before salt firms the surfaces.
  3. 3
    Slide in the 900 g chicken thighs skin-side-up and the 400 g kabocha wedges, arranging them in a single layer so each piece sits in the liquid. Cut a circle of parchment a little smaller than the pot, with a small vent hole in the center, and lay it directly on the surface as a drop-lid (otoshibuta). Simmer gently 18 minutes; the drop-lid circulates the modest liquid over everything without a hard boil.
  4. 4
    Lift the parchment. Stir in the 20 g / 4 tsp light soy and tuck in the 150 g shimeji clusters. Replace the parchment and simmer 8 more minutes, until the kabocha is knife-tender and the chicken reads 75°C / 165°F at the bone. The kabocha will have shed some starch and lightly thickened the broth.
  5. 5
    Reduce the heat to its lowest setting. Ladle a little of the hot broth into a bowl with the 50 g / 3 tbsp white miso and whisk to a smooth slurry, then stir it back into the pot. Do not let it boil after the miso goes in; boiling drives off the miso aroma and dulls it. Stir the 5 g / 1 tsp rice vinegar through off the boil.
  6. 6
    Taste the broth. It should read gently savory and lightly sweet, with the miso as a rounded background rather than a salt hit. If thin, a touch more miso slurry; if flat, the smallest pinch of salt; it should not need much. Let it stand 3 minutes off heat so the flavors settle and the kabocha drinks in the broth.
  7. 7
    Spoon a thigh, a wedge or two of kabocha, and a cluster of shimeji into each shallow bowl, ladle the broth over, and scatter the 20 g sliced scallion across. Serve with steamed short-grain rice alongside, not under; nimono is eaten with rice, not poured over it.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture vegetable→delicata squash and sweet potato

    Swap the kabocha for 250 g delicata squash (sliced into rings, skin on) and 150 g sweet potato in 3 cm chunks. Both are softer than kabocha, so add them at the soy stage rather than the start so they do not dissolve. A sweeter, autumnal version.

  • Regional vegetable→daikon and burdock

    Replace the squash with 300 g daikon in 3 cm half-moons and 80 g burdock (gobo) shaved and soaked in water. Add both at the very start with the chicken; daikon and burdock need the full simmer to go tender. This pushes the dish toward a classic chikuzenni profile, earthier and less sweet. Increase the dashi to 850 ml since the root vegetables drink more.

  • Regional flavor→red-miso, more assertive

    Use 40 g aka (red) miso in place of the 50 g white. Red miso is aged and far more assertive, so use less and expect a deeper, saltier, more rustic dish closer to a miso-nikomi. Cut the soy to 12 g to keep the salt in check.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • kombu and katsuobushi (for dashi) → dashi powder (hondashi) Instant dashi granules dissolved per package directions make a quick stock. Convenient and widely used in Japanese homes, though the flavor is flatter and saltier than freshly drawn dashi; cut the added soy if using it.
  • hon-mirin → mirin-fu seasoning plus a pinch of sugar The sweetened substitute lacks the depth and gloss of real fermented mirin. Use the same volume and add a small pinch of sugar to approximate; the dish reads slightly thinner.
  • white miso (shiro miso) → awase (blended) miso Awase sits between white and red. Use the same weight; the dish reads a touch more savory and less sweet than with pure shiro.
  • bone-in skin-on chicken thighs → boneless skin-on chicken thighs Very common in Japanese home cooking. Reduce the first simmer to 12 minutes since boneless thighs cook faster. The broth loses a little body from the missing bones but stays clean.

Notes

Why dashi, not browning. Nimono builds flavor through the simmering liquid rather than the Maillard sear that anchors a Chinese or Korean braise. The depth comes from umami synergy: glutamate from the kombu and the miso, plus inosinate from the katsuobushi and guanylate from the shimeji. Those nucleotides multiply the perceived savoriness of the glutamate several-fold, which is why a clear, lightly seasoned simmer tastes deeply satisfying without any browning or fat. A weak dashi has nowhere to hide here.

Why you must not boil the dashi. Kombu held above roughly 60 to 65°C releases alginates and mannitol that turn the stock slimy and faintly bitter; katsuobushi simmered rather than steeped off heat goes sour and aggressively fishy. The discipline of pulling the kombu before the boil and steeping the bonito off heat is the entire craft of dashi. Over-extraction is the most common failure.

Why the seasoning order matters (sa-shi-su-se-so). Japanese simmering follows a canonical order: sugar (sa), salt (shi), vinegar (su), soy (se from the old word seuyu), miso (so). Sugar molecules are larger and penetrate food more slowly than salt, so sugar goes in first; if salt or soy goes in early it firms the surface and blocks the sugar from getting in. Miso goes last because heat destroys its aroma. This recipe follows that order: sugar with the dashi, soy at the midpoint, miso off the boil.

Why the drop-lid (otoshibuta). A lid that rests directly on the food, rather than on the pot rim, keeps the modest braising liquid circulating over the tops of the chicken and squash without a vigorous boil that would break them apart. The parchment version is the home cook's otoshibuta; the vent hole lets steam escape so it does not lift off. It is how nimono cooks evenly in a shallow pool of liquid.

Why the miso is whisked in off the boil. Miso is a living fermented paste, and its aromatic esters and its enzymes are heat-sensitive. Boiling miso flattens it to a one-note salty paste and can make it grainy. Slurrying it with a little hot broth first prevents clumps, and adding it at the lowest heat at the very end keeps the fragrance intact. The same rule governs miso soup.

What to do with the spent kombu. The simmered kombu is not waste; slice it thin and add it back to the pot for the last few minutes, or simmer it briefly with a little soy and mirin to make a quick tsukudani relish for the rice. Japanese home cooking rarely discards it.
