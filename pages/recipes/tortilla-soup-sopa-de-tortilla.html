Sopa de tortilla (Mexican tortilla soup)

A clear chicken-and-tomato broth scented with toasted pasilla chile and epazote, finished at the table with fried tortilla strips, diced avocado, crumbled queso fresco, Mexican crema, and a wedge of lime. The structural Mexican comfort soup: a thin broth-based dish where the garnish is half the meal, the tortilla strips provide structural texture, and the layering of components at the table is the eating experience.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the broth base

  1. 600 g bone-in skin-on chicken pieces (thighs and drumsticks)
  2. 2000 ml water
  3. 150 g white onion, halved — (1 small; half for the broth, half for the salsa base below)
  4. 15 g garlic cloves, smashed — (4 cloves; for the broth)
  5. 12 g kosher salt
  6. 2 each bay leaf

Phase 2, the tomato-chile salsa

  1. 500 g ripe tomatoes (Roma or vine)
  2. 75 g white onion, halved — (¼ medium, raw)
  3. 10 g garlic cloves, peeled — (3 cloves, raw)
  4. 15 g dried pasilla chile (or ancho) — (2 chiles; the structural canonical Mexico City chile for this soup; ancho is the accessible substitute)
  5. 2 g epazote, dried or fresh (optional but structural)
  6. 30 g neutral oil (for frying the salsa)

Phase 3, the tortilla strips (the structural garnish)

  1. 8 each corn tortillas (day-old preferred, stale is structurally better than fresh for crisping) — (8 tortillas; cut into 0.5 cm wide strips; the structural texture-and-name of the soup)
  2. 300 ml neutral oil (for frying tortilla strips)
  3. 2 g fine sea salt (for the fried strips)

Phase 4, the table garnishes (essential, not optional)

  1. 200 g ripe Hass avocado, diced just before serving — (1 large; the structural creamy cooling element)
  2. 150 g queso fresco, crumbled
  3. 150 g Mexican crema (or sour cream thinned with 2 tbsp milk)
  4. 2 each fresh lime wedges — (2 limes cut into 6 wedges each; the finishing acid added at the table)
  5. 20 g fresh cilantro, chopped — (small handful)
  6. 5 g one extra dried pasilla chile, toasted and crumbled (optional table garnish for extra heat) — (1 chile; the canonical Mexico City table garnish, diners crumble over their bowl for additional smoky chile)

Execution

  1. 1
    Build the broth. Combine the 600 g chicken pieces, 2000 ml water, 75 g halved onion (half of the broth-base onion), 15 g smashed garlic, 12 g salt, and 2 bay leaves in a large stockpot. Bring to a simmer over medium-high; skim off any gray scum that rises in the first 10 minutes (this scum is coagulated blood proteins and is bitter). Reduce to medium-low, cover, simmer 35 to 40 minutes until the chicken is tender and falling off the bone. Lift the chicken out; let cool. Strain the broth through a fine-mesh sieve, discarding the solids. Shred the chicken meat with two forks; discard the bones and skin.
  2. 2
    Toast and rehydrate the pasilla chiles. While the broth simmers, remove the stems and seeds from the 15 g dried pasilla chiles. Toast the chiles in a dry skillet over medium heat for 30 to 45 seconds per side, until pliable, fragrant, and slightly darker (do not burn; scorched chiles read bitter). Transfer to a small heatproof bowl; cover with 200 ml of boiling water; soak 15 minutes until soft and rehydrated.
  3. 3
    Char the tomatoes. Place the 500 g tomatoes on a sheet pan; broil 6 to 8 minutes, turning once, until the skins are blistered and blackened in spots and the tomatoes have softened. The char is the structural step that adds depth to the soup base; raw tomatoes produce a thinner, less complex broth. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Blend the salsa base. Combine the charred tomatoes (skins and all), the rehydrated pasilla chiles (with about 60 ml of their soaking liquid), the 75 g remaining raw onion, the 10 g raw garlic, and the 2 g epazote in a blender. Blend on high for 60 seconds until smooth.
  5. 5
    Fry the salsa. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide saucepan over medium-high heat until shimmering. Pour the blended salsa into the hot oil (it will spit aggressively). Cook 5 to 6 minutes, stirring, until the salsa has darkened and concentrated slightly, and the kitchen smells deeply of tomato and chile. The fry-the-salsa step is structurally what gives the soup its depth; skipping it produces a thinner, raw-tasting broth. Red oil rendering
  6. 6
    Combine and simmer the soup. Pour the strained chicken broth into the saucepan with the fried salsa. Add the shredded chicken back to the soup. Bring to a simmer over medium heat; simmer 15 minutes uncovered to integrate the flavors. Taste; adjust salt. The soup should be a clear-ish reddish-brown broth with the shredded chicken suspended throughout, deeply savory with a faint smoky pasilla aromatic.
  7. 7
    Fry the tortilla strips. While the soup simmers, heat the 300 ml / 1¼ cups neutral oil in a deep skillet to 175°C / 350°F. Fry the 8 tortillas worth of strips in 3 to 4 batches, 90 seconds per batch, until golden and crisp. Lift with a spider; drain on paper towels; immediately salt with the 2 g fine sea salt while still hot.
  8. 8
    Set up the table garnishes. Dice the 200 g avocado just before serving (cut avocado browns within 30 minutes). Arrange the avocado, 150 g crumbled queso fresco, 150 g Mexican crema, 12 lime wedges, 20 g chopped cilantro, and the optional crumbled toasted pasilla chile in small bowls on the table. Each garnish goes on each diner's soup at the table; this self-serve assembly is structural to the eating experience.
  9. 9
    Serve. Ladle the hot soup into wide shallow bowls. Top each bowl with a generous handful of the fried tortilla strips (the strips must go on at the moment of serving, not pre-mixed; they crisp on top of the hot broth and slowly soften as the diner eats, providing textural contrast through the meal). Each diner then adds avocado, queso fresco, a drizzle of crema, a squeeze of lime, and a scatter of cilantro. The dish is constructed at the table; this is the canonical Mexican presentation.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→vegetarian version (with vegetable stock and beans)

    Replace the chicken broth with 2000 ml of vegetable stock. Replace the shredded chicken with 300 g of cooked black beans or pinto beans. Reduce the simmer in step 1 to 10 minutes (since you are not poaching chicken). The dish becomes a vegetarian sopa de tortilla; the legume protein replaces the chicken structurally.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→sopa de lima (Yucatecan citrus version)

    Use chicken broth as base. Skip the pasilla chile entirely. Add the juice of 2 limes and the zest of 1 lime in the last 5 minutes of the simmer. Skip the fried salsa (the Yucatecan version uses raw tomato added at the end). Garnish with extra lime wedges and habanero chile. The dish becomes sopa de lima, the structural Yucatecan citrus-forward expression of tortilla soup.

  • Regional occasion→with hominy (the Tlaxcala-leaning expression)

    Add 250 g of cooked hominy (canned, drained) to the soup in the last 15 minutes. The dish takes on a structural pozole-leaning character; halfway between sopa de tortilla and pozole rojo. A common Tlaxcala variation.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dried pasilla chile (or ancho) → dried ancho chile (use the same weight) Ancho is the dried poblano, slightly sweeter and less smoky than pasilla. The dish reads slightly milder; acceptable substitute when pasilla is unavailable (ancho is the more widely distributed of the two in US markets).
  • epazote, dried or fresh → omit (no direct substitute exists) Epazote is structurally distinctive and has no good substitute; the dish reads slightly less Mexican without it but is still recognizable. Available at Mexican groceries fresh or dried; do not substitute oregano or other herbs.
  • Mexican crema → sour cream thinned with 2 tbsp whole milk Mexican crema is structurally thinner and slightly tangier than American sour cream; thinning sour cream approximates the consistency.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → corn tortillas (day-old preferred, stale is structurally better than fresh for crisping) make it yourself Fresh corn tortillas made from masa, then dried 24 hours and cut into strips, produce dramatically more flavorful tortilla strips than supermarket tortillas.

Notes

The garnish is half the dish. Sopa de tortilla without the garnish set (fried tortilla strips, avocado, queso fresco, crema, lime, cilantro) is a thin chicken-and-tomato broth. The garnishes are structurally what make the dish: the tortilla strips provide texture and the dish-name itself, the avocado provides creamy fat to balance the broth, the queso fresco provides salt-and-tang, the crema provides cooling counter to the chile, the lime provides the bright finishing acid, the cilantro provides aromatic lift. Skipping any of these flattens the dish significantly. The Mexican table-service tradition lets each diner customize the balance; this is part of the dish design.

Stale tortillas are structurally better. Day-old (or even older) corn tortillas crisp better and faster than fresh tortillas because the lower moisture content lets the oil reach the cooking temperature on the surface faster. Fresh tortillas can be used but take longer to crisp and tend to absorb more oil. The traditional Mexican household uses tortillas that are 1 to 2 days old for tortilla soup specifically; saving leftover tortillas for the soup is the canonical practice.

Why fry the salsa. The "freír la salsa" technique (frying blended salsa in hot oil before incorporating into the soup) is structural to Mexican cooking; the hot-oil contact develops new Maillard products in the salsa, concentrates the flavor by driving off water, and changes the color from bright red to deeper red-brown. Salsa added to the soup without the fry produces a thinner, raw-tasting broth that reads as undercooked. The fry step is non-negotiable for canonical Mexican soups, sauces, and braises.

Epazote is structurally distinctive. Epazote (Dysphania ambrosioides) is a Mexican herb with no good substitute; the flavor is anise-medicinal-citrus, distinct and immediately identifiable. It is used canonically in Mexican tomato-and-bean dishes (sopa de tortilla, frijoles de la olla, quesadillas de huitlacoche, mole verde). Available at Mexican groceries fresh (in small bunches) or dried (in small bags); the dried version is acceptable but the fresh is dramatically better. Do not substitute oregano, basil, or any Mediterranean herb; the flavor profile is too different and the dish reads wrong.

Storage. The broth keeps 5 days refrigerated and freezes 3 months. Reheat gently; the shredded chicken can dry out on reheat (add a splash of stock to compensate). The garnishes do not store; prepare fresh just before serving. Fried tortilla strips keep 24 hours at room temperature in a sealed container (re-crisp briefly in a 175°C / 350°F oven for 3 minutes if they have softened). The dish is structurally a make-the-broth-ahead, prepare-garnishes-fresh preparation.

Sources
  • Mexican central tradition (Mexico City and Tlaxcala) — Sopa de tortilla (also called sopa azteca in some Mexican regions) is the canonical Mexican broth-based soup, found across central Mexico with regional variations. The Mexico City version uses pasilla chile; the Tlaxcala version uses a more aggressive guajillo profile; the Yucatecan version (sopa de lima) is the citrus-forward variant. This recipe is the central-Mexican Mexico City expression, which is the most internationally recognized version.
