Beef bourguignon

The Burgundian red-wine beef braise: chuck cubes browned hard, lardon-rendered fat as the structural base, deglazed with a full bottle of dry red Burgundy (or pinot noir), aromatic bouquet garni, slow oven braise 3 hours until the collagen converts to gelatin, finished with sautéed pearl onions and pan-glazed cremini mushrooms tucked in just before service. The structural rule is heat discipline (a tremor, never a boil, throughout the braise so the protein stays tender rather than seizing), and the structural ingredient is real Burgundy (a fruity acidic pinot, not a heavy California cab); the dish reads like the wine it was cooked in.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a 3 hour active project plus a 3 hour oven braise, and is structurally better when made the day before and rested overnight in the refrigerator. The braise extracts collagen and the cooled-and-reheated dish has more cohesive sauce body. Plan for day 1 = cook to the end of the braise + cool overnight; day 2 = warm the braise to a tremor, finish with the onions and mushrooms, serve. Same-day service is acceptable but reads less integrated.
  • Open the bottle of red Burgundy or pinot noir 1 hour before the cook begins; the wine benefits from aeration and the dish reads better when the wine is not freshly-poured-from-cork-tight bottle. Drink a glass while cooking; that is also Burgundian tradition.
  • Brown the beef in small batches (no more than a single uncrowded layer). Crowding the pan turns the hard sear into a steam; the Maillard crust is the structural source of the deep brown sauce color and savory depth. Plan for 4 to 5 separate batches over 25 to 30 minutes; this is the slow patient phase of the dish.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the lardons and brown the beef

  1. 200 g slab bacon or pancetta, in 1 cm cubes, rind removed if present, cut into 1 cm cubes (the lardons render to crispy bacon-fat cubes that go into the finished stew) — works; heavily smoked bacon overwhelms the wine)
  2. 1500 g beef chuck (boneless chuck roast), trimmed of silverskin, cut into 5 cm / 2 inch cubes (uniform size is structural for even braising; smaller cubes overcook and shred, larger cubes do not penetrate fully) — which dry out during the long braise)
  3. 12 g kosher salt (for seasoning the beef)
  4. 2 g black pepper, freshly cracked
  5. 25 g all-purpose flour

Phase 2, the aromatic base and braise

  1. 250 g yellow onion, large dice, large dice, 1.5 cm pieces — (1 large onion; will partially melt into the sauce during the braise)
  2. 150 g carrot, large dice, large dice, 1.5 cm pieces — (2 medium carrots)
  3. 25 g garlic, smashed, smashed with the side of a knife, papery skin removed — (6 to 8 cloves)
  4. 1 × 6 oz / 170 g can tomato paste
  5. 750 ml red Burgundy or pinot noir (full bottle, dry, medium-body)
  6. 500 ml beef stock (homemade or low-sodium)
  7. 1 each bouquet garni (thyme, bay, parsley stems tied with twine), 6 sprigs fresh thyme + 2 bay leaves + 10 parsley stems tied tightly with kitchen twine into a single bundle (the twine lets you fish it out at the end) — (1 bundle; will be removed before service)

Phase 3, the garnish (sautéed separately, added at the end)

  1. 250 g pearl onions (frozen peeled, or fresh blanched and peeled)
  2. 350 g cremini mushrooms (or button), quartered, cleaned, halved or quartered depending on size (target 2 to 3 cm pieces)
  3. 30 g unsalted butter (for the garnish sauté)
  4. 5 g granulated sugar (for the pearl onion glaze)

Phase 4, the finish

  1. 10 g flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped, finely chopped just before serving — (a small handful; bright fresh herb finish to cut the rich gelatinous sauce)

Execution

  1. 1
    Salt the 1500 g / 3.3 lb beef chuck cubes with the 12 g / 2½ tsp salt and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper 30 minutes ahead. Pat the surface dry with paper towels (any surface water prevents the sear). Toss in the 25 g / 3 tbsp flour to coat lightly. Preheat the oven to 160°C / 325°F. Set a 6 to 7 L heavy-bottomed Dutch oven over medium heat.
  2. 2
    Render the lardons: add the 200 g / 7 oz cubed bacon to the cold Dutch oven; set over medium-low heat. Render slowly 8 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the lardons are deeply golden and crispy and the fat has rendered out to about 60 g of rendered fat in the pan. Remove the crispy lardons with a slotted spoon to a paper-towel-lined plate; reserve. Pour off all but about 30 g of the rendered fat; reserve the rest in a small heatproof bowl for the garnish sauté. Lardon rendering
  3. 3
    Increase heat to medium-high. Brown the floured beef cubes in 4 to 5 batches, never crowding the pan (a single uncrowded layer, ~7 to 8 cubes per batch), 3 to 4 minutes per side until each face has a deep brown crust. Transfer the browned cubes to a plate after each batch. Adjust the heat as the fond darkens; the bottom of the pot should be coated with a dark brown but not black fond. If the fond starts to scorch, add 30 ml / 2 tbsp of the wine and scrape it loose with a wooden spoon between batches. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Reduce heat to medium. Add the 250 g / 1 large onion (large dice), 150 g / 2 carrots (large dice), and 25 g / 6 to 8 cloves smashed garlic to the Dutch oven; sauté 6 to 7 minutes until the onion is soft and translucent and the surface of the pot has begun to brown again. Add the 45 g / 3 tbsp tomato paste; cook 2 to 3 minutes, stirring constantly, until the paste has darkened from bright red to brick red and is sizzling visibly in the fat (the bloom releases its glutamate concentration into the fat). Blooming aromatics
  5. 5
    Pour in the 750 ml / 1 bottle of red Burgundy or pinot noir all at once. Scrape the bottom of the pot with a wooden spoon to deglaze the fond into the wine (this is the deep brown color of the finished sauce; do not skip). Bring to a hard boil; reduce by about half (about 8 to 10 minutes; the smell shifts from raw wine to a clean fruity tang). The reduction concentrates the wine and cooks off the harsh alcohol notes. Deglazing
  6. 6
    Add the 500 ml / 2 cups beef stock, the browned beef cubes (and any accumulated juices), and the bouquet garni (1 bundle). Stir to settle the beef into the liquid; the meat should be just barely covered by the liquid. If short, add hot water to just-cover. Bring to a bare simmer (tremor only, never a vigorous boil; visible bubbles breaking the surface but no rolling boil). Cover with the lid slightly ajar (to allow 10 percent evaporation).
  7. 7
    Transfer the Dutch oven to the preheated 160°C / 325°F oven. Braise 2½ to 3 hours, checking every 45 minutes to confirm the temperature is a tremor (not a boil) and to stir gently. The beef is done when a fork inserted into a cube slides in with no resistance and the cube can be pulled apart with the slightest pressure; if there is still resistance at 2½ hours, give it another 30 to 45 minutes. The collagen has fully converted to gelatin; the sauce has thickened to a glossy spoon-coating consistency.
  8. 8
    While the braise is in the final 30 minutes, sauté the garnish vegetables. In a separate wide skillet over medium-high heat, add 15 g / 1 tbsp of the reserved bacon fat plus 15 g / 1 tbsp of the butter. Add the 250 g / 8 oz pearl onions; sauté 5 to 6 minutes until lightly browned on multiple sides. Add the 5 g / 1 tsp sugar and 30 ml / 2 tbsp of water; reduce heat to medium-low. Cook 3 to 4 minutes more, shaking the pan, until the sugar has caramelized and the onions are glossy with a light brown glaze.
  9. 9
    In the same skillet, add the remaining 15 g / 1 tbsp butter. Increase heat to medium-high. Add the 350 g / 12 oz quartered cremini mushrooms; cook 6 to 8 minutes without stirring for the first 3 minutes (let them release their water and start to brown). Then stir occasionally until the mushrooms have shrunk by half, browned deeply on multiple sides, and the pan is dry of liquid. Season with a pinch of salt. Maillard sear
  10. 10
    When the braise is done, remove from the oven. Fish out the bouquet garni and discard. Add the sautéed pearl onions, sautéed mushrooms, and reserved crispy lardons to the braise. Stir gently to integrate. Let stand 10 minutes uncovered to allow the garnish to warm through and the sauce to settle. Taste; adjust salt (the long braise concentrates flavors and the stew often needs a final ½ tsp salt at this point). Top with the 10 g chopped parsley just before serving. Serve in shallow bowls over buttered egg noodles or mashed potatoes; the sauce is structural to the dish and should pool generously around the meat.
Safety
  • Beef chuck braised to fully fork-tender (internal 90°C / 195°F) rather than a medium-rare doneness Beef bourguignon is a braise of high-collagen chuck cooked well past the USDA published-safe target of 145°F internal; the dish is fully cooked through to fork-tender (around 90°C / 195°F internal) for the canonical texture, which is when the collagen has fully converted to gelatin. This is structurally a different cooking regime than steakhouse beef and the USDA whole-muscle target does not apply in the same way; the long simmer above 75°C provides a generous pasteurization equivalency for any pathogens present. The food-safety attention in a braise is on (a) chilling leftovers rapidly within 2 hours of cooking and (b) reheating to 75°C / 165°F throughout before serving, not on the internal temperature at first service. Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein pivot→as coq au vin (the chicken-in-red-wine sibling)

    Substitute the 1500 g beef chuck with 1500 g of bone-in chicken thighs and drumsticks (skin-on); reduce the braise time to 50 to 60 minutes total. The aromatic base, wine, garnish, and finish are otherwise identical. Coq au vin and bourguignon are structurally the same dish executed with different proteins, both rooted in the Burgundian tradition. See the coq-au-vin recipe page for full treatment with the chicken-specific timing.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as a daube provençale (the southern French variant with orange peel and olives)

    Replace the bouquet garni with: 6 sprigs fresh thyme + 2 bay leaves + the peel of half an orange (avoid the white pith) + 1 strip of dried orange peel. Add 100 g of pitted niçoise olives in the last 15 minutes of the braise. Skip the cremini mushrooms and pearl onions; the daube does not include them. The result is structurally daube provençale, the southern French braise that reads sunnier and brighter than the Burgundian bourguignon; the orange peel and olive contribute Mediterranean fruit-and-brine that the Burgundian version lacks.

  • Occasion occasion→as a make-ahead two-day preparation (the canonical Burgundian timing)

    Execute steps 1 through 7 on day 1; cool the Dutch oven uncovered for 1 hour, then cover and refrigerate overnight. On day 2, scrape off any solidified fat from the surface (the fat solidifies on the cold sauce and is easy to lift off; about 60 to 80 g). Warm the braise on the stovetop over medium-low to a tremor (40 to 50 minutes from cold). Execute steps 8 through 10. The result is structurally the same dish but with deeper integration and a more cohesive sauce; the canonical Burgundian Sunday-dinner timing.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • red Burgundy or pinot noir (full bottle, dry, medium-body) → a medium-bodied non-Burgundian red (Côtes du Rhône, Beaujolais cru, Spanish Garnacha, or Italian Sangiovese) Real Burgundy or quality pinot noir is the structural choice; the dish reads like the wine. Acceptable substitutes are medium-bodied fruit-forward reds with bright acidity; Côtes du Rhône, cru Beaujolais (Morgon, Brouilly), Spanish Garnacha, or Italian Sangiovese all work. Do not use heavy oaky cabernet sauvignon, syrah/shiraz, or zinfandel; the dish reads jammy and the tannins coat the palate unpleasantly. Do not use cooking wine; the added salt destroys the dish.
  • slab bacon or pancetta, in 1 cm cubes → thick-cut American bacon (unsmoked or lightly smoked) French lardons are the structural canonical choice; pancetta gets very close to the same texture and flavor; thick-cut unsmoked or lightly smoked American bacon works. Heavily smoked American bacon (Applewood, Hickory) overwhelms the wine and reads as a different dish; if heavily smoked is all that is available, blanch the bacon in boiling water for 2 minutes to leach out some of the smoke before rendering.
  • beef chuck (boneless chuck roast) → beef short ribs (boneless) or brisket Chuck is the canonical cut for the high collagen content and even shape; boneless short ribs work and produce a richer more fatty stew; brisket works and produces a slightly more shreddy texture. Do not substitute leaner cuts (round, sirloin, eye of round) which dry out completely during the long braise.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → beef stock (homemade or low-sodium) make it yourself The stock is structural in the sauce body. Homemade roasted-bone beef broth has the gelatin and depth that commercial beef stock lacks (commercial is mostly salt water with brown food coloring); the dish reads dramatically better with real broth.

Notes

Beef bourguignon is a wine-driven dish, and the wine selection is structural. The dish reads like the wine it was cooked in, amplified by the reduction. A thin imbalanced wine produces a thin imbalanced stew; a deep fruit-forward acidic wine produces a deep fruit-forward acidic stew. The classical pairing is real red Burgundy (pinot noir from the Côte d''Or), but a good California or Oregon pinot noir produces an excellent and more affordable version. Do not use heavy oaky cabernet sauvignon or syrah; the tannin profile is wrong for the slow braise and the dish reads jammy and astringent. The wine you cook with should also be a wine you would happily drink with the finished dish; "cooking wine" with added salt is a different product and does not belong in serious cookery.

The Maillard sear on the beef is structurally where the dish gets its dark brown color and savory depth. Crowding the pan turns the sear into a steam, the beef expels its water rather than browning, and the resulting stew is pale and watery. Four to five separate uncrowded batches over 25 to 30 minutes is the patient way; this is the most important phase of the dish. The browned fond on the bottom of the Dutch oven after the sears is the structural concentrate that the wine deglaze pulls into the sauce.

The garnish is sautéed separately and added at the end, structurally for two reasons: (1) braised pearl onions and braised mushrooms turn mushy and lose their texture during the long oven cook, and (2) sautéing them separately develops a Maillard browning that adds a second layer of savory depth to the finished stew. The Julia Child template that has dominated American home cooking is right about this; some shortcuts that braise everything together produce a one-note stew where every component has the same texture.

The make-ahead is genuinely better, not just convenient. The overnight rest in the refrigerator lets the gelatin and wine integrate, the fat solidifies and lifts off the top (allowing portion-control of richness), and the flavors integrate from a disparate collection of brown elements into a single cohesive deep-brown sauce that reads like a single thing. Plan for the day-ahead cook if at all possible; same-day service is acceptable but not canonical.

Storage. Keeps 4 to 5 days refrigerated; reheats well on the stovetop over medium-low heat to a tremor, adding 30 ml of water or stock if the sauce has tightened too much. Freezes well; portion into 500 g containers, freeze flat, keeps 3 months at -18°C / 0°F. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator, warm to a tremor on the stovetop. Do not microwave; the wine sauce separates and the gelatin breaks.

Sources
  • Burgundian canon (codified through Escoffier and stabilized in the modern English-speaking world by Julia Child's 1961 Mastering the Art of French Cooking) — The dish is a peasant Burgundian preparation elevated by Escoffier into the haute cuisine canon; Julia Child's 1961 Boeuf Bourguignon recipe is the most influential English-language reference and the structural template that most American home cooks follow. The defining elements: chuck (the high-collagen cut that converts to gelatin during the long braise), lardons (cubed slab bacon or pancetta rendered as the structural fat and aromatic base), red Burgundy or pinot noir (the canonical wine; the dish reads like the wine, so a thin or imbalanced wine produces a thin or imbalanced stew), bouquet garni (thyme, bay, parsley stems tied with kitchen twine), pearl onions sautéed separately and glazed (added at the end, not braised through), cremini or button mushrooms sautéed separately (added at the end, not braised through), and the resting-overnight integration (the dish reads dramatically better on day two as the gelatin and wine reduce and integrate).
