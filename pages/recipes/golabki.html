Gołąbki (Polish stuffed cabbage rolls)

Wilted cabbage leaves wrapped around a filling of ground pork, par-cooked rice, sautéed onion, and dill, packed tightly into a Dutch oven, blanketed with a light tomato-and-stock sauce, and slow-baked until the cabbage is silken-tender and the filling is set. The Polish cabbage roll; the Russian and Ukrainian (holubtsi) and Romanian (sarmale) versions are siblings.

6servingsmakes 12 to 14 rolls; serves 6 (2 rolls per person) with sides

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The cabbage leaves need to be separated and wilted before rolling. The Polish method is to core the whole head and submerge in boiling water for 2 to 3 minutes at a time, peeling leaves off one by one as they soften. Plan 15 to 20 minutes for this step.
  • The rice goes in par-cooked (boiled 8 minutes, drained), not raw and not fully cooked. Raw rice will not soften in time during the 90-minute bake and stays crunchy; fully cooked rice goes mushy. Par-cooked is the structural compromise.
  • Cool the par-cooked rice and the sautéed onion before mixing into the raw pork. Hot ingredients start cooking the meat as it sits, which throws off the texture.
  • Pack the rolls tightly in the pot. Loose packing lets the rolls float in the sauce and unroll during the bake; tight packing keeps them shaped.

Phase 1, the cabbage

  1. 1500 g large green cabbage (or savoy cabbage, see notes), tough outer leaves removed; whole head cored from the bottom with a sharp knife (cut a deep cone around the core and pull it out)
  2. 4000 ml water (for blanching the cabbage)
  3. 20 g kosher salt (for the cabbage blanch)

Phase 2, the filling

  1. 100 g long-grain white rice (jasmine or basmati works), rinsed under cold water until clear, then par-boiled (see Step 3)
  2. 200 g yellow onion, peeled and finely diced, finely diced — (1 large onion; sautéed in butter and cooled before going into the filling)
  3. 30 g unsalted butter (for the onion)
  4. 700 g ground pork (80/20 fat content)
  5. 1 each large egg (binder for the filling) — (1 egg; the binder that holds the filling together inside the rolled cabbage)
  6. 8 g kosher salt (for the filling)
  7. 4 g black pepper, freshly ground
  8. 5 g sweet paprika (Hungarian preferred)
  9. 12 g fresh dill, finely chopped
  10. 10 g fresh parsley, finely chopped
  11. 10 g garlic, peeled and microplaned to a paste — (3 cloves; the aromatic)

Phase 3, the sauce and bake

  2. 500 ml chicken stock or beef stock
  3. 1 × 6 oz / 170 g can tomato paste

  5. 4 g kosher salt (for the sauce)
  6. 6 g granulated sugar

Phase 4, service

  1. 200 g sour cream (for serving)

Execution

  1. 1
    Bring the 4000 ml / 4 liters water to a hard boil in a large pot with the 20 g / 2 tbsp kosher salt. Submerge the cored cabbage head core-side down, weighting with a heatproof plate if needed to keep it submerged. After 3 minutes, the outer layer of leaves will be soft and pliable; pull the head out with tongs, peel off the 4 to 6 outermost leaves (which should come away easily), and return the cabbage to the boiling water. Repeat in 2 to 3 minute cycles, pulling 4 to 6 leaves each cycle, until you have 14 to 16 large pliable leaves. Lay the leaves out flat on a kitchen towel to drain.
  2. 2
    Once the leaves are out, lay each one flat and shave down the thick central rib with a knife held parallel to the leaf surface. The rib should be thinned to roughly the thickness of the leaf around it so the leaf rolls without cracking. Discard the rib trimmings.
  3. 3
    Par-cook the rice. Bring 1 liter of water to a boil in a small pot with a pinch of salt. Add the 100 g / ½ cup rinsed long-grain rice and boil 8 minutes (the rice is firm with a slight crunch in the center, deliberately under-cooked). Drain in a fine-mesh sieve and rinse briefly under cold water to stop the cook. Set aside to cool.
  4. 4
    Cook the onion for the filling. In a small skillet, melt the 30 g / 2 tbsp butter over medium heat. Add the 200 g / 1 large diced yellow onion and cook 6 to 8 minutes, stirring every 2 minutes, until soft and just golden. Spread on a plate to cool fully (5 to 10 minutes); hot onion starts cooking the pork as it sits.
  5. 5
    In a large bowl, combine the 700 g / 1½ lb ground pork, the cooled par-cooked rice (about 200 g cooked weight), the cooled sautéed onion, the 1 egg, the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt, the 4 g / 1¼ tsp black pepper, the 5 g / 2 tsp paprika, the 12 g / 3 tbsp dill, the 10 g / 2 tbsp parsley, and the 10 g / 3 cloves microplaned garlic. Mix thoroughly with your hands for 60 to 90 seconds; the mixture should be uniform and slightly tacky.
  6. 6
    Form the rolls. Preheat the oven to 165 °C / 325 °F. Take one cabbage leaf, rib-side down. Place 60 to 70 g (about 3 tbsp) of filling near the rib end. Fold the rib end over the filling, fold in the two sides, and roll up tightly toward the tip of the leaf, like a small burrito. Place seam-side down. Repeat with the remaining leaves; you should get 12 to 14 rolls.
  7. 7
    Make the sauce. In a bowl, whisk together the 800 g / 2 cans crushed whole peeled tomatoes, the 500 ml / 2 cups stock, the 30 g / 2 tbsp tomato paste, the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt, and the 6 g / 1½ tsp sugar. Taste and adjust; the sauce should read clearly tomatoey, lightly salted, with the sweetness barely registering as a balance to the acid.
  8. 8
    Assemble for baking. Tear or chop any leftover cabbage leaves (the inner small ones that did not separate cleanly) and spread across the bottom of a 6 to 7 quart Dutch oven; this layer prevents the rolls from sticking and burning. Arrange the cabbage rolls seam-side down in the pot in a tight single layer; if needed, stack a second layer on top. Tuck the 2 bay leaves between the rolls. Pour the tomato sauce over the rolls; the sauce should come about three-quarters of the way up the rolls. If the rolls are not nearly submerged, add a splash more stock.
  9. 9
    Cover with the lid (or with parchment paper and foil tightly sealed if your pot has no lid). Bake at 165 °C / 325 °F for 90 minutes. The cabbage should be silken-tender, the filling should be cooked through and firm, and the sauce should be deeply tomato-flavored. Pull from the oven and rest covered for 10 minutes before serving.
  10. 10
    Serve 2 rolls per plate, spooned over with several spoonfuls of the tomato-stock braising sauce. Dollop generously with the 200 g / ¾ cup sour cream alongside; the sour cream is the canonical Polish finish and tempers the tomato acid. Boiled potatoes or mashed potatoes are the traditional accompaniment.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cabbage→with savoy cabbage (the easier-rolling option)

    Replace the green cabbage with a large savoy cabbage. Savoy leaves are softer and more pliable raw than green cabbage leaves; the blanch is shorter (1 to 2 minutes per cycle) and the leaves roll without cracking even without much trimming of the central rib. Many Polish cooks prefer savoy for gołąbki specifically because of this ease.

  • Regional cabbage→gołąbki kiszone (with sauerkraut leaves, the winter version)

    Replace the fresh blanched cabbage leaves with whole sauerkraut leaves (sold at Polish or Ukrainian groceries as "soured cabbage leaves" or "kapusta kiszona w liściach"). The leaves are already pliable and acidic; skip the blanching entirely, just rinse to dial back the harshest acid. The dish becomes tangier and more deeply Polish-winter; this is the canonical preparation in some regional traditions.

  • Protein protein→pork-and-beef blend (more common in Polish-American kitchens)

    Replace the 700 g ground pork with 350 g ground pork and 350 g ground beef. The blend is the standard American-Polish version and reads slightly more substantial; the pure pork is more traditionally Polish.

  • Regional cuisine→Romanian sarmale (with smoked pork and dill)

    Use pickled-cabbage leaves (as in the kiszone variation above). Replace 200 g of the ground pork with 200 g finely chopped smoked bacon. Add 2 g / 1 tsp dried thyme to the filling. Reduce the tomato in the sauce by half and add 200 ml additional stock; the Romanian version is lighter on tomato than the Polish version. Garnish with extra dill at serving.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • green cabbage → savoy cabbage (preferred for ease) or napa cabbage Savoy is the easier-rolling option many cooks prefer. Napa cabbage works but the leaves are smaller and the rolls more delicate; you will need 16 to 18 smaller rolls instead of 12 to 14 larger ones.
  • long-grain white rice → jasmine rice, basmati rice, or short-grain rice All work. Short-grain rice produces a slightly stickier filling; long-grain produces the canonical Polish texture. Avoid arborio or sushi rice, both too starchy.
  • fresh dill → dried dill weed at one-third the quantity Fresh dill is dramatically better; dried works in a pinch. Quantity: 4 g / 1 tsp dried dill weed for the 12 g fresh.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → chicken stock or beef stock make it yourself Homemade stock adds depth to the braising sauce that boxed stock cannot match; the difference is visible after the 90-minute braise where every flavor has integrated.

Notes

Why par-cooked rice. Raw rice in the filling will not fully hydrate during the 90-minute braise; the rice stays crunchy in the center even when the meat is fully cooked. Fully cooked rice in the filling goes mushy during the braise and the texture reads wrong. Par-cooked (boiled 8 minutes, still firm in the center) is the structural compromise that lets the rice finish hydrating inside the rolled cabbage and arrive at the table as tender separated grains.

Why the cabbage layer at the bottom of the pot. The discarded trimmings and small inner leaves get spread across the pot bottom before the rolls go in. This layer does two structural jobs: it prevents the bottom rolls from sticking and burning against the pot, and it adds cabbage flavor and body to the sauce. Some Polish cooks add chopped raw bacon to this layer; it renders during the bake and enriches the sauce.

The seal-the-leaves move. The seam-side-down placement plus the tight packing in the pot means the rolls cannot unroll during the bake; the weight of the sauce holds them together and the cabbage cooks down around the filling, sealing everything in place. Cooks who do not pack tightly find their rolls unraveling and the filling escaping into the sauce.

Make-ahead and reheat. Gołąbki are the canonical Polish make-ahead dish. They can be assembled and refrigerated raw 24 hours ahead (cover with the sauce and refrigerate; bake from cold, adding 15 minutes to the bake time). They reheat beautifully; refrigerated cooked rolls hold 4 to 5 days and reheat covered in a 150 °C oven for 30 minutes, or in a low microwave with a splash of water. Freeze in single-portion containers for 3 months; thaw overnight and reheat covered.

Sour cream is structural at service. Polish kitchens do not consider gołąbki finished without sour cream. The cool, faintly tangy dairy tempers the tomato acid and adds richness; a dollop on each plate or a small bowl on the table for diners to self-serve is canonical. American versions sometimes skip it; the Polish version does not.
