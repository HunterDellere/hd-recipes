Matcha shortbread

Buttery shortbread tinted jade and built around ceremonial matcha, where the tea is the lead flavor, not a tint. A high ratio of matcha to flour, sifted in late and baked low so its chlorophyll and aroma survive, gives a cookie that is grassy-sweet and genuinely tastes of tea rather than just looking green.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The dough must chill at least 1 hour (firm enough to slice cleanly) before baking. Make the log, then chill while the oven heats and you clear up. It can also be made days ahead, or frozen, and sliced straight from cold.
  • Use the freshest, greenest matcha you have, and sift it. Matcha is the entire flavor here, so a dull, old, or bitter powder makes a dull, bitter cookie. Sifting is non-optional: matcha clumps, and an unsifted clump bakes into a bitter green fleck.
  • Bake low and pull pale. These bake at a low temperature and should not brown; browning means the matcha has scorched and gone bitter and khaki. Pull them when set but still green and barely colored at the edges.

The dough

  1. 170 g unsalted butter, softened, soft but still cool, ~18°C
  2. 90 g granulated sugar
  3. 2 g fine sea salt
  4. 4 g vanilla bean paste
  5. 14 g ceremonial-grade matcha, sifted
  6. 220 g all-purpose flour

To finish

  1. 25 g granulated sugar, for rolling optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Cream butter and sugar briefly. Beat the 170 g / 1½ sticks softened butter with the 90 g / scant ½ cup granulated sugar and the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt until just smooth and combined, about 1 minute, not light and fluffy. Shortbread wants a dense, tender crumb, not an aerated one; over-creaming pumps in air that makes the cookie puffy and crumbly rather than crisp and snappy. Beat in the 4 g / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste.
  2. 2
    Sift the matcha in with the flour. Sift the 14 g / 3 tbsp matcha together with the 220 g / 1¾ cups all-purpose flour into a bowl, sifting them together distributes the tea evenly and breaks every matcha clump. Add this to the butter in two additions, mixing on low just until the dough comes together into a uniform jade mass with no dry streaks. Stop the moment it clumps; overworking develops gluten and toughens the cookie.
  3. 3
    Roll into a log and chill. Tip the dough onto a sheet of parchment and shape it into a tight cylinder about 5 cm / 2 inches across, rolling it inside the parchment to smooth and compact it (this also rolls out air pockets that cause crumbling). If using, unwrap and roll the log in the 25 g / 2 tbsp granulated sugar to coat the edge. Wrap and chill at least 1 hour until firm enough to slice cleanly.
  4. 4
    Slice and bake low. Heat the oven to 160°C / 325°F and line two sheets with parchment. Slice the cold log into rounds about 1 cm / ⅜ inch thick and lay them 3 cm apart. Bake 14 to 16 minutes, rotating once, until the cookies are set and dry to the touch but still vividly green, with at most the faintest pale color at the very edges. They should NOT brown.
  5. 5
    Cool to crisp. Let the cookies firm up on the sheet for 5 minutes (they are fragile straight from the oven), then move to a rack to cool completely. They crisp as they cool. Fully cooled, they keep their snap and color best in an airtight tin away from light for about a week.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion a sandwich cookie→matcha shortbread with white chocolate

    Sandwich pairs of cooled cookies with a thin layer of melted and re-set white chocolate, or half-dip each cookie in white chocolate. The dairy sweetness of white chocolate is the classic foil to matcha bitterness and makes the cookies read more like a dessert than a tea biscuit.

  • Texture a more tender, melting crumb→powdered-sugar shortbread

    Replace the granulated sugar in the dough with the same weight of powdered (icing) sugar. The finer sugar dissolves completely and gives a more delicate, sandy, melt-in-the-mouth crumb rather than a crisp snap, the Scottish-shortbread texture.

  • Regional roasted, mellower tea flavor→hojicha shortbread

    Swap the matcha for 14 g of finely ground hojicha (roasted green tea) powder. The cookie loses the jade color (it turns warm brown) but gains a toasty, caramel-like roasted-tea flavor that is less grassy and less bitter, easier for matcha skeptics.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • ceremonial-grade matcha → culinary-grade matcha (increase by ~20%) Culinary matcha is more bitter and duller green; in a sweet, buttery bake it is acceptable and far cheaper than wasting ceremonial grade on a cookie. Use a bit more to push the flavor through the butter and sugar. This is the rare case where culinary grade is the sensible choice.
  • vanilla bean paste → vanilla extract Extract works; the paste is preferred only for the visible seeds and slightly higher aromatic load. In a green cookie the seed flecks are barely visible anyway, so extract is nearly equivalent here.
  • all-purpose flour → replace 30 g of the flour with cornstarch Subbing a little cornstarch for flour lowers the protein and gives a more tender, shortbready snap. A common shortbread trick; optional.

Notes

Why the matcha ratio is high. Most green-tinted bakes use a token amount of matcha for color and taste mostly of butter and sugar with a vague vegetal background. This recipe uses roughly three tablespoons of matcha to under two cups of flour specifically so the tea is the dominant flavor: assertively grassy, faintly bitter, savory-sweet, a cookie that tastes of matcha rather than a cookie that is merely green. That much matcha only works if the powder is good and fresh, which is why grade matters more here than the cost might suggest, and why this is the rare bake where buying decent matcha pays off in the eating.

Why low and pale. Matcha is heat-sensitive in two ways that govern the bake. Its vivid green chlorophyll degrades to a dull khaki under high heat, and its delicate aroma compounds and sweetness scorch into bitterness if the cookie browns. Baking at a low 160°C and pulling the cookies the instant they are set, before any real browning, protects both the color and the flavor. A matcha cookie baked at standard 180°C until golden comes out olive-brown and bitter; the same dough baked low and pale stays jade and sweet-grassy.

Why not to over-cream or overwork. Shortbread's appeal is a dense, tender, crisp crumb, and two steps protect it: creaming the butter only until smooth rather than fluffy (so you do not beat in air that puffs and crumbles the cookie) and mixing in the flour only until it just comes together (so you do not develop the gluten that would toughen it). The chill is the third structural step, it firms the butter so the cookies hold their shape and slice cleanly, and it relaxes the dough so it does not spread. Cold dough, low oven, pale finish: those three give a crisp, green, genuinely tea-flavored shortbread.
