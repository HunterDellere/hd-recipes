Puff pastry (homemade rough puff)

Flour and cold butter laminated through 4 to 6 fold-and-rest cycles into a flaky, layered dough. Quicker than classic French puff pastry (which uses a butter block) but produces 95% of the rise and flake. A weekend-baking commitment. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 4 items ▾

Dairy, eggs & refrigerated 1

  1. 250 g Butter (unsalted)

Pantry 2

  1. 250 g All-purpose flour
  2. 5 g Fine sea salt

Other 1

  1. 120 ml ice-cold water

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: combine the 250 g flour, 5 g salt, and 250 g cold butter cubes in a bowl. Toss to coat the butter with flour; do not rub in (the butter must stay in distinct cubes). Add the 120 ml ice water; stir gently with a spatula until a shaggy dough forms with visible butter chunks. Do not knead or homogenize.
  2. 2
    Stub: turn the dough onto a floured counter. Pat into a rough rectangle (15 cm × 25 cm). Fold in thirds (envelope fold). Wrap in plastic; refrigerate 30 minutes.
  3. 3
    Stub: roll the chilled dough on a floured surface to a rectangle about 20 cm × 35 cm. Fold in thirds again. Refrigerate 30 minutes. Repeat this fold-and-rest cycle 3 more times (4 folds total for rough puff; 6 folds for restaurant-grade).
  4. 4
    Stub: after the final rest, roll to desired thickness (3 mm for most applications) and cut to size. Use immediately or wrap and freeze flat for up to 2 months.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the rough-puff vs classic-puff distinction (rough puff laminates butter cubes throughout the dough; classic puff uses a butter block "beurrage" wrapped in a flour-water "détrempe" — classic produces more layers but takes longer), the fold-counting math (4 folds = 81 layers, 5 folds = 243 layers, 6 folds = 729 layers), the European-butter requirement (lower water content, higher fat — laminates better than 80% American butter), the temperature management (the dough must stay cold throughout — if it warms, the butter melts into the flour and the lamination collapses), the resting science (gluten relaxation is what allows the dough to roll without springing back), the freezer behavior (lamination survives freezing well; freeze flat in 250 g sheets for ready use), and the practical reality that good frozen all-butter puff pastry (Pepperidge Farm Puff Pastry Sheets, Dufour) is genuinely excellent and the homemade project is more about the experience and the ability to control thickness than the kitchen need. The stub above outlines a working rough puff; the deeper drafting pass should add the chemistry, the troubleshooting, and the cultural context.
