Challah

The Ashkenazi braided egg bread, built oil-based and dairy-free (pareve) rather than on butter, sweetened with honey, and hydrated by whole eggs plus yolks for color and a tender, feathery pull. A neutral oil enrichment at roughly 12% of the flour weight keeps the dough firm enough to braid cleanly into a six-strand loaf, where butter would slacken it. Two egg-wash coats lay down the lacquer-deep mahogany sheen that defines the loaf, baked to a soft, custard-tinted crumb that tears in long strands and toasts into the best French toast there is.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a same-day bake of about 4 hours, but most of it is passive. The active work is the mix (15 minutes), the braid (15 minutes), and the egg wash and bake. Between those sit a first rise of about 90 minutes and a final proof of 45 to 60 minutes. Read the whole sequence before starting so the braid does not catch you mid-rise.
  • A six-strand braid is the classic but takes practice. If this is your first challah, divide the dough into 3 strands and braid it like hair; the loaf is just as good and the technique is forgiving. The six-strand instructions are in step 6 with a simpler 3-strand fallback noted there.
  • The dough is enriched and slightly sticky but braidable. Resist adding flour past the amount called for; a dry challah dough braids stiffly and bakes dense. Keep the bench lightly floured and work with a bench scraper rather than dumping on more flour.
  • Reserve one egg yolk from the dough eggs for the wash, or use a separate egg. The double egg-wash (one coat before the final proof, one just before baking) is what builds the signature deep-glossy mahogany sheen; a single coat reads matte by comparison.

Phase 1, the dough

  1. 480 g bread flour
  2. 180 g warm water, 38°C
  3. 100 g whole eggs, room temperature, lightly beaten — (2 large eggs; eggs supply secondary hydration, the protein for a tender set crumb, and the yolk that tints the interior gold)
  4. 36 g egg yolks, room temperature — (2 large yolks; the extra yolks push the crumb richer and more custard-yellow than whole eggs alone, the lecithin tenderizes without the slackening of more whites)
  5. 55 g neutral oil (refined avocado, grapeseed, or a clean vegetable oil)

Phase 2, egg wash and finish

  1. 18 g egg yolk, for the wash, beaten with the water and salt below until fully liquid and strainable — (1 large yolk; a yolk-forward wash browns deeper and glossier than a whole-egg wash)
  2. 5 g water, for the wash
  3. 1 g fine sea salt, for the wash — (a pinch; salt breaks down the egg proteins so the wash lies flat and bakes to an even mahogany, not a patchy one)
  4. 10 g sesame or poppy seeds, optional topping, scattered over the wet egg wash just before baking optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Mix the dough. In the bowl of a stand mixer fitted with the dough hook, combine the 480 g / 4 cups bread flour, the 7 g / 2¼ tsp instant yeast, and the 25 g / 2 tbsp granulated sugar; whisk briefly to distribute. Keep the 9 g / 1½ tsp fine sea salt to one side for the moment, concentrated salt in direct contact with yeast at this stage can slow the rise. Add the 180 g / ¾ cup warm water (38°C), the 100 g / 2 whole eggs, the 36 g / 2 egg yolks, the 55 g / ¼ cup neutral oil, and the 60 g / 3 tbsp honey. Mix on low (speed 2 on a KitchenAid) for 2 minutes to bring it together into a shaggy, sticky mass. Enriched dough
  2. 2
    Develop the gluten. Add the 9 g / 1½ tsp fine sea salt now that the flour buffers it from the yeast. Raise to medium (speed 4) and knead for 8 to 10 minutes. The dough climbs from sticky and slack to a smooth, elastic, cohesive ball that wraps the hook and clears the sides of the bowl, leaving them mostly clean. Because oil coats gluten far less aggressively than butter, this dough develops to a strong windowpane without the long fight a brioche puts up: stretch a walnut-sized piece thin between your fingers; it should pull to a translucent membrane that holds light without tearing. If it tears, knead 2 to 3 minutes more. The dough should feel tacky but not wet, and firm enough that you can imagine braiding it. Enriched dough
  3. 3
    First rise. Scrape the dough into a lightly oiled bowl, turn it once to coat, cover with a damp towel or a fitted lid, and let it rise at room temperature (21 to 23°C) until roughly doubled, about 90 minutes. Challah rises faster and higher than brioche because the lower fat load weighs it down far less. It is ready when a fingertip pressed into the dough leaves a dent that fills back slowly, not one that springs straight back (underproofed) or stays fully dented (overproofed).
  4. 4
    Divide for the braid. Turn the dough onto a lightly floured bench and gently press out the large gas bubbles. For a six-strand braid, divide the dough into 6 equal pieces of about 140 g each, weigh them for an even loaf. For the simpler three-strand braid (recommended for a first attempt), divide into 3 pieces of about 280 g each. Cover the pieces with a towel and let them rest 10 minutes; this relaxes the gluten so the strands roll out without snapping back.
  5. 5
    Roll the strands. Working one piece at a time, flatten it, roll it up tightly into a log to build surface tension, then roll the log under your palms into an even rope, thicker in the middle and tapered slightly at the ends, about 35 to 40 cm long for a six-strand loaf (a touch shorter for three strands). If a rope resists stretching and shrinks back, set it aside, work the others, and come back to it; the rest gives the gluten time to relax. Keep the bench only lightly floured; too much flour makes the strands slip apart instead of fusing at the seams.
  6. 6
    Braid. For six strands: pinch all six rope-ends together tightly at the top and fan the strands out. Number them 1 to 6 left to right. The repeating move is: take the far-right strand (6) over to the far left, then the second-from-left strand over to the far right, and repeat (always outer-right-over-to-the-far-left, then second-from-left-over-to-the-far-right), re-numbering positions as you go. Keep the braid snug but not stretched. When you reach the ends, pinch them together tightly and tuck both sealed ends under the loaf for a clean finish. For three strands: pinch the tops together and braid exactly like hair, left-over-middle, right-over-middle, repeating to the end, then pinch and tuck. Transfer the braided loaf to a parchment-lined sheet pan.
  7. 7
    First egg wash and final proof. Beat the 18 g / 1 yolk with the 5 g / 1 tsp water and the 1 g pinch of fine sea salt until fully liquid, then strain it. Brush a thin, even coat over the entire braid, getting into the crevices between the strands but not pooling at the base where it would scorch. Reserve the rest of the wash, covered, for the second coat. Cover the loaf loosely with a damp towel and proof at room temperature until visibly puffy and nearly doubled, 45 to 60 minutes. The dough is ready when a gentle fingertip press in a thick part springs back slowly and leaves a faint dent. Meanwhile, heat the oven to 175°C / 350°F with a rack in the lower-middle.
  8. 8
    Second wash and bake. Brush the proofed loaf with a second thin coat of the strained egg wash; the two coats are what build the deep, lacquered mahogany sheen. If using them, scatter the 10 g / 1 tbsp sesame or poppy seeds over the wet wash now. Bake 30 to 38 minutes, rotating the pan halfway, until the crust is a deep glossy mahogany and the internal temperature at the center reads 88 to 90°C / 190 to 195°F on an instant-read thermometer. Challah's honey and egg make it brown fast; if the top is dark by 22 minutes, tent loosely with parchment or foil and finish to temperature. Pull by temperature, not color alone, an underbaked center stays gummy in the thick middle of the braid.
  9. 9
    Cool before slicing. Slide the loaf, still on its parchment, onto a wire rack within a few minutes of leaving the oven so steam does not condense and soften the crust. Cool at least 45 minutes before slicing or tearing. The crumb is still setting as it cools; cutting hot compresses it into a gummy streak. Finished challah should tear in long, soft, slightly stringy strands with a fine even grain, a tender pull, and a thin, sheeny, faintly sweet crust.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion shape and occasion→round spiral challah for the High Holidays

    For Rosh Hashanah, shape a single long rope (or a loose three-strand braid) and coil it into a spiral on the sheet pan, tucking the outer end under, to symbolize the cyclical year. Add 60 g / ½ cup raisins or 40 g / ¼ cup soaked sultanas, kneaded in at the end of the gluten phase, for the traditional sweet round loaf. The spiral proofs and bakes the same; check it is done at temperature since the dense coil center sets last.

  • Texture flavor direction→water challah, leaner and more bread-forward

    Drop the eggs to a single whole egg (50 g) and the yolks entirely, increase the water to 230 g / 1 cup, and cut the honey to 40 g / 2 tbsp. This is the older, leaner Sephardic-leaning style, a paler crumb that reads more like a true bread and less like enriched pastry, with a chewier bite. It stales faster, so eat it the day it bakes or turn it to toast.

  • Occasion richer, more festive crumb→extra-rich honey challah

    Push the oil to 80 g / ⅓ cup and add a third egg yolk (18 g) to the dough. The crumb goes softer, more golden, and more cake-like, closer to the brioche end of the enriched-dough spectrum while staying dairy-free and pareve. The dough gets slightly slacker, so chill it 20 minutes before braiding if it feels too soft to handle cleanly.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • bread flour → all-purpose flour All-purpose (10 to 11% protein) works and yields a slightly softer, more tender crumb, but the braid holds its definition less crisply and the loaf rises a touch lower. If using it, knead 2 to 3 minutes longer in the gluten phase. Do not use cake or pastry flour; there is not enough protein to support the braid.
  • honey → maple syrup, agave, or additional granulated sugar Maple syrup substitutes 1:1 and adds its own caramel note. Agave is the most neutral 1:1 swap. To use only granulated sugar, replace the honey with 50 g / ¼ cup sugar and add 10 g / 2 tsp extra water to make up the lost moisture; the crust will brown a little less aggressively without the honey fructose.
  • instant yeast → active dry yeast Active dry works but should be bloomed first: stir the yeast and a pinch of the sugar into the 180 g / ¾ cup warm water (38°C) and wait 8 to 10 minutes for it to foam before mixing. Use the same 7 g / 2¼ tsp weight. If the mixture does not foam, the yeast is dead; start over with fresh yeast.
  • neutral oil → light olive oil or melted refined coconut oil A light (not extra-virgin) olive oil adds a faint fruity background that suits challah well. Melted refined coconut oil works and stays pareve; use it warm and liquid so it incorporates evenly, and accept a slightly firmer crumb once cool. Avoid strong extra-virgin olive oil, its pepper-and-grass notes dominate the delicate honey-egg flavor.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → honey make it yourselfstub An aromatic infused honey, steeped with orange peel, vanilla, or a sprig of thyme, carries through the crumb and the crust, turning a plain challah into a subtly perfumed loaf without changing the method. The infusion is a passive overnight steep, and the honey is otherwise used exactly as the recipe calls for it.

Notes

Why oil, not butter, and why it matters. The defining structural choice in challah is that the enrichment is oil and eggs, not butter. The practical driver is kosher law: a pareve (neither-meat-nor-dairy) bread can be served at a meat meal, which a butter-rich brioche cannot. But the choice also changes the dough and the eating profile in ways worth understanding. Liquid oil disperses through the dough and coats gluten strands far less aggressively than solid butter, which crystallizes and waterproofs the flour. So challah never needs the strict develop-gluten-first, add-fat-last discipline that brioche demands, and it never needs an overnight cold proof to firm the fat for shaping. The dough comes together in one mix, stays firm and braidable at room temperature, and rises faster and higher because the lower fat load weighs it down less. The trade-off is a leaner, more bread-forward crumb: challah tears in soft strands and toasts crisp, where brioche melts richer and denser. Neither is better; they are different breads built for different tables.

The honey is a functional choice, not just a flavor one. Honey is roughly 40% fructose, and fructose browns through the Maillard reaction at a lower temperature than the sucrose in table sugar. That is why a honey challah develops its deep mahogany crust faster and more reliably than a sugar-only loaf, and why the crust reads rounder and more floral. It also holds moisture (honey is hygroscopic), so a honey-sweetened crumb stays soft a day longer than a sugar-only one. The small dose of granulated sugar alongside it is there to set the overall sweetness and feed the yeast cleanly without pushing the loaf into dessert territory; challah should read faintly sweet, the kind of sweetness that works equally under butter and jam or under a roast-chicken gravy.

The eggs and yolks do double duty. The whole eggs are part of the hydration and bring protein that sets into a tender, fine crumb. The two extra yolks are pure enrichment: yolk is about a third fat and carries lecithin, a natural emulsifier that tenderizes the crumb and tints it a deep custard yellow without adding the extra whites that would toughen and dry the loaf. This is the lever that separates a pale, ordinary egg bread from a golden, rich challah. If you want an even more golden crumb, the extra-rich modification adds a third yolk; if you want a leaner, paler, chewier loaf, the water-challah modification drops the yolks entirely.

The double egg-wash is the signature. One coat of egg wash before the final proof, a second just before baking, is what builds challah''s lacquer-deep, glossy mahogany sheen. The first coat seals the surface and lets the rising braid hold its definition; the second coat, brushed onto the proofed loaf, is the layer that bakes to the high gloss. Use a yolk-forward wash thinned with a little water and broken down with a pinch of salt: the yolk browns deepest, the water lets it brush into an even film, and the salt denatures the egg protein so the wash lies flat instead of streaking. A single whole-egg coat reads matte and patchy by comparison.

Braiding mechanics and the firmness that allows them. A six-strand braid is the classic Sabbath loaf, but the braid only works because the oil-enriched dough is firm enough to hold a defined rope, where a slacker butter dough would smear. The keys are even strands (weigh them), a tightly rolled log inside each rope to build surface tension, and a snug-but-not-stretched braid so the strands fuse at the seams as they proof without tearing as they expand. The single most common braiding mistake is over-flouring the bench, which keeps the strands from fusing and leaves visible gaps after baking; keep the surface barely dusted and let the dough''s own slight tack hold the braid together. If the six-strand pattern is intimidating, the three-strand braid is genuinely just as good and far more forgiving; braid it like hair and no one at the table will mind.

Storage and the best use of day-old challah. Challah is best the day it bakes; the honey keeps it soft a day longer than a lean loaf, but it still stales. Wrap the cooled loaf in a clean towel or beeswax wrap at room temperature for up to 2 days, or slice and freeze for up to 2 months. Day-two challah is the definitive French toast and bread-pudding base: the soft, custard-rich, slightly stale crumb soaks the egg custard without dissolving, and the staling actually improves its structure for soaking. Thick-sliced, dipped, and griddled in butter (for a non-pareve breakfast) it is arguably the best use of the loaf after the first warm slice torn straight off the cooling rack.

  • Adapted from the Ashkenazi Jewish challah tradition, the oil-enriched, honey-sweetened braided egg bread of the Sabbath and holiday table. — Challah is the lean-enriched cousin of brioche. Both are yeast-raised egg breads, but the enrichment is fundamentally different: challah carries its richness through eggs and a modest dose of neutral oil rather than a heavy butter load, which keeps it dairy-free (pareve, so it can be served at a meat meal under kosher law) and keeps the dough firm enough to braid.
