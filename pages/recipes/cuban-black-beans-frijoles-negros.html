Cuban black beans (frijoles negros)

The structural Cuban side: dried black beans cooked tender from a cold-water soak, then finished in a sofrito of olive oil, onion, green bell pepper, garlic, cumin, oregano, bay, with a splash of dry white wine and a teaspoon of sugar to round the bitter edge of the bean skin. The cooked bean liquid is reused and reduced into the finished pot for body, color, and umami.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Dry black beans need an 8 to 12 hour cold soak (or a quick-soak: boiled 1 minute, rested off heat 1 hour). Plan the night before. Canned beans work but require the substitution branch since the bean broth is structural.
  • Do not salt the beans until they are fully tender. Salt added early in the cook hardens the bean skins and prevents full hydration; the beans will still taste underdone after 90 minutes if salted at the start.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the bean cook

  1. 400 g dried black beans, soaked 8 to 12 hours in cold water, drained
  2. 2000 ml cold water
  3. 2 each bay leaves
  4. 60 g green bell pepper, whole (for the bean cook), one half of a green bell pepper, whole (will be discarded) — (½ pepper; infuses pepper aromatics into the cooking liquid)

Phase 2, the sofrito

  1. 45 ml extra virgin olive oil
  2. 180 g yellow onion, fine dice — (1 large onion)
  3. 150 g green bell pepper, fine dice — (1 medium pepper; green is canonical Cuban, red is Puerto Rican)
  4. 25 g garlic, minced — (6 cloves)
  5. 5 g ground cumin
  6. 3 g dried oregano (Mexican or Mediterranean)

Phase 3, the simmer and finish

  1. 60 ml dry white wine (Spanish style)
  2. 10 g kosher salt
  3. 5 g granulated sugar
  4. 15 ml red wine vinegar (or sherry vinegar)
  5. 2 g black pepper, cracked

Execution

  1. 1
    Soak the 400 g / 2 cups dried black beans in 2000 ml cold water for 8 to 12 hours. Drain.12 h
  2. 2
    In a heavy-bottomed pot combine the drained beans with 2000 ml / 8 cups fresh cold water, the 2 bay leaves, and the 60 g whole green bell pepper half. Bring to a hard boil, then reduce to a steady simmer and cook 75 to 90 minutes, partially covered, until the beans are fully tender (a bean crushed between two fingers should mash with no resistance). Do not salt yet. Top up with hot water if the level drops below the beans.
  3. 3
    When the beans are tender, fish out and discard the bay leaves and the whole pepper half. Reserve 250 ml / 1 cup of the bean cooking liquid in a measuring cup. Drain the beans into a colander set over a second bowl to catch the remaining liquid (also reserve).
  4. 4
    Wipe out the pot. Heat the 45 ml / 3 tbsp olive oil over medium heat. Add the 180 g diced onion and 150 g diced green bell pepper; sweat 6 to 8 minutes until the onion is translucent and the pepper has softened with light golden edges. Do not brown aggressively; the sofrito should be soft and aromatic, not Maillard-toasted.
  5. 5
    Add the 25 g / 6 cloves minced garlic; cook 60 seconds until fragrant. Add the 5 g / 2 tsp cumin and 3 g / 1 tsp oregano; stir 30 seconds to bloom the spices in the hot oil. The oil should turn deep brick-red and the kitchen should smell aggressively of cumin. Blooming aromatics
  6. 6
    Add the 60 ml / ¼ cup dry white wine; scrape the sofrito loose from the pot bottom. Cook 90 seconds until the wine has reduced and the alcohol has burned off (sniff; raw alcohol smell should be gone). Deglazing
  7. 7
    Return the drained beans to the pot, along with the 250 ml reserved bean cooking liquid. Add the 10 g / 2½ tsp salt, 5 g / 1 tsp sugar, and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper. Stir to combine. Bring to a steady simmer, partially covered, and cook 20 to 25 minutes for the flavors to meld and the liquid to thicken. The beans should look slightly soupy with a glossy black gravy, not dry. Add more reserved bean broth if the pot looks dry.
  8. 8
    Off heat, stir in the 15 ml / 1 tbsp red wine vinegar. The acid brightens the dish and is the structural finish; without it the beans taste flat and one-note. Taste; adjust salt and vinegar if needed. Rest covered 5 minutes before serving.
  9. 9
    Serve over white rice, with diced raw onion, lime wedges, and a splash of additional olive oil at the table. The beans are also the structural base for "moros y cristianos" (cooked with the rice together rather than served alongside).

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion serving format→as moros y cristianos (beans and rice cooked together)

    After the sofrito step, add 300 g / 1½ cups rinsed long-grain white rice and 600 ml total liquid (mix of bean broth and water) along with the cooked beans and seasonings. Bring to a hard boil, reduce to lowest heat, cover tightly, cook 18 minutes. Rest covered 10 minutes off heat. The result is the integrated one-pot version of the dish.

  • Protein protein addition→with rendered chorizo

    Render 100 g sliced Spanish chorizo (cured, not Mexican raw) in the pot before starting the sofrito. Pour off all but 30 ml of the rendered fat (skip the olive oil), proceed with the sofrito in the chorizo fat. The dish reads dramatically more savory and is the canonical Sunday version in many Cuban-American households.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dried black beans → 2 cans (~880 g) drained black beans + 250 ml chicken or vegetable stock Skip step 1 and 2. Start at step 4 with the sofrito. Use the canned beans plus stock in place of beans plus reserved bean broth. The flavor is acceptably close but slightly less complex than dried beans cooked from a soak.
  • dry white wine (Spanish style) → dry sherry, or chicken stock Dry sherry leans the dish more Spanish-formal; chicken stock removes the acid component but stays in the dish family. The white wine is the most balanced choice.
  • red wine vinegar → sherry vinegar, or fresh lime juice Sherry vinegar is the closest Cuban canonical substitute. Lime brightens the dish toward a more Caribbean profile.

Notes

Why no salt during the bean cook. Salt in the simmer liquid bonds with the magnesium and calcium ions in the bean skin pectin and toughens the skins. Beans cooked in salted water can take twice as long to soften, and often refuse to ever fully soften. Add salt only once the beans are already tender; the salt then penetrates the cooked beans without affecting the skin.

The sugar-and-vinegar finish. Black bean skins carry tannins and a slightly bitter compound that reads as a faint sourness in the back of the palate. A teaspoon of sugar lifts the bitter compounds out of perception by adding a bass-note sweetness; a tablespoon of vinegar at the end brightens the dish and balances the sugar. The pair are the Cuban signature finish; many home cooks skip them and produce flat-tasting beans without understanding why.

Why reuse the bean broth. The cooking liquid carries dissolved bean proteins, starch, and bean-pigment anthocyanins that give the finished dish its glossy black color, its slightly thickened body, and an umami bass note. Pouring off the broth and refilling with water produces a paler, thinner, blander dish.

The Cuban green pepper rule. Cuban sofrito uses green bell pepper (slightly bitter, vegetal); Puerto Rican sofrito uses red bell pepper and ají dulce (sweet, fruity). Using red pepper in Cuban beans makes the dish read Puerto Rican; technically fine, structurally wrong.

Storage. Improves dramatically overnight; the flavors integrate and the sofrito mellows into the beans. Keeps 5 days refrigerated. Reheat over low heat with a splash of water or broth. Freezes well; portion into 500 g containers.
