Recipe

Salt-and-pepper squid (Cantonese jiao yan you yu, 椒盐鱿鱼)

Cross-hatch-scored squid tubes dredged in a cornstarch-and-rice-flour mix, deep-fried 90 seconds at 175°C, tossed with toasted-and-ground white-pepper salt and crisped chili-garlic-scallion. The 90-second high-heat cooking window for squid, executed at deep-fry intensity. Pinecone curls from the scoring, salt-pepper crust on every cut edge.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Start the jasmine or steamed white rice first so it finishes within minutes of the squid; this dish is canonically eaten over rice. A 15-minute rinse-and-simmer for jasmine runs in parallel with the squid prep and toasted-pepper-salt blend.
  • Pat the scored squid bone-dry with paper towels before dredging. Wet squid sheds the cornstarch-rice-flour shell into the oil and fries naked; the entire crisp texture depends on a dry surface.
  • Heat the oil to 175 °C and confirm with a probe thermometer before the first piece goes in. Below 165 °C the squid steams in soggy batter; above 185 °C the coating browns before the 90-second cook is complete and the squid goes rubbery.
  • Do not marinate the salted squid longer than 5 minutes. The salt and Shaoxing pull moisture and start tenderizing immediately; past 5 minutes the texture turns mushy and the dredge no longer adheres.

Phase 5, aromatic crisp

  1. 15 ml neutral oil (for aromatic stir-fry)

  4. 30 g scallion, sliced (whites and greens) — (2-3 scallions)

Execution

  1. 1
    Score and prep the squid. Lay each cleaned tube flat with the inside facing up. Hold a sharp knife at a 30 to 45 degree angle and make shallow parallel cuts across the surface at 5 mm intervals (cutting only halfway through). Rotate 90 degrees and make a second set of perpendicular cuts at 5 mm intervals. Cut the scored sheet into 3 cm × 3 cm squares. Leave the tentacles whole. Pat dry with paper towels, the squid must be dry before dredging or the coating slides off. Sprinkle with 4 g / 1 tsp salt, 5 ml / 1 tsp Shaoxing, and 1 g / ½ tsp white pepper. Toss gently. Set aside 5 minutes (no longer; longer marinade toughens squid). Scoring squid (cross-hatch)
  2. 2
    Make the salt-pepper finish. Heat a small dry skillet over medium heat. Add 4 g / 2 tsp whole white peppercorns and 1 g / ½ tsp Sichuan peppercorns. Toast 60 to 90 seconds, swirling continuously, until fragrant. Transfer to a mortar; grind to a coarse powder. Combine in a small bowl with 4 g / 1 tsp fine sea salt and 0.5 g / ¼ tsp five-spice (if using). Set aside.
  3. 3
    Heat the frying oil. Pour the 1500 ml / 6 cups neutral oil into a wok or heavy pot. Heat to 175°C / 350°F (use a thermometer; the oil temperature is the variable that determines whether the squid hits the 90-second window cleanly). The oil should shimmer and a wooden chopstick dipped in should produce a steady stream of small bubbles.
  4. 4
    Dredge and fry. In a wide bowl, whisk together 60 g cornstarch, 30 g rice flour, 2 g baking powder, and 2 g fine sea salt. Add the squid pieces (in small batches of about 8 to 10 pieces) and toss to coat thoroughly; press the dredge into the cut surfaces of the scoring. Lift each piece and shake off excess. Lower into the 175°C oil and fry 60 to 90 seconds, until pale gold and the crust is set. Do not crowd the pot; fry in 3 to 4 batches, returning the oil to 175°C between batches. Remove with a spider strainer to a wire rack (not paper towels, paper steams the bottom and softens the crust). The squid will continue to cook briefly on residual heat; the timing target is 90 seconds total contact, not 90 seconds of color development.
  5. 5
    Sizzle the aromatic crisp. While the last batch fries, heat a separate wok or skillet over medium-high heat. Add 15 ml / 1 tbsp neutral oil, the 8 g sliced chili, 16 g minced garlic, and 30 g sliced scallion. Stir-fry 60 seconds; the garlic should turn pale gold and crisp at the edges, the scallion should wilt and the chili should brighten. Pull off heat.
  6. 6
    Toss and serve. Transfer all the fried squid to the aromatic-crisp wok. Sprinkle with 6 to 8 g (about 2 tsp) of the salt-pepper mixture. Toss vigorously for 15 to 20 seconds, the goal is even seasoning distribution, not further cooking. Taste; the squid should read salty-aromatic-spicy with the white pepper hitting first. Adjust salt-pepper if needed. Transfer to a serving plate. Serve immediately while the crust is at peak crispness; the dish degrades visibly within 5 minutes of plating as steam from the squid softens the coating. Lemon or lime wedges on the side are optional but traditional.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→salt-and-pepper prawns (jiao yan xia)

    Substitute 500 g large head-on shell-on prawns (16/20 count) for the squid. Skip the scoring (prawns do not need it). Skip the dredge marinade, toss the prawns dry in salt and Shaoxing only. Dredge in the cornstarch-rice-flour mix and fry 90 to 120 seconds at 175°C until the shells turn brilliant orange and crisp. The shells are eaten, they crisp under the deep fry into a fully edible salty-aromatic crust. Same salt-pepper finish.

  • Protein protein→salt-and-pepper pork ribs (jiao yan pai gu)

    Substitute 600 g pork spare-rib pieces (cut into 4 cm pieces by your butcher). Marinate the pork in 10 ml Shaoxing + 5 ml light soy + 1 tsp white pepper + 1 egg white for 30 minutes. Dredge in the cornstarch-rice-flour mix. Deep-fry 6 to 8 minutes at 175°C until cooked through and crispy (use a thermometer to confirm internal 70°C / 160°F). Same aromatic crisp and salt-pepper finish.

  • Occasion cooking method→wok-fried (no deep fry)

    Skip the deep-fry oil bath; instead, dredge the squid lightly and pan-fry in 60 ml / ¼ cup oil in a wok over very high heat for 90 seconds total (45 seconds per side). The crust is less dramatic and the squid less uniformly crisp, but the dish works for a single-portion home preparation when the deep-fry setup is overkill. The texture goes from "battered" to "lightly crisped"; some cooks prefer this version.

  • Heat spice level→aggressively spicy (Hunan-style adaptation)

    Add 8 g (2 tsp) Korean gochugaru or coarse-ground Sichuan chili powder to the salt-pepper mixture. Increase fresh chili to 15 g (2 small Thai bird chilies). The dish becomes recognizably spicy rather than aromatic-spicy; reads as a Hunan or Sichuan adaptation rather than the Cantonese standard.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole white peppercorns → pre-ground white pepper Acceptable but the toasted-fresh aroma is significantly diminished. Use 1 to 1.5 tsp pre-ground in place of 4 g toasted-and-ground whole. The dish loses the structural toasted-pepper aromatic; defensible as a shortcut when whole peppercorns are unavailable.
  • rice flour → all extra cornstarch (90 g total) Acceptable but the crust will be less crisp and slightly more chewy. The rice-flour-and-cornstarch blend is the textbook Cantonese fry coating; cornstarch alone produces a thinner, more sticky shell.
  • fresh squid → frozen squid, fully thawed and patted dry Frozen squid is often fresher than "fresh" supermarket squid that has sat on ice for several days. 1:1 substitute by thawed weight. Defrost overnight in the fridge and pat very dry before scoring.
  • baking powder in dredge → omit Acceptable; the crust will be very slightly denser. Most Cantonese restaurant versions include baking powder for the lift; home versions often skip it.

Notes

The 90-second window. Squid cookery operates on a strict rule (see ingredients/squid): tender at 90 seconds of high heat or 30 to 45 minutes of low simmer, rubber in between. Salt-and-pepper squid is the textbook 90-second high-heat application, deep-fried at 175°C, the squid pieces hit the oil cold, the surface proteins denature into the crisp crust within the first 30 seconds, the heat penetrates the 1 cm wall of the tube within another 30 to 60 seconds, and the squid is cooked. Any longer and the squid contracts hard, expels water, and the dredge soaks back wet. The cook's discipline is to count the seconds and not to worry about whether the crust has "browned enough"; the crust browns in seconds at 175°C, and pulling early is always safer than pulling late.

Why the cross-hatch scoring matters here. The scoring (see techniques/scoring-squid) does three things in this dish: it makes the tube curl into pinecone shapes when it hits the oil (the visual signature of restaurant-style salt-and-pepper squid), it dramatically increases surface area for the dredge to adhere to (so each pinecone bump has its own crisp salt-pepper coat), and it shortens the heat-penetration time from outside to inside (which lets the 90-second window cleanly cook through the meat without overshooting). Skip the scoring and the squid pieces lay flat in the oil, the dredge slides off the smooth surface, and the cooking time stretches into the rubber zone.

Why Cantonese cooks use cornstarch + rice flour. The cornstarch-rice-flour blend is the textbook Cantonese fry coating and appears across the cuisine (salt-pepper anything, Cantonese fried chicken, sweet-and-sour pork). Cornstarch alone produces a thin, slightly sticky shell that softens within 2 minutes of plating; rice flour alone produces a more brittle, almost gritty crust. The 2:1 cornstarch-to-rice-flour blend produces a crust that is structurally crisp from the cornstarch, additionally crisp from the rice-flour starch granules that fry up into tiny porous puffs, and stable for 5 to 7 minutes after plating before steam softens it. Many home cooks skip the rice flour out of pantry inertia; it is genuinely worth keeping a bag in the cupboard for this dish and Cantonese fried-chicken applications.

Salt-pepper composition. The classic Cantonese jiao yan is white pepper plus salt, in a roughly 1:2 ratio of pepper-to-salt by weight, toasted and ground fresh. Restaurant versions often add Sichuan peppercorn (for a faint má numbing tingle that backgrounds the pepper) and sometimes five-spice powder (for the aromatic complexity). Both additions are traditional and not "drift"; the ratio above (4 g white pepper, 1 g Sichuan, 4 g salt, 0.5 g five-spice) is a Hong Kong restaurant standard. The salt-pepper is also good on roasted potatoes, fried eggs, and grilled chicken; make a double batch and keep it in a sealed jar for 2 weeks.

Eating mechanics. Salt-and-pepper squid is finger food in Cantonese eating culture, pick up each piece by hand or with chopsticks and eat directly. The crust degrades within 5 to 8 minutes of plating as steam from the squid softens the coating; the dish is meant to be eaten the moment it lands on the table. Beer (especially Tsingtao or another Asian lager) is the traditional pairing; a crisp dry white wine (riesling, gruner) also works. The dish is part of a multi-dish Cantonese seafood meal alongside steamed fish, stir-fried Chinese broccoli, and rice; it is rarely the only dish at the table.
