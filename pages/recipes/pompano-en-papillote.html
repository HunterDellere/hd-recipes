Pompano en papillote

Two pompano fillets sealed in a parchment crescent over a butter, shallot, garlic, dry white wine, and lemon base, a small folding of shrimp and lump crab tucked alongside, and an anchovy-deepened sauce, baked hard in a fully preheated oven until the packet puffs and steams the fish in its own rendered fat plus wine. Opened at the table. Pompano's high, clean fat makes the sealed packet nearly foolproof.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Preheat the oven to 220°C / 425°F with a sheet pan inside, and do not open a packet into a cold or half-heated oven. The entire method depends on a thermal shock: the packet must hit a fully saturated hot oven so the wine and rendered fat flash to steam and inflate the parchment within the first few minutes. A slow climb to temperature steams the fish flat and the packet never puffs.
  • Cut and fold the parchment before you build anything. For each packet, fold a 40 cm sheet of parchment in half, draw and cut a large half-heart so the open sheet is a full heart, and lay the filling on one lobe near the fold. After loading, seal by making small overlapping pleated folds around the curved edge, starting at the top of the heart and working to the point, each fold pinning the one before; twist the final point tight. The seal must be continuous, an unpleated gap vents the steam and the packet collapses.
  • Have the H Mart counter scale, gut, and fillet a whole 450 to 500 g golden pompano into two skin-on fillets, and take the frame home; the whole fish is the better value per gram of meat and the carcass makes a fast fumet. Pat the fillets bone-dry before they go in the packet, since surface water dilutes the wine-butter base and slows the puff.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the fish

  1. 240 g golden pompano fillets, skin on, pin-boned, from one 450 to 500 g whole fish, scaled, gutted, and filleted at the counter; patted completely dry on both sides
  2. 3 g kosher salt
  3. 1 g black pepper, freshly ground

Phase 2, umami base (the sauce)

  1. 42 g unsalted butter, softened, plus a little extra to grease the parchment
  2. 40 g shallot, minced fine, minced to 2 mm dice — (1 large shallot)
  3. 9 g garlic, minced fine, minced or grated to a paste — (3 cloves)
  4. 6 g anchovy fillets, oil-packed, minced to a paste — (2 fillets; the glutamate spine of the sauce, dissolves invisibly)
  5. 60 g dry white wine

Phase 3, the shellfish fold

  1. 80 g shrimp, peeled and deveined, tails removed, cut into 1 cm pieces — (about 6 medium shrimp)
  2. 70 g lump crab meat, picked, picked over for shell fragments, kept in large lumps

Phase 4, aromatics and finish

  1. 8 g flat-leaf parsley, chopped, leaves and tender stems, chopped
  2. 80 g cherry tomatoes, halved, halved through the equator — (about 6 tomatoes; for sweetness and a second glutamate note)
  3. 60 g lemon, half juiced (~15 g juice) into the sauce; half cut into thin half-moons for the packets — (1 lemon; the finishing acid)
  4. 14 g extra-virgin olive oil, for brushing the parchment and the fillets

Execution

  1. 1
    Preheat the oven to 220°C / 425°F with a sheet pan on the middle rack. Let it come fully to temperature, 15 minutes past the indicator beep, while you build the packets; the packet must meet a saturated hot oven or it will not puff. Pat the 240 g / 2 pompano fillets bone-dry on both sides and season each with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper, divided between them. Set aside while you build the base.
  2. 2
    Build the umami base. In a skillet over medium-low heat, melt 28 g / 2 tbsp of the 42 g butter (reserve the rest for the parchment and to dot the packets). Add the 40 g / 1 minced shallot and a pinch of the salt and sweat until translucent and soft, 4 to 5 minutes, without browning; browning pushes the base into the wrong register. Add the 9 g / 3 cloves minced garlic and the 6 g / 2 minced anchovy fillets and cook 60 seconds, mashing the anchovy until it dissolves into the fat. The anchovy is the glutamate spine of the dish and will read as savory depth, not as fish.
  3. 3
    Deglaze and reduce. Pour in the 60 g / ¼ cup dry white wine, raise the heat to medium, and reduce by half, 2 to 3 minutes, scraping the fond. Off heat, stir in the 15 g / juice of half the lemon and half of the 8 g / 2 tbsp parsley. Taste: the base should be bright, savory, and a touch tart, the acid and the anchovy in balance. This reduced butter-wine-anchovy-lemon liquor is what will flash to steam and poach the fish; keep it loose, not dry.
  4. 4
    Dress the shellfish. In a bowl, fold the 80 g / 6 shrimp pieces and the 70 g / ½ cup lump crab together with about a third of the warm base, just enough to coat. The shrimp will finish cooking in the packet; the crab is already cooked and only needs to warm through, so keep the lumps intact and do not overwork them.
  5. 5
    Fold and butter the parchment. For each of two 40 cm parchment sheets, fold in half, cut a large half-heart, open to a full heart, and lightly brush one lobe with some of the reserved butter and a little of the 14 g / 1 tbsp olive oil so the fish does not stick and the skin steams clean. Lay 3 to 4 cherry-tomato halves (from the 80 g total) on the buttered lobe as a bed, then set one seasoned pompano fillet skin-side down on top.
  6. 6
    Load each packet. Spoon half of the remaining warm base over and around each fillet, then mound half of the 150 g shrimp-and-crab mixture alongside it. Lay two lemon half-moons (from the remaining half lemon) over the fish, dot with a little of the reserved butter, and scatter the rest of the cherry-tomato halves. Keep the filling on the lobe and clear of the cut edge so the seal stays clean.
  7. 7
    Seal each packet. Fold the empty lobe over the filling so the cut edges meet. Starting at the top of the heart, make small overlapping pleated folds around the curved edge, each fold pinning the previous one, working down to the point; twist the final point tight and tuck it under. The seal must be continuous and snug, with no vent, or the steam escapes and the packet will not inflate. The packet should look like a loosely puffed half-moon with a little air already trapped inside.
  8. 8
    Bake hard. Pull the hot sheet pan from the oven, set both packets on it (they should sizzle on contact), and return it to the 220°C / 425°F oven. Bake 13 to 15 minutes; within the first 5 minutes the wine and the fish's rendered fat flash to steam and the parchment should visibly balloon and brown at the edges. A fully puffed, browned packet at 14 minutes means the fish inside is steamed through; a flat packet means the seal vented and you should still rest it but expect a wetter result. Do not open to check, opening releases the steam that is cooking the fish.
  9. 9
    Rest and open at the table. Slide each puffed packet onto a warm plate and carry it to the table intact; the drama is the point. Cut or tear the parchment open at the top with scissors or a knife tip, standing back from the vented steam, and let the trapped aromatic cloud release. Spoon the pooled butter-wine-shellfish liquor back over the fillet, scatter the reserved 4 g / 1 tbsp parsley and a final squeeze from any remaining lemon, and eat straight from the parchment with crusty bread to mop the liquor. The flesh should lift cleanly from the skin in firm, moist flakes at 54 to 58°C internal.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein cut→with a whole butterflied pompano instead of fillets

    Use one whole 450 to 500 g pompano, scaled, gutted, and butterflied flat (backbone removed, head on or off). Build a larger single packet, lay the base and shellfish inside the cavity, and bake 18 to 22 minutes by thickness. The presentation is the classic whole-fish-in-parchment; the bone and skin add gelatin and body to the liquor.

  • Regional shellfish→with crawfish tails (the deep-Louisiana version)

    Swap the shrimp and crab for 150 g / 1 cup peeled crawfish tails, already cooked, folded into the base off heat. Crawfish only need to warm through, so they read sweeter and more regional; the bake time is unchanged.

  • Texture sauce→toward a sauce velouté finish (Antoine's style)

    After reducing the wine, whisk in 10 g / 1 tbsp flour to make a quick roux with the butter, then loosen with 80 g / ⅓ cup fish fumet made from the reserved pompano frame. The base thickens to a true velouté that clings to the fillet. This version is no longer gluten-free-adjacent and pushes the dish into the old-line New Orleans register.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • golden pompano fillets → black sea bass, branzino, or rainbow trout fillets The packet supplies external fat, so leaner branzino and black sea bass both work; add 1 to 2 minutes for a thicker bass fillet and pull a touch earlier, since lean fish dry faster than pompano even in a packet. Trout is oilier and behaves most like pompano. Avoid thin flatfish, which overcook before the packet puffs.
  • lump crab meat → extra shrimp, or bay scallops If good lump crab is not available, replace it with another 70 g of chopped shrimp, or with bay scallops, which sweeten the fold. Avoid imitation crab, which weeps water into the packet and dilutes the liquor.
  • dry white wine → fish fumet or clam juice plus a squeeze of lemon For an alcohol-free packet, replace the wine with an equal weight of fumet (made from the reserved frame) or bottled clam juice, plus an extra few grams of lemon juice to restore the acid the wine would have carried. Reduce it the same way.
  • anchovy fillets → a few drops of fish sauce, or omit Anchovy is the hidden glutamate; if you object to it, 3 to 4 drops of fish sauce stirred into the reduced base gives the same savory lift. Omitting it entirely leaves the dish lighter and more one-dimensional, which is acceptable but less Creole.

Notes

Why pompano, and why the packet. The two halves of this dish are inseparable. En papillote is a steam method disguised as a bake: the sealed parchment traps the wine and the fish''s own rendered fat, which flash to vapor in the hot oven and poach the fillet in a fat-and-wine emulsion rather than dry air. That method is unforgiving of lean fish, which dry the moment the steam runs out. Pompano solves it: at 8 to 12% evenly distributed fat, the flesh self-bastes as its proteins contract, so the doneness window is wide and the packet is nearly foolproof. Pull a branzino packet two minutes late and the fish is chalky; pull a pompano packet two minutes late and it is still moist. This is the structural reason the dish is built on this specific fish and not on snapper or sole.

The glutamate and acid phases. Creole cooking is not shy, and a butter packet with nothing savory under it reads flat. The dish carries two glutamate sources: the anchovy minced into the base (which dissolves invisibly and reads as depth, not fish) and the shellfish itself, shrimp and crab, both rich in free glutamate and the nucleotides that multiply it. The finishing acid runs through the whole build, the dry white wine reduced into the base and the lemon added both to the liquor and as half-moons inside the packet. Butter, anchovy, and shellfish on one axis; wine and lemon on the other. Skip either axis and the packet steams into something rich but inert.

Preheat is the whole game. The single most common failure is loading a beautiful packet into an oven that has not fully recovered its temperature. The puff is a thermal-shock event: the packet must hit a saturated 220°C oven so the liquid inside flashes to steam fast enough to inflate the parchment before the fish has set. A slow climb steams the fish flat, the packet never balloons, and the showmanship is lost. Preheat with the sheet pan inside, give it the full 15 minutes past the beep, and set the packets onto a pan that is already hot enough to sizzle.

The seal and the fold. The parchment heart is folded so the curved seam can be pleated continuously: small overlapping folds from the top of the heart down to the point, each one pinning the last, finished with a tight twist at the tip. Any unpleated gap is a vent, and a vented packet will not hold pressure. If you are nervous, a packet of parchment crimped inside a second layer of foil holds steam more reliably for a first attempt, at the cost of the parchment-only browning. The puff is both the cooking mechanism and the doneness gauge: a fully inflated, edge-browned packet at 14 minutes is done; a flat one vented.

Sourcing and the whole-fish value. Buy a whole golden pompano at H Mart or any Asian seafood counter (usually Trachinotus blochii, farmed, 450 to 500 g) and have them scale, gut, and fillet it, taking the frame home. The whole fish is meaningfully cheaper per gram of meat than pre-cut fillets, and the carcass simmered 20 minutes with the shallot trim, a bay leaf, and the wine makes a clean fumet that doubles as the alcohol-free substitution liquid or the base for the velouté modification. A 450 to 500 g fish yields two ~120 g fillets, exactly two packets. Fresh pompano shows clear domed eyes, bright silver flanks with a gold belly wash, and tight scales; the gold fading to gray is the species'' own freshness clock.

Doneness. The flesh sets from translucent to opaque as the collagen sheets gelatinize at 55 to 60°C; the target is 54 to 58°C at the thickest point, where the fat is rendered and mobile but not driven off. Because the packet is opaque, trust the puff and the time rather than probing, which vents the steam. If you must check, slip a thin probe through the parchment at the very end of the bake; the flesh should read in the mid-50s and lift in firm, moist flakes from the skin.
