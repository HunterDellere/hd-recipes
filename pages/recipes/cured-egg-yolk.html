Cured egg yolk (salt-sugar dry cure)

Raw yolks buried in a 70:30 salt-sugar bed for a week, rinsed, then dried in a low oven or dehydrator until they grate like a hard cheese. Translucent, deep amber, dense umami concentrate.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a 9-day minimum project, not a recipe to start the day you want cured yolks. Day 1: build the cure bed and bury the yolks. Days 1 to 7: passive refrigerator cure, no attention required. Days 7 to 9 (or 7 to 12 if air-drying): dehydration. Plan backward from the day you want finished grateable yolks.
  • Buy the eggs the same day you start the cure. Yolk freshness governs whether the membrane holds intact when transferred into the cure bed; eggs that have been in the fridge a week are more likely to rupture during separation and contaminate the bed.
  • Reserve a glass or ceramic dish at least 5 cm deep with a footprint that holds 12 yolks without touching, plus enough fridge real estate to keep it undisturbed for a week. Metal vessels react with the salt cure and stain.
  • After day 7, decide your dehydration path before pulling from the cure. Dehydrator (24 to 48 hours), low oven with cracked door (6 to 8 hours), or cheesecloth air-dry in the fridge (5 to 7 more days). The choice determines when the yolks are actually ready to grate.

  4. 4 each star anise, optional aromatic, broken into pointsoptional

Yolks

  1. 12 each fresh large eggs (the freshest you can buy)

Execution

  1. 1
    Whisk 600 g / 2½ cups kosher salt and 250 g / 1¼ cups granulated sugar in a large bowl until uniform. The 70:30 ratio is the technique; pure salt cures dehydrate too aggressively and the yolk reads chalky and unpleasantly saline, while pure sugar does not pull enough water and the yolk stays tacky and unstable. The sugar moderates osmotic pressure and contributes a faint caramelized depth as the cure progresses. Stir in the optional 5 g / 2 tsp cracked black peppercorns or 4 / 8 g broken star anise points.
  2. 2
    In a glass or ceramic dish at least 5 cm / 2 inches deep, spread half (~425 g / 2 cups) of the salt-sugar mixture in an even bed. Use the back of a spoon to make 12 shallow wells in the surface, spaced so the yolks will not touch.
  3. 3
    Separate 12 large eggs one at a time over a small bowl, then transfer each yolk into a well in the cure bed. Working over a small bowl gives you a chance to discard a broken yolk without losing the rest. The whites are not used; refrigerate them in an airtight container for meringues, cocktails, or bread washes.
  4. 4
    Cover the yolks gently with the remaining cure mixture; they should be fully buried with no surface visible. Do not press; the yolk membrane is fragile in the first hour and excessive pressure breaks it.
  5. 5
    Cover the dish with plastic wrap or a tight-fitting lid. Refrigerate at 2-4°C / 35-40°F for 7 days. The yolks dehydrate steadily over the first 5 days, going from soft and yielding to firm enough to lift cleanly with a fork. By day 7 they should feel dense and slightly rubbery to gentle pressure, deeply orange, and translucent at the edge.7 d 0 h
  6. 6
    After 7 days, brush the cure off each yolk with the back of a teaspoon. Rinse very briefly under cold running water (no more than 5 seconds) to remove the last cure grains. Pat completely dry with paper towels. The cured yolks at this stage are eat-on-bread firm, savory, and rich; for a grate-able final product they need further drying. The 12 yolks should now weigh roughly 130 g total, down from 216 g raw.
  7. 7
    For grate-able yolks: dehydrate for 2-3 days at low heat. The reliable methods are (a) a dehydrator at 50°C / 120°F for 24-48 hours; (b) the lowest oven setting (typically 65-90°C / 150-200°F) with the door cracked, on a wire rack over a sheet tray, for 6-8 hours; (c) air-dry wrapped in cheesecloth, hanging in a cool dry pantry or refrigerator for 5-7 days. The oven method is fastest; air-drying produces the most concentrated result.2 d 0 h
  8. 8
    Test by grating: a fully dried yolk grates on a microplane into fluffy bright-orange shavings without smearing. If the yolk gums up the rasp, it needs more drying time. Final cured yolks should weigh roughly 6-8 g each, down from 18 g raw, a 55-65% mass loss.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cure aromatics→Italian-leaning cure

    Replace the optional Chinese aromatics with 5 g / 2 tsp finely chopped fresh rosemary and the zest of 1 lemon (~5 g / 2 tsp). The herb-citrus oils migrate into the surface and the yolk reads as a domestic answer to bottarga. Especially good for grating over pasta and risotto.

  • Regional cure aromatics→East Asian aromatic cure

    Add 15 g / 2 tbsp dried bonito flakes (katsuobushi) mixed into the salt-sugar bed and the optional star anise. The bonito glutamates penetrate the surface as the yolk dehydrates, layering an additional umami floor that is purpose-built for grating over steamed rice, congee, or chilled noodles.

  • Texture cure time→partial cure for spreadable yolks

    Pull at day 4 and skip the dehydration step. The result is a soft, jammy, intensely seasoned yolk that spreads on toast rather than grates. Refrigerated, it lasts about ten days; the texture continues to firm but does not hit grate-able.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • kosher salt (Diamond Crystal) → Morton kosher salt (use 360 g / 1¾ cup) or fine sea salt (use 400 g / 2 cups) Different crystal density. Morton kosher is denser by volume; fine sea salt is denser still. Match by mass and you stay correct.
  • granulated sugar → light brown sugar A subtle caramel note migrates into the yolk over the cure. Especially good for the rosemary-lemon variant; less coherent with the East Asian aromatic version.

Notes

How to use cured yolk: grate finely on a microplane over hot starches as a finishing umami concentrate. Canonical applications are pasta (carbonara-adjacent without the egg-as-sauce, or as a finish over cacio e pepe), risotto, hot rice with butter, congee, polenta, soft-boiled eggs on toast, raw beef tartare, sliced tomatoes with olive oil, and roasted vegetables where a salt-fat punch lifts the dish. The yolk is intensely savory; a dish for two takes no more than half a yolk grated, and the better discipline is to grate at the table per portion rather than dust the whole pan.

Why salt and sugar together: the cure is mostly osmotic dehydration plus protein denaturation. Salt drives both; sugar moderates the rate. A pure-salt cure dehydrates the yolk too fast and unevenly, locking the surface into a hard skin while the center is still tacky, and the resulting flavor reads aggressively saline rather than savory-rich. The 70:30 ratio is the well-tested home version of an industrial salt-sugar cure that many fine-dining kitchens use commercially.

Yolk freshness matters. The membrane around a fresh yolk is intact and elastic, which lets the yolk lift cleanly from the cure bed without breaking. Older yolks have membranes that have begun to weaken from storage and rupture under handling. Buy eggs the day you start the cure if possible; pasture-raised yolks with their deeper carotenoid load also produce a more dramatically colored finished cure.

Storage of finished yolks: airtight container in the refrigerator, indefinitely. The cure has dehydrated the yolk past the threshold for microbial activity, similar to a hard cheese. Grate as needed. They will continue to slowly lose moisture over months; this is fine, just adjust by grating a little less per dish as they age. Two-month-old cured yolks read more sharply concentrated than fresh ones.

Why this is faster than a true salted duck egg yolk: traditional salted duck egg yolks are brine-cured in shell for 30-40 days, then steamed before separation. The home dry-cure on chicken yolks reaches a usable concentrated grateable state in 9 days total. The duck-egg version reads richer and redder; the chicken version is brighter, slightly saltier, and more accessible. Both have legitimate use cases.
