Seafood stock (shellfish and fin-fish bone broth)

A clean, aromatic seafood stock built from shrimp shells, white-fish bones (cod, halibut, snapper carcasses), crab shells, and a Western mirepoix (onion, fennel, leek, garlic). Roasted-shell version for cioppino and bouillabaisse depth; raw-shell version for delicate fumets and risotto al frutti di mare. The stock cooks fast (45 minutes) compared to meat broths because fish bones release their flavor and gelatin quickly, and over-simmering produces a bitter, fishy-aggressive result.

Phase 1, shells and bones

  1. 200 g shrimp shells (saved from prep, frozen until needed)
  2. 500 g fish bones and head (cod, halibut, snapper, branzino), gills removed and rinsed — (2 to 3 carcasses from filleted fish; ask the fishmonger to save them when you buy fillets, free of charge)
  3. 300 g crab shells (cleaned, picked, broken into pieces) — (shells from 1 Dungeness or 2 to 3 blue crabs; the brown butter cleaned out, gills removed)
  4. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil

Phase 2, mirepoix

  1. 200 g yellow onion, large dice — (1 medium onion)
  2. 150 g fennel bulb, large dice (reserve fronds) — (½ medium bulb)
  3. 100 g leek, white and pale green only, sliced — (1 medium leek)
  4. 30 g garlic head, halved across (skin on) — (½ head, skin on)

Phase 3, simmer

  1. 250 ml dry white wine
  2. 2000 ml cold water
  3. 2 each bay leaves
  4. 4 g black peppercorns
  5. 10 g fresh parsley stems (no leaves) — (stems from a small bunch)
  6. 3 each fresh thyme — (3 sprigs)

Execution

  1. 1
    For the Mediterranean (roasted-shell) version, heat oven to 200 °C / 400 °F. Toss the 200 g shrimp shells and 300 g crab shells with the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil on a heavy roasting pan. Roast 15 to 20 minutes, until the shells turn deep red-orange and smell powerfully seafood-roasted. Skip this step for a delicate fumet (raw version, used for sole sauces, risotto al frutti di mare); roast for cioppino, bouillabaisse, paella, or any tomato-based seafood preparation.
  2. 2
    Transfer the roasted shells (or the unroasted shells, for a fumet) to an 8-quart stockpot along with the 500 g of fish bones and head. Add the 200 g onion, 150 g fennel bulb, 100 g leek, and halved 30 g garlic head. Pour the 250 ml / 1 cup dry white wine into the roasting pan (if used), scrape up any fond, and add to the stockpot.
  3. 3
    Add the 2000 ml / 8 cups cold water, 2 bay leaves, 4 g / ¾ tsp peppercorns, 10 g parsley stems, and 3 thyme sprigs. Bring to the threshold of a simmer over medium heat (about 90 °C / 195 °F); skim any foam that rises in the first 10 minutes (gray scum, denatured surface proteins from the bones). Reduce to a bare tremor of a simmer and cook 45 minutes uncovered. Stop at 45 minutes; over-simmering past 60 minutes makes the stock taste bitter and aggressively fishy as the bones break down further.
  4. 4
    Strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a second pot, pressing the solids lightly to extract liquid. Discard the spent shells and bones. For an even cleaner stock, strain again through cheesecloth. The finished stock should be a deep amber-gold (roasted version) or pale gold (fumet version), aromatic, and lightly viscous; cooled and refrigerated, it should set into a soft jelly within a few hours, the visual signature of properly extracted shellfish stock.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→classical French fumet de poisson (no roast)

    Skip the shell-roasting step entirely; use only fish bones (no crab or shrimp shells), and cook 25 to 35 minutes only (the bones release flavor faster than shellfish). The stock reads cleaner and more delicate; the canonical sauce base for sole meuniere, sole Veronique, and any French white-fish dish where the stock should not dominate.

  • Occasion time-shortcut→pressure-cooker version

    Roast the shells as written. Transfer everything to the Instant Pot. High pressure 25 minutes, natural release. Strain. The stock is 80 percent of the simmered version in flavor.

Notes

This is a stub recipe. The drafting framework is in place but the full operational treatment needs expansion: the chemistry behind the 45-minute simmer rule (collagen extracts and gelatin partitions in the first 30 minutes; past 60 minutes the bone calcium and remaining tissue start to leach off-flavors); the difference between a fumet and a stock (fumet is the French term for a stock made fast from delicate ingredients; the cioppino version is technically a stock, not a fumet); the storage rules (3 days refrigerated, 4 months frozen in 250 ml containers, gels softly when cold which signals proper extraction); the use cases (cioppino, bouillabaisse, paella, risotto al frutti di mare, fish soups, white-wine pan sauces); and the failure modes (over-simmering, under-roasting the shells for the Mediterranean version, using cold-water-extracted bones that have not been rinsed of blood).
