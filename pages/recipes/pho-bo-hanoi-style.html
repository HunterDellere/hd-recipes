Pho bo (Hanoi-style beef pho)

Northern Vietnamese beef noodle soup. Beef bones and a fist of marrow simmered with charred onion and ginger, star anise, cinnamon, cardamom, clove, and coriander seed for five hours into a clear, deeply-savory broth. Served over flat rice noodles with paper-thin raw sirloin (cooked by the broth at the table), boiled brisket, scallion, cilantro, white onion, and a wedge of lime. Northern pho is plain at the table; the broth is the dish, no hoisin, no sriracha, no plate of herbs.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The broth needs 5 to 6 hours of simmering plus a 30-minute blanch-and-rinse step at the start; plan a full afternoon. The broth holds 5 days in the fridge and freezes 3 months, so make a double batch.
  • Char the onion and ginger over an open flame (gas burner or grill) until blackened on multiple sides. The charring contributes Maillard products that are structural to canonical pho aroma; skipping it produces a flat-tasting broth. Use a pair of long tongs and a sheet of foil to catch ash.
  • Toast the whole spices in a dry skillet before adding to the broth. Untoasted spices contribute muddy bitterness; toasted spices contribute clean aromatic depth.
  • Slice the raw sirloin while it is still partially frozen. Paper-thin slices (2 mm or less) cook in 15 seconds when the hot broth hits them at the table; thicker slices stay raw. A 30-minute freezer firm-up makes the slicing possible with a sharp knife.
  • Cook the noodles last and assemble each bowl individually. Pho is not built in a pot; it is composed in each bowl with hot broth poured over cold ingredients at the table.

Mise en place by phase 6 phases ▾

Phase 1, broth (bones)

  1. 2000 g beef marrow bones (femur or knuckle), cut into 5 cm sections
  2. 800 g beef brisket, point cut
  3. 400 g beef oxtail (optional, for extra body) optional

Phase 2, broth aromatics (charred)

  1. 300 g yellow onion, unpeeled, halved through the equator; charred over open flame until blackened on the cut surfaces and skin — (2 large onions; the char is non-negotiable, produces the smoky-Maillard backbone of canonical pho broth)
  2. 80 g fresh ginger, unpeeled, sliced lengthwise into 1 cm thick slabs; charred over open flame until blackened on both sides — (a 10 cm / 4 inch knob)

Phase 3, broth spices (toasted)

  1. 6 each star anise — (6 whole pods; the dominant pho aromatic, anise-front)
  2. 2 each cinnamon stick, broken into 3 cm pieces
  3. 6 each green cardamom pods, lightly cracked with the flat of a knife to expose seeds — (6 pods; contributes floral-citrus aromatic complexity)
  4. 6 each whole cloves — (6 cloves; warm-pungent spice backbone)
  5. 8 g coriander seeds
  6. 4 g fennel seeds

Phase 4, broth seasoning

  1. 6000 ml water (cold)
  2. 30 g rock sugar (or yellow rock sugar)
  3. 60 g fish sauce
  4. 15 g kosher salt, to taste

Phase 5, bowl assembly

  1. 400 g flat rice noodles (banh pho), dried
  2. 400 g beef sirloin or eye-of-round, sliced paper-thin (2 mm or less), partially frozen for 30 minutes for slicing, then sliced across the grain on a slight bias
  3. 800 g cooked brisket (from the broth pot), pulled at the 2-hour mark, sliced 3 mm thick against the grain — (reused from the broth pot, see step 6)

Phase 6, garnishes (Hanoi-canonical, minimal)

  1. 60 g scallion, sliced thin, sliced 3 mm thin, white and green parts together — (6 scallions)
  2. 30 g cilantro leaves and tender stems, rough chopped
  3. 80 g white onion, sliced paper-thin, sliced 2 mm thin pole-to-pole, then soaked in ice water 10 minutes to crisp and mellow — (½ small white onion; the canonical pho garnish, raw onion bite balances the rich broth)
  4. 2 each lime wedges, cut into 6 wedges — (1 wedge per bowl; Hanoi tradition uses lime sparingly, mainly to brighten the broth)
  5. 10 g fresh red chili (bird-eye), sliced thin optional — (2 chilies; on the side for diners who want heat)

Execution

  1. 1
    Blanch the bones. Place the 2000 g beef bones, 800 g brisket, and 400 g oxtail (if using) in a stockpot. Cover with cold water by 5 cm. Bring to a hard boil over high heat (do not skim, the blanch step is to drive out the impurities, not extract flavor). Boil 10 minutes; the water will go cloudy and dark with scum. Pour off the entire pot through a colander. Rinse the bones and meat under cold running water to remove any clinging scum. This step is what produces a clear pho broth; skipping it yields a cloudy, gray-tinged broth.
  2. 2
    Char the aromatics. Hold each onion half cut-side-down directly in the flame of a gas burner (or under a hot broiler) with tongs for 4 to 6 minutes until blackened across the cut surface and the skin is fully charred. Char the ginger slabs the same way, 3 to 4 minutes per side. The smoke is normal; ventilate the kitchen. The aromatics should look like they were forgotten on a grill, not lightly toasted.
  3. 3
    Toast the spices. In a dry skillet over medium heat, toast the 6 star anise, 2 cinnamon sticks, 6 cracked cardamom, 6 cloves, 8 g coriander seeds, and 4 g fennel seeds, stirring constantly for 90 seconds to 2 minutes until the spices smell deeply fragrant and just begin to darken. Pull immediately; toasted spices burn fast. Wrap the toasted spices in a piece of cheesecloth tied with kitchen twine, or place in a tea ball or spice bag. Wrapping prevents seeds and broken cinnamon from clouding the broth. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  4. 4
    Start the simmer. Return the blanched bones and meats to a clean stockpot. Add the 6000 ml / 25 cups cold water; the bones should be covered with 5 cm clearance. Add the charred onion halves, the charred ginger, and the wrapped spice packet. Bring slowly to a bare simmer over medium-low heat, about 30 minutes. Once at a simmer, reduce heat to maintain a tremor at the surface; never a rolling boil (rolling boil emulsifies fat into the broth and clouds it). Skim any foam that rises in the first hour. Simmer partially covered (lid cracked) for 5 hours total.5 h
  5. 5
    Pull the brisket at the 2-hour mark. The brisket is fully cooked at this point (about 90°C / 195°F internal). Lift out with tongs; transfer to a bowl, cover and refrigerate (chilled brisket slices more cleanly than warm). Continue simmering the bones, oxtail, onion, ginger, and spice packet for the remaining 3 hours.
  6. 6
    Finish the broth. At the 4.5-hour mark, add the 30 g rock sugar, 60 g / 4 tbsp fish sauce, and 15 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt to the simmering broth. Stir to dissolve. Simmer 30 more minutes to integrate the seasoning. The broth should be a deep amber color, clear, with visible droplets of fat on the surface and a strong smell of star anise and beef.
  7. 7
    Strain. Pour the broth through a fine-mesh sieve lined with cheesecloth into a clean pot. Discard the bones, spice packet, charred aromatics; reserve any meat clinging to the bones for the bowls. You should have about 4500 ml / 19 cups of finished broth. Skim excess surface fat with a ladle, leaving a thin layer of clear amber fat (the canonical pho fat slick).
  8. 8
    Slice the raw beef. Pull the partially-frozen 400 g sirloin and slice paper-thin (2 mm or less) across the grain on a slight bias. Arrange on a small plate; refrigerate until bowl assembly. Slice the chilled 800 g brisket the same way (3 mm thick, across the grain). Arrange on a separate plate.
  9. 9
    Cook the noodles just before serving. Bring a separate pot of water to a hard boil. For dried banh pho, soak in warm water 20 minutes first, then drop into boiling water for 30 to 60 seconds until just tender. For fresh banh pho, loosen the noodles in a colander, then dip in boiling water for 10 to 15 seconds only. Drain immediately; portion 1 to 1.5 cups cooked noodles per bowl.
  10. 10
    Assemble each bowl. The broth must be at a hard rolling boil at the moment of serving; this is the only heat that will cook the raw sirloin at the table. Mound the cooked noodles in a deep bowl. Lay 3 to 4 slices of cooked brisket and 4 to 5 slices of raw sirloin on top of the noodles. Scatter a generous handful of sliced scallion, a small handful of cilantro, and a small mound of soaked white onion over the meats. Ladle 400 ml / 1.5 cups of hard-boiling broth directly over the meats; the raw sirloin will turn from red to gray-pink in 15 to 30 seconds. Serve immediately with a lime wedge and optional sliced chili on the side. Diners squeeze the lime over the broth (Hanoi tradition uses lime sparingly; Saigon tradition uses more) and eat with chopsticks (noodles and meat) and a spoon (broth).

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→Saigon-style pho (Southern Vietnamese)

    Add a side plate of fresh garnishes for the table: Thai basil sprigs, mung bean sprouts (raw, blanched 15 seconds in boiling water), culantro or sawtooth herb sprigs, extra lime wedges, sliced bird-eye chili. Also serve hoisin sauce and sriracha in small dishes on the side; diners spoon the hoisin and sriracha into a personal dipping sauce for the brisket (not into the broth). The Saigon version is herbier, sweeter (often more rock sugar in the broth), and visually busier than the Hanoi original.

  • Protein protein→pho ga (chicken pho)

    Substitute a whole chicken (1800 g) plus chicken backs and necks (500 g) for the beef bones, brisket, and oxtail. Skip the bone-blanch step; chicken bones rinse instead of blanch. Simmer 90 minutes (not 5 hours); chicken broth extracts flavor much faster. Reduce the spice load: use 3 star anise, 1 small cinnamon stick, skip the cardamom and cloves. The aromatic profile shifts from beef-and-anise to chicken-and-coriander; pho ga is a structurally different dish that shares the bowl format.

  • Occasion occasion→pho bo for a weeknight (pressure cooker shortcut)

    Use an Instant Pot or stovetop pressure cooker for the broth. Blanch the bones the same way. Pressure cook the bones, charred aromatics, and water on high pressure for 90 minutes; natural release. Add the wrapped spice packet, brisket, and seasoning; pressure cook another 30 minutes. The broth is lighter-bodied than the 5-hour simmer version but defensible for a weeknight bowl. The brisket is properly cooked at this point.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • beef marrow bones (femur or knuckle) → beef neck bones or beef shank bones Marrow is the canonical pho bone for the fat-and-gelatin contribution. Neck and shank bones work as a substitute, slightly less fatty broth; the difference is acceptable.
  • rock sugar (yellow rock sugar) → palm sugar or dark brown sugar Rock sugar is the canonical Vietnamese broth sweetener; palm sugar produces a slightly more caramel-toned broth; dark brown sugar is acceptable. Use the same weight.
  • flat rice noodles (banh pho), dried → fresh banh pho (if available) or rice noodles 5 mm wide Fresh banh pho is dramatically better than dried; check Vietnamese groceries. The dish reads identical with either format; the fresh version has slightly more chew and absorbs broth differently.

Notes

Why the bone blanch. The 10-minute hard boil at the start of broth-making is what drives the dissolved blood proteins, marrow residue, and surface impurities out of the bones; pouring off the cloudy first water and rinsing the bones produces a clear, golden-amber finished broth. Skipping this step is the single most common reason home-cooked pho broth looks cloudy and gray rather than clear and amber. The blanch wastes about 10 minutes and zero flavor; the actual broth-making starts with the clean second pot.

Why the char on onion and ginger. The blackened surfaces of the charred aromatics contribute Maillard-derived smoky-caramel notes that are structural to canonical pho broth aroma. A pho broth made with raw (uncharred) onion and ginger tastes flat and lacks the deep, almost-smoky backbone that defines the dish. The char is not browning; the onion and ginger should be visibly blackened, almost burnt-looking, before they go in the pot.

Why the spice toast. Whole spices contain a mix of volatile aromatic oils (the desired pho flavors) and harsher, bitter compounds (tannins, certain alkaloids). The dry-toast for 90 seconds drives off the harsh compounds and concentrates the aromatic oils; toasted spices contribute clean aromatic depth where untoasted spices contribute muddy bitterness. The same spices added untoasted produce a duller, slightly off-tasting broth.

Why no rolling boil during the simmer. Pho broth depends on the slow extraction of collagen from bones (becomes gelatin, which is the textural backbone of the finished broth, the slight thick-mouthfeel that distinguishes great pho from thin pho). High heat (rolling boil) emulsifies bone marrow fat into the broth as tiny droplets, which both clouds the broth visually and makes it taste greasy. The bare simmer (a tremor at the surface) extracts gelatin without emulsifying fat; the fat instead floats to the top as a clear amber slick that can be skimmed or left as the canonical pho topping.

Why raw sirloin at the table. The defining textural moment of canonical Hanoi pho is the raw beef cooked by the hot broth at the moment of serving. The paper-thin sirloin slices hit boiling-hot broth and cook in 15 to 30 seconds; the meat goes from raw-red to gray-pink, still tender and juicy, never reaching the chalky-overcooked texture of beef simmered in stock. A pho with pre-cooked beef tastes structurally different; the raw-cooked-at-the-table moment is the dish.

Why Hanoi style is plain at the table. The Hanoi original assumes the broth is the dish; the broth must speak alone. Garnishes like Thai basil, bean sprouts, hoisin, and sriracha (Southern Vietnamese and Vietnamese-American conventions) crowd the broth flavor and were not part of the dish as it originated. A Hanoi pho served plain (just scallion, cilantro, white onion, lime) is the canonical experience; the diner who wants to add hoisin to a Hanoi pho is, from a Hanoi perspective, missing the point. Both traditions are legitimate; the distinction is geographical and worth understanding.

Storage. The broth holds 5 days in the fridge and 3 months frozen. Freeze in quart containers; thaw in the fridge the day before use. The cooked brisket holds 3 days refrigerated. Cooked noodles do not store well; cook fresh for each meal. The raw sirloin must be used within 2 days of slicing; freeze the unused portion immediately if delayed.

Sources
  • Adapted from Hanoi (Northern Vietnamese) tradition — Pho originated in early-20th-century Northern Vietnam, likely in Nam Dinh province south of Hanoi, as a fusion of French beef-stew technique (the cinnamon-clove-cardamom spice profile traces partly to pot-au-feu) and existing Vietnamese rice-noodle dishes. Hanoi pho is structurally clean and plain: clear broth, no garnish plate, no hoisin, no sriracha. The Southern (Saigon) version, which evolved later, adds bean sprouts, Thai basil, lime, hoisin, and sriracha as table garnishes. This recipe teaches the Hanoi original; for Saigon-style, add the garnish plate as described in modifications.
