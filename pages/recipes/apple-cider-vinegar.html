Apple cider vinegar

Apple scraps (cores and peels) plus water plus a little sugar, fermented for 4 to 6 weeks under a cloth, produce a live unpasteurized cider vinegar with complex apple character. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 4 items ▾
  1. 800 g apple scraps (cores, peels, bruised flesh) from organic apples — (scraps from ~6 to 8 apples; organic preferred so wild yeast is intact on the skins)
  2. 50 g cane sugar
  3. 1000 ml filtered water, room temperature
  4. 30 g splash of unfiltered apple cider vinegar (optional, as starter)

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: dissolve the 50 g / ¼ cup sugar in the 1000 ml / 4 cups filtered water in a wide-mouth glass jar (2 L / 2 quart capacity, cleaned but not sterilized). Add the 800 g / scraps from 6 to 8 apples; press down so the scraps are submerged in the sugar water. Top with the 30 g / 2 tbsp starter vinegar if using.
  2. 2
    Stub: cover the jar with a clean kitchen towel or coffee filter secured with a rubber band (the fermentation needs air exchange but a barrier against fruit flies). Place in a warm dark spot (21 to 27 °C / 70 to 80 °F is ideal). Stir daily for the first 7 days with a wooden spoon to keep the apple scraps submerged and prevent surface mold. The mixture will start bubbling within 2 to 3 days as the wild yeast on the apple skins begins fermenting the sugar into ethanol.
  3. 3
    Stub: after 1 to 2 weeks, the bubbling slows and the liquid smells distinctly alcoholic (this is hard cider). Strain out the apple scraps through a fine-mesh sieve, return the liquid to the jar, recover with the towel. The acetobacter (introduced from the air, the apple skins, or the starter vinegar) will now begin oxidizing the ethanol into acetic acid. A thin gelatinous film (the "mother of vinegar") may form on the surface; this is normal and a sign of healthy fermentation.
  4. 4
    Stub: continue fermentation at room temperature for 3 to 4 more weeks, tasting weekly. The vinegar is done when the alcohol smell is gone and the liquid tastes distinctly acidic with a clean apple character (4 to 6 percent acetic acid; a pH meter reads 2.5 to 3.5 when ready). Bottle in clean glass jars; the vinegar is shelf-stable indefinitely. The mother can be reserved as starter for the next batch.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the two-stage fermentation chemistry (yeast converts sugar to ethanol in the absence/limited-access of oxygen; acetobacter converts ethanol to acetic acid in the presence of oxygen, with the surface mother as the bacterial colony), why organic apples matter (commercial apples are washed and may have surface fungicides that inhibit wild yeast), the role of the starter vinegar (unfiltered commercial vinegar contains live acetobacter and skips the 1 to 2 week waiting period for wild colonization), the temperature dependence (slower at room temperature below 20 °C, can stall above 30 °C), the smell-and-taste cues for each stage (sweet → bubbly-fruity-alcoholic → vinegary), the failure modes (surface mold from insufficient submersion, spoilage smells from contamination, kahm yeast formation as a white film distinct from the mother), the safe pH ranges, the comparison to commercial cider vinegar (homemade is more aromatic, more apple-character, slightly less precisely 5% acid), the use cases where homemade is best (vinaigrettes, pickles, finishing) and where commercial is fine (high-volume cooking applications), the long-term storage of the live mother (can be perpetuated indefinitely, like a sourdough starter), the seasonal pattern (autumn after apple harvest is the natural production window).
