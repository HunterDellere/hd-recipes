Recipe

Apple cider vinegar

Apple scraps (cores and peels), water, and a little sugar fermented for 4 to 6 weeks under a cloth. A two-stage cure: wild yeast on the apple skins converts sugar to ethanol, then airborne acetobacter converts ethanol to acetic acid. Yields a live, unpasteurized cider vinegar with apple character lost in commercial bottling.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 4 items ▾
  1. 800 g apple scraps (cores, peels, bruised flesh) from organic apples — (scraps from ~6 to 8 apples; organic preferred so wild yeast on the skins is intact and not killed by surface fungicides)
  2. 50 g cane sugar
  3. 1000 ml filtered water, room temperature
  4. 30 g splash of unfiltered raw apple cider vinegar (optional, as starter)

Execution

  1. 1
    Dissolve the 50 g / ¼ cup cane sugar in the 1000 ml / 4 cups filtered water in a wide-mouth glass jar (2 L / 2 quart capacity, cleaned but not sterilized; some residual ambient yeast on the jar is helpful, not harmful). Stir until the sugar is fully dissolved before adding fruit; undissolved sugar at the bottom can produce uneven fermentation pockets.
  2. 2
    Add the 800 g / scraps from 6 to 8 apples to the sugar water. Use cores, peels, and bruised flesh; whole apples can be used but slow the fermentation slightly because the skin keeps the wild yeast contained on the surface rather than dispersed through the liquid. Press down so the scraps are submerged in the sugar water; if any pieces float, weight them down with a fermentation glass weight, a small ceramic ramekin, or a plastic ziplock bag of brine. Top with the 30 g / 2 tbsp starter raw apple cider vinegar if using (skip if you want to ferment the wild way; the trade is an extra week of cure time).
  3. 3
    Cover the jar with a clean kitchen towel or a coffee filter secured with a rubber band; the fermentation needs air exchange but a barrier against fruit flies (which are an active threat at every stage). Place in a warm dark spot at 21 to 27 °C / 70 to 80 °F; an interior cabinet, the top of a refrigerator, or a kitchen counter out of direct sunlight all work. Stir daily with a clean wooden spoon for the first 7 days to keep the apple scraps submerged and prevent surface mold (the floaters are the most vulnerable to mold contamination).
  4. 4
    Within 2 to 3 days, the mixture will start bubbling as the wild yeast on the apple skins (Saccharomyces cerevisiae and related species) begins fermenting the dissolved sugar into ethanol. The smell shifts from sweet apple to sweet-and-bubbly to faintly alcoholic-cider over days 3 to 7. The bubbles may slow or pause briefly around days 4 to 5; this is normal as the yeast population shifts from a fast initial bloom to a steady-state fermentation, not a stall.
  5. 5
    After 1 to 2 weeks, the bubbling slows and the liquid smells distinctly alcoholic; this is hard cider, the intermediate product. Strain out the 800 g apple scraps through a fine-mesh sieve (compost the scraps; they have given up most of their flavor and pectin to the liquid). Return the liquid to the jar, recover with the towel.
  6. 6
    The acetobacter (introduced from the air, the apple skins, or the starter vinegar if used) now begins oxidizing the ethanol into acetic acid; this is the second stage of the cure. A thin gelatinous film, the "mother of vinegar," may form on the surface within 1 to 2 weeks; this is the visible signal that healthy fermentation is underway. The mother is harmless and can be left in the jar (it will grow further) or removed with a clean spoon and saved for the next batch.
  7. 7
    Continue fermentation at room temperature for 3 to 4 more weeks (4 to 6 weeks total elapsed time from the start), tasting weekly with a clean glass straw or pipette. The cure is complete when the alcohol smell is gone and the liquid tastes distinctly acidic with a clean apple character; a pH meter reads 2.5 to 3.5 when ready, corresponding to 4 to 6 percent acetic acid. Bottle the finished vinegar in clean glass jars or vinegar bottles; the vinegar is shelf-stable indefinitely. Reserve the mother as starter for the next batch.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional fruit→pear scrap vinegar

    Substitute pear scraps (cores and peels from 6 to 8 ripe Bartlett, Bosc, or Anjou pears) for the apple scraps. Reduce the sugar to 30 g / 2 tbsp because pears contain more residual sugar than apples. The cure runs slightly longer (5 to 7 weeks) because pears have a thinner natural yeast load. The result is a softer, more floral vinegar with a pronounced pear character.

  • Regional fruit→mixed orchard scrap vinegar

    Use a mix of apple, pear, and quince scraps in any ratio. Quince adds aromatic complexity (the high pectin and the peculiar honey-floral aroma of quince carry through), apples provide the wild-yeast load, pears smooth the finish. Reduce sugar to 35 g / 2½ tbsp. The cure runs the same 4 to 6 weeks.

  • Regional cuisine→whole-fruit cider-and-vinegar (the autumn project version)

    Use 1.5 kg of whole crushed apples (any cultivar; cider apples like Kingston Black or Roxbury Russet are traditional but supermarket Galas work) and skip the sugar entirely. The crushing releases more juice and the wild yeast load is higher; the cure runs 6 to 8 weeks but produces a more complex vinegar that drinks closer to a sipping vinegar than a vinaigrette acid. This is the homestead-cider tradition; it requires a fruit press or significant manual mashing.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • cane sugar → honey, maple syrup, or unrefined cane sugar (sucanat, rapadura) Any fermentable sugar works. Honey produces a slightly slower cure (the natural antibacterial compounds in honey delay yeast bloom by 1 to 2 days) but contributes a subtle floral note. Maple syrup gives the cure a faintly maple finish. Use the same volume as cane sugar.
  • organic apples → conventional apples washed with a vinegar-water rinse Conventional supermarket apples may have surface fungicides that inhibit wild yeast. A 30-second rinse in 1:10 vinegar-to-water restores most of the wild-yeast viability. Add a starter culture (the optional raw apple cider vinegar) to compensate for any remaining inhibition.
  • starter raw apple cider vinegar → a piece of mother of vinegar (50 g) from a previous batch A mother is the strongest starter and reduces total cure time by 1 to 2 weeks. Float on the surface of the cider after the first-stage fermentation completes.

Notes

The two-stage fermentation chemistry. Apple scrap vinegar runs as two distinct biochemical processes in sequence: (1) yeast fermentation, in which Saccharomyces cerevisiae and related wild yeasts on the apple skins convert dissolved sugars (the added cane sugar plus the residual fructose and glucose in the apple scraps) to ethanol via the standard glycolysis-and-pyruvate-decarboxylation pathway, producing CO2 as a byproduct (the visible bubbling); (2) acetic acid fermentation, in which Acetobacter species oxidize the ethanol to acetic acid via the two-step alcohol-dehydrogenase-and-aldehyde-dehydrogenase pathway, consuming oxygen at the air-liquid interface. Stage 1 is anaerobic and runs whether or not the surface is exposed; stage 2 is obligate aerobic and depends on surface contact with the air. The two stages overlap in the middle of the cure (acetobacter begins working as soon as ethanol is available, even before stage 1 completes), but the overall sequence runs from sweet to alcoholic to vinegary.

Why organic apples matter. Commercial conventional apples are washed with chlorine and may carry residual surface fungicides (captan, mancozeb, or thiabendazole are common). Both inhibit the wild yeast on the apple skin; without that wild yeast load, the first-stage fermentation depends entirely on ambient yeast in the kitchen, which is unreliable. Organic apples are not chlorine-washed and carry an intact wild-yeast population, which is why they work consistently in this recipe. If conventional apples are the only option, a 1:10 vinegar-water rinse partially restores wild-yeast viability (the vinegar dissolves residual fungicides without sterilizing the surface).

The role of starter vinegar. Adding 30 g of unfiltered raw apple cider vinegar as starter introduces an active acetobacter culture and shortcuts the 1 to 2 week wait for ambient acetobacter to colonize the surface. The cure runs roughly 1 week faster with starter than without. Bragg-style or other widely-available raw unfiltered cider vinegars work; pasteurized commercial vinegar does not (the live bacteria are killed in pasteurization).

Temperature and time. Below 20 °C the wild yeast bloom is sluggish and the first stage may take 3 weeks rather than 1 to 2; below 18 °C the cure may stall entirely. Above 30 °C the yeast population shifts toward heat-tolerant strains that can produce off-flavors (banana ester from isoamyl acetate, or stale-bread aromas from Brettanomyces wild yeast); the cure also accelerates such that the second stage may run before the first stage completes, producing a confused intermediate product. The 21 to 27 °C window is the working sweet spot.

Smell-and-taste cues for each stage. Days 1 to 3: sweet apple, no fermentation activity yet. Days 3 to 7: sweet-and-bubbly, increasing alcohol smell, visible bubbles, foam at the surface. Days 7 to 14: alcoholic-fruity, faint vinegar at the back of the nose, surface starting to look glassy as the bubbles slow. Days 14 to 28: alcohol fades, vinegar grows, mother begins to form on the surface (translucent jelly disc). Days 28 to 42: clean apple-vinegar smell, no residual alcohol, mother thickens. Days 42 to 56: peak vinegar, ready to bottle.

Failure modes. Surface mold (white fuzz or colored patches): insufficient submersion of the apple scraps allowed mold to grow on the floaters; skim, push the scraps under, and continue. Spoilage smell (sulfur, rotten eggs): contamination with non-target microbes or apple scraps that were already starting to rot when added; discard the batch. Kahm yeast (white film distinct from the mother): forms when the alcohol drops too low between the first and second stages, often because of cold temperatures; skim and continue, or warm the cure up to bring it back to working temperature. No acetobacter activity for 4 weeks after stage 1 completes: temperature is too low or the kitchen has no ambient acetobacter; add a fresh starter (raw apple cider vinegar) and warm the cure to 24 to 26 °C.

Comparison to commercial cider vinegar. Bragg-style commercial unfiltered apple cider vinegar runs through a similar process at industrial scale, with the difference that commercial production uses a controlled pure-culture acetobacter inoculum and runs in a continuous flow process rather than a batch process. The home version is more apple-aromatic, more variable batch-to-batch, and slightly less precisely 5 percent acetic acid (typically 4 to 6 percent rather than the labeled 5 percent). For most home applications (vinaigrettes, pickles, marinades, finishing) the home version is preferable; for high-volume cooking applications where the precise acidity matters, commercial is the safer choice.

The seasonal pattern. Autumn (September to November in the Northern Hemisphere), immediately after apple harvest, is the natural production window. Apples are at peak sugar and peak wild-yeast load, the kitchen scraps from autumn baking and applesauce production are abundant, and the cool-but-not-cold autumn temperatures match the recommended 21 to 27 °C cure window. A single autumn batch produces enough vinegar for a household for a year.

Storage and use of the live mother. The mother of vinegar can be perpetuated indefinitely, like a sourdough starter. Store it in a small jar of mature vinegar in the refrigerator between batches; warm to room temperature before using. The mother grows over time and can be split (a piece of mother gifted to a friend starts their next batch). Some serious home fermenters maintain a single mother for decades, passing it down through families.
