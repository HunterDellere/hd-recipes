Rice kheer (chawal ki kheer)

Whole milk reduced by 60% with basmati rice, sweetened with sugar, perfumed with green cardamom and saffron, finished with chopped pistachios and almonds. The structural North Indian milk-rice pudding, eaten warm or cold, served at every festival and Sunday meal. Reduction is the technique; patience is the only difficulty.

  2. 60 g basmati rice
  3. 100 g granulated sugar (or jaggery for traditional sweetness)

  5. 0.1 g saffron threads

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, base

  1. 1500 ml whole milk (HTST-pasteurized, not ultra-pasteurized)
  2. 60 g basmati rice

Phase 2, sweetening

  1. 100 g granulated sugar (or jaggery for traditional sweetness)

Phase 3, aromatics

  1. 6 each green cardamom pods — (crushed in a mortar to release the seeds, pods discarded, just the seeds go in)
  2. 0.1 g saffron threads
  3. 5 ml rosewater (optional, traditional in some households)

Phase 4, garnish

  1. 25 g unsalted pistachios, slivered
  2. 25 g unsalted almonds, slivered or sliced
  3. 20 g cashews, chopped (optional)

Execution

  1. 1
    Steep the saffron. Place the 0.1 g saffron threads in a small bowl. Pour over 30 ml of milk warmed to roughly 60°C / 140°F (warm to the touch but not hot). Set aside; the saffron will release its color and aroma over the next 10 to 15 minutes while the kheer base cooks.
  2. 2
    Begin the reduction. Pour the 1500 ml / 6 cups whole milk into a heavy-bottomed 4-quart pot (a Dutch oven or thick-bottomed saucepan). Bring to a low simmer over medium heat (small bubbles forming around the edge, no rolling boil). The heavy bottom is structural, milk scorches readily on thin pans, and the burnt-milk taste cannot be removed once it forms.
  3. 3
    Add the rice. Add the 60 g rinsed and drained basmati. Stir to distribute. Reduce heat to medium-low (the surface should bubble gently). Stirring constantly with a silicone spatula or wooden spoon, scraping the bottom of the pot every 30 seconds to prevent the milk solids from sticking and scorching. Simmer 35 to 40 minutes; the milk will reduce by approximately one-third in this period and the rice will cook through and start to break down at the edges.
  4. 4
    Continue the reduction. After 35 to 40 minutes, the milk should have visibly reduced and the rice should be soft but still distinct. Continue simmering and stirring for another 20 to 25 minutes. The milk will continue reducing, the surface will develop a gentle skin that you fold back into the body (do not skim, that skin is concentrated milk solids and is the structural depth of kheer), and the rice will partially break down into the milk, thickening it. Pull when the volume has reduced by approximately 60% (from 1500 ml to about 600 ml of finished kheer base) and the consistency is that of a thick rice-and-cream porridge that just-coats the back of a spoon.
  5. 5
    Sweeten and aromatize. Add the 100 g sugar, the steeped saffron-milk (with the threads), and the cardamom seeds (crushed in step 3). Stir thoroughly. Simmer 3 to 4 minutes; the sugar will dissolve completely and the cardamom and saffron aromatics will integrate into the kheer. Taste; the sweetness should be balanced (kheer is meant to be moderately sweet, not aggressively sugary). Adjust if needed.
  6. 6
    Finish. Pull off heat. Stir in the 5 ml rosewater (if using). Add half the chopped pistachios, almonds, and cashews (if using); reserve half for garnish. Stir gently. Let stand 5 minutes off heat to let the kheer thicken slightly as it cools (kheer thickens noticeably as it cools; pull when the consistency is one notch thinner than your desired final texture).
  7. 7
    Plate and serve. Transfer to small individual bowls (kheer is rich and small portions are correct, 100 to 120 ml per bowl). Top each portion with the reserved chopped nuts. Serve warm, room temperature, or chilled per preference; many North Indian households serve kheer chilled in summer and warm in winter. Refrigerate any leftovers; kheer keeps 4 to 5 days and the flavor deepens overnight.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion sweetener→with jaggery (gur) instead of sugar

    Substitute 120 g jaggery (Indian unrefined cane sugar) for the 100 g granulated. Crush the jaggery into small pieces; do not add directly to the simmering milk (jaggery can curdle hot milk because of its acidic notes). Instead, dissolve the jaggery in 30 ml warm water, simmer 1 minute to make a syrup, cool slightly, then stir into the slightly-cooled kheer in step 5. Jaggery is the more traditional sweetener and adds a deeper caramel note; the dish reads more rustic-traditional and slightly more amber-colored.

  • Texture rice→with broken-rice (small-grain or risotto-style)

    Substitute 60 g Arborio or short-grain rice. The kheer comes out creamier (the higher starch content thickens more aggressively) and the grains break down more thoroughly. Some Bengali payesh recipes specifically use broken-rice for this texture. Reduce cooking time by 5 to 8 minutes (short-grain rice cooks faster).

  • Occasion tradition→sevai kheer (vermicelli kheer)

    Replace the rice with 60 g of broken vermicelli (sevai), the thin pasta-like noodles sold at Indian groceries. Toast the dry vermicelli in 1 tbsp ghee until golden-brown (3 to 4 minutes) before adding to the milk. Reduce cooking time to 25 to 30 minutes total (vermicelli cooks much faster than rice). The dish becomes sevai kheer, the more common version in many Pakistani households and at Eid celebrations.

  • Occasion tradition→sago kheer (sabudana kheer)

    Replace the rice with 60 g sago pearls (small tapioca pearls, sabudana). Soak sago in cold water 30 minutes before adding. Reduce cooking time to 20 minutes (sago cooks faster than rice and breaks into translucent pearls). Common during Hindu fasting (vrat) periods when grain is restricted; sago is permitted on most fast lists.

  • Occasion reduction shortcut→with evaporated milk (faster)

    Replace 500 ml of the whole milk with 1 can (354 ml) evaporated milk. Reduce total cooking time to 30 to 40 minutes (the evaporated milk is already partially reduced). The kheer reads slightly less complex than the long-reduced full whole-milk version but is acceptable for weeknight cooking. Many Pakistani households use this approach as a routine shortcut.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole milk → see ingredients/whole-milk substitutions Whole milk is structural to kheer. The long reduction is what produces the dish; reduced-fat or non-dairy milks produce notably different (lesser) results.
  • basmati rice → medium-grain or short-grain rice See modifications. Different texture but acceptable. Long-grain non-basmati rice produces a closer approximation than short-grain.
  • green cardamom pods → ground cardamom (use ½ tsp) See ingredients/green-cardamom. Acceptable; the dish reads slightly less aromatic but works.
  • saffron → a few drops of rose water + small pinch of turmeric for color Saffron is genuinely irreplaceable for the aroma. Skipping saffron loses depth but the kheer is still a complete dessert; use rose water at 5 to 8 ml as the aromatic note and accept that the dish is missing saffron.

Notes

Why HTST whole milk, not UHT. Ultra-pasteurized (UHT) whole milk is heated to 138°C / 280°F during processing, which partially denatures the casein and lactalbumin proteins. UHT milk reduces less effectively (the proteins are already partially altered) and produces a kheer that reads slightly thinner and slightly cooked-cardboard. Standard high-temperature short-time (HTST) pasteurized milk produces noticeably better kheer; the cooked-milk-solid notes that develop during reduction are the entire point, and they develop best from un-altered milk proteins. Read the carton; "ultra-pasteurized" or "UHT" is what to avoid for reduction-based sweets.

The reduction is the dish. Kheer is structurally a milk-reduction dessert with rice as the carbohydrate component. The 60% reduction is non-negotiable; cooks who shortcut to a 30% reduction produce thin, watery kheer. The Maillard-derived caramel notes that develop during the 60+ minute simmer are what give kheer its depth and its faintly sweet-roasted character; they develop only with sustained heat over time. Pressure cookers, microwaves, and other shortcuts produce kheer that is recognizably different and noticeably less good.

Cardamom timing. Adding the cardamom seeds at the start of the reduction would lose much of the aromatic to the long simmer; adding them at the end of the cook (step 5) preserves the volatile aromatics. The same logic applies to the rose water (added off-heat) and the saffron-steeped milk (added in step 5). The structural rule across South Asian sweets: aromatic spices and floral waters go in late.

Eating mechanics. Kheer is served at every Hindu and Muslim festival in North India and Pakistan: Diwali, Eid, weddings, anniversaries, Sunday family lunches. The portion size is small (100 to 120 ml per bowl) because the dish is rich and is part of a multi-course meal. Eaten with a spoon, often after a savory meal as the closing course. Refrigerated kheer sets up to a softer pudding-like texture that some prefer over warm-and-flowing; both are valid. The day-after kheer with the cardamom and saffron deepened overnight is widely considered better than fresh.

  • North Indian / Pakistani household tradition — Kheer (खीर, also called payasam in South India and payesh in Bengal) is among the oldest recorded Indian sweets, with references in ancient Sanskrit texts. The North Indian rice kheer is the version most familiar in the diaspora; South Indian payasam uses jaggery and varied bases (rice, vermicelli, lentils, sago); Bengali payesh leans heavier on milk and lighter on rice.
