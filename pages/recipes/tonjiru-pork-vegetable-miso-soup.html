Tonjiru (豚汁): pork and root-vegetable miso soup

The substantial winter miso soup of the Japanese household, built on a base of dashi (or homemade pork broth) and loaded with sliced pork belly, daikon, carrot, burdock, konnyaku, and shimeji mushrooms. The miso enters at the end, off-heat, as always. A meal-scale soup rather than a side: tonjiru with a bowl of rice and a quick pickle is one of the standard simple winter dinners across Japan, and the dish is also the canonical "morning after a hard work day" recovery meal.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Steamed Japanese short-grain rice is the canonical partner and must be on FIRST. Rinse 360 g / 1¾ cups rice three times, soak 30 minutes, then start in the cooker (or stovetop covered at a bare simmer 15 minutes, 10 minute rest off heat). The rice timeline of about 60 minutes overlaps the entire mise-and-cook so both arrive hot together.
  • Dashi must be ready before the pork browns. The recipe needs 1200 ml of room-temperature dashi at step 4; if you do not have made-ahead dashi, build a quick kombu-and-katsuobushi dashi at least 20 minutes ahead so it has time to cool to room temperature.
  • Pre-cut all the vegetables before any cooking starts. The active cook is brisk (about 30 minutes) and the burdock needs to soak 5 minutes in acidulated water immediately after shaving to prevent oxidation; trying to prep mid-cook produces oxidized burdock and overcooked pork.
  • The miso goes in OFF heat at the end and the pot never returns to the burner. Boiling miso destroys its volatile esters and produces a flat, salty-only bowl; this is the single most important rule of any miso soup.

Execution

  1. 1
    Prep all the vegetables before any cooking starts (the cook is brisk once the pork browns, and the mise should be ready). Peel the 200 g daikon and cut into icho-giri (quarter-moons): halve the round daikon lengthwise to make two half-cylinders, then halve each half-cylinder lengthwise again to make four quarter-rounds, then slice across into 5 mm-thick quarter-moons. Peel the 100 g carrot and cut into half-moons (halve the long round of carrot lengthwise, then slice into 5 mm half-rounds) or roll-cut for irregular wedges. Scrub (do not peel) the 80 g burdock root and shave into sasagaki: hold the burdock at one end like a pencil and run the knife blade against it at a low angle, rotating the root as you go, producing thin uneven flakes 2 to 3 cm long. Immediately drop the burdock shavings into a bowl of cold water with 1 tsp rice vinegar to stop oxidation, soak 5 minutes, drain. Parboil the 150 g konnyaku: drop the block into a small pot of boiling water for 60 seconds, drain, cool briefly, then tear by hand into rough 2 cm chunks (tearing is structural, the irregular surfaces grip more broth). Separate the 80 g shimeji into individual stems if using.
  2. 2
    Brown the pork. Heat a 4-quart heavy pot (Dutch oven, donabe, or any heavy stockpot) over medium-high heat. Add the 8 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame oil. When the oil is shimmering and just before it smokes, add the 300 g sliced pork belly in a single layer (if it doesn't fit, brown in two batches). Cook undisturbed for 60 to 90 seconds until the bottom face is deep mahogany, then stir and continue to cook for another 90 seconds. The pork should be about 70% cooked through with significant rendered fat in the pot bottom. The browning is structural for tonjiru flavor, an under-browned pork base produces a dish that reads pale and uninteresting; aim for visible Maillard color on most of the pork pieces. Maillard sear
  3. 3
    Add the root vegetables to the rendered fat. Without draining off the pork fat (it's a structural flavor element), add the 200 g daikon, the 100 g carrot, the 80 g drained burdock, and the 150 g konnyaku to the pot with the pork. Stir-fry over medium-high heat for 3 to 4 minutes; the vegetables will absorb some of the pork fat and the burdock and konnyaku will lightly brown at the edges. The daikon should still be hard, the burdock should look slightly translucent. This brief pre-fry is structural: it builds an additional flavor layer (vegetable-and-fat Maillard) on top of the pork-and-sesame-oil base and is what distinguishes tonjiru from a generic pork-and-vegetable soup.
  4. 4
    Add the dashi and simmer the vegetables tender. Pour in the 1200 ml / 5 cups dashi (room temperature dashi is fine; it will heat up with the hot pot contents). Bring to a low simmer over medium-high heat, then immediately reduce to medium-low to maintain a gentle, steady simmer (visible bubbling but not a rolling boil). Skim any foam that forms on the surface in the first 5 minutes with a spoon (the foam is rendered fat and impurities, removing it produces a clearer cleaner soup). Simmer covered for 15 to 18 minutes, until the daikon is tender enough to be easily pierced with a chopstick or fork tip but still holds its shape. The carrot and burdock will be tender at this point; the konnyaku is essentially unchanged from its starting texture (which is correct). The dashi will have reduced slightly and absorbed flavor from the pork and vegetables.
  5. 5
    Add the mushrooms in the last 3 minutes. If using the 80 g shimeji or maitake, add them to the simmering soup in the last 3 minutes of the cook. Mushrooms cook quickly and a longer simmer turns them rubbery; the brief late addition keeps them tender with structural integrity. Stir gently. After 3 minutes, the mushrooms should be wilted and tender. Taste the broth: it should read deeply savory from the dashi, pork, and rendered vegetable flavors; the salt level should be moderate (the miso adds significant additional salt at the next step). Adjust dashi if the soup has reduced too much (add 100 ml hot water).
  6. 6
    Add the miso off-heat. Pull the pot completely off the burner. Place a small fine-mesh sieve into the soup (a tea strainer or any small sieve works) and add the 100 g miso into the sieve. With the back of a spoon or whisk, press the miso through the sieve while moving the sieve in slow circles through the broth; the sieve breaks up clumps and dissolves the miso evenly. Alternatively, ladle 120 ml of hot broth into a small bowl, whisk the miso into the bowl until smooth, then pour back into the pot. The broth transitions from clear-amber-with-vegetables to a deep cloudy-brown with the miso fully integrated. Do not return the pot to heat under any circumstances. The off-heat rule is the most important rule of any miso soup, boiling miso destroys its volatile esters and produces a flat, salty-only bowl.
  7. 7
    Plate and serve. Ladle into 4 large bowls (about 400 ml each), distributing the pork, vegetables, and konnyaku evenly. Scatter the 30 g sliced scallion greens over each bowl. Pass the shichimi togarashi at the table for each diner to add to taste (the canonical move is a small pinch per bowl); some Japanese cooks add the shichimi directly to each bowl in the kitchen. Serve immediately with a bowl of plain Japanese rice on the side (the rice is essential, tonjiru is built to be eaten alongside rice rather than alone), and optionally a small pickle (tsukemono, takuan, or a quick-pickled cucumber). Reheating tonjiru to a boil ruins the miso character; if you have leftovers, gently re-warm to about 75°C in a covered pot, never re-boil.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional regional variation→Hokkaido-style tonjiru with butter and corn

    After the miso is added, swirl in 15 g / 1 tbsp unsalted butter and add 80 g sweet corn kernels (fresh or frozen). The butter-and-corn addition is the Hokkaido signature; the cold-climate region's dairy-and-corn-friendly cooking informs the local tonjiru, producing a richer, sweeter, more visually colorful dish than the central or southern Japanese versions. Some Hokkaido cooks also add a splash of milk or a dollop of butter directly to each individual bowl.

  • Dietary pork-free version→vegetarian kenchinjiru-leaning version

    Omit the pork and pork-browning step. Increase the sesame oil to 15 g / 1 tbsp and use it to stir-fry the root vegetables before adding the dashi (this is the canonical kenchinjiru opening). Add 100 g cubed firm tofu (drained and pressed) and 60 g rehydrated dried shiitake (sliced) to the soup with the root vegetables; the shiitake soaking liquid (60 ml) should replace an equal volume of dashi. The dish is now adjacent to kenchinjiru (the Zen Buddhist temple soup); see the kenchinjiru recipe on this site for the full temple-cuisine treatment.

  • Occasion extra-rich version→tonjiru with cooked udon noodles

    After the miso is added, add 200 g cooked udon noodles (fresh or frozen; cook per package directions in a separate pot of boiling water, drain, add to the soup). The dish becomes miso-udon-tonjiru, a substantial one-bowl meal. Each diner gets a slightly smaller portion of soup (250 ml plus the noodles) but with substantially more carbs. This is the standard Japanese izakaya or casual-restaurant treatment for tonjiru when served as a main course.

  • Occasion pressure-cooker shortcut→electric or stovetop pressure cooker

    After browning the pork and stir-frying the vegetables, transfer everything to a pressure cooker, add the dashi, and pressure-cook on high pressure for 10 minutes (Instant Pot) or 8 minutes (stovetop pressure cooker), then natural release for 10 minutes. The pressure cook produces fully tender root vegetables in less than half the time of stovetop simmering, with similar (perhaps slightly more flat) flavor. Add the mushrooms and miso after pressure release, the mushrooms get 3 minutes simmering with the pot uncovered, the miso is added off-heat as always.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • pork belly, sliced thin → pork shoulder slices, pork loin slices, or chicken thigh slices Pork shoulder is the leaner pork substitute; the dish reads less rich but still recognizable. Pork loin is leaner still and dries out under the brown-and-simmer technique; not recommended. Chicken thigh slices produce a "torijiru" (chicken soup) variant that is a recognized dish in its own right; the dish reads cleaner and lighter, season slightly more aggressively with the miso since chicken contributes less fat to the broth.
  • kombu-katsuobushi dashi → hondashi (instant), shiitake-kombu vegetarian dashi, light chicken stock Hondashi (1 tbsp per 1200 ml hot water) is the everyday Japanese household shortcut and is acceptable for tonjiru where the many other flavors mask the simpler dashi character. Shiitake-kombu dashi makes the dish vegetarian-base if the pork is also swapped. Light chicken stock works as a Western-pantry substitute; the dish reads slightly less Japanese but is fine.
  • daikon → turnip, kohlrabi, or icicle radish Turnip (white turnip) is the closest Western root substitute; cooks tender in the same time, slightly more peppery and less sweet. Kohlrabi (the swollen-stem brassica) works in similar size and cook time. Icicle radish or watermelon radish are alternative root crops at some farmers markets.
  • burdock root (gobō) → salsify (oyster plant), parsnip, or omit Salsify is the closest Western substitute (similar fiber profile, similar oxidation behavior); produces a recognizable tonjiru. Parsnip is sweeter and less earthy, less canonical. Omitting is acceptable but the dish loses a structural flavor element; consider adding 20 g extra of dried shiitake during the simmer to compensate for the lost depth.
  • konnyaku → firm tofu cubes, or omit Firm tofu cubes (1.5 cm cubes, drained) provide a protein-block element but lack the bouncy chew; the dish reads recognizable but missing konnyaku's distinctive bite. Omitting is acceptable; the soup loses a textural component but is still a complete tonjiru.
  • shimeji or maitake mushrooms → fresh shiitake, oyster mushroom, or omit Fresh shiitake (sliced) is the supermarket-available substitute and works in any tonjiru. Oyster mushroom (Pleurotus) provides a meatier texture, slightly less Japanese-canonical. Omitting is acceptable but the dish loses an umami source; consider increasing the miso to 110 g to compensate.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → kombu-katsuobushi dashi (ichiban or niban) make it yourself Homemade dashi takes 20 minutes from cold water to strained dashi and is dramatically better than any instant or store-bought version. For tonjiru specifically, niban dashi (the second extraction from spent ichiban kombu and katsuobushi, made by simmering them in fresh water for 8 to 10 minutes) is the canonical household choice and is essentially free if you're making ichiban dashi anyway. The dish forgives less-perfect dashi than the everyday miso soup does because of all the other flavor sources, but a fresh dashi base still produces a meaningfully better bowl.

Notes

Why tonjiru is structurally different from the everyday miso soup. The standard Japanese household miso soup (tofu, wakame, scallion) is a delicate side dish, sipped between bites of rice and other dishes, made in 10 minutes, sized at 200 ml per bowl. Tonjiru is a different category of dish: a meal-scale soup with substantial protein and vegetable content, 400 ml per bowl, that takes 50 minutes including prep, and that is the centerpiece of a casual meal rather than a side. The structural differences are: (1) the pork is browned first to build Maillard flavor (no other miso soup does this), (2) the root vegetables get a 15-minute simmer to soften (the everyday soup has no simmering vegetables), (3) the miso is roughly double the everyday ratio per liter, (4) the bowl is larger and the diner is expected to spoon-eat rather than sip. The dish is not a "fancy" miso soup, it is a fundamentally different preparation that happens to use miso as a seasoning, the way American chowder and consommé both use stock but are different categories.

Why sliced pork belly. The thin-sliced pork belly (3 mm thick, 4 cm long) is structural for several reasons. (1) Thin slices brown rapidly without burning, producing the Maillard layer that anchors the broth flavor. (2) The thin slices render their fat into the pot in 2 to 3 minutes, where thicker cuts would still be releasing fat after 10 minutes (over-fattying the broth). (3) The thin slices cook through to safe internal temperature during the brief sear plus the 18-minute simmer, with no risk of toughness. (4) The shape eats easily with chopsticks alongside the diced vegetables, the dish is built for chopstick eating with a spoon for the broth, and thin slices fit that format. Korean BBQ "samgyeopsal" pork belly slices from any Asian market are perfect for this dish; American supermarket "thick-cut bacon" is too thick and too cured (the salt and smoke profile is wrong for Japanese cooking). If only thicker pork belly is available, freeze it for 30 minutes to firm up, then slice as thin as possible.

The browning step matters. Many household tonjiru recipes skip the browning, simply adding raw pork to the simmering dashi. This produces a recognizable tonjiru but a flat one; the Maillard browning step is what distinguishes a great tonjiru from a generic one. The sesame oil contributes its own aromatic on top of the rendered pork fat, and the sequence (oil, hot pork, rendered fat, vegetables in the rendered fat) produces a layered flavor base that pure-simmering cannot match. Some traditional cookbooks omit the browning to keep the soup clear; this is regional and contested, and the modern household tradition (especially in colder northern Japan) has settled firmly on the browning approach for the deeper flavor.

Sasagaki vs julienne for burdock. The canonical Japanese cut for burdock in tonjiru and kinpira is sasagaki: thin, irregular, wood-shaving-like flakes produced by holding the burdock like a pencil and "sharpening" it with a knife at a low angle, rotating as you shave. The technique produces uneven sloped surfaces that grip broth and absorb seasoning more aggressively than uniform julienned matchsticks. American cooks unfamiliar with the technique often substitute julienne, which is acceptable but produces a more uniform texture and a slightly less complex eating experience. The first few times you sasagaki burdock the technique feels awkward; after a 20-second cut it becomes natural. A julienne peeler or a sharp Y-peeler is acceptable as a shortcut, producing shavings that are similar to sasagaki.

Konnyaku tearing vs cutting. The traditional Japanese household instruction for konnyaku in tonjiru is to tear it by hand rather than cut it with a knife. The tearing produces irregular surfaces with more surface area, which absorb more broth flavor; the cleanly cut surfaces of knife-cut konnyaku stay flavor-poor. The difference is meaningful enough that a Japanese grandmother would scold a grandchild for using a knife on the konnyaku for tonjiru. Parboil first (the 60-second aku-nuki to remove the alkaline odor), drain, cool briefly, then tear into 2 to 3 cm chunks with the fingers.

The double-miso ratio. Tonjiru uses about 100 g of miso per 1200 ml of broth (8.3% by weight), where the everyday miso soup uses about 60 g per 800 ml (7.5% by weight) - tonjiru is moderately more concentrated. The reason is the dish''s much higher solids content: with 600+ g of pork, vegetables, and konnyaku in the pot, the broth is the seasoning vehicle for the solids and needs to be more intensely seasoned to come through against the mass. Reduce the miso to 75 g for a less assertive soup; increase to 120 g for a more aggressive Hokkaido style.

The leftover question. Tonjiru is one of the few miso soups that actually improves on the second day, with one caveat. The first day, the dish is at peak: fresh dashi, fresh miso, brightest seasoning. Refrigerated overnight, the dashi-and-vegetable flavors integrate further into the pork and the konnyaku, producing a deeper and more harmonious eating experience. The caveat is that the miso has lost some of its volatile esters during storage; the dish is more complex but slightly less vivid in miso character. The right reheat is gentle (medium-low heat, covered, stirring once or twice) up to about 75 to 80°C, never to a boil. Some Japanese cooks add a small additional pinch of fresh miso to the reheated soup just before serving to restore the bright miso character. Leftovers keep refrigerated for 3 to 4 days; the dish freezes acceptably for 1 to 2 months but the konnyaku and the vegetables degrade in texture, so freezing is the last-resort option.

Make-ahead and entertaining. The full dish (through phase 5, before the miso) can be made up to 24 hours ahead and refrigerated. The miso goes in just before serving, after a gentle reheat to about 75°C. The scallion and shichimi are added at the bowl as always. This is the right strategy for entertaining, where tonjiru is a substantial winter-dinner-party soup that can be the centerpiece of a casual gathering.

The shichimi togarashi finish. Shichimi togarashi is a Japanese seven-spice blend: red chili pepper, sansho (Japanese mountain pepper), citrus peel (dried), white sesame, black sesame, hemp seed, and nori (seaweed). It''s available at every Japanese grocery and most American supermarkets'' international aisles (S&B brand is the most common). For tonjiru, a small pinch per bowl is the canonical finish, contributing a subtle heat and a complex aromatic that lifts the soup. The aroma is the main contribution, not the heat (the chili content is moderate); avoid heavy pinches that would dominate the dish. Some Japanese cooks prefer ichimi togarashi (single-spice red chili powder, simpler and hotter) instead of shichimi; both are correct. Skipping the spice entirely is acceptable, the dish is fine without, but a Japanese diner would expect the option to be on the table.

Sources
  • Adapted from the Japanese household tradition of tonjiru / butajiru — Tonjiru (豚汁, literally "pork soup") is also called butajiru in some regions, the Japanese language uses both readings for the same compound. The dish is a 20th-century development that combines the older Japanese tradition of miso-based vegetable soups (kenchinjiru, suiton) with the post-Meiji incorporation of pork into Japanese cooking. The structural innovations vs other miso soups are: the pork is browned first to build Maillard flavor before the broth is added, the cook time is much longer (20 to 30 minutes vs 5 to 10 for the everyday miso soup) to soften the root vegetables, and the miso amount per liter of broth is roughly double the everyday bowl, producing a more concentrated and heartier soup. The dish is regional in some details (Hokkaido tonjiru uses a stronger red miso and may include butter; Kansai tonjiru leans more toward a delicate awase miso; Tokyo-style is in the middle), but the basic structure is universal.
