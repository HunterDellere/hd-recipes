Roasted pear and gorgonzola tartine

Bosc pear roasted in butter and balsamic until caramelized, smeared over a toasted sourdough slice with gorgonzola dolce, scattered with toasted walnuts, finished with thyme, balsamic, and cracked pepper. Open-faced lunch or light dinner.

2servings2 tartines; serves 2 as lunch or 4 as a small first-course

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat oven to 200°C / 400°F with a rack in the middle. Toast the 30 g walnut halves on a small sheet pan for 6 to 8 minutes until fragrant and lightly darkened; transfer to a plate to cool. (If you skip toasting, the walnuts read flat against the rich gorgonzola; the 6 minutes earns its time.)
  2. 2
    Prep the pear. Halve the 200 g pear lengthwise, scoop out the seed core with a melon baller or small spoon, and slice each half into 4 mm wedges (you should get 12 to 16 wedges total). Cutting too thick produces wedges that need long cooking and lose their shape; too thin and they collapse during the roast.
  3. 3
    Roast the pear. In a small oven-safe skillet or a baking dish, melt the 15 g / 1 tbsp butter over medium heat until lightly foamy. Add the pear wedges in a single layer, cut-side down. Drizzle with the 8 ml / ½ tbsp balsamic vinegar, sprinkle with 1 g / ½ tsp thyme, 0.5 g salt, and 0.5 g / ¼ tsp pepper. Cook on the stovetop 2 minutes (the cut faces start to caramelize), then transfer the skillet to the oven. Roast 12 to 14 minutes, until the pear is tender, the cut faces are deeply caramelized, and the butter-balsamic in the pan has reduced to a glaze.
  4. 4
    Toast the bread. While the pear roasts, butter both sides of the 2 sourdough slices with the 10 g / 2 tsp soft butter. Toast in a wide skillet over medium heat for 90 seconds per side, until both sides are deeply golden and crisp. (Alternative: toast under a broiler 60 seconds per side.) Transfer to plates.
  5. 5
    Assemble. Spread each toast with 30 g / 1 oz gorgonzola dolce, going all the way to the edges. Arrange 6 to 8 roasted pear wedges across each tartine, drizzle with any pan glaze from the skillet, scatter the toasted walnuts (broken roughly in halves), and add the optional arugula if using.
  6. 6
    Finish and serve. Drizzle each tartine with 2.5 ml / ½ tsp aged balsamic vinegar, sprinkle 0.5 g / ¼ tsp thyme leaves, and finish with a pinch of flaky sea salt and an extra crack of black pepper. Serve immediately while the pear is warm and the gorgonzola has softened against it.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion cheese choice→gorgonzola piccante (sharp aged version)

    Use 50 g gorgonzola piccante, crumbled (not spread; piccante does not spread cleanly). Distribute crumbles across each toast before adding pear. The dish reads sharper and more complex; the sweet roasted pear has more cheese flavor to balance against. Reduce the finishing balsamic by half (the cheese already provides more mineral edge).

  • Protein protein→with prosciutto

    Drape 30 g prosciutto di Parma over each tartine after the pear and before the walnuts. The salt-fat-sweet triangle (cured pork + sweet pear + salty cheese) is the canonical Italian fall-cheese-board condensed onto a slice of bread. Skip the arugula; the prosciutto provides the salt-savory note that arugula otherwise carries.

  • Occasion preparation style→cold version (pear sliced raw)

    Skip the roast entirely. Slice the just-ripe Bosc pear into 4 mm wedges, brush with 5 ml lemon juice to prevent browning. Toast the bread, spread with gorgonzola, layer raw pear, walnuts, balsamic, thyme, salt, pepper. Lighter, faster, summer-leaning. The flavor is brighter; the warm-and-melted character of the original is replaced by fresh-and-crisp.

  • Occasion spread→with honey instead of balsamic

    Drizzle the finishing layer with 5 ml / 1 tsp wild honey (chestnut, buckwheat, or a darker varietal) instead of balsamic. The dish reads sweeter and more dessert-leaning; pairs well with afternoon tea or a Sauternes-style sweet wine. Skip the thyme and use rosemary instead for a more aromatic register.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • Bosc pear → Anjou pear Closest cooking behavior. Less buttery in the roast but holds shape similarly. Use just-ripe.
  • gorgonzola dolce → Cambozola or a soft brie + a crumble of sharp blue Cambozola is the closest single-cheese substitute. The split (brie for spreadability, blue for character) approximates dolce's combination.
  • sourdough → crusty country bread (pain de campagne, ciabatta) Any rustic country-style bread with a chewy crumb works. White sandwich bread does not, the structural integrity is part of the dish.
  • walnuts → pecans or hazelnuts Pecans read sweeter and softer; hazelnuts read more aromatic. Both work; walnut is the canonical pairing with pear-gorgonzola.
  • aged balsamic → balsamic glaze or balsamic reduction Aged balsamic is syrupy and sweet-tart; if unavailable, reduce 100 ml regular balsamic vinegar over medium heat with 5 g sugar until syrupy (about 15 minutes), cool, and use as you would the aged.

Notes

Why Bosc and not Bartlett. Bosc holds shape under heat; Bartlett collapses into mush within 15 minutes of roasting. The dish needs visible pear-wedge structure for the visual identity (and so the pear-and-walnut bites have textural definition); Bartlett kills both. Anjou is acceptable; Comice is too soft to roast.

Dolce vs piccante. Gorgonzola dolce is the right choice for the warm-tartine format because it spreads cleanly onto warm toast and softens further as it meets the warm pear, melting into the bread. Piccante crumbles rather than spreads and is the right choice when you want sharp salt-and-mineral pops against the sweet pear; that is the modification, not the default. The default tartine (gorgonzola dolce, warm) reads as comfortable and rich; the piccante version reads as sharper and more deliberate.

The five-ingredient logic. Pear, gorgonzola, walnut, balsamic, thyme. Each ingredient does specific work and removing one drops the dish: pear is the sweetness and structural fruit; gorgonzola is the salty fatty mineral umami; walnut is the texture and bitter-tannin counter; balsamic is the acid and sweetness amplifier; thyme is the green-aromatic top note. Pepper and salt are seasonings, not ingredients in the same sense. Arugula is optional but often improves the dish by adding a peppery green that bridges thyme and walnut.

Eating mechanics. Open-faced; eat with knife and fork rather than picking up. The pear is hot and the cheese has melted partway; the walnuts and arugula stay distinct. Serve as a lunch with a glass of red wine (Chianti, Barbera) or a cool aromatic white (Pinot Grigio, Gewürztraminer); as a first course before pasta or risotto; or as an autumn weeknight dinner with a simple green salad. Best made and eaten within 5 minutes; the toast goes soft as the gorgonzola and pear juices soak in.
