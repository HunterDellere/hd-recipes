White bean and tuna salad

The Tuscan fagioli e tonno: creamy cannellini, good oil-packed tuna left in meaty flakes, raw red onion bled of its harshness in acid, parsley, capers, and a lemon-and-red-wine-vinegar dressing built directly on the beans. A no-cook pantry assembly whose entire quality lives in the grade of the tuna, the texture of the beans, and the order in which the acid goes on.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Soak the sliced red onion in the red wine vinegar for at least 15 minutes before the salad comes together. Raw red onion dropped straight into the bowl reads sulfurous and sharp; the acid soak leaches the harsh thiosulfinates, turns the slices pink, and the vinegar it leaves behind becomes the acid backbone of the dressing. This is the one passive step, so start it first.
  • Drain the canned beans and let them sit in warm tap water for a minute, then drain again. The canning liquor is salty and faintly slimy; rinsing it off lets you control the seasoning from zero. Pat the beans dry on a towel so the dressing clings rather than slides.
  • Do not drain the tuna over the sink. Pour the oil it is packed in into the dressing; good tuna oil is the most flavorful fat in the dish and discarding it throws away the umami you paid for.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the onion soak

  1. 70 g red onion, halved and sliced pole-to-pole into thin half-moons, 2 mm — (½ medium onion; pole-to-pole slices hold their shape and bleed evenly in the acid)
  2. 30 g red wine vinegar

Phase 2, the salad body

  1. 480 g cannellini beans, cooked or canned, drained, rinsed in warm water, patted dry
  2. 200 g oil-packed tuna (yellowfin or ventresca in olive oil), lifted out in large meaty flakes, oil reserved
  3. 60 g celery, inner pale stalks with leaves, thin-sliced on a slight bias, 2 mm; leaves chopped and reserved — (2 inner stalks; the pale heart stalks are tender and sweet, the dark outer ribs are stringy and bitter)
  4. 15 g flat-leaf parsley, leaves picked, roughly chopped
  5. 15 g capers in brine or salt-packed, drained; if salt-packed, rinsed and soaked 10 minutes

Phase 3, the dressing

  1. 4 g oil-cured anchovy fillet, minced to a paste with the side of a knife — (1 fillet; the glutamate source, it dissolves invisibly into the dressing and reads as depth, not fish)
  2. 3 g garlic, grated to a paste on a microplane — (1 small clove; raw and minimal, a background note not a front)
  3. 5 g dijon mustard
  4. 20 g reserved tuna packing oil
  5. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  6. 15 g lemon, juiced; zest reserved — (juice of ¼ lemon plus a little zest; the bright finishing acid that the vinegar alone cannot supply)
  7. 3 g fine sea salt
  8. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Execution

  1. 1
    Start the onion. Put the 70 g / ½ red onion, sliced into thin half-moons, in a small bowl with the 30 g / 2 tbsp red wine vinegar. Toss to coat and leave to sit 15 to 20 minutes. The acid pulls the harsh thiosulfinates out of the raw onion, softening the bite and turning the slices bright pink. Both the onion and the vinegar it has been sitting in go into the salad later, so nothing is discarded.
  2. 2
    Prepare the beans. Drain the 480 g / two cans cannellini beans into a colander and rinse under warm tap water for 20 seconds to wash off the salty, faintly viscous canning liquor. Let them drain fully, then turn them onto a clean towel and pat dry. Dry beans take a dressing; wet beans dilute it. Handle gently, cannellini have a thin skin and crush easily, and the dish wants whole intact beans, not a mash.
  3. 3
    Flake the tuna. Lift the 200 g / two jars oil-packed tuna out of its oil in large meaty flakes with a fork, letting the pieces stay roughly 2 cm. Pour the reserved tuna oil into your dressing bowl, it is the single most flavorful fat here. Leave the flakes large; tuna shredded to fine threads turns the salad pasty and loses the textural contrast of bean against fish.
  4. 4
    Build the dressing. In the bowl with the reserved tuna oil, mash the 4 g / 1 anchovy fillet and the 3 g / 1 grated garlic clove together until the anchovy dissolves into a paste. Whisk in the 5 g / 1 tsp dijon mustard, then the vinegar drained off the soaking onion (set the onion slices aside), the 15 g / juice of ¼ lemon with a little zest, and the 30 g / 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil. Whisk hard until it emulsifies into a thick, glossy dressing. Taste: it should read sharp and savory, more aggressive than you want the final salad, because the beans will mute it.
  5. 5
    Dress the beans first. Add the dried cannellini beans to the dressing and fold gently with a wooden spoon, coating every bean. Let them sit 5 minutes. Beans are bland and absorbent; dressing them alone and early lets the bean interior take on the acid and salt from the inside, which is the difference between seasoned beans and beans sitting in a puddle of dressing. Season here with about half the 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt and taste.
  6. 6
    Fold in the rest. Add the drained pink onion slices, the 60 g / 2 stalks sliced celery with its chopped leaves, the 15 g / ½ cup chopped parsley, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp drained capers. Fold once or twice to combine. Then add the 200 g flaked tuna last and fold only enough to distribute, two or three gentle turns; folding tuna in early breaks the flakes down into paste.
  7. 7
    Adjust and rest. Crack the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper over the top. Taste and correct: it likely needs the rest of the fine sea salt and possibly a second squeeze of lemon, because the beans pull seasoning inward as they sit. The finished salad should taste bright, briny, and savory in that order, with the anchovy invisible and the lemon clearly present. Let it sit 10 minutes at room temperature before serving so the flavors marry; do not refrigerate, cold flattens the olive oil and the tuna both. Serve with grilled or torn crusty bread to mop the dressing that pools at the bottom of the bowl.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein make it a fuller meal→add jammy egg and salt-cured tomato

    Quarter two 7-minute jammy eggs and lay them over the dressed salad. Salt-cure 150 g / 1 cup halved cherry tomatoes on a rack 20 minutes to shed water, then fold them in at the end with the tuna. This pulls the dish toward the niçoise register without becoming a composed salad; it stays a tossed bowl.

  • Texture cooler-weather version→warm white bean and tuna

    Warm the rinsed beans in a wide skillet with 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil and the grated garlic over low heat for 3 minutes, just to take the chill off, then build the salad against the warm beans. The warmth opens the olive oil aromatics and the beans drink the dressing faster. Hold the tuna and herbs until off the heat so they do not cook.

  • Regional fennel-forward Sicilian lean→shaved fennel and orange

    Replace the celery with 80 g / ½ bulb fennel shaved paper-thin on a mandoline, and add the segments of half a blood orange. The fennel brings an anise sweetness and the orange a softer acid that reads more southern-Italian than the lemon-and-vinegar Tuscan base. Cut the red wine vinegar to 20 g / 1 tbsp so the orange is not overwhelmed.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • oil-packed tuna → oil-packed albacore, bonito (palamita), or good canned salmon Albacore is paler and milder with a firmer flake; bonito is darker and more intense and reads more traditionally Mediterranean; canned wild salmon makes a different but legitimate bean salad. Avoid water-packed tuna entirely, it is dry, breaks to shreds, and brings none of the flavorful oil the dressing depends on.
  • cannellini beans → great northern beans, butter beans, or chickpeas Great northern are slightly firmer and less creamy but the closest swap. Large butter beans (lima) give a plush, almost custardy bite and a more rustic plate. Chickpeas change the dish into something nuttier and firmer, good but no longer fagioli e tonno; if using them, lean harder on the lemon.
  • red wine vinegar → white wine vinegar, sherry vinegar, or champagne vinegar All sit in the same bright wine-vinegar register. Sherry vinegar is the most characterful and pushes the dish toward the Spanish side of the Mediterranean. Avoid balsamic, its sweetness and dark color muddy a salad that wants to read clean and bright.
  • oil-cured anchovy fillet → colatura di alici, fish sauce, or a few drops of Worcestershire Colatura (Italian anchovy extract) is the most authentic, 5 g / 1 tsp dissolved into the dressing. A scant 3 g / ½ tsp fish sauce gives the same glutamate hit. The anchovy is the umami source and is not optional; skipping it leaves the salad tasting thin even when everything else is right.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → cannellini beans, cooked or canned make it yourselfstub A pot of cannellini cooked from dried at a bare tremor in salted, aromatic water gives a creamier interior and an intact, unbroken skin that canned beans rarely match, plus you control the salt from zero. The bean liquor is a usable bonus. For a dish this minimal, where the bean is half the plate, home-cooked beans are the single biggest upgrade available.

Notes

Why this is not a niçoise. The two dishes share oil and fish and a bright dressing, but their logic is opposite. Niçoise is a composed salad, every component plated separately and salted on its own terms, eaten at room temperature as an arrangement. Fagioli e tonno is a tossed bowl built around the bean: everything goes in one dish, the beans are the structural mass, and the tuna is a flavoring more than a centerpiece. Niçoise is French and Provençal; fagioli e tonno is Tuscan, the everyday antipasto of the bean-cooking heartland of central Italy. Do not garnish this one to look composed; it should look like what it is, a generous rustic bowl.

The tuna is the whole game. This dish has three ingredients doing real work and no cooking to mask a weak one, so the tuna grade is decisive. Good oil-packed tuna (Spanish or Italian ventresca, the fatty belly, or a solid yellowfin in olive oil) comes out in large, moist, savory flakes and carries an oil worth keeping. Cheap water-packed tuna comes out dry, gray, and shredded, with packing water that tastes of nothing. The price gap is real and so is the difference on the plate. If the only good tuna you can find is expensive, buy less of it and stretch the beans; a smaller amount of excellent tuna beats a larger amount of bad.

The acid sequencing is the technique. There are two acids and they do different jobs at different times. The red wine vinegar goes on the onion first, where it strips the raw harshness and stains the slices pink, then carries forward into the dressing as the base acid. The lemon goes in at the end as the bright top note, because lemon's volatile aromatics fade if they sit, while vinegar's deeper acid holds. Dressing the beans alone before anything else goes in is the move most home versions skip; bland absorbent beans need to take the seasoning from the inside, and a bean dressed in isolation for five minutes tastes seasoned all the way through rather than coated.

Why no cheese. The Tuscan original carries no cheese, and the dish is better for it. Parmesan or pecorino would compete with the anchovy for the umami slot and weigh down a salad whose appeal is brightness and clean bean texture. The glutamate here comes from the anchovy, the capers, and the tuna itself, which is plenty. If you want richness, add more good olive oil, not cheese.
