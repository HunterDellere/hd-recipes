Oyster sauce (homemade)

Fresh oysters simmered with their liquor, soy sauce, sugar, and aromatics, then strained and reduced into a glossy dark sauce. The Cantonese flavor base built from scratch — fresher and significantly more complex than commercial bottles. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 7 items ▾
  1. 500 g fresh shucked oysters with their liquor — (about 24 medium oysters; the liquor is structural — do not drain)
  2. 500 ml water
  3. 60 ml light soy sauce
  4. 15 ml dark soy sauce
  5. 25 g rock sugar (or granulated sugar)
  6. 4 g cornstarch
  7. 30 ml cold water (for slurry)

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: combine the oysters with their liquor and 500 ml / 2 cups water in a heavy saucepan. Bring to a bare simmer over medium heat (do not boil — boiling extracts bitter compounds). Simmer 45 minutes; the oysters will fall apart and release their flavor into the liquid.
  2. 2
    Stub: strain through a fine-mesh sieve lined with cheesecloth into a clean saucepan, pressing on the solids to extract every drop. Discard the spent oyster solids (or reserve for a separate use; they have given everything they will).
  3. 3
    Stub: add the 60 ml light soy, 15 ml dark soy, and 25 g rock sugar to the strained liquid. Bring to a low simmer; reduce by about one-third (to about 250 ml / 1 cup) over 30 to 40 minutes. The sauce should darken to a deep glossy brown.
  4. 4
    Stub: whisk the 4 g cornstarch with 30 ml cold water and stream into the simmering sauce; cook 60 seconds while stirring until glossy and slightly thickened (the sauce should coat the back of a spoon). Pull off heat. Cool to room temperature, then transfer to a sterilized glass jar. Refrigerate.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the oyster-quality requirements (which oyster varieties produce the best sauce — Pacific vs Atlantic, brininess profile, where to source), the chemistry of the long simmer (why slow simmer rather than aggressive reduction, the role of the oyster liquor in structural umami, why dark soy specifically), the comparison to commercial Lee Kum Kee (which uses oyster extract powder + caramel coloring + MSG and tastes flatter), the storage and shelf life (3 to 4 weeks refrigerated), and the practical note that great commercial oyster sauce (Megachef, Lee Kum Kee Premium) is genuinely good and the homemade project is more about the experience and the ability to control sweetness than the kitchen need.
