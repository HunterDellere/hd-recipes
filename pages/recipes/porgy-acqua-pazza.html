Porgy acqua pazza

A whole porgy poached in acqua pazza, the Neapolitan "crazy water": a shallow fast pan-broth of blistered cherry tomatoes, smashed garlic, dry white wine, water, and good olive oil, bloomed anchovy and chili for depth, the fish poaching only 8 to 12 minutes while its head and bones lend the liquid body. The broth becomes a light brodo spooned over the fish and mopped with grilled bread.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The fish is whole and poaches fast. Build and reduce the broth FIRST so it is simmering and seasoned before the porgy ever goes in; the fish poaches only 8 to 12 minutes by thickness and there is no time to fix a flat broth once it is in the pan. Have the porgy scaled, gutted, gills removed, rinsed, and patted dry, sitting at room temperature, before you light the burner.
  • Grill the bread BEFORE the fish goes into the broth, not after. The brodo is the point of this dish and you want toasted bread ready to mop it the moment the fish is plated; bread grilled after the fish means the fish sits and cools while the broth goes lukewarm. Toast it, rub it with the cut garlic, and have it on the table.
  • Use a lidded pan wide enough to hold the whole fish flat with the broth coming up its sides, a 30 cm / 12-inch sauté pan or a shallow braiser. A pan too small forces you to curl or break the fish; a pan too wide spreads the broth too thin to poach in.

  2. 18 g garlic cloves, 4 cloves, peeled and smashed flat with the side of a knife, left whole — (4 large cloves)
  3. 1 g red pepper flakes
  4. 8 g oil-packed anchovy fillets, 2 fillets, blotted of their oil — (2 fillets; the savory anchor, they melt invisibly into the broth)
  5. 250 g cherry tomatoes, half left whole, half halved, to give both burst pockets of juice and intact blistered skins

Phase 2, the broth (liquid and acid)

  1. 120 g dry white wine
  2. 240 g water, hot from the kettle
  3. 12 g flat-leaf parsley, stems and leaves separated; stems bruised and added to the broth, leaves roughly chopped for finishing — (½ small bunch; stems in the broth for depth, leaves at the end for lift)

Phase 3, finish (acid and aromatics)

  1. 30 g lemon, half the lemon cut into wedges for the table; the other half reserved for a squeeze over the broth at the end — (½ lemon for finishing, ½ for the table)
  2. 15 g extra-virgin olive oil, for finishing

To serve

  1. 160 g sourdough or country bread, for grilling, 4 thick slices, grilled or toasted, one cut clove of garlic rubbed over the hot surface — (4 slices; the brodo is the point, the bread is how you eat it)

Execution

  1. 1
    Prep the porgy. Confirm the fishmonger scaled, gutted, and removed the gills; rinse the 650 g / 1 whole porgy inside and out and scrape any dark blood line from the spine cavity with the back of a small spoon (the blood line is the strongest source of fishy flavor). Pat completely dry inside and out. Cut two shallow diagonal slashes 1 cm deep into the thickest part of each side; the slashes let heat and broth reach the deep flesh evenly and give a doneness cue, since the flesh inside them opaques first. Rub the 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt over the skin, into the slashes, and inside the cavity. Set the fish aside at room temperature while you build the broth.
  2. 2
    Grill the bread now, before the fish cooks. Toast the 160 g / 4 slices sourdough on a grill pan, under a broiler, or in a dry skillet until charred at the edges and crisp; rub one cut garlic clove (taken from the 18 g / 4 cloves below, or a spare) over each hot slice. Set the bread on the table. It needs to be ready the moment the fish is plated so it can mop the hot brodo.
  3. 3
    Bloom the aromatics. In a wide lidded sauté pan or shallow braiser over medium, warm the 45 g / 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil. Add the 18 g / 4 smashed garlic cloves and the 1 g / ½ tsp red pepper flakes; cook 60 to 90 seconds until the garlic is fragrant and just beginning to color at the edges, not browned (browned garlic turns acrid and dominates the broth). Add the 8 g / 2 anchovy fillets and mash them into the oil with a wooden spoon; they will sizzle, foam, and melt away in 30 to 45 seconds, dissolving into a savory, salty base. This is the glutamate anchor of the dish.
  4. 4
    Blister the tomatoes. Raise the heat to medium-high and add the 250 g / 1½ cups cherry tomatoes (half whole, half halved). Cook, stirring occasionally and pressing the halved ones with the spoon, for 3 to 5 minutes until the skins blister and split and the cut faces release their juice and begin to concentrate. The whole tomatoes hold pockets of bright juice; the halved ones break down into the broth. This concentration step builds the tomato glutamate that, with the anchovy, carries the umami of the dish.
  5. 5
    Build the broth. Pour in the 120 g / ½ cup dry white wine and let it bubble hard for 60 to 90 seconds, scraping the pan, until the sharp alcohol smell cooks off and the raw-wine edge softens into acidity. Add the 240 g / 1 cup hot water and the bruised parsley stems (from the 12 g / ½ bunch parsley; reserve the chopped leaves for finishing). Bring to a gentle simmer and let it cook 2 to 3 minutes to marry. Taste: it should read bright, savory, and lightly salty, balanced between tomato sweetness, wine acidity, and anchovy salt. Adjust now, since you cannot reseason aggressively once the fish is in.
  6. 6
    Poach the fish. Lay the 650 g / 1 whole porgy into the simmering broth; the liquid should come a third to halfway up the fish, not submerge it. Spoon some broth and a few tomatoes over the exposed top, cover, and poach at a bare simmer (never a boil, which toughens and dries the lean flesh) for 8 to 12 minutes by thickness: 8 minutes for a fish ~2.5 cm thick at the spine, 10 for ~3 cm, 12 for ~3.5 cm. Halfway through, baste the top of the fish once with the broth. The head and bones release collagen and gelatin into the liquid as it poaches, giving the brodo its body. Check at the earliest time by inserting a chopstick or thin knife into the deepest slash: the flesh should be opaque to the spine and lift cleanly from the bone. If it still shows a translucent core, re-cover and continue 90 seconds at a time.
  7. 7
    Finish with acid and aromatics. Pull the pan off the heat the moment the fish is just opaque at the spine; carryover will finish it. Discard the spent parsley stems. Squeeze the reserved ½ lemon (from the 30 g lemon) into the broth and stir; taste and correct, the brodo should taste lively and bright, not flat. Scatter the chopped parsley leaves over the fish and broth. Drizzle the raw 15 g / 1 tbsp finishing olive oil over the fish for fruity uncooked aroma.
  8. 8
    Plate and serve. Transfer the whole porgy to a warm shallow bowl or rimmed platter, or portion it at the pan by lifting the top fillet off the bones and then the bottom fillet off the skin. Ladle the brodo, tomatoes, and softened garlic generously around and over the fish. Serve immediately with the grilled garlic bread and the lemon wedges (the table half of the 30 g lemon); each diner spoons broth over the fish, mops the rest with bread, and squeezes lemon to taste. The brodo at the bottom of the bowl is the best part of the dish.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein format→fillets instead of a whole fish

    Use 2 skin-on porgy or branzino fillets (about 180 g each) when whole fish is not available. Build the broth identically, but because boneless fillets contribute no collagen, first simmer a handful of fish frames or a few shrimp shells in the water for 10 minutes and strain, so the brodo still has body. Slide the fillets in skin-side up and poach 4 to 6 minutes by thickness. The plate loses the whole-fish drama but the broth stays right.

  • Regional briny depth→puttanesca-leaning acqua pazza

    Add 30 g / 3 tbsp pitted Niçoise olives and 15 g / 1 tbsp rinsed capers to the broth along with the tomatoes. The dish shifts toward the saltier, brinier Campanian variation; cut the kosher salt on the fish to 4 g / 1 tsp to account for the added salinity from the olives and capers.

  • Heat heat and herb→a more aromatic, summer version

    Swap the parsley for a mix of basil and parsley, tearing 8 g / ¼ cup basil leaves in at the very end off heat, and double the red pepper flakes to 2 g / 1 tsp. The basil reads distinctly Campanian and the higher chili pushes the dish toward an arrabbiata-adjacent warmth.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole porgy → whole branzino, orata, black sea bass, or red snapper All are lean white round fish of the same size class and poach identically, contributing the same collagen body from head and bones. Match a 600 to 700 g whole fish and time by thickness at the spine. Branzino and black sea bass are the most common Western-counter stand-ins.
  • cherry tomatoes → whole canned San Marzano tomatoes, hand-crushed Out of cherry-tomato season, use about 200 g / ¾ cup drained canned San Marzano, crushed by hand, and blister them a touch longer to drive off the extra liquid. The broth reads rounder and less bright; add an extra squeeze of lemon at the finish to compensate.
  • dry white wine → dry vermouth, or water plus a splash of white wine vinegar Dry vermouth brings a faint herbal note and works cleanly. With no wine at all, use the full water quantity plus 1 tbsp / 15 g white wine vinegar added with the lemon at the end, so the dish keeps its acid backbone.
  • oil-packed anchovy fillets → colatura di alici or fish sauce, a few drops A teaspoon of colatura (Italian anchovy syrup) or a scant ½ teaspoon of fish sauce delivers the same melted-anchovy glutamate without the fillets. To keep the dish strictly meat-free, omit the anchovy and lean harder on the blistered tomato for umami; the broth reads cleaner and less savory.

Notes

Why "crazy water." Acqua pazza is the poaching liquid, not the fish. The name is Neapolitan fisherman's slang for the thrown-together broth of seawater, a few tomatoes, garlic, chili, and the bones of the day's smaller catch, cooked fast on the boat or at the dock. The whole point is that the cheap whole fish and its own skeleton make the liquid worth eating; the brodo is the dish as much as the flesh, which is why the grilled bread to mop it is not a side but a structural part of the meal.

The umami and acid structure. The glutamate anchor is the blistered cherry tomato plus the two melted anchovy fillets; concentrating the tomatoes over higher heat before the liquid goes in is what builds savory depth rather than thin tomato-water. The finishing acid is layered: the white wine cooked into the broth, the natural acidity of the tomato, and the squeeze of fresh lemon stirred in off heat at the very end. The lemon at the finish is non-negotiable in a poach this gentle, since without a bright top note the broth reads flat against the richness of the olive oil.

Seasoning curve. Salt enters in three registers: the kosher-salt cure on the fish (which seasons the flesh from the surface and firms it slightly through partial protein denaturation), the anchovy melting salt into the broth base, and the final taste-and-correct before the fish goes in. Do most of the seasoning before the fish enters the pan, because once it is poaching you cannot stir and reduce aggressively without breaking the delicate flesh; the broth must be right going in.

Why poach, never boil. Porgy is a lean fish (3 to 6% fat) with a narrow doneness window; the connective tissue between muscle blocks gelatinizes at 55 to 60 °C and the flesh dries fast past 60 °C at the spine. A bare simmer holds the broth at the gentle heat that cooks the fish through without seizing it, while a hard boil agitates and toughens the flake and clouds the brodo. The whole-fish format helps here too: the skin and rib cage slow heat into the deep flesh and the bones shield the thickest muscle, widening the margin for the cook.

Sourcing the porgy. H Mart, 99 Ranch, and Mediterranean and Italian fish counters carry whole porgy on ice, usually well under the price of branzino or snapper for the same Sparidae structure. Pick a fish with clear convex eyes, bright red gills, tight scales, a thin clean sheen rather than gummy slime, and a clean ocean smell. Have it scaled, gutted, and de-gilled at the counter and verify all three at home; porgy scales are small and tenacious and the gut cavity is the first thing to spoil. A 600 to 700 g fish fits one wide pan and serves two with bread.

Sources
  • Adapted from the Neapolitan and Campanian acqua pazza tradition, the fisherman's whole-fish poach in "crazy water" of the Amalfi and Ischia coast. — The dish is a study in restraint and timing. The broth is built fast and aggressively before the fish ever enters: tomatoes are blistered to concentrate glutamate, garlic and chili are bloomed in oil, anchovy melts in as a savory anchor, and wine plus water plus the fish's own head and bones turn it into a light brodo in minutes. The fish then poaches gently, 8 to 12 minutes by thickness, never boiling. Overcook the broth's aromatics or boil the fish and the dish collapses into either a heavy sauce or a dry poach; held to the right register it is one of the cleanest expressions of whole-fish cookery.
