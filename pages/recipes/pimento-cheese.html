Pimento cheese

The "Southern caviar": sharp orange cheddar, cream cheese, mayonnaise, and diced jarred pimentos bound into a thick spreadable spread with cracked black pepper, a pinch of cayenne, garlic powder, and onion powder. Served on crackers, in sandwiches, melted on a burger, or stuffed into celery. Every Southern household has a slightly different version and every household defends theirs.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The cream cheese must be at room temperature (about 20 °C / 68 °F) to incorporate smoothly with the mayo and cheese. Cold cream cheese stays in lumps and produces a grainy texture. Pull the block out of the fridge 60 minutes before starting; if rushed, microwave 15 seconds at 50 percent power.
  • Hand-grate the cheddar from a block; do not use pre-shredded bagged cheese. Pre-shredded cheese is coated with cellulose powder that prevents it from binding into the smooth pimento-cheese paste; the result is a sandy, clumpy spread.
  • The spread improves with at least 1 hour of refrigeration before serving (overnight is better); the flavors integrate and the cream cheese binds the structure. Same-day pimento cheese is acceptable but reads slightly less than 24-hour-rested.

  2. 230 g cream cheese, full-fat, at room temperature, softened to room temperature, about 60 min out of the fridge
  3. 120 g Duke's mayonnaise (or Hellmann's full-fat outside the South)
  4. 110 g jarred diced pimentos, drained, drained in a fine-mesh sieve; pat dry between paper towels

Phase 2, the seasoning

  1. 3 g black pepper, freshly cracked
  2. 1 g cayenne pepper
  3. 2 g garlic powder
  4. 2 g onion powder
  5. 5 ml Worcestershire sauce
  6. 2 g kosher salt

Execution

  1. 1
    In a large bowl, beat the 230 g softened cream cheese alone with a wooden spoon or hand mixer for 60 seconds until smooth, loose, and pliable. If the cream cheese is still cold and lumpy at this stage, microwave the bowl 10 seconds at 50 percent power and try again.
  2. 2
    Add the 120 g / ½ cup mayonnaise; beat 30 seconds to fully incorporate. The mixture should be smooth and uniformly off-white with no lumps of cream cheese.
  3. 3
    Add the 3 g / 1½ tsp black pepper, 1 g / ½ tsp cayenne, 2 g garlic powder, 2 g onion powder, 5 ml / 1 tsp Worcestershire, and 2 g / ½ tsp salt. Stir to fully blend; the spread should now be a pale-yellow seasoned cream base.
  4. 4
    Fold in the 340 g grated cheddar with a sturdy wooden spoon or rubber spatula. Mix thoroughly until every strand of cheddar is coated with the cream base; the texture should be thick and chunky with visible cheese strands, not smooth. Some Southern cooks prefer to "smash" the cheese with the back of the spoon during mixing to partially break the strands; others fold gently to keep the strands intact. Either is correct.
  5. 5
    Drain the 110 g jarred pimentos thoroughly in a fine-mesh sieve, then pat dry between paper towels (any excess pimento liquid in the spread produces a wet pimento cheese that reads as soup; the drained dry pimentos hold their shape and color). Fold the dried pimentos into the cheese mixture; mix gently to distribute (do not over-mash, the orange flecks of pimento should be visible throughout).
  6. 6
    Taste; adjust seasoning. The pimento cheese should read structurally cheesy (cheddar-forward), with a creamy mayo-cream-cheese binding, visible flecks of orange pimento, aggressive black pepper, and a quiet cayenne back-of-palate warmth. Adjust salt, cayenne, or black pepper if needed.
  7. 7
    Transfer to an airtight glass jar or container. Press the surface flat to eliminate air pockets. Refrigerate at least 1 hour (overnight is dramatically better; the flavors integrate and the spread firms to the canonical scoopable texture). Pimento cheese stays good for 1 week refrigerated.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion serving format→as classic pimento cheese sandwiches (the Masters Tournament version)

    Spread 60 g pimento cheese on a slice of soft white sandwich bread (Sunbeam, Wonder, or Pepperidge Farm). Top with a second slice. Cut crusts off (canonical for the Masters sandwich); cut diagonally into 4 triangles. Wrap individually in wax paper. The Augusta National "Pimento Cheese Sandwich" served at the Masters Tournament has been on the menu since 1934 and costs $1.50; the structural version.

  • Occasion serving format→as grilled pimento cheese sandwich (melted)

    Spread 90 g pimento cheese between two slices of soft white bread, buttered on the outside. Grill in a skillet over medium heat 3 minutes per side until the bread is deep gold and the cheese is fully molten. The grilled version is the structural Southern lunch-counter sandwich.

  • Occasion serving format→as a pimento-cheese burger topping

    Add a 50 g dollop of cold pimento cheese on top of a hot grilled beef burger in the last 30 seconds of the cook; close the grill lid (or cover with a foil tent) until the cheese melts and oozes down the sides. The melted-pimento-cheese-burger is a Carolina barbecue tradition.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • sharp orange cheddar cheese → extra-sharp white cheddar plus 1 g paprika (for color) White cheddar is structurally not Southern pimento cheese; orange is canonical. The paprika-tinted white cheddar version is acceptable but not canonical. Avoid mild cheddar (too bland), Colby (too soft), or Monterey Jack (too mild).
  • Duke's mayonnaise → Hellmann's full-fat (Best Foods on West Coast); never Miracle Whip Duke's is the structural Southern brand and is meaningfully different from Hellmann's (extra egg yolk, no sugar, slightly tangier). Hellmann's is the closest national substitute. Miracle Whip is sweet and reads wrong; it will pull the dish out of Southern frame.
  • jarred diced pimentos → jarred roasted red peppers, drained and diced; not fresh-roasted Pimentos are a specific small sweet pepper (Capsicum annuum, pimento variety) sold pickled in jars; they are slightly sweeter and slightly more pickled-tangy than generic roasted red peppers. The jarred-roasted-red-pepper substitute is acceptable in a pinch; fresh-roasted bell pepper is the wrong texture.

Notes

Why orange cheddar. Pimento cheese is structurally an orange spread; the color is half the visual signal of the dish. The orange comes from annatto added to the cheddar (a Southern cheese-making tradition); white cheddar lacks the structural color and the dish reads as something else. Cracker Barrel sharp cheddar, Tillamook extra-sharp, or any orange supermarket cheddar works.

Why cream cheese plus mayo. The two-binder approach is the structural Southern move. The cream cheese provides body and creaminess that holds the spread on a cracker without dripping; the mayo provides looseness and the canonical tangy back-of-palate that makes pimento cheese taste like pimento cheese rather than like cream-cheese-and-cheddar. The 2:1 cream-cheese-to-mayo ratio is the structural mid-range; some households go heavier on cream cheese (denser), others heavier on mayo (looser); both are correct.

Why jarred not fresh pimentos. Jarred diced pimentos are a specific Southern grocery product (Goya, Dromedary) that have been simmered in their own juice and acidified slightly; they have a structural sweet-tang and a soft texture that distinguishes them from fresh roasted red bell pepper. The recipe relies on the jarred product; fresh-roasted bell pepper has too much water, too bright a vegetal flavor, and the wrong texture.

Why the overnight rest. The flavors of pimento cheese integrate dramatically over the first 24 hours: the cream cheese binds the loose ingredients into a smooth scoopable structure, the cayenne and Worcestershire deepen, the cheese strands soften and meld. Same-day pimento cheese reads as cheese-with-spread; 24-hour pimento cheese reads as pimento cheese.

Pairings. Ritz crackers, saltines, celery sticks, soft white bread sandwiches, grilled cheese, burger topping, deviled-egg filling, stuffed jalapeños, biscuit spread. A glass of sweet tea or a cold beer. The Masters Tournament pairing is a beer in a paper cup; the church-supper pairing is sweet tea in a glass.

Storage. Keeps 1 week refrigerated in an airtight container; the flavor improves through day 3 and is at peak between days 2 and 5. Does not freeze well; the cream cheese and mayo separate on thaw. Make a fresh batch every week if you eat pimento cheese regularly.

Sources
  • Southern (especially Carolina, Georgia, and Mississippi) home and church-supper canon — Pimento cheese (always "pim-uh-na" not "pim-en-toe" in the South) is the structural Southern pantry condiment, served at every church supper, baby shower, wedding shower, Masters Tournament (the official sandwich since 1934), and as a year-round refrigerator staple. The defining elements are: sharp orange cheddar (the structural cheese, never white cheddar which reads wrong), full-fat cream cheese (the binder and the creaminess), full-fat mayonnaise (Duke's in the South, Hellmann's elsewhere), jarred diced pimentos (the eponymous structural pepper, do not substitute fresh roasted bell pepper), and a small amount of spice (black pepper, cayenne, garlic powder, onion powder). The exact ratio varies by household; this recipe is a structurally balanced reference version.
