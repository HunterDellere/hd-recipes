Seared cod with roasted cauliflower and brown butter cream

Two cod fillets seared to a deep gold crust over high-browned roasted cauliflower, finished with a Norman-style pan sauce of brown butter, shallot, white wine, and cream tightened with dijon and lifted with capers, lemon, and dill. A weeknight-fast plate built on the North-French logic that cream and cultured tang carry lean white fish where olive oil cannot.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Thaw frozen cod fully (overnight in the refrigerator) and pat it bone dry, then salt and pat again before searing. Surface moisture is the enemy of both the crust and the sauce: wet fish steams instead of browning, and water shed into the pan thins the cream.
  • Get the cauliflower into a hot oven first. It needs 25 to 30 minutes and should come out as the sauce finishes; sear the cod and build the sauce while it roasts.
  • Stage every sauce component (shallot minced, wine, cream, dijon, capers, lemon, dill) on the counter before the cod hits the pan. Once the fish is seared you have a 6 minute window to brown the butter and build the sauce while the cod rests.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, roast the cauliflower

  1. 600 g cauliflower, cut into 3 to 4 cm florets, cut from the underside of the head so the stem holds them intact
  2. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  3. 5 g kosher salt
  4. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked
  5. 15 g parmigiano-reggiano, finely grated, grated on a microplane

Phase 2, sear the cod

  1. 340 g cod fillets, about 2.5 cm thick, fully thawed, patted bone dry; skin removed if present
  2. 3 g kosher salt
  3. 20 g all-purpose flour, for dredging optional
  4. 14 g neutral oil (refined avocado, grapeseed, or rice bran)

Phase 3, brown butter cream sauce

  1. 45 g unsalted butter
  2. 5 g anchovy fillet, minced, minced to a paste — (1 oil-packed fillet; dissolves invisibly, reads as savory depth not fish)
  3. 40 g shallot, fine-minced, minced to 1 to 2 mm dice — (1 large shallot)
  4. 60 g dry white wine (Sauvignon Blanc or unoaked Chardonnay)
  5. 120 g heavy cream
  6. 8 g dijon mustard
  7. 15 g capers, drained
  8. 15 g lemon, juice of half, plus ½ tsp finely grated zest — (½ lemon)
  9. 5 g fresh dill, chopped

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the oven to 230C / 450F. Toss the 600 g cauliflower florets with the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil, 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt, and 1 g / ¼ tsp black pepper directly on a rimmed sheet pan, then turn the florets cut-face-down in a single uncrowded layer. The dry, high heat caramelizes the brassica sugars and drives off the sulfurous volatiles that low, wet cooking releases. High-heat dry roasting
  2. 2
    Roast 25 to 30 minutes, undisturbed for the first 18 to 20 so the cut faces build an unbroken brown crust, then a single toss to color the second face. The florets are done when the cut faces are deep mahogany and a paring knife slides through the stem with no resistance. Hold them in the warm oven while you finish the fish.
  3. 3
    While the cauliflower roasts, prep the cod. Pat the 340 g cod fillets bone dry, season both sides with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt, and let them sit 10 minutes, then pat once more (the salt pulls a film of moisture you want gone before the sear). If using, dredge the fillets in the 20 g / 2½ tbsp flour and shake off all but a thin veil.
  4. 4
    Heat the 14 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a wide stainless skillet over medium-high until it shimmers and the first wisp of heat rises. Lay the cod presentation-side down, press each fillet flat for 10 seconds for full contact, then sear undisturbed 3 to 4 minutes to a deep gold crust. Flip and cook 1 to 2 minutes more to 52 to 54C internal. Transfer to a plate; the fish climbs to its final doneness by carryover while you build the sauce. Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Lower the heat to medium and add the 45 g / 3 tbsp butter to the same pan. Swirl past the foam until the milk solids turn amber and the butter smells of toasted hazelnut, about 2 minutes. Stir in the 5 g / 1 minced anchovy fillet and let it dissolve completely into the fat, 30 seconds; it disappears and leaves only glutamate depth. Brown butter (beurre noisette)
  6. 6
    Add the 40 g / 1 large minced shallot and sweat 60 to 90 seconds to translucent, no color. Pour in the 60 g / ¼ cup dry white wine, scrape the fond off the pan, and reduce until the pan is nearly dry and syrupy, about 90 seconds. This cooks off the raw-alcohol edge and concentrates the shallot. Deglazing
  7. 7
    Pour in the 120 g / ½ cup heavy cream and simmer until it coats the back of a spoon (nappe), 2 to 3 minutes. Pull the pan off the heat and whisk in the 8 g / 1 tsp dijon mustard, the 15 g / 1 tbsp drained capers, the juice of the half lemon (15 g) and ½ tsp of its grated zest. Taste: it should read bright, savory, and a touch sharp against the rich cream. Stir in the 5 g / 2 tbsp chopped dill off heat so it stays green.
  8. 8
    Spread the 600 g roasted cauliflower across two warm plates and shower it with the 15 g / 3 tbsp grated parmigiano-reggiano while it is hot enough to melt. Set a cod fillet on each bed and spoon the brown butter cream generously over the fish so it pools into the cauliflower. Serve at once.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→halibut, haddock, or hake

    Halibut is denser and more forgiving (pull at 52C, it holds a wider window); haddock and hake behave almost identically to cod (same lean flaky structure, same 54C pull). For a thicker halibut steak, add a minute per side in the sear and rest a touch longer.

  • Regional a leaner, brighter sauce→crème fraîche pan sauce

    Swap the 120 g heavy cream for 120 g crème fraîche, added off heat at the end rather than simmered (it can split under a hard boil). The sauce reads tangier and more cultured, closer to the Norman original, and you can skip the dijon since the crème carries its own acid.

  • Occasion one-pan sheet dinner→roast the cod on the cauliflower

    Skip the sear. After the cauliflower has roasted 18 minutes, clear two gaps, nestle the salted cod fillets in, brush with olive oil, and roast 8 to 10 minutes more to 54C. Build the brown butter cream in a small saucepan. Lower crust, far less cleanup, the right call on a true weeknight.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • fresh dill → fresh tarragon, chervil, or flat-leaf parsley Tarragon pushes the sauce toward the anise-and-cream register of a classic sauce béarnaise cousin; chervil is the gentlest and most French; parsley is the neutral fallback. Use the same 5 g, stirred in off heat.
  • dry white wine → dry vermouth or unsalted fish stock plus a squeeze of lemon Dry vermouth (Noilly Prat) is the classic restaurant swap and keeps in the pantry far longer than wine; use 45 g since it is more concentrated. For an alcohol-free sauce, deglaze with 60 g fish stock and add an extra 5 g lemon juice for the acid the wine would have carried.
  • cauliflower → romanesco or broccoli Romanesco roasts identically with a nuttier edge; broccoli softens about 25 percent faster, so pull it a few minutes sooner. Both want the same 230C dry roast.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → dijon mustard make it yourself Five minutes of grinding brown mustard seed with white wine and a week of mellowing in the fridge yields a sharper, more aromatic dijon than any jar, and here it is doing structural work (acid plus emulsion stabilizer) where its quality is tasted directly in the sauce.

Notes

Why brown butter under cream. Cod is the leanest white fish on the counter, and lean fish reads flat without an outside source of savory depth. Browning the butter before the cream goes in builds a layer of Maillard milk-solid flavor that a straight cream sauce never develops, and it is the single change that takes this from "fish in cream" to a sauce with a spine. The dissolved anchovy compounds that depth with glutamate; you will not taste it as anchovy, only as a sauce that tastes like it was built on stock.

The doneness rule. Pull the cod at 52 to 54C in the thickest part and let carryover finish it. The USDA target of 63C / 145F leaves cod chalky and dry because there is almost no intramuscular fat to hold moisture. The flake should just separate under gentle pressure and still look glossy at the center when it comes off the heat. An instant-read thermometer is worth more on lean fish than on any steak.

Frozen is fine, wet is not. Frozen-at-sea cod is often better than the "fresh" cod that has sat thawed on ice for days. The only discipline frozen fish demands is thorough thawing and thorough drying: thaw overnight in the fridge, then press the fillets between paper towels until the surface is matte, not slick. A wet fillet will not crust, and the water it sheds breaks the sauce.

Where the sauce breaks and how to save it. If the cream is simmered too hard it can look grainy; pull it off heat and whisk in a teaspoon of cold water or an extra knob of cold butter to bring it back. If it goes too thick while you plate, loosen with a splash of the pasta-less cooking liquid or warm water. Add the lemon and dill only at the very end, off heat: acid added into a hard-boiling cream can curdle it, and dill cooked in the sauce turns drab.
