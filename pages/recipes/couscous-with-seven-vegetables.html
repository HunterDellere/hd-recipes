Couscous with seven vegetables

The canonical Moroccan Friday meal: medium-grain semolina couscous hand-steamed three times over a deep saffron-and-ginger broth of lamb, chickpeas, and seven layered vegetables (carrot, turnip, zucchini, cabbage, pumpkin or butternut squash, sweet potato, and tomato), the grains fluffed and oiled between steamings until they emerge separate, light, and perfumed with the broth steam. The structural defining technique is the triple steam in the couscoussier; box-instant couscous boiled in stock is a different (and inferior) dish. The dish that gives Maghreb cuisine its name to the world.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Soak the chickpeas overnight (or quick-soak). Canned chickpeas work as a fallback (add late).
  • A couscoussier (a tall two-piece pot with a perforated steaming insert) is the canonical vessel; the bottom holds the broth and stew, the top holds the couscous over the steam. The closest home substitute is a tall stockpot with a fine-mesh sieve or steamer basket fitted snugly over the rim and sealed with a damp kitchen towel wrapped around the join (the seal forces the steam through the couscous rather than escaping at the side). Cheesecloth-lined sieves work too. Aluminum foil over the top of the steamer, crimped tight, completes the seal.
  • Bloom the saffron 15 minutes before the broth starts. Grind 0.4 g threads with a pinch of sugar, steep in 50 g hot water.
  • Plan 3 hours total. The dish is a project; budget for it. The flavor and the grain texture are worth the time and structurally cannot be shortcut.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the soaks and saffron bloom

  1. 200 g dried chickpeas, soaked overnight in 1 L cold water, drained
  2. 0.4 g saffron threads, ground with a pinch of sugar in a mortar — (a large pinch)
  3. 50 g hot water (not boiling), for saffron

Phase 2, the broth and lamb

  1. 800 g lamb shoulder, bone-in pieces, cut into 5 cm pieces with the bone (or substitute lamb shanks cut into 2 to 3 pieces by the butcher)
  2. 45 g extra-virgin olive oil
  3. 300 g yellow onion, thinly sliced (about 3 mm) — (2 medium onions)
  4. 16 g garlic clove, peeled, finely minced — (4 cloves)
  5. 25 g fresh ginger, peeled and finely grated — (a 5 cm knob)
  6. 5 g ground turmeric
  7. 3 g ground cumin
  8. 3 g sweet paprika
  9. 3 g black pepper, freshly cracked
  10. 18 g kosher salt
  11. 1 × 28 oz / 794 g can canned crushed tomatoes (or 200 g fresh, grated)
  12. 2000 g water
  13. 30 g fresh cilantro and parsley, tied in a bundle — (small bunch each, tied together; pulled out at the end)

Phase 3, the seven vegetables

  1. 300 g carrots, peeled, cut into 5 cm batons (about 4 medium carrots) — (vegetable 1)
  2. 250 g turnips, peeled, cut into 3 cm wedges (about 2 medium turnips) — (vegetable 2; canonical in seven-vegetable couscous)
  3. 300 g zucchini, cut into 5 cm batons (about 2 medium zucchini) — (vegetable 3; goes in late, cooks fast)
  4. 300 g savoy or green cabbage, cut into 8 wedges, core retained to hold each wedge together — (vegetable 4; about ¼ medium cabbage)
  5. 400 g butternut squash or pumpkin, peeled, seeded, cut into 5 cm chunks — (vegetable 5)
  6. 300 g sweet potato, peeled, cut into 5 cm chunks (about 1 medium sweet potato) — (vegetable 6)
  7. 200 g ripe tomato, cut into 6 wedges (or large dice if too ripe to wedge) — (vegetable 7)

Phase 4, the couscous

  1. 500 g medium-grain couscous (not instant)
  2. 600 g cold water (for the couscous rinses)
  3. 45 g extra-virgin olive oil (for the couscous)
  4. 60 g unsalted butter (for the final couscous)
  5. 8 g fine sea salt (for the couscous)

Execution

  1. 1
    Cook the soaked 200 g chickpeas in 1 L fresh water 45 minutes until tender; drain and reserve. Bloom the 0.4 g saffron with 1 g sugar and 50 g hot water; steep 15 minutes.
  2. 2
    In the bottom of a couscoussier (or a tall stockpot), heat the 45 g / 3 tbsp olive oil over medium-high heat. Brown the 800 g lamb pieces on 2 sides, 8 minutes total. Reduce heat to medium; add the 300 g / 2 onions and sweat 10 minutes until golden. Add the 16 g / 4 garlic and 25 g grated ginger; cook 60 seconds. Add the 5 g / 2½ tsp turmeric, 3 g / 1½ tsp cumin, 3 g / 1½ tsp paprika, and 3 g / 1½ tsp black pepper; stir 30 seconds.
  3. 3
    Add the 200 g crushed tomatoes, the bloomed saffron liquid, the 2000 g / 8 cups water, the cilantro-and-parsley bundle, and 10 g / 2½ tsp of the kosher salt. Bring to a simmer; cover and braise gently 45 minutes while you work on the first couscous steam.
  4. 4
    First couscous rinse-and-fluff: in a wide shallow bowl, spread the 500 g dry couscous. Sprinkle with 200 g of the cold water (mixed with 4 g of the fine sea salt) and 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil; rub the grains between your palms for 2 minutes, breaking up any clumps until the grains are evenly moistened and separated. Let stand 5 minutes to absorb.
  5. 5
    Transfer the moistened couscous to the steamer insert (lined with cheesecloth if your insert has large holes). Set the steamer over the simmering lamb broth; if there is a gap at the join, seal with a damp cloth or aluminum foil so the steam is forced up through the couscous, not around it. Steam uncovered 20 minutes from when you see the first steam rising through the grains. Do not cover; the couscous needs to vent. The grains will plump and become al dente.
  6. 6
    Turn the steamed couscous out into the wide bowl. Sprinkle with 200 g cold water plus 4 g salt, and 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil. Working with a spatula at first (the grains are very hot), break up any clumps and re-roll between palms once cooled enough to touch. The grains should look swollen, separate, and glossy with oil. Rest 5 minutes.
  7. 7
    Second steam: return the couscous to the steamer over the broth (which has been simmering all this time). Steam 20 minutes. After 45 minutes of lamb braise, the meat should be fork-tender; lift it out to a plate, cover, and reserve.
  8. 8
    After the second steam, turn out and rub again with the remaining 200 g water and 15 g olive oil; rest 5 minutes.
  9. 9
    Add the vegetables to the broth in order of cooking time: first the 300 g carrots, 250 g turnips, 400 g squash, and 300 g sweet potato (these need 30 minutes); cover and simmer 15 minutes. Then add the 300 g cabbage wedges, 200 g tomato wedges, and the cooked chickpeas (these need 15 minutes); add the remaining 8 g / 2 tsp salt. Simmer 15 minutes. In the last 8 minutes, add the 300 g zucchini batons (which cook fastest).
  10. 10
    Third and final couscous steam: return the couscous to the steamer over the now vegetable-rich broth. Steam 15 to 20 minutes; the steam from the vegetables perfumes the grains beautifully in this final pass. The couscous should be tender, fluffy, and slightly toasted-fragrant.
  11. 11
    Turn the steamed couscous into a large wide serving platter. Add the 60 g / 4 tbsp butter, cut into small pieces, and fold gently with a fork until the butter melts into the hot grains. Taste a few grains; the seasoning should read of warm spice, lamb broth, and faint saffron. The grains should be perfectly separate, fluffy, and glossy.
  12. 12
    Mound the couscous on the platter; arrange the lamb pieces and the vegetables on top in a colorful array. Ladle 200 g of the hot broth over the top of the platter; serve the remaining broth in a small bowl on the side so each diner can wet their portion to taste. Discard the herb bundle. Serve immediately; the canonical service is family-style from the platter, with each diner eating from the section closest to them.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein with chicken instead of lamb→with bone-in chicken

    Substitute 1.2 kg bone-in chicken thighs and drumsticks for the lamb. Reduce the initial lamb-braise to 25 minutes (chicken cooks faster). The dish reads lighter and is a common variant; the chicken-and-vegetable couscous is essentially Friday-meal couscous adapted for a non-lamb table.

  • Occasion with tfaya topping (onion-raisin-cinnamon caramel)→as couscous tfaya

    Make a separate topping: slowly caramelize 400 g sliced onions in 30 g butter with 1 cinnamon stick, 1 tsp ground ginger, and a pinch of saffron for 45 minutes until deeply golden and jammy; stir in 80 g golden raisins (pre-soaked in warm water 10 min, drained), 30 g / 2 tbsp honey, and 1 tsp orange-flower water; cook 5 more minutes. Mound this onion-raisin-cinnamon topping on the couscous beneath the lamb at service. The dish becomes the canonical couscous tfaya, the sweet-onion-topped version often served at weddings and celebrations.

  • Dietary vegetarian variant→as vegetarian seven-vegetable couscous

    Skip the lamb. Double the chickpeas (to 400 g dried). Use vegetable stock for the broth. Add 30 g extra olive oil and a second pinch of saffron for depth. The dish reads cleaner and more vegetable-forward; common in vegetarian Moroccan cooking and acceptable in the tradition.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • medium-grain couscous (not instant) → instant couscous, prepared per package directions Instant couscous is acceptable as a major shortcut but produces a structurally different and inferior dish; the grains are pre-steamed and lack the layered absorption of broth steam. If using instant, prepare separately and serve as the bed for the lamb-and-vegetable stew; the dish is then a generic Moroccan stew over couscous rather than the canonical triple-steamed Friday meal.
  • butternut squash or pumpkin → kabocha squash or hubbard squash Any dense winter squash works; kabocha is especially good (sweet, creamy, holds its shape in the long simmer). Acorn squash is less ideal because the skin is hard to remove and it tends to fall apart.

Notes

Why the triple steam is structural. The hand-rolling-and-oiling-between-steamings technique is what produces the structural texture that defines proper couscous: grains that are tender but distinct, that have absorbed broth steam without becoming gummy, and that carry a fluffy, light, and slightly toasted character. Each steam cycle does specific work: the first hydrates the dry grains, the second deepens the absorption and re-separates them after the slight compression of the first steam, the third perfumes the grains with the vegetable-rich final broth and brings them to their finished texture. Boxed instant couscous boiled in stock skips all of this and produces a uniformly hydrated, slightly gummy grain that is recognizably couscous-adjacent but lacks the structural lightness of the triple-steam version.

The seven-vegetable formula is symbolic, not strict. The number seven is traditional and culturally meaningful (often associated with completeness and abundance in North African folk numerology); the actual vegetables vary by region, season, and family. Some traditional formulas include fava beans, eggplant, leek, or red bell pepper in place of one of the canonical seven. The principle is the layered variety, not the specific count; six or eight vegetables in the same proportions work fine. The cooking order matters more than the count: dense root vegetables go in first, leafy and quick-cooking vegetables go in last.

Versatility. The broth-and-vegetable-stew portion of this dish is essentially a Moroccan winter pot-au-feu and stands alone with bread instead of couscous. The triple-steam technique transfers to other grain steamings (Cape Malay yellow rice, Egyptian roz bil shaareyah). The lamb-and-spice base is the structural template for many Moroccan slow braises beyond couscous.

Pairs across Moroccan (canonical, especially the Friday meal tradition), broader Maghreb (similar steamed-couscous dishes exist in Algeria and Tunisia under regional names), and contemporary North African diaspora cookery. Especially central to family meals in Morocco where Friday couscous is a weekly institution.

Storage. The stew portion keeps 4 days refrigerated and freezes 2 months; the couscous keeps 3 days and reheats best with a brief re-steam (place in a sieve over simmering water 5 minutes). Leftover couscous can also be turned into cold couscous salad with vinaigrette, herbs, dried fruit, and toasted nuts. The freshly-steamed-and-buttered grain is the structural ideal; reheated couscous loses some of the lightness.
