Recipe

Daing na bangus

Butterflied milkfish given a light vinegar cure (a daing) in cane vinegar, crushed garlic, cracked black pepper, and salt for several hours, then patted bone-dry and pan-fried skin-crisp. The acid-garlic marinade firms the dense flesh, brightens it, and tempers the rich oily belly; served for breakfast with garlic fried rice, a fried egg, sliced tomato, and a spiced-vinegar dip.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a make-ahead dish: the fish needs at least 3 hours in the cure, and overnight is better. Start the marinade in the morning for a next-day breakfast, or the night before. The cure is the long-lead step and the entire flavor mechanism; do not shortcut it.
  • Pat the fish bone-dry before it touches the pan. Surface moisture from the marinade is the enemy of crisp skin: a wet fish steams and spatters violently in hot oil instead of searing. Blot both sides hard with paper towels and let the fish air-dry on a rack for 10 minutes while the pan heats.
  • If you want the classic garlic fried rice (sinangag) partner, cook day-old jasmine rice first so it is hot and ready when the fish comes out: the fish fries in under 12 minutes and is best straight from the pan. Day-old, fully cooled rice fries into separate grains; fresh hot rice turns gummy.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the cure (start 3+ hours ahead)

  1. 600 g deboned, butterflied milkfish (bangus), thawed if frozen, rinsed, patted dry; opened flat skin-side-down — (1 butterflied fish; buy it pre-deboned and butterflied from the H Mart freezer case or seafood counter, the fine Y-bones make hand-deboning impractical)
  2. 120 g cane vinegar
  3. 30 g garlic, crushed and roughly minced — (about 8 cloves; crushing ruptures the cells and develops the allicin that carries through the cure)
  4. 6 g whole black peppercorns, cracked coarse with the bottom of a heavy pan
  5. 8 g kosher salt

Phase 2, the fry

  1. 30 g neutral oil (canola, rice bran, or peanut)

Phase 3, the spiced vinegar dip (sukang sawsawan)

  1. 60 g cane vinegar
  2. 10 g garlic, finely minced — (about 3 cloves; raw, pungent)
  3. 30 g red onion, finely diced — (about ¼ small onion; soaks and softens in the acid)
  4. 2 g whole black peppercorns, cracked coarse

Phase 4, the breakfast plate (almusal)

  1. 100 g eggs — (2 large eggs, fried sunny-side-up, one per plate)
  2. 120 g tomato, sliced into wedges — (1 medium ripe tomato; the cool acid-sweet foil to the rich fried fish)
  3. 400 g steamed jasmine rice (ideally day-old, for garlic fried rice)

Execution

  1. 1
    Open the 600 g butterflied milkfish flat, skin-side-down, in a shallow dish or zip-top bag large enough to hold it without folding. Run your fingers across the flesh to check for any stray bones the deboner missed; pull them with tweezers. Score the thicker shoulder of the flesh with two shallow slashes so the cure penetrates evenly.
  2. 2
    Build the cure directly on and around the fish. Scatter the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt evenly over the flesh side, then the 30 g / 8 cloves crushed garlic and the 6 g / 2 tsp cracked black peppercorns. Pour the 120 g / ½ cup cane vinegar over and around the fish so the flesh side sits in the marinade. The salt and acid together are the cure: salt draws surface water and seasons; the cane vinegar denatures the surface proteins, firming the dense flesh and brightening the mild meat while tempering the oily belly.
  3. 3
    Cover and refrigerate at least 3 hours, ideally overnight. The fish should sit flesh-side-down so the cut surface stays in contact with the 120 g / ½ cup cane vinegar marinade. Flip once at the halfway point if the fish is not fully submerged. Do not exceed about 12 hours: past that, the acid over-firms the surface into a tough, chalky cure rather than the brightened, just-firmed texture you want.
  4. 4
    While the fish cures (or just before frying), build the spiced vinegar dip (sukang sawsawan). Combine the 60 g / ¼ cup cane vinegar, the 10 g / 3 cloves finely minced garlic, the 30 g / ¼ onion finely diced red onion, and the 2 g / ¾ tsp cracked black peppercorns in a small bowl. Let it sit at least 15 minutes so the raw garlic and onion mellow into the acid. This raw cane-vinegar dip is the finishing acid of the dish, brightening every bite of the rich fried fish at the table.
  5. 5
    Lift the cured fish from the marinade and pat it completely, aggressively dry on both sides with paper towels; brush off the clinging bits of the 30 g / 8 cloves cure garlic and the cracked pepper (they will burn in the pan). Discard the spent marinade. Lay the fish skin-side-up on a rack and let it air-dry 10 minutes while the pan heats. A bone-dry surface is the single requirement for crisp skin and a fish that sears instead of steaming and spattering.
  6. 6
    Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide nonstick or well-seasoned cast-iron skillet over medium-high until it shimmers and a drop of water flicked in dances and evaporates instantly. Lay the fish skin-side-down, pressing gently with a spatula for the first 20 seconds so the skin makes full contact and does not curl. Fry undisturbed 4 to 5 minutes: the subcutaneous and belly fat render, the skin crisps to a deep golden sheet, and the flesh sets opaque from the bottom up.
  7. 7
    Flip carefully and fry the flesh side 2 to 3 minutes, just until the thickest part is opaque to the center and the edges have caught a little color. The dense milkfish flesh holds together; the rendered belly fat keeps it moist across a wide window. Pull the fish to a rack (not paper towels, which steam the crisp skin soft) skin-side-up. Total fry time is about 7 to 8 minutes for a butterflied fish.
  8. 8
    Fry the 2 eggs sunny-side-up in the same pan (the fish-and-garlic-scented oil is a feature), and warm the 400 g jasmine rice. For the classic garlic fried rice (sinangag), fry a spoon of minced garlic in a little oil until pale gold, then toss in the cold day-old rice and fry over high heat until the grains separate and catch toasty spots. Slice the 120 g tomato into wedges.
  9. 9
    Plate the almusal: a mound of the 400 g / 2 cups garlic fried rice, the crisp-skin fish skin-side-up, a sunny egg, and the tomato wedges alongside. Set the bowl of spiced vinegar dip (sukang sawsawan) at the center. Each bite is a forkful of fish dipped in the bright raw vinegar, alternated with garlicky rice, the runny egg yolk binding it, the cool tomato cutting the richness. Serve immediately while the skin is crisp.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional acid→cured in coconut vinegar (sukang tuba)

    Replace the 120 g / ½ cup cane vinegar in the cure with the same weight of coconut vinegar (sukang tuba). The coconut vinegar is funkier and more aromatic, with a faint fermented-sap note; the cured fish reads earthier and more traditional in many southern Philippine households. Keep the cure time the same. Use coconut vinegar in the dip too for a unified acid profile.

  • Texture method→grilled instead of fried (inihaw-style)

    Cure as written, pat very dry, then grill the butterflied fish skin-side-down over medium-hot coals or a ridged grill pan, 4 to 5 minutes a side, until the skin chars and crisps. Brush lightly with neutral oil to prevent sticking. The smoke adds a charcoal dimension the pan-fry lacks; the dish moves toward inihaw na bangus while keeping the daing cure.

  • Heat heat→spiced cure and a chili vinegar dip

    Add 1 to 2 sliced red chilies (siling labuyo or thai chili) to the cure and 1 sliced chili to the spiced vinegar dip. The heat threads through both the marinade and the finishing acid, building a low warmth against the rich belly fat. Common in households that like their sawsawan with bite.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • milkfish (bangus) → Spanish mackerel, pompano, branzino, or rainbow trout, butterflied The cure-and-crisp method wants a firm-fleshed fish with some fat to render under the skin. Pompano and trout are closest for the self-basting belly character; Spanish mackerel brings more oil and a stronger flavor the acid cure tames well; branzino is leaner and cleaner, so watch the pan. None has the specific marbled milkfish belly-fat pocket, but all take the cure and fry crisp.
  • cane vinegar → apple cider vinegar or coconut vinegar Apple cider vinegar is the most available substitute, slightly sharper and more fruit-forward; the cured fish reads a touch tarter. Coconut vinegar is the more traditional alternative and funkier. Avoid distilled white vinegar in the raw dip, where it reads harsh and metallic.
  • kosher salt → fine sea salt Use about 6 g / 1 tsp fine sea salt in place of the 8 g / 2 tsp Diamond Crystal kosher salt; fine sea salt is denser per spoon, so it weighs more per unit volume. The cure works identically.

Notes

Why a cure and not just a marinade. Daing is structurally a light cure, not a flavor soak. The salt and the cane vinegar work together on the surface of the dense milkfish flesh: the salt draws out a thin layer of surface water and begins to season the meat, while the acetic acid denatures the surface proteins the same way it does in kinilaw and ceviche, firming the flesh and turning it slightly opaque at the edges. The result after a few hours is a fish whose surface is firmed and brightened and whose oily belly is tempered by the acid, so the pan-fry crisps a pre-seasoned, pre-firmed surface rather than seasoning raw fish from the outside in. Three hours is the minimum to see the effect; overnight is better. Past about 12 hours the acid over-firms the surface into something tough and chalky, the same way a ceviche left too long goes from bright to rubbery.

Why bone-dry before frying. The marinade leaves the fish wet, and wet fish is the enemy of crisp skin. Surface water flashes to steam in the hot oil, the temperature drops, the skin goes soft and gummy instead of crisp, and the oil spatters violently. Blotting both sides hard with paper towels and then air-drying on a rack for ten minutes pulls the surface moisture low enough that the fish sears on contact. The skin renders its subcutaneous fat into a crackling sheet, and the rendered belly fat bastes the flesh from below. This single step is the difference between crisp-skin daing and a sad steamed-then-fried fish.

The umami and acid architecture. The savory depth comes from the milkfish itself (the firm meat and the rich rendered belly fat) amplified by the garlic that perfumes both the cure and the fry, plus the garlic in the dip. The finishing acid is the raw cane vinegar, deployed twice: once as the cure that firms and brightens the fish, and again as the spiced-vinegar dip (sukang sawsawan) at the table. The dip is non-negotiable; it is the bright raw acid that cuts the rich fried fish bite by bite. Filipino breakfast is built on this contrast of a rich fried protein against a sharp vinegar dip, and daing na bangus is the canonical example.

Why the breakfast plate is built the way it is. The almusal architecture (fish, garlic fried rice, fried egg, tomato, vinegar dip) is a balance of registers. The garlic fried rice (sinangag) is the starchy, savory base; the runny egg yolk is the binding richness; the sliced tomato is the cool sweet-acid foil; and the vinegar dip is the sharp finishing acid that ties the rich fish to the plate. Day-old, fully cooled rice is structural to the sinangag: the staled grains have retrograded starch that fries into separate, toasty grains, where fresh hot rice clumps into gummy paste.

Sourcing the bangus. The fine intermuscular Y-bones are why you buy milkfish deboned and butterflied rather than fighting it at home. H Mart, 99 Ranch, Seafood City, and most Filipino and Southeast Asian grocers carry vacuum-packed boneless butterflied bangus in the freezer case (plain or already marinated daing-style) and fresh at the deboning counter. For the plain frozen fillet, look for clean white flesh with the belly-fat band intact, an unbroken seal, no gray freezer-burn patches, and thaw it overnight in the refrigerator. Pat a thawed fish especially dry, since frozen-then-thawed fish sheds extra water that will sabotage the crisp.

Sources
  • Adapted from the Filipino daing/paksiw vinegar-cure canon — Daing is the Filipino practice of curing fish in salt and acid (and here garlic and pepper) before drying or frying; daing na bangus is the milkfish version, a breakfast (almusal) fixture across the Philippines. The dish is built on the same vinegar-as-primary-seasoning logic as adobo and kinilaw, but the acid here is a marinade and light cure rather than a braising liquid: a few hours of contact firms the dense milkfish flesh through partial surface protein denaturation, brightens the mild meat, and cuts the richness of the oily belly fat before the fish ever hits the pan. Bangus is famous for its dense network of fine intermuscular Y-bones, so the recipe assumes a deboned, butterflied bangus from the H Mart freezer case or deboning counter.
