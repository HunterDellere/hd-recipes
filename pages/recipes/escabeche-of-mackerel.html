Recipe

Escabeche of mackerel

Mackerel fillets dredged and shallow-fried to a set crust, then submerged hot in a warm escabeche of olive-oil-fried garlic, onion, and carrot deglazed with sherry vinegar and dry white wine, scented with bay, pimentón, saffron, and toasted fennel and peppercorn. The marinade is poured over the fish still warm and the dish rests at least overnight; the acid surface-cooks and preserves the oily flesh while the aromatics infuse. Served cold or at room temperature, never hot, with bread to catch the vinegar oil. The Spanish acid-cure-as-preservation logic applied to the fish most prone to spoilage and most rewarded by it.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a make-ahead dish by design. After you fry the fish and pour the warm marinade over it, the escabeche must rest refrigerated at least 12 hours, ideally 24, before serving. Plan to cook it the day before you want to eat it.
  • Serve it cold or at room temperature, never hot. If serving from cold, pull the dish from the refrigerator 30 to 45 minutes ahead so the olive oil, which sets cloudy and semi-solid when cold, melts back to clear and the aromatics read.
  • Buy the freshest mackerel you can find and check it cold; oily fish oxidizes fast. Have the fish filleted and pin-boned before you start, and keep it on ice until the moment it goes into the flour.
  • The marinade is layered hot over the fried fish in a single non-reactive dish (glass or ceramic, never bare aluminum or cast iron; the acid reacts and reads metallic). Have that dish ready before you start frying.

Phase 1, the fish and fry

  1. 500 g mackerel fillets, skin on, pin-boned, from about 4 small whole mackerel or 2 large; cut each fillet crosswise into 2 or 3 pieces about 6 cm long
  2. 7 g kosher salt
  3. 60 g all-purpose flour, spread on a plate for dredging
  4. 120 g extra-virgin olive oil, for frying

Phase 2, the escabeche marinade

  1. 180 g extra-virgin olive oil
  2. 30 g garlic cloves, sliced thin, peeled and sliced into thin coins — (about 8 cloves; sliced not crushed, so they fry to soft golden coins rather than scorching)
  3. 200 g yellow onion, halved and sliced thin, peeled, halved pole to pole, sliced into 3 mm half-moons — (1 large onion; sliced thin so it softens fully in the warm oil)
  4. 120 g carrot, sliced into thin coins, peeled, sliced into 2 mm coins on a slight bias — (2 medium carrots; thin coins so they soften and take on the marinade)
  5. 10 g oil-packed anchovy fillets, finely chopped, to melt into the aromatic base — (3 to 4 fillets; they dissolve into glutamate depth and do not read as fish, the canonical Spanish umami builder)
  6. 2 g bay leaves — (3 leaves, fresh or dried)
  7. 2 g fennel seed, lightly crushed in a mortar
  8. 3 g black peppercorns, left whole, lightly cracked
  9. 4 g smoked paprika (pimentón de la Vera, dulce)
  10. 0.1 g saffron threads, crushed and bloomed in a tablespoon of warm water — (a generous pinch, about 20 threads; optional but traditional, lends color and musk)
  11. 120 g sherry vinegar (Jerez)
  12. 120 g dry white wine
  13. 3 g kosher salt

Phase 3, to serve

  1. 6 g flat-leaf parsley, roughly chopped
  2. 1 each lemon, in wedges — (1 lemon; a final bright acid at the plate, on top of the marinade vinegar)
  3. 200 g crusty bread — (half a loaf; to mop the vinegar-infused oil, non-negotiable)

Execution

  1. 1
    Salt and dredge the fish. Cut the 500 g / 1 lb mackerel fillets crosswise into 6 cm pieces and pat very dry on paper towels. Season all sides with the 7 g / 2 tsp kosher salt and let sit 10 minutes; the salt firms the surface protein so the dredge adheres and the flesh stays intact in the fry. Spread the 60 g / ½ cup all-purpose flour on a plate. Just before frying, dredge each piece in the flour and shake off all but a whisper; a thick coat turns gummy in the marinade rather than crisp. Dry brining
  2. 2
    Fry the fish to set, not to brown hard. Heat the 120 g / ½ cup frying olive oil in a wide skillet over medium-high until it shimmers and a flour speck sizzles on contact, about 180°C / 355°F. Lay the fish in skin-side down, working in two batches so the pan is not crowded, and fry 90 seconds per side until the flour crust is set and pale gold and the flesh is just cooked through. This is a shallow fry to firm and seal the fish, not a deep golden fry; the marinade does the rest of the flavor work. Lift each piece to the non-reactive serving dish (glass or ceramic) and arrange in a single snug layer. Pour off and discard the spent frying oil, but do not wipe the pan; the browned flour fond stays. Batter frying (wet-batter shallow-fry for jeon, pajeon, fritters)
  3. 3
    Build the aromatic base. Wipe the pan to remove burnt flour specks, then add the 180 g / ¾ cup marinade olive oil over medium-low heat. Add the 30 g / 8 cloves sliced garlic and fry gently until pale gold and fragrant, about 2 minutes; do not let it brown past blond or the marinade turns bitter. Add the 200 g / 1 onion sliced thin and the 120 g / 2 carrots in coins, and sweat slowly, stirring, for 8 to 10 minutes until the onion is fully translucent and limp and the carrot has softened but holds its shape. Stir in the 10 g / 4 chopped anchovy fillets and cook 1 minute until they dissolve into the oil; they are the umami spine of the marinade and will not read as fish. Blooming aromatics
  4. 4
    Bloom the spices off direct high heat. Pull the pan to low. Add the 2 g / 3 bay leaves, the 2 g / 1 tsp crushed fennel seed, and the 3 g / 1 tsp lightly cracked black peppercorns; stir 30 seconds in the warm oil to release their volatiles. Take the pan off the heat entirely and stir in the 4 g / 2 tsp smoked paprika and the bloomed 0.1 g / pinch saffron with its soaking water; paprika scorches and turns acrid in seconds over direct heat, so the residual warmth of the oil is what blooms it. Spice tempering (tadka / tarka)
  5. 5
    Acidulate and simmer the marinade. Return the pan to medium. Pour in the 120 g / ½ cup sherry vinegar and the 120 g / ½ cup dry white wine, add the 3 g / 1 tsp kosher salt, and bring to a gentle simmer. Let it bubble 3 to 4 minutes; this drives off the raw alcohol harshness from the wine and softens the vinegar from a sharp top-note into a rounded acidity, while the marinade emulsifies loosely as the oil and acidulated liquid bubble together. Taste a drop on a spoon: it should read aggressively acidic and savory, because the fish and the overnight rest will mellow it considerably.
  6. 6
    Drown the fish warm. Pour the hot marinade, oil, aromatics, and all, evenly over the fried mackerel so every piece is fully submerged under the liquid and the oil rises to cap the surface; the warm marinade penetrating warm fish is what carries the aromatics into the flesh. Distribute the onion, carrot, and bay over the top. Let cool on the counter 30 minutes, until no longer steaming, then cover with a fitted lid or parchment pressed to the surface and refrigerate at least 12 hours, ideally 24. Do not skip the rest; on the first night the fish tastes fried and the marinade tastes sharp, by the next day the two have become one dish.
  7. 7
    Serve cold or at room temperature. Pull the dish from the refrigerator 30 to 45 minutes before serving so the olive oil, which sets cloudy and waxy when cold, melts back to clear; the dish is meant to read at cool room temperature, not fridge-cold and never hot. Lift the fish and a generous spoonful of the onion, carrot, and oil onto plates. Scatter the 6 g / 2 tbsp chopped parsley over the top, set out the lemon wedges from 1 lemon for a final fresh acid at the plate, and serve with the 200 g / half loaf crusty bread to mop the vinegar-saturated oil. The oil left in the dish, steeped with smoked paprika, saffron, and sherry vinegar, is itself a prize; spoon it onto the bread.
Safety
  • Holding fried, acid-cured mackerel refrigerated for up to a week rather than eating it the day it is cooked Mackerel is a scombroid species: held above 40°F, bacteria convert its naturally high histidine into histamine, which is heat-stable and not destroyed by frying, vinegar, or refrigeration. Buy from a counter that handles ice well, keep the fish cold until it goes into the flour, and cook it within a day of purchase; the acid cure preserves fresh fish but cannot reverse temperature abuse. The fish is then fully fried through before it enters the marinade (this is cooked fish, not a raw cure), and the acidic oil-capped marinade holds it. Keep the fish fully submerged under the oil at all times, refrigerate within two hours of pouring the warm marinade, and eat within 5 to 6 days. Discard if the marinade ever smells of ammonia. Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→sardines, fresh anchovies, or chicken thigh (escabeche de pollo)

    Small oily fish behave identically; sardines and fresh anchovies fry in 45 seconds per side and need only 8 to 12 hours of marinade since they are thinner. For escabeche de pollo, the older land-animal version of the dish, brown bone-in chicken thighs fully through in the oil first (8 to 10 minutes), then build the same marinade and simmer the chicken in it for 20 minutes before resting; the longer cook and tougher protein want both the heat and the overnight.

  • Dietary vegetable escabeche→escabeche de verduras (no fish)

    Substitute the fish entirely with thick-cut zucchini, halved mushrooms, cauliflower florets, or whole baby carrots, fried or roasted until tender, then marinated the same way. The dish becomes a Spanish vegetable conserva and is fully vegan; the anchovy can be dropped or replaced with a teaspoon of miso for the glutamate.

  • Regional a sweeter, Andalusian register→with raisins and a touch of honey

    Add 30 g / 3 tbsp raisins and 10 g / 2 tsp honey to the marinade with the vinegar and wine. The sweetness against the sharp vinegar pulls the dish toward the agridulce Moorish-Andalusian style; reduce the marinade an extra minute so the honey integrates.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • mackerel → sardines, fresh anchovies, bluefish, or Spanish mackerel (sawara) Any oily, full-flavored fish suits escabeche; the acid was made for exactly this lipid load. Sardines and fresh anchovies fry faster and marinate shorter. Avoid lean white fish like cod or halibut, which go cottony and dry in the acid rather than benefiting from it.
  • sherry vinegar (Jerez) → white wine vinegar or red wine vinegar Sherry vinegar is the rounded, nutty Spanish standard and the right choice. White wine vinegar is the cleaner substitute; cut it with a teaspoon of honey to round its sharper edge. Red wine vinegar darkens the marinade and reads more rustic but works. Cider vinegar is too fruity here.
  • smoked paprika (pimentón de la Vera, dulce) → sweet Hungarian paprika plus a drop of liquid smoke, or hot pimentón (picante) The smoke is the signature; plain sweet paprika gives color and fruit but loses the defining note. If you only have sweet Hungarian paprika, a single drop of liquid smoke in the marinade approximates it. Hot pimentón (picante) makes a spicier dish; use half and half with dulce.
  • dry white wine → dry fino or manzanilla sherry, or additional vinegar plus water Fino or manzanilla sherry is the most Spanish move and adds a saline almond note; use the same quantity. To skip alcohol, replace the wine with 60 g / ¼ cup additional sherry vinegar plus 60 g / ¼ cup water, accepting a slightly sharper marinade.

Notes

Why escabeche is a preservation method, not a marinade. The name comes from the Arabic sikbaj, a medieval vinegar stew, carried into Iberia and out to Latin America and the Philippines, where it became the parent of dishes like Filipino paksiw and escabeche. The original purpose was to keep cooked fish edible for days without refrigeration: the fish is fried (the heat kills surface organisms and sets the protein), then sealed under acid and oil (the low pH and the oxygen-excluding oil cap suppress regrowth). Refrigeration has made the keeping function optional, but the flavor logic is inseparable from it. The dish must rest; eating it the day it is cooked is eating an unfinished thing.

Why mackerel specifically. Of all the fish escabeche is made from, the oily blue fish are the most rewarded by it. Mackerel carries 12 to 18% polyunsaturated fat, the fastest-oxidizing lipid of any common finfish and the source of its undeserved fishy reputation. The acid does two jobs at once here: it preserves, and it cleaves the perceived oiliness, converting the very quality that makes mackerel hard to love when mishandled into the quality that makes escabeche of mackerel one of the great cold fish dishes. A lean white fish gains nothing from the acid bath and loses moisture; this dish exists for the oily fish.

The fry is a seal, not a sear. Resist the instinct to fry the fish to deep golden-brown crisp. The flour coat is there to set the surface and give the marinade something to cling to, not to provide crunch; any crunch you build in the pan dissolves overnight in the acid anyway. Fry just until the crust sets pale gold and the flesh turns opaque, then move on. The browning you want is in the marinade, from the garlic, the anchovy, and the bloomed pimentón.

The two acid phases. The marinade vinegar is a long, infused, mellowed acid that has had a day to work into the fish. The lemon wedge at the table is a fresh, volatile, bright acid that sits on top. They are not redundant; the first is the structure of the dish and the second is the lift at the moment of eating. Skipping the lemon leaves the dish reading flat despite all the vinegar in it, because the marinade acid has gone soft and round by serving time and wants a sharp top-note against it.

Serving temperature is the most common error. This dish is cold-kitchen food, eaten at cool room temperature. Served fridge-cold, the olive oil is waxy and the flavors are muted and locked; served hot, the whole acid-cure logic collapses and you have soggy fried fish in vinegar. Forty-five minutes on the counter before serving is the difference between a dull dish and a complete one. In Spain this is tapas-bar and home-larder food, made in advance and pulled out when people arrive.

Sources
  • Adapted from the Iberian escabeche tradition, the pre-refrigeration acid-cure used across Spain and Portugal for oily fish, game birds, and vegetables. — Escabeche is a preservation method first and a flavor method second. The fish is fried to set the surface protein and build a dredge crust, then drowned in a hot acidulated marinade of vinegar, olive oil, wine, and fried aromatics. The acid (pH well below 4.5) denatures surface protein and creates an environment hostile to spoilage organisms, which is why a properly made escabeche keeps a week or more refrigerated and historically kept for days at ambient temperature. Mackerel is the ideal subject: its 12 to 18% polyunsaturated fat is the fastest-spoiling lipid of any common finfish, and the same acid that preserves it also cuts the perceived oily-fishiness that undercooked mackerel can carry. The dish is always served cold or at room temperature, after the marinade has had at least one overnight to penetrate; eating it hot defeats the entire logic.
