Pickled mustard greens (suan cai, 酸菜)

Whole heads of mustard greens lacto-fermented in a 3% salt brine for seven to ten days into a sour, crunchy, deeply funky pickle. The Cantonese and Sichuan tables both reach for it.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Greens

  1. 1000 g Chinese mustard greens (gai choy / xue li hong / suan cai cabbage)

Brine

  1. 1500 ml water
  2. 45 g kosher salt

Aromatics, optional

  1. 5 g Sichuan peppercorns optional
  2. 20 g fresh ginger, sliced optional — (2 inch piece, sliced thin)
  3. 4 each dried Chinese chiliesoptional

Execution

  1. 1
    Wash the 1000 g mustard greens carefully. Trim any browned tips. Pull the heads apart into individual leaves so the brine can reach every surface; large heads can be halved lengthwise instead. Lay the leaves on a clean tray in a single layer and air-dry 4 hours, or until they wilt visibly and lose 10 to 15% of their weight. The wilt step concentrates flavor and reduces water that would otherwise dilute the brine.4 h 10 min
  2. 2
    Bring the 1500 ml / 6 cups water to a boil with the 45 g / 3 tbsp kosher salt and any optional aromatics (5 g / 2 tsp Sichuan peppercorns, 20 g sliced ginger, 4 dried chilies if using). Stir to fully dissolve the salt. Pull off heat and cool completely to room temperature; warm brine kills the lactic acid bacteria you want to encourage, and chlorinated tap water suppresses them, so cold-and-clean is the rule.
  3. 3
    Pack the wilted greens into a clean 2-liter glass jar (a wide-mouth jar is easier to load and unload). Press them down hard with a wooden spoon as you pack to expel air; trapped air pockets harbor unwanted yeasts and molds. Pour the cooled brine over to fully submerge the greens.
  4. 4
    Weigh the greens down with a brine-bag (a zip-top bag filled with extra brine, sealed, and pressed onto the surface) or a clean glass weight. Every leaf must stay below the brine surface; any leaf that floats up will go moldy within 48 hours. Cover the jar with a clean cloth secured with a rubber band, or with a fermentation airlock lid. Do not seal airtight: the lacto-fermentation produces CO2 that needs to escape, and a sealed jar will eventually pop.
  5. 5
    Hold at 18 to 22°C / 65 to 72°F, out of direct sunlight, for 7 to 10 days. From day 3, you should see small bubbles rising through the brine and the brine itself going cloudy: this is healthy lactic fermentation. The greens will progress from bright green to olive to a uniform deep yellow-green. Taste a leaf at day 7: it should be tangy, faintly funky, crisp, and noticeably saltier than fresh. If still under-tangy, give 2 more days; if already aggressively sour, refrigerate immediately to slow the ferment.9 d 18 h
  6. 6
    When the greens are at desired tang, refrigerate the whole jar. They keep 6 months in the fridge, slowly developing more sourness; pull them at month 3 to 4 for the cleanest tang-to-funk balance. To use, lift out a leaf or two with clean tongs (never fingers, never a wet utensil), squeeze out the brine, slice as the recipe directs, and return the rest to the jar. Save the brine: it is a finished ingredient itself, used in suan cai yu fish soup and Northeastern dumplings.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional profile→Sichuan-leaning (for hot-and-sour preparations)

    Use all the optional aromatics: Sichuan peppercorns, ginger, dried chilies. Add 8 g / 1 tbsp light brown sugar to the brine. Result is the suan cai used in suan cai yu (Sichuan pickled-cabbage fish), with more aromatic complexity and a faint sweetness.

  • Regional profile→Northeastern (suan cai bai cai)

    Replace mustard greens with Napa cabbage. Salt-pack the cabbage instead of brine: layer wedges with 30 g salt per 1 kg cabbage in the jar, weight, and let the cabbage release its own brine. Ferment 14 to 21 days at 4 to 10°C / 40 to 50°F (refrigerator or cold cellar). The slower cold ferment produces the cleaner, less aggressive suan cai used in Northeastern dumpling fillings and pork-and-cabbage stew.

  • Occasion speed→a 3-day quick pickle

    Increase salt to 60 g / 4 tbsp (4% brine) and ferment at 24 to 26°C / 75 to 78°F. The greens will reach mild tang in 3 days. Less complexity, less funk, but acceptable when timing forces a shortcut.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • Chinese mustard greens → collard greens or Russian kale Substitute 1:1 by weight. The flavor will be milder (no mustard-pungency) but the structural behavior in fermentation is the same. Not a true suan cai, but a defensible pickle.
  • kosher salt (Diamond Crystal) → fine sea salt Use 30 g / 2 tbsp fine sea salt for the brine. The brine percentage is what matters; 45 g DC ≈ 30 g fine sea salt by mass.

Notes

How to use. Squeezed and chopped into a small dish on the table beside congee, where two or three pieces per spoonful contribute the salt-sour-crunch contrast that plain rice porridge needs. Stir-fried briefly with rendered pork belly, scallions, and chili for the classic suan cai chao rou over rice. Layered into beef noodle soup. Folded into Northeastern pork-and-cabbage dumplings. Floated in suan cai yu fish soup. Eaten on its own with rice and a fried egg.

The 3% brine is the entire structural rule. Lower than 2.5% and unwanted yeasts and molds win the fermentation race against the lactic acid bacteria. Higher than 4% and the bacteria themselves are inhibited and the ferment stalls. The 3% mark is the safe, classical concentration that food-science laboratories converged on for vegetable lacto-pickles, and it is what every traditional Chinese suan cai recipe corresponds to once you back-calculate from the salt-to-water ratios.

Failure modes. White surface yeast (kahm yeast) appearing as a thin film on the brine is non-toxic but indicates greens have surfaced; skim it off and re-submerge. Black, blue, or pink fuzzy mold means contamination has won; discard the entire batch and re-do with cleaner technique. The brine smelling cleanly tangy and faintly funky is healthy; smelling rotten or sulfurous is not. Trust your nose.
