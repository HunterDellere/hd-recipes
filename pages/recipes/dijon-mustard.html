Dijon mustard

Brown and yellow mustard seeds soaked in white wine, ground smooth, finished with cider vinegar and salt. Sharp, hot, and creamy in a way no jar ever is.

16 servings20 min active+ 2 d resteasyFrenchsauce
vegangluten-free

Mise en Place

Servings

Soak

  1. 60 g brown mustard seeds — (⅓ cup)
  2. 30 g yellow mustard seeds — (3 tbsp)
  3. 180 ml dry white wine, something dry like Sauvignon Blanc or unoaked Chardonnay — (¾ cup)
  4. 60 ml cold water — (¼ cup)

Finish

  1. 30 ml apple cider vinegar — (2 tbsp)
  2. 6 g kosher salt — (1 tsp Diamond Crystal)
  3. 6 g honey or sugaroptional — (1 tsp; balances bite, not for sweetness)

Execution

  1. 1
    Combine both mustard seeds, wine, and water in a glass or ceramic bowl (not metal; the heat reaction with raw mustard reads bitter against aluminum or carbon steel). Cover and refrigerate 36–48 hours. The seeds will absorb the liquid, swell, and soften enough to grind smooth.5 min
  2. 2
    After the soak the seeds should be plump and squashable between two fingers. If they still feel hard at the core, give it another 12 hours; undersoaked seeds blend gritty.1 min
  3. 3
    Transfer the entire mixture (seeds plus any unabsorbed liquid) to a high-speed blender or food processor. Add the vinegar, salt, and honey if using.2 min
  4. 4
    Blend on high for 60–90 seconds. The mixture will go from gritty paste to thick, smooth mustard with the consistency of softened butter. Scrape down the sides twice. If it seizes or refuses to move, splash in a tablespoon of cold water and keep going. The texture should be spreadable, not stiff.2 min
  5. 5
    Taste. The mustard at this point is shockingly hot, almost wasabi-grade, because the myrosinase enzyme has just hydrolyzed the glucosinolates and released the volatile isothiocyanates. This is correct; do not panic.1 min
  6. 6
    Transfer to a clean glass jar, press a piece of plastic wrap directly onto the surface, seal, and refrigerate. The bite mellows by half over the first 24 hours and stabilizes after 4–5 days. Once mellowed, the heat stays put for months.1 min

Substitutions

  • dry white wine → verjuice The traditional Burgundy ingredient: unripe grape juice. Sharper acidity, no alcohol. If you can find it, use it.
  • apple cider vinegar → white wine vinegar More austere; closer to the Maille brand profile. Cider gives a softer, more American character.
  • brown mustard seeds → all yellow mustard seeds Yields a milder, sweeter mustard, closer to American yellow than Dijon. The brown is what makes Dijon Dijon.

Notes

The chemistry: mustard seeds contain glucosinolates and the enzyme myrosinase in separate compartments. Crushing the seed in water mixes them, and the enzyme cleaves the glucosinolates into isothiocyanates — the volatile compounds that punch the back of the nose. Brown seeds carry sinigrin (sharper, hotter), yellow seeds carry sinalbin (gentler, more sulfurous). The Dijon character comes from the brown:yellow ratio of about 2:1 plus the wine acidity.

Why no heat: cooking the mustard kills the myrosinase before it can do its job. All the bite comes from the cold soak and the cold blend.

Why metal-free: mustard reacts with reactive metals (aluminum, untreated cast iron, copper) and picks up an off-bitterness within hours. Glass, ceramic, stainless steel are fine.

Why the mellow: freshly blended mustard is dominated by the shortest-lived isothiocyanates, which evaporate and react with water over the first 1–2 days. What's left is the stable, rounded heat that makes a real Dijon recognizable.

Storage: airtight in the fridge, 6 months easily. The flavor actually improves through month 2. Discard if it grows pink or fuzzy on top, but properly salted mustard is hostile to most spoilage organisms.
