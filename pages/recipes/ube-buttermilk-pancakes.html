Ube buttermilk pancakes

Ube halaya folded into a real cultured-buttermilk batter. The buttermilk's low pH is the whole trick: its acidity holds the anthocyanin firmly in the violet-to-pink zone, so the stack keeps its color with even less baking soda than a soured-milk version. Coconut cream and toasted coconut finish the plate in place of maple.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Pull the ube halaya from the fridge 15 minutes before mixing. Cold jam is too stiff to fold cleanly and tears streaks of unincorporated batter; at room temperature it disperses in a few folds without overworking the gluten.
  • Toast the desiccated coconut first and tip it onto a cold plate. Pan carryover takes toasted coconut from gold to bitter in under a minute, and it is the finishing aromatic, so it cannot be a hurried afterthought at the end.
  • These cook on a lower-heat pan than plain buttermilk pancakes. Ube anthocyanin degrades above 80 °C in dry heat, and aggressive browning flips the violet underside to brown-grey. Medium-low, butter foaming but not browning, is the target.

Execution

  1. 1
    Toast the 8 g / 4 tsp desiccated coconut in a small dry stainless skillet over medium-low heat, shaking constantly, until the shreds turn uniform pale gold and smell intensely roasted, about 90 seconds. Tip immediately onto a cold plate; pan carryover takes it from gold to bitter in under a minute.
  2. 2
    Whisk together in a large bowl: 145 g / 1¼ cups all-purpose flour, 8 g / 2 tsp granulated sugar, 5 g / 1¼ tsp baking powder, 2 g / ⅓ tsp baking soda, and 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt. The low baking soda is deliberate and buttermilk makes it possible. Ube anthocyanin is a pH indicator: vivid violet at pH 5.5 to 7.0, drifting blue-grey above 7.5, and toward pink-red below 4. Real buttermilk (pH about 4.4) is far more acidic than the vinegar-soured milk a classic ube pancake leans on, so it buffers the batter well below the blue-grey line, and just ⅓ tsp of soda both lifts the stack and lands the finished pH squarely in the violet zone.
  3. 3
    In a second bowl, whisk the 50 g / 1 large egg until broken up, then whisk in the 200 ml / ¾ cup + 1 tbsp well-shaken buttermilk, 28 g / 2 tbsp melted cooled butter, and 4 ml / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste. Keep the butter barely warm; above 70 °C it cooks the egg proteins and can seize into shards against the cold buttermilk.
  4. 4
    Pour the wet into the dry. Fold with a spatula, 6 to 8 strokes, stopping at streaks of flour. Add the 110 g / scant ½ cup room-temperature ube halaya in a single dollop on top, then fold 4 to 5 strokes more, only until the violet is distributed but lighter swirls still show. Folding the dense, sugary halaya in last rather than whisking it into the wet keeps it from weighing down the gas cells the buttermilk-and-soda reaction has already started. Rest the batter 3 minutes while the pan heats; the starch hydrates and the first wave of CO2 builds structure. Acid-milk souring
  5. 5
    Set a wide skillet or griddle over medium-low heat, one notch below plain pancakes. Add a 2 g pat of butter and let it melt fully. When the foam subsides but the butter has not darkened toward amber, the pan is at roughly 160 °C / 320 °F, below the threshold for visible Maillard browning. That is what you want: hard browning on the underside reads brown-grey through the violet crumb. The pancakes still set and rise, they just stay lighter colored.
  6. 6
    Pour 60 to 70 ml / ¼ cup batter per pancake. Cook undisturbed until bubbles break the surface and the edges set dry, 2½ to 3 minutes, slightly longer than plain pancakes since the lower heat slows the surface set. Flip once and cook the second side 90 seconds to 2 minutes, until the center springs back when pressed lightly. The finished pancake is pale lavender on both faces with darker violet flecks where the halaya pooled.
  7. 7
    Wipe the pan between batches and add a fresh 2 g pat of butter each time. Burnt milk solids from the last round lower the browning point and will char the next pancake before its interior sets. Hold finished pancakes on a wire rack over a sheet pan in a 95 °C / 200 °F oven; stacking straight off the pan traps steam and makes them soggy.
  8. 8
    Plate four pancakes per serving. Dollop the chilled 60 g / ¼ cup coconut cream over the top, drizzle the warmed 30 g / 2 tbsp ube halaya across the cream in thin ribbons, and shower the toasted 8 g / 4 tsp desiccated coconut over the whole stack. Skip the maple syrup: halaya is already 35 to 45% sugar by weight, and the coconut-cream-and-jam finish is the canonical Filipino pairing for ube breakfasts, echoing grated coconut over coconut sauce on sticky rice.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture texture→extra-fluffy whipped-white pancakes

    Separate the egg. Whisk the yolk into the wet as written. Beat the white to soft peaks and fold into the finished batter in two additions after the halaya goes in. The whipped white adds a foam-based lift that offsets the density the halaya pulls into the crumb, for a taller stack.

  • Occasion occasion→ube-and-cheese pancakes

    Fold 40 g / ¼ cup grated mild cheddar or queso de bola into the batter with the halaya. The salty-sharp cheese against the sweet violet jam is the ensaymada logic; it sharpens the halaya rather than competing. Skip the coconut cream and finish with a squeeze of calamansi or lime, which reads cleaner against the cheese.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • cultured buttermilk → whole milk soured with lemon or calamansi juice Stir 12 ml / 2½ tsp acid into enough whole milk to reach 200 ml and rest 5 minutes. It is less acidic than real buttermilk, so the anthocyanin sits closer to the blue-grey line; keep the baking soda at 2 g and do not raise it. Calamansi is the closest to the canonical Filipino flavor.
  • ube halaya → mashed cooked purple sweet potato + sweetened condensed milk (2:1 by weight) + 3 ml ube extract For the cook without Filipino groceries. Cook Stokes Purple or Okinawan purple sweet potato until soft, rice it fine, weigh 75 g and combine with 37 g sweetened condensed milk and 3 ml ube extract. It reads more sweet-potato-earthy than canonical halaya, but the color holds and the fold works the same.
  • coconut cream → lightly sweetened whipped heavy cream (60 g cream + 5 g powdered sugar) For the cook without a can of coconut milk on hand. Whip cold cream with the sugar to soft peaks. It reads richer and less coconut-bright; the toasted desiccated coconut shower picks up some of the missing aromatic.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → ube halaya make it yourselfstub Homemade halaya from frozen grated ube tastes brighter and less candied than even the best jarred version; the yam character reads through the dairy instead of being buried under sweetened condensed milk.
  • → coconut cream, scooped off the top of a refrigerated full-fat coconut milk can make it yourselfstub Fresh-pressed coconut milk from grated mature coconut yields a cream layer with more aromatic top notes than canned, which reads genuinely brighter against the violet halaya.

Notes

Why cultured buttermilk instead of vinegar-soured milk. This is the whole reason the recipe exists as its own page alongside the soured-milk ube pancakes on this site. Anthocyanin color is a pH problem, and buttermilk solves it better than a quick acid soak. Real cultured buttermilk sits around pH 4.4, well below the roughly 5.5 of vinegar-soured milk, so it buffers the batter deep in the violet-to-pink zone and gives you margin against the blue-grey drift that even a little too much baking soda causes. The higher acid load also means less soda is needed to hit a good lift, which is a second lever in the same direction. The tradeoff is that a very acidic batter can read faintly tangy against the sweet halaya, which is why the sugar and the coconut finish stay in.

Why fold the halaya in last. Ube halaya is roughly 35 to 45% sugar and 8 to 14% fat by weight: dense and viscous. Whisking it into the wet phase weighs down the leavening from the first second and gives a flatter, more leaden cake. Folding it in after the wet meets the dry preserves the gas-cell network the reaction has already started, for a taller stack with visible violet veining rather than a uniformly tinted batter.

Why no maple syrup. Maple is the default North American pancake finish, but its woody-caramel aromatics fight ube and the brown color muddies the violet. The canonical Filipino sweet-breakfast finish is coconut-based (latik, grated young coconut, coconut caramel). Coconut cream plus toasted desiccated coconut is the streamlined version and lets the halaya read clean.
