Yaki udon (stir-fried udon noodles)

Thick, chewy udon noodles stir-fried over high heat with sliced pork belly, cabbage, carrot, scallion, and a soy-mirin-oyster-sauce glaze that reduces and clings to the noodles. The Kitakyushu-origin lighter, soy-based cousin to yakisoba , faster, deeper, and the canonical Japanese weeknight noodle stir-fry.

Execution

  1. 1
    Mise en place is non-negotiable. Yaki udon cooks in 4 to 5 minutes once the wok hits temperature; if anything is not pre-cut and pre-portioned at the stove, the dish will overcook. Whisk the glaze: combine the 30 ml light soy, 5 ml dark soy, 30 ml mirin, 15 ml sake, 15 ml oyster sauce, 4 g sugar, and 30 ml dashi/water in a small bowl. Slice the pork belly 3 mm thick. Cut the cabbage, carrot, mushrooms, and scallions as specified. Have the noodles thawing on the counter (frozen udon: leave at room temperature 10 minutes, or microwave briefly, just enough to separate; fresh vacuum-pack: rinse briefly under cold water to release the package shape; dried: cook to package directions, drain, and rinse with cold water).
  2. 2
    Render the pork belly. Heat a 12-inch carbon-steel wok or wide stainless skillet over high heat until lightly smoking. Add the 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil and immediately add the sliced pork belly in a single layer (do not crowd; if the wok is small, do this in two batches). Stir-fry 2 to 3 minutes, breaking apart any clumps, until the pork edges are deep mahogany and the rendered fat coats the wok bottom. Pull the pork to one side of the wok with the spatula; do not remove. The rendered pork fat is the cooking medium for the rest of the stir-fry. Maillard sear
  3. 3
    Build the vegetable layer. Add the carrot to the cleared side of the wok and stir-fry 30 seconds (carrot needs the longest heat exposure). Add the cabbage and mushrooms; stir-fry 60 seconds, tossing aggressively to expose surfaces to the wok bottom. The vegetables should hold a slight crunch and develop spotty char marks; do not cook to soft. Add the scallion whites (reserve the greens for the finish); stir-fry 30 seconds.
  4. 4
    Add the noodles. Push the protein and vegetables to the sides of the wok to clear a center hot zone. Add the 400 g udon noodles in a single mound and pour 2 tbsp of the glaze directly over the noodles (this prevents sticking and starts caramelizing the soy on the noodle surface). Stir-fry 60 seconds in the hot zone, separating the noodles with chopsticks or a spatula , udon clumps stubbornly when cold and needs the heat-and-glaze help to release. Once the strands are loose, fold in the protein and vegetables from the wok edges.
  5. 5
    Glaze and toss. Pour the remaining glaze over the entire stir-fry. Toss aggressively for 60 to 90 seconds, lifting and folding so every noodle is coated. The glaze should reduce visibly and turn glossy, clinging to the noodles rather than puddling at the bottom. The wok will pop and steam; this is correct. Listen for the sound shift from a wet sizzle to a drier sticky-pop , that signals the glaze has reduced enough.
  6. 6
    Finish. Pull the wok off the heat. Add the scallion greens and 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil; toss 15 seconds (the sesame oil at this stage carries the aroma without burning). Taste a noodle; the salt should be assertive but not aggressive (the soy and oyster sauce do most of the work; salt is rarely needed). If flat, add a 5-finger pinch of kosher salt and toss. Transfer to two wide bowls. Top each bowl with optional shichimi tōgarashi (1/4 tsp tableside) and a small handful of katsuobushi flakes (the warm noodles cause the flakes to wave and curl , the "dancing bonito" effect). Serve immediately; yaki udon does not hold well and the noodle texture goes from chewy to gummy if it sits more than 5 minutes.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→with shrimp (kanto-style yaki udon)

    Substitute 200 g peeled medium shrimp for the pork belly. Stir-fry 90 seconds in the oil until just curled and pink; pull from the wok and reserve, returning at the noodle stage so they do not overcook. Add 1 tbsp dashi at the noodle stage (the shrimp do not render fat and the wok needs the moisture). The dish reads cleaner and lighter; the canonical Tokyo-extension version.

  • Dietary protein→vegetarian (with extra-firm tofu)

    Substitute 200 g extra-firm tofu (pressed 30 minutes, cubed 2 cm) for the pork belly. Pan-fry the tofu in 30 ml oil for 4 minutes until golden on multiple sides; pull and reserve. Add 30 ml extra dashi at the noodle stage. The dish loses the rendered-pork-fat richness; compensate with 1 tbsp extra oyster sauce or a teaspoon of miso whisked into the glaze.

  • Regional cuisine→Kokura-original Worcestershire-style yaki udon

    Replace the soy-oyster glaze with: 30 ml Bull-Dog tonkatsu sauce (or any Japanese Worcestershire-style sauce), 15 ml ketchup, 15 ml light soy, 5 g sugar, 30 ml dashi. The dish reads more yakisoba-adjacent , sweeter, sharper, more umami-and-fruit-forward , and is the original Kokura style. Use dried udon (rehydrated by boiling 8 minutes, drained, rinsed) for full historical accuracy; fresh udon also works.

  • Regional flavor→curry yaki udon (kare udon-yaki)

    Replace the glaze with: 1 tbsp Japanese curry roux block (S&B Golden, dissolved in 60 ml hot dashi), 15 ml soy sauce, 15 ml mirin. Add the curry slurry at the same point as the original glaze. The dish becomes substantially richer and curry-forward; serve with a soft-cooked egg on top broken into the noodles at the table. A modern Japanese home-kitchen variation.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • frozen udon noodles → fresh vacuum-pack udon, then dried udon (last resort) Frozen Sanuki-style udon (Sanuki-no-yume from Marukame, Hime, or Sanuki Hayauchi brands) is the gold standard , the freezing process preserves the chewy bounce that defines udon. Vacuum-pack fresh is acceptable but slightly softer. Dried udon needs an upstream boil-and-rinse and produces a noticeably less bouncy noodle.
  • pork belly → thinly-sliced pork shoulder or thinly-sliced beef chuck For pork shoulder: leaner, slightly less rich; cook 30 seconds longer to render fat. For beef: pulls the dish toward gyu-yaki-udon territory; use 1 tbsp extra mirin to round the flavor.
  • oyster sauce → fish sauce + 1 tsp extra mirin For dishes where oyster sauce is unavailable; the umami load is similar but the texture is thinner. Reduce the glaze 30 seconds longer to compensate for the missing body.

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

Notes

Why thick udon and not soba or ramen. Udon is wheat-flour-and-salt noodle, made fat (5 to 8 mm diameter), bouncy, and chewy. The thickness gives it a glaze-holding surface area that thin noodles cannot match , a stir-fried udon noodle carries roughly twice the glaze per gram of noodle as a stir-fried ramen noodle. The chewy texture also resists overcooking in the wok; thin noodles (soba, vermicelli) turn gummy at high heat under glaze. Yakisoba uses a different noodle (alkaline ramen-style noodles, called "yakisoba men" despite the name), which produces a different dish entirely.

Kokura vs. Tokyo split. The original Kokura yaki udon is a Worcestershire-sauce-based dish with dried udon and is more closely related to yakisoba (the Worcestershire sauce comes from Britain via the postwar military presence in Kitakyushu and was adopted as a Japanese-pantry sauce). The Tokyo and broader-Japan home version uses a soy-and-mirin-based glaze with fresh or frozen udon and reads as a distinctly Japanese dish. This recipe is the soy-glaze version; the Worcestershire-style is in the modifications.

The udon temperature window. Frozen udon must be thawed-but-cold when it hits the wok. Fully-cold-from-freezer noodles will not separate quickly enough, will lower the wok temperature, and will produce a steamed rather than stir-fried texture. Fully-thawed-or-warm noodles will be too soft and break under spatula pressure. The right state: pliable enough to bend without breaking, still cool to the touch. Microwave 30 seconds and stop, or counter-thaw 10 minutes before stir-fry. Vacuum-pack fresh udon should be rinsed under cold water for 10 seconds to release the noodles from each other and shed surface starch.

The "dry sizzle" finish point. The audible cue separates a glossy, clinging-glaze yaki udon from a soggy, puddled one. As the glaze reduces, the wok sound shifts from a wet steam-sizzle (high pitch, lots of moisture) to a drier sticky-pop (lower pitch, more caramelization). The transition takes 30 to 60 seconds. Pull at the dry-sticky-pop sound , going past it scorches the soy and turns the dish bitter; stopping before it leaves the noodles too saucy and the dish reads as udon-in-broth rather than stir-fried.

Eating temperature. Yaki udon must arrive at the table within 60 seconds of leaving the wok. Udon's bounce is heat-dependent , at 70°C, the chewy texture is at its peak; below 50°C, the noodles stiffen as the gluten retrogrades. A bowl of yaki udon left to cool 5 minutes is a noticeably worse dish than the same bowl eaten immediately. Plate fast, serve fast, eat fast. This is also why yaki udon is sold as a takeaway-resistant dish in Japan; it does not survive a packaging-and-transport delay the way ramen broth does.

The katsuobushi finish. Scattering a small handful of katsuobushi flakes on top of hot yaki udon is a finishing touch worth doing if you have the flakes on hand. The heat from the noodles below makes the flakes wave and curl ("dancing bonito"), which is part of the sensory experience, and the flakes contribute an additional inosinate hit that layers on the soy-oyster-sauce glaze. Optional but improves the dish meaningfully when present.
