Chicken nihari (Pakistani slow-braised stew)

Bone-in chicken slow-braised in a deeply spiced ghee-and-onion base for 90 minutes, the gravy thickened with toasted atta flour and finished with a julienned-ginger and crisped-onion garnish, fresh lemon. The chicken adaptation of the canonical Mughlai-Pakistani beef nihari that traditionally simmered overnight; the chicken version delivers the same flavor architecture in a Sunday afternoon.

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the masala paste. In a small blender or mortar, blend the 30 g rough-chopped ginger, 30 g garlic, and 60 ml water into a smooth paste. Set aside. The ginger-garlic paste is structural across South Asian cooking; pre-blending is cleaner than working from whole pieces during the cook.
  2. 2
    Toast and bloom the whole spices. Heat the 75 g ghee in a heavy 4 to 5 quart Dutch oven or wide pot over medium heat until shimmering. Add the 4 cracked cardamom pods, 4 cloves, 2 bay leaves, 5 g cinnamon stick piece, 5 g / 2 tsp cumin seeds, and 3 g cracked black peppercorns. Bloom 30 to 45 seconds; the cumin will sizzle and darken slightly, the cinnamon and cardamom will release a pronounced warm-aromatic smell, and the kitchen will fill with the toasted-spice aroma that defines South Asian cooking. Pull immediately when the cumin is fragrant; do not let the spices blacken. Blooming aromatics
  3. 3
    Brown the onion. Add the 250 g sliced onion to the spiced ghee. Stir-fry over medium heat for 12 to 15 minutes, stirring every 30 to 60 seconds, until the onion has gone from white to gold to deep mahogany-brown. The onion will progress through stages: translucent in the first 4 minutes, pale gold at 8 minutes, deep gold at 11 minutes, mahogany at 14 minutes. Pull when the slices are uniformly deep brown but not burned. Brown onions are the structural base of nihari color and depth; pale onions produce a pale-and-flat dish. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Bhuna the masala. Reduce heat to medium-low. Add the ginger-garlic paste from step 1; the moisture will hit the hot ghee and steam dramatically. Stir-fry 60 seconds; the rawness of the paste cooks off and the smell goes from sharp to warm. Add the 12 g ground coriander, 8 g Kashmiri chili powder, 2 g cayenne, 2 g turmeric, and 4 g ground fennel. Stir-fry 60 to 90 seconds, splashing with 1 to 2 tbsp water if the spices threaten to scorch (the spices need brief contact with hot ghee to bloom but cannot sit in dry ghee for more than a minute without burning). The kitchen smells deeply of spice; the ghee should pool slightly red-orange at the edges of the pan, the visual marker that the bhuna has worked. Red oil rendering
  5. 5
    Sear the chicken. Add the 1200 g chicken pieces to the masala. Toss for 3 to 4 minutes; the chicken does not need to brown deeply (it will simmer for over an hour), but the surface should turn from raw-pink to opaque-tan and pick up the masala color. Sprinkle with 12 g / 1 tbsp salt and toss.
  6. 6
    Add liquid and braise. Pour in the 1200 ml / 5 cups water (or chicken stock for richer gravy). Stir to combine. Bring to a simmer over medium-high heat, then reduce to medium-low (the surface should tremble, not boil aggressively). Cover with a tight lid. Simmer 75 to 90 minutes; the chicken will cook through within the first 25 minutes but the longer simmer extracts gelatin from the bones and integrates the spices into the gravy. Stir every 20 minutes to prevent the bottom from scorching.
  7. 7
    Toast the atta flour. While the nihari simmers, heat a small dry skillet over medium heat. Add the 30 g atta flour. Toast 4 to 5 minutes, stirring constantly, until the flour goes from pale-cream to medium-tan and smells distinctly of toasted wheat. The toasting deactivates the raw-flour starchiness and adds a nutty depth that defines authentic nihari (skipping this step produces a gummy, raw-tasting gravy). Pull from heat; let cool 2 minutes.
  8. 8
    Slurry and thicken. After the chicken has braised 75 minutes, whisk the toasted atta with the 120 ml / ½ cup cold water in a small bowl until smooth (no lumps). Stream the slurry into the simmering nihari while stirring continuously. Continue simmering 10 to 15 minutes; the gravy will thicken to a glossy coat-the-spoon consistency that clings to the chicken pieces. Taste; adjust salt. The dish is done when the chicken is tender to a fork-poke and the gravy is thick enough that a spoon dragged through the bottom of the pot leaves a brief trail.
  9. 9
    Plate and garnish. Transfer the nihari to a wide serving bowl. Scatter the 20 g julienned ginger, 60 g fried onions, 20 g chopped cilantro, and 10 g sliced green chilies over the surface. Sprinkle with 2 g / 1 tsp fresh garam masala. Serve immediately with naan, roti, or basmati rice and lemon wedges on the side. Each diner squeezes lemon over their portion at the table; the acid is structural to the eating experience and lifts the deep braise into balance.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→beef nihari (the canonical version)

    Substitute 1.5 kg beef shank or short rib (with bones), cut into 5 cm pieces. Brown the beef in batches in step 5 instead of the brief chicken sear (4 minutes per batch over high heat). Increase liquid to 1800 ml / 7½ cups. Increase braise time to 4 to 6 hours over very low heat (or pressure cook 90 minutes at high pressure). The dish is the original Pakistani breakfast nihari and is what the recipe is named for; chicken is the modern weeknight adaptation. Serves 8.

  • Protein protein→mutton or goat nihari

    Substitute 1.5 kg bone-in mutton or goat shoulder/leg, cut into 5 cm pieces. Brown in batches like beef. Liquid: 1500 ml. Braise 2.5 to 3 hours over low heat. The dish reads more aggressively flavored than chicken and slightly less rich than beef; a Pakistani favorite for special occasions.

  • Occasion pressure cooker→instant pot / pressure cooker

    Same masala-and-bhuna steps in the pot on saute mode. Add chicken and 800 ml / 3⅓ cups water (less than stovetop version because pressure cooking does not lose liquid to evaporation). Pressure cook on high 18 minutes, natural release 10 minutes. Open, stir in atta slurry, simmer on saute 8 to 10 minutes to thicken. Total cook time drops to 35 minutes from 1.5 hours; flavor is 80% of the slow-stovetop version.

  • Heat heat level→mild for kids or non-spice-eaters

    Reduce cayenne to 0 (omit) and Kashmiri chili powder to 4 g (½ tbsp). The dish loses some color depth and becomes a warmly-spiced rather than spicy stew. Acceptable for diners who find Pakistani heat overwhelming.

  • Occasion special occasion→enriched / celebration nihari

    Stir 50 g / 2 tbsp ghee into the gravy in the last 2 minutes of cooking and 60 ml / ¼ cup full-fat yogurt (whisked with 1 tsp cornstarch to prevent splitting) into the gravy off-heat just before serving. Top with 30 g additional fried onions and a generous handful of cilantro. The dish becomes the special-occasion nihari served at Pakistani weddings and Eid feasts.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • atta flour → all-purpose flour (use ¾ the quantity) See ingredients/atta-flour. Acceptable substitute; the gravy reads slightly different (less wheat-aromatic, slightly thinner). 25 g all-purpose flour for 30 g atta is the right ratio.
  • ghee → unsalted butter + neutral oil (50/50) See ingredients/ghee. Acceptable but the dish loses some of the structural toasted-milk-solid aromatic that defines authentic nihari.
  • fresh-ground fennel seeds → pre-ground fennel powder Acceptable; pre-ground fennel loses aroma faster than other ground spices but the dish is still recognizably correct.
  • bone-in chicken thighs and drumsticks → boneless chicken thighs (use 1000 g instead of 1200 g) The bone-in version is significantly better, the gelatin from the bones thickens and enriches the gravy. Boneless is acceptable for convenience; reduce braise time to 35 minutes (the chicken cooks faster without bone insulation).

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → fried onions (birista) make it yourself Homemade crispy fried shallots (or onions) are dramatically more aromatic than the bagged supermarket version, and the rendered oil from frying them can be saved and used for tempering the next dish.
  • → chicken stock make it yourself Real gelatin-rich chicken stock as the braising liquid produces a structurally richer gravy than water. The difference is significant in slow-braised dishes like nihari.
  • → garam masala make it yourselfstub Fresh-toasted-and-ground garam masala is dramatically more aromatic than supermarket pre-ground; the volatile aromatics that define garam masala oxidize within months once ground.

Notes

The seven-flavor target. Pakistani nihari is built on a precise balance of seven flavors: deep umami from the slow-braised meat and bones, layered warmth from the toasted whole spices, structural body from the ground coriander, color and gentle heat from the Kashmiri chili, the distinctive licorice-anise note from the ground fennel (without which the dish reads as a generic curry rather than as nihari), the spritely lift from the ginger garnish and lemon at the table, and the textural contrast of the crisp fried onions against the soft braised meat. All seven are non-negotiable; reducing the fennel or skipping the lemon turns the dish into a different (lesser) curry.

Why bone-in chicken is structural. The bones release gelatin into the gravy over the 90-minute simmer, transforming the consistency from "watery soup" to "spoon-coating gravy." Boneless chicken cooked the same way produces a thinner gravy that has to be over-thickened with atta, throwing off the spice-to-thickener balance. If only boneless chicken is available, supplement with 200 g chicken bones (carcass, wings) tied in cheesecloth and simmered with the dish.

The atta thickening. The toasted-flour slurry is what distinguishes nihari from other braises. Cooks who skip the atta toasting end up with raw-flour-tasting gravy (the starches need heat to cook out their raw character); cooks who skip the slurry-into-cold-water step (whisking the toasted atta with cold water before adding to hot pot) end up with lumps. The technique is conservative and safe: toast the dry atta until it smells nutty, slurry with cold water until smooth, stream into the simmering pot while stirring. Done correctly, the gravy thickens to a glossy clinging consistency; done wrong, it goes lumpy or pasty.

Beef vs chicken vs mutton, the historical context. The original Pakistani nihari was beef nihari, cooked in tandoor-oven pots overnight at the dawn-prayer hour, served with naan as a Mughlai breakfast tradition. Chicken nihari emerged in the late 20th century as a same-day adaptation; mutton nihari is the special-occasion version. All three are valid; the chicken version is the entry point for cooks trying nihari at home for the first time, the beef version is the destination once the technique is comfortable, and the mutton version is the celebration-level dish. The recipe above is calibrated for chicken; the modifications give the path to beef and mutton.

Eating mechanics. Nihari is a meal-on-its-own when served with naan or roti, the bread scoops up the gravy and chicken, lemon is squeezed at every bite, and the green chili and ginger garnish are eaten as crunchy aromatic counterpoint. With basmati rice it becomes a different dish (more curry-and-rice, less stew-and-bread), still excellent. The dish reheats beautifully and many cooks consider day-two nihari as good as day-one or better; the spices integrate further overnight. Refrigerate up to 4 days; freezes 2 to 3 months.
