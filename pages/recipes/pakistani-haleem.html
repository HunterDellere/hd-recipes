Recipe

Pakistani haleem (slow-cooked wheat, lentil, and meat porridge)

Cracked wheat, four kinds of lentils, and bone-in mutton or beef slow-braised together for 4 hours, then aggressively mashed and stirred until the grains, legumes, and meat dissolve into a single thick savory porridge. Finished with crisp fried onions, julienned ginger, fresh cilantro, green chili, and lemon. The defining Ramadan and Muharram dish of Pakistani Muslim cooking; structurally a porridge that takes a full afternoon to make and that no shortcut can fully replicate.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a 13-hour dish from start to finish if including the overnight bean and wheat soak. The active time is 90 minutes spread across an afternoon; the rest is unattended simmer. Plan the soak the night before and the cook for a Saturday afternoon.
  • Soak the wheat and dals overnight. The cracked wheat needs 8+ hours of cold-water soak to soften enough to break down during the 4-hour cook; skipping the soak adds 90 minutes to the cook and the wheat will still be slightly grainy.
  • Crisp the fried onions in advance, up to 24 hours ahead. They are a structural finish, not garnish; the textural contrast and the caramelized depth carry the dish.

Execution

  1. 1
    The night before: combine the 200 g cracked wheat, 80 g chana dal, 60 g masoor dal, 60 g urad dal, and 60 g moong dal in a large bowl. Cover with the 2000 ml cold water; soak 8 to 12 hours at room temperature. Drain and rinse in the morning before cooking.
  2. 2
    Start the meat braise. Combine the 1200 g beef shank, the 2500 ml / 10 cups water, and a pinch of salt (about 5 g) in a large 6 to 8 quart Dutch oven or stockpot. Bring to a boil over high heat; skim off the gray scum that rises in the first 15 minutes (this scum is coagulated blood proteins and is bitter; skimming gives a cleaner-tasting final dish). Reduce heat to medium-low, cover, simmer 90 minutes.
  3. 3
    Add the soaked grains and dals. Add the drained wheat-and-dal mixture to the simmering meat broth. Stir; cover, continue simmering at medium-low for 2 hours (120 minutes), stirring every 20 minutes to prevent the bottom from scorching. The grains and dals will progressively break down into a thick porridge; the wheat takes longest to disintegrate.
  4. 4
    Meanwhile, build the masala. While the haleem simmers, heat the 100 g ghee in a wide skillet over medium heat. Add the 300 g diced onion; cook 12 to 15 minutes, stirring frequently, until deeply mahogany-brown. Add the 100 g ginger-garlic paste; cook 60 to 90 seconds until the rawness has cooked off. Add the 10 g whole spices (cardamom, cloves, bay, cinnamon); bloom 30 seconds. Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Add the ground spices: 12 g / 2 tbsp ground coriander, 10 g / 1½ tbsp Kashmiri chili powder, 3 g / 1 tsp cayenne, 3 g / ¾ tsp turmeric, half (3 g) of the garam masala. Stir-fry 60 to 90 seconds, splashing with 2 to 3 tbsp water if the spices threaten to scorch. The ghee should pool red-orange at the edges. Red oil rendering
  6. 6
    Combine the masala with the haleem. After the haleem has simmered for 2 hours with the grains, pour the entire masala mixture (ghee, onions, spices, all of it) into the haleem pot. Stir to integrate. Add the 20 g / 1½ tbsp kosher salt. Continue simmering another 60 minutes, stirring every 10 to 15 minutes; the dish thickens, the meat gives up the last of its gelatin, and the spices integrate into the porridge.
  7. 7
    Mash and integrate. After the third hour, the meat should be falling off the bones. Remove the bones and any large meat chunks; shred the meat with two forks and return it to the pot (discard the bones). Now mash the entire pot aggressively: use a hand-held immersion blender for 30 to 60 seconds total, OR use a wooden potato masher and work it through the pot for 5 to 8 minutes, OR transfer half the dish to a stand blender, pulse 3 to 4 times, and return to the pot. The target texture is a thick uniform porridge where individual grains and meat fibers are barely visible; the dish should be smooth-but-textured, not a fully pureed soup.
  8. 8
    Final adjust. Simmer another 10 to 15 minutes uncovered, stirring constantly to prevent scorching, until the haleem is thick enough that a wooden spoon dragged through the bottom leaves a brief trail. Stir in the remaining 3 g / 1½ tsp garam masala. Taste; adjust salt. The dish should be deeply savory, complex-spiced, with a creamy mouthfeel from the dissolved dals and a faint meaty depth from the dissolved beef.
  9. 9
    Plate and garnish. Ladle into wide shallow bowls. Top generously with the 100 g fried onions, 30 g julienned ginger, 30 g chopped cilantro, 15 g torn mint, 15 g sliced green chilies, and (optionally) a small dusting of chaat masala. Pass the 16 lemon wedges separately; each diner squeezes 2 wedges over their portion, the acid is structural to balancing the dish. Serve with naan or pita on the side for scooping.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→chicken haleem (faster, less traditional)

    Replace the beef shank with 1200 g bone-in skin-on chicken thighs. Reduce the initial meat simmer from 90 minutes to 25 minutes. Total cook drops from 4 hours to about 3 hours. The dish reads lighter and less complex; chicken haleem is the modern weeknight adaptation and is gaining acceptance in Pakistan but is not the canonical Muharram/Ramadan version.

  • Occasion occasion→pressure cooker (Instant Pot, drops cook time to 2 hours)

    Combine the soaked grains, soaked dals, beef, and 2000 ml water in a 6-quart pressure cooker. Pressure cook high 60 minutes; natural release 20 minutes. Open, shred the meat off the bones. Add the masala (made separately on the stovetop as written). Pressure cook another 15 minutes high; natural release. Mash with immersion blender. The dish reads 85% of the slow-stovetop version; significantly faster.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→Hyderabadi haleem (with saffron and almonds)

    Add a pinch (0.3 g) of saffron threads, soaked in 30 ml hot water, in the last 15 minutes. Add 50 g of slivered toasted almonds at the same time. Increase the cardamom in the whole-spice mix to 6 pods. The dish becomes the canonical Hyderabadi version, richer and more aromatic; the most internationally famous haleem style.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • cracked wheat (dalia) → pearled barley (use the same weight) Barley is the structural backup grain when cracked wheat or bulgur are unavailable. The dish reads slightly less grain-aromatic but recognizably correct. Use the same weight and the same overnight soak.
  • bone-in beef shank or short rib → bone-in lamb or mutton shoulder Mutton is the canonical alternative; the dish reads slightly leaner-aggressive. Same weight, same braise time. Boneless cuts do not work; the gelatin from the bones is structural.
  • four dals (chana, masoor, urad, moong) → three dals (omit masoor) or two dals (chana + urad only) The four-dal blend is the canonical Hyderabadi-Karachi target; three or two is acceptable when not all are available. The dish reads slightly thinner with fewer dals; increase the chana dal slightly to compensate (90 g instead of 80 g if running 3 dals; 120 g if running 2).

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → fried onions (birista) make it yourself Homemade crispy fried shallots are dramatically more aromatic than the bagged supermarket version, and the rendered oil from frying can be saved for the next dish. The structural finish of haleem; the fried onions are not optional.
  • → garam masala make it yourselfstub Fresh-toasted-and-ground garam masala is dramatically more aromatic than supermarket pre-ground; the volatile aromatics oxidize within months once ground. Haleem rewards the fresh blend more than most dishes because the long cook integrates the spice deeply.

Notes

Ramadan and Muharram context. Haleem is the canonical Pakistani Muslim Ramadan and Muharram dish, and the reason carries through to its physical structure. Ramadan haleem (broken-fast dish) is dense, calorie-rich, and slow-digesting; the four-dal blend provides sustained energy through the long fast day, and the rich ghee-and-meat base satisfies the body after 14+ hours without food. Muharram haleem (commemorating the martyrdom of Imam Hussain) is communal and prepared in large quantities at mosques and at home, served to all comers regardless of sect; the dish is structurally a gesture of communal generosity, and the all-day cook is part of the ritual. The dish reads markedly different in a non-religious context; it is heavier than diners expect.

The mash is structural. Haleem is defined by the porridge texture, not the ingredients themselves. The same wheat, dals, and meat cooked without the aggressive mash would be a stew. The mash dissolves the wheat into the gravy, breaks down the dal cell walls, shreds the meat fibers, and produces the single-textured creamy porridge that is the dish. Modern Pakistani kitchens use immersion blenders or stand mixers (the dough hook works); traditional kitchens used a long wooden paddle worked through the pot for 30 to 45 minutes. The texture target is uniform but with faint visible fibers of meat and grains; fully pureed haleem reads like baby food and is wrong.

The garnish is structural. Haleem without the fried onion, ginger, cilantro, mint, chili, and lemon is a heavy beige porridge that diners will find oppressive. The garnishes provide textural contrast (crisp onions, crunchy raw ginger), aromatic lift (mint, cilantro), bright heat (green chili), and finishing acid (lemon). Each diner adds the garnishes individually at the table, customizing the balance. The chaat masala dust is optional but Pakistani; it adds the structural tangy-sulfurous note that pulls the dish into the Pakistani idiom rather than the Arabic harees idiom from which it descends.

Why the four-dal mix. Each dal contributes a different texture and flavor: chana dal stays slightly firm and provides earthy body, masoor dal cooks down to a creamy puree and helps the dish bind, urad dal provides the characteristic creamy mouthfeel and a slight stickiness, moong dal cooks fast and softens the overall texture. The four together produce a complex layered porridge; a single-dal haleem reads flatter. The exact proportions vary regionally; this Karachi-Lahore version uses roughly equal parts of all four with a slight emphasis on chana dal for body.

Storage. Refrigerated in a sealed container, 5 days; the dish thickens considerably as it cools (the starches retrograde) and may need a splash of water on reheat. Freezes well for 3 months; thaw overnight in the refrigerator and reheat gently with a splash of water. The dish improves significantly overnight as the spices and dals integrate further; many Pakistani cooks consider day-two haleem as the proper version and day-one as undercooked.

Sources
  • Pakistani-Mughlai Muslim tradition (Hyderabadi, Karachi, Lahore expressions) — Haleem (also called daleem) is the canonical Pakistani Muslim slow-cooked porridge, deeply tied to Ramadan (broken fast) and Muharram (Shia mourning). The dish descends from Arabic harees (cracked wheat and meat porridge), brought to South Asia by Muslim traders and Mughal kitchens, and adapted with the addition of multiple lentils and the South Asian spice profile. Three regional Pakistani versions: Hyderabadi haleem (the most internationally famous, heavy on the saffron and the Arab influence), Karachi haleem (the everyday Pakistani version, leaner spice profile), and Lahori haleem (richer, more aggressive on the ghee and the brown onion). This recipe is the Karachi-Lahori expression, which is the most accessible for home cooks.
