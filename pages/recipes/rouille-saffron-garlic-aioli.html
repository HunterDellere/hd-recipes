Rouille (saffron-garlic aioli for bouillabaisse)

The Provençal saffron-and-garlic mayonnaise that crowns bouillabaisse and bourride, built from soaked bread (or a boiled potato), pounded raw garlic, saffron-infused broth, egg yolk, and a slow stream of olive oil. The name (literally "rust" in French, for the deep red-orange color) refers to the saffron-cayenne tint that distinguishes rouille from a plain aioli. Spread on toasted baguette croutons, dropped into the broth, or stirred directly into the soup as a thickener-flavor-bomb.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Rouille is a partner sauce. The canonical recipe uses warm broth pulled from a bouillabaisse pot; if making standalone, bloom a generous pinch of saffron in 60 ml warm seafood stock at least 15 minutes before starting so the carotenoids dissolve. Cold or dry saffron lands the sauce dull yellow rather than rust-red.
  • Bring the egg yolk and olive oil to room temperature before mounting. Cold ingredients refuse to emulsify cleanly into the bread-and-garlic base and the rouille breaks within minutes.
  • Use a mortar and pestle if you have one. The food-processor version is functionally equivalent but loses the textural slight-graininess of pounded garlic that defines the classical Marseille sauce.

The base

  1. 30 g stale baguette or country bread, crust removed, torn into rough pieces
  2. 60 ml broth from the bouillabaisse pot (or fish stock), warm
  3. 0.1 g saffron threads (extra, if using standalone fish stock) — (a small pinch, about 10 threads; only if making rouille standalone without bouillabaisse broth, otherwise omit)
  4. 15 g garlic clove, peeled, germ removed if visibly green; halved lengthwise — (3 to 4 cloves; aggressive raw garlic is canonical; reduce to 2 cloves for a milder version)
  5. 3 g fine sea salt

The emulsion

  1. 18 g large egg yolk — (1 yolk; the lecithin emulsifier; pasteurized yolks recommended if serving raw to vulnerable diners; see homemade alternatives for the bread-only no-egg version)
  2. 1 g red pepper flakes (or piment d'Espelette if available)
  3. 200 g extra-virgin olive oil

The finish

  1. 5 g fresh lemon juice

Execution

  1. 1
    Soak the 30 g / ¾ cup torn bread in the 60 ml / ¼ cup warm saffron broth for 2 to 3 minutes, until the bread has fully absorbed the liquid and turned a deep yellow-orange from the saffron. Squeeze the bread gently with the back of a spoon to fully saturate. If making rouille standalone (no bouillabaisse broth available), warm the fish stock with the additional 0.1 g pinch of saffron crumbled in for 5 minutes off the heat, then proceed.
  2. 2
    In a heavy mortar, pound the 15 g / 3 to 4 garlic cloves with the 3 g / ½ tsp salt to a smooth paste; the salt is abrasive and breaks the garlic down faster than pounding alone. Work the paste 1 to 2 minutes until completely smooth, with no visible garlic chunks. Alternative: process the garlic and salt in a small food processor 30 seconds; the result is functionally the same but with less texture. Mortar pounding (clay vs granite)
  3. 3
    Add the saffron-soaked bread to the mortar (or food processor); pound or pulse until the bread is fully incorporated into the garlic paste, 1 to 2 minutes. The mixture should be a uniform pale-orange paste with no visible bread pieces. Add the 18 g / 1 egg yolk and the 1 g / ¼ tsp red pepper flakes; pound or pulse to incorporate, 30 seconds.
  4. 4
    Mount with the 200 g / 1 cup olive oil in a slow steady stream, pounding constantly with the pestle (or processing on low) to keep the emulsion stable. Start with a near-drip rate; as the emulsion sets and visibly thickens (after the first 50 ml or so), the oil can be added in a thin steady stream. The finished rouille should be the texture of mayonnaise (thick enough to hold a spoon stand-up shape) and a deep red-orange color from the saffron and red pepper flakes. Egg-yolk emulsion
  5. 5
    Whisk in the 5 g / 1 tsp lemon juice; taste and adjust salt and red pepper flakes (the rouille should read garlic-forward, with a noticeable but not painful heat, and the saffron should come through as a faint mineral-honey note in the finish). Transfer to a small serving bowl. Serve at room temperature, alongside the bouillabaisse with toasted baguette croutons; each diner spreads rouille on the croutons and floats them in the broth, or stirs the rouille directly into the soup.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Dietary no-egg version→as a bread-only emulsion (the older Provençal canonical version)

    Skip the egg yolk; double the bread to 60 g and the saffron broth to 120 ml. Pound the bread, garlic, salt, and red pepper flakes to a paste; mount with olive oil in the same slow-stream technique. The bread starch acts as the emulsifier in place of egg lecithin; the result is slightly less stable than the yolk version and benefits from being made just before service. This is the older Marseille canonical preparation, predating widespread egg use in the sauce.

  • Regional potato base instead of bread→as the Toulon-style potato-thickened version

    Replace the 30 g bread with 75 g of waxy potato (Yukon Gold), peeled, boiled until soft, drained, and mashed; pound the potato into the garlic paste and proceed. The result is firmer, denser, and reads more aioli-adjacent; common in the western Provençal coast and into the Spanish border regions.

  • Occasion food-processor speed→in a small food processor instead of a mortar

    Combine the garlic, salt, soaked bread, yolk, and red pepper flakes in a small food processor (1 to 2 cup capacity); pulse to a uniform paste; with the motor running on low, drizzle in the olive oil through the feed tube in a slow steady stream until fully incorporated. The result is functionally identical to the mortar version but with a slightly smoother texture (no slight-grain from the mortar pounding). The mortar is canonical and produces a better mouthfeel; the food processor is faster.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • piment d'Espelette → red pepper flakes (crushed red pepper) Piment d'Espelette is a Basque-Provençal mild dried red chili (Scoville 1,000 to 4,000) with a fruitier, more complex profile than red pepper flakes; difficult to find outside specialty stores. Crushed red pepper flakes are the practical substitute and the standard supermarket replacement; the dish reads slightly hotter and less complex.
  • saffron → no good substitute; reduce dish ambition Saffron is structural in rouille (both the color and the flavor); without it the sauce is a Provençal aioli with cayenne, not a rouille. Use ¼ tsp paprika as a color-only substitute and the dish becomes "spicy aioli" rather than rouille specifically.
  • extra-virgin olive oil → half olive oil and half neutral oil A fruity-not-bitter EVOO is canonical, but cooks who find pure EVOO too aggressive can use half EVOO and half neutral oil (grapeseed, sunflower) for a more delicate finish. The dish reads slightly less Provençal but is more accessible.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → stale baguette or country bread, crust removed, torn into rough pieces make it yourself A naturally-leavened country loaf carries the slight tang and open-crumb structure that absorbs the saffron broth and emulsifies cleanly. Stale (2 days old) is best for rouille because the gluten has set and the crumb tears into uniform pieces. Any good baguette or country sourdough works; the bread is the textural and structural thickener.

Notes

Rouille and aioli are cousins, not the same sauce. Aioli (the Provençal and Catalan canon) is garlic, salt, and olive oil pounded into an emulsion with no other thickener; the sauce is a structural challenge because the only emulsifier is the small amount of natural mucilage in raw garlic, and the emulsion breaks easily. Rouille adds bread (or potato) as a starch thickener, egg yolk as a lecithin emulsifier, and saffron and chili as the defining flavor-and-color signatures; the result is more stable, more flavorful, and structurally a different sauce. Both are mounted with olive oil in the same technique, but rouille is the bouillabaisse-specific finish and aioli is the broader Provençal table sauce.

The bread-vs-potato decision is regional. Marseille and the eastern Provençal coast use bread (the classical canonical version, documented in Reboul); Toulon and the western coast toward the Spanish border use potato (more aioli-adjacent, firmer texture). Both are correct; the bread version reads softer and more saffron-forward, the potato version reads firmer and more garlic-forward. For bouillabaisse specifically the bread version is the more traditional pairing.

The egg-or-no-egg decision is historical. The original 19th-century rouille was bread-and-oil only; egg yolk was added to the canonical preparation in the early 20th century as the technique stabilized in restaurant kitchens. The egg version is more stable and easier to mount; the no-egg version is the older Marseille canonical preparation and reads slightly looser and more rustic. Either is correct.

Service. Rouille is canonically spread on toasted baguette croutons (rounds of bread, toasted, rubbed with raw garlic) which float on the broth at service; each diner spoons rouille onto a crouton, drops the crouton into their bowl of broth, and watches the rouille slowly dissolve into the soup. The alternative is to stir a spoonful of rouille directly into the broth at the table, which thickens and flavors the broth in one move; this is less canonical but common in modern restaurant service. The leftover rouille is excellent on toast, with grilled fish, or spooned onto a baked potato; it keeps 3 days refrigerated in a sealed jar.

Stability. The rouille emulsion is stable for 3 days refrigerated; bring back to room temperature 30 minutes before service for the best texture. If the emulsion breaks (separates into oil and paste), reconstitute by whisking 1 tsp of warm water into the broken sauce in a clean bowl and slowly streaming the broken rouille back in; the water rebuilds the emulsion structure.
