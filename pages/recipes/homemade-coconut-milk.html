Coconut milk (homemade from grated mature coconut)

Mature grated coconut blended with hot water and squeezed through cheesecloth, twice. The first pressing yields thick "coconut cream" used for finishing curries; the second pressing yields thinner "coconut milk" used as a base. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 2 items ▾
  1. 300 g mature coconut, grated (fresh or frozen unsweetened)
  2. 600 ml hot water (about 80°C / 175°F)

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: place the 300 g grated coconut in a heat-safe bowl. Pour over 300 ml of the hot water. Let stand 10 minutes; the coconut will absorb water and the fat will start to separate.
  2. 2
    Stub: transfer the soaked coconut and water to a high-powered blender. Blend on high 60 seconds.
  3. 3
    Stub: line a fine-mesh sieve with two layers of cheesecloth set over a clean bowl. Pour the coconut-water mixture through. Twist the cheesecloth and squeeze (carefully — the liquid is warm) to extract every drop. This first pressing is "thick coconut milk" or "coconut cream" and is what most curry recipes mean by "coconut milk" or "coconut cream."
  4. 4
    Stub: return the squeezed coconut pulp to the blender. Add the remaining 300 ml hot water. Blend 60 seconds. Strain through fresh cheesecloth, squeezing thoroughly. This second pressing is "thin coconut milk" used as a base liquid in soups and braises.
  5. 5
    Stub: combine the two pressings (or store separately if a recipe specifies). Refrigerate; use within 2 days. Frozen in ice-cube trays, 2 months.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the mature-vs-young coconut question (mature is for cooking; young is for drinking water and the soft jelly), the coconut-cream-vs-coconut-milk distinction (the cream rises during refrigeration and can be skimmed for richer applications), the canned-vs-homemade trade-off (canned is genuinely good and the homemade project is mostly for fresh-coconut access in tropical climates or for the experience), the freshness and oxidation behavior (homemade coconut milk goes off fast — 2 days max), and the splitting-during-cooking issue (homemade is more prone to splitting because it lacks the stabilizers in canned coconut milk; whisk briefly before adding to hot dishes). The stub above produces working coconut milk; the deeper drafting pass should add the practical context.
