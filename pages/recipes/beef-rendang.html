Beef rendang

Padang dry-cooked beef. Chuck or shank simmered in spice paste and coconut milk for three to four hours until the liquid completely evaporates and the meat finishes by frying in its own released coconut oil, producing a black-brown lacquered exterior and a paste that coats every fiber. The wet stage in the middle is kalio; the dry endpoint is rendang.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Start the rice late. Rendang reduces for three to four hours, but the active management intensifies dramatically in the last 45 minutes once the coconut oil begins to split. Time the rice so it finishes 10 minutes before the rendang reaches its dry endpoint.
  • Use a wide, heavy pan, at least 30 cm / 12 inch, ideally cast iron or a wide enameled dutch oven. The reduction depends on surface area; a tall narrow pot triples the cook time and the meat tends to braise rather than dry-fry. Crowding kills the dish.
  • Toast the terasi (5 g / 1 tsp wrapped in foil over a burner for 60 seconds per side) before pounding the paste. Raw terasi does not integrate properly into the bumbu and the dish ends up smelling fishy in the finished plate.
  • Have a wooden spoon dedicated to this dish. The kecap-and-coconut-oil residue stains plastic spoons; the wooden spoon is what every Indonesian grandmother uses for the constant stirring of the last hour.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, beef

  1. 1200 g beef chuck or shank, trimmed of large fat caps, cut into 4 cm / 1½ inch cubes against the grain
  2. 8 g kosher salt

Phase 2, bumbu spice paste

  1. 30 g dried red Holland or Fresno chilies, stems pulled, soaked in hot water 20 minutes to soften, seeds left in
  2. 200 g shallot, peeled, rough chopped — (about 8 to 10 medium shallots; the volume looks excessive and is correct, shallot is the structural body of the paste)
  3. 50 g garlic, peeled — (about 12 to 15 cloves)
  4. 40 g fresh ginger, peeled, rough chopped — (about 5 cm / 2 inch knob)
  5. 40 g galangal, fresh, peeled, rough chopped — (about 5 cm / 2 inch knob; not interchangeable with ginger, this is the piney-citrus aromatic backbone)
  6. 30 g candlenut, rough chopped — (about 6 to 8 nuts; thickens the paste and contributes oil-rich body. Macadamia is the closest substitute)
  7. 4 g ground turmeric
  8. 30 g lemongrass, outer layers stripped, lower pale heart only, sliced paper-thin
  9. 5 g toasted terasi

Phase 3, aromatics added whole

  1. 40 g lemongrass, bruised with the back of a knife, tied in knots — (2 to 3 large stalks; bruised whole stalks infuse over the long cook then get fished out)
  2. 6 each kaffir lime leaves, bruised between palms — (or 4 leaves of the daun salam Indonesian bay if you can find it, used here as the substitute)
  3. 1 each cinnamon stick — (about 5 cm / 2 inch; cassia, not Ceylon, for the deeper warm note)
  4. 2 each star anise — (2 whole pods)
  5. 4 each cloves — (4 whole cloves)

Phase 4, coconut and braise

  1. 3 × 13.5 oz / 400 ml can full-fat coconut milk
  2. 60 g desiccated coconut, toasted in a dry skillet over medium heat 4 to 5 minutes until deep golden, then ground coarse in a spice grinder
  3. 15 g palm sugar, shaved from the disc
  4. 15 g tamarind paste
  5. 8 g kosher salt, additional for the braise

Execution

  1. 1
    Salt the 1200 g cubed beef with the 8 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt and rest at room temperature 30 minutes while you build the paste. The salt draws surface moisture and starts to break down the muscle fibers, giving the long braise a head start on tenderization.
  2. 2
    Drain the soaked 30 g chilies, reserving 60 ml / ¼ cup of the soaking liquid. Pound or blend the chilies, 200 g shallot, 50 g garlic, 40 g ginger, 40 g galangal, 30 g candlenut, 4 g / 1½ tsp turmeric, 30 g sliced lemongrass heart, and 5 g toasted terasi to a smooth paste, working in the chili soaking liquid as needed to keep the blender moving. The target texture is wet, smooth, slightly grainy from the candlenut and lemongrass fiber, brick-orange in color. About 4 to 5 minutes of pulse-and-scrape work in a food processor; longer if pounding by hand.
  3. 3
    Heat 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in the wide heavy pan over medium heat. Add the spice paste and fry, stirring constantly, until the raw chili-shallot smell shifts to a deeper, jammy, almost caramelized aroma and the paste darkens from bright brick-orange to deep brick-red with visible oil separating at the edges, 12 to 15 minutes. This is the bumbu bloom, the single most flavor-determining step. Stop too early and the paste tastes raw and harsh in the finished dish; the long fry deepens the Maillard products in the shallot-garlic-chili paste and renders out the candlenut oil.
  4. 4
    Add the 40 g bruised lemongrass, 6 bruised kaffir lime leaves, 1 cinnamon stick, 2 star anise, and 4 cloves to the paste. Fry 60 seconds to bloom the aromatics in the hot oil. Blooming aromatics
  5. 5
    Add the 1200 g salted beef cubes. Toss to coat in the paste; the beef will not brown, it slowly cooks in the paste-and-coconut bath that follows. Stir 2 minutes to seal the surface in spice.
  6. 6
    Pour in the 1200 ml / three cans of coconut milk, the 15 g / 1 tbsp palm sugar, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp tamarind paste. Stir to combine. Bring to a bare simmer (not boil, hard boiling breaks the coconut emulsion early and the dish gets greasy). Reduce heat to maintain a gentle simmer, partially covered (lid cracked, not closed), for 90 minutes. Stir every 15 minutes. The liquid should slowly reduce by half and the color should shift from cream-yellow to a richer brick-orange. This is the kalio stage, a wet curry; if you stop here you have a fine dish, but rendang is not done.
  7. 7
    Uncover completely and add the 60 g toasted-and-ground kerisik (desiccated coconut). Stir thoroughly. Continue simmering uncovered, stirring every 10 minutes, for another 60 to 75 minutes. The liquid will continue to reduce; the sauce shifts from cream-orange to deep amber to brown. Around the 60-minute mark the texture will start to look glazed and the bottom of the pan will show streaks of bright orange oil separating from the brown solids. This is the coconut oil beginning to split out; the dish is now transitioning from braise to fry.
  8. 8
    The final stage. Stir constantly now (you cannot leave the pan), scraping the bottom on every pass. The remaining liquid evaporates in 15 to 25 minutes; the spice paste deepens from brown to nearly black; the released coconut oil becomes the cooking medium and you can hear the dish go from a wet simmer to a quiet sizzle as the last water boils off. Pull when the meat is glossy, deeply lacquered, almost black-brown, and the paste clings tightly with very little free oil pooling. Taste the 8 g / 1½ tsp additional salt in now if needed; the seasoning at this concentration reads dramatically differently than it did during the braise.
  9. 9
    Pull off heat. Fish out the lemongrass stalks, cinnamon stick, star anise, kaffir lime leaves, and cloves; they have given their flavor and the texture is unpleasant in the finished plate. Rest 10 minutes off heat; the spice paste continues to set on the meat and the dish reaches its final lacquered texture. Serve over steamed jasmine rice with sliced cucumber and tomato (timun and tomat, the Padang cooling foil) and a side of sambal.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→kalio (the wet-stage version)

    Pull at the end of the 90-minute partial-cover simmer (after step 6). The dish is still a wet curry at this point, the meat tender but the sauce intact at 50 percent reduction. Kalio is an earlier and legitimate stage of the same dish; some Padang restaurants serve both and let diners choose. Add a final 5 g salt to compensate for the diluted seasoning at this stage.

  • Protein protein→chicken rendang (rendang ayam)

    Substitute 1200 g bone-in, skin-on chicken thighs for the beef. Cut the total simmer time roughly in half, 45 minutes covered plus 30 to 40 minutes uncovered. Chicken does not need the four-hour collagen breakdown that beef chuck requires; pushing the cook time to beef-rendang length will dry the chicken into stringy fibers.

  • Dietary protein→jackfruit rendang (vegan)

    Substitute 1000 g drained young green jackfruit (canned in brine, not syrup) for the beef. Add a small amount of soy sauce or vegan fish sauce (10 g / 2 tsp) to compensate for the missing beef glutamate. Cut total cook time to 90 minutes; jackfruit does not need the long collagen breakdown. The fibrous jackfruit texture mimics shredded beef remarkably well.

  • Heat heat level→mild (Western-friendly) version

    Drop the dried chilies to 15 g and remove the seeds before soaking. The dish loses some of its lacquered red-black color and the lingering throat-heat that defines Padang cooking; the spice depth remains intact. Do not eliminate chili entirely, the capsaicin is part of the long-cook flavor development, not just heat.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • beef chuck or shank → beef brisket (point cut) Brisket runs slightly fattier than chuck and produces a richer, glossier finished dish; it also breaks down faster, pull 20 minutes earlier than the chuck timeline. Avoid brisket flat (too lean) and short rib (too fatty, the dish goes greasy).
  • kaffir lime leaves → daun salam (Indonesian bay leaf) or bay leaf Daun salam is the canonical leaf in rendang and is harder to source. Kaffir lime leaves are the working Western substitute; bay leaf is acceptable but flatter. Three bay leaves for six kaffir lime leaves.
  • desiccated coconut (for kerisik) → frozen grated coconut, thawed and toasted The fresh-frozen version produces a richer kerisik with more aromatic depth. Toast slightly longer (6 to 8 minutes) because of the higher moisture content.

Notes

Why the dry endpoint matters. Rendang as a dish is defined by the point where the simmer transitions to a fry. As long as free liquid is in the pan, the meat is braising in coconut milk and absorbing flavor wet; once the water evaporates and the coconut oil splits out, the meat is frying in spice-saturated coconut oil and developing a different texture and flavor profile entirely. The lacquered surface, the almost-black color, the way the paste clings to every fiber, these only happen in the final dry-cook stage. A rendang pulled at the kalio (wet) stage is a fine dish; a rendang pulled at the dry stage is a fundamentally different one.

Why the bumbu bloom takes 12 to 15 minutes. The wet aromatic paste has to lose its raw edge before the long braise begins. Underdone bumbu produces a dish where the chili reads raw, the shallot tastes oniony, and the candlenut never integrates; the long braise will not fix this. The 12-to-15-minute fry develops Maillard products on the shallot-garlic-chili matrix, renders the candlenut oil out into the paste, and concentrates the spice oils. Stop when the paste visibly darkens and oil begins to separate at the edges of the pan.

Why kerisik. Toasted-and-ground coconut (kerisik) is the textural and flavor thickener of the final stage. The toasted aromatic depth balances the long-cooked sweetness of the reduced coconut milk; the fiber provides body so the finished dish coats the meat in a thick paste rather than a thin glaze. Skipping kerisik produces a thinner, less complex finished sauce.

Storage. Rendang famously keeps. The combination of dry-cook stage (very low free water), high oil content (rendered coconut oil), and antimicrobial spice load means the finished dish holds 5 to 7 days in the fridge and 3 months frozen. Reheats well in a covered pan over low heat, splash of water to loosen. Many Padang cooks consider day-two or day-three rendang superior to fresh; the spices integrate further on standing.

Why this is not a curry. Rendang is technically a dry-cooked spiced meat, not a curry. The Western convention of describing it as "Indonesian beef curry" obscures the structural difference: a curry has sauce, a rendang does not. The wet version (kalio) is closer to what Western diners expect from the description "beef curry." Both are legitimate; the labels matter.

  • Adapted from Padang (Minangkabau, West Sumatran) tradition — Rendang originated as a preservation technique.
