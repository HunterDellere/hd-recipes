Ube halaya

Filipino jam of grated purple yam (Dioscorea alata) slow-cooked with coconut milk, sweetened condensed milk, evaporated milk, and butter until thick, glossy, and spreadable. Stub.

12servingsabout 600 g / 2 cups (1 standard 12 oz jar plus change)
1 h 30 mintotal1 h 10 min active
mediumdifficulty
Filipinosaucevegetarian

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 7 items ▾
  1. 454 g grated ube (frozen, thawed and drained; or fresh boiled and riced)
  2. 1 × 13.5 oz / 400 ml can full-fat coconut milk
  3. 200 g sweetened condensed milk
  4. 150 ml evaporated milk
  5. 30 g unsalted butter
  6. 1 g fine sea salt
  7. 3 g vanilla bean paste

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: combine 454 g grated ube, 240 ml / 1 cup full-fat coconut milk, 200 g sweetened condensed milk, 150 ml / ½ cup evaporated milk, and 1 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt in a heavy 3-quart non-reactive saucepan. Whisk to dissolve any lumps in the condensed milk.
  2. 2
    Stub: bring to a bare simmer over medium heat (steam rising, no rolling boil — boiling above 80 °C degrades the anthocyanin pigments and the color shifts from vivid violet toward muddy brown-purple). Drop the heat to low immediately and cook 45 to 60 minutes, stirring with a flat-edged wooden spoon every 2 to 3 minutes initially and constantly during the final 15 minutes, until the mass thickens to the texture of soft, glossy mashed potatoes and pulls cleanly away from the pan bottom when the spatula passes through.
  3. 3
    Stub: cut the heat. Stir in 30 g / 2 tbsp unsalted butter and 3 g / ½ tsp vanilla bean paste until the butter melts and the surface goes glossy. Transfer to clean glass jars, cool to room temperature uncovered (about 30 minutes), then seal and refrigerate.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the anthocyanin chemistry (cyanidin-3-glucoside and peonidin-3-glucoside, pH-sensitive violet that holds in the natural range of cooked ube and dairy; degrades above 80 °C), the four-dairy enrichment reasoning (coconut milk for the lipid carrier and Filipino-traditional flavor; sweetened condensed for sugar and Maillard-caramel depth; evaporated for protein and cooked-milk depth; butter for gloss and mouthfeel), the fresh-vs-frozen ube question (fresh purple yam needs to be boiled 45 minutes and riced before use; frozen grated ube — sold by Tropics, Ramar, and various Filipino brands at H Mart, Seafood City, and online — is genuinely good and the working pantry ingredient outside the Philippines), the macapuno variant (with strips of macapuno coconut folded in at the end), the burning-on-the-bottom problem (heavy pan, low heat, constant stirring in the last 15 minutes; if it scorches, do not stir in the burnt layer — the bitter compounds spread through the whole batch), the storage profile (refrigerated 2 to 3 weeks, frozen 3 months), and the buttered-glass-jar trick (rub the inside of the jar with a thin film of butter before filling; the halaya lifts cleanly when scooped weeks later).
