Beet greens and feta filo pie

A Greek hortopita built on beet greens: the greens wilted and squeezed bone-dry, bound with feta, scallion, dill, and egg, then sealed between buttered-and-oiled filo layers and baked to a shattering golden crust. The squeeze-dry step is the whole dish; wet greens steam the filo soggy, dry greens let it shatter. The feta and egg carry the calcium that pairs with the beet-green oxalate, and a hit of lemon zest in the filling keeps the iron-mineral leaves bright under the rich pastry.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Thaw the filo correctly and ahead: overnight in the refrigerator, then 1 to 2 hours at room temperature still sealed in its plastic. Filo thawed too fast tears and sticks into an unusable block. Unroll it only when the filling is made and the butter-oil is melted, and keep the stack covered with parchment or a barely-damp (never wet) towel the entire time you work, exposed sheets dry to brittle shards in 2 to 3 minutes.
  • The squeeze-dry step is the dish. After wilting, the greens must be wrung out hard in a clean towel or pressed in a sieve until almost no liquid releases; budget time for it to cool enough to handle. Skipping or rushing this is the single most common way the pie fails, the crust goes soggy from below.
  • Have everything for the filling cooked, squeezed, cooled, and mixed before you open the filo. Once the filo is unrolled the assembly must move steadily so the sheets do not dry out; this is not a stage to stop and chop.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the greens

  1. 500 g beet greens (the leaves and stems off 4 to 5 medium beets), rinsed in two changes of cold water, thick stems cut from the leaves and finely chopped, leaves coarsely chopped, kept in two separate piles — (2 large bunches; the leaves wilt down to about a third of their raw volume, then lose more water in the squeeze)
  2. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  3. 60 g scallions, sliced thin, white and pale-green parts sliced thin, dark-green tops reserved or sliced in too — (1 bunch, about 5 to 6 scallions; the canonical allium of hortopita, milder and greener than onion)
  4. 9 g garlic cloves, minced, peeled and minced fine — (2 to 3 cloves)
  5. 2 g kosher salt

Phase 2, the filling

  1. 250 g feta, crumbled, drained from its brine and crumbled into rough 1 cm pieces
  2. 60 g Greek yogurt, full-fat
  3. 2 each eggs, lightly beaten — (2 large; the binder that sets the filling into a sliceable custard-bound mass rather than a loose pile)
  4. 15 g fresh dill, chopped, fronds chopped, tough stems discarded
  5. 3 g fresh lemon zest, zested on a microplane, no white pith — (zest of 1 lemon; the limonene that keeps the cooked greens bright under the rich pastry)
  6. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked
  7. 0.5 g ground nutmeg

Phase 3, the pastry

  1. 340 g filo pastry sheets, thawed overnight in the fridge then 1 to 2 hours at room temperature, kept covered while you work
  2. 80 g unsalted butter, melted, melted and combined with the olive oil below into one brushing fat
  3. 40 g extra-virgin olive oil, for brushing
  4. 6 g sesame seeds optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Wilt the greens. Wash the 500 g beet greens in two changes of cold water. Warm the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil in a wide pot over medium-high. Add the finely chopped stems and cook 2 minutes; add the 60 g sliced scallions and the 9 g / 2 to 3 minced garlic, cook 1 minute until fragrant. Add the chopped leaves in handfuls with the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt, tossing each addition down, and cook 3 to 4 minutes until fully wilted and collapsed. Tip into a sieve set over a bowl and let cool enough to handle, 10 minutes. Blooming aromatics
  2. 2
    Squeeze the greens bone-dry. This is the step the whole pie depends on. Working in two batches, gather the wilted greens into a clean kitchen towel, twist hard over the sink, and wring until almost no liquid releases; a 500 g raw bunch should give up a startling amount of water and reduce to a tight dense ball. Pull the squeezed ball apart with your fingers so the greens are loose again rather than a compressed clump. Residual water here steams the filo soggy from inside, so squeeze harder than feels necessary.
  3. 3
    Make the filling. In a large bowl, combine the squeezed greens, the 250 g crumbled feta, the 60 g / ¼ cup Greek yogurt, the 2 beaten eggs, the 15 g / ½ cup chopped dill, the 3 g / zest of 1 lemon, the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper, and the 0.5 g / ⅛ tsp nutmeg. Mix thoroughly with your hands or a fork until the filling is cohesive and the feta is evenly distributed. Taste a small pinch (the egg is raw, taste judiciously): it should read salty-savory from the feta, bright from the lemon and dill, with the greens as the backbone. Add salt only if it reads flat, the feta usually carries enough.
  4. 4
    Set up the filo station. Heat the oven to 190°C / 375°F with a rack in the lower-middle. Melt the 80 g butter and stir in the 40 g / 3 tbsp olive oil to make one brushing fat. Brush a 23x33 cm / 9x13 inch baking pan with the fat. Unroll the thawed filo and lay it flat near the pan; immediately cover the stack with a sheet of parchment topped with a barely-damp towel, and uncover only to lift one sheet at a time. A torn sheet is fine, the next brushed layer bonds over it; patch and proceed rather than discard.
  5. 5
    Build the bottom crust. Lay one filo sheet in the pan, letting the edges overhang, and brush it all over with the butter-oil. Repeat, stacking and brushing, for 8 sheets total (if your sheets are smaller than the pan, alternate the direction of overhang so the base is fully covered). Brush each sheet, the fat between layers is what makes the crust shatter; an unbrushed sheet bakes into a leathery layer. This is the base the filling sits on.
  6. 6
    Fill and fold. Spread the entire filling evenly over the bottom crust, pressing it into a level layer to the edges. Fold the overhanging filo edges in over the filling to partly enclose it, brushing the folded flaps with fat as you go. This seals the filling and reinforces the rim.
  7. 7
    Build the top crust and score. Lay and brush the remaining filo sheets (8 sheets) over the top one at a time, brushing each with the butter-oil, tucking the edges down inside the pan rim to seal the pie like a parcel. Brush the very top generously. With a sharp knife, score through only the top few layers of filo (not down into the filling) into 12 squares; pre-scoring lets the pie bake through cleanly and makes it cut neatly without shattering the whole crust. Sprinkle the optional 6 g / 2 tsp sesame seeds over the top and flick a little water across the surface (the steam helps the top layers separate and crisp).
  8. 8
    Bake. Bake at 190°C / 375°F for 40 to 50 minutes, until the top is deep golden-brown and crisp and the filo sounds hollow and shatters when tapped. If the top browns before 40 minutes, tent loosely with foil and continue; the pie needs the full time for the egg-bound filling to set and the bottom crust to dry and crisp. A pie pulled early has a set top but a pale, soggy base.
  9. 9
    Rest and cut. Let the pie rest 15 to 20 minutes before cutting. This is functional: the egg-bound filling continues to set as it cools, and a pie cut straight from the oven slumps and weeps while a rested one holds clean square slices. Cut along the score lines with a sharp serrated knife. Serve warm or at room temperature, the latter is how it is most often eaten in Greece, with a tomato-cucumber salad and lemon wedges.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion individual hand pies (triangles)→beet-green and feta triangles (spanakopita-style)

    Cut the filo into long strips about 8 cm wide, brushing each strip with the butter-oil. Place a spoonful of filling at one end and fold flag-style into triangles, brushing the outside. Bake on a lined sheet at 190°C / 375°F for 20 to 25 minutes until golden. This is the party-meze format; the same filling yields roughly 24 triangles. They freeze well unbaked and bake straight from frozen with 5 extra minutes.

  • Occasion a coiled village pie (strifti)→a spiral greens pie

    Lay 2 brushed filo sheets, spoon a line of filling along one long edge, and roll into a long thin cigar. Coil the cigar into a snail in an oiled round pan, continue with more filled cigars building the spiral outward until the pan is full. Brush the top, bake at 180°C / 350°F for 45 to 55 minutes. The strifti (twisted) shape gives more crisp-crust-to-filling ratio and a rustic look; it is the country-pie format across northern Greece.

  • Texture a richer, custard-set filling (galatopita-leaning)→greens pie with a softer custard bind

    Add a third egg and 80 g / ⅓ cup whole milk or evaporated milk to the filling, and reduce the feta to 200 g. The filling sets into a softer, more custardy interior rather than a dense cheese-and-greens mass; bake 5 to 10 minutes longer to set the extra liquid. This reads more like a quiche-greens-pie hybrid and is gentler for cooks who find a feta-forward filling too salty-sharp.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • beet greens → Swiss chard, spinach, or a mix of wild greens Swiss chard is the closest (same species); use the same weight and the same squeeze-dry discipline. Spinach is the spanakopita classic, milder and sweeter; it wilts and releases water faster, so squeeze it especially hard. A mix of chard, beet greens, dandelion, and a handful of herbs is the truest hortopita and the best use of a foraged or farmers-market haul; keep the total at 500 g squeezed-equivalent.
  • feta → Bulgarian sirene, a feta-and-ricotta blend, or a dairy-free feta Bulgarian sirene is the closest swap (a brined sheep-and-goat cheese, functionally feta). Cutting the feta with 100 g ricotta or cottage cheese gives a milder, creamier, less salty filling, good for those who find feta aggressive. Dairy-free feta works for a vegan pie (also swap the yogurt for a plant yogurt and the eggs for 2 tbsp / 18 g ground flax in 90 g water, and brush with all olive oil instead of butter); the texture is looser but the dish holds.
  • unsalted butter (brushing fat) → all olive oil, or ghee All olive oil (replace the butter gram-for-gram) is the rustic-village choice and makes a vegan-adjacent, slightly coarser, more savory crust, very traditional for hortopita. Ghee gives an even more brittle, nutty crust and does not burn as readily. A butter-and-oil blend (as written) is the balance of brittle shatter and savory depth.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → feta, crumbled make it yourselfstub You cannot easily make real PDO feta at home, but marinating a block of good feta in olive oil with lemon zest, oregano, and chili for a few days deepens it and softens the salt-aggression, and the herb-infused marinating oil becomes the brushing fat or the salad dressing that goes alongside the pie; it is the small upgrade that ties the meal together.

Notes

Why the squeeze-dry step is the entire dish. Beet greens are 88 percent water raw, and even after wilting they hold a great deal of it. Filo is so thin (under half a millimeter per sheet) that any moisture released from the filling during baking steams the bottom crust limp from the inside, and the pie that should shatter instead bends and weeps. Wringing the wilted greens bone-dry in a towel, hard enough that almost no liquid releases, is what lets the filo crisp through to the base. This is the single step that separates a hortopita that cuts into clean crisp squares from one that collapses into a soggy mess. Squeeze harder than feels reasonable; you cannot over-do it.

Why the fat between every layer is structural. Filo contains no laminated butter and no leavening; it is just stretched wheat dough. The shatter comes entirely from the fat the cook brushes between the sheets at assembly. Each brushed sheet bakes into a crisp plane mechanically isolated from its neighbors by a film of set fat, so the crust fractures cleanly instead of baking into one continuous leathery layer. An unbrushed sheet anywhere in the stack reads as a chewy band in the finished crust. The butter gives the finest brittle shatter; the olive oil blended into it adds a savory note and keeps the butter from setting rock-hard, which is the right balance for a savory pie. All-olive-oil is the rustic village version and equally correct.

The calcium pairing and the lemon lift, in this format. As in any beet-green dish, the heavy oxalate load wants a calcium partner in the same meal; here the feta, the Greek yogurt, the eggs, and the calcium-rich greens themselves cover it without any special effort, which is part of why a cheese-bound greens pie is such a natural format for this leaf. The lemon zest folded into the filling does the second job every cooked-beet-green dish needs: cooked greens drift toward a flat earthy register, and the limonene in the zest plus the bright dill and scallion keep the iron-mineral leaves lifted under the rich buttered pastry. Zest rather than juice in the filling, the juice would add water the squeeze-dry step worked to remove.

Filo handling is the part that intimidates and shouldn''t. The two rules cover almost everything: thaw it slowly (overnight in the fridge, then to room temperature still wrapped), and keep the working stack covered at all times so the exposed sheets do not dry to shards in the two or three minutes they sit in open air. Beyond that, tears do not matter in a layered pie, the next brushed sheet bonds over any tear, so patch and keep moving rather than discarding torn sheets. Cooks new to filo should buy the thicker country-style (horiatiko) sheets, which are sturdier and far more forgiving at a small cost to the final delicacy.

Make-ahead, serving temperature, and leftovers. The pie is excellent warm and arguably better at room temperature, which is how it is most often eaten in Greece, as a meze, a lunchbox slice, or a light supper with salad. It is a strong make-ahead dish: assemble it fully (unbaked), refrigerate up to a day, and bake straight from the fridge with 5 to 10 extra minutes; or bake it and serve at room temperature within the day. Store baked leftovers at room temperature loosely covered rather than refrigerated, because the cold sets the fat hard and softens the crust; re-crisp slices in a hot oven (200°C / 400°F for 8 to 10 minutes) rather than a microwave, which steams the filo limp. Unbaked triangles (the meze modification) freeze well and bake straight from frozen.
