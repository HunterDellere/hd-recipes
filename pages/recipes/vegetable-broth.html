Vegetable broth

Kombu, dried shiitake, onion, carrot, celery, leek, and a tomato simmered briefly into a clean, deeply umami-rich vegetable broth. The east-meets-west default, with modifications for purely Western mirepoix and Japanese kombu-shiitake dashi.

Execution

  1. 1
    Set up the umami foundation. Place the 15 g kombu and 25 g dried shiitake in the bottom of an 8-quart stockpot. The kombu and shiitake will start extracting glutamate and ribonucleotides into the cold water before the heat goes on; this cold-soak start is the right approach for both, and saves time over a separate dashi step.
  2. 2
    Add the vegetables. Layer the 1 onion, 1 leek, 2 carrots, 3 celery stalks, 1 plum tomato, and 1 halved garlic head over the kombu and shiitake. Pour the 3000 ml / 12 cups cold water over and add the 2 bay leaves, 5 g / 1 tsp peppercorns, and optional 8 g parsley stems.
  3. 3
    Bring slowly to a simmer over medium heat. Watch closely once the temperature climbs above 60°C / 140°F: if the kombu is left in past this point, it can release bitter compounds (kelp slime) that make the broth read marine-and-bitter rather than savory-and-clean. Pull the kombu out at the moment the first bubbles break the surface (about 8 to 10 minutes in); pure dashi technique calls for kombu removal before any boiling.
  4. 4
    Simmer 30 to 40 minutes at a bare tremor (just below 90°C / 195°F). Vegetable stock cooks faster than animal stocks because there are no bones to extract gelatin from; over-simmering past 45 minutes drives the vegetable flavors past sweet-and-clean into cooked-down-vegetal. The shiitake should be fully rehydrated and have given up most of their flavor by minute 30.
  5. 5
    Strain. Lift the vegetables and shiitake out with a spider. (The rehydrated shiitake are excellent; reserve them in fridge to use in stir-fries or as a soup garnish; they have already given their flavor to the broth but the texture is still good.) Strain the broth through a fine-mesh sieve into a second pot.
  6. 6
    Cool quickly. Vegetable broth is more delicate than animal stocks and develops off-flavors faster at room temperature. Divide between wide shallow pans in an ice bath, stirring every 5 minutes. Refrigerate as soon as the temperature drops below 10°C / 50°F.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional profile→pure Japanese kombu-shiitake dashi

    Skip the vegetables and herbs entirely. Use only 20 g kombu and 30 g dried shiitake in 2 L / 8 cups cold water. Cold-soak 30 minutes. Heat slowly to just below simmer (60 to 70°C / 140 to 160°F), maintain at that temperature for 20 minutes (do not boil; kombu will go bitter). Strain. Used in shojin ryori (Buddhist vegetarian cuisine), Japanese vegetarian noodle soups, and as the base for vegetarian Japanese braises and simmered dishes.

  • Regional profile→pure Western mirepoix (no kombu / shiitake)

    Skip the kombu, shiitake, and tomato. Use the same vegetables and herbs (mirepoix only). Add 30 g dried porcini mushrooms in their place for some umami contribution; rehydrate in a small bowl with 200 ml hot water for 20 minutes, add the soaking liquid (decanted off any grit) to the broth pot. The result is a Western-leaning vegetable stock that pairs better with European cooking applications. Notably less rich than the kombu-shiitake version; this is why most professional kitchens default to chicken stock for vegetable-forward dishes.

  • Regional profile→roasted-vegetable broth

    Roast the onion, carrot, celery, leek, tomato, and garlic head at 220°C / 425°F for 30 to 35 minutes until edges char and onion is deeply caramelized. Then proceed with the recipe (kombu and shiitake added to the pot with the roasted vegetables and water). The result is darker, richer, and more savory; pairs especially well with mushroom risotto, roasted-vegetable soups, and as a deglaze for vegetable braises.

  • Occasion time-shortcut→pressure-cooker version

    Use the same ingredients and water in an Instant Pot. Add kombu only at the end (after pressure release), letting residual heat extract for 5 minutes; pressure-cooking the kombu can produce off-flavors. High pressure 20 minutes, natural release. The broth is slightly cloudier; flavor extraction is 85 to 90% of the simmered version.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • kombu → a tablespoon of light miso whisked in at the end Different but adjacent umami source. Add 15 g white miso to the strained broth (do not boil it; whisk into hot but not simmering broth). The broth reads slightly hazier and miso-leaning; acceptable for soups and braises, less ideal for clean sauce work.
  • dried shiitake → dried porcini Different mushroom flavor profile; porcini reads more European, shiitake more East Asian. Use the same weight (25 g) and proceed identically. The broth pairs better with European cooking (Italian risotto, French sauces) than the shiitake version.
  • plum tomato → 15 g sun-dried tomato or 15 g tomato paste Both contribute the natural glutamate that fresh ripe tomato provides. Acceptable substitutes when fresh ripe tomatoes are out of season.

Notes

Why the kombu-shiitake combination. Kombu contributes monosodium glutamate (MSG) naturally, the foundational umami compound; dried shiitake contributes inosinate-style ribonucleotides; the two together produce a synergistic umami amplification (research-confirmed: combining a glutamate source with a ribonucleotide source amplifies perceived savory intensity by 5 to 10x compared to either alone). This is the entire structural premise of Japanese dashi and the reason a vegetable broth built on the kombu-shiitake foundation tastes substantially deeper than a Western mirepoix-only broth at the same ingredient weight.

Why no carrot-skinning. The skin of carrots, onions, and leeks contributes color, mineral notes, and additional umami compounds that peeling discards. The only exception is dirty produce; wash thoroughly but do not peel. The onion skin in particular produces the gold color that distinguishes vegetable broth from "vegetable water."

Why short cook time. Vegetable broth has no bones, no collagen, no extended gelatin-extraction chemistry. The flavor extraction from vegetables is fast (15 to 30 minutes); past that, the vegetables lose their bright top-notes and the broth tastes cooked-down rather than fresh-extracted. The 30 to 40 minute simmer is the sweet spot.

Use cases. The east-meets-west default version is the universal vegetarian/vegan substitute for chicken stock: risotto, vegetable soups, braised greens, simmered grains, deglaze. The pure dashi version is for Japanese cooking (vegetarian miso soup, simmered tofu, soba dipping sauce, shojin ryori). The pure mirepoix version is for Western cooking applications where the kombu-shiitake umami would read out-of-place (a French gratin, an Italian minestrone, etc.). The roasted version is for deeper, richer dishes (mushroom risotto, French onion soup made vegetarian, vegetable braises).

Storage. Refrigerated: 5 days (vegetable broth keeps less long than animal stocks because there is no fat layer to seal it). Frozen in 500 ml containers: 4 months. Frozen in ice cube trays: 4 months. Vegetable broth loses brightness faster than animal stocks; use within 3 months of freezing for best results, or use specifically for cooking applications where the freshness of the broth is less critical (long-simmered braises, risotto, etc.).
