Ukoy

Filipino shrimp and vegetable fritters: a thin rice-flour batter wrapped around mung bean sprouts, sweet potato, and carrot matchsticks with a single shrimp pressed into each disc, deep-fried to a glassy shatter and served with a sharp garlic-chili-cane-vinegar dip.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The sawsawan (vinegar-garlic-chili dip) needs a 20 minute steep before serving. Build it FIRST so the garlic and chili have time to mellow into the vinegar; freshly mixed sawsawan reads raw-garlic-sharp and undercuts the fried fritter.
  • Marinate the shrimp 10 to 30 minutes in fish sauce and soy. The salt diffuses into the muscle and is the only seasoning the shrimp gets; under-marinated shrimp read bland against the seasoned vegetable shell.
  • Use ice-cold water for the rice flour batter and mix it just before frying. Cold water keeps the starch granules from pre-gelatinizing; warm or rested batter goes gummy and the fritters fry stodgy rather than lacy.
  • Heat the oven to 95 degrees C with a wire rack on a sheet pan before the oil reaches frying temperature. Fritters move from the oil directly to the rack to hold while you fry the remaining batches; piling them on paper towel steams the shell and kills the crunch.

Phase 1, dipping sauce (sawsawan)

  1. 120 ml cane vinegar
  2. 10 ml fish sauce
  3. 6 g garlic, finely minced — (2 medium cloves)
  4. 1 each bird's eye chili, stemmed and thinly sliced — (or 2 if you want heat)

Phase 2, shrimp marinade

  1. 115 g large shrimp, peeled and deveined, tails removed
  2. 10 ml fish sauce
  3. 10 ml light soy sauce

Phase 3, vegetables and batter

  1. 170 g mung bean sprouts, rinsed, fully drained
  2. 225 g carrots, peeled, cut into 6 cm × 3 mm matchsticks
  3. 225 g sweet potato, peeled, cut into 6 cm × 3 mm matchsticks
  4. 265 ml ice-cold water
  5. 170 g white rice flour
  6. 4 g kosher salt

Phase 4, frying

  1. 1200 ml neutral oil for frying

Execution

  1. 1
    For the sawsawan: combine 120 ml / ½ cup cane vinegar, 10 ml / 2 tsp fish sauce, 6 g / 2 cloves minced garlic, and 1 sliced bird's eye chili in a small bowl. Stir and set aside, the garlic and chili need a 20-minute steep in the acid to mellow into the vinegar before serving.
  2. 2
    In a separate bowl, toss 115 g / 8 large peeled shrimp with 10 ml / 2 tsp fish sauce and 10 ml / 2 tsp light soy sauce. Marinate 10–30 minutes, the salt diffuses into the shrimp's muscle and seasons it from the inside; this is the only seasoning the shrimp gets, so do not skip it.
  3. 3
    In a large bowl, toss 170 g / 6 oz mung bean sprouts with 225 g / 8 oz carrot matchsticks and 225 g / 8 oz sweet potato matchsticks until evenly distributed. Set aside.
  4. 4
    In a medium bowl, whisk 265 ml / 9 fl oz ice-cold water with 170 g / 1 cup white rice flour and 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt until smooth. The batter should pour like heavy cream, thin enough to coat each strand of vegetable, thick enough to cling. Cold water keeps the starch granules from pre-gelatinizing; warm batter goes gummy and the fritters fry stodgy.
  5. 5
    Pour the batter over the vegetables. Fold with a spatula until every matchstick and sprout is evenly coated. The mixture looks underbatered, that is correct; ukoy is held together by the fried shell, not by the batter volume.
  6. 6
    Heat oven to 200°F (95°C) with a wire rack set in a rimmed sheet pan to hold finished fritters. In a wide Dutch oven or wok, heat 1200 ml / 5 cups neutral oil over medium-high to 375°F / 190°C. Use an instant-read thermometer; the temperature is the recipe.
  7. 7
    Form each ukoy: scoop 85 g / ½ cup of the vegetable mixture into a large heatproof spoon (a serving spoon or a small ladle), pressing gently to mound. Lay 1 marinated shrimp flat across the top, then top with another 85 g / ½ cup of vegetable mixture and press the whole assembly into a 8 cm / 3-inch disc roughly 1.5 cm / ½-inch thick. The shrimp should be fully sandwiched.
  8. 8
    Slide the formed ukoy off the spoon directly into the 190 °C oil, use a second spoon or a small offset spatula to coax it off in one piece. Form a second ukoy and slide it in. Do not crowd the oil; two at a time keeps the temperature recovery fast.
  9. 9
    Fry 2 minutes on the first side until the underside is golden and rigid. Flip with a slotted metal spatula. Fry the second side 2 minutes until uniformly deep gold and the shrimp shows pink-orange through the lacy shell. Total fry time is 4 minutes; longer and the 225 g sweet potato turns mealy, the shell goes too dark.
  10. 10
    Lift to the wire rack with the slotted spatula. Season immediately with 1 pinch kosher salt while hot, surface salt adheres to the oil-wet shell. Transfer to the warm oven to hold while frying the rest.
  11. 11
    Between batches: skim any browned batter shrapnel out of the oil with a spider, left in, it burns and contaminates the next round. Return oil to 190 °C before forming and frying the next two ukoy. Repeat until all 8 fritters are done.
  12. 12
    Serve immediately with the sawsawan. The shell stays glassy for 5–10 minutes; longer than that and steam from the inside softens it from underneath. There is no save, eat them hot.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→pure vegetable ukoy (ukoy gulay)

    Skip the shrimp and the marinade. Add an extra 85 g / 3 oz vegetables (a mix of julienned squash, kabocha, and additional sweet potato). Vegan-friendly; the sawsawan still drives the dish.

  • Texture shape→small ukoy bites

    Use 30 g / 2 tbsp of vegetable mix per fritter and a small whole shrimp (30 count or smaller); fry 1.5 minutes per side. Yields 24 small fritters; party-snack format. Higher surface-to-mass ratio means more crunch per bite at the cost of vegetable presence.

  • Regional cuisine→Visayan ukoy with kalabasa

    Replace the sweet potato with peeled, julienned kabocha or butternut squash. The squash sweetness reads more directly; reduce the sweet potato's ambiguity.

  • Regional flavor→annatto ukoy

    Bloom 4 g / 1 tbsp annatto seeds in 30 ml / 2 tbsp warmed neutral oil for 2 minutes; strain. Whisk the colored oil into the batter. The annatto adds a marigold-orange color and a faint earthy note that is traditional in Pampanga-style ukoy.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • cane vinegar → rice vinegar + ¼ tsp brown sugar per ¼ cup Closest functional approximation. Lacks the slight molasses note; readable on a side-by-side, fine standalone.
  • mung bean sprouts → finely julienned green papaya or daikon Loses the sprout-specific sweet grassy note; gains slightly firmer crunch. Acceptable when sprouts are unavailable.
  • white rice flour → cornstarch + all-purpose flour, 1:1 Mimics the shatter-crisp shell within ~10–15%. The shell goes soggy 3–4 minutes faster; serve immediately. Pure rice flour is the right tool if you can get it.
  • large shrimp → small bay shrimp, several per fritter Filipino home cooking often uses very small whole shrimp left intact, head-on. More Maillard-roasted shrimp flavor in each bite; messier visual.
  • bird's eye chili → serrano chili Less heat, slightly grassier note. Use 1½ where the recipe calls for 1.

Notes

Why rice flour, not wheat: rice flour has no gluten. When the cold batter hits 190 °C oil, the surface starch gelatinizes, dehydrates, and locks into a glassy matrix within 15 seconds, there is no stretchy gluten network to absorb steam back into chewiness. This is the texture of ukoy. A wheat-flour batter at the same ratio fries up to a dense, chewy fritter; the dish is an ingredient choice, not a recipe.

Why ice-cold water: cold water keeps the rice-flour starch granules from pre-gelatinizing in the batter. A warm batter starts forming the gel before frying, gives off steam too slowly, and the result is gummy through the middle. The cold-water rule is the same as for tempura, and for the same molecular reason.

Why 190 °C, not lower: at 175 °C the batter sets too slowly; the vegetables release water faster than the shell hardens, and the fritter is greasy. At 190 °C the shell sets fast enough that the vegetables steam-cook inside the shell rather than oiling it. Above 200 °C the shell goes too dark before the inside cooks through. The 5-degree window is the recipe.

Why immediately serve: fried rice-flour shells go from glassy-shatter to brittle-and-soft within ~15 minutes as steam from the inside rehydrates the shell from underneath. There is no oven-hold or microwave that recovers it. Form, fry, eat — this is a hot-station dish, not a buffet one.

The sawsawan is the recipe''s acid lever. Without it, the fritter is greasy and one-note. With it, every bite resets — the sharp cane vinegar cuts the oil, the garlic adds top-note pungency, the chili heats, the fish sauce brings glutamate from underneath. Serve a generous puddle per person, not a tablespoon.
