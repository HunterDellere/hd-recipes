Canh chua cá (Vietnamese tamarind sour soup with catfish)

Southern Vietnamese sour soup built on a tamarind-acidified broth, with pineapple, tomato, taro stem (bạc hà), okra, and bean sprouts simmered briefly, catfish steaks slipped in for a 4 to 5 minute poach, and the bowl finished with raw rau ngổ, culantro, fried garlic, and Thai chili. The dish lives or dies on the acid-sugar-salt-umami curve, calibrated so the tamarind reads bright and not muddy.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Steamed jasmine rice is the partner and must be on FIRST. Rinse 360 g / 1¾ cups rice three times, start in the cooker (or stovetop covered at a bare simmer 18 minutes, then 10 minute rest off heat). The 45 minute rice timeline overlaps the entire cure-extract-build-poach sequence.
  • Cure the catfish with salt and rice vinegar 10 minutes before any cooking. The surface cure neutralizes the slight mud-note of farm-raised catfish and firms the protein so the steaks do not break in the simmer; both effects need the full 10 minutes to develop.
  • Tamarind extraction needs a 15 minute hot-water soak. Start it the moment the fish cure goes on; the two passive windows overlap exactly.
  • The fish poaches in a 4 to 5 minute window at a bare simmer (85 to 90 degrees C). Boiling the broth at that stage breaks the catfish into shreds; have the heat dialed down before the steaks slide in.

Phase 4, finishing herbs and aromatics

  2. 8 g culantro (ngò gai), thinly sliced
  3. 4 g Thai bird chili, thinly sliced (optional) — (1 to 2 chilies; for diners who want heat; passed at the table rather than added to the pot)
  4. 12 g garlic, finely minced — (3 cloves; for the fried-garlic finish)
  5. 20 ml neutral oil
  6. 1 each lime wedges — (1 lime cut into wedges; passed at the table for diners who want extra acid)

Execution

  1. 1
    Cure the fish. Rinse the 600 g catfish steaks under cold water with 1 tbsp salt and 1 tbsp rice vinegar rubbed gently over the surface (this neutralizes the slight mud-note characteristic of farm-raised catfish), then rinse again in clean cold water and pat dry. Dust the steaks lightly with 4 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt on both sides. Set aside for 10 minutes while you build the broth, the surface cure firms the protein and reduces the risk of breakage in the simmer.
  2. 2
    Extract the tamarind. Place 50 g tamarind pulp in a heatproof bowl with 200 ml / ¾ cup hot water (just-off-boil). Soak 15 minutes. Mash with a fork to break up the pulp, then push the slurry through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean bowl, scraping the underside of the sieve to capture the fruit purée and discarding the seeds and fibers. You should have roughly 200 ml of medium-thick tamarind liquid. Reserve.
  3. 3
    Bloom the aromatics. In a 4 L / 4 qt heavy pot, heat 20 ml / 1½ tbsp neutral oil over medium. Add 30 g sliced shallot and 12 g smashed garlic; cook 2 to 3 minutes until softened and the edges turn golden. Do not brown deeply, the broth should read clean, not roasted. Blooming aromatics
  4. 4
    Build the sour broth. Add the 200 ml tamarind liquid and 1400 ml / 6 cups water to the pot. Bring to a gentle simmer. Add 45 ml / 3 tbsp fish sauce and 25 g / 2 tbsp palm sugar; stir to dissolve. Taste; the broth should land in the sour-salty-sweet curve, the acid bright and forward, the salt deep, the sugar present but in support. Adjust: more tamarind if it reads flat, more sugar if too sharp, more fish sauce if it lacks depth. The broth is the dish; spend the 90 seconds to calibrate it now. Tamarind souring (sour-broth extraction and balance)
  5. 5
    Add the firm vegetables. Slip in the 200 g pineapple wedges and 150 g taro stem batons. Simmer 4 to 5 minutes, the taro stem should soften but still hold a slight crunch; pineapple gives up a small amount of its juice into the broth and starts to round the acid. Add the 200 g tomato wedges and 100 g okra pieces; simmer another 2 minutes, the tomatoes should slump slightly but hold their wedge shape, okra should be just-tender.
  6. 6
    Poach the catfish. With the broth at a bare simmer (no rolling boil, which breaks the fish), slip the 600 g salted catfish steaks gently into the pot in a single layer. Do not stir; nudge with a spoon only to submerge. Hold the surface at 85 to 90°C / 185 to 195°F for 4 to 5 minutes for 2 to 3 cm steaks; the flesh should turn opaque white through to the bone and the steaks should be just-firm to a chopstick tap. Fish doneness target is the internal flesh at 60 to 63°C / 140 to 145°F at the thickest point. Overshoot ruins the texture; pull on the leading edge of doneness. Oil poaching
  7. 7
    Fry the finishing garlic. While the fish poaches, heat 20 ml / 1½ tbsp neutral oil in a small pan over medium. Add 12 g minced garlic; fry, stirring constantly, 60 to 90 seconds until the garlic turns light gold (not deep brown, which reads bitter). Remove from heat immediately; the residual heat continues the browning by another half-shade.
  8. 8
    Finish and serve. Off the heat, scatter 100 g mung bean sprouts over the surface of the soup (the residual heat wilts them just enough). Tear or chop the 15 g rau ngổ and 8 g culantro coarsely; reserve half for the table, scatter the other half over the pot. Spoon the fried-garlic oil over the surface. Ladle into wide bowls, distributing fish, vegetable, and broth proportionally. Pass the sliced Thai chili, the remaining herbs, and the lime wedges at the table for diners to dress their own bowls. Serve immediately with steamed jasmine rice in separate bowls; the canonical eating order is a spoon of broth-and-vegetable over rice, then a chopstick-pull of fish, alternating.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein fish→shrimp canh chua (canh chua tôm)

    Substitute 500 g large head-on shell-on shrimp for the catfish. Skip the salt cure. Add the shrimp at the end of Phase 6 (after the vegetables), simmer 90 seconds to 2 minutes until the shells turn brilliant orange and the flesh just opaque. The shrimp version is lighter and reads slightly sweeter; tighten the tamarind by 5 g to compensate.

  • Protein fish→snakehead (cá lóc) or sea bass

    Both work; cá lóc is the canonical Mekong-delta choice and the broth reads slightly meatier with it. Sea bass fillets work but are more delicate, reduce the poach to 3 to 4 minutes and handle with care.

  • Regional pineapple→green mango

    In peak green-mango season, substitute 150 g green-mango wedges for the pineapple. The broth reads sharper and more grassy; reduce tamarind to 35 g to keep the total acid in balance. A regional Mekong variation.

  • Occasion serving format→family-style with bún (rice vermicelli)

    Serve the canh chua over a bowl of cool rice vermicelli noodles instead of with side rice. Ladle the hot soup over the noodles; the noodles soak up the broth. A common Saigon café service.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • tamarind pulp (block, seeded) → concentrated tamarind paste (jarred, seedless) Use 35 g of the concentrated jarred paste in place of 50 g block pulp. Skip the hot-water soak; whisk the paste directly into 200 ml of hot water until smooth. The flavor is slightly flatter than fresh-pulp extraction but the dish still works. Avoid "tamarind sauce" products which include sugar and vinegar.
  • taro stem (bạc hà) → celery, cut into 4 cm batons Celery cooks faster (add at the same point as tomato, not at Phase 5) and reads crunchier and more vegetal. The broth-soaking-up function of taro stem is lost; the dish reads less authentic but is recognizably canh chua.
  • rau ngổ (rice paddy herb) → fresh dill plus a few cilantro stems Rau ngổ has a distinct citrus-cumin top note (from limonene and cuminaldehyde) that no single herb replaces. A 70/30 dill-to-cilantro mix is the closest visual and aromatic approximation. The dish loses its defining top note; the rest of the structure carries.
  • culantro (ngò gai) → cilantro at 1.5× the weight Culantro is more intense than cilantro (same family) and has a slightly bitter back-note. Cilantro is the workable substitute when culantro is unavailable; the dish reads slightly thinner aromatically but works.
  • okra → omit Okra is structural in canh chua for its mucilage and the slight thickening it provides; if omitted, the broth reads slightly thinner. Acceptable to leave out for diners who dislike okra; the dish remains canh chua.

Notes

Why tamarind, not lime. Canh chua is a sour soup, but the sourness is structurally tamarind, not citrus, and the two are not interchangeable. Tamarind acid (tartaric and malic, with the fruit's natural sugars and a slight roasted-fruit back-note from the pulp) reads deep, rounded, and lingering on the palate; lime acid (citric, with bright top notes from the peel oils) reads sharp, fast, and short. Replacing tamarind with lime produces a thinner, brighter soup that reads as something else, often described by Vietnamese cooks as "not canh chua, just sour fish soup." The lime wedges at the table are a finishing accent for diners who want extra brightness, not the structural acid; the structural acid is in the pot from the tamarind extraction (see techniques/tamarind-souring for the chemistry).

The pineapple-tomato-bạc hà axis. The three structural vegetables play different roles. Pineapple contributes its own organic acids (citric, malic) plus enzymatic sweetness (the bromelain breaks down briefly in the simmer but the residual sugars carry); it rounds the tamarind from sharp to layered. Tomato adds glutamate (free umami) and a small amount of additional acidity, and its pectic structure thickens the broth slightly without making it muddy. Taro stem (bạc hà) is the spongy aerial stem of certain taro varieties, and its function is mechanical: the cellular structure is full of air channels that soak up broth as it cooks, so the stem becomes an edible broth-delivery vehicle on the spoon. Without bạc hà, every spoonful is liquid; with it, each bite has a textural contrast of soft-spongy stem holding sour-sweet broth. This is why celery, the most common substitute, is structurally different rather than identical, celery has cellulose tubes that hold their crunch and do not absorb the broth the same way.

Fish handling. Catfish has a slightly mud-note on the skin from its bottom-feeding biology, and the rinse-with-salt-and-vinegar step is the Vietnamese-traditional way to neutralize it. The vinegar's mild acid mobilizes geosmin (the soil-note compound from blue-green algae the fish ingests) into solution, and the salt rinse carries it away. This step is not optional; skipping it produces a soup that tastes faintly earthy in a way that reads off. The salt-cure (a light dusting with sea salt for 10 minutes) also pre-firms the protein so the steaks hold their shape under simmer rather than flaking apart.

Simmer, not boil. Canh chua broth should never see a hard boil once the fish is in. The target is 85 to 90°C / 185 to 195°F surface, just below boil, with steady steam but no rolling agitation. Hard boil flakes the fish, clouds the broth (suspended albumin), and over-extracts the vegetables. The pot should look quiet, with steady steam rising from the surface, not a turbulent bubble pattern.

Layered vegetable timing. The vegetables go in by hardness, not by recipe convenience. Pineapple and taro stem need 6 to 7 minutes to soften but hold structure; tomato and okra need 2 to 3 minutes (longer turns tomato into pulp and okra to slime); bean sprouts go off-heat (raw with residual heat) for crunch. Adding everything together produces some-mushy-some-raw vegetables and a less coherent dish. This is the same logic as a French-style mirepoix-then-vegetable layer-build, just adapted to Mekong-delta pantry.

Why three herbs at the end. Rau ngổ (rice paddy herb), culantro (ngò gai), and the optional Thai chili are not garnish, they are the third stratum of the dish (the first is the tamarind broth, the second is the simmered vegetable bed). Rau ngổ has the cumin-citrus top note from limonene and cuminaldehyde, characteristic of the herb and very hard to substitute; culantro contributes the bitter-grassy backbone that pulls the soup together; chili adds heat where wanted. The herbs go in at the end so the volatile aromatic compounds (mostly monoterpenes that boil off below 100°C / 212°F) reach the bowl rather than escaping as steam during the cook.

Eating mechanics. Canh chua is a soup course in a Vietnamese meal, not a standalone main; it is served alongside steamed jasmine rice and often with a fried or grilled fish dish (cá kho tộ, the caramel-braised fish, is the canonical pairing), with the canh chua functioning as the sour-bright counter to the rich-sweet caramelized fish. The eating order is alternating: a spoon of broth-and-vegetable over rice, a chopstick-pull of caramel fish over rice, then back to canh chua. The two dishes together with rice are dinner. Eaten alone, canh chua reads more thinly than at the actual Vietnamese table.
