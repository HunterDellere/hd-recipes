Recipe

Peruvian ceviche

The Peruvian benchmark of raw fish: cubed white fish cured 5 to 15 minutes in leche de tigre (lime juice, ají amarillo, red onion, ginger, garlic, cilantro, salt), the lime juice denaturing the surface proteins to a tender opaque finish while the interior stays translucent. Served on a wide plate with sweet potato, choclo (large-kernel Peruvian corn), and a wedge of cancha (toasted corn).

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The fish must be sushi-grade. Source from a fishmonger who handles raw fish; flash-frozen sushi-grade fish (frozen at minus 35°C for 15 hours or colder) is safer and equally valid. Supermarket white fish marked sashimi-grade is acceptable; generic supermarket fish fillets are not.
  • The cure window is structural and short: 5 minutes for a translucent center, 10 minutes for a more opaque cure, 15 minutes as the maximum before the fish reads cooked-through. Set a timer. Peruvian ceviche is canonically cured short, the fish should still have visible translucence at the center.
  • Use small fresh Peruvian limones if available (smaller, more intensely sour than standard Persian limes). Otherwise use Persian limes and adjust the quantity up by about 30%.
  • Prepare the sweet potato and corn before starting the ceviche; both are served alongside and need to be ready at the same time the fish finishes its cure.

Phase 1, the accompaniments (start first)

  1. 400 g sweet potato, peeled, cut into 2 cm thick rounds — (1 medium sweet potato; the structural sweet accompaniment that balances the lime acid; boiled in lightly salted water with a piece of orange peel)
  2. 200 g choclo (large-kernel Peruvian corn) or sweet corn, cooked corn on the cob, kernels cut off the cob; or canned choclo
  3. 40 g cancha (toasted Peruvian corn, optional)

Phase 2, the leche de tigre

  1. 200 g fresh lime juice (Peruvian limones or Persian limes)
  2. 100 g red onion, sliced very thinly (2 mm) for the leche de tigre; reserve another 80 g for the final ceviche bowl — (½ medium red onion for the marinade; aggressive raw onion is structural)
  3. 30 g ají amarillo paste
  4. 8 g fresh ginger, finely grated on a microplane — (2 cm piece; the structural aromatic of leche de tigre)
  5. 4 g garlic clove, peeled, finely grated on a microplane — (1 medium clove)
  6. 10 g fresh cilantro stems and leaves, finely chopped; reserve 5 g of leaves for the final garnish
  7. 6 g fine sea salt

Phase 3, the fish

  1. 500 g sushi-grade white fish (corvina, sea bass, halibut, fluke), skinned, deboned, cut into 1.5 to 2 cm cubes (the structural ceviche dice; not too small)

Phase 4, the bowl assembly

  1. 80 g red onion (for the bowl), sliced very thinly (2 mm), soaked in cold water 5 minutes, drained (the soak mellows the raw onion bite) — (about ⅓ medium onion; added at the end for fresh raw structure)
  2. 6 g fresh ají limo (or substitute serrano), thinly sliced, stems removed, seeds in for canonical heat; thinly sliced into rings — (1 ají limo or 1 serrano; the structural fresh heat; canonical Peruvian ceviche uses ají limo)
  3. 5 g fresh cilantro leaves (for garnish)

Execution

  1. 1
    Phase 1, prepare the accompaniments. Bring a small pot of lightly salted water to a simmer; add the 400 g sweet potato rounds and a strip of orange peel (the orange peel adds a structural floral background note). Simmer 15 to 18 minutes until the sweet potato is tender when pierced. Drain; cool to warm. Meanwhile prepare the 200 g choclo (cook fresh corn on the cob 5 minutes if not pre-cooked, or drain canned choclo). Both should be ready by the time the ceviche cure starts.
  2. 2
    Phase 2, build the leche de tigre. In a non-reactive bowl (glass or stainless steel; do not use aluminum, which reacts with the lime acid), combine the 200 g lime juice, 100 g thinly sliced red onion, 30 g ají amarillo paste, 8 g grated ginger, 4 g grated garlic, 5 g chopped cilantro stems, and 6 g salt. Stir to combine; let rest 5 minutes for the flavors to integrate. The structural leche de tigre should read deeply lime-and-chili, with the onion just starting to soften and tint the marinade slightly pink.
  3. 3
    Phase 3, cure the fish. Add the 500 g cubed white fish to the leche de tigre; stir gently to coat each piece. Let rest 5 to 15 minutes at room temperature (5 for a translucent center, 10 for a more opaque cure, 15 for the maximum). Stir once at the midway point to ensure even cure. The structural visual indicator: at 5 minutes the fish surface has turned opaque white and the center is still slightly translucent (sashimi-firm); at 15 minutes the fish is uniformly white through (cooked-feeling). Both are correct; choose based on preference and audience.
  4. 4
    Phase 4, assemble the bowls. Lift the cured fish from the leche de tigre with a slotted spoon; reserve the leche de tigre liquid (it is structural and is served alongside or drunk separately, see notes). Divide the fish among 4 wide shallow bowls (or one large platter for family-style). Scatter the 80 g fresh red onion (soaked and drained), 6 g sliced ají limo, and 5 g cilantro leaves over the fish. Spoon about 60 g of the leche de tigre over each portion.
  5. 5
    Serve immediately with the accompaniments. Arrange 2 rounds of warm sweet potato and a small mound of corn (50 g per plate) alongside the ceviche; scatter 10 g cancha over (if using). Serve at once; ceviche is structurally a freshly-finished dish and reads less well at the 15-minute post-cure mark. Drink the reserved leche de tigre as a shot on the side (Peruvian ceviche bars serve it in small glasses), or pour over the finished plate, or save in the refrigerator for the next ceviche.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as Nikkei-Peruvian ceviche (the Japanese-Peruvian fusion)

    Add 30 g light soy sauce and 20 g rice vinegar to the leche de tigre; reduce the lime juice to 160 g. Cut the fish slightly smaller (1 cm cubes). Garnish with 5 g toasted sesame seeds and 5 g thinly sliced nori. The result is closer to a sashimi-and-ceviche hybrid that originated in Japanese-Peruvian restaurants in Lima in the 1970s and was popularized internationally by Nobu Matsuhisa. Structurally a complete and canonical dish; not the same as classical Peruvian ceviche.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as ceviche mixto (mixed seafood)

    Replace 250 g of the white fish with 150 g cooked shrimp (briefly poached, peeled, and chilled) and 100 g squid rings (briefly poached, chilled). Use the same leche de tigre and cure the same way; the shrimp and squid are pre-cooked so the cure is for flavor, not texture. The result is ceviche mixto, the canonical Peruvian seafood variation; structurally complete and a brilliant way to use mixed seafood from a coastal market.

  • Regional protein→as ceviche de pescado a la chiclayana (with a sweet potato puree base)

    Mash the cooked sweet potato with 15 g olive oil and 3 g salt; spread on the bottom of the serving plate. Spoon the cured ceviche over the puree. The Chiclayo-region variation reads structurally Peruvian with the structural sweet-acid balance emphasized through the puree base; canonical and elegant for plated service.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • sushi-grade white fish (corvina, sea bass, halibut, fluke) → flash-frozen sushi-grade fish Flash-frozen fish (frozen at minus 35°C for 15 hours or colder, the FDA parasite-kill standard) is structurally safer for raw service than fresh fish and is equally valid. Many Peruvian ceviche restaurants use flash-frozen exclusively. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator before use.
  • ají amarillo paste → a blend of 1 part habanero paste plus 2 parts roasted yellow bell pepper puree (use the same weight as the paste) Ají amarillo is the structural Peruvian chili character and is non-substitutable for canonical ceviche flavor. The substitute approximates rough color and rough heat without the floral note; the dish reads recognizably different but is mechanically functional.
  • choclo (large-kernel Peruvian corn) or sweet corn → standard sweet corn or hominy Choclo is structurally larger and starchier than American sweet corn; canned choclo is the closest substitute. Standard sweet corn (fresh or canned) works mechanically though reads slightly sweeter and smaller. Hominy (drained from a can) is acceptable.

Notes

Peruvian ceviche is the structural national dish of Peru and one of the great raw-fish preparations of the world. The dish was declared National Cultural Heritage of Peru in 2004 and is protected by Peruvian law that defines the canonical preparation (white fish, lime juice, ají chili, red onion, cilantro, salt, with sweet potato and corn as the canonical accompaniments). Master Peruvian ceviche and the technique transfers to tiradito (the Nikkei-Peruvian sashimi style), ceviche mixto (mixed seafood), and the broader Latin American ceviche family.

The short cure is the structural feature. Peruvian ceviche is cured 5 to 15 minutes (not 30 minutes, not an hour, not overnight); the fish should still show visible translucence at the center when served. This is structurally different from the Mexican-style ceviche tradition (which cures for hours and produces a uniformly cooked-through fish) and is what distinguishes Peruvian ceviche as a structural specialty: the fish texture is closer to sashimi than to cooked fish, the lime cure flavors and partially denatures the surface protein, and the result reads as a fresh fish dish rather than as a pickled-fish preparation.

The leche de tigre is structural. The marinade of lime juice, ají amarillo, red onion, ginger, garlic, cilantro, and salt is structurally the second flavor pillar of the dish (the fish is the first). Without ají amarillo, the marinade reads as plain lime and the dish loses its structural Peruvian character; without the ginger and garlic, the marinade reads flat. The structural Peruvian leche de tigre is also drunk as a chaser to the ceviche (small glasses on the side; the Peruvian hangover cure) and is considered a structurally complete component of the dish, not just a marinade to discard.

The sushi-grade fish is non-negotiable. Ceviche cures the fish through acid denaturation, not through heat; the acid kills surface bacteria but does not kill parasites (the structural risk in raw fish). Source from a fishmonger who handles sushi-grade fish; flash-frozen sushi-grade fish (frozen at minus 35°C for 15 hours or colder, the FDA parasite-kill standard) is structurally safer than fresh fish and equally valid for ceviche. Generic supermarket fish fillets are not sushi-grade and present structural parasite risk; do not substitute.

The sweet-potato-and-corn accompaniments are structural. Peruvian ceviche is served with sweet potato (cooked, warm) and choclo (Peruvian large-kernel corn, cold or warm) as canonical accompaniments. The sweet potato balances the lime acid with structural sweetness; the corn provides structural starch-and-crunch contrast. Cancha (toasted Peruvian corn) adds a structural crunchy garnish. Without the accompaniments, the dish reads as good ceviche but not as canonical Peruvian; with them, the structural sweet-and-starchy balance comes into focus.

The cure window is critical. At 5 minutes the fish surface has just turned opaque and the interior reads sashimi-translucent; at 10 minutes the fish is more uniformly opaque with a tender bite; at 15 minutes the fish reads fully cooked through and is the conservative-cure maximum. Past 15 minutes the lime acid continues to denature the fish protein and the texture turns dry and chalky; this is the structural failure mode and is unrecoverable. Set a timer; do not improvise the cure window.

Versatility. The leche de tigre marinade transfers to: tiradito (Nikkei-Peruvian sashimi-cut fish briefly soaked in leche de tigre, served on the plate with the marinade pooled around), ceviche of cooked shrimp or octopus (the cure window is shorter, 2 to 3 minutes, since the seafood is already cooked), and as a marinade for grilled fish (use as a finishing acid after cooking). The accompaniment of sweet potato plus choclo works as the structural side for tiradito and grilled coastal fish dishes throughout Peru.

Storage. Peruvian ceviche is structurally a freshly-finished dish and does not store well. Eat within 30 minutes of the cure. Refrigerated leftovers continue to cure and turn chalky within 4 hours; not recommended. The leche de tigre alone (without fish) keeps 2 days refrigerated and is structurally a brilliant beverage chaser or marinade base. Make the ceviche fresh; do not cook for advance preparation.

  • The Peruvian canon. Ceviche (cebiche) has pre-Columbian roots on the Peruvian coast; the modern stabilized form (white fish, lime juice, ají amarillo, red onion, sweet potato, corn) hardened in the 20th century as the Peruvian national dish. Declared National Cultural Heritage of Peru in 2004 and protected by Peruvian law that defines the canonical preparation. Distinct from the Mexican-style ceviche (which uses small dice, longer cure, and tomato addition) and from the Ecuadorian (which uses a tomato-based marinade). — The Peruvian preparation is structurally distinguished by: short cure (5 to 15 minutes, not hours), large dice (1.5 to 2 cm cubes, not small), the leche de tigre marinade including ají amarillo and a touch of ginger and garlic (not just lime juice), and the canonical accompaniments (sweet potato, choclo, cancha). The Nikkei-Peruvian school adds soy sauce and rice vinegar (the Nobu Matsuhisa influence); this is a modern variation. The canonical Peruvian ceviche uses lime, ají, and salt as the three structural pillars.
