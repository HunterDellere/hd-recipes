Recipe

Tahdig

The Persian crispy rice base: long-grain basmati soaked, parboiled in heavily salted water, drained at the al-dente window, then layered back into a butter-and-yogurt-coated pot and steamed under a towel-wrapped lid until the bottom crust turns deep saffron-gold and the top grains stay perfectly separate. The defining technique of Iranian rice cookery and the structural skill the entire Persian table is built around.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Soak the basmati at least 2 hours, ideally 4. Unsoaked rice cooks unevenly in the parboil, the grains never fully elongate, and the steam phase produces a gummy tahdig instead of a separate-grain pilaf with a crisp base.
  • Bloom the saffron 20 minutes before you need it. Grind 0.3 g threads with a pinch of sugar in a mortar, then steep in 30 g hot (not boiling) water. The bloom is the golden liquid you drizzle over the rice; saffron added dry never releases its color or aroma.
  • Use a heavy-bottomed pot with a tight-fitting lid wrapped in a clean kitchen towel. The towel absorbs condensation that would otherwise drip back into the rice and ruin the crust. Nonstick pots make crust release easy but read less golden; stainless or cast aluminum produces the deepest crust at the cost of a tense flip.

Phase 1, the parboil

  1. 400 g aged basmati rice, rinsed 4 to 5 times in cold water until the water runs nearly clear, then soaked 2 to 4 hours in cold salted water (water plus 10 g salt), drained
  2. 3000 ml water
  3. 30 g kosher salt

Phase 2, the saffron bloom

  1. 0.3 g saffron threads, ground with a pinch of sugar in a mortar to a fine powder — (a generous pinch; Persian or Spanish saffron, deep red threads)
  2. 1 g granulated sugar — (a pinch; helps the mortar grind the threads)
  3. 30 g hot water (not boiling)

Phase 3, the crust and steam

  1. 60 g unsalted butter, melted
  2. 30 g neutral oil
  3. 80 g whole-milk plain yogurt
  4. 18 g large egg yolk — (1 yolk; binds the yogurt-rice crust layer; optional but reads more lacquered)
  5. 2 g fine sea salt

Execution

  1. 1
    Bring the 3000 ml / 12 cups water and 30 g / 2 tbsp salt to a rolling boil in a wide 5-quart pot. Drain the soaked 400 g / 2 cups basmati and add to the boiling water. Cook uncovered 4 to 6 minutes, stirring once gently, until the grains have elongated and are al dente (tender on the outside, a thin firm core when bitten, the same window as al dente pasta). Drain immediately into a fine-mesh sieve and rinse briefly with warm (not cold) water to stop the cook. The grains should look long and separate, not clumped.
  2. 2
    While the rice parboils, grind the 0.3 g saffron threads with the 1 g pinch of sugar in a mortar to a fine red powder. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp hot water and stir; cover with a saucer and let steep 15 to 20 minutes. The liquid will turn deep amber-orange. This bloomed saffron is the structural color carrier for the tahdig.
  3. 3
    In a small bowl, scoop out about 200 g (roughly a quarter) of the drained parboiled rice. Stir in the 80 g / ⅓ cup yogurt, the 18 g / 1 egg yolk, the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt, and 15 g / 1 tbsp of the bloomed saffron liquid. This is the crust layer; it should look pale gold and slightly clumpy from the yogurt binding.
  4. 4
    Return the empty parboil pot to medium heat. Add the 60 g / 4 tbsp melted butter and 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil; swirl to coat the bottom. Spread the yogurt-rice mixture across the bottom in an even layer about 1 cm thick, pressing gently with the back of a spoon to fill the entire pan floor. This is the layer that will become the crust.
  5. 5
    Mound the remaining parboiled rice loosely on top of the yogurt-rice layer in a conical pyramid (taller in the center, sloping down at the edges). Do not press or pack; the loose pyramid lets steam rise through the rice during the steam phase, which is what keeps the upper grains separate. Drizzle the remaining bloomed saffron liquid over the top in random streaks (do not stir; the orange streaks become the visual signature of the finished pilaf).
  6. 6
    Poke 4 to 5 holes through the rice pyramid down to the crust layer with the handle of a wooden spoon. These vents are how steam exits the bottom and circulates through the upper grains; without them the crust steams instead of frying.
  7. 7
    Cover the pot with a tight-fitting lid wrapped in a clean kitchen towel (wrap the towel under the lid and fold the corners over the top so it cannot touch a burner). Cook on medium-high heat for 10 minutes until you hear steady sizzling and see steam puffing from the lid edge; the crust phase is beginning. Reduce to low heat (the lowest steady flame your stove holds) and cook undisturbed 45 to 55 minutes. Do not lift the lid; opening it releases the steam that finishes the upper grains.
  8. 8
    After 45 minutes, listen and smell. The pot should smell of toasted basmati and butter with a faint saffron note; the sizzling should be quiet and steady. If you smell burning sugars or hear no sizzle, pull immediately. The standard cook is 50 to 55 minutes total at low heat.
  9. 9
    Remove the pot from the heat and set on a wet folded kitchen towel on the counter for 5 minutes. The thermal shock helps release the crust from the bottom. Run a thin spatula around the edge to loosen any rice stuck to the pot wall. Then either: (a) invert a wide platter over the pot and flip the whole pot upside down in one decisive motion (the classic Persian table presentation, with the golden crust on top), or (b) spoon the loose rice off the top into a serving bowl, then lift the crust out in pieces with the spatula to plate separately. The crust should be deep golden to amber and audibly crunchy.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional potato-bottom tahdig→with sliced potato as the crust layer (tahdig sib-zamini)

    Skip the yogurt-rice crust mixture entirely. After adding the butter and oil to the empty parboil pot, lay a single layer of peeled, thinly sliced (3 mm) waxy potato (about 200 g, 1 medium Yukon Gold) directly into the fat; season with the 2 g salt and drizzle with 15 g of the bloomed saffron liquid. The potato fries into the crust over the steam phase. Pyramid the remaining rice on top and proceed. The potato tahdig is structurally lighter and more delicate; useful when serving a richer khoresh like fesenjan.

  • Regional flatbread-bottom tahdig→with lavash flatbread as the crust layer (tahdig nan)

    Skip the yogurt-rice mixture. After adding the butter and oil, lay a single sheet of lavash (about 30 g, trimmed to fit the pot bottom) directly into the fat; brush the top of the lavash with another 15 g melted butter and the bloomed saffron. Pyramid the remaining rice on top and proceed. The lavash tahdig is the most dramatic visual; the bread shatters into shards at the flip. Pairs especially well with kebabs.

  • Occasion mixed-in polo variant→as zereshk polo (barberry rice) or baghali polo (fava and dill rice)

    After parboiling, layer the rice into the pot in alternating stripes with the polo ingredient: for zereshk polo, scatter 60 g of dried barberries (rinsed, briefly fried in 15 g butter with 10 g sugar) between rice layers; for baghali polo, scatter 200 g blanched fava beans and 30 g chopped fresh dill between rice layers. Proceed with the saffron drizzle and steam. The pot still produces tahdig at the bottom; the upper rice is now a complete dish in itself.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • aged basmati rice → standard basmati rice Standard (not aged) basmati works but the grains elongate less and the crust reads slightly less golden. Increase the soak to 4 hours and reduce parboil time to 4 minutes. Jasmine rice does not work; the starch profile produces a gummy tahdig.
  • whole-milk plain yogurt → whole-milk Greek yogurt thinned with 20 g water Greek yogurt is too thick and produces a dense crust layer that steams instead of frying. Thin it with water to approximate the body of regular plain yogurt before mixing into the crust rice.
  • unsalted butter → ghee or samna (clarified butter) Ghee is more canonical in some Persian households and reads cleaner because the milk solids that would otherwise burn at the long steam are already removed. Use the same weight. The crust browns slightly faster; check at 40 minutes.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → whole-milk plain yogurt make it yourselfstub Home-cultured yogurt has a brighter tang and a cleaner body than supermarket plain yogurt; the slight acidity carries into the crust and reads as faint tahdig complexity.

Notes

Why parboil and steam instead of absorption. The Persian rice technique exists because absorption-method rice (water in, lid on, simmer until absorbed) produces gummy rice by Iranian standards. The parboil-and-steam method drains the starchy parboil water (carrying away the surface amylose that causes clumping), then cooks the rice in its own steam under a sealed lid. The upper grains finish in dry heat and stay perfectly separate; the bottom layer, in direct contact with fat, fries into the crust. This is structurally a different dish from absorption pilaf; both methods produce cooked rice but only the parboil-and-steam yields long, fluffy, separated basmati with a crackling base.

The crust signal. The right tahdig moment is announced by smell, not by timer. At the 45-minute mark the pot should smell of toasted basmati, melted butter, and faint blooming saffron. If you smell any acrid edge (caramelized sugar going past amber, butter solids burning), pull immediately. If the pot smells of nothing, the crust has not formed; give it another 5 to 10 minutes. The textural target is a deep golden-amber color, audible crunch when tapped with a spoon, and clean release from the pot when the spatula is run around the edge. A pale tahdig is undercooked; a black tahdig is burnt and bitter.

Versatility. Tahdig is the rice base under nearly every Persian meal. Pair with: ghormeh sabzi (herb-and-bean stew with lamb and dried lime), fesenjan (walnut-pomegranate chicken or duck stew), khoresh-e gheymeh (yellow-split-pea stew with crispy potato matchsticks), joojeh kabab (saffron-yogurt chicken skewers), mast-o-khiar (yogurt-cucumber side), kashk-e bademjan (eggplant-whey dip). Beyond Persian, the technique transfers cleanly to any pilaf application that wants a crisp base; serve with grilled lamb, roasted chicken, slow-braised short ribs.

Pairs across Persian (canonical), broader Iranian diaspora, Afghan and Tajik (where similar parboil-and-steam techniques produce qabili palaw and osh palav), and contemporary fusion contexts where the technique is borrowed onto non-Persian rice dishes.

Storage. Tahdig is best fresh; the crust softens within an hour. The loose upper rice keeps 3 days refrigerated and reheats well in a steamer or microwave with a damp paper towel. The crust does not reheat to its original crackle; eat it that day. Leftover crust is excellent crumbled over yogurt soup or eaten cold as a snack.

  • The Iranian rice canon. Tahdig (literally "bottom of the pot") is the prized crisp layer that signals correct chelow technique; documented across Persian home cookery for centuries. — Three structural tahdig schools exist: plain rice (the canonical form, used here), potato-bottom (sliced waxy potato laid in the fat first, the potato becomes the crust), and lavash-bottom (a sheet of flatbread laid in the fat). All three rely on the same parboil-and-steam logic; the difference is what touches the pot bottom. The yogurt-rice variant here produces a deeply golden, slightly tangy crust that pairs with every Persian khoresh and is the most forgiving entry point.
