Pollo al ajillo (Spanish garlic chicken with sherry)

Bone-in chicken pieces oil-poached in copious olive oil with a head of crushed garlic and a sprig of bay, then deglazed with dry Fino sherry, the sauce reduced into a glossy garlic-and-sherry emulsion. The canonical Spanish tapas-and-tavern chicken: chicken cooked low and slow in garlic-infused oil, finished with a sharp sherry deglaze that cuts the richness and brings the savory backbone.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • A whole head of garlic is the structural correct quantity, not a typo. The dish is named for the garlic. The cloves cook to mellow buttery sweetness in the oil bath; eating whole cooked garlic cloves is the point.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the chicken

  1. 1200 g bone-in skin-on chicken thighs and drumsticks
  2. 12 g kosher salt
  3. 2 g fresh-ground black pepper

Phase 2, the aromatic oil

  1. 150 ml extra-virgin olive oil (Spanish Picual)
  2. 60 g whole head of garlic, cloves separated and lightly crushed (skin on) — (1 whole head, about 15 to 20 cloves; the cloves stay skin-on, which protects them from burning and produces a sweeter melted garlic)
  3. 2 each bay leaves
  4. 3 g fresh thyme — (3 sprigs)
  5. 2 each dried chiles de árbol or guindilla peppers (whole, optional for gentle heat) — (2 dried chiles; canonical in the Andalusian version, brings background heat without dominating)

Phase 3, the deglaze

  1. 180 ml dry sherry (Fino or Manzanilla)
  2. 3 g sweet smoked paprika (pimentón de la Vera dulce)

Phase 4, finish

  1. 10 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, chopped — (small handful)
  2. 1 each fresh lemon, cut into wedges — (1 lemon, cut into 4 wedges)

Execution

  1. 1
    Salt the 1200 g chicken pieces with the 12 g / 1 tbsp salt and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper, rubbing under and over the skin. Let rest uncovered on a rack in the refrigerator at least 30 minutes (or up to 24 hours; the dry-brine pulls moisture out of the skin and lets it crisp dramatically better during the sear).
  2. 2
    Heat the 150 ml / ⅔ cup olive oil in a wide heavy skillet or shallow Dutch oven (a 30 cm / 12 in cast iron is ideal) over medium-high heat until shimmering. Pat the chicken pieces dry with paper towels (the dry-brine has surfaced moisture; pat it off for a clean sear).
  3. 3
    Brown the chicken skin-side down in a single layer, undisturbed, for 7 to 9 minutes until the skin is deep mahogany and releases easily from the pan. Flip and brown the second side 4 to 5 minutes. The skin should be visibly crisp and the chicken just-cooked at the surface (it will not yet be cooked through). Remove the chicken to a plate. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Reduce heat to medium-low. Add the 60 g / 1 head crushed-skin-on garlic cloves to the chicken-flavored oil. The cloves should sizzle gently but not bubble aggressively (if the oil is too hot, the garlic burns and the dish reads bitter; if too cool, the garlic stays raw and harsh). Cook the garlic 8 to 10 minutes, stirring every 2 minutes, until the cloves are uniformly deep golden, soft to a knife-poke, and the oil smells deeply of garlic. Add the 2 bay leaves, 3 g fresh thyme, and the 2 dried chiles in the last 2 minutes. Blooming aromatics
  5. 5
    Return the chicken to the pan, skin-side up, nestling it into the garlic-and-aromatic oil. The oil should come up about halfway on the chicken pieces (the structural oil-poach geometry). Reduce heat to low; cook gently uncovered for 12 to 15 minutes, basting the chicken with the hot oil every 4 minutes, until the chicken reaches 75°C / 165°F internal at the thickest part of the thigh. The skin stays crisp on top because it is exposed to air; the bottom of the chicken slowly oil-poaches at low temperature.
  6. 6
    Remove the chicken to a warm platter, skin-side up. Pour off all but 60 g / ¼ cup of the garlic-oil from the pan (reserve the poured-off oil; it is liquid gold for vegetables, eggs, bread). Leave the garlic cloves and aromatics in the pan.
  7. 7
    Pull the pan off heat briefly. Add the 3 g / 1 tsp sweet pimentón directly into the residual oil; stir 10 seconds in the residual heat to bloom (burnt paprika reads bitter, so do this off heat or on very low heat).
  8. 8
    Return to medium-high heat; immediately deglaze with the 180 ml / ¾ cup Fino sherry. It will steam and bubble aggressively. Scrape up any browned bits from the pan bottom. Reduce 3 to 4 minutes until the sherry has reduced by half and emulsified with the oil into a glossy spoonable sauce; the alcohol cooks off and the sherry concentrates into a savory glaze.
  9. 9
    Return the chicken (and any accumulated juices) to the pan; toss briefly to coat the chicken in the sherry-garlic-pimentón sauce. Scatter the 10 g chopped parsley over the top. Serve directly from the pan with crusty bread for mopping the sauce (essential), or over plain white rice. Pass the lemon wedges; a small squeeze on each piece lifts the dish. Eat the whole garlic cloves; squeeze them out of their skins onto the bread, they have transformed into sweet melted garlic paste.

Equipment

wide heavy skillet or shallow Dutch oven (30 cm cast iron ideal)

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→gambas al ajillo (the shrimp version, the most famous Spanish ajillo dish)

    Replace the chicken with 500 g large peeled raw shrimp. Skip the long sear and the oil-poach; instead, sauté the garlic in the oil first as written (8 to 10 minutes to golden), add the shrimp and the pimentón at the same time, cook 90 seconds per side until just opaque, deglaze with the sherry, finish in 60 seconds. Total cook is 12 minutes. Serve in a small earthenware cazuela with crusty bread; the canonical Spanish tapas dish.

  • Occasion occasion→oven-finish version (more reliable for dinner parties)

    Brown the chicken in steps 1 to 3 as written. Skip the stovetop oil-poach. After step 4 (garlic poach), nestle the chicken back into the garlic oil, transfer the whole pan to a 180°C / 350°F oven, and roast 20 to 25 minutes until 75°C / 165°F internal. The oven heat is gentler and more consistent than stovetop; less basting required. Finish with the pimentón-and-sherry deglaze on the stove as written.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→pollo a la chilindrón (Aragonese version, with tomato and bell pepper)

    After the garlic-oil poach in step 4, add 200 g chopped red bell pepper and 300 g canned San Marzano tomato; cook 8 minutes until pepper is tender and tomato has reduced. Return the chicken; braise 15 minutes covered. Skip the sherry deglaze (the tomato carries enough acid). Skip the pimentón (the dish reads pepper-forward without it). The dish is structurally distinct from ajillo; a Spanish-regional alternative for the same chicken cut.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dry sherry (Fino or Manzanilla) → dry white wine + a pinch of fine sea salt Functional but the dish loses the briny yeast character of Fino. The Andalusian signature is muted; the dish reads as a French-leaning garlic chicken rather than a Spanish one.
  • whole head of garlic (15 to 20 cloves) → half a head (8 to 10 cloves) For diners with weaker garlic tolerance. The dish reads recognizably similar but flatter; the full head is the structural target.
  • bone-in skin-on chicken thighs and drumsticks → bone-in skin-on chicken breasts, halved through the bone Breasts are leaner and easier to overcook; reduce the oil-poach time to 10 minutes and pull at 70°C / 158°F to avoid drying out. The dish reads recognizably similar but slightly less rich.

Notes

Why oil-poach instead of just sauté. The structural premise of pollo al ajillo is gentle cooking in garlic-infused oil, not aggressive frying. A 90°C to 110°C oil bath cooks the chicken through without driving moisture out of the meat; the result is dramatically more tender than chicken sautéed in a thin film of oil. The crisp top skin (exposed to air) and the gently-oil-poached underside is the structurally correct texture; the dish should read as "chicken cooked in olive oil" not "chicken fried in olive oil."

Skin-on garlic cloves vs peeled. Skin-on garlic in the oil bath produces a sweeter, more mellow garlic flavor because the skin protects the cloves from direct contact with the hot oil and slows the Maillard browning to a gentler caramelization. Peeled garlic cloves brown faster and can edge into bitterness if not watched carefully. The traditional Spanish technique is skin-on. The skins slip off easily after cooking; eaters can pop the soft sweet cloves out of their skins onto bread.

The sherry deglaze is the dish-defining moment. Fino sherry is structurally what makes this dish read as Spanish rather than French or Italian. The wine's bone-dry character, briny flor yeast notes, and almond aromatic deglaze the chicken fond, cut through the rich garlic oil, and concentrate into a glossy savory glaze that coats the chicken. White wine substitutes mechanically but the dish loses its Spanish signature; the sherry is structurally non-negotiable for the canonical version.

Storage. Refrigerated in a sealed container, 3 to 4 days; the dish improves significantly overnight as the garlic-and-sherry sauce integrates into the chicken. Reheat gently in a covered pan over low heat with a splash of water or stock to loosen the sauce; do not microwave (the skin loses its crisp). The reserved garlic oil keeps refrigerated 2 weeks; use it for vegetables, fried eggs, bread crisps, or another batch of ajillo (cycle through it 2 to 3 times before discarding).
