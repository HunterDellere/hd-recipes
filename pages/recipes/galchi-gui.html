Recipe

Galchi-gui (salt pan-fried hairtail)

Cross-cut hairtail segments salt-cured and rested to firm the delicate ribbon flesh and pull surface water, patted bone-dry, lightly dredged in flour, and pan-fried in moderate oil until the skin is golden and crisp and the fine white meat lifts cleanly off the central spine. Flipped exactly once, gently, because beltfish shreds under any second turn. Served as a rice-table fish with a cho-ganjang dip of soy, vinegar, scallion, and sesame, and a lemon wedge for the table.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Salt the segments and rest them 15 to 20 minutes, then pat them bone-dry just before they hit the pan. Dry surface is the whole game: a wet hairtail steams, sticks, spatters, and tears instead of crisping, and the salt rest both firms the fragile flesh and pulls the surface water that prevents a golden skin. Do not skip the second pat-dry after the rest.
  • Hairtail is fragile and must be flipped exactly once. The ingredient page is blunt about this: the flesh lifts off the central spine in clean flakes with almost no force, which means a tentative or repeated flip shreds the segment. Slide a thin spatula fully under each piece and turn it in one motion, then leave it alone.
  • Start Korean short-grain rice FIRST as a parallel track. Galchi-gui is a rice-table fish, best eaten the moment it leaves the pan while the skin is crispest; the rice (a roughly 15 minute simmer plus 10 minute rest) should be finished and resting before the first segment goes into the oil.

Execution

  1. 1
    Prep the fish. The counter should have gutted the ribbon, trimmed the dorsal fin, and cross-cut it into 7 to 8 cm segments; verify and rinse each of the 450 g / about 1 fish worth of segments inside and out under cold water, scraping any dark matter from the spine cavity. Score one shallow slash into the thickest face of each segment so the salt and heat reach the spine and the flesh has somewhere to release tension instead of curling. Pat every segment completely dry, inside the cut and out.
  2. 2
    Salt-cure. Rub the 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt evenly over both faces of all the segments, working it into the slashes. Rest the salted segments on a rack, uncovered, for 15 to 20 minutes. On hairtail the cure does double duty: it firms the delicate, low-collagen flesh through surface protein denaturation so the segments hold together through the flip, and it draws cell water into beads on the surface that you will blot away for a crisp skin. Meanwhile, with the rice already started, build the dip.
  3. 3
    Mix the cho-ganjang dip. In a small bowl whisk the 30 g / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, the 15 g / 1 tbsp rice vinegar, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp water, then stir in the 15 g / 1 stalk minced scallion, the 4 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, and the 2 g / 1 tsp crushed sesame seeds. The soy is the glutamate anchor and the rice vinegar is the finishing acid; taste and adjust so the dip reads bright and salty against the mild fish. Set aside.
  4. 4
    Dry and dredge. After the cure, pat the segments bone-dry a second time with paper towels, blotting the beaded moisture off the silver skin and out of the slashes; residual water is what makes a segment steam, stick, and tear. Spread the 30 g / ¼ cup all-purpose flour on a plate and press each segment into it on both faces for a thin, even coat, then lift and tap off all excess. The dredge should be a sheer dusting, not a crust; a heavy coat goes gummy in the pan.
  5. 5
    Heat the oil. Film a nonstick or well-seasoned skillet with the 45 g / 3 tbsp neutral oil over medium heat until it shimmers and a pinch of flour dropped in sizzles steadily, about 170 to 180 °C. Moderate heat is the rule: too hot and the thin skin scorches and the flour burns before the flesh sets; too cool and the segments absorb oil and the skin goes greasy-soft. The oil should sizzle on contact but not smoke.
  6. 6
    Fry the first side. Lay the segments into the oil away from you, skin-side down, giving each room; work in two batches if the pan is crowded, since a crowded pan drops the oil temperature and steams the surface. Fry undisturbed for 4 to 5 minutes. Do not move or poke the fish; the surface needs uninterrupted contact to dehydrate, brown, and release on its own. The first side is done when the lower edge has gone opaque and golden and the segment slides freely when you nudge the pan.
  7. 7
    Flip once, gently. This is the only turn the fish gets. Slide a thin spatula fully under each segment and turn it in one confident motion; hairtail flesh flakes off the spine with almost no force, so a tentative half-flip or a second turn shreds the delicate ribbon into the pan. Fry the second side 3 to 4 minutes, until the skin is crisp and deep gold and a chopstick slipped into the slash meets flesh that is opaque to the spine and flakes cleanly. The thin segments cook fast, so pull the moment the flesh at the bone turns opaque rather than chasing more color. Drain briefly on a rack, not paper, so the underside stays crisp.
  8. 8
    Serve immediately, while the skin is at its crispest. Plate the segments over or beside the cooked 300 g / 2 cups short-grain rice, scatter the 5 g / fine ginger julienne over the top if using, and add a 50 g / lemon wedge per diner. Each bite goes through the cho-ganjang dip; squeeze the lemon over the fish at the table for a second, sharper hit of acid against the fried richness. Lift the white flesh off the central spine with chopsticks and eat it with the crisp skin and a forkful of rice.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional method→salt-grilled (sogeum-gui) instead of pan-fried

    Skip the flour dredge entirely. Salt-cure and dry the segments as written, brush them lightly with 8 g / 2 tsp neutral oil, and grill or broil 7 to 10 cm from the heat for 4 to 5 minutes per side, flipping once, until the skin blisters and chars at the edges and the flesh flakes off the spine. This is the more traditional galchi-gui register: smokier, drier-skinned, no flour shell. Watch closely, the lean ribbon flesh dries fast under direct heat.

  • Heat heat→with a gochugaru-spiked dip

    Stir 3 g / 1½ tsp gochugaru and 3 g / 1 grated clove garlic into the cho-ganjang dip, and add a few thin slices of green chili. The dip shifts from clean soy-and-vinegar toward the fuller, spicier yangnyeom-jang register that some Korean tables prefer with fried fish; the fish technique is unchanged.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • hairtail (galchi) → butterfish (pomfret), small whole croaker, or Spanish mackerel Butterfish is the closest match for the salt pan-fried register: mild, tender, fine-grained, and built to flake off a central bone. Small whole croaker pan-fries cleanly in a light dredge. Spanish mackerel works but reads oilier and fishier, so lean harder on the rice-vinegar dip and the lemon. Keep the skin on so it crisps the way hairtail skin does, and cut larger fish into cross sections so they sit and flip like segments.
  • all-purpose flour → rice flour or potato starch Rice flour or potato starch fry to an even crisper, lighter shell and make the dish gluten-free; potato starch browns a touch faster, so watch the heat. The flesh and dip are unchanged. Pair with a tamari or certified gluten-free soy in the dip.
  • rice vinegar → apple cider vinegar or lemon juice Cider vinegar is close in acidity with a faint fruit note; lemon juice leans the dip brighter and more floral. Both land the finishing acid; use the same volume.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

Notes

The cure is the key step. Hairtail is the most delicate of the rice-table fish: short muscle blocks, almost no connective tissue, flesh that lifts off the spine in soft sheets. Left untreated it weeps water and falls apart in the pan. The salt cure fixes both problems at once. Salt draws cell water to the surface during the 15 to 20 minute rest, where you blot it away, and the same osmotic action firms the surface proteins so the segment holds its shape through frying and the single flip. The two pat-dry passes, before the cure and after, are the most important non-obvious moves in the recipe; a segment that goes into the oil wet will steam, stick, and shred.

The one-flip rule. Because the binding collagen is sparse, hairtail flesh has no structural margin for handling. The technique is to lay each segment down skin-side first, leave it completely alone until the surface has dehydrated and browned enough to release on its own, then turn it exactly once with a thin spatula slid all the way under the piece. A second flip, or a tentative flip that catches only half the segment, tears the ribbon into the pan. This is the same fragility that forbids stirring in the braised galchi-jorim version of the fish; here it dictates a patient first side and a single confident turn.

Pan-fried or grilled. Galchi-gui spans both a shallow-fry and a salt-grill in the Korean tradition, and the dish is the same idea executed in two heat environments. The pan-fried version (this recipe) uses a light flour dredge to build an even, shatter-prone shell and crisps the whole surface in oil; the grilled version (see the modification) skips the flour, blisters the skin under direct heat, and reads smokier and drier. Both depend on the same cure-and-dry foundation, and both flip only once.

Umami and acid. The glutamate source is the soy sauce in the cho-ganjang dip, which seasons the mild, clean-flavored hairtail flesh from the outside and supplies the savory depth the lean fish is too delicate to carry on its own. The finishing acid arrives twice: the rice vinegar whisked into the dip, which cuts the fried richness on every bite, and the lemon wedge at the table for a brighter, sharper second hit. A fried fish without an acid counter reads heavy and flat; this one is built to stay bright against the rice.

Temperature discipline over heat. The instinct with fried fish is to crank the burner, but the thin hairtail skin and the flour dredge both scorch well before a hot pan would set the interior. Moderate heat, around 170 to 180 °C, crisps the skin in step with the lean flesh cooking to the spine. If the skin is browning faster than the flesh is setting, the oil is too hot; pull the pan off the heat for a few seconds rather than fighting it. The thin segments cook through quickly, so the real risk at the end is pushing past moist into dry; pull the moment the flesh at the bone turns opaque.

Sourcing the fish. Hairtail (galchi) is a counter and ice-bed fish, sold at H Mart, Hannam, 99 Ranch, and most Korean, Chinese, and Japanese grocers, very often previously frozen since the guanine-coated skin oxidizes fast. Judge it by the silver: a good specimen has a bright, even, reflective metallic coat with no yellow or rust-brown patches, firm flesh that springs back, and a clean marine smell. Yellowing of the coat, a dull chalky surface, or any sour or ammoniated note means the surface lipids have oxidized and the fish is past its window. Have the counter gut it, trim the dorsal fin, and cross-cut it into 7 to 8 cm segments; doing it at home with a meter-long slippery ribbon is a chore. Pat the segments very dry before salting.

Gluten-free path. Swap the all-purpose flour for rice flour or potato starch and use a tamari or certified gluten-free soy in the dip; the segments fry up if anything crisper, and the dish loses nothing.
