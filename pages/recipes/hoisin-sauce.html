Hoisin sauce (homemade)

Fermented soybean paste (doubanjiang or yellow miso) blended with sweet soy, garlic, Chinese five-spice, sesame oil, rice vinegar, sugar, and a touch of peanut butter for body. The structural Cantonese sweet-savory condiment: the sauce that lacquers char siu, sits on the table with Peking duck, and gets brushed on Vietnamese banh mi grilled meats. The homemade version reads dramatically fresher and more aromatic than supermarket jars, with the sweet-savory balance the cook controls.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the fermented base

  1. 40 g doubanjiang (Chinese fermented broad bean paste) or yellow miso
  2. 30 g smooth natural peanut butter (no added sugar)

Phase 2, the soy and sweetener

  1. 45 g dark soy sauce (the sweet thick Cantonese soy)
  2. 15 g light soy sauce (for additional saltiness)
  3. 45 g dark brown sugar
  4. 15 g honey
  5. 15 g rice vinegar

Phase 3, the aromatic spice

  1. 8 g garlic, finely grated — (2 cloves)
  2. 2 g Chinese five-spice powder
  3. 8 g toasted sesame oil
  4. 1 g gochugaru or cayenne (for gentle heat, optional)
  5. 30 ml water (for thinning)

Execution

  1. 1
    Combine the wet ingredients. In a small heavy-bottomed saucepan, combine the 40 g doubanjiang (or yellow miso), 30 g smooth natural peanut butter, 45 g dark soy sauce, 15 g light soy sauce, 45 g dark brown sugar, 15 g honey, 15 g rice vinegar, 8 g grated garlic, 2 g five-spice powder, optional 1 g gochugaru, and 30 ml water. Whisk vigorously to combine; the mixture will look chunky and uneven at the start as the peanut butter and doubanjiang resist incorporation.
  2. 2
    Cook to integrate. Place the saucepan over medium-low heat. Stir constantly with a silicone spatula or wooden spoon (the sauce burns readily on the pan bottom because of the sugar). Cook 4 to 5 minutes, until the sauce comes to a gentle simmer, the doubanjiang dissolves completely, the peanut butter melts into the liquid, and the mixture transforms from chunky to glossy uniform paste. The sauce will thicken slightly as it cooks; the texture target is thick spoonable but still pourable (about the consistency of warm honey).
  3. 3
    Adjust the consistency and balance. Pull off heat. The sauce thickens further as it cools; if it is already too thick on the heat, add another 10 to 15 ml of warm water. If it is too thin, return to heat and simmer another 2 minutes. Taste: the sauce should be sweet-savory-umami-faintly-spicy in balance, with a pronounced fermented-soybean depth. If flat, add another 5 g of dark soy. If too sweet, add another 5 g of rice vinegar. If too aggressive (the doubanjiang sometimes reads sharp), add another 10 g of brown sugar.
  4. 4
    Finish with sesame oil. Stir in the 8 g toasted sesame oil. The sesame oil is added off-heat so the aromatic compounds (sesamol, methylpyrazine) are not driven off by heat; this is the structural Chinese finishing technique for any sauce that includes sesame oil.
  5. 5
    Cool and store. Pour the hoisin into a clean glass jar; cool to room temperature (about 15 minutes uncovered) before sealing and refrigerating. The sauce will continue to thicken over the first 24 hours as the starches in the peanut butter and the soybean paste hydrate fully; the final texture is the thick lacquering consistency that hoisin is known for. Use after the 24-hour rest for peak quality; keeps 3 weeks refrigerated.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion occasion→thicker char-siu lacquering version

    Reduce the water from 30 ml to 0 ml. Add an extra 15 g of honey and 10 g of brown sugar. Cook the sauce 8 to 10 minutes instead of 5, until very thick (the consistency of jam). The result is a lacquering hoisin suitable for brushing on char siu, Peking duck, or roasted meats; sticks to the meat surface and caramelizes during the final roast.

  • Occasion occasion→thinner dipping-sauce version

    Increase the water from 30 ml to 60 ml. Add 15 g of additional rice vinegar. The result is a thinner pourable hoisin suitable for Vietnamese pho dipping, banh mi brushing, and as a dressing component. Closer to nuoc cham in viscosity than to traditional thick hoisin.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as Vietnamese pho dipping sauce (with sriracha)

    Reserve 60 g of the finished hoisin. Mix with 30 g of sriracha and 10 g of fresh lime juice. The result is the structural Vietnamese pho-table condiment (each diner adds a small amount to their bowl); structurally distinct from pure hoisin but built on the same base.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • doubanjiang (Chinese fermented broad bean paste) → yellow miso (use the same weight) See ingredients/doubanjiang. Yellow miso is the most accessible substitute and produces a structurally similar fermented-soybean depth with a slightly more Japanese profile. Acceptable substitute; the dish reads slightly less Chinese.
  • dark soy sauce → regular soy sauce + 1 tsp molasses (to approximate the dark soy caramel notes) See ingredients/dark-soy-sauce. Dark soy is structurally distinct from regular soy; the caramel additions and longer aging give the dark soy its characteristic thick sweet character. The molasses substitution approximates the missing notes; the dish reads recognizably similar.
  • smooth natural peanut butter → tahini (use the same weight) See ingredients/sesame-paste. Tahini is the Middle Eastern sesame paste; structurally similar in its oil-and-protein composition and produces a similar glossy body. The dish reads slightly more sesame-forward and less peanut-savory; valid alternative.

Notes

The fermented-soybean base is structural. Traditional commercial hoisin uses a specific fermented soybean paste (mei dou jang or fermented yellow soybeans) that is difficult to source outside Chinese groceries. Doubanjiang (fermented broad bean paste with chili) is the accessible substitute that produces the closest structural match; yellow miso is the second-best substitute. The fermented-soybean character is what gives hoisin its depth; substituting regular soy sauce alone produces a soy-based sauce, not a hoisin. The fermentation note is non-negotiable.

Why peanut butter. The peanut butter is not for peanut flavor; it provides the structural body and gloss that distinguishes hoisin from a thin soy-based sauce. The natural oils in the peanut butter emulsify with the water and soy to produce the thick spoonable texture that characterizes commercial hoisin. Tahini (sesame paste) is the structural alternative; both work mechanically. A hoisin without the nut-butter body reads thinner and less canonical; adding cornstarch as a substitute does not produce the same mouthfeel.

Five-spice as the structural aromatic. Chinese five-spice (typically star anise, cloves, Chinese cinnamon, fennel seed, Sichuan peppercorn) is the canonical hoisin aromatic; the warm-spice profile is what makes hoisin read as Cantonese and not as a generic sweet-savory sauce. Pre-made five-spice powder is fine for everyday use; for premium hoisin, toast the whole spices briefly and grind fresh (the aromatic intensity is dramatically higher). Skipping the five-spice produces a hoisin-shaped sauce without the structural Chinese identity.

Storage. Refrigerated in a sealed glass jar, 3 weeks at peak quality. The sauce thickens significantly during the first 24 hours of storage as the starches hydrate; the post-rest texture is the structural target. Does not freeze well (the emulsion separates on thaw); keep refrigerated only. The sauce develops slightly deeper fermented notes over the first week of storage as the doubanjiang continues to evolve; the second-week sauce is structurally better than the first-day sauce. Make a batch on Saturday for use over the next 3 weekends.

When commercial is fine. For casual sandwich-spread use or for recipes where hoisin is a small component (3 tbsp in a stir-fry sauce), commercial hoisin from Koon Chun or Lee Kum Kee is structurally fine and the difference is hard to detect. For finishing applications where hoisin is the primary flavor (char siu lacquer, Peking duck dipping sauce, banh mi brushing), the homemade version is dramatically better and worth the 15 minutes of work. Knowing when to make and when to buy is part of cooking efficiently.

Sources
  • Cantonese tradition (Guangdong, Hong Kong, southern China) — Hoisin (海鮮 in Chinese, literally "seafood" though the sauce contains none) is the canonical Cantonese sweet-savory condiment, traditionally made with fermented soybean paste, sweet sugar, garlic, sesame, and Chinese five-spice. The name "seafood sauce" likely refers to its use as a dipping sauce for seafood at Cantonese banquets rather than its ingredients. Commercial hoisin (Koon Chun, Lee Kum Kee, Pearl River Bridge) is widely available but the homemade version is dramatically fresher and the cook controls the sweet-savory balance. Used canonically for: char siu lacquering, Peking duck dipping, mu shu pork wrapper sauce, Vietnamese banh mi grilled meat brushing.
