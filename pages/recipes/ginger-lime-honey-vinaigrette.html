Ginger-lime-honey vinaigrette

A bright, sharp, gently sweet vinaigrette built on microplaned ginger, fresh lime juice, honey, a small hit of chili, and a neutral oil. The microplaned ginger acts as a structural particulate stabilizer; the dressing emulsifies on hand-whisking and holds integrated for 20 to 30 minutes, longer with a brief re-shake. Pairs across grain salads, raw cabbage slaws, charred broccoli, grilled fish, and as a quick dipping sauce for raw vegetables.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Aromatic base

  1. 25 g fresh ginger, microplaned to a paste, peeled with the edge of a spoon, microplaned to a paste (not grated coarse, not chopped)
  2. 3 g thai chili, finely minced, stem trimmed, seeds in for heat, finely minced — (1 to 2 small red chilies; scotch bonnet or habanero work, use half deseeded)

Acid and sweet

  1. 60 g lime juice, fresh
  2. 25 g honey
  3. 5 g kosher salt

Oil

  1. 80 g neutral oil (peanut, grapeseed, or rice bran)

Execution

  1. 1
    Peel a 6 cm knob of ginger with the edge of a spoon (scrape, do not peeler-strip; the spoon removes the thin skin without losing the flesh underneath). Microplane the ginger over a small bowl, catching the released juice with the paste. Stop when you have 25 g / 2 tbsp; the rest goes back in the fridge in a sealed bag. The microplaned paste should look wet and stringy, not chopped or coarse-grated; the wetness is the released cell juice that does the emulsifier work.
  2. 2
    Trim and finely mince the 3 g / 1 to 2 thai chilies, seeds in for full heat (seeds out for milder). Add the chili to the bowl with the ginger.
  3. 3
    Add the 60 g / 4 tbsp lime juice, the 25 g / 1½ tbsp honey, and the 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt to the ginger-and-chili bowl. Whisk vigorously for 20 to 30 seconds to dissolve the salt and the honey; the salt particles should disappear, and the honey should integrate without streaks. The mixture should look uniformly cloudy from the suspended ginger particles.
  4. 4
    Stream in the 80 g / ⅓ cup neutral oil in a thin steady drizzle while whisking constantly. The dressing tightens as the oil emulsifies into the lime-honey-ginger base; the microplaned ginger acts as the structural emulsifier (the same role mustard plays in a classic French vinaigrette), and the dressing should ribbon off the whisk and slowly settle back into the surface. Whisk for 10 to 15 seconds after the last of the oil to integrate. Taste; the dressing should read sharp-acid first, then sweet, then heat in the throat. Salt up if it tastes flat; honey up by 5 g (1 tsp) if too sharp. Vinaigrette emulsion

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture aromatic→with garlic alongside the ginger

    Add 5 g / 1 small clove microplaned garlic to the aromatic base. The dressing reads more Southeast Asian (closer to a nuoc cham-vinaigrette hybrid); good with grilled fish and raw cabbage slaws.

  • Regional profile→with fish sauce as the savory anchor

    Reduce the salt to 2 g / ½ tsp and add 12 g / 2 tsp fish sauce. The dressing gains a structural glutamate-and-umami layer; the dish drifts decisively toward Southeast Asian (Vietnamese nuoc cham territory). Good with rice noodles, grilled shrimp, and green papaya salad.

  • Regional oil→with toasted sesame oil for an East Asian register

    Replace 20 g of the neutral oil with 20 g toasted sesame oil (keep 60 g neutral as the primary). The dressing reads decisively Korean or Japanese; good with shredded cabbage, grilled scallions, and seared tofu.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • lime juice, fresh → fresh lemon juice, or yuzu juice Lemon reads milder and more European; yuzu reads more aromatic and Japanese. Both work structurally. Bottled lime juice (and bottled lemon juice) is not a substitute; the cooked-citrus off-notes carry through the finished dressing.
  • honey → palm sugar, or maple syrup, or light brown sugar Palm sugar reads more caramel-rich and is the West African choice; maple syrup reads slightly grassy and is the most North American; brown sugar is the most accessible and reads neutral-sweet. All work; the dressing character shifts slightly with each.
  • thai chili → scotch bonnet (½ deseeded), or habanero (½ deseeded), or fresno chili (1 whole) Scotch bonnet and habanero are the West African choice and bring a fruity-tropical aroma; fresno is milder and rounder. All work. Avoid dried chili flakes (they bloom but read coarser and less aromatic than fresh).
  • neutral oil → extra-virgin olive oil (if reaching toward Mediterranean) EVOO works but the peppery polyphenols compete with the chili in a way that reads muddled; for a cleaner dressing, stay neutral. EVOO is the right choice if the dressing is being paired with a Mediterranean grain bowl or a tomato-and-cucumber salad.

Notes

Why microplaned ginger and not grated. A microplane reduces the ginger fibers to a wet paste and releases the structural cell juice; a box-grater or food-processor grate produces coarser threads that do not emulsify and instead sit as suspended particulate. The microplane is the difference between a dressing that holds for 30 minutes and one that breaks in 5. If a microplane is unavailable, the second-best option is mortar-and-pestle pounding of the peeled ginger to a paste; the third option is a fine-grate followed by a 30-second mash with the back of a spoon against the bowl. Avoid the food processor; the volume is too small to process and the ginger oxidizes brown in the spinning blade.

Why the dressing breaks (and how to rebuild). The emulsion in this dressing is stabilized by the microplaned ginger and not by an egg yolk or mustard, so it is less stable than a classic French vinaigrette and re-breaks faster on storage. After 20 to 30 minutes of standing, you will see a small oil layer separating on top; this is normal and the dressing is fine. Rebuild by giving the jar a vigorous 10-second shake or a 30-second whisk; the emulsion reforms. If the dressing has been refrigerated overnight, let it come to room temperature first (the cold oil is too viscous to re-emulsify) and then whisk or shake.

Storage and batch scaling. The dressing refrigerates 4 to 5 days in a sealed jar; the ginger continues to release water and the dressing thins slightly over 48 hours. The recipe scales linearly to 2x or 3x; beyond 3x, scale the chili last and add to taste because chili heat varies plant-to-plant. Freezing is not recommended; the emulsion does not survive a thaw and the ginger texture goes mushy.

The dressing in context. Built primarily for the savory millet salad with mango and mint, and for grain bowls with peanut-coated chicken thigh; equally good as a quick marinade for grilled fish (20 minutes contact time, no longer or the lime cooks the surface), as a dipping sauce for raw vegetables (cucumber, carrot, jicama), and as a finishing drizzle on roasted carrots or charred broccoli. The dressing is structurally a no-cook preparation; the only cooking question is whether to bloom the thai chili briefly in the oil (which produces a redder oil and a slightly milder dressing) or to keep the chili fresh-minced (which produces the sharper, more aromatic version, and is the default here).
