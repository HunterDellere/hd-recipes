Recipe

Overnight oats with warm spice and grated pear

Old-fashioned rolled oats soaked overnight in kefir (or thick yogurt thinned with milk) with cardamom, cinnamon, nutmeg, a small spoon of maple, and a pinch of salt. The 8-hour cold soak softens the oats to a creamy porridge texture without cooking, and the lactic acid in the kefir partially breaks down the phytic acid in the bran for improved mineral absorption. Grated pear, toasted walnuts, and a final pinch of flaky salt go on at serving. The pear is grated, not chopped, because the cells release their juice into the cold porridge and amplify the perceived sweetness without added sugar.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is an overnight recipe: 10 minutes of active work the night before, then 8 hours of refrigerator rest. Plan accordingly; you cannot shortcut the soak. A 4-hour soak gives gritty oats; the full 8 hours is when the bran fully hydrates and the texture turns creamy.
  • Toast the walnuts the night before too. They cool while the oats soak and stay crisp because they go on top at serving rather than mixing in.
  • Grate the pear at serving, not the night before. Grated pear oxidizes within 2 hours and turns brown; it must be fresh-grated onto each portion just before eating.
  • Use 4 small wide-mouth glass jars (8 oz mason jars work well) rather than one large container. Individual portions hold their structure better in the refrigerator and grab-and-go is the entire point of the format.

Phase 1, the soaking base

  1. 160 g old-fashioned rolled oats (not quick-cook, not steel-cut)
  2. 400 g plain kefir, whole-milk
  3. 100 g whole milk
  4. 30 g maple syrup
  5. 2 g fine sea salt

Phase 2, the warm spice

  1. 1 g ground cardamom
  2. 2 g ground cinnamon
  3. 0.5 g freshly grated nutmeg
  4. 5 g vanilla bean paste

Phase 3, the toasted nuts (toast the night before, store airtight)

  1. 60 g walnut halves, raw, toasted in a dry skillet over medium heat for 5 minutes until fragrant; cooled and roughly chopped

Phase 4, the serving topping (fresh-grate each morning)

  1. 240 g ripe Bartlett or Bosc pear, washed, cored, grated on the large holes of a box grater directly onto each portion at serving — (2 medium pears; one half pear per jar at serving. Bartlett is juicier and sweeter, Bosc is firmer and reads more pear-pear. Both work)
  2. 2 g flaky sea salt (Maldon or similar) — (a small pinch per jar; the textural-and-aromatic salt that contrasts with the soft porridge and amplifies the pear sweetness)
  3. 20 g maple syrup, extra (optional) optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Toast the walnuts. Heat a dry small skillet (cast iron or stainless, not nonstick) over medium heat. Add the 60 g / ½ cup walnut halves and toast 4 to 5 minutes, swirling the pan every 30 seconds, until the surface is one shade darker and the nuts smell distinctly toasted. Tip onto a plate to cool fully (the residual pan heat over-toasts). Roughly chop and store in a small airtight container at room temperature. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  2. 2
    In a large mixing bowl or wide jar, whisk together the 400 g / 1¾ cup kefir, the 100 g / ½ cup whole milk, the 30 g / 2 tbsp maple syrup, the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt, the 1 g / ½ tsp ground cardamom, the 2 g / 1 tsp ground cinnamon, the 0.5 g / ¼ tsp freshly grated nutmeg, and the 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste until smooth and the spices are evenly suspended.
  3. 3
    Stir in the 160 g / 1¾ cup rolled oats with a wooden spoon. The mixture will look very loose, almost soupy; this is correct, the oats absorb roughly 40% of the liquid over the 8-hour soak and the porridge tightens significantly.
  4. 4
    Divide the mixture across four 8 oz wide-mouth glass jars (about 165 g / ⅔ cup per jar). Cover each with its fitted lid. Refrigerate at least 8 hours, preferably overnight. The texture continues to develop over the first 24 hours and the porridge is best between 8 and 36 hours.
  5. 5
    At serving (the next morning, or any of the next 4 mornings): pull one jar from the refrigerator. The porridge should be thick and creamy; if it feels too thick, stir in a splash (15 to 30 g / 1 to 2 tbsp) of cold milk to loosen. Grate one half of a fresh pear (about 60 g) directly over the top using the large holes of a box grater, including the juice that releases onto the porridge. Scatter 15 g / 2 tbsp of the toasted chopped walnuts and a small pinch (about 0.5 g) of flaky sea salt over the pear. If extra sweetness is wanted, drizzle 5 g / 1 tsp of maple syrup; taste first, the pear is often enough.
  6. 6
    Eat directly from the jar with a long-handled spoon, or turn out into a shallow bowl. The contrast on the spoon should be: creamy spiced oats on the bottom, juicy raw grated pear in the middle, crunchy toasted walnut and flaky salt on top.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion fruit→apple in winter, berries in summer, stone fruit in late summer

    Apple: grate one half tart-sweet apple (Honeycrisp, Pink Lady) at serving, same technique as pear. Berries: 60 g fresh raspberries or blueberries scattered on top (do not grate). Stone fruit: half a ripe peach or nectarine diced into 1 cm pieces, with the juice. All substitute by season; the warm-spice base holds across fruit types.

  • Texture nut→with toasted almonds, pecans, or pepitas

    Almonds: 60 g sliced almonds toasted 3 minutes. Pecans: 60 g pecan halves toasted 5 minutes, chopped. Pepitas: 40 g raw pepitas toasted 4 minutes (no salt). All work in 1:1 weight, with pepitas being the nut-free option for school lunches.

  • Regional cuisine direction→with chai spice and date

    Replace the cardamom-cinnamon-nutmeg with 4 g / 2 tsp of a chai spice blend (cardamom, cinnamon, ginger, cloves, black pepper, fennel). At serving, top with 30 g chopped Medjool dates instead of the grated pear, plus the toasted walnuts. The breakfast moves into a chai-tea-adjacent register.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • plain kefir, whole-milk → thick whole-milk yogurt thinned 50/50 with whole milk, or buttermilk Greek yogurt thinned with milk approximates the kefir tang and consistency. Buttermilk substitutes 1:1 and is slightly thinner and tangier. Plant-based alternatives (cashew kefir, coconut yogurt) work for a vegan version but have less protein and the porridge reads softer.
  • old-fashioned rolled oats → thick rolled oats (Bob's Red Mill Extra Thick) or oat-and-grain mix Extra thick rolled oats hold more bite after the overnight soak and are the preferred choice for cooks who want more texture. Quick-cook oats turn to mush. Steel-cut oats do not hydrate enough overnight, they need 24+ hours and even then read undercooked.
  • maple syrup → honey, agave, or omit Honey substitutes 1:1 and reads slightly more floral. Agave is the most neutral sweetener. Omitting entirely and relying on the grated pear for sweetness is the lowest-sugar option and is perfectly viable; the spice and salt carry the flavor.

Notes

Why kefir over plain milk. The lactic acid bacteria in kefir produce two things the dish needs: lactic acid (which lowers the pH of the soak from milk''s 6.7 to about 4.5, partially denaturing the protein structures in the oat bran and accelerating hydration) and free amino acids (which provide a more complex savory background than plain milk lactose). The kefir also partially breaks down phytic acid in the bran, the compound that binds minerals like zinc and iron and reduces their bioavailability. The dish reads creamier, tangier, and is nutritionally distinct from a milk-only soak.

Why grated pear, not chopped. Grating breaks the pear cells and releases their juice into the porridge, where it amplifies the perceived sweetness without added sugar and softens any remaining oat bite. Chopped pear sits on top as a discrete element; grated pear integrates. The texture also matters: grated pear gives the porridge a fine fruit-pulp distribution that reads as continuous sweetness across the spoon, while chunks read as alternating bites of "porridge" and "fruit." Both are valid; grated is the design intent here.

The salt point. Oats without salt taste flat, almost cardboard. The 2 g / ½ tsp in the soak brings out the cereal sweetness and finishes the spice palate. The flaky salt pinch at serving is a textural-and-aromatic event, not a seasoning correction; the crystals dissolve on the tongue with a small saline burst that amplifies the next bite. Both salts are non-negotiable.

Make-ahead and storage. The jars hold 4 days refrigerated. After day 4, the oats begin to break down into a more uniform paste (still edible but less textural) and the spice aromatics begin to flatten. For longer storage, freeze in individual jars (leave 1 cm of headspace for expansion); thaw overnight in the refrigerator before the morning of consumption. Do not freeze with the pear or walnuts on top; those go on at serving.

Why no plastic. Glass jars are the right tool for overnight oats: they do not absorb spice aromatics, they do not impart any off-flavors over 4 days, and they go straight from refrigerator to spoon without intermediate dishware. Plastic containers absorb the cardamom and cinnamon volatiles over multiple uses and will subtly off-flavor future contents. Wide-mouth jars are easier to eat from than narrow-mouth.
