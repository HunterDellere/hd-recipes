Homemade tahini

Hulled sesame seeds toasted lightly at 160 °C and ground with neutral oil to a smooth, pourable paste. Fresher and more aromatic than commercial bottles, especially against supermarket house-brand tahini.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 3 items ▾
  1. 200 g hulled white sesame seeds
  2. 50 g neutral oil (avocado, refined sunflower, or grapeseed)
  3. 2 g fine sea salt optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the oven to 160 °C / 325 °F. Spread the 200 g / 1½ cups hulled sesame seeds on a sheet pan in a single even layer. Toast for 10 to 12 minutes, shaking the pan every 3 minutes to redistribute the seeds and ensure even browning. The seeds should be uniformly pale gold and fragrant, with no scattered dark patches; pull at the first hint of nutty aroma. The line between properly toasted and over-toasted is narrow; over-toasted seeds produce dark, harsh paste that drifts toward Chinese sesame paste in flavor profile.
  2. 2
    Cool the toasted seeds completely on the pan, ~10 minutes. Cool seeds grind more efficiently than warm seeds because the residual heat softens the cell walls and produces a stickier intermediate stage in the food processor; cooling restores the brittle structure that breaks cleanly under the blade. Skipping the cooling stage is a common shortcut that produces a less smooth final paste.
  3. 3
    Transfer the cooled seeds to a food processor with the steel blade attachment, or to a high-speed blender (a high-speed blender like a Vitamix is faster but produces more heat from blade friction; a food processor is slower but lower-heat). Process for 2 minutes at full speed; the seeds break down to a coarse meal, then to a thick paste. The mixture will appear to seize at the 1 to 2 minute mark, balling up and climbing the sides of the bowl; this is the natural intermediate stage and not a failure. Continue processing through the seize.
  4. 4
    With the processor running, stream in the 50 g / ¼ cup neutral oil through the feed tube; the oil incorporates and the seized paste loosens visibly within 10 to 15 seconds of full incorporation. Continue processing for 6 to 8 more minutes (8 to 11 minutes total processing time), scraping the sides every 2 minutes to ensure all the meal incorporates into the paste. The paste goes through three distinct stages: coarse meal (0 to 2 min), thick seized paste (2 to 4 min), thinning paste with surface gloss (4 to 7 min), smooth pourable tahini (7 to 11 min). Patience here; the smoothness develops only at the end of the process.
  5. 5
    Add the optional 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt; pulse 5 to 6 times to incorporate without overworking. Transfer to a clean glass jar; the finished tahini should pour like cold honey, slightly slower, with no visible graininess in a thin smear. If the texture is still grainy, return to the processor for another 2 to 3 minutes. Refrigerate; the tahini holds 6 to 9 months refrigerated, with the surface oil separating slightly over weeks (stir before each use to redistribute).
  6. 6
    The Adjustment: tahini that is too thick (does not pour, sits in the jar like soft butter) had insufficient oil or insufficient processing time; thin with 15 to 30 g additional neutral oil and re-process for 2 minutes. Tahini that is too thin (pours like cream) had excess oil; let stand at room temperature for 30 minutes for the sesame fines to settle, then pour off the surface oil if more concentrated paste is wanted. Tahini that tastes bitter was over-toasted; the bitterness cannot be removed and the batch is best used in dishes where the bitterness is masked (mixed into hummus, blended into sauces with strong other flavors).

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→Chinese sesame paste (zhi ma jiang)

    Use unhulled black or unhulled white sesame seeds (skip the hulled-only constraint); toast at 180 °C / 350 °F for 14 to 16 minutes until deeply browned (well past the Levantine point); use 60 g sesame oil rather than neutral oil. The result is the Chinese sesame paste used in dandan noodles, ma jiang mian, and Northern Chinese hot-pot dipping sauces. Distinct ingredient from Levantine tahini, used in distinct cuisines, do not confuse the two.

  • Protein seed→black sesame tahini

    Substitute hulled black sesame seeds for hulled white sesame seeds. Toast at the same 160 °C for the same 10 to 12 minutes (black seeds are harder to read for color so go by smell only). The result is a dramatically darker paste with a more pronounced earthy-roasted aroma; goes into Korean and Japanese applications and as a contrast garnish on white tahini-based dishes.

  • Occasion salt→salted tahini for finishing

    Increase the salt to 4 g / 1 tsp and pulse to incorporate. The salted version is for direct use as a finishing drizzle (over roasted carrots, on labneh, on grilled chicken) where the salt is the only seasoning; do not use in hummus or sauces where additional salt is added separately, or the dish reads over-salty.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • hulled white sesame seeds → unhulled white sesame seeds Unhulled seeds produce a darker, harsher tahini with a slight bitter edge from the hull tannins. The bitterness can be partially mitigated by a 30-second cold-water rinse and full drying before toasting, but the result is still recognizably different from properly hulled tahini. For Levantine applications, hulled seeds are the correct ingredient; unhulled is a different product.
  • neutral oil (avocado, refined sunflower, grapeseed) → untoasted sesame oil Untoasted (cold-pressed) sesame oil intensifies the sesame character without adding the heavy roasted note of toasted sesame oil. Use the same volume; the resulting tahini reads more aggressively sesame-flavored. Toasted sesame oil is too aggressive and produces an oddly Chinese-flavored Levantine tahini; do not substitute.

Notes

The hulled-versus-unhulled question. Sesame seeds come in two forms: hulled (with the outer seed coat removed mechanically) and unhulled (whole seeds with the coat intact). The hull contains tannins and oxalates that produce a bitter, harsh quality in tahini; the hulled seed produces a smoother, brighter, more pleasant paste. Levantine tradition uses exclusively hulled seeds; this is structural to the product, not a preference. Unhulled seeds produce a darker, more bitter paste closer to Chinese sesame paste in character, and the substitution genuinely produces a different ingredient rather than a worse version of the same one.

The toasting temperature window. The 160 °C / 325 °F window for 10 to 12 minutes is the precise Levantine toast: deep enough to develop the Maillard pyrazines that give the paste its characteristic toasted-aroma top note, but shallow enough that the sesame oils do not begin to oxidize and the paste retains a light, fresh character. Higher temperatures (180 °C and above) push the paste toward a darker, more aggressively roasted profile that is correct for Chinese sesame paste but wrong for Levantine tahini; the same recipe with the wrong toast temperature produces a fundamentally different ingredient.

The grind time and the seizing-and-relaxing chemistry. During processing, sesame seeds go through three distinct phases: (1) the coarse meal phase (0 to 2 minutes), in which the steel blade or high-speed blender is breaking the cell walls and releasing the oil from the seed matrix; (2) the seizing phase (2 to 4 minutes), in which the released oil rehydrates the seed proteins and creates a thick, sticky paste that climbs the sides of the bowl and resists further processing; (3) the relaxing phase (4 to 11 minutes, after the streamed oil incorporates), in which the additional oil thins the paste and allows the residual seed solids to break down to the smooth pourable consistency of finished tahini. Stopping the process during the seize phase produces a thick, gritty failed batch; pushing through and adding the oil unlocks the smooth final state.

Why food processors work better than blenders for this volume. Food processors with a steel blade attached to the bottom of a wide flat bowl move the seeds through the blade with consistent contact across the volume; blenders with a vortex-shaped blade depend on the spinning motion to draw seeds down to the blade, and the seized intermediate phase blocks the vortex and causes the blender to spin around an empty zone above the blade. For 200 g batches and below, a food processor is the working choice. For 500 g and larger batches, a high-speed blender like a Vitamix can work but requires manual scraping every 60 seconds.

The home-versus-commercial comparison. Home tahini is fresher and lighter than commercial tahini, with a more pronounced sesame top note and a less industrial-uniform texture. The trade-off is shelf life: home tahini keeps 6 to 9 months refrigerated; commercial bottles keep 12 to 18 months on the shelf because of more aggressive industrial processing (UHT-equivalent heat treatment, finer grind, oil emulsifier additives in some brands). For everyday use, home tahini is the better product; for cooking applications where the sesame is a background flavor or where shelf-life matters, commercial is fine.

Sesame oil separation in storage. All natural unstabilized tahini separates over weeks: the heavier sesame solids settle to the bottom and a layer of clear sesame oil rises to the top. This is normal and expected. Stir vigorously with a long-handled spoon or fork before each use to redistribute the oil. Tahini that has been in the fridge for 2 to 3 months may need 60 seconds of vigorous stirring; the cold thickens the oil and slows the redistribution. Some commercial brands add emulsifiers to prevent separation; this is a stability-versus-purity trade.

Use. Tahini is the foundation of hummus (where it provides the emulsifying body and the sesame top note), of baba ghanoush (eggplant hummus, by the same logic), of Israeli-style schug-and-tahini-on-toast, of Levantine tahini sauce (tahini, lemon, garlic, water; whisked to a pale creamy emulsion), of tahini-based salad dressings, and as a finishing drizzle on roasted vegetables and grilled meats. Black tahini and salted tahini variants extend the range; the white unsalted version is the workhorse pantry product.
