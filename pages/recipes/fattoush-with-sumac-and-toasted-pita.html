Fattoush with sumac and toasted pita

Levantine bread salad: oven-crisped day-old pita over chopped raw vegetables, dressed with a sumac, pomegranate molasses, and lemon vinaigrette. The bread goes in last and stays audible to the fork.

Phase 4, herbs and finish

  1. 30 g flat-leaf parsley, leaves and tender stems, washed, dried thoroughly, leaves picked from the woody stems, roughly chopped — (1 large bunch; parsley is a structural ingredient here, not a garnish)
  2. 12 g fresh mint leaves, leaves stripped, larger leaves torn — (1 small bunch)
  3. 40 g purslane, leaves and tender stems optional — (1 small bunch; if available — farmers' market in summer; otherwise omit)
  4. 2 g sumac, for finishing

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the oven to 200 °C / 400 °F. Tear the 180 g / 2 loaves of day-old pita into 3 to 4 cm shards along the natural seam between the top and bottom rounds; the thinner halves crisp faster and more evenly than whole-loaf wedges. Spread the shards in a single layer on a sheet pan.
  2. 2
    Drizzle the 25 g / 2 tbsp olive oil over the pita and toss with hands so each shard glistens; sprinkle the 3 g / 1 tsp sumac and the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt evenly across. Bake 8 to 10 minutes, tossing once at the 5-minute mark, until the shards are deep gold at the edges and audibly crisp when knocked on the pan. The sumac is added now, on the bread, so its anthocyanin-rich tartness is fixed into the oil-soaked surface; sprinkling sumac on already-crisp bread later just leaves dust.
  3. 3
    Transfer the toasted pita to a plate to cool. The shards continue to crisp as they cool because residual moisture flashes off; do not stack them while warm or the steam will soften them back. Hold uncovered.
  4. 4
    Make the dressing in a small jar or wide bowl. Combine the 8 g / 2½ tbsp sumac, the 20 g / 1 tbsp pomegranate molasses, the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, the 4 g / 1 clove microplaned garlic, the 3 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. Whisk or shake until uniform. Let stand 5 minutes ; the sumac is a crushed dried drupe, and a brief hydration in the acid extracts more of its malic-acid-rich color and tannic sourness into the dressing than freshly mixed sumac delivers. Then stream in the 60 g / ¼ cup olive oil while whisking; the dressing is a temporary emulsion, broken-and-shaken, not a tight mayonnaise. Vinaigrette emulsion
  5. 5
    Salt the tomato wedges. Cut the 350 g / 3 tomatoes into 1.5 cm wedges and toss with a pinch (~2 g / ½ tsp) of fine sea salt in a colander set over a bowl; let drain 10 minutes. The salt draws out cellular water from the tomato (osmosis through the cut surface); the wedges firm up, the salad does not become a tomato puddle, and the drained juice can be added back to the dressing for tomato-water depth.
  6. 6
    While the tomatoes drain, prep the rest of Phase 3 and 4. Cut the 300 g / 2 to 3 cucumbers into 1 cm half-moons. Slice the 100 g / 6 radishes 2 mm thick. Tear the 250 g / 2 romaine hearts into 3 cm pieces. Slice the 30 g / 4 scallions thin. Roughly chop the 30 g / 1 large bunch parsley (stems and all, if the stems are tender ; they carry as much aromatic character as the leaves). Strip the 12 g / 1 small bunch mint leaves and tear the larger ones. If using purslane, pick the 40 g / 1 small bunch off the woody stems.
  7. 7
    Whisk 1 tbsp / 15 g of the drained tomato juice from the colander back into the dressing. The tomato juice is glutamate-bearing (free amino acids that build during ripening), and folding it into the vinaigrette is the hidden umami move that makes a vegetable salad taste like a composition rather than a list of vegetables.
  8. 8
    In a wide bowl, combine the cucumber, drained tomato wedges, radish, romaine, scallion, parsley, mint, and (if using) purslane. Pour two-thirds of the dressing (~80 g / 6 tbsp) over the vegetables and toss with hands to coat every surface; greens with raw garlic dressing on them need 30 seconds of toss-time to read seasoned, not just dressed.
  9. 9
    Taste a leaf with a tomato wedge and a cucumber piece together. If it reads flat, add another 1 g / pinch of fine sea salt and a 5 g / 1 tsp splash of lemon juice; if it reads aggressive, an extra splash of olive oil rounds it. The dressing should taste tart, savory, and faintly sweet from the molasses, not jammy.
  10. 10
    Add the toasted pita just before serving. Pour the remaining one-third of the dressing (~40 g / 3 tbsp) over the bread, toss once more so the shards pick up a thin sheen but do not soak, and turn the salad onto a wide platter. Scatter the 2 g / ½ tsp finishing sumac across the top. Serve within 5 minutes ; the entire dish is engineered around the audible bread; once the pita absorbs the dressing into its interior crumb (about 10 minutes), the salad shifts from fattoush to soggy bread salad, which is a different, lesser dish.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→fattet-style (with garlic-yogurt and chickpeas)

    Layer 200 g / ¾ cup cooked chickpeas under the salad and spoon 200 g / ¾ cup garlic-yogurt sauce (Greek yogurt + 1 microplaned garlic clove + 5 g / 1 tsp lemon juice + salt) over the top before the pita. The dish becomes adjacent to fattet hummus, the warm Levantine bread-and-yogurt casserole.

  • Protein protein→with grilled halloumi

    Pan-sear 200 g / 7 oz halloumi in 1 tbsp / 15 g olive oil over high heat, 90 seconds per side until deeply browned. Cube and fold in with the vegetables. The salty squeak of the cheese reads against the sour dressing the way feta reads against olives.

  • Occasion season→winter fattoush, with cabbage

    Replace the romaine with 200 g / 4 cups thinly shredded napa or savoy cabbage. The dish loses summer freshness but the structure (sour dressing on raw vegetables, audible bread) holds; cabbage carries the dressing better than lettuce in the cold months.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • pita bread → lavash, taboon, or rustic country bread torn into shards Any thin sturdy flatbread works; rustic country bread (sourdough, ciabatta) torn into pieces drifts the dish toward Italian panzanella but remains coherent. Avoid sandwich bread, which has too much enriched-dough sweetness.
  • pomegranate molasses → reduced pomegranate juice (cook 240 ml / 1 cup down to 60 ml / ¼ cup) plus 1 tsp / 5 g lemon juice Pomegranate molasses is the bottled version of this reduction; making it on demand is a 15-minute job. Do NOT substitute grenadine ; grenadine is sugar syrup with red dye, not pomegranate.
  • sumac → lemon zest plus a pinch of salt Reads brighter and more floral than sumac; the dish loses the savory tannic depth that defines fattoush. Use only when sumac is genuinely unavailable; the spice ships well by mail order and is worth ordering.
  • purslane → watercress, or omit and add another 10 g of mint Purslane is the traditional inclusion (succulent, lemony, slightly mucilaginous); watercress reads peppery and is the closest texture; omitting is also legitimate ; purslane is the optional ingredient, not the structural one.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → day-old pita bread (Lebanese-style, thinner) make it yourself Home-baked pita has a thinner, more delicate top crust that crisps to a different texture in the oven. Day-two stale homemade pita is the canonical fattoush bread; the puff at the bake plus the dehydration on day two is the right physical structure.
  • → pomegranate molasses make it yourself Pomegranate juice reduced to a syrup over 30 to 40 minutes produces a brighter, fresher, less-cooked-tasting molasses than commercial bottles, which often add citric acid and sugar. The 4:1 reduction is the only step.

Notes

Why three sumac applications. Sumac is the dried and ground drupe of Rhus coriaria; the red color comes from anthocyanins and the sour from malic and tartaric acids on the seed coat. Hydrating sumac in fat (the bread oil) extracts the lipid-soluble color compounds; hydrating in acid (the dressing) extracts more of the water-soluble organic acids; the dry finish on top puts intact red flecks on the surface where the eye reads them as the signature of the dish. One sumac sprinkle gets you part of the spice's character; three applications get you the full picture.

Why day-old bread. Fresh pita has too much interior moisture; in the oven it dries and crisps unevenly, with leathery centers and burned edges. Day-old (or older) pita has already lost 30 to 40 percent of its baked moisture to staling; the second-stage oven crisp is then uniform and the shards stay audible against the dressing for the first 10 minutes of the salad's life. If you only have fresh pita, leave it uncovered on a rack for 4 to 6 hours before toasting.

The umami source is the salted tomato juice folded into the vinaigrette plus the parsley itself (fresh herbs carry free glutamate at meaningful levels); the finishing acid is the layered sumac-pomegranate-lemon dressing. Both axes are baked into the structure of the recipe rather than added as garnish.

Build the salad in this order: bread first, dressing second, vegetables third, herbs fourth, dressing-toss fifth, bread-on-top last. Reversing any of the last three steps causes the bread to absorb the dressing into its interior, the herbs to wilt against the warm bread, or the salad to be tossed twice (which bruises the romaine). The order is the recipe.

Sources
  • Adapted from the Levantine mezze tradition; the dish is rural Lebanese-Syrian-Palestinian peasant cooking, built to repurpose stale khubz alongside whatever raw vegetables and herbs the garden produced. — The defining axis is sour: sumac, pomegranate molasses, and lemon stack three different sour molecules (malic-acid-rich anthocyanins from the sumac drupe, citric-and-tartaric-rich molasses, and lemon citric acid) so no one acid carries the dish alone.
