Recipe

Chinese lion's head meatballs (shīzi tóu)

Large braised pork meatballs from Yangzhou (Huaiyang cuisine), in which an unusually high proportion of water and finely diced water chestnut produces a tender, juicy meatball that sits among a bed of napa cabbage and braises in a clear soy-and-rice-wine broth for an hour. The dish is named for the appearance of the meatballs nestled in the cabbage, the round meatball is the lion's head, the wilted napa around it is the mane. The technique is structurally different from Italian or Thai meatballs: more water is bound into the meat by extended hand-paddling that extracts myosin, the meatballs are barely seared (just enough to set the surface) before going into the braise, and the long slow cook produces a texture closer to silky tofu than to the dense, springy meatballs of other traditions.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Start jasmine rice as a PARALLEL TRACK in the last 30 minutes of the 60 minute braise. Lion's head is plated over rice or alongside it as the starch carrier for the broth; rice that is not ready when the meatballs come out leaves the cabbage and meatballs cooling on the platter.
  • The meatballs need 8 to 10 minutes of hand-paddling to extract myosin and bind the unusually high water proportion into the meat. Skipping or shortening this step is the single most common failure mode, the meatballs fall apart in the braise. Plan the prep time; this is a 35 minute active step before the braise even starts.
  • Hand-chop the pork shoulder rather than using pre-ground if at all possible. The discrete, coarser hand-chop texture is structural to the silky-tender meatball; pre-ground pork produces a denser, springier ball. If you must use pre-ground, request shoulder grind with visible fat flecks rather than fine grind.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the meatball mixture

  1. 400 g ground pork (75/25 shoulder)
  2. 100 g pork belly, skin removed, fine-diced, skin removed and reserved for stock; the lean-and-fat belly diced to 3 to 4 mm cubes (do not grind, the discrete fat cubes are structural, they render during the braise and produce small pockets of liquid fat throughout the meatball)
  3. 80 g water chestnuts, peeled and finely diced, fresh water chestnuts peeled and diced to 3 to 4 mm cubes; canned water chestnuts (rinsed) diced similarly
  4. 8 g ginger, finely grated on a microplane, microplane-grated to a paste, discard any fibrous residue left on the plane — (1-inch piece; the ginger aroma in lion's head is foundational but should not read aggressive, microplane-grated ginger paste distributes evenly through the mixture)
  5. 25 g scallions, white-and-pale-green parts only, finely minced, the white-and-pale-green parts of 3 scallions, finely minced; reserve the dark green tops for the garnish — (about 3 scallion bottoms; the canonical Chinese pork-aromatic combination of scallion-and-ginger goes in here)
  6. 15 g Shaoxing wine
  7. 10 g light soy sauce
  8. 2 g white pepper, freshly ground
  9. 4 g kosher salt
  10. 35 g large egg white — (1 white from a large egg; binds the meatball and contributes to the silky texture, the white is used rather than the yolk because the protein matrix is structural for this dish, where the yolk would add fat that the pork belly already provides)
  11. 12 g cornstarch
  12. 90 ml cold water (for paddling into the meat)

Phase 2, the cabbage bed and aromatics

  1. 600 g napa cabbage, separated into individual leaves; the tougher base ribs torn from the leaves and roughly chopped; the leafy green portions torn into 4 to 5 cm pieces — (1 small head or half a large head; the bed under the meatballs, which absorbs the rendered pork fat and the braising broth and contributes its own sweet brassica flavor, structural for the dish, not a garnish)
  2. 15 g ginger, sliced into 3 mm coins, unpeeled, sliced into thin coins — (1-inch piece; for the broth, the slicing produces broad surface area for the ginger to release its aromatic compounds into the braise)
  3. 30 g scallions, white parts smashed lightly with the flat of a knife — (2 scallions; the white parts smashed and the green tops reserved for the garnish, the smashing releases the scallion's aromatic compounds without breaking the structure of the whole stalk, so they can be pulled out at the end)
  4. 30 g neutral oil for searing

Phase 3, the braising broth

  1. 800 ml chicken or pork stock (homemade or unsalted store-bought)
  2. 15 g light soy sauce
  3. 30 g Shaoxing wine
  4. 6 g rock sugar (or granulated sugar)

Phase 4, finishing

  1. 10 g scallion greens, thinly sliced on the bias — (reserved dark green tops from Phase 1 and 2; for garnish)
  2. 3 g toasted sesame oil (optional, for a few drops on the surface of the bowl) optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Prep the meat mixture components first. Dice the 100 g pork belly into 3 to 4 mm cubes, keep these discrete, this is structural texture and they should not be ground into the rest of the mix. Peel and dice the 80 g water chestnuts to 3 to 4 mm cubes. Microplane the 8 g ginger. Mince the 25 g scallion whites. Measure the wet ingredients (Shaoxing wine, soy sauce, salt, egg white, cornstarch) into a small bowl. Measure the 90 ml / 6 tbsp cold water in a separate cup, this goes in last, in three portions, with kneading between each.
  2. 2
    Mix the meatballs. In a wide mixing bowl, combine the 400 g ground pork, the 100 g diced pork belly, the 80 g diced water chestnuts, the 8 g grated ginger, the 25 g minced scallion whites, the 15 g / 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine, the 10 g / 2 tsp light soy sauce, the 2 g / 1 tsp white pepper, the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt, the 35 g / 1 egg white, and the 12 g / 1½ tbsp cornstarch. Mix with hands or a sturdy silicone spatula for 60 to 90 seconds, long enough to fully incorporate everything and to extract significant myosin (the mixture goes from loose-and-shaggy to noticeably sticky and cohesive). The texture should pass the spoon-stick test: a spoon stuck vertically into the mixture should hold upright for 2 to 3 seconds rather than fall over immediately. Myosin extraction
  3. 3
    Paddle in the cold water (the structural step). With the mixture in front of you and the 90 ml / 6 tbsp cold water in a cup, work the water into the meat in three additions. Pour the first 30 ml / 2 tbsp over the mixture and stir vigorously with the silicone spatula or a chopstick in one direction (always the same direction, clockwise or counterclockwise, but consistent) for 30 to 45 seconds. The mixture will initially separate (the water sitting on top of the meat) and then suddenly bind (the water disappears into the matrix as the myosin extracts and absorbs it). Add the second 30 ml of water; stir 30 to 45 seconds the same way until it disappears. Add the final 30 ml; stir 45 to 60 seconds until the mixture is fully cohesive and the water is fully integrated. Total paddling time: 5 to 7 minutes. The end-state mixture is unusually wet, smooth, and slightly translucent compared to a Western meatball mixture, this is correct.
  4. 4
    Shape the four large meatballs. The lion's head is a large meatball, traditionally about 130 to 140 g each, roughly 7 to 8 cm diameter, the "lion's head" of the name. With wet hands (the mixture is sticky, water on the hands prevents grabbing), portion the mixture into 4 equal portions of about 130 g each. Toss each portion gently between two wet hands like a baseball, the toss helps round the meatball and works air out of the surface; aggressive shaping compresses the texture you've been building. Place the shaped meatballs on a plate or sheet pan. The meatballs are very wet and may slump slightly; that is correct. They will firm up during the brief sear and during the braise.
  5. 5
    Prep the napa cabbage bed. While shaping the meatballs, separate the 600 g napa cabbage into individual leaves. Tear the tougher base ribs from the leafy portions; roughly chop the ribs to 3 cm pieces and tear the leaves to 4 to 5 cm pieces. Keep the ribs and leaves separate, the ribs go in first as the bed, the leaves go on top after the meatballs are nestled in. Slice the 15 g ginger into thin coins and lightly smash the 30 g scallion whites with the flat of a knife.
  6. 6
    Sear the meatballs (lightly). Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide heavy skillet or wok over medium-high heat. The meatballs are wet and delicate, handle them gently. With a wide spatula or two large spoons, transfer the meatballs to the hot pan in a single layer. Sear undisturbed for 90 to 120 seconds on the first side, just until a pale golden-brown crust forms, this is structural for the dish, the crust sets the meatball's shape so it doesn't fall apart in the braise, but the sear should be light and quick. With the wide spatula, gently turn each meatball to expose a fresh face; sear another 60 to 90 seconds. The meatballs are still raw in the center and the surface is pale-gold rather than the deep mahogany of a Western seared meatball; that is correct. Transfer the seared meatballs to a plate. Maillard sear
  7. 7
    Build the braise. In a wide pot or Dutch oven (large enough for the 4 meatballs to sit in a single layer with the napa around them, 24 to 28 cm diameter is right), arrange the chopped napa ribs in a 2 to 3 cm layer at the bottom; this is the bed. Lay the 15 g ginger coins and the 30 g smashed scallion whites on top of the ribs. Nest the four seared meatballs in the cabbage bed, with the leaves of napa torn around and over them. Pour in the 800 ml chicken or pork stock; the liquid should come to roughly the top of the cabbage but the meatballs should not be fully submerged (the meatball's top surface remains exposed and braises in the steam rather than the liquid, this is structural, lion's head is a braise not a soup). Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy sauce, the 30 g / 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine, and the 6 g / 1 tsp rock sugar. Bring to a low boil over medium-high heat, then immediately reduce to the bare simmer.
  8. 8
    Braise for 60 minutes. Cover the pot with a lid (a tight lid is correct, the braise is a sealed-environment cook). Reduce heat to maintain the barest simmer, small bubbles breaking the surface every few seconds, not an active simmer. The temperature inside the covered pot should be just below boiling, around 95 to 98°C. Over the 60 minutes, several things happen: (1) the cabbage wilts and releases its water into the broth, increasing the braising liquid by 200 to 300 ml, (2) the meatballs cook through internally, the cornstarch-and-egg-white-and-myosin matrix sets, the water bound into the meat stays bound, the pork belly cubes render their fat into small internal pockets, (3) the broth absorbs pork fat and gelatin from the meatballs and becomes rich, slightly glossy, deeply savory, and (4) the meatballs absorb broth aromatics and seasoning. Do not lift the lid during the 60 minutes (or at most once at the 30-minute mark to confirm progress); each lift releases steam and lengthens the cook time. The dish is done when the meatballs are firm to the touch on top (the structure has set) and the broth is fragrant and deeply flavored.
  9. 9
    Final adjustments. After the 60-minute braise, lift the lid. Taste the broth, it should read deeply savory, slightly sweet from the rock sugar, with a clear ginger-and-scallion-and-Shaoxing-wine perfume and a soy backbone. Adjust salt if needed; the dish should NOT read aggressively salty (it's a clear-broth dish), but it should be unmistakably seasoned. If the broth has reduced too much (more than a quarter of the volume gone), add 100 ml hot water. If the broth is too thin (very rare with this technique), uncover and simmer 5 to 10 minutes to reduce. Fish out the smashed scallion whites with tongs and discard; the ginger coins can stay (they're part of the served broth) or be removed for a cleaner plating. Drizzle the 3 g / ½ tsp toasted sesame oil over the broth surface if using (optional).
  10. 10
    Plate and serve. Transfer each meatball to a wide shallow bowl with a slotted spoon, accompanied by a generous mound of the braised napa cabbage and several ladles of broth, the cabbage-and-broth around the meatball is half the dish. Scatter the 10 g sliced scallion greens over the top. Serve immediately with steamed white rice on the side (long-grain jasmine or short-grain Chinese rice, your preference); the rice catches the broth and the meatball is eaten with chopsticks or a spoon, broken apart on the plate and dipped through the broth. This is a banquet-scale dish in Chinese tradition, it can be the centerpiece of a 4-to-6-dish family meal, paired with a stir-fried green vegetable (water spinach, choy sum), a cold appetizer (smashed cucumbers, pickled radish), and rice.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional sauce direction→red-braised (红烧, hong shao) version

    The dark-braised counterpart to the clear-broth version above, and the more commonly seen lion's head outside of Yangzhou. For the broth: replace the light soy sauce in Phase 3 with 25 g / 5 tsp dark soy sauce plus 10 g / 2 tsp light soy sauce; increase the rock sugar to 15 g / 1 tbsp; add 1 star anise pod and a 2 cm cinnamon stick. The broth braises down to a darker mahogany-glossy sauce that coats the meatballs and the cabbage. The red version reads richer and more festive; the white version reads more refined and is the canonical banquet preparation in Yangzhou tradition.

  • Protein protein addition→pork-and-shrimp version

    Replace 100 g of the ground pork with 100 g of finely chopped peeled shrimp (the Cantonese refinement of lion's head, common in upscale dim sum and Cantonese banquet versions). The shrimp adds glutamate, sweetness, and a slight bounce to the texture. Same technique otherwise. This is the version served at many high-end Cantonese restaurants in Hong Kong and Guangzhou.

  • Occasion serving form→mini lion's head (acceptable banquet variation)

    Shape 12 to 16 small meatballs (30 to 40 g each) instead of 4 large ones; reduce the braise time to 35 to 40 minutes. The dish reads more like a Chinese hot-pot preparation than the classic large-meatball lion's head, but the flavor is identical. This is the right move for a multi-dish banquet where lion's head is one of six dishes and the large meatball would be overwhelming.

  • Occasion one-pot simplification→skip the sear and add directly to the braise

    A simpler weeknight version: skip the sear in Phase 6 entirely. Bring the broth and the cabbage bed to a low boil in the wide pot, then gently lower the raw meatballs into the broth with a spoon. Reduce to the bare simmer and braise covered for 65 to 70 minutes (slightly longer to compensate for no sear). The dish reads slightly less aromatic on the meatball surface but is otherwise identical; this is the home-cook simplification and is the right move when the wok or skillet is unavailable.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • ground pork shoulder + pork belly blend → ground pork shoulder alone (500 g, no belly) plus 25 g additional neutral oil The all-shoulder version with added oil approximates the fat content of the shoulder-and-belly blend; the dish reads slightly less complex in the rendered-fat pockets but is fully recognizable. Lean ground pork (90/10 loin) does not work for this dish, the meatball reads dry and dense.
  • water chestnut → jicama, bamboo shoots, or finely diced lotus root (blanched first) Jicama is the closest 1:1 substitute for the crunch; bamboo shoots from a can (rinsed) work well; lotus root needs a 30-second blanch in boiling water before dicing to remove the raw-starch flavor. Skipping the crunch element entirely produces a dish that reads more uniform and less interesting; do not skip if possible.
  • napa cabbage → savoy cabbage, bok choy, or yu choy Savoy cabbage is the closest 1:1 substitute (similar leaf-rib distinction, slightly tougher). Bok choy (full-sized, not baby) works with a slightly different sweet-vegetal character. Yu choy (Chinese flowering cabbage) is more delicate and wilts faster; reduce its braising time by 15 minutes by adding only in the last 45 minutes. Green cabbage is too dense and reads wrong for this dish.
  • Shaoxing wine → dry sherry, mirin, or omit Dry sherry is the canonical Western substitute for Shaoxing, same flavor profile (aged rice wine with similar volatile aromatics), same 1:1 substitution. Mirin is sweeter and works at 0.75x the volume. Omitting entirely produces a dish that reads less wine-cooked and more straight-stock; acceptable for cooks avoiding alcohol but a meaningful flavor loss.
  • chicken or pork stock → low-sodium chicken broth, dashi, or vegetable stock Low-sodium chicken broth is the supermarket substitute; the dish loses some body. Dashi (Japanese kombu-katsuobushi stock) produces a meaningfully different but interesting cross-cultural version. Vegetable stock works for a pescatarian-leaning preparation but the dish loses its meaty depth. Homemade stock is meaningfully better than any store-bought version for this dish, see homemade alternative.
  • rock sugar → granulated sugar or light brown sugar Rock sugar is the canonical Chinese braise sugar and is available at any Chinese grocery; the rock crystals dissolve slowly during the braise and produce a slightly glossier sauce than granulated sugar. Granulated white sugar is the direct substitute and works at 1:1; the dish is no worse for the swap. Light brown sugar adds a faint molasses note that some cooks prefer in the red-braised version.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → chicken or pork stock (homemade or unsalted store-bought) make it yourself Homemade unsalted chicken stock is meaningfully better in this dish than any store-bought version. The dish is a clear-broth braise where the broth is half the experience; the body and gelatin in homemade stock produce a final dish where the meatballs are bathed in a silky, deeply flavored liquid, where store-bought broth produces a meatball bathed in thin salted water with soy flavor. Make 2 L in an afternoon and freeze in 1-cup portions; this dish (and many others) is then accessible on a weeknight.

Notes

Why the high-water technique. The structural element that distinguishes Chinese lion''s head from every other meatball tradition in the world is the high internal water content, 90 ml of cold water paddled into 500 g of meat, or 18% water by weight. No Italian meatball, no Thai larb meatball, no Swedish köttbullar, has remotely this much water in the mixture; most Western meatball traditions use a panade of starch and milk that contributes maybe 4 to 6% water by weight. The Chinese technique is structurally different: the cold water is paddled directly into the meat in three additions, and the extracted myosin protein matrix binds the water inside the meatball, trapping it in a network of denatured protein strands. During the long braise, this water stays bound, it does not weep out into the broth (which would happen with under-paddled meat) and the meatball stays internally moist throughout the 60-minute cook. The result is a meatball texture closer to silken tofu than to the dense bouncy texture of Western meatballs; the bite reads almost custard-like, with the discrete fat-pocket and water-chestnut-crunch elements punctuating the soft matrix. This is the entire point of lion''s head, and it is impossible to produce without the water-paddling step.

The one-direction-stir rule. The traditional Chinese instruction for myosin extraction is to stir in one direction only, always clockwise or always counterclockwise, never reversing. The mechanism is structural: the myosin matrix forms long protein strands that align along the direction of stir, and reversing direction disrupts the alignment and weakens the matrix. The home-cook version of this is to use a chopstick or a stiff spatula and stir in vigorous one-direction circles. The mixture goes from loose-and-shaggy to noticeably elastic in 60 to 90 seconds; the elastic, pull-up-and-fall-back texture is the cue that the myosin has fully extracted and the matrix is set. Adding water before the myosin matrix has formed will produce a soupy, broken mixture; add water only after the meat passes the spoon-stick test.

The pork belly cubes are structural. The discrete 3 to 4 mm cubes of pork belly are not interchangeable with additional ground pork. During the 60-minute braise, the cubes render their fat slowly into small internal pockets, when you bite into a properly made lion''s head, you should hit small pockets of liquid pork fat that release onto the tongue. This is the textural signature of the dish, and it is impossible to produce with ground belly (the fat distributes uniformly and the pockets are too small to perceive). Hand-chopped, not machine-ground, is the canonical Yangzhou technique for the whole meatball, the slight unevenness of hand-chopped meat produces more discrete fat-and-lean elements throughout. For most cooks, ground shoulder plus hand-diced belly cubes is the practical compromise that gives 90% of the canonical texture.

Why the light sear, not a hard sear. Western meatballs get a deep mahogany Maillard crust on multiple faces before going into the sauce; lion''s head gets only a pale-gold light sear that just sets the surface and prevents the meatball from falling apart in the braise. The reason is the dish''s flavor profile: lion''s head is a refined, clear-broth dish where the meatball''s flavor should read as pork-and-aromatic, not pork-and-Maillard. A deeply seared meatball would over-flavor the clear broth and shift the dish toward the red-braised register. The Maillard browning that defines Western meatballs is intentionally minimized here. The same light sear shows up in many Huaiyang and Cantonese preparations where the dish''s flavor balance depends on un-overshadowed protein.

The napa cabbage bed is structural. The 600 g of napa is not a side dish, it is a structural component of the braise. The cabbage does three things: (1) cushions the meatballs and prevents them from sticking to the pot bottom, (2) releases its water into the broth and increases the braising liquid by 200 to 300 ml over the 60 minutes (which is why the initial broth level can be below the top of the meatballs, the cabbage water makes up the difference), and (3) absorbs pork fat and broth aromatics and becomes its own near-equal-component of the final dish. The eating experience is meatball-and-cabbage-and-broth in roughly equal proportions on the spoon. Skimping on the cabbage (using only 300 g, for example) produces a meatball-centric dish that loses the canonical balance.

Why white pepper, not black. White pepper is the canonical Chinese pepper for pork (and for most pork dishes in Chinese, Vietnamese, and other Southeast Asian cuisines). It is more aromatic and less sharp than black pepper, with a slightly fermented-funky character. For lion''s head specifically, the white pepper compounds the ginger-scallion-Shaoxing aromatic register; black pepper would compete with it. The difference is meaningful, a lion''s head with black pepper reads slightly off-cuisine, even if a Western diner cannot articulate why.

The bare-simmer rule. The 60-minute braise must happen at the barest simmer, around 95 to 98°C internal pot temperature, with small bubbles breaking the surface every few seconds rather than an active rolling simmer. If the pot boils, the meatballs are agitated and the texture coarsens; the bound water also tends to weep out under aggressive heat. The barely-bubbling lid-on environment is the right cook. On most stovetops this is the lowest setting, sometimes with a heat diffuser between the pot and the burner; on a gas range with weak low settings, the pot may need to sit slightly off-center on the burner to drop the heat. The dish forgives small variations but punishes a rolling simmer.

Make-ahead and leftovers. The full cooked dish (meatballs, cabbage, broth) holds in the refrigerator for 4 days and improves over the first 2 days as the flavors integrate further. Reheat in a covered pot over medium-low heat, adding 100 ml of water or stock if the broth has thickened too much. The meatballs alone freeze well, freeze in a sealed container submerged in their broth, and reheat directly from frozen in additional broth for 20 to 25 minutes. The raw meatball mixture does not hold well, the bound water begins to weep out after 4 to 6 hours of refrigeration. Shape and cook the meatballs the same day you mix them.

Banquet context and serving. Lion''s head is a banquet-scale dish in the Yangzhou tradition, it would not typically be eaten as a single-dish dinner, but as part of a 4-to-6-dish meal. The companion dishes are typically: a stir-fried leafy green (water spinach with garlic, or choy sum with oyster sauce), a cold appetizer plate (smashed cucumbers, pickled radish, century egg with tofu), a quick stir-fry (Shanghainese stir-fried prawns, or kung pao chicken if mixing in Sichuan), and rice. For a small-table dinner of 2 to 4 people, the 4 meatballs serve as the centerpiece, with rice and a green vegetable on the side; the dish reads as the protein course of a Chinese family-style meal. Western diners unfamiliar with the format sometimes expect the meatball to be one of several mains; in Chinese context it is the main course, and the cabbage-and-broth is half of what makes it a complete dish.

Sources
  • Adapted from the Huaiyang (Yangzhou, Jiangsu) tradition — Lion's head meatballs (狮子头, shīzi tóu) are one of the signature dishes of Huaiyang cuisine, the cuisine of the Yangzhou and Huai'an regions of Jiangsu province, on the Yangtze River. Huaiyang is one of the four great traditions of Chinese cuisine (alongside Cantonese, Sichuan, and Shandong) and is known for refined knife work, delicate seasoning, and braised seafood and pork preparations. The dish dates to at least the Sui dynasty (7th century) and was reportedly a favorite of the Qianlong Emperor (18th century). The canonical version uses pork shoulder with a small proportion of pork belly for fat, finely hand-chopped rather than machine-ground (the hand-chop is structural, it produces a coarser, more discrete-textured mince than machine grinding, and the dish texture depends on this), water chestnut for crunch, ginger and scallion for aromatics, and a clear soy-rice-wine braise on a bed of napa cabbage. Two major sub-variants exist: the white (清炖, clear-braised) version with a pale broth, and the red (红烧, soy-braised) version with a darker, more reduced sauce. This recipe is the white version, which is the more classical and refined preparation; the red version is described in the modifications.
