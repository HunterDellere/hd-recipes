Recipe

Senegalese-inspired savory millet salad with mango, mint, and ginger-lime

Pearl millet steamed to a fork-fluffed crumb, then tossed with diced ripe mango, slivered red onion macerated in lime, oil-toasted peanuts, mint, cilantro, and a ginger-lime-honey vinaigrette built on a small hit of habanero. The frame borrows from Senegalese thiakry (traditionally a sweet steamed-millet preparation with sour milk and sugar) and reroutes it into a savory grain salad on West African pantry vocabulary: peanut, ginger, scotch-bonnet heat, lime. The thesis is the contrast between the fluffy mildly-sweet grain crumb and the aggressive aromatic dressing, with the mango carrying a structural sweet-juicy load against the heat.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Start the millet first and let it cool fully before composing. Hot or warm millet wilts the mint and cilantro on contact and turns the mango mushy; the cooled crumb is the structural base for the dressing. Plan 25 minutes of cook plus 15 to 20 minutes of cool-down before assembly.
  • Macerate the slivered red onion in lime juice for 15 minutes before the toss. Unmacerated red onion overpowers the ginger and habanero; the acid soak shifts it from aggressive to bright and mellows the sulfur volatiles.
  • Source pearl millet from a West African or Indian grocery. Foxtail and finger millet cook with different water ratios (see notes) and the texture is meaningfully different; do not substitute without adjusting.

Phase 1, millet crumb

  2. 10 g neutral oil for toasting
  3. 400 ml water

Phase 2, lime-macerated red onion

  1. 80 g red onion, peeled, halved through the root, sliced pole-to-pole into 2 mm slivers — (½ medium onion; pole-to-pole slicing reads less aggressive than equator-cut rings and the slivers macerate evenly)
  2. 15 g lime juice, fresh
  3. 1 g kosher salt

Phase 3, ginger-lime-honey vinaigrette

  4. 2 g thai chili, finely minced, stem trimmed, seeds left in for full heat, finely minced — (1 small red chili; scotch bonnet or habanero is the canonical West African heat, see substitutions, use ~½ deseeded for similar punch)
  5. 3 g kosher salt
  6. 60 g neutral oil (peanut, grapeseed, or rice bran)

Phase 4, finish

  1. 250 g ripe mango (Ataulfo, Kent, or Alphonso), cheeks cut from the pit, score-cubed into 1 cm dice, scooped out with a spoon
  2. 60 g raw peanuts, skin-on, oil-toasted (see Step 7) and roughly chopped
  3. 8 g mint leaves, packed, leaves picked from stems, torn or coarsely chopped just before assembly
  4. 10 g cilantro leaves with tender upper stems, leaves and tender upper stems picked, the rest discarded
  5. 25 g scallion, green parts only, green parts thinly sliced on a sharp bias — (2 scallions, greens only; the whites are too aggressive raw against the already-macerated red onion)

Execution

  1. 1
    Rinse the 200 g / 1 cup dry pearl millet in a fine-mesh sieve under cold running water for 30 seconds, agitating with your fingers, until the rinse water runs clear. Drain thoroughly. The rinse removes surface starch and dust; rinsing matters more for millet than for rice because millet kernels release a faintly bitter wash that mutes the cooked grain if left.
  2. 2
    In a 3-quart heavy saucepan with a tight-fitting lid, warm the 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil over medium heat. Add the drained millet and toast in the dry oil, stirring constantly with a wooden spoon or spatula, for 90 to 120 seconds until the grains darken one shade and the smell turns distinctly popcorn-grain. The dry-toast is what separates a pilaf-style millet that reads aromatic from a boiled-in-water millet that reads cleanly cooked but flat. Pull from heat the moment the popcorn note is fully developed; another 30 seconds and the toast goes bitter. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  3. 3
    Add the 400 ml / 1⅔ cups water and the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt to the toasted millet. Bring to a hard boil over high heat, stirring once to break up any clumps, then drop the heat to a bare simmer (the lowest setting where bubbles still break the surface). Cover tightly and cook 18 to 22 minutes; do not lift the lid during the cook. At 18 minutes, listen at the lid; if you hear sizzle, the water is gone, pull from heat. If you still hear gentle bubbling, give it another 2 to 4 minutes.
  4. 4
    Off heat, leave the covered pot to rest for 10 minutes; the lid traps steam that finishes hydrating the kernels and lets the crumb separate cleanly when forked. After the rest, uncover and immediately fluff the millet with a fork; rake the fork through in straight lines without stirring (stirring compacts the grain into porridge). The cooked millet should be light, fluffy, separated kernels with a faint chewy bite. Spread on a wide plate or sheet pan to cool to warm-not-hot; hot millet on the platter wilts the herbs and breaks the dressing.
  5. 5
    While the millet rests, macerate the red onion. In a small bowl, combine the 80 g / ½ medium-onion worth of slivered red onion with the 15 g / 1 tbsp lime juice and the 1 g / ¼ tsp kosher salt. Toss to coat and leave at room temperature for 15 minutes. The acid plus salt knocks down the raw allium sulfur compounds and softens the texture; the onion goes from white-raw to pink-translucent and the bite becomes pleasant rather than aggressive. Acid-milk souring
  6. 6
    Build the vinaigrette. In a small bowl or jar, combine the 12 g / 1 tbsp microplaned ginger, the 45 g / 3 tbsp lime juice, the 18 g / 1 tbsp honey, the 2 g / 1 small minced thai chili, and the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt. Whisk to dissolve the salt and honey. Stream in the 60 g / ¼ cup neutral oil in a thin steady drizzle while whisking constantly; the dressing tightens as the oil emulsifies into the lime-and-honey base. The microplaned ginger acts as a structural particulate stabilizer; the dressing will not hold the emulsion as long as a mustard-based vinaigrette but will stay integrated for 20 to 30 minutes. Taste; the dressing should read sharp-acid up front, then sweet, then heat in the throat; if flat, more salt; if too sharp, another small pinch of honey. Vinaigrette emulsion
  7. 7
    Toast the peanuts. In a small dry skillet over medium-low heat, place the 60 g / ½ cup raw skin-on peanuts and toast, shaking the pan every 10 to 15 seconds, for 8 to 10 minutes until the skins darken slightly and the peanuts smell distinctly nutty. The pan should be hot enough that you hear a faint crackle but not so hot that you smell anything burnt; medium-low is the right setting. Transfer the toasted peanuts immediately to a plate to stop the carryover cook, cool 2 minutes, and chop roughly into halves and rough quarters; whole peanuts read awkward in the salad, dust-fine reads like sand. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  8. 8
    Assemble. Spread the warm-not-hot 200 g / 1 cup dry-weight cooked millet across the bottom of a wide shallow bowl or platter. Drizzle one-third of the ginger-lime-honey vinaigrette (~50 g / 3 tbsp) over the millet and toss gently with a fork or two large spoons to absorb. The warm grain pulls the dressing into its structure.
  9. 9
    Scatter the 250 g / 1 large mango worth of diced mango across the dressed millet. Add the macerated red onion with any lime juice from the maceration bowl (the pink-tinged liquid is structural acid, do not discard). Toss gently with a fork to integrate the mango and onion into the grain; the mango cubes should be visible throughout the salad, not piled in one corner. Drizzle another third of the vinaigrette (~50 g / 3 tbsp) and toss again.
  10. 10
    Scatter the chopped 60 g / ½ cup toasted peanuts evenly across the top, then the 25 g / 2 scallions worth of sliced scallion greens, then the 8 g / ½ cup torn mint, then the 10 g / ½ cup cilantro leaves-and-tender-stems. Drizzle the remaining vinaigrette (~50 g / 3 tbsp) across the top. Serve within 20 minutes of assembly while the herbs are still vivid; the salad holds 2 to 3 hours covered at room temperature but the mint and cilantro begin to look tired after 30 minutes and the mango begins to weep water into the grain after 90 minutes.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→with grilled peanut-coated chicken thigh

    Rub 4 boneless skinless chicken thighs (about 600 g) with 5 g / 1 tsp suya spice or, in its absence, a paste of 30 g / 2 tbsp ground roasted peanuts, 3 g / 1 tsp paprika, 1 g / ¼ tsp cayenne, 2 g / ½ tsp ground ginger, 2 g / ½ tsp salt. Grill or pan-sear 6 minutes per side over medium-high heat, rest 5 minutes, slice into 1 cm strips, and lay across the assembled salad. The peanut crust on the chicken echoes the toasted peanuts in the salad; the dish becomes a full main course on the suya-spice register.

  • Regional grain→with fonio instead of millet

    Replace the 200 g pearl millet with 200 g fonio (the West African ancient grain, more authentic to Senegalese pantry than millet). Fonio cooks in 5 minutes with 1:1.5 grain-to-water ratio and a 5-minute steam-finish; it produces a fluffier, more particulate crumb than millet. The dish reads even closer to West African register; pearl Thiakry tradition uses pearl millet, but fonio is the ancestral Senegalese grain and Pierre Thiam has been the principal chef bringing it to international attention.

  • Occasion fruit→with diced pineapple instead of mango

    Replace the 250 g ripe mango with 250 g ripe pineapple (cored, cut into 1 cm dice). Pineapple is sharper and more enzymatic (the bromelain enzyme attacks the herbs over time, so dress and serve quickly), but the structural role (sweet, juicy, holds in a dressing) is preserved. The dish drifts toward Caribbean rather than West African register.

  • Regional heat→with scotch bonnet for canonical West African heat

    Replace the thai chili with ½ a deseeded scotch bonnet (or habanero) chili, finely minced, in the vinaigrette. Scotch bonnet carries the same heat level as thai chili but a markedly fruitier, more aromatic profile; it is the canonical West African chili and reads more honestly in the dish. Wear gloves when handling and do not touch your face.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • pearl millet → fonio, or quinoa, or coarse semolina couscous Fonio is the most West-African-coherent substitute and cooks faster (5 minutes plus 5-minute rest); quinoa is the most accessible Western alternative and reads similarly fluffy and gluten-free; coarse semolina couscous is wheat-based (not for celiac diners) but produces the lightest, most particulate crumb. All work in this dish; pearl millet is the closest to the canonical Senegalese thiakry base.
  • thai chili (for canonical West African heat) → scotch bonnet (½ deseeded), or habanero (½ deseeded), or fresno chili (1 whole, much milder) Scotch bonnet (atarodo in Yoruba) and habanero are the same Capsicum chinense species and the canonical West African heat source; both bring a fruity-tropical aroma alongside the heat. Fresno is milder and rounder; use a full chili rather than half for similar punch. Thai chili (the recipe default) is hot and clean but reads Southeast Asian rather than West African.
  • honey → palm sugar (jaggery) or light brown sugar, dissolved in the lime juice before adding Palm sugar is the more West African choice and reads slightly more caramel-rich than honey. Dissolve 18 g / 1 tbsp grated palm sugar in the lime juice over low heat for 30 seconds before building the rest of the vinaigrette, or pound it with the salt in a mortar before adding the lime. Light brown sugar works the same way and is the most accessible substitute.
  • ripe mango (Ataulfo, Kent, Alphonso) → ripe peach, ripe nectarine, or ripe papaya Peach and nectarine are the closest North American summer analogs; papaya is the closest tropical analog and reads slightly muskier. Avoid frozen mango chunks (they release too much water as they thaw and soak the grain); if frozen is the only option, thaw in a colander, pat dry, and dress just before serving.
  • peanuts, raw skin-on → dry-roasted peanuts (already toasted, skip Step 7), or chopped cashews Dry-roasted peanuts (unsalted) are an honest shortcut; the home-toast is freshest but the prepackaged version is acceptable. Cashews are the closest West-African-coherent substitute for cooks with peanut allergy in the household; the dish loses some structural conviction (peanut is the bigger West African pantry staple) but reads cleanly.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → ginger-lime-honey vinaigrette (the assembled dressing as a unit) make it yourself The vinaigrette doubles or triples easily and keeps 4 to 5 days refrigerated; making a batch as a standalone dressing yields enough for a week of grain salads, fish or chicken marinades, and dipping for raw vegetables. The standalone recipe documents the ratios and the rebuild-when-broken procedure.

Notes

Why pearl millet specifically. Pearl millet (Pennisetum glaucum, called sanio in Wolof and bajra in Hindi) is the Senegalese staple grain; thiakry as a sweet preparation, and savory grain-salad derivatives, are built on pearl millet specifically. Foxtail millet (the small-kernel Chinese xiaomi) cooks similarly but reads stickier and slightly more aromatic-popcorn; finger millet (the small reddish-brown ragi from southern India) cooks denser and earthier. For this dish, pearl is the right choice. Fonio (Digitaria exilis, the West African ancient grain) is even more honest to Senegalese cookery but harder to source outside of West African groceries and online specialty grocers; if you have it, swap it in (see modifications). Both pearl millet and fonio carry the gluten-free crumb that makes the salad accessible to celiac diners.

Why the dry-toast matters. Skipping the 90-second dry-toast yields a millet that tastes cleanly cooked but flat; the popcorn-aromatic pyrazines and furans that develop on the toasted grain surface read through the finished dish as a structural aromatic base. The same logic applies to Italian risotto (the toasting phase before stock addition), to Persian rice pilaf (the oil-toast before water), and to West African thiakry preparations where the steamed millet is sometimes briefly pan-toasted. The technique is shared across cuisines; the toast is the difference between aromatic depth and clean-but-flat grain.

The umami source is the toasted peanuts plus the salt-and-acid maceration on the onion plus the salt in the vinaigrette. The dish does not carry an obvious glutamate-rich ingredient (no fish sauce, no aged cheese, no fermented seasoning), and this is honest to the West African register where the umami often comes from groundnut paste and fermented locust bean (iru / dawadawa). The finishing acid is the lime in three places (the maceration, the vinaigrette, the residual liquid in the macerate bowl). For cooks who want a deeper umami anchor, see the modification for peanut-coated chicken thigh; the suya-spice register on the chicken introduces a second umami layer through the dry-spice roast on the meat.

Authenticity caveat. This dish is inspired by Senegalese thiakry tradition and built on West African pantry vocabulary, but it is not a traditional preparation. Real thiakry is sweet, eaten with sweetened sour milk or yogurt, sometimes with raisins or nutmeg; it is served as a breakfast or dessert, not as a main-course salad. The savory grain salad framing on the same base is a modern adaptation, the kind that Pierre Thiam and other contemporary West African chefs have popularized in their work bringing West African grains and pantry to international restaurants. Treated honestly as such; the dish reads as Senegalese in vocabulary, modern in form.

Make-ahead notes. The cooked-and-cooled millet refrigerates 3 to 4 days in an airtight container; the vinaigrette refrigerates 4 to 5 days (the emulsion breaks but rewhisks). The mango should be diced no more than 4 hours before serving (longer and it weeps water into the bowl); the herbs should be torn within 30 minutes of serving. For a make-ahead lunch, prep the millet, vinaigrette, and onion-maceration the night before, and assemble fresh at meal time with the mango, peanuts, and herbs.
