Tzatziki

The Greek garlic-cucumber-yogurt dip and sauce: thick strained yogurt, salted-and-drained grated cucumber, raw garlic pounded with salt, olive oil, lemon, and fresh dill. Cool, structural, and the universal pairing for grilled lamb and chicken, gyro and souvlaki, kebabs, fried vegetables, raw vegetable platters, and grilled flatbread. Built in 15 minutes with one structural patience step (the cucumber salt-drain).

  2. 5 g fine sea salt (for salting the cucumber)

The base

  1. 400 g whole-milk Greek yogurt (full-fat, true strained Greek style)
  2. 8 g garlic clove, peeled, pounded to a paste in a mortar with the salt below, or finely grated on a microplane — (2 medium cloves; raw garlic is canonical; the pounded paste integrates more cleanly than a grated mince)
  3. 3 g fine sea salt (for the sauce)

The finish

  1. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  2. 10 g fresh lemon juice (or red wine vinegar)
  3. 5 g fresh dill, leaves only, finely chopped

Execution

  1. 1
    Set a fine-mesh sieve over a bowl. Halve the 300 g / 1 large cucumber lengthwise, scrape out the seeds with a teaspoon, and grate the flesh on the large holes of a box grater into the sieve. Toss with the 5 g / ¾ tsp salt and let drain 20 to 30 minutes; the salt draws water out of the cucumber via osmosis. The drained water (about 100 g, half the original cucumber weight) is the failure mode of unprepared cucumber; this is the water that would otherwise dilute the sauce within an hour of mixing.
  2. 2
    After the salt-drain, gather the grated cucumber in a clean tea towel and squeeze firmly over the sink to remove any remaining water; the cucumber should reduce to about 150 g of dry shreds and feel almost like sauerkraut. Do not skip this squeeze; the residual water is the difference between a structural dip and a runny sauce. Discard the drained liquid and the squeeze water.
  3. 3
    In a mortar, pound the 8 g / 2 garlic cloves with the 3 g / ½ tsp salt to a smooth even paste, 1 to 2 minutes; the salt is structurally abrasive and breaks the garlic down faster than pounding alone. Alternative: grate the garlic on a microplane and stir with the salt to a paste. The paste is the canonical preparation; coarsely minced garlic reads aggressive and uneven in the finished dip.
  4. 4
    In a medium bowl, combine the 400 g / 1¾ cups Greek yogurt and the garlic-salt paste; whisk gently with a wooden spoon to combine without breaking the yogurt structure. Add the squeezed cucumber, the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil, the 10 g / 2 tsp lemon juice, and the 5 g / 2 tbsp chopped dill; fold to combine.
  5. 5
    Taste and adjust: tzatziki should read yogurt-forward with assertive garlic, gentle cucumber crunch, fresh dill, and a clean salt-and-acid finish. If flat, more salt and lemon; if too aggressive, more yogurt; if the cucumber dominates, more garlic. Refrigerate at least 1 hour before serving so the flavors integrate; tzatziki served immediately reads disjointed and the garlic dominates. Serve cold as a dip for raw vegetables, grilled flatbread, or pita; or as a sauce for grilled or roasted lamb, chicken, fish, gyro and souvlaki, fried eggplant or zucchini, kafta and kebabs, falafel, grain bowls, or roasted vegetables.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional herb pivot→as a mint tzatziki (more canonical in some Greek regions and across the Turkish-Iranian sphere)

    Replace the dill with 5 g / 2 tbsp finely chopped fresh mint; add 2 g / 1 tsp dried mint as well for a deeper layered mint profile. The mint version reads brighter and more cooling; canonical for serving with grilled lamb and pairs especially well with the warmer spice register (cumin, coriander, sumac) of Levantine and Turkish cookery.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as a Turkish cacık (thinner, pourable, finely diced cucumber)

    Use 200 g yogurt thinned with 100 g cold water; finely dice the cucumber instead of grating (skip the salt-drain because the dice is small enough to stay structural in a thinner sauce); use mint instead of dill; reduce the olive oil to 15 g. The result is structurally cacık, served as a soup-adjacent cold dish or as a thin pourable sauce; the canonical pairing for grilled lamb skewers (şiş kebap), pilaf, and the broader Turkish table.

  • Occasion flavor pivot→as a roasted-garlic tzatziki (milder, sweeter)

    Roast a whole head of garlic (drizzled with olive oil, wrapped in parchment, 200°C / 400°F oven, 40 minutes) until the cloves are soft and golden. Squeeze the roasted cloves into a paste; use 30 g / 2 tbsp of the paste in place of the raw garlic. The result reads sweeter, mellower, more rounded; pairs especially well with roasted vegetables, sweet root vegetable bowls, and grain plates where aggressive raw garlic would dominate.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole-milk Greek yogurt (full-fat, true strained Greek style) → whole-milk regular yogurt, strained 2 hours Line a fine-mesh sieve with cheesecloth or a clean tea towel, set over a bowl, and pour in 600 g of whole-milk regular yogurt; refrigerate 2 hours, then transfer the strained yogurt (about 400 g) to the recipe. The strain produces an authentic thick base; supermarket "Greek" yogurt that has been thickened with stabilizers reads grainy and works less well.
  • fresh lemon juice → red wine vinegar Lemon is the more internationally common Greek choice; red wine vinegar is canonical in some regional preparations (especially across the Greek mainland) and reads slightly sharper and less floral. Use the same 10 g amount; both are correct, with regional preference.
  • fresh dill → fresh mint, or a 50/50 dill-mint blend, or fresh oregano (sparingly) Dill is the most international Greek choice; mint is canonical in many regional and Turkish-influenced preparations; the 50/50 blend reads complex and modern; fresh oregano (1 tbsp finely chopped, less than the dill amount) is canonical in some Cretan preparations and reads more savory.

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

Notes

The cucumber salt-drain is the single non-negotiable technique in tzatziki. Cucumbers are 96 percent water; without the salt-drain-and-squeeze, the cucumber releases its water gradually into the yogurt over the first 2 to 4 hours of sitting, producing a thin watery sauce that has the right flavor but completely wrong texture. The salt-drain pulls about half the cucumber weight out as water in 25 minutes; the squeeze removes another 30 g; the cucumber reduces from 300 g of fresh shreds to about 150 g of structural shreds that retain crunch and flavor without releasing water. This is the difference between authentic tzatziki and the watery dip that home cooks often produce on first attempt.

The 1-hour rest before serving is the second non-negotiable. Tzatziki served immediately reads disjointed: the garlic dominates because the raw allicin has not yet integrated, the cucumber tastes separate from the yogurt because the salt has not equilibrated, the dill reads aggressive because the volatiles have not bloomed in the dairy fat. After 1 hour refrigerated, the components integrate into a single structural sauce; after 4 hours, the integration is complete and the sauce is at peak. Plan tzatziki as a make-ahead component, never an immediate-service sauce.

Versatility. Tzatziki is structurally one of the broadest dip-and-sauce hybrids in the Mediterranean pantry. Use it as: the canonical sauce for gyro, souvlaki, lamb kebabs, kefta, and chicken kebabs (the Greek and broader Mediterranean grill canon); a dip for raw vegetables (cucumber, carrot, radish, fennel, bell pepper, cherry tomato), grilled flatbread, pita, and grilled bread; a sauce for grilled or roasted lamb (leg of lamb, lamb chops, lamb shoulder), grilled chicken, grilled or roasted fish (especially salmon, branzino, sea bass, mackerel); a sauce for fried vegetables (eggplant, zucchini, breaded cauliflower, fritters), falafel, and crispy chickpeas; a stir-in for grain bowls (farro, freekeh, bulgur, brown rice) with roasted vegetables; a sauce for roasted potatoes (a Greek diner classic), baked potatoes, and grilled potato wedges; a topping for shakshuka, baked eggs, and savory breakfast bowls; a cooling component on spicy plates (Indian, Mexican, North African) where a yogurt sauce balances heat; and as a sandwich spread for lamb, vegetable, and grain-based wraps and pita pockets.

Pairs across Greek, Cretan, Cypriot, broader Eastern Mediterranean, Turkish (with cacık-style modifications), Levantine, North African, contemporary American Mediterranean cookery, and modern Israeli kitchens.

Storage. Keeps 4 days refrigerated in a sealed container; the cucumber continues to release a small amount of water (the salt-drain is structural but not absolute), so a small pool may form on the surface, which is normal; stir before serving and pour off any excess. The flavor peaks at day 1 to 2; after day 4 the cucumber crunch fades and the dill darkens, still serviceable but past peak.
