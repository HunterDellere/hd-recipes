Homemade doubanjiang (short-cure version)

A 4-6 week home version of Pixian-style chili bean paste, using fermented broad beans and dried chilies. Stub. The authentic 1-3 year cure is not practical at home; this is the closest short-cycle approximation.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Bean ferment base

  1. 500 g dried fava (broad) beans, peeled — (soaked overnight, then fermented separately for 2-3 weeks before this recipe begins)
  2. 5 g koji rice (Aspergillus oryzae starter) — (for the bean ferment; available from sake-brewing suppliers)

Chili paste

  1. 800 g fresh red erjingtiao chilies (or Mexican guajillo + chiles de árbol blend) — (stems removed, rough-chopped, salted at 12% of weight overnight)
  2. 96 g kosher salt — (12% of fresh chili weight; for the chili salt-press)

Combined cure

  1. 500 g fermented broad beans (from above)
  2. 800 g salted chili paste (from above)
  3. 50 g wheat flour — (toasted lightly; for body)
  4. 30 ml baijiu or vodka — (alcohol layer at the top to suppress mold during cure)

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: ferment the broad beans separately first. Soak peeled fava beans overnight, drain, steam 30-40 minutes until tender. Spread on a tray, cool to body temperature, dust with koji starter, and ferment in a warm dark place 5-7 days until covered with a fluffy white-green Aspergillus mat. Then sun-cure or oven-dry at 60°C / 140°F for 24-48 hours until the beans are dry and lightly crumbly.7 d 0 h
  2. 2
    Stub: process the chilies. Stem fresh red chilies, rough chop, toss with 12% kosher salt by weight, pack into a clean jar, weight under brine, and ferment 14-21 days at 18-22°C / 65-72°F. The chilies will release their own brine, soften, and develop a pickled-fermented character.17 d 12 h
  3. 3
    Stub: combine and cure. Mix the fermented dried broad beans, salted fermented chili paste, and lightly-toasted wheat flour together into a thick paste. Pack into a wide-mouth jar, smooth the surface, pour 30 ml baijiu over the top to seal, cover with a breathable cloth (not airtight) and leave at warm room temperature (20-25°C / 68-77°F) for 4-6 weeks, stirring weekly and tasting at week 4 onward. The paste is ready when the bean and chili flavors have integrated into a single complex profile.21 d 0 h

Notes

This is a stub. To flesh out: the koji-cultivation chemistry (Aspergillus oryzae enzymatic breakdown of bean proteins into free amino acids), the sun-curing tradition (open ceramic vats in Pixian, alternating sun and shade), the 1-2 year industrial cure that produces real Pixian doubanjiang, the substitution rationale for koji rice as the modern home shortcut, and the 4-6 week mark as the earliest a home version is usable (still well short of restaurant-grade flavor depth). The recipe assumes a cook with significant fermentation experience and access to specialty ingredients (dried fava beans, koji starter, fresh red chilies in season). For most home cooks, buying authentic Pixian doubanjiang at an Asian grocery is the right answer; this homemade version is a project for cooks with strong interest in Sichuan fundamentals.

Realistic timeline: 8 to 10 weeks from start to first usable paste. The flavor improves dramatically with another 6 to 12 months of slow cure but the home environment usually isn't stable enough for that long.

Failure modes: visible non-target mold (orange, black, fuzzy white) at any point in the bean ferment or combined cure means contamination; discard. The beans should develop a soft white-green Aspergillus mat in koji ferment, not other colors. The chili ferment can produce kahm yeast (white film on brine) which is harmless and skimmed off; other surface molds are not.
