Xiao long bao aspic (pork skin and bone gelatin)

Concentrated pork-skin and pork-bone broth, simmered hard for hours and chilled to set into a sliceable aspic. The structural trick behind xiao long bao and sheng jian bao (the cubes of aspic melt back to broth inside the dumpling under steam, producing the burst of soup that defines the form). Leftover aspic doubles as a body-builder for any soup, braise, or sauce. Stub.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Stub: source pork skin (fresh from a butcher or frozen at Chinese groceries) at least a day ahead. The aspic structure depends on the high-collagen skin; pork bones alone produce a much weaker gel.
  • Stub: plan for the overnight chill. The aspic must set in the fridge for at least 8 hours before it can be cut into cubes; warm aspic is unusable for the dumpling-filling application.

Phase 1, blanch and rinse

  1. 500 g pork skin (fresh, scraped of any clinging fat)
  2. 500 g pork bones (neck, knuckle, or trotter)
  3. 2000 ml cold water (for blanch)

Phase 2, simmer

  1. 1500 ml cold water (fresh, after blanch)
  2. 20 g fresh ginger, smashed — (1 inch knob)
  3. 3 each scallion whites — (smashed)
  4. 30 ml Shaoxing wine

Phase 3, season

  1. 6 g kosher salt

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: blanch the 500 g pork skin and 500 g pork bones in the 2000 ml cold water. Bring to a hard boil, boil 5 minutes, drain, rinse. Scrape the blanched skin to remove any remaining hair, fat, or impurities; this is the structural cleanup step that makes the aspic clear rather than cloudy.
  2. 2
    Stub: combine the blanched skin and bones in a clean pot with the 1500 ml fresh cold water, 20 g ginger, 3 scallion whites, and 30 ml Shaoxing wine. Bring to a bare simmer, skim any foam, and hold at a tremor (below 90 C) for 3 to 4 hours. The skin will dissolve almost entirely into the liquid; the bones will contribute flavor without dissolving. Do not boil; a hard boil emulsifies fat and produces a cloudy aspic.
  3. 3
    Stub: strain through a fine-mesh sieve, then again through cheesecloth for a clear set. Season the strained liquid with the 6 g kosher salt while still warm; the salt dissolves cleanly and the aspic seasons evenly. Pour into a shallow square or rectangular container (the cube shape is structurally important for the dumpling application) and cool to room temperature, then refrigerate at least 8 hours and ideally overnight.
  4. 4
    Stub: the chilled aspic should be firm enough to cut into 5 mm cubes with a knife; it should hold a clean edge without weeping. If the aspic is too soft to cube (the skin-to-bone ratio was off, or the simmer was too short), the rescue is to re-warm, add 5 g powdered gelatin per 500 g liquid, dissolve, and re-chill. The aspic keeps refrigerated 5 days; freeze in 100 g portions for up to 3 months.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion time-shortcut→powdered gelatin in stock

    Stub: for the cook who has stock on hand and wants the aspic structure without the day-long cook, bloom 15 g powdered gelatin in 200 ml cold pork or chicken stock, then warm to dissolve. Chill in a shallow container. The result is a functional aspic for dumpling-filling use; the flavor depth of a real pork-skin-and-bone aspic is missing, but the structural function is preserved.

  • Regional flavor depth→red-braise-spiced aspic

    Stub: add 2 star anise, 1 small cassia stick, and 5 g rock sugar to the simmer phase. The resulting aspic carries a faint red-braise note that complements pork-and-crab xiao long bao or a Shanghainese sheng jian bao filling.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • pork skin → extra pork trotter or knuckle bones Pork trotter is mostly skin and connective tissue and is the closest available substitute. Use 750 g trotter to replace the 500 g skin plus 500 g bones; the gel will be slightly less firm but functionally equivalent.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the gel chemistry (collagen triple-helix denatures above 65 C, hydrolyzes to gelatin over hours of low simmer, sets back into a stable gel below 25 C; pork skin is roughly 30 percent collagen by dry weight, which is why a small batch of skin produces an aspic strong enough to cube), the technique difference from clear pork broth (this is the same starting ingredient but a much harder reduction and a much higher skin proportion, producing a 4 to 6 times stronger gel than a normal broth), the dumpling-application detail (the cubes go into the dumpling filling cold and re-liquefy under steam, producing the soup burst; cubes that are too soft melt into the filling at room temperature and lose the structural surprise), the leftover-uses framing (the aspic is a concentrated flavor-and-body asset that can boost soup mouthfeel dramatically, used as a fortifier for beef broth, chicken broth, or vegetable broth at a 1 cup aspic : 4 cup broth ratio), and the storage-and-portioning notes (freeze in 100 g portions for soup-fortification use, 500 g portions for a full dumpling batch).
