Maeuntang (Korean spicy fish stew)

A whole black sea bass sectioned with the head and spine intact, poached into a gochugaru-and-doenjang broth built on anchovy-kelp stock. Radish simmers first to a sweet translucence, the fish goes in only when the broth is set, and the pot finishes with tofu, chili, scallion, and a raw crown of crown daisy or watercress. The carcass is the point: its collagen gives the broth a gelatinous body that fillets cannot. A shared, fiery, umami-deep one-pot meal eaten over rice.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Make the anchovy-kelp stock FIRST, or have a substitute ready. The stock is a 25-minute task (kelp steeped cold then warmed, anchovies simmered 10 minutes, both strained) and the entire stew is built on it. Start it before you touch the fish. If you are short on time, a quick dashi or even lightly salted water plus extra doenjang will carry, but the broth loses depth.
  • Start short-grain rice in parallel. Maeuntang is eaten as a shared pot with individual bowls of rice, the broth spooned over. Time the rice (about 12-minute simmer plus 10-minute rest) so it is finished when the stew is, since the fish should be eaten the moment it is poached through.
  • The radish simmers BEFORE the fish goes in. This is the one piece of timing that cannot be collapsed: the radish needs 12 to 15 minutes in the seasoned broth to turn sweet and translucent, while the delicate fish needs only 6 to 10 minutes and goes in last. Adding them together gives you crunchy radish and overcooked, disintegrating fish. Read steps 4 through 6 before you start.

Mise en place by phase 6 phases ▾

Phase 1, anchovy-kelp stock

  1. 10 g kombu (dried kelp), wiped with a damp cloth, not rinsed — (one 10 x 10 cm piece; the surface white bloom is glutamate, leave it on)
  2. 25 g dried anchovies (large, for stock), heads and black guts pinched out to avoid bitterness — (about 12 to 15 large myeolchi; the gut removal is the difference between clean and bitter stock)
  3. 1400 ml water

Phase 2, fish

  1. 800 g whole black sea bass, scaled and gutted, head and frame kept on, scaled, gutted, gills removed, blood line scraped from the spine cavity, rinsed, sectioned crosswise into 4 cm pieces through the bone with the head split in half; head and spine included
  2. 4 g kosher salt

Phase 3, seasoning paste (yangnyeom)

  1. 18 g gochugaru (Korean chili flakes)
  2. 30 g gochujang (Korean chili paste)
  3. 20 g doenjang (Korean fermented soybean paste)
  4. 18 g garlic, minced — (5 to 6 cloves; pungent and structural in Korean stews)
  5. 8 g fresh ginger, minced

Phase 4, vegetables and tofu

  1. 300 g Korean radish (mu) or daikon, peeled, cut into 2.5 cm cubes or thick half-moons — (about ⅓ large Korean radish; simmers first, turns sweet and translucent and sweetens the broth)
  2. 200 g firm tofu, cut into 2 cm slabs — (about ½ block; firm holds its shape against the fish in the pot)
  3. 30 g green chili (Korean cheongyang or serrano), sliced on the bias — (2 chilies; fresh green heat and brightness added near the end)
  4. 40 g scallion, cut into 4 cm batons — (3 to 4 stalks; goes in near the end for sharp allium lift)

Phase 5, finish

  1. 50 g crown daisy (ssukgat) or watercress, tough lower stems trimmed, cut into 5 cm lengths — (2 large handfuls; the raw herbal crown added off heat. Watercress is the easy substitute)
  2. 4 g toasted sesame oil

Phase 6, serving

  1. 600 g steamed Japanese short-grain rice

Execution

  1. 1
    Build the anchovy-kelp stock first. Put the 10 g / one 10 cm piece kombu in a pot with the 1400 ml / 6 cups water and let it steep 15 minutes off heat (a cold steep extracts the kelp glutamate without the slimy alginates a hard boil would draw out). Pinch the heads and black guts off the 25 g / 12 to 15 dried anchovies and add the cleaned bodies. Bring to a gentle simmer over medium heat; the moment it simmers, lift out the kombu (boiled kelp turns the stock bitter and slimy). Simmer the anchovies 10 minutes, then strain out and discard the anchovies. You should have about 1.2 L of clean, savory stock. Set it aside.
  2. 2
    Prep and pre-salt the fish. If the counter did not section it, scale, gut, and pull the gills, then scrape the dark blood line from the inside of the spine cavity with a spoon (the blood line is the single biggest source of fishy flavor). Rinse and cut the whole fish crosswise into 4 cm sections through the bone with a heavy knife, splitting the head in half; keep the head and spine, they are the gelatin. Pat the pieces dry and salt them with the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt. Rest 10 minutes; the brief salt firms the surface and draws out a little surface water, which keeps the flesh from clouding the broth later.
  3. 3
    Mix the seasoning paste (yangnyeom). In a small bowl stir together the 18 g / 3 tbsp gochugaru, the 30 g / 1½ tbsp gochujang, the 20 g / 1 tbsp doenjang, the 18 g / 5 to 6 cloves minced garlic, and the 8 g / 1 tbsp minced ginger into a loose paste. Letting it sit while the radish cooks lets the gochugaru hydrate and bloom its color into the fats already in the paste.
  4. 4
    Start the radish in the seasoned broth. Bring the 1.2 L anchovy-kelp stock to a simmer in a wide, shallow pot. Whisk in the full seasoning paste (the 18 g gochugaru, 30 g gochujang, 20 g doenjang, 18 g garlic, 8 g ginger) until the broth turns a deep, even red. Add the 300 g / 2.5 cm-cubed Korean radish. Simmer 12 to 15 minutes, uncovered, until the radish is translucent at the edges and tender to a skewer; this is also where the doenjang and gochugaru lose their raw edge and the broth deepens. Taste: it should read savory, gently spicy, and faintly sweet from the radish. Adjust salt only with a little more doenjang, not table salt.
  5. 5
    Poach the fish. Slide the salted fish sections into the simmering broth, head pieces and spine first since they take longest, then the body sections. Tuck the 200 g / 2 cm-slab firm tofu in around the fish. Keep the pot at a steady simmer, not a hard boil (a violent boil shreds the lean flake and clouds the broth). Do not stir; nudge gently if needed. Cook 6 to 10 minutes by the thickness of the sections, until the flesh is opaque and just pulls from the bone at the thickest piece (an internal 55 to 58 °C). Skim any gray foam that rises.
  6. 6
    Add the aromatics and fresh heat. Scatter in the 30 g / 2 sliced green chili and the 40 g / 3 to 4 stalk scallion batons. Simmer 2 minutes more; the chili and scallion go in late so they keep their fresh, bright bite and lift the deep fermented base rather than stewing into it. Taste the broth one last time and correct: a little more gochugaru for color and warmth, a touch more doenjang for salt and depth.
  7. 7
    Finish off heat with the herbal crown. Pull the pot off the heat. Pile the 50 g / 2 handfuls crown daisy (or watercress) over the top and, if using, drizzle the 4 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil over the surface. The residual heat wilts the greens in about 30 seconds while keeping their grassy, slightly bitter aroma intact; that raw herbal brightness against the fiery, gelatin-rich broth is the finishing register of the dish. Bring the pot to the table and serve immediately, ladling broth, fish, tofu, and radish into bowls over the 600 g / 3 cups steamed short-grain rice.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein fish→with a different whole fish

    Swap the black sea bass for any fresh whole striped bass, rockfish, porgy, or red snapper at the same ~800 g size. Section through the bone the same way and keep the head and frame for the gelatin. Firmer fish (striped bass, rockfish) hold the poach with more margin; softer fish (snapper) want the lower end of the 6 to 10 minute window. The technique is unchanged.

  • Texture broth body→with added fish-head stock

    For a noticeably richer, more gelatinous broth, simmer extra fish heads and frames (from the fishmonger, often free) in the anchovy-kelp stock for 20 minutes before straining, then build the stew on that. The extra collagen sets the chilled leftovers to a firm wobble and pushes the stew toward the restaurant-style maeuntang body.

  • Heat heat→jiritang (the non-spicy version)

    Omit the gochugaru and gochujang entirely and roughly halve the doenjang; season the clear anchovy-kelp-and-fish broth with salt and a splash of the green chili only. This is jiritang, the clean, clear-broth sibling of maeuntang, meant to showcase the fish itself. The radish, tofu, and crown-daisy handling stay identical.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole black sea bass → striped bass, rockfish, porgy, red snapper, or thick cod chunks Any fresh firm white fish with a usable carcass works; striped bass and rockfish are closest in flake and both bring a collagen-rich frame. Cod is acceptable as bone-in steaks or thick chunks when whole fish is unavailable, but you lose the carcass gelatin, so build the stock harder on anchovy and kelp to compensate for the thinner body.
  • Korean radish (mu) → daikon Daikon is the standard substitute and the same species register; Korean mu is a touch denser and sweeter and holds the cube shape slightly better through the long simmer, but daikon is functionally interchangeable here.
  • crown daisy (ssukgat) → watercress, or a mix of watercress and chrysanthemum greens Crown daisy is the canonical herbal crown, grassy and faintly bitter; watercress is the easiest substitute and brings a similar peppery-green lift. Both go in off heat so they wilt without losing their aroma. Spinach is a last resort, it lacks the bitter-aromatic edge that defines the finish.
  • dried anchovies for stock → extra kombu plus a splash of fish sauce, or a packet of dashi The anchovy-kelp stock is the umami base. If you have no stock anchovies, double the kombu for a stronger kelp glutamate and add 1 tbsp fish sauce for the savory-fish front; a quick katsuobushi-kombu dashi also works and reads slightly cleaner and more Japanese.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → gochujang (Korean chili paste) make it yourselfstub A scratch gochujang of gochugaru, fermented soybean powder (meju), glutinous rice, and barley malt develops a depth and clean sweetness that mass-market tubs flatten with corn syrup; in a stew where the paste is a primary flavor, the difference reads clearly.

Notes

Why the whole carcass, not fillets. Maeuntang is engineered around the parts most cooks throw away. The head and spine of a black sea bass are dense in collagen; under the 15-to-25-minute simmer that collagen hydrolyzes to gelatin, and the gelatin is what gives the broth its body, the faint set you see when the leftovers chill. Build the same stew on lean skinless fillet and the broth comes out thin and watery no matter how much paste you add. Sectioning a whole fish through the bone and including the head is therefore both the flavor play and the value play: you pay for one fish and the cheapest part does the most work.

The umami architecture. The savory depth comes from three stacked glutamate sources: the anchovy-kelp stock (inosinate from the dried anchovies, glutamate from the kombu, the two synergize for an umami far greater than either alone), the doenjang (a fermented-soybean glutamate paste, and the ingredient that specifically tames the fishy notes), and the fish bones themselves (free glutamate and gelatin released into the simmer). The gochujang adds a fourth, fermented-and-sweet layer plus body. This is why the stew tastes complete without any meat stock; the umami is built from the sea and the ferment, not from chicken or beef.

The brightness and finishing register. A stew this deep and fiery needs a counter, and maeuntang supplies it at the end rather than the start: the late-added fresh green chili and scallion bring sharp, volatile heat and allium lift that have not had time to stew flat, and the off-heat crown of crown daisy or watercress lands a raw, grassy, faintly bitter top note over the rich broth. Some households also finish with a small splash of acid, a squeeze of lemon or a few drops of rice vinegar at the table, which cuts the richness further; it is optional and not canonical but it works the same way the late chili does, resetting the palate between spoonfuls.

Why radish first, fish last. The radish needs 12 to 15 minutes to turn sweet and translucent and to give its sweetness up to the broth, and that long simmer is also where the gochugaru and doenjang shed their raw edge. The fish needs only 6 to 10 minutes and is fragile. Cook them together and you get crunchy radish and shredded, overcooked fish. The radius of error on the fish is small: pull it the moment the flesh turns opaque and lifts from the bone, around 55 to 58 °C, and hold the pot at a simmer rather than a hard boil, since a rolling boil tears the lean flake apart and clouds the broth with expelled protein.

Sourcing and handling the fish. Buy the fish whole and as fresh as possible, on the ice bed at H Mart or a coastal counter: clear domed eyes, bright red gills, tight scales, firm flesh, clean ocean smell. Ask them to scale and gut it but keep the head and frame on. At home, scrape the blood line from the spine cavity and rinse well; the blood line and any gut remnants are the main sources of off-fishiness, and a clean cavity plus the doenjang and ginger in the broth give a stew that reads deep and savory rather than fishy. A 700 to 900 g fish is right for 3 to 4 people; scale the radish and tofu with the pot.

Serving and leftovers. Maeuntang is a shared-pot dish: it comes to the table in the cooking pot, often kept at a low flame, and each diner ladles broth, a piece of fish, tofu, and radish into a bowl and eats it over or alongside a personal bowl of short-grain rice. Eating around the bones is expected; the cheek and collar meat are prized. Leftovers keep 2 days refrigerated and the broth sets to a soft gel from the carcass gelatin; reheat gently to a simmer, never a hard boil, and refresh with a little fresh scallion and chili.

Sources
  • Adapted from the Korean maeuntang canon — Maeuntang (매운탕, literally "spicy soup") is the Korean spicy fish stew built on a whole fish broken down to sections, head and spine included. It is the canonical fate of a fresh whole fish in Korean home cooking once the prime fillet has been claimed for hoe (raw) or gui (grilled), and it is engineered around the carcass: the collagen-dense head and frame are exactly what give the broth its gelatinous body, so the stew is also the value play on a whole fish. The seasoning is a yangnyeom of gochugaru, gochujang, doenjang, garlic, and ginger; the umami spine is anchovy-kelp stock plus doenjang plus the fish bones; the brightness comes from chili, scallion, and a final crown of crown daisy (ssukgat) or watercress. Radish simmered to sweet translucence is the structural vegetable.
