Homemade panko

Crustless white pullman bread oven-dried at low temperature, then pulsed in a food processor to the characteristic jagged flake shape that produces panko's signature crispy-fried texture. Stub.

8servingsabout 400 g of panko (enough for a dozen batches of meatballs or 6 batches of fried cutlets)
15 minactive· 1 h rest
easydifficulty
Japaneseside

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 1 item ▾
  1. 800 g crustless white pullman bread (one full loaf), crusts trimmed off all six sides of the loaf; the resulting crustless interior cut into roughly 3 cm cubes

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: trim the crusts off all six sides of the pullman loaf. Cut the crustless interior into rough 3 cm cubes. Spread on a sheet pan in a single layer.
  2. 2
    Stub: dry in a 95°C / 200°F oven for 75 to 90 minutes, stirring once at the 45-minute mark, until the bread is dry throughout but not yet browned. The cubes should snap rather than bend, with no remaining moisture in the center, this is critical, any residual moisture produces a soggy panko that goes stale within days.
  3. 3
    Stub: cool fully on the sheet pan (15 to 20 minutes). Working in batches, pulse the dried cubes in a food processor for 4 to 6 short pulses each, until the bread breaks into jagged flakes 3 to 8 mm long, not a fine powder. Sift through a coarse-mesh strainer if there is fine dust, returning the larger flakes to the storage container and discarding the dust (or reserving for breading another dish).

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: why the electrical-current-baked shokupan loaf is structurally different from supermarket pullman (the crustless interior, the uniformly soft crumb), why the low-temperature dry rather than a toast (preserves the white color and the airy structure where toasting collapses the cells and produces dense crumbs), the storage behavior (sealed glass jar at room temp for 2 months, freezer for a year), and the comparison to American-style dry breadcrumbs (which are made by toasting and grinding to a uniform sand, a different product). The same loaf, dried at the same temperature but pulsed finer, produces what some Italian-American kitchens call "Italian breadcrumbs", usable but missing panko's structural crispness. The technique scales linearly; double the loaf for 800 g of panko, halve for 200 g. The dried cubes can be stored before pulsing if you want to grind only what you need at a time.
