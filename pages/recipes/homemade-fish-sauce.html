Fish sauce (homemade Vietnamese-style)

Anchovies layered with sea salt at a 1:3 ratio, sealed in a glass or ceramic vat, and left to ferment in a warm dark place for 12 to 18 months. The endogenous fish enzymes hydrolyze the proteins into the amber liquid that defines Southeast Asian cooking. Stub — a months-long project, not a weekend recipe.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 2 items ▾

Spices & dried herbs 1

  1. 500 g unrefined sea salt (no iodine, no anti-caking agents)

Other 1

  1. 1500 g whole fresh anchovies (or other small oily fish — sardines, herring, smelt)

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: rinse the anchovies briefly under cold water (do not gut). Layer fish and salt in alternating layers in a 2 L glass jar or food-grade ceramic crock: a layer of fish, sprinkle salt, layer of fish, more salt, until all fish and salt are used. End with a salt layer.
  2. 2
    Stub: weight the fish down with a clean glass disc or plastic-bag-of-water to keep them submerged in the brine that will form. Cover the jar with a tight lid and place in a warm dark spot (ideal: 25 to 30°C / 77 to 86°F). The fish will release liquid in the first few days and the salt will dissolve into a saturated brine.
  3. 3
    Stub: let ferment 12 to 18 months. Do not open the jar for the first 6 months; opening introduces oxygen and contamination. After 6 months, you can carefully open and check progress; the liquid should be clear amber, the fish should have largely dissolved, and the smell should be intensely fish-sauce-like. Continue fermenting until the liquid is clear amber-brown and tastes structurally savory with no raw-fish off-notes.
  4. 4
    Stub: when ready, strain through cheesecloth into clean glass bottles. Seal and store at room temperature; the fish sauce will continue to age and develop. Use within 2 to 3 years for peak flavor.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the fish-selection logic (which anchovy species ferment best, why oily fish are essential, what happens when you try to make fish sauce from white fish), the salt science (why iodized salt fails, why 1:3 is the structural ratio — too little salt and the fish putrefies, too much and the enzymes are inhibited), the temperature window (why 25 to 30°C is the sweet spot — too cool and fermentation stalls, too warm and putrefactive bacteria outcompete the desired enzymatic hydrolysis), the aging behavior (the difference between 12-month, 18-month, and 24-month sauces), the safety considerations (botulism risk in unsalted ferments — the high salt of fish sauce is what makes it safe; do not improvise lower-salt versions), the regional variations (Vietnamese nuoc mam, Thai nam pla, Korean aekjeot, Filipino patis — different fish, different temperatures, different aging windows), and the practical note that a fully-fermented quality store-bought fish sauce (Red Boat 40°N) is genuinely excellent and the homemade project is more about the experience than the kitchen need. The stub above is correct in outline; the deeper drafting pass should add the chemistry and the safety and the cultural context.
