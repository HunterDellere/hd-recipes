Fresh soy milk (homemade, from soaked soybeans)

Soaked soybeans blended with water, cooked at low simmer, strained through cheesecloth — the foundational base for fresh douhua, hot-set silken tofu, and any application where the soy milk itself is the dominant flavor. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 3 items ▾
  1. 250 g dry soybeans (yellow, food-grade)
  2. 1500 ml cold water (for blending)
  3. 1 g pinch of salt

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: drain the soaked soybeans (they should have roughly doubled in size and the skins should slip off easily when pinched). Rinse under cold water.
  2. 2
    Stub: blend the drained beans with the 1500 ml / 6 cups cold water in a high-powered blender for 90 seconds, until the mixture is smooth and creamy with no visible bean fragments.
  3. 3
    Stub: pour the blended mixture into a heavy-bottomed pot. Bring to a simmer over medium heat, stirring constantly with a wooden spoon to prevent scorching at the bottom (raw soy milk scorches readily). Once simmering, reduce heat and cook 20 minutes, stirring occasionally; the cooking eliminates the raw "beany" enzymes (especially trypsin inhibitor) and develops the characteristic flavor.
  4. 4
    Stub: line a fine-mesh sieve with two layers of cheesecloth set over a clean pot. Pour the cooked mixture through the cheesecloth. Twist the cheesecloth and squeeze (carefully — it is very hot) to extract every last drop of milk. The leftover pulp (okara) is itself an ingredient and can be used in baking or as a vegetable stir-fry base; do not discard.
  5. 5
    Stub: stir in the pinch of salt if using. Use immediately for hot-set silken tofu (recipes/mapo-tofu-poured-set, sweet douhua) or cool to room temperature and refrigerate for use within 3 days. The fresh soy milk has a noticeably more savory, more bean-forward flavor than commercial soy milk and produces a fuller-bodied curd.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the soak-time logic (8 hours minimum for cold-water soak, 4 hours acceptable for warm-water soak, why over 24 hours leads to fermentation), the role of the cooking step in deactivating trypsin inhibitor (food safety, why raw soy milk is mildly toxic), why salt timing matters (early salt slightly thins the milk; late salt is invisible), the okara byproduct (soybean pulp, used in Korean kongbiji jjigae and Japanese unohana), the difference between Asian-grocery yellow soybeans (smaller, sweeter, the traditional choice for douhua) and supermarket organic soybeans (larger, more variable), the ratio of bean-to-water for different protein-density needs (1:6 for douhua, 1:8 for drinking, 1:10 for thin soup), and the storage and shelf-life behavior. The stub recipe above is enough to make working soy milk; the deeper drafting pass should add the chemistry and the cultural context.
