Maple-roasted pears with vanilla cream

Halved pears roasted cut-side down in maple and butter until the sugars caramelize and the flesh goes silky, deglazed into a spiced pan syrup, and served warm over a lightly whipped vanilla cream sweetened with honey. No cane sugar, and a warm-against-cold contrast that makes a panna cotta feel like a full dessert course.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Choose pears that are ripe but still firm (Bosc holds its shape best, Anjou is fine). A soft, fully ripe pear collapses to mush in the oven; an underripe one stays hard and grainy. It should yield slightly at the neck when pressed.
  • The pears can roast ahead and be served warm or at room temperature; the cream can be whipped up to 2 hours ahead and held in the fridge. Both can be made before dinner and assembled at dessert.
  • If serving alongside a panna cotta, roast the pears while the panna cotta sets so the warm fruit meets the cold custard at the table.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Pears

  1. 4 each firm-ripe pears, halved lengthwise, cored with a melon baller or teaspoon, stem left on one half for looks — (Bosc or Anjou; ripe but not soft)
  2. 30 g unsalted butter
  3. 80 g maple syrup, Grade A dark
  4. 5 g vanilla bean paste
  5. 1 each cinnamon stick, whole
  6. 1 each lemon, about half is juiced; you need only about 1 tbsp / 15 ml — (the finishing acid that keeps the syrup from cloying)
  7. 1 g fine sea salt

Vanilla cream

  1. 240 ml heavy cream, cold
  2. 20 g honey

Finish

  1. 30 g walnuts or pecans, toasted and roughly chopped

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the oven to 200 °C (400 °F). Halve the 4 pears lengthwise and scoop out the cores with a melon baller or teaspoon, leaving a clean hollow; leave the stem on one half of each pear for presentation. Rub all the cut faces with about 15 ml (1 tbsp) juice from the lemon to keep them from browning while you set up; you will use only about half the lemon, so reserve the rest for the syrup.
  2. 2
    Build the roasting pan. Use a wide 30 cm / 12-inch oven-safe skillet or a large baking dish, big enough for all 8 pear halves to sit flat cut-side down in a single layer without overlapping; if yours is smaller, roast in two batches or across two pans, because crowded pears steam instead of caramelizing. Melt the 30 g / 2 tbsp butter in the pan, then stir in the 80 g / ¼ cup maple syrup, half the 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste (reserve the rest for the cream), the 1 cinnamon stick, and the 1 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt. Warm on the stovetop just until it bubbles and smells of caramel, about 1 minute.
  3. 3
    Roast cut-side down. Lay the pear halves cut-side down in the maple syrup mixture, spooning a little of the 30 g / 2 tbsp butter and syrup over the rounded backs. Roast 20 to 25 minutes; at the 12-minute mark, baste the cut faces with the pan syrup (tilt the pan and spoon it back over) so they stay glazed and do not dry-scorch at the edges. The pears are done when the cut faces are burnished and glazed a deep amber and a knife slides into the thickest part with no resistance. Roasting cut-side down puts the flat face in direct contact with the hot pan syrup, which is where the browning happens; maple's lower caramelization threshold means that face colors well at this temperature. If the pears are very firm, give them the full 25 minutes.
  4. 4
    Whip the cream. While the pears roast, whip the 240 ml / 1 cup cold heavy cream with the 20 g / 1 tbsp honey and the reserved vanilla bean paste to soft, floppy peaks, the cream should mound on a spoon but still slump and pour, not stand stiff. Soft peaks are deliberate: this is a sauce-like cream to pool the warm pears into, not a piped topping. Hold in the fridge.
  5. 5
    Reduce the pan syrup. Lift the roasted pears onto serving plates or into shallow bowls, cut-side up. Set the skillet over medium heat and let the pan juices bubble and reduce 1 to 2 minutes to a glossy, pourable syrup; if it has tightened too far, loosen with a teaspoon of water. Lift out the 1 cinnamon stick and discard it. Taste and add a squeeze more lemon if it reads flat.
  6. 6
    Assemble. Spoon a pool of the soft vanilla cream beside or under each warm pear half, drizzle the reduced maple syrup over the pears and cream, and scatter the 30 g / ¼ cup toasted chopped walnuts or pecans. Serve warm, the contrast of the hot caramelized pear against the cold cream is the whole point. On its own, plate two halves per person; alongside a panna cotta, plate one half per person and pass the rest.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture flavor→maple-roasted apples

    Swap the pears for firm apples (Honeycrisp, Braeburn), halved and cored the same way. Apples hold more structure and need 25 to 30 minutes; add a pinch of grated nutmeg to the pan. The dish reads more autumnal, closer to a deconstructed baked apple.

  • Occasion occasion→a fuller plated dessert with crumble

    Scatter a cane-free oat crumble over the pears for the last 10 minutes of roasting: rub 40 g oats, 30 g flour, 30 g cold butter, 1 tbsp maple syrup, and a pinch of salt into clumps. Now it eats like an individual pear crumble with the cream as the sauce.

  • Occasion flavor→boozy, with a splash of spirit in the syrup

    Off the heat, stir 1 tbsp / 15 ml bourbon, dark rum, or Poire Williams into the reduced pan syrup. The alcohol softens as it hits the hot syrup and leaves an aromatic warmth; do not add it before roasting or it cooks off entirely.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • maple syrup, Grade A dark → honey, 70 g / 3 tbsp Also cane-free. Honey caramelizes faster still, so watch the pears from minute 18 and pull before the syrup scorches. The flavor turns floral rather than caramel-woodsy. See ingredients/honey.
  • heavy cream (whipped) → Greek yogurt or crème fraîche, loosened with a little milk For a tangier, lighter accompaniment. Sweeten with the same 1 tbsp honey. Do not whip; just stir smooth and spoonable.
  • firm-ripe pears → firm peaches or nectarines, halved and pitted In summer, stone fruit roasts the same way but faster, 15 to 18 minutes. Skin-on is fine; the skin slips off after roasting if you prefer.

Notes

Why roast cut-side down. The flat cut face of the pear, pressed into the hot maple butter, is where caramelization happens: direct contact between the pear''s own sugars, the maple, and the hot pan drives the browning that gives the dish its depth. Roasting the pears rounded-side down, or standing them up, wastes that contact and leaves the fruit merely soft and sweet rather than caramelized. Turn them cut-side up only after they come out, to show the burnished face.

Why maple in the pan, honey in the cream. The two sweeteners are doing different jobs. In the roasting pan, maple''s lower caramelization threshold and its caramel-woodsy flavor are exactly what you want browning against the pear. In the cold cream, a mild honey dissolves cleanly and adds a floral lift without competing; maple in the cream would muddy it. Splitting them plays each to its strength.

Why soft-peak cream. This cream is a sauce, not a topping. Whipped only to soft, slumping peaks, it pools around the warm pear and the maple syrup runs into it, so every spoonful is warm fruit, cold cream, and syrup at once. Whip it stiff and it sits as a separate dollop; the dessert loses its cohesion.

Make-ahead. The pears can roast several hours ahead and be gently rewarmed, or served at room temperature; the syrup reheats with a splash of water. Whip the cream up to 2 hours ahead and hold it cold. Add the toasted nuts only at the plate so they stay crisp. This makes it an easy dinner-party dessert: everything is done before guests arrive and assembly takes two minutes.
