Recipe

Thai pork larb meatballs

A meatball reformatting of larb moo, the canonical northeastern Thai (Isan) and Lao minced-pork salad. The classic larb is a loose minced-meat preparation eaten with sticky rice and fresh herbs; here the same flavor system, fish sauce, lime, toasted rice powder, fresh herbs, dried chili, shallot, is rolled into bite-sized meatballs, browned hard in a skillet to maximize the toasted-meat flavor that anchors the dish, then tossed off-heat with the larb dressing and a heap of mint, cilantro, and scallion. The result eats like larb in cocktail-meatball form: high-acid, fish-sauce-forward, fresh-herb-heavy, with the toasted rice powder providing the structural toasted character and a faint sandy texture on the meatball surface.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Sticky rice is the partner, it needs to start FIRST. Soak 200 g / 1 cup glutinous rice in cold water at least 4 hours ahead (overnight is better). When you begin the meatballs, drain the soaked rice and put it on to steam (recipe thai-sticky-rice). A 30-minute steam runs in parallel with the rice powder toast, meatball mix, and sear, finishing within a few minutes of the meatballs hitting the dressing.
  • If you forgot the overnight soak: quick-soak the rice 1 hour in hot tap water before steaming. The texture is slightly less even but workable. Plain jasmine rice is the wrong substitute, the meal needs the sticky-rice mouthfeel that picks up dressing.
  • Pull the herbs (mint, cilantro, scallion) out of the fridge and wash them before anything else. They need to be dry and at room temperature when they hit the warm meatballs in the final toss; cold wet herbs drop the dish temperature and dilute the dressing.
  • Toast the raw glutinous rice for the powder in the first 10 minutes of cooking, it needs to cool fully before grinding, which takes about 15 minutes. Toasting and cooling can happen while you mince the shallots and mix the meatballs.

Execution

  1. 1
    Toast the rice powder. Place the 30 g / 3 tbsp glutinous rice in a heavy dry skillet (cast iron, carbon steel, or stainless, not nonstick, which masks the color cues) over medium heat. Add the 2 makrut lime leaves (optional). Stir continuously with a wooden spoon for 10 to 14 minutes. The rice will go through three phases: first the residual moisture cooks off (the rice stays chalky white for 4 to 5 minutes), then the rice begins to color (pale gold to medium amber over 4 to 5 minutes), and finally the rice reaches deep amber with a strong nutty popcorn-adjacent aroma (the last 2 to 4 minutes). Pull immediately at the deep-amber peak; the rice will continue to darken in residual pan heat. Spread on a plate to cool fully (5 to 10 minutes) before grinding, hot rice gums up in the grinder. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  2. 2
    Grind the cooled rice. Transfer the 30 g / 3 tbsp toasted rice (and the cooled lime leaves, if used) to a mortar and pestle and pound for 60 to 90 seconds, or pulse in a spice grinder or dedicated coffee grinder for 5 to 10 seconds. The right grind is coarse-sand, you should see individual grain fragments, and no single grain should feel gritty between the teeth, but the texture should be discernibly textural rather than powder-fine. Set aside.
  3. 3
    Mix the meatballs. While the rice cools, do the meatball mise: mince the 40 g shallots (for the meatball), microplane the 8 g garlic, mince the 8 g lemongrass inner core. In a wide bowl, combine the 500 g ground pork, the minced shallot, the microplaned garlic, the minced lemongrass, the 10 g / 2 tsp fish sauce, the 2 g / 1 tsp white pepper, and the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt. Mix with hands or a sturdy silicone spatula for 60 to 90 seconds, long enough to fully incorporate everything and to extract the myosin (the mixture turns from loose-and-shaggy to slightly-sticky-and-cohesive), short enough not to over-work. The end-point cue: a small pinched bit holds together when squeezed.
  4. 4
    Shape the meatballs. With damp hands, portion the mixture into 20 small meatballs of about 25 g each, a 3 cm diameter, smaller than the Italian meatballs above, sized for one-bite eating in a larb-style preparation. Roll each between damp palms with light pressure. Lay on a plate as you go. The smaller size is structural for this dish, large meatballs read as a different dish entirely, the bite-sized format is what makes this "larb in meatball form" rather than "Thai meatballs."
  5. 5
    Sear the meatballs. Heat the 20 g / 1.5 tbsp neutral oil in a 30 cm / 12-inch heavy skillet (or a wok) over medium-high heat until shimmering. Add the meatballs in a single layer with space between them. Sear undisturbed for 90 seconds on the first side, then turn each with tongs to expose a fresh face and sear 60 seconds; turn again, 60 seconds. After 3 faces are deeply browned, add a splash of water (about 30 g / 2 tbsp) to the pan, cover with a lid, and steam for 60 seconds, this finishes the interior cooking through to the safe-temperature 71°C / 160°F without over-drying the surface. Pull the lid, let any remaining water evaporate (30 seconds), and transfer the meatballs to a wide bowl. Maillard sear
  6. 6
    Mix the larb dressing. In a small bowl, whisk together the 25 g / 1.5 tbsp fish sauce, the 30 g / 2 tbsp lime juice, the 6 g / 1 tsp palm sugar (mash with the back of a spoon if the sugar is solid; it should dissolve fully into the dressing), and the 3 g / 1½ tsp Thai dried chili flakes. Taste and adjust, the dressing should read aggressively salty, aggressively sour, with a back-of-throat heat. If the dressing reads flat, add more lime; if it reads thin, add more fish sauce; if it reads cloying, reduce the sugar. This dressing is the most important seasoning step in the dish; spend the 30 seconds to taste and adjust.
  7. 7
    Toss everything together. To the bowl with the still-warm meatballs, add the dressing, the 30 g shallot rings, the 30 g toasted rice powder (almost all of it; reserve 5 g / 1 tsp for the garnish), the 15 g mint leaves, the 15 g chopped cilantro, the 6 g sliced culantro (optional), and the 30 g sliced scallions. Toss gently with two spoons or with damp hands until everything is uniformly coated. The toss should happen while the meatballs are still warm (40 to 50°C surface), the warmth helps the dressing carry into the meatball surface, where cold meatballs would just sit in pooled dressing.
  8. 8
    Plate and serve. Transfer to a wide shallow platter or a large flat bowl. Scatter the reserved 5 g / 1 tsp toasted rice powder over the top for the visual texture. Serve immediately with the 400 g / 2 cups warm sticky rice (in its traditional bamboo basket if you have one, or in a small bowl), and the 200 g / mixed plate of raw vegetables (cabbage leaves, cucumber spears, long beans, lettuce). The eating method: pinch off a small ball of sticky rice with your fingers, dip or roll it lightly through the larb (picking up some sauce, some herbs, maybe a meatball), and eat. Alternatively, wrap a meatball-with-sauce-and-herbs in a cabbage or lettuce leaf and eat as a hand-roll. The dish is communal, a table of 4 shares the platter, dipping into the same dish.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→larb gai (chicken larb) or larb neua (beef larb)

    Substitute 500 g ground chicken thigh for the pork (larb gai is one of the most common Thai restaurant preparations); the dish reads slightly lighter and the dressing carries similarly. For larb neua, use 500 g ground beef chuck (80/20); the dish reads heavier and more aggressive in flavor, sear hot and fast to get a strong Maillard crust on the beef. Both substitute 1:1 by weight; the rest of the recipe is identical.

  • Texture serving form→traditional loose larb (not meatballs)

    Skip the shape-and-sear meatball process entirely. Heat 20 g / 1½ tbsp neutral oil in a wok or heavy skillet over medium-high heat. Add 8 g / 2 tsp lemongrass mince and 8 g / 2 cloves microplaned garlic, fry 30 seconds. Add the 500 g ground pork (no panade, no shaping), break it up with a wooden spoon and stir-fry for 4 to 6 minutes until the moisture has cooked off and the meat is in fine cooked granules with light browning. Add the 10 g / 2 tsp fish sauce in the pan, toss, then pull the wok off heat. Add the dressing, shallot, rice powder, and herbs as in step 7. Serve. This is the canonical Lao-Thai loose larb, the meatball version is the variation, not the original.

  • Heat heat level→mild version for kids

    Reduce the Thai chili flakes to 1 g / ½ tsp in the dressing, omit entirely if the audience is heat-averse. The dish still reads as larb but loses the back-of-throat heat that defines the canonical version. Some Thai cooks finish with a small bowl of additional chili flakes on the side for individual heat adjustment; this is the right move for mixed-heat-tolerance audiences.

  • Regional cuisine direction→Vietnamese-leaning bún chả-style

    Skip the toasted rice powder entirely. Reduce the lime in the dressing to 20 g / 4 tsp and add 30 g / 2 tbsp rice vinegar plus 15 g / 1 tbsp additional sugar. Serve over cold rice vermicelli noodles in a wide bowl, with the same herbs and raw vegetables and an additional handful of bean sprouts and Thai basil leaves. The dish moves from northeastern Thai larb toward central-Vietnamese bún chả; both are Tai-Kadai-and-Vietnamese-adjacent traditions, and the conversion is structurally simple.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • ground pork (75/25 shoulder) → ground chicken thigh, ground turkey thigh, ground beef chuck (80/20), or finely chopped firm tofu Chicken thigh is the most common variation (larb gai); turkey thigh is the supermarket substitute; beef chuck makes larb neua; firm tofu makes a vegan version that takes the dressing well but lacks the protein chew. All substitute 1:1 by weight.
  • glutinous (sticky) rice → jasmine rice (toasted similarly), or commercial pre-toasted rice powder Jasmine rice toasted and ground works at 1:1 with a slightly less chewy character and slightly more delicate aroma, the dish is recognizable but loses a small percentage of its body. Commercial pre-toasted rice powder (Cock Brand, Lobo, or any Thai grocery brand) substitutes 1:1 but has lost most aromatic complexity in storage; use 1.2x the weight to compensate for the lost flavor intensity.
  • makrut lime leaves → lime zest (microplane-grated) or omit Lime zest in the toasting pan produces a similar but less complex citrus-aromatic; use the zest of half a lime. Skipping entirely is acceptable, the lime leaves are an upgrade, not a structural requirement.
  • lemongrass → omit; or use a small piece of lemongrass paste from a tube (significantly weaker) Fresh lemongrass is structural, there is no clean substitute for the citrus-piney aromatic. Lemongrass paste in a tube (Gourmet Garden brand etc.) is significantly weaker; use 3x the volume (24 g / 5 tsp) and accept a meaningful flavor loss. Dried lemongrass is essentially useless. If fresh is unavailable, omit and accept a less aromatic but still recognizable larb.
  • palm sugar → light brown sugar, dark brown sugar, or coconut sugar Light brown sugar is the closest substitute and is what most home cooks use without palm sugar on hand; substitute 1:1. Coconut sugar has a similar caramelly character. Dark brown sugar is too molasses-forward for this application and reads off. Granulated sugar substitutes structurally but produces a flatter, less complex dressing.
  • Thai dried red chili flakes (prik bon) → red pepper flakes plus a pinch of cayenne, or fresh Thai chili (chopped) 1 tsp red pepper flakes plus ½ tsp cayenne approximates the Thai dried flake heat and color. Fresh Thai chili (3 to 5 small chilis, finely chopped) is structurally different but is a defensible Thai-canonical substitute, the dish reads fresher and brighter, slightly less roast-chili-deep.
  • sawtooth coriander (culantro) → extra cilantro plus a pinch of fresh oregano Culantro is the canonical Thai-Lao herb and is not widely available outside Asian and Latin American groceries. Cilantro alone is the standard substitute and is fine; adding a pinch (½ tsp / 1 g) of fresh oregano leaves approximates the slightly bitter, more aggressive character that culantro contributes.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → steamed sticky rice make it yourselfstub Sticky rice cooked the traditional Lao way (soaked overnight, then steamed in a bamboo basket over boiling water for 25 minutes) is dramatically better than any rice-cooker shortcut. The right rice is glutinous rice from a Thai or Lao grocery; the right tool is a bamboo basket and the bottom of a pot of boiling water; the right technique is to soak overnight. The result is a sticky-but-not-mushy rice that holds shape when pinched in the fingers and is the structural accompaniment to larb. A future drafting session should write this recipe out fully, it's a single technique used across many Lao-Thai dishes.

Notes

Why the meatball reformat works. The canonical Lao-Thai larb is a loose, minced preparation, the meat is finely chopped or coarsely ground and stir-fried briefly in its own fat, then tossed with the dressing while still warm. The eating experience is loose-meat-and-dressing scooped onto sticky rice. The meatball reformat preserves the entire flavor system (fish sauce, lime, toasted rice powder, fresh herbs, chili, shallot) but changes the form factor to a discrete bite-sized meatball. This works because larb''s flavor structure doesn''t depend on the loose-meat texture; the dressing carries on any meat surface, and the toasted rice powder grips the meatball just as well as it grips loose minced meat. The reformat is useful in three contexts: (1) party food, where loose larb is hard to serve cleanly and meatballs solve that, (2) bento-style packed lunches where a meatball stays discrete in a container, and (3) introducing larb to eaters unfamiliar with the dish, where the meatball form is more visually familiar than the loose-meat form. Note that this is not a traditional Lao or Thai preparation; an Isan or Vientiane cook would recognize the flavors but find the form unfamiliar.

The toasted rice powder is non-negotiable. Without khao khua, the dish reads as "ground pork with fish sauce and herbs", perfectly tasty but ambiguous in cuisine. With khao khua, the dish reads immediately as larb to anyone familiar with Lao or Thai cooking. The toasted, nutty, slightly bitter character of the powder is what anchors the flavor in the northeastern Thai-Lao tradition, and the slight sandy texture on the meatball surface is part of the eating experience. Do not substitute pre-ground rice flour for toasted rice powder, they are not the same thing, and the pre-ground rice flour produces a dish that reads pasty rather than toasted. The 12 to 14 minutes of dry-toasting is the structural step; budget the time.

Why the dressing reads aggressive. Larb dressing, fish sauce, lime juice, sugar, chili, is one of the most aggressively-flavored dressings in any global cuisine. Tasted on its own, it reads as too salty, too sour, too pungent. Tossed with the meatballs, fresh herbs, and rice powder, the same dressing reads as perfectly balanced; the meat absorbs the salt, the herbs absorb the acid, the rice powder absorbs the heat-and-fish-sauce volatiles, and the integrated dish reads complete. This is the structural design of many Southeast Asian dressings (Vietnamese nuoc cham, Thai nam pla prik, Cambodian tuk trey); the dressing is calibrated to be too much for the dressing on its own but exactly right for the dish it''s meant to season. Trust the numbers, 25 g fish sauce and 30 g lime juice for 500 g of pork is the canonical ratio and the dressing will read correct on the finished dish even if it tastes alarming in the small bowl.

The fresh-herb mountain is structural. A larb without a large quantity of fresh herbs is not larb; the herbs are not garnish, they are a structural component of the dish. The 15 g mint, 15 g cilantro, and 30 g scallion (plus 6 g optional culantro) for 500 g of pork is roughly 13% of the dish weight as fresh herbs, which would be unusual in most Western preparations and is canonical for larb. The herbs contribute (1) the fresh, bright top-note that contrasts with the toasted-rice and roasted-meat bottom notes, (2) the menthol-cooling effect of mint that contrasts with the chili heat, (3) the umami of cilantro and culantro stems that compounds the fish-sauce umami, and (4) the visual contrast of green-flecked white-and-brown meat that signals "this is a Southeast Asian salad dish, not a meatball dish." Skimping on the herbs is the single most common home-cook mistake on larb; double the herbs if you are unsure.

Why white pepper, not black. White pepper is the canonical Southeast Asian and Chinese pepper for pork. It is more aromatic and less sharp than black pepper, with a slightly fermented-funky character (the white pepper berry is the same plant as black pepper, but with the dark outer husk fermented and removed before drying). For larb specifically, white pepper compounds the fish-sauce aromatic; black pepper would compete with it. The difference is meaningful, a larb with black pepper reads slightly off, even if a Western diner can''t articulate why.

The sticky-rice question. Larb is eaten with sticky rice in northeastern Thailand and Laos; that is not negotiable for cooks who want the canonical experience. Sticky rice is glutinous rice (the same rice toasted for khao khua) soaked overnight and steamed in a bamboo basket, producing a dense, chewy, finger-eaten rice that is rolled into balls and used as the scooping utensil. Long-grain jasmine rice is the central-Thailand substitute and is what most American Thai restaurants serve; it works but loses the structural "rice as utensil" element. Western diners often serve larb over a bed of jasmine rice as if it were a topping; this is a US-restaurant adaptation, not a Lao-Thai preparation. The right service is larb on one plate, sticky rice in a separate basket, raw vegetables on a third plate, and the diner assembles each bite at the table.

The salt math (why the meatball is under-salted on its own). The 4 g / 1 tsp salt in the meatball plus the 10 g / 2 tsp fish sauce (which is about 25% sodium chloride by weight) puts the meatball''s internal salt at about 1.2%, which would read under-seasoned if eaten alone. The remaining seasoning comes from the dressing toss at the end, which delivers an additional 25 g / 1.5 tbsp fish sauce and the dressing carries onto every meatball surface. The integrated salt is correct for the dish; do not over-salt the meatball at the mixing stage or the final dish will read aggressive.

Why pork shoulder, not lean. Larb traditionally uses pork shoulder because the rendered fat carries the dressing onto the meat surface and produces the slightly slick, dressing-coated character of the canonical dish. Lean ground pork (90/10 loin) makes a dish that reads dry and the dressing pools at the bottom of the bowl rather than coating the meat. Bone-in pork shoulder finely chopped by hand (the Old World Lao technique) is the upgrade for cooks who want the most authentic version, the hand-chopped texture is structurally different from machine-ground, with more discernible meat-grain rather than uniform paste. For most cooks, supermarket ground pork shoulder is the right pick.

Make-ahead and leftovers. The meatballs can be shaped and refrigerated up to 12 hours before searing; the seasonings continue to penetrate during the rest and the meatballs may eat slightly better the next day. The toasted rice powder can be made up to a week in advance and stored airtight at room temperature (it loses aromatic intensity over time but stays usable). The dressing can be mixed up to 4 hours in advance. The full dressed dish does not hold well, the herbs wilt within 30 minutes of dressing, and the toasted rice powder absorbs the dressing and turns pasty within an hour. Cook only what you''ll eat in the next 30 to 45 minutes, and store any leftover meatballs separately from the dressing for next-day repurposing.

Sources
  • Adapted from northeastern Thai (Isan) larb moo and Lao laab moo — Larb (Thai spelling, ลาบ) and laab (Lao spelling, ລາບ) are the same dish from the same culinary lineage, the canonical minced-meat salad of the Tai-Kadai-speaking peoples of northeastern Thailand and Laos. The dish is the national dish of Laos and one of the signature dishes of Isan (the northeastern Thai region that shares language, kinship, and culinary tradition with lowland Laos). The structural elements are: a minced or finely chopped meat (pork is the most common but chicken, beef, duck, fish, and even raw beef larb dip exist in the regional canon), a dressing of fish sauce and lime juice with dried chili and sometimes a touch of palm sugar, toasted rice powder (khao khua) for the toasted character and dressing body, fresh herbs (mint, cilantro, sawtooth coriander/culantro, scallion), and shallots. The dish is served with sticky rice (khao niao) and a plate of raw vegetables (cabbage, green beans, cucumber, long beans) for scooping. The meatball reformat here is not a traditional Thai or Lao preparation, but the flavor system is fully canonical and the dish reads as recognizable larb to anyone who has eaten the original. The reformat is structurally similar to Thai-restaurant moo ping (grilled pork skewers) and Lao sai oua (grilled sausage), where the same flavor system is applied to a meat-on-a-stick form.
