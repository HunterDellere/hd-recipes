Lamb tagine with prunes and almonds

The Moroccan sweet-savory tagine: cubed lamb shoulder slow-braised in a ras el hanout spice base with onion, ginger, saffron, and cinnamon until the meat collapses, then finished with prunes plumped in a syrup of honey, orange-flower water, and cinnamon, and crowned with whole blanched almonds toasted golden in oil. The structural defining feature of the dish is the late addition of the sweetened prunes; the lamb braises savory first, then the prunes layer a deep fruit-forward sweetness on top in the final 30 minutes. A close relative of m’rouzia, the Moroccan celebratory tagine traditionally served at Eid al-Adha.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Bloom the saffron 15 minutes before the braise starts. Grind 0.4 g threads with a pinch of sugar, steep in 50 g hot water.
  • Serve with steamed couscous (canonical) or warm flatbread for sopping the sauce. The dish is built around a starch to absorb the fragrant, deeply spiced liquid.
  • Ras el hanout varies dramatically by brand and origin. Look for a Moroccan-sourced blend or use the homemade-style blend in this recipe (cumin, coriander, ginger, cinnamon, cardamom, paprika, turmeric, black pepper, nutmeg, allspice). Supermarket "Moroccan seasoning" is usually a poor substitute.
  • The almonds are blanched first, then fried golden in oil; this is the canonical Moroccan technique for the finishing nut. Toasted-in-the-oven almonds are an acceptable shortcut but lack the slightly puffed, more golden surface of the fried version.

Phase 1, the saffron bloom and lamb seasoning

  1. 0.4 g saffron threads, ground with a pinch of sugar in a mortar — (a large pinch)
  2. 1 g granulated sugar — (a pinch; for the mortar grind)
  3. 50 g hot water (not boiling)
  4. 1000 g lamb shoulder, boneless, trimmed of excess fat, cut into 5 cm cubes
  5. 14 g kosher salt

  6. 2 g ground coriander
  7. 2 g ground cinnamon

  12. 2 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Execution

  1. 1
    Bloom the saffron: grind the 0.4 g saffron with the 1 g sugar; add the 50 g hot water; cover and steep 15 to 20 minutes.
  2. 2
    Season the 1000 g lamb cubes with 8 g / 2 tsp of the kosher salt; let stand at room temperature 30 minutes while you prep the rest.
  3. 3
    Heat the 45 g / 3 tbsp olive oil in a heavy 5-quart Dutch oven (or tagine base) over medium-high heat. Add the lamb cubes in a single layer (in 2 batches if needed; do not crowd); brown deeply on 2 sides, 6 to 8 minutes total per batch. Transfer browned lamb to a plate.
  4. 4
    Reduce heat to medium; add the 400 g / 2 onions (sliced) to the pot with the lamb fond. Sweat 12 to 15 minutes, stirring occasionally, until fully softened and golden (not deeply caramelized; the onions cook into a soft braise base under the lamb).
  5. 5
    Add the 16 g / 4 garlic cloves and 20 g grated ginger; cook 90 seconds. Add the 3 g / 1½ tsp cumin, 2 g / 1 tsp coriander, 2 g / 1 tsp cinnamon, 1 g / ½ tsp ground ginger, 3 g / ½ tbsp turmeric, 2 g / 1 tsp paprika, 0.5 g / ¼ tsp nutmeg, and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper. Stir 30 seconds to bloom the spices.
  6. 6
    Return the lamb to the pot. Add the bloomed saffron liquid, the 2 g / 1 cinnamon stick, the 400 g / 1½ cups water, and the remaining 6 g / 1½ tsp salt. Bring to a steady simmer; cover; reduce heat to low. Braise 90 to 120 minutes, stirring every 30 minutes, until the lamb is fully fork-tender (the meat collapses under gentle spoon pressure but still holds its cube shape).
  7. 7
    While the lamb braises, make the prune syrup: in a small saucepan, combine the 250 g prunes, 300 g water, 60 g / 3 tbsp honey, and the second 2 g / 1 cinnamon stick. Bring to a simmer; cook 15 to 20 minutes, gently stirring, until the prunes are plumped and the syrup has reduced to a thin glaze that coats the prunes (about 100 g liquid remaining). Off heat, stir in the 5 g / 1 tsp orange-flower water. Set aside.
  8. 8
    Fry the almonds: heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a small skillet over medium heat. Add the 80 g blanched almonds; fry 3 to 4 minutes, tossing constantly, until deep golden brown (the color should be cashew-yellow to deep amber; pull when 30 seconds before they look done because they continue browning out of the oil). Transfer to a paper-towel-lined plate and salt very lightly with a pinch (under 1 g) of fine sea salt.
  9. 9
    When the lamb is fully tender, remove the cinnamon stick from the braise. Pour the prunes and their syrup into the lamb pot, gently folding so the prunes distribute through the sauce without crushing. Cover and simmer 10 to 15 minutes more for the flavors to integrate; the sauce will thicken to a syrupy gravy and the prunes will plump further with the lamb juices.
  10. 10
    Taste. The dish should read sweet-savory: lamb-and-spice-deep underneath, honey-prune-sweet on top, with a long warming finish from the cinnamon and the orange-flower. Adjust salt in 2 g increments.
  11. 11
    Plate: transfer to a wide shallow serving dish or directly to the tagine. Scatter the 80 g fried almonds over the top and sprinkle the 10 g sesame seeds. Serve with steamed couscous (canonical) or warm flatbread (khobz, pita) for sopping the syrupy sauce.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion m'rouzia (the holiday/preserved version)→as the concentrated m'rouzia tagine

    Double the honey (to 120 g) and braise the lamb until it shreds (an additional 30 to 45 minutes after fork-tender). Reduce the final sauce until it is dark and very thick (syrupy and almost candied). Increase the cinnamon to 4 g and add ½ tsp ground galangal. Traditionally served at Eid al-Adha and kept at room temperature for weeks (the sugar and reduction preserve the dish); the home version is best refrigerated.

  • Occasion with apricots and pistachios instead of prunes and almonds→as a spring sweet-savory tagine

    Substitute 250 g dried apricots for the prunes (same poach with honey and orange-flower water) and 80 g shelled pistachios for the almonds (toast in a dry pan 4 minutes, no oil-fry needed). The dish reads brighter, more floral, and slightly less rich; common as a Ramadan-iftar variant or a spring tagine.

  • Occasion with quince (autumn variant)→with quartered quince instead of prunes

    Substitute 4 peeled, cored, and quartered quinces (about 600 g) for the prunes. Poach the quince in the same honey-water-cinnamon syrup 25 minutes until just tender. Add to the lamb in step 9 and simmer 15 minutes to integrate. The dish becomes adjacent to a Persian khoresh-e beh and is an autumnal Moroccan home variant.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • pitted prunes → dried apricots or dried figs Dried apricots are the second most common fruit in Moroccan sweet-savory tagines; figs are less common but excellent. Both substitute structurally for prunes; the dish reads slightly less deep and slightly more bright.
  • orange-flower water → rose water Rose water is a fallback; both are structural Moroccan floral notes but orange-flower is more typical with prune-and-lamb tagines. Reduce to ½ tsp if using rose water; it is more potent.
  • whole blanched almonds → slivered almonds, toasted dry in a 180°C oven 5 minutes Slivered almonds toast faster and more evenly than blanched whole; the dish reads slightly less dramatic visually but is structurally correct. Whole blanched almonds are the canonical finish.

Notes

Why the prunes are poached separately. The structural distinction between this dish and a generic lamb-and-fruit braise is the parallel cook: the lamb braises in the savory spice base, the prunes plump in a sweet syrup, and the two are married in the final 10 to 15 minutes. Cooking the prunes from the start of the lamb braise breaks them down to mush and leaches their sweetness uniformly into the sauce, producing a one-note jammy stew. The parallel cook keeps the prunes as distinct sweet pockets (each one a concentrated burst of honey-cinnamon-orange-flower fruit) against the savory lamb-and-spice background. This dual-track structure is the structural defining feature of Moroccan sweet-savory tagines.

Why ras el hanout is built from scratch here. Commercial ras el hanout blends vary so dramatically by brand and source that the recipe cannot rely on a single can. The home-built spice mix in this recipe (cumin, coriander, cinnamon, ginger, turmeric, paprika, nutmeg, pepper) is a defensible everyday ras el hanout structure; a serious Moroccan ras el hanout might add cardamom, mace, allspice, rose petals, lavender, and 10 to 15 other components. If you have a quality jar of ras el hanout from a trusted Moroccan source (or a Middle Eastern spice merchant), substitute 15 g / 1 tbsp of the blend for the cumin-coriander-cinnamon-ginger-turmeric-paprika-nutmeg combination.

Versatility. The savory-then-sweet braise structure applies across the Moroccan tagine canon (lamb-with-quince, beef-with-pears, chicken-with-grapes-and-mint). The fried-almond finish is the canonical Moroccan crown for any sweet-savory tagine and travels onto rice pilaf, roast meats, and as a snack salted with cumin.

Pairs across Moroccan (canonical, especially the imperial cities of Fez and Marrakech where sweet-savory tagines are most developed), broader Maghreb, Andalusian (the Spanish Moorish tradition retained many of these flavor combinations), and contemporary fine-dining where Moroccan sweet-savory technique has been absorbed.

Storage. This tagine improves dramatically with a day in the refrigerator; the prune sweetness fully integrates into the lamb sauce and the spices deepen. Keeps 5 days refrigerated and freezes 2 months. Reheat gently covered with a splash of water; the almonds should be added fresh at service (they go stale and lose crunch on storage). Many Moroccan cooks make the dish a day ahead specifically for the integration; serve day-two when possible.

Sources
  • The Moroccan celebratory canon. Lamb-with-prunes tagine (often called tagine bel barkok or, in its richest form, m'rouzia) is documented across Moroccan home cookery; the dish is traditionally associated with Eid al-Adha and large family gatherings. — M'rouzia is the most concentrated form (very high honey-to-meat ratio, lamb cooked until it shreds, the dish almost a confit; intended as a preserved dish that holds for weeks at room temperature). The everyday version here is a less-concentrated sweet-savory tagine: lamb fork-tender but still in cubes, prunes plumped separately and folded in, almonds toasted and scattered on top. Both are structurally Moroccan; the m'rouzia version is the holiday extreme.
