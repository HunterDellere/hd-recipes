Soft-boiled congee egg

A 6½-minute soft-boiled egg cracked into the hot bowl just before eating: white set, yolk still molten, the heat of the porridge finishes the cook and binds yolk into broth.

4servings4 soft-boiled eggs, one per bowl of congee
9 mintotal

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Congee is the partner. The egg is engineered to be cracked into the hot bowl at the moment of eating, so the congee must be at a hard simmer when the eggs come out of the ice bath. Start the congee at least 90 minutes before the eggs; a 6½ minute soft-boil plus a 90 second ice shock is the entire egg timeline.
  • Use eggs straight from the refrigerator. The 6½ minute timing is calibrated for cold-into-boiling-water; room-temperature eggs overshoot to a fully set yolk in the same window.
  • Prepare the ice bath BEFORE the water boils. Lifting hot eggs into a not-yet-ready bath gives the carryover cook 60 extra seconds and pushes a jammy yolk into a fully set one.

Mise en place by phase 2 phases ▾

Eggs

  1. 4 each large eggs, refrigerator-cold

Cook and shock

  1. 2000 ml water (enough to cover the eggs by 2 cm)
  2. 500 g ice — (for the shock bath)

Execution

  1. 1
    Bring the 2000 ml / 8 cups water to a rolling boil in a small saucepan. Prepare an ice bath in a wide bowl: 500 g / ~3 cups ice with cold water just to cover.
  2. 2
    Lower the 4 cold eggs into the boiling water gently with a slotted spoon. Cold-into-hot is the rule: dropping cold eggs into hot water is what produces the firm-white-runny-yolk timing the recipe is calibrated for. Reduce heat to a steady but not vigorous boil and cook exactly 6 minutes 30 seconds for jammy yolks (or 6 minutes for fully runny, 7 minutes for set-jammy edges). Ice bath shock
  3. 3
    Lift the eggs out and plunge them straight into the prepared 500 g / 3-cup ice bath. Hold there 60 to 90 seconds until cool enough to handle; the rapid temperature drop arrests the carryover cook and contracts the egg from the shell, which is what makes a soft egg peel cleanly.
  4. 4
    Crack each egg gently against the rim of the empty serving bowl and slip it directly into the hot congee at the table just before eating. The cook diner stirs the yolk through the porridge as they eat: the heat of the congee finishes the cook and the yolk thickens the broth into a richer, glossier texture. Serving order matters: ladle the hot congee, then crack the egg, then eat. Sitting on top too long lets the white firm and the yolk set.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture cook level→fully poached, near-onsen texture

    Cook 5 minutes 30 seconds instead of 6:30. White is barely set, yolk fully fluid; the egg breaks down completely into the porridge as you stir. Pair with denser congees that can carry it.

  • Occasion preparation→tea-egg style (over-night marinated)

    After the ice bath, gently crack the shell all over without removing it, then steep the eggs 8 hours to overnight in cooled tea-egg brine (1 L water, 60 ml soy, 4 g black tea, 2 star anise, 1 cinnamon stick, 6 g sugar, brought to boil and cooled). Peel before serving. Soft yolk, marbled white, deep tea-soy flavor. The chinese-tea-eggs recipe covers the full method.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • large eggs → extra-large eggs Add 30 seconds to the cook (so 7 minutes for jammy). The larger the egg, the longer the heat needs to penetrate to the yolk.

Notes

Why the table-side crack matters. Congee is one of the few preparations where the egg is functionally raw at the moment of serving and finishes cooking in the bowl. A pre-peeled egg sitting on top of hot porridge for two minutes overcooks the yolk into a normal jammy state; a freshly-cracked-just-before-eating egg has a yolk that flows the moment the spoon touches it. The whole point of the soft-boiled congee egg is that the cook gets a different texture in every spoonful for the first thirty seconds: pure runny yolk, yolk-thickened porridge, plain porridge with a piece of soft white. Pre-cracking destroys that arc.

How this differs from a standard soft-boiled egg. The yolk target is the same, but the serving format is different. A standard soft-boiled egg is peeled, sliced, and eaten as a discrete object on toast or noodles. The congee egg is a structural component of the broth: the yolk is the secondary thickener and the white is the protein bite. Treat it as cooking that finishes in the bowl, not as a topping.

Failure modes. Eggs cracked before the ice bath produce stringy white tails as albumen leaks and cooks. Eggs cooked from room temperature instead of cold add about 30 to 45 seconds to the timing, so a 6:30 starts to overshoot toward fully set. Eggs over-shocked (more than 2 minutes in ice) lose enough heat that the yolk firms in the shell while peeling, defeating the technique.
