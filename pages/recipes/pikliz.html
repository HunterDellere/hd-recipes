Recipe

Pikliz

The Haitian fiery cabbage relish: thinly shredded green cabbage, carrot, onion, bell pepper, and scotch bonnet packed into a jar with vinegar, lime juice, salt, and a few peppercorns and cloves, allowed to ferment-pickle at room temperature for a day then refrigerated. The structural condiment of Haitian cooking, paired with griot, fried plantains, rice and beans, fish.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Pikliz needs 24 hours minimum at room temperature for the structural pickle to mature; the flavor reads better at 48 hours and reaches peak at 1 week refrigerated. Make at least the day before any griot or Haitian dinner.
  • Wear nitrile gloves when chopping the scotch bonnets. The capsaicin sticks to skin oils and migrates to eyes hours later.
  • Use a 1 L glass jar with a tight-fitting lid. Plastic absorbs the chili oils and tints permanently. The structural pikliz jar lives in the refrigerator and is refilled across many meals.
  • Shred the vegetables thin (2 to 3 mm) for the structural texture. A mandoline produces uniform shreds quickly; a sharp chef knife works with care. Do not use a food processor (produces uneven chunks and bruises the cabbage).

The vegetables

  1. 400 g green cabbage, core removed, very thinly shredded (2 to 3 mm) on a mandoline or with a sharp chef knife — (about ½ small head; the structural pikliz vegetable; not interchangeable with red cabbage or napa)
  2. 150 g carrots, peeled, julienned or coarsely grated — (2 medium carrots; the structural sweet-orange counterpoint)
  3. 100 g white onion, very thinly sliced (2 mm) — (½ medium onion)
  4. 100 g red bell pepper, very thinly sliced (2 mm) — (½ medium pepper; the structural sweet-pepper color and depth)
  5. 30 g scotch bonnet chilies (or habanero), stems removed, seeds in for canonical heat (wear gloves); thinly sliced — (about 4 scotch bonnets; the structural heat of pikliz; halve the chili count for a milder pikliz)
  6. 30 g shallot, thinly sliced (optional) — (1 small shallot; the supporting aromatic; some Haitian households use it, others skip)

The brine

  1. 400 g white vinegar (5% acidity)
  2. 60 g lime juice (fresh) — (juice of 2 limes; the structural Caribbean acidic brightener)
  3. 12 g fine sea salt
  4. 3 g whole black peppercorns
  5. 0.5 g whole cloves — (3 cloves; the supporting warm spice)

Execution

  1. 1
    Combine the vegetables. In a large bowl, toss together the 400 g shredded cabbage, 150 g carrots, 100 g sliced onion, 100 g sliced bell pepper, 30 g sliced scotch bonnet, and 30 g sliced shallot. Sprinkle the 12 g salt over the vegetables; toss to distribute. Let rest 10 minutes; the salt draws moisture from the cabbage and starts the structural pickle process.
  2. 2
    Pack the jar. Pack the salted vegetable mixture tightly into a clean 1 L glass jar with a tight-fitting lid (a wide-mouth Mason jar works well). Press down firmly with a clean spoon to compact the vegetables; the cabbage should release some liquid into the jar.
  3. 3
    Add the brine. Pour the 400 g white vinegar and 60 g lime juice over the vegetables in the jar; add the 3 g peppercorns and 0.5 g cloves. The vegetables should be completely submerged in the brine; if not, add additional vinegar in 20 g increments until they are covered. Tap the jar gently to release air bubbles; the structural pickle requires anaerobic conditions for the cabbage to soften and absorb the flavors.
  4. 4
    Mature at room temperature. Seal the jar tightly; leave at cool room temperature 24 hours. The structural pickle develops over this window: the cabbage softens slightly, the scotch bonnet infuses the vinegar with structural heat, the carrot and bell pepper take on the chili-and-vinegar character. Do not refrigerate during this window; the room-temperature mature is structural for the canonical Haitian pickle character.1 d 0 h
  5. 5
    Refrigerate and use. After the 24-hour room-temperature mature, transfer to the refrigerator. The pikliz is ready to use immediately; the flavor continues to mature for the first 1 to 2 weeks of refrigeration. Use as a structural condiment for griot, fried plantains, rice and beans, fried fish, fried chicken; spoon over the top or arrange in a small bowl alongside. The structural eating motion is to take a forkful of meat and a forkful of pikliz together; the acid-and-heat-and-crunch is the canonical Haitian flavor balance.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Heat heat level→as a milder pikliz (for cooks who prefer less heat)

    Reduce the scotch bonnet to 15 g (2 chilies instead of 4); seed all the chilies before slicing. The dish reads structurally Haitian and recognizably as pikliz, with the heat backbone dropped to a more accessible level. Useful adaptation for guests new to scotch bonnet.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as a Jamaican-style escovitch dressing (the structural cousin)

    Substitute the lime juice with 60 g additional white vinegar; add 5 g grated fresh ginger and 5 g whole allspice berries to the brine; reduce the cabbage to 200 g and add 200 g sliced cucumber. Mature 24 hours as written. The result is closer to Jamaican escovitch dressing (the pickled vegetables served over fried fish) and is the structural cousin to Haitian pikliz; both share the Caribbean acid-and-heat-and-cabbage architecture with regional adjustments.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • scotch bonnet chilies → habanero (same weight) Same species, comparable heat, slightly different flavor profile. The dish reads recognizably similar.
  • white vinegar (5% acidity) → apple cider vinegar or white wine vinegar White vinegar is canonical and reads structurally sharp and clean. Apple cider vinegar reads slightly softer with a fruity background; white wine vinegar reads more elegant. All work; the canonical Haitian pickle uses plain white vinegar.
  • green cabbage → no acceptable substitute for canonical pikliz Green cabbage is structural to pikliz; the dense crunch and the structural absorption of the vinegar-and-chili brine depend on the specific cabbage character. Red cabbage tints the brine purple and reads visually wrong; napa cabbage is too tender and goes mushy in the pickle; bok choy is structurally different. Use green cabbage.

Notes

Pikliz is the structural Haitian condiment and one of the most important Caribbean pickles. The function is structural: pikliz cuts the rich-fried-fat of griot, accents the starchy density of fried plantains and rice and beans, and brightens any heavy Haitian plate with a structural acid-and-heat-and-crunch contrast. Without pikliz, Haitian cuisine reads as good but heavy; with pikliz, the structural flavor balance comes into focus and the cuisine reveals its full character.

The room-temperature mature is structural. The 24-hour rest at cool room temperature allows the scotch bonnet to infuse the vinegar with structural heat, the cabbage to soften and absorb the flavors, and the carrot and bell pepper to take on the chili-and-vinegar character. Refrigerating immediately (skipping the room-temperature window) produces a milder less integrated pikliz that reads structurally as pickled vegetables rather than as canonical Haitian pikliz. The room-temperature step is non-negotiable; refrigerate only after the mature is complete.

The heat is the point. Canonical Haitian pikliz runs aggressively hot; 4 whole scotch bonnets in a 1 L jar produces a heat level that reads as serious for any cook not raised in the Caribbean. This is structurally correct and is what defines the dish in Haiti. The mild modification (2 chilies, seeded) is a reasonable accommodation for guests; do not market the milder version as canonical pikliz to Haitian guests.

The thin shred is structural. Shredding the cabbage and other vegetables thin (2 to 3 mm) allows the brine to penetrate quickly and produces the structural texture (tender-crisp, with a slight bite). Thicker chunks (1 cm and up) take days to mature and produce a structurally different pickle that reads as chunky chow-chow rather than as canonical pikliz. A mandoline is the right tool; a sharp chef knife works with patience. Avoid the food processor; the uneven chunks and the bruised cabbage produce a structurally wrong pickle.

The vegetable balance is structural. The canonical pikliz is roughly 50% cabbage, 20% carrot, 15% onion, 15% bell pepper, by weight, with scotch bonnet to taste. Adjusting the ratios within 10% produces a recognizably canonical pikliz; large shifts (50% carrot, for example) produce a structurally different pickle. The cabbage dominance is the structural foundation; the other vegetables are supporting players.

Versatility. Pikliz works as a condiment for almost any Caribbean fried protein: griot (canonical), Haitian fried chicken, Jamaican escovitch fish, Dominican chicharrón de pollo, fried snapper. Outside the Caribbean canon, pikliz works as a condiment for: Cuban or Puerto Rican rice and beans, tacos al pastor (in lieu of the traditional salsa verde), grilled sausages, hot dogs, sandwiches, salads (as a structural acid component in a vinaigrette substitute), and as a brilliant accompaniment to any grilled or roasted meat that benefits from an acid-and-heat contrast.

Storage. After the 24-hour room-temperature mature, refrigerate. The pikliz keeps 1 month refrigerated and reads better at week 1 than at day 1 (the flavor matures continuously through the first 2 weeks). The vegetables stay crunchy through 3 to 4 weeks; past that the cabbage softens and the pikliz reads more pickled-vegetable and less structurally complete. The vinegar brine that remains after the vegetables are eaten is structurally a pikliz vinegar; use it as a substitute for plain vinegar in vinaigrettes, marinades, or as a quick acid for greens.

  • The Haitian canon. Pikliz is structurally Haitian, a colonial-era development from French pickling techniques and African scotch-bonnet-and-acid traditions; the modern stabilized recipe (cabbage, carrot, onion, bell pepper, scotch bonnet, vinegar, lime, salt) hardened in the 19th and 20th centuries as the universal Haitian condiment. Documented across Haitian home cookery and as the canonical companion to griot in Haitian restaurants worldwide. — Two structural versions: the fresh-pickle (this recipe, room-temperature for 24 hours then refrigerated) and the longer-cured (refrigerated immediately, slower mature, milder character). The fresh-pickle is canonical and the dominant Haitian preparation. Some households add a few cloves of garlic, some add a small piece of fresh ginger, some skip the peppercorns; the structural ingredients (cabbage, scotch bonnet, vinegar, lime, salt) are non-negotiable.
