Puerto Rican mofongo

The Puerto Rican mortar dish: green plantains fried to a deep gold, then mashed in a pilón with garlic, chicharrón, olive oil, and a splash of broth into a dense aromatic mound. Shaped into a half-sphere in the bowl, sometimes hollowed out and stuffed with shrimp, chicken, or beef stew. A dish that wears its African-Caribbean technique on its surface.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Use only green (firm, unripe) plantains. Yellow or yellow-and-black plantains are too sweet and starchy-soft; they produce mashed-banana mofongo rather than the structural Puerto Rican dish.
  • A wooden pilón (mortar) is the canonical tool but is not strictly required. A heavy ceramic mortar with a wooden pestle, or a sturdy bowl with the bottom of a heavy glass, produces equivalent results. Do not use a food processor; the smooth puree is structurally wrong.
  • Chicharrón is structural to canonical mofongo. Look for fresh chicharrón at Latin American groceries (often sold at deli counters); pork rinds from the snack aisle are the everyday substitute. Mofongo without pork or pork rinds is mofongo de vegetales, a structurally different and lighter dish.

  2. 80 g chicharrón (fresh pork rinds) or commercial pork rinds, broken into 1 cm pieces
  3. 45 g extra-virgin olive oil
  4. 100 g chicken stock, hot
  5. 4 g kosher salt (for the mash)

Phase 3, optional broth for serving

  1. 500 g chicken or beef broth, warm

Phase 4, the finish

  1. 8 g fresh cilantro, chopped
  2. 60 g lime, cut into wedges — (2 limes, 8 wedges; squeezed over the mofongo or into the broth)

Execution

  1. 1
    Peel the plantains. Cut off both ends of each 200 g plantain; score the skin lengthwise in 3 places (only through the peel, not into the flesh). Soak the scored plantains in warm salted water 5 minutes; the soak lifts the skin from the flesh and allows clean peeling. Peel off the skin in strips with a paring knife or thumbnail. Cut each peeled plantain into 2.5 cm thick rounds.
  2. 2
    Phase 1, fry the plantains. Heat the 500 g neutral oil in a heavy deep skillet (4 cm of oil) over medium heat to 165°C / 330°F (test with a wooden spoon; bubbles should form around the handle when dipped in). Fry the plantain rounds in batches (do not crowd) for 6 to 8 minutes per batch until the surface is deep gold and the interior is tender when pierced with a fork. Transfer to a paper-towel-lined plate; sprinkle with about 3 g of the 6 g salt while hot. The plantains should be cooked through but not crisp at the surface; this is structural for the mash that follows. Repeat with the second batch.
  3. 3
    Phase 2, start the mash in the pilón. While the plantains are still hot (the mash works only on hot plantains; cooled plantains turn rubbery and resist mashing), add the 24 g whole garlic cloves to a large wooden pilón or heavy mortar (a 1 L capacity bowl works). Smash the garlic to a rough paste with the pestle (about 60 seconds). Add the 80 g broken chicharrón; smash 30 seconds to break the rinds into small pieces and combine with the garlic. Mortar pounding (clay vs granite)
  4. 4
    Add the plantains. Add about 200 g (one serving) of the hot fried plantains to the pilón. Pound and mash with the pestle into a coarse paste, working from the center outward; the texture should be uniform but with visible plantain structure (not a smooth puree). Add 10 g of the olive oil and 25 g of the warm chicken stock and 1 g of salt; mash to incorporate, adjusting the stock if too dry. The finished mofongo should hold together when pressed into a mound and read as a dense fragrant mash with visible bits of plantain, garlic, and chicharrón. Mortar pounding (clay vs granite)
  5. 5
    Shape and serve. Pack the mofongo into a small bowl (about 200 ml capacity) to shape into a dome; invert onto a serving plate. Repeat with the remaining plantains, garlic, chicharrón, olive oil, and stock to make 4 individual mofongo mounds, working in batches in the pilón. Each serving is about 200 g finished mofongo.
  6. 6
    Phase 4, finish and serve. Scatter the 8 g chopped cilantro over the mofongo mounds. Spoon a small amount of the warm 500 g broth into a small bowl on the side of each plate (the broth is poured over the mofongo at the table, structurally the African fufu-and-soup pairing). Serve with the 60 g lime wedges; the lime squeeze adds the structural finishing acid. Canonical Puerto Rican pairings: shrimp in garlic sauce (camarones al ajillo), stewed chicken (pollo guisado), or beef stew (carne guisada) spooned over the top.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion cuisine pivot→as mofongo relleno de camarones (shrimp-stuffed mofongo)

    After shaping each mofongo mound, hollow out a 4 cm wide well in the center with the back of a spoon (a cup-shape). Meanwhile, sauté 400 g peeled shrimp in 30 g olive oil with 4 minced garlic cloves, 100 g diced tomato, 80 g diced bell pepper, 60 g dry white wine, 2 g cumin, 1 g smoked paprika, 4 g salt; cook 4 minutes until the shrimp are just opaque. Spoon the shrimp-and-sauce into the hollowed mofongo; finish with cilantro and a squeeze of lime. The stuffed version is the canonical restaurant preparation and is structurally a complete meal.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as Dominican trifongo (combination of plantain, yuca, and yautía)

    Replace 400 g of the plantains with 200 g yuca (cassava, peeled and cut into 2 cm chunks) and 200 g yautía (taro, peeled and cubed). Boil the yuca and yautía 20 minutes until tender; fry the plantains as written. Mash all three together in the pilón with the garlic, chicharrón, olive oil, and stock. Trifongo is the structural Dominican variant and reads slightly more starchy-dense than canonical Puerto Rican mofongo.

  • Dietary as vegetarian mofongo (mofongo de vegetales)→with no pork, finished with fried vegetables

    Omit the chicharrón. Sauté 100 g sliced mushrooms, 80 g diced bell pepper, and 60 g diced onion in 30 g olive oil 6 minutes until soft; add to the pilón with the plantains and garlic. Increase the olive oil to 60 g to compensate for the missing pork fat. Use vegetable stock instead of chicken. The vegetarian version is structurally lighter than canonical mofongo and is the dietary adaptation; it reads recognizably different from the canonical pork-based dish.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • chicharrón (fresh pork rinds) → commercial pork rinds (the snack-aisle variety) or crispy bacon, crumbled Fresh chicharrón from a Latin American grocery is the canonical choice and produces the deepest pork flavor. Commercial pork rinds are the everyday substitute and work mechanically; they are slightly drier and less fatty. Crispy bacon crumbles read different but provide the structural pork-fat-and-crunch contribution.
  • green plantains (firm, unripe) → no acceptable substitute Ripe (yellow or black) plantains are too sweet and too soft for mofongo; the dish requires the structural starchy density of the green plantain. Green bananas are smaller and less starchy and produce a flat-tasting mofongo. If green plantains are unavailable, cook a different dish.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → chicharrón (fresh pork rinds) or commercial pork rinds make it yourselfstub Homemade chicharrón from pork skin (rendered, simmered, then deep-fried to a crisp) produces dramatically better mofongo than commercial pork rinds; the structural pork-fat character and the crisp texture are clearly tasted in the mash. A weekend project worth doing once and freezing in portions.
  • → chicken stock, hot make it yourself Homemade stock contributes structural depth to the mofongo mash; the difference is most apparent in the broth poured over the mound at service, which is the structural flavor of the dish.

Notes

Mofongo is the structural Puerto Rican showcase dish for plantain technique and one of the clearest examples of African culinary survival in the Caribbean. The technique (boiling or frying a starchy vegetable, mashing in a wooden mortar, shaping into a mound, serving with broth or stew) is structurally West African fufu, transferred to the Caribbean through the African slave trade and adapted to local plantain. The Spanish contributions (garlic, olive oil) and the colonial-era addition of chicharrón complete the dish; the result is structurally Puerto Rican, not African or Spanish in isolation.

The plantain must be green. Mofongo depends on the structural density and starch character of the unripe plantain; ripe plantains are too sweet and too soft and produce a mashed-banana version that reads as a different dish entirely. The visual test: the plantain skin should be solid green (or green with the smallest hint of yellow at the tips), the flesh should be hard to the squeeze, and the cut surface should be pale yellow with a slight astringent smell. If the skin is mostly yellow or showing brown spots, the plantain has passed the green stage and is the wrong ingredient.

The fry temperature is structural. Plantains for mofongo are fried at 165°C / 330°F (medium heat), not at the higher 180°C / 360°F used for tostones (twice-fried plantain rounds). The lower temperature cooks the plantain through to tender without crisping the surface; mofongo plantains should be soft enough to mash easily, not crisp enough to stand alone as a fried snack. Mashing crisp plantains produces a dry sandy texture that no amount of olive oil or stock can rescue.

The pilón (wooden mortar) is canonical and produces meaningfully better mofongo than a food processor. The slow rough pounding of the pilón breaks the plantain in irregular pieces and incorporates the garlic and chicharrón as distinct visible elements; the resulting texture is structurally complete with mixed-sized fragments that produce flavor density in each bite. A food processor produces a smooth uniform puree that reads as a different dish; the structural fragmenting is the point. A heavy ceramic mortar with a wooden pestle works well; a heavy bowl with the bottom of a heavy glass produces acceptable results with more effort.

The chicharrón is structural to canonical mofongo. The pork rinds (whether fresh chicharrón from a Latin American grocery or commercial pork rinds from the snack aisle) contribute structural pork-fat richness, salt, and a slight crunch contrast that distinguishes mofongo from generic mashed plantain. Mofongo de vegetales (the vegetarian version) is a real and acceptable dish but reads as a different preparation; the canonical Puerto Rican mofongo includes chicharrón as a structural ingredient.

The shape-as-mound presentation is structural. Mofongo is shaped into a dome on the plate (packed into a small bowl and inverted) and served with broth on the side or stew on top. The dome shape allows the broth to soak into the mofongo as the diner eats, and the structural eating motion is to break off pieces of the mound and dunk in the broth or fold with the stew. A flat mofongo plate loses the structural interactive element that defines the dish.

The broth-on-the-side is the canonical service. A small bowl of warm chicken or beef broth accompanies the mofongo plate; the diner pours small amounts of broth over the mofongo as they eat, which keeps the mash moist and integrates the broth flavor into the dish. This is structurally the African fufu-with-soup pairing transferred to the Caribbean; without the broth, the mofongo reads as too dry and structurally incomplete. The stuffed-mofongo restaurant preparation (with shrimp, chicken, or beef stew in the hollowed center) is the contemporary alternative service and is also structurally complete.

Storage. Mofongo is at its best within 15 minutes of mashing; the structural texture degrades quickly as the mash cools (the plantain starch retrogrades and the mass tightens). Refrigerated, 2 days; reheat in a 180°C / 350°F oven covered with foil 10 minutes (do not microwave, the texture turns gummy). Mofongo does not freeze well. Cook fresh whenever possible; the small batch size (4 servings) is structurally aligned with the dish's freshness requirement.

  • The Puerto Rican canon, with roots in West African fufu (mashed starchy vegetable mound, served alongside soup or stew). The dish stabilized in colonial Puerto Rico through African slave cookery; the pilón (wooden mortar) and the mash-and-shape technique are structurally African. The Spanish contributions are the garlic and olive oil; the indigenous Taíno contribution is the plantain itself. — Two structural versions: mofongo (mashed and shaped as a mound, eaten with broth or stew alongside) and trifongo (a Dominican variant combining plantain, yuca, and yautía).
