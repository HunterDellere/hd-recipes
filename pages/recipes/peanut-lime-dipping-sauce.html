Peanut-lime dipping sauce

The Southeast Asian peanut-and-coconut dipping sauce in the satay family: roasted peanuts ground to a coarse paste, coconut milk, fish sauce, lime, palm sugar, garlic, ginger, dried chili. Pourable-thick, sweet-salty-sour-spicy-nutty, and the universal partner for grilled meat skewers, raw vegetable platters, fresh and rice-paper rolls, rice noodles, grilled tofu, and rice bowls. The structurally most versatile sauce in the Indonesian-Malaysian-Thai-Vietnamese satay tradition.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

The peanut base

  1. 100 g roasted unsalted peanuts (or use the homemade dry-roasted version), pulsed in a food processor to a coarse-medium grind, with some texture remaining; not a smooth paste
  2. 1 × 13.5 oz / 400 ml can full-fat coconut milk

The aromatics

  1. 8 g garlic clove, peeled, finely minced or pounded to a paste — (2 medium cloves)
  2. 10 g fresh ginger, peeled, finely grated on a microplane
  3. 2 g red pepper flakes (or sambal oelek, or a small dried chili soaked and chopped)

The seasoning

  1. 20 g fish sauce (Vietnamese, ideally 40N or higher)
  2. 30 g palm sugar (or light brown sugar)
  3. 30 g fresh lime juice
  4. 5 g toasted sesame oil (optional)

The body

  1. 30 g warm water

Execution

  1. 1
    In a food processor (4 to 7 cup capacity), pulse the 100 g / ⅔ cup roasted peanuts 10 to 12 times to a coarse-medium grind, scraping the sides between pulses; the texture should be like coarse crumbs with some structural pieces remaining (about 1 to 2 mm), not a smooth paste. The texture preservation is structural for the sauce; pure smooth peanut butter reads flat and the satay character disappears. Transfer to a small bowl.
  2. 2
    In a small saucepan over medium-low heat, combine the 150 g / ⅔ cup coconut milk, 8 g / 2 garlic cloves, 10 g / 2 tsp grated ginger, and 2 g / ½ tsp red pepper flakes; bring to a gentle simmer (small bubbles around the edge, no rolling boil) and cook 3 minutes, stirring occasionally, to bloom the aromatics in the coconut fat. The bloom is structural for flavor depth; raw garlic and ginger added to the cold sauce read aggressive and one-dimensional, while bloomed aromatics integrate with the coconut and develop a rounded sweetness.
  3. 3
    Pull the saucepan off the heat. Add the ground peanuts, 20 g / 4 tsp fish sauce, 30 g / 2 tbsp palm sugar, and 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil; whisk to combine. The sauce will thicken visibly as the peanut starches absorb the liquid; this is correct. Whisk in the 30 g / 2 tbsp warm water in a steady stream to thin to the desired consistency, somewhere between heavy cream and runny peanut butter; the sauce should pour off a spoon in a slow ribbon and coat the back of the spoon when dipped.
  4. 4
    Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp lime juice last (after the sauce has cooled slightly off the heat); the lime juice is structurally added off-heat to preserve the bright top note. Whisk to combine; taste and adjust: the sauce should read in equal balance across nutty (from the peanuts), creamy (from the coconut), salty-umami (from the fish sauce), sweet (from the palm sugar), sour (from the lime), and gentle heat (from the chili). If flat, more salt and lime; if dominant in any direction, dial back via the opposing axis (more lime balances dominant sweetness; more sugar balances dominant lime).
  5. 5
    Serve at room temperature or slightly warm. Use as a dip for grilled satay skewers (chicken, beef, pork, lamb), tofu skewers, raw vegetable platters (cucumber, carrot, radish, bell pepper, jicama), fresh spring rolls and rice paper rolls, fried spring rolls, grilled prawns and shrimp; as a pour-over for cold rice noodles (Vietnamese vermicelli bowls), warm rice bowls, and grain bowls; as a dressing for cabbage salads, gado-gado-style vegetable salads, and crunchy slaws; as a finishing drizzle for grilled tofu, grilled fish, and roasted vegetables.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as a Thai satay sauce (with kaffir lime and Thai basil)

    Add 2 to 3 finely shredded kaffir lime leaves and 5 g / 2 tbsp finely chopped Thai basil at the end (off-heat, with the lime juice). Reduce the red pepper flakes to 1 g and add 5 g / 1 tsp red curry paste to the coconut milk during the bloom step. The result reads structurally Thai, with the floral kaffir lime and the warm curry-paste base; canonical for serving with grilled chicken or pork satay skewers.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as Indonesian sambal kacang (thicker, more concentrated dipping sauce)

    Halve the coconut milk to 75 g; double the peanuts to 200 g; replace the red pepper flakes with 15 g / 1 tbsp sambal oelek; add 10 g / 2 tsp tamarind paste in place of half the lime juice. The result is structurally sambal kacang, the thick clinging dip canonical for Indonesian and Malaysian satay; reads denser and more concentrated, the right body for dipping rather than pouring.

  • Dietary dietary→as a vegetarian or vegan version

    Replace the fish sauce with 25 g / 5 tsp soy sauce or tamari (slightly more than the fish sauce because soy reads less concentrated); add 5 g / 1 tsp white miso for the missing umami depth (the miso replaces the long-fermented amino acid complexity of fish sauce). The vegan version reads slightly less aggressive and more savory; pairs especially well with grilled tofu, vegetable bowls, and rice plates.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • roasted unsalted peanuts (food-processor ground) → natural peanut butter (no added sugar or oil, well-stirred) Use 90 g / 6 tbsp natural peanut butter (slightly less than the whole-peanut weight because the smooth butter reads more concentrated). The sauce reads slightly less textured but functionally identical; useful when a quick sauce is needed or when whole peanuts are unavailable. Avoid sweetened or oil-stabilized peanut butter; both read wrong in this application.
  • palm sugar → light brown sugar or coconut sugar Both are workable; light brown reads slightly more molasses-heavy, coconut sugar reads closer to palm sugar. White granulated sugar works in a pinch but reads flat.
  • fish sauce → soy sauce (for vegetarian) or 50/50 fish sauce and Worcestershire sauce See modifications for the full vegetarian conversion. The 50/50 fish-and-Worcestershire blend brings a slightly richer umami profile that pairs especially well with beef satay; not canonical but a useful variation.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → roasted unsalted peanuts (or use the homemade dry-roasted version) make it yourself Home-roasted peanuts (raw peanuts, deep oven roast) read fundamentally fresher and more aromatic than supermarket roasted peanuts; for a sauce where the peanut character dominates, the difference is clearly tasted in the finished result.
  • → full-fat coconut milk make it yourselfstub Home-pressed coconut milk from fresh or desiccated coconut reads cleaner and more aromatic than canned coconut milk and the cream-to-thin separation is more pronounced; useful for sauces where the coconut character is structural.
  • → fish sauce (Vietnamese, ideally 40N or higher) make it yourselfstub Long-fermented home fish sauce reads dramatically more complex than industrial fish sauce; the depth carries through into any finished sauce that uses fish sauce as the umami spine.

Notes

The aromatic-bloom step is the structural difference between a memorable satay sauce and a flat one. Garlic, ginger, and chili added directly to a cold or warm sauce read raw, sharp, and one-dimensional; the same aromatics simmered for 3 minutes in coconut fat at gentle heat undergo two transformations. The garlic alliinase compounds break down to milder sulfur compounds; the ginger gingerols partially convert to shogaols (more rounded, less aggressive); and the chili capsaicin disperses into the fat, distributing heat evenly through the sauce instead of leaving sharp pockets. The 3-minute bloom is short but structurally non-negotiable.

The texture decision matters across applications. The coarse-grind peanut version (this recipe) reads structurally interesting and brings a pleasant nubbly mouthfeel; pairs especially well with applications where the sauce sits on or beside a textured ingredient (grilled meat, raw vegetables, fresh rolls). The smooth peanut-butter version (substitute) reads more uniform and clings more cleanly to thin or smooth surfaces; pairs especially well with rice noodles, soft tofu, and pour-over rice bowls. Both are correct; choose based on the application and personal texture preference.

Versatility. Peanut-lime dipping sauce is the most cuisine-flexible single sauce in the Southeast Asian pantry. Use it as: the canonical dip for grilled satay skewers (Indonesian, Malaysian, Thai, Vietnamese variations of grilled chicken, beef, pork, lamb on bamboo skewers); a dip for grilled or fried tofu and tempeh; a dip for raw vegetable platters and crudités (cucumber, carrot, radish, jicama, bell pepper, snow pea, sugar snap, blanched broccoli, asparagus); a dipping or drizzle sauce for fresh spring rolls (gỏi cuốn) and rice paper rolls (the Vietnamese diaspora canon, often served alongside or in place of nuoc cham); a sauce for fried spring rolls (chả giò) and fried wontons; a pour-over for cold or warm rice noodle bowls (vermicelli bowls, cold sesame noodles in a Southeast Asian register); a pour-over for warm rice bowls and grain bowls with grilled protein and vegetables; a dressing for cabbage salads, gado-gado-style mixed vegetable salads (the Indonesian canon), and crunchy slaws (especially with cabbage, carrot, cucumber, and herbs); a finishing drizzle for grilled fish, grilled prawns, and grilled vegetables; a sauce for grilled or roasted chicken (a brilliant alternative to barbecue sauce in cross-cuisine grill cookery); and as a sandwich or wrap spread for chicken, tofu, and vegetable wraps.

Pairs across Indonesian, Malaysian, Thai, Vietnamese, broader Southeast Asian, modern American grill cookery (especially Asian-influenced barbecue), contemporary fusion plates, and modern Australian-Pacific cuisine. The combination of nut richness, coconut body, fish sauce umami, lime brightness, palm sugar caramel, and gentle heat is one of the broadest flavor profiles in any single sauce, and translates across most savory applications.

Storage. Keeps 5 days refrigerated in a sealed jar; thickens significantly in the cold (the coconut fat firms; the peanut starches set). Bring to room temperature 30 minutes before use, or warm gently in a saucepan with 1 to 2 tbsp warm water and re-whisk to restore pourable consistency. Freezing is acceptable for up to 1 month; thaw overnight in the refrigerator and re-whisk vigorously to restore the texture (the coconut fat may separate and need re-emulsifying with the warm water).

Sources
  • The broader Southeast Asian satay tradition (Indonesian sambal kacang, Malaysian kuah kacang, Thai nam jim satay, Vietnamese sốt đậu phộng). The structural template (roasted peanut, coconut, fish sauce, lime or tamarind, palm sugar, chili, aromatics) is shared across the region with regional ingredient variations; the version here is a Vietnamese-Thai-leaning preparation built for cross-cuisine use. — The Indonesian and Malaysian versions tend to be thicker and more concentrated (a clinging dip for grilled satay skewers); the Thai version reads slightly thinner and brighter with kaffir lime and Thai basil overtones; the Vietnamese version (popularized via the diaspora and modern street food) is closer to a finishing sauce and the most internationally familiar form. This recipe sits between Vietnamese and Thai, prioritizing pourability and cross-cuisine usability over regional purity.
