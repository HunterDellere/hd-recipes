Recipe

Goi cuon (Vietnamese fresh rolls with shrimp and pork)

Translucent rice-paper rolls wrapping cold rice vermicelli, poached shrimp halved lengthwise, sliced poached pork belly, lettuce, mint, Thai basil, and chives, served with two dipping sauces: a hoisin-peanut for richness and a nuoc cham for brightness. The shrimp is laid first against the paper so it shows through the wrap as the visual signature; the roll is tight and clean, never bursting at the seam.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Poach the pork belly and shrimp first. Both need to cool fully before assembly; warm protein melts the rice paper and ruins the roll. Build in a 30-minute cooling window.
  • Cook the rice vermicelli and rinse cold; let it drain fully. Warm or wet noodles weaken the rice paper and tear the seam during the roll.
  • Have all components in arms-reach of the rolling surface: a wide shallow bowl of warm water (about 40°C / 105°F) for the rice paper, a damp tea towel laid flat as a rolling surface, the shrimp halves arranged on a small plate, the pork slices ready, the cooked vermicelli portioned, herbs picked, lettuce torn. Rolling is a 90-second operation per roll; stopping to fetch ingredients kills momentum.
  • Make the dipping sauces before rolling. Both improve on 15-minute rest and the rolling phase is when you want every component ready.

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, shrimp

  1. 300 g shrimp, large, shell-on — (about 16 to 20 shrimp; the shell-on poach extracts the most flavor from the shrimp; substitute pre-peeled but the rolls read slightly flatter)
  2. 1000 ml water for shrimp poach
  3. 5 g kosher salt for shrimp poach

Phase 2, pork

  1. 400 g pork belly, skin-on
  2. 1500 ml water for pork poach
  3. 20 g ginger, bruised, sliced into coins, bruised — (a 3 cm knob)
  4. 20 g scallion whites, bruised — (2 scallions, whites only; greens reserved for nuoc cham)
  5. 8 g kosher salt for pork poach

Phase 3, vermicelli and rolling components

  1. 150 g rice vermicelli (bun), dried
  2. 12 each rice paper (banh trang), 22 cm round — (12 sheets plus 2 to 3 extras for tears; Three Ladies brand is the canonical Vietnamese rice paper)
  3. 100 g butter lettuce or red leaf lettuce, leaves separated, tough ribs removed, torn into 8 cm pieces — (about 6 to 8 leaves; provides structural body to the roll and a crisp-cool contrast)
  4. 30 g mint, leaves picked
  5. 20 g Thai basil, leaves picked
  6. 30 g cilantro, leaves and tender stems, sprigs whole
  7. 20 g garlic chives or regular chives, cut into 8 cm lengths
  8. 1000 ml water for rice paper dip

Phase 4, hoisin-peanut dipping sauce

  1. 60 g hoisin sauce
  2. 30 g natural peanut butter
  3. 60 g coconut milk or water
  4. 10 g lime juice
  5. 5 g sambal oelek or sriracha
  6. 20 g crushed roasted peanuts for garnish

Phase 5, nuoc cham dipping sauce

  1. 45 g fish sauce
  2. 120 ml water (warm)
  3. 30 g lime juice
  4. 30 g granulated sugar
  5. 10 g garlic, minced fine — (2 cloves)
  6. 5 g fresh bird-eye chili, sliced thin — (1 chili)
  7. 30 g carrot, finely shredded for garnish

Execution

  1. 1
    Poach the pork. Place the 400 g pork belly in a saucepan with the 1500 ml / 6 cups water, 20 g bruised ginger, 20 g bruised scallion whites, and 8 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt. Bring to a bare simmer; cook 30 to 35 minutes until fork-tender (the pork should yield to a chopstick poke). Pull and let cool 15 minutes; then slice into 3 mm thin slices against the grain. Refrigerate while you continue.
  2. 2
    Poach the shrimp. Bring 1000 ml / 4 cups water with 5 g / 1 tsp salt to a hard boil. Add the 300 g shell-on shrimp; cook 90 seconds to 2 minutes until just pink and opaque. Pull immediately with a slotted spoon to an ice bath; the cold shock stops the cook and firms the texture. Once cooled (3 minutes), peel and devein the shrimp, then slice each shrimp lengthwise in half (so each shrimp produces two flat shrimp halves with the C-curve flat against the rice paper for visual signature).
  3. 3
    Cook the vermicelli. Bring a fresh pot of water to a hard boil. Drop in the 150 g rice vermicelli; cook 2 to 3 minutes until just tender (taste a strand). Drain immediately; rinse aggressively under cold running water for 60 seconds to stop the cook and wash off surface starch. Drain again; toss with a few drops of neutral oil to prevent clumping. Set aside.
  4. 4
    Make the hoisin-peanut sauce. Combine the 60 g / 4 tbsp hoisin, 30 g / 2 tbsp peanut butter, and 60 g / ¼ cup coconut milk in a small saucepan. Warm over low heat 2 minutes, whisking, until smooth and pourable. Pull from heat; whisk in the 10 g / 2 tsp lime juice and 5 g / 1 tsp sambal. Transfer to a small bowl; top with the 20 g / 2 tbsp crushed peanuts at the moment of serving.
  5. 5
    Make the nuoc cham. In a small bowl, whisk the 30 g / 2 tbsp sugar with the 30 g / 2 tbsp lime juice until the sugar dissolves. Whisk in the 45 g / 3 tbsp fish sauce, 120 ml / ½ cup warm water, 10 g minced garlic, and 5 g sliced chili. Rest 15 minutes for flavors to integrate. Top with the 30 g / 2 tbsp shredded carrot just before serving.
  6. 6
    Set up the rolling station. Place a wide shallow bowl of warm water (about 40°C / 105°F, comfortable to dip a finger in) on the counter. Lay a clean damp tea towel flat next to the water bowl as the rolling surface (the damp surface prevents the rice paper from sticking). Arrange all the components within arm reach: shrimp halves on a plate (curve down), pork slices, drained vermicelli, lettuce, mint, basil, cilantro, chives.
  7. 7
    Roll the first wrap. Dip one rice paper sheet into the warm water for 4 to 6 seconds only, just until the sheet softens slightly at the edges (it will still feel stiff in the center; it continues to hydrate after it leaves the water). Lay flat on the damp tea towel. Working quickly (the sheet fully softens in 30 seconds): place 3 shrimp halves cut-side-down in a horizontal line across the lower third of the sheet (these will show through the wrap as the visual signature). Top with 2 pork slices, a small handful of vermicelli (about 30 g cooked), a lettuce leaf piece, a few sprigs of mint, Thai basil, and cilantro, and 1 to 2 chive lengths sticking out one end.
  8. 8
    Roll tight. Fold the bottom edge of the rice paper up over the filling, pressing gently to tuck the filling into a cylinder. Fold in the right and left edges over the cylinder ends (the chive ends should still stick out). Continue rolling forward, snug but not crushing, until the roll is fully sealed seam-side-down. Transfer to a plate (do not stack rolls, they stick together). Cover with a damp paper towel as you roll the rest.
  9. 9
    Repeat with remaining rice paper and filling, making 12 rolls total. The first 2 rolls will be technique-rough; by roll 4 the rhythm settles. If a rice paper tears, discard and use a new sheet; do not patch.
  10. 10
    Serve immediately at room temperature with both dipping sauces on the side. The rolls can be made up to 2 hours ahead (cover with a damp towel, refrigerate, return to room temperature 20 minutes before serving); past 2 hours the rice paper dries and toughens.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→goi cuon ga (chicken fresh rolls)

    Substitute 300 g poached chicken breast (sliced into 5 mm strips) for the pork belly; keep the shrimp. Lighter and more common in Vietnamese-American restaurants; the pork-belly version is more canonical in Vietnam.

  • Dietary protein→goi cuon chay (vegetarian)

    Skip the shrimp and pork entirely. Substitute: 200 g pressed firm tofu (cut into 5 mm strips, pan-fried until golden), 100 g shredded jicama or apple (for crunch), 100 g julienned cucumber. Use the hoisin-peanut sauce only (nuoc cham is fish-sauce-based); or make a vegetarian nuoc cham using soy sauce plus a pinch of seaweed flakes instead of fish sauce.

  • Regional cuisine→banh trang cuon thit heo (DIY-at-the-table version, Da Nang style)

    Skip the assembly step. Set out a platter of all components (shrimp halves, sliced pork belly, vermicelli, lettuce, herbs, sliced cucumber, sliced green mango, sliced star fruit) along with a stack of rice papers and a bowl of warm water. Diners build their own rolls at the table, customizing each one. The Da Nang Central Vietnamese style; convivial and interactive.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • shrimp, large, shell-on → pre-peeled and deveined shrimp Convenient but the rolls read slightly flatter; the shell-on poach extracts more flavor and produces firmer-textured shrimp. If using pre-peeled, reduce poach time to 60 to 90 seconds.
  • pork belly, skin-on → pork shoulder or pork tenderloin (cooked similarly) Pork shoulder works but lacks the canonical pork-belly fat sweetness. Pork tenderloin is the leanest substitute, drier in the roll; brine 30 minutes in 5% salt water before poaching to compensate.
  • rice paper (banh trang) → no real substitute; switch to lettuce wraps if banh trang unavailable Wheat-flour wrappers, dumpling skins, and spring roll sheets are all structurally different products. If banh trang cannot be sourced, switch the format to lettuce wraps with the same fillings.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → hoisin sauce make it yourself Fresh-fermented homemade hoisin has deeper bean and five-spice complexity than commercial bottles. A 30-minute project that yields a 6-month supply.

Notes

Why brief dip. The rice paper continues to hydrate after leaving the water; a sheet dipped for 6 seconds will be fully pliable by the time you have laid out the filling and started the first fold. A sheet dipped for 15 seconds is already over-hydrated, sticks to itself, tears at the fold, and ends up wet-flabby in the finished roll. The dip should leave the paper feeling stiff at the edges; experience tells you when to pull.

Why shrimp halves face down against the paper. The cut, flat face of the shrimp half pressed against the wet rice paper creates the visual signature of professional goi cuon: pink shrimp halves clearly visible through the translucent wrap, evenly spaced and oriented. Whole shrimp (uncut) produces a lumpy, less-photogenic roll. Halving lengthwise (with a thin sharp knife from the back of the shrimp through the belly) produces two flat halves that lay cleanly.

Why two dipping sauces. The hoisin-peanut sauce (sweet-rich, dominant in Vietnamese-American restaurants) pairs with the bright herbal rolls by adding richness and weight. The nuoc cham sauce (bright-tart-salty-spicy, the more authentic Vietnamese choice) pairs with the rolls by adding acid and contrast. Both are legitimate; serving both lets each diner build their own preference and gives every bite a different profile. Restaurants in Vietnam most often serve nuoc cham only; Vietnamese-American restaurants more often serve hoisin-peanut.

Why the chive sticks out. The canonical Vietnamese goi cuon has a chive end protruding from one side of the roll. Function: a small visual flag of fresh aromatic, and a textural-aromatic moment when the diner bites that end. Decoration and information at once.

Why room temperature, not cold or hot. The rolls are designed to be served at the temperature they end up at after assembly (around 15 to 18°C / 60 to 65°F, slightly cool from the chilled protein and noodles but not refrigerator-cold). The lettuce and herbs taste muted from the fridge; the warmth of the room (or a warm room) brings the herbal volatiles forward. A roll served cold reads as muted; a roll served warm has melted rice paper. Room temperature is the working window.

Storage. Goi cuon does not store well. The rice paper dries and toughens within 4 hours, even covered. Make and eat the same day; if you must store, cover with a damp towel and refrigerate, then warm back to room temperature 20 minutes before serving. The components store well separately: pork holds 3 days, shrimp 2 days, vermicelli 24 hours.

Why no fish sauce in the roll itself. The herbs, vermicelli, and proteins are all unseasoned in the roll; all the salt and umami come from the dipping sauce. This is the structural design of goi cuon as a dish: bland-clean wraps that are dressed by the sauce. Adding fish sauce or salt to the components inside the roll makes the dish read flat and over-seasoned; the sauce architecture cannot fix it.

Sources
  • Adapted from Southern Vietnamese tradition (goi cuon, sometimes called summer rolls or fresh spring rolls) — Goi cuon (literally "rolled salad") is the cold, unfried Vietnamese rice-paper roll, distinct from cha gio (the fried version, sometimes mistranslated as "spring roll"). The dish is southern Vietnamese in origin, served at room temperature as a starter or as part of a larger meal of Vietnamese sharing plates. The hoisin-peanut sauce is the more common dipping sauce in Vietnamese-American restaurants; nuoc cham is the more authentic Vietnamese choice, especially in Hanoi. This recipe offers both because they pair differently with the same roll.
