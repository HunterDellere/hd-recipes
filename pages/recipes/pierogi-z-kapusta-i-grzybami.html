Pierogi z kapustą i grzybami (sauerkraut and mushroom pierogi)

Tender hand-rolled wheat dough filled with cooked sauerkraut and dried porcini, boiled to set the dough, then pan-fried in butter with caramelized onion until the crust crisps and turns golden. The vegetarian Christmas Eve pierogi of the Polish Wigilia tradition; the filling is structural, the dough is the vehicle, the butter-onion finish is the dish.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The dough needs a 30-minute rest after kneading to relax the gluten and roll out evenly. Make it first, then prepare the filling while it rests; the timing is well-matched.
  • The filling needs to cool fully before forming the pierogi. Hot filling melts the dough and tears the seal during folding. Plan for the filling to cool 30 to 45 minutes (or refrigerate to speed it).
  • The dried porcini soaking liquid is structural; do not discard. Strain it through a coffee filter to remove silt and use it as the cooking liquid for the kraut. The umami load is half the value of the porcini.
  • Make pierogi in batches. Roll, fill, and seal 12 at a time so the dough does not dry out while you work; cover the remaining dough with plastic-free damp cloth to keep it pliable.
  • Boil and pan-fry in two distinct phases. Boiled pierogi without the butter-onion sear are bland; pan-fried without first boiling crack and leak filling. The two-step is structural.

Phase 1, the dough

  1. 400 g all-purpose flour, plus more for the work surface
  2. 4 g fine sea salt
  3. 1 each large egg — (1 egg; binds the dough and tenderizes it through the fat in the yolk)
  4. 60 g sour cream (full-fat)
  5. 150 ml warm water (35 to 40 °C)
  6. 15 g neutral oil

Phase 2, the filling

  1. 25 g dried porcini mushrooms
  2. 400 ml warm water (for soaking the porcini)
  3. 500 g sauerkraut (refrigerated raw, not canned shelf-stable), drained from its brine, squeezed firmly to remove excess liquid (do not rinse; the lactic-acid tang is structural), chopped roughly to 1 to 2 cm pieces
  4. 200 g yellow onion, peeled and finely diced, finely diced — (1 large onion; cooks down into the kraut as the second flavor base)
  5. 40 g unsalted butter (for the filling)
  6. 3 g caraway seed, lightly crushed
  7. 1 each bay leaf — (1 leaf; aromatic for the kraut simmer)
  8. 2 g black pepper, freshly ground
  9. 2 g kosher salt

Phase 3, the cook and finish

  2. 30 g kosher salt (for the boiling water)
  3. 80 g unsalted butter (for the pan-fry finish)
  4. 200 g yellow onion (for the finish), peeled and finely diced, finely diced — (1 large onion; the structural caramelized-onion finish on the pan-fry)

Phase 4, service

  1. 200 g sour cream (for serving)
  2. 10 g fresh dill, hand-torn (optional) optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the dough. In a large bowl, whisk together the 400 g / 3¼ cups all-purpose flour and the 4 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt. In a separate small bowl, whisk together the 1 egg, the 60 g / ¼ cup sour cream, the 150 ml / ⅔ cup warm water, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil until smooth. Pour the wet into the dry and stir with a wooden spoon until a shaggy dough forms. Turn out onto a lightly floured surface and knead by hand for 8 to 10 minutes until the dough is smooth, elastic, and slightly tacky but not sticky. Form into a ball, wrap in beeswax wrap or place in a covered bowl with a damp cloth, and rest at room temperature for 30 minutes while you make the filling.
  2. 2
    Soak the porcini. Place the 25 g / ¾ oz dried porcini in a heatproof bowl and pour over the 400 ml / 1⅔ cups warm water (not boiling, the proteins are heat-sensitive). Let soak 20 to 25 minutes until the mushrooms are pliable. Lift the porcini out with a slotted spoon (do not pour, the grit settles at the bottom), squeeze gently over the bowl, and chop finely (3 to 4 mm pieces). Strain the soaking liquid through a coffee filter or paper-towel-lined sieve to remove the silt; reserve.
  3. 3
    Cook the filling. In a heavy 3-quart saucepan, melt the 40 g / 3 tbsp butter over medium heat. Add the 200 g / 1 large diced yellow onion and cook 8 to 10 minutes, stirring every 2 minutes, until deep golden brown. The onion is the structural base for the filling; do not rush this caramelization.
  4. 4
    Add the 500 g / 2½ cups drained chopped sauerkraut, the chopped porcini, the 3 g / 1 tsp lightly crushed caraway, the 1 bay leaf, the 2 g / ¾ tsp black pepper, and 200 ml / ¾ cup of the reserved porcini soaking liquid (save the rest for another use or discard). Stir to combine. Simmer uncovered over medium-low heat for 30 to 35 minutes, stirring every 5 minutes, until the kraut has softened, the liquid has fully reduced, and the filling is dry enough to hold its shape when pressed with a spoon. The dry finish is structural; wet filling tears pierogi during folding.
  5. 5
    Pull from heat. Discard the bay leaf. Taste; the filling should read tangy from the kraut, deeply umami from the porcini, slightly aromatic from the caraway. Add the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt if it reads flat. Spread on a wide plate or sheet pan to cool to room temperature, 30 to 45 minutes (or refrigerate to speed; chill at least 20 minutes). Hot filling tears the dough.
  6. 6
    Form the pierogi. Divide the rested dough in half (keep one half covered with the damp cloth to prevent drying). On a lightly floured surface, roll the first half to 2 mm thickness. Cut 7 to 8 cm rounds with a round biscuit cutter or the rim of a drinking glass. Re-roll scraps. You should get 15 to 18 rounds per half-dough batch.
  7. 7
    Place 1 heaping teaspoon (about 15 g) of cooled filling in the center of each round. Lift one edge over the filling to meet the other, forming a half-moon. Pinch the edges together firmly with your fingers, starting from one corner and working to the other, pressing out any trapped air as you go. The seal must be complete; any gap leaks filling during boiling. Optionally crimp the sealed edge with a fork or with a decorative pleat for visual interest. Place the formed pierogi on a flour-dusted sheet pan; do not let them touch (they stick). Repeat with the second half of the dough.
  8. 8
    Bring the 4000 ml / 4 liters water to a hard rolling boil in a large pot with the 30 g / 2 tbsp kosher salt. Drop the pierogi in batches of 10 to 12 (do not crowd). They sink at first, then rise to the surface within 60 to 90 seconds. Once they have all risen, simmer for another 2 to 3 minutes; the dough sets fully and the filling heats through. Lift out with a slotted spoon and transfer to a colander to drain briefly, then to a plate lightly oiled to prevent sticking. Repeat in batches until all pierogi are boiled.
  9. 9
    For the butter-onion finish: in a wide heavy skillet over medium heat, melt the 80 g / 5½ tbsp butter. Add the 200 g / 1 large diced yellow onion (the finish onion) and cook 10 to 12 minutes, stirring every 2 minutes, until deep golden brown and soft. The slow caramelization is structural; rushed onions read bitter and pale.
  10. 10
    Add the boiled pierogi to the buttered onion skillet in a single layer (cook in two batches if needed). Pan-fry over medium heat for 2 to 3 minutes per side, until both sides are crisp and golden brown and the buttered onion clings to the dumpling skin. Push gently with a spatula; the pierogi should not stick if the butter is hot enough.
  11. 11
    Serve immediately on warm plates, spooning the buttered onion from the pan over the pierogi. Dollop generously with the 200 g / ¾ cup sour cream and scatter with the 10 g / ¼ cup torn fresh dill if using. Traditional service is family-style: a large platter of pierogi with a small bowl of sour cream on the table for diners to dollop their own.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional filling→ruskie (potato and farmer's cheese)

    Replace the sauerkraut-mushroom filling with: 600 g potatoes (peeled, boiled until tender, mashed), 250 g farmer's cheese or pressed full-fat ricotta (drained), 1 small onion finely diced and sautéed in 30 g butter until golden, salt, pepper. Mix and cool. Form, boil, and pan-fry as written. Pierogi ruskie are the most ubiquitous Polish pierogi and the most popular filling in Polish-American kitchens.

  • Protein filling→meat (z mięsem)

    Replace the sauerkraut-mushroom filling with: 500 g leftover braised meat (pot roast, brisket, or pork shoulder), finely chopped or ground, 1 onion finely diced and sautéed in 30 g butter, 1 hard-boiled egg finely chopped, salt, pepper, a splash of meat juices for moisture. The traditional Polish use for braising leftovers.

  • Regional cuisine→Ukrainian varenyky (the Ukrainian sibling)

    Same dough, same filling options. Form into smaller pinched-edge dumplings (5 to 6 cm rounds) and serve with melted butter and fresh dill but without the heavy pan-fried onion finish. Ukrainian varenyky are gentler in presentation than the Polish butter-onion finish.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dried porcini → dried mixed wild mushrooms, or dried shiitake Mixed wild mushroom blends (porcini, morel, chanterelle) work identically. Dried shiitake carry comparable umami but read recognizably Asian; the pierogi taste recognizably different but acceptable.
  • refrigerated raw sauerkraut → canned or jarred sauerkraut, rinsed briefly to dial back the harsh acid Shelf-stable sauerkraut is acceptable when the live ferment is unavailable; rinse briefly to remove some of the harsh vinegar-and-metal tang that develops in pasteurization. The fermented depth is missing but the dish is recognizable.
  • sour cream (for the dough) → plain whole-milk yogurt, or buttermilk, or 60 g whole milk + 1 tsp lemon juice The sour cream is for tenderness and tang; all of these substitutes work. Plain yogurt is the closest match.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → sauerkraut (refrigerated raw, not canned shelf-stable) make it yourself Homemade sauerkraut is genuinely better than any commercial product and takes 30 minutes of active work plus a 2 to 3 week wait. The lactic-acid complexity reads through the long-cooked filling in a way that commercial kraut cannot match.

Notes

Why the filling must be dry. Wet filling melts the dough during folding, tears the seal during the lift from cutting board to pot, and leaks during the boil. The 30 to 35 minute simmer phase is the structural step that produces a filling dry enough to hold its shape; do not skip or shorten it. The filling should look like cooked cabbage that holds a spoon-drag across the pan, not like cabbage in liquid.

Why the boil-then-pan-fry. Pierogi cooked by boiling alone are pale, soft, and bland; pierogi cooked by pan-frying alone crack and leak filling because the dough has not set. The two-step is structural: boil to set the dough and heat the filling through, then pan-fry in butter and onion to add the crispy crust and the carry-over butter flavor that makes the dish memorable. Skipping either step produces a worse dish.

The caramelized-onion finish. The 200 g of onion cooked slowly in 80 g of butter is the structural finishing move and the moment when pierogi transform from boiled dumplings into a dish. Polish kitchens often double or triple the butter-onion finish; the onion-and-butter sauce is the point.

Make-ahead and freezing. Formed unboiled pierogi freeze beautifully. Arrange formed pierogi on a flour-dusted sheet pan, freeze solid (2 hours), then transfer to a freezer bag. Cook from frozen by dropping directly into boiling water; add 1 to 2 minutes to the boil time. Stored frozen pierogi hold 3 months. Boiled and pan-fried pierogi reheat acceptably in a hot pan with a knob of butter but are best fresh.

Polish Wigilia tradition. Pierogi z kapustą i grzybami are one of the 12 traditional dishes of the Polish Christmas Eve Wigilia feast, served on a meatless table. The kraut and mushroom combination is structural to the meatless tradition; the porcini contributes the umami depth that a meatless feast would otherwise lack.
