Infused honey (thyme, rosemary, chili, lavender)

Aromatic honey made by submerging fresh herbs, dried chilies, or other aromatics in raw honey at 50 °C for 2 to 4 hours. The honey takes on the herb aromatic without driving off its native floral character. Stub.

16servingsabout 250 g infused honey (1 cup)
15 minactive+ 4 h rest
easydifficulty
Mediterraneansauce

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 2 items ▾
  1. 250 g raw honey (acacia, wildflower, or thyme — match to the infusion)
  2. 10 g fresh thyme sprigs (or rosemary, or 2 dried chilies, or 1 tbsp dried lavender) — (small handful for thyme; adjust by aromatic strength)

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: combine the 250 g honey and the aromatic in a heatproof glass jar. Set the jar in a 50 °C water bath (a yogurt maker, a sous-vide bath, or a low oven held at 50 °C all work) for 2 to 4 hours. Heat above 60 °C drives off the volatile aromatics in both the honey and the herb; below 40 °C the infusion runs too slow.
  2. 2
    Stub: strain through a fine sieve to remove the herb material. Decant into a clean glass jar. The infused honey holds 6 months at room temperature; the aromatic peaks at week 2 to week 6, then gradually flattens.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the aromatic compound chemistry (terpenes in thyme and rosemary, capsaicin in chili — capsaicin is fat-soluble so chili-infused honey is weaker than chili-infused oil), the heat sensitivity of honey enzymes (50 °C is the upper threshold for preserving glucose oxidase and the floral volatiles), the variety pairings (acacia honey for delicate herbs like lavender; thyme honey doubled with fresh thyme for amplification; wildflower honey for chili), the ratio chart (10 g fresh herb per 250 g honey is a starting point, scale by aromatic strength), and the application examples (drizzled on cheese, into vinaigrettes, glazed onto carrots, swirled into yogurt, brushed onto roasted root vegetables).
