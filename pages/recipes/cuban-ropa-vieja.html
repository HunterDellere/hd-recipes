Cuban ropa vieja

The Cuban national dish: brisket simmered until tender, shredded into rough strands (the old clothes the name describes), and finished in a sofrito of onion, bell pepper, garlic, tomato, cumin, oregano, and dry sherry. Served over white rice with sweet plantains, black beans, and a wedge of lime.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The brisket needs a 3-hour simmer to become tender enough to shred. Start in the early afternoon for a dinner service, or make the entire dish the day before; ropa vieja tastes meaningfully better at 24 hours.
  • Reserve at least 500 ml of the brisket cooking broth before discarding the rest; the broth is structural to the final sauce.
  • White rice and sweet plantains are the canonical pairings, not optional sides. Start the rice 30 minutes before the final sofrito phase; fry the plantains 5 minutes before serving.

Execution

  1. 1
    Phase 1, simmer the brisket. Combine the 1500 g brisket, 200 g halved onion, 40 g halved garlic head, 3 bay leaves, 100 g carrot, 3 g peppercorns, 18 g salt, and 3000 ml water in a large heavy pot (8 L). Bring to a boil over high heat; skim the gray scum that rises in the first 10 minutes. Reduce to a bare simmer (small bubbles breaking the surface), partially cover, and cook 2.5 to 3 hours until the brisket shreds easily when pulled with two forks. Lift the brisket out; rest 15 minutes covered. Strain the broth; reserve 500 ml for the sauce. Discard the spent aromatics.
  2. 2
    Shred the brisket. Once the brisket is cool enough to handle, pull it apart along the grain with two forks into rough strands about 4 to 5 cm long (the old clothes shreds that name the dish). Do not chop with a knife; the irregular ragged texture is structural to ropa vieja. Set aside.
  3. 3
    Phase 2, build the sofrito. Heat the 60 g olive oil in a large heavy skillet or wide Dutch oven (5 L) over medium heat. Add the 300 g diced onion, 200 g green bell pepper, and 150 g red bell pepper; sweat 10 to 12 minutes with the lid off, stirring every 2 minutes, until the vegetables are soft, slightly golden, and reduced in volume by half. The slow sweat is structural; rushing this step at high heat produces a raw-tasting sofrito.
  4. 4
    Bloom the spices. Add the 20 g minced garlic; cook 60 seconds until fragrant (do not brown; brown garlic turns bitter). Add the 40 g tomato paste; cook 2 minutes, stirring constantly, until the paste darkens to a deep brick-red and the oil separates at the edges (the structural Maillard browning of tomato paste). Add the 4 g cumin, 3 g oregano, and 4 g smoked paprika; toast 30 seconds in the hot oil until fragrant. Spice tempering (tadka / tarka)
  5. 5
    Deglaze and build the sauce. Add the 120 g dry sherry; stir to scrape up any fond from the pan bottom. Cook 2 minutes to evaporate the alcohol. Add the 400 g crushed tomatoes, 500 ml reserved brisket broth, 2 bay leaves, and 6 g salt; bring to a simmer. Deglazing
  6. 6
    Phase 3, combine and finish. Add the shredded brisket to the sauce; stir to coat. Reduce heat to medium-low; simmer uncovered 25 to 30 minutes until the sauce thickens noticeably and clings to the beef, and the flavors fully integrate. Add the 80 g halved olives and 20 g capers in the last 5 minutes (added too early they cook out their structural brine character). Taste and adjust salt; ropa vieja should read deeply beefy with a structural sofrito base, smoky from the paprika, savory from the olives and capers, with a bright finish from the sherry. Discard the bay leaves.
  7. 7
    Serve. Spoon ropa vieja over hot white rice (about 200 g cooked rice per plate); scatter the 10 g chopped cilantro over the top. Serve with the 60 g lime wedges on the side; the lime squeeze at the table is the structural finishing acid. The canonical Cuban plate also includes maduros (fried sweet plantains) and a small bowl of black beans on the side; both are non-negotiable for the full Cuban dinner experience.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as vaca frita (fried cow, the crispy-edge version)

    Skip the sofrito-with-sauce. After shredding the brisket, heat 60 g of olive oil in a large heavy skillet over high heat. Spread the shredded beef in a single layer; fry 4 to 5 minutes undisturbed until the bottom develops a deep crisp crust. Flip and crisp the other side 3 to 4 minutes. Meanwhile, in a separate small pan, sauté 200 g diced onion and 4 cloves minced garlic in olive oil 8 minutes; combine with the crisped beef; finish with 30 g lime juice. Vaca frita is the canonical alternative Cuban preparation using the same simmered-and-shredded brisket; the structural difference is the crisp-fry instead of the sauce-finish.

  • Occasion as a slow cooker version (the modern weeknight shortcut)→cooked entirely in a slow cooker

    Combine the brisket, sofrito ingredients (raw, no pre-cook needed), tomatoes, sherry, spices, and 500 ml water or beef broth in a 6 L slow cooker. Cook on low 8 hours. Shred the brisket in the slow cooker insert; add the olives and capers; cook on high 15 minutes more to finish. The slow cooker version reads slightly less structurally developed than the canonical version (the sofrito does not get the same caramelization) but is a brilliant weeknight shortcut.

  • Protein protein→with flank steak (the alternative canonical cut)

    Replace the brisket with 1500 g flank steak. Simmer 90 minutes instead of 3 hours (flank cooks faster than brisket and overcooks if simmered as long). Shred along the long grain into structurally cleaner ropa vieja strands. The flank version reads slightly leaner and more uniform than the brisket version and is the canonical second choice in Cuban cookery.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dry sherry (or dry white wine) → dry vermouth or unsweetened apple cider Dry sherry is the canonical Cuban choice and is structurally close to the dry white wine that Spanish settlers brought; dry vermouth reads slightly more aromatic and works well; unsweetened apple cider reads sweeter and is the alcohol-free option. Avoid sweet sherry, which throws the dish out of balance.
  • pimento-stuffed Spanish olives → green olives plus a small dose of roasted red pepper The pimento-stuffed olive is the canonical Cuban choice (the pimento adds a structural sweet-pepper note inside the olive brine). Plain green olives plus 15 g chopped roasted red pepper provides approximately the same flavor balance.
  • beef brisket (flat, with some fat cap) → beef chuck roast or flank steak Chuck roast is the closest substitute in behavior and produces a similar shredded texture; flank steak is the canonical alternative and shreds along the long grain. Both work; brisket produces the richest dish.

Notes

Cuban ropa vieja is the structural national dish of Cuba and one of the great cross-Atlantic synthesis dishes. The origin lies in Sephardic Jewish Spain (the original ropa vieja was a Castilian leftover dish using the boiled meat from cocido, a Sunday stew of meat and chickpeas); Spanish colonists carried the technique to Cuba in the 16th century, and the Caribbean version evolved with the addition of tropical tomato, bell pepper, sherry, and a more aggressive olive oil and garlic load. The resulting dish is structurally Cuban, not Spanish; the layered sofrito and the brisket-with-sherry preparation defines it as a New World creation.

The brisket-and-sofrito sequence is structural. The brisket simmers in plain water with aromatics; the resulting broth carries beef depth that the sofrito sauce then absorbs. Skipping the long simmer (browning the brisket and proceeding straight to the tomato sauce, for example) produces a structurally different dish that reads more like Italian-American beef ragu than Cuban ropa vieja; the gentle long simmer is what produces the structural texture and the broth character that defines the dish.

The sofrito is the second structural feature. Cuban sofrito is olive-oil-based with onion, green bell pepper (canonical and structural; red bell pepper is supporting only), garlic, tomato, and oregano. This is structurally different from Caribbean recao-based sofrito (which uses culantro and ají dulce; canonical in Puerto Rico and the Dominican Republic). The Cuban version is Mediterranean-leaning and reads more European; the Puerto Rican-style version is herbaceous and reads more Afro-Caribbean. Do not blend the two; the dish identity depends on the structural sofrito character.

The sherry is non-negotiable. Cuban cooking is built on the Spanish colonial pantry and dry sherry (jerez seco) is the structural cooking wine. The sherry contributes a dry oxidized depth that white wine approximates but does not match; if sherry is unavailable, use dry vermouth (closer than wine), unsweetened apple cider (closer than wine), or worst-case dry white wine. Sweet sherry destroys the dish; always use dry.

The olive-and-caper finish is the structural brine note. Spanish pimento-stuffed olives and capers are added in the last 5 minutes of cooking; added too early they cook out their brine character. The brine note balances the rich beef and tomato sauce and is the structural finish that distinguishes ropa vieja from a generic beef stew. Skipping the olives and capers produces a flatter dish; use both.

The canonical Cuban plate includes white rice, sweet plantains (maduros), and black beans. These are not optional sides; the structural Cuban dinner balance depends on the carb, the sweet, and the legume alongside the meat-and-sauce. A piece of crusty Cuban bread (pan cubano, similar to a French baguette but with lard in the dough) is the canonical fourth component. Serve all four together on one plate or on a small spread.

Storage. Refrigerated, 5 days. The dish tastes meaningfully better at 24 hours than at the immediate cook; make the day before serving when possible. Freezes 3 months in 500 g portions; reheat gently in a covered pot with a splash of water to restore the original sauce consistency. The cooked brisket broth (the strained simmer liquid) is a brilliant beef broth for any future dish; freeze in 250 g portions for 3 months.
