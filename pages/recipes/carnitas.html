Carnitas

The Michoacán pork confit: bone-in shoulder slowly simmered in lard with citrus, milk, and aromatics until tender, then cranked up to crisp the surface against the rendered fat. Shredded into ragged pieces with caramelized edges and yielding centers, served on warm corn tortillas with cilantro, white onion, salsa verde, and lime.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The 3-hour cook is unattended for most of the window; the pork simmers in the lard at a tremor while you do other things. Start mid-afternoon for a 6 pm dinner.
  • You need enough lard to nearly submerge the pork. This is the structural confit technique; the lard temperature is gentle (95 to 100°C) and the cooking is by fat conduction rather than direct heat. Skimping on the lard produces a pork-shoulder-stew rather than carnitas.
  • The lard is reusable. After the carnitas finish, strain the lard through a fine-mesh sieve and refrigerate; it keeps 2 months and produces the most flavorful pork-spiked lard for the next carnitas session, or for refried beans, or for tamale masa.
  • Warm the corn tortillas just before serving on a dry comal; cold tortillas crack around hot carnitas. Have the garnish (cilantro, white onion, lime) staged before the final crisp.

Phase 1, the confit

  1. 1800 g pork shoulder (Boston butt), bone-in, cut into 6 cm chunks, fat cap and bone left in for flavor
  2. 500 g lard (manteca)
  3. 250 g whole milk
  4. 200 g orange, halved, juiced; peel reserved and added whole — (1 large navel orange; the juice goes into the pot, the peels go in whole; the structural citrus bright-bitter)
  5. 200 g white onion, quartered, skin-on — (1 medium onion)
  6. 40 g garlic head, cut crosswise in half, skin-on — (1 whole head)
  7. 3 each bay leaves
  8. 5 g whole Mexican canela stick
  9. 4 g dried Mexican oregano
  10. 18 g kosher salt

Phase 2, the taco assembly

  1. 360 g corn tortillas (4 inch / 10 cm) — (18 tortillas, 3 per serving)
  2. 30 g fresh cilantro, leaves and tender stems, chopped
  3. 120 g white onion (for garnish), finely diced (2 mm) — (½ medium onion)
  4. 90 g limes, cut into wedges — (3 limes, 12 wedges)
  5. 200 g salsa verde or salsa roja

Execution

  1. 1
    Phase 1, set up the confit. Season the 1800 g pork chunks generously with the 18 g kosher salt (work it into all surfaces; the salt is the structural cure). Let rest 15 minutes at room temperature. Heat the 500 g lard in a large heavy Dutch oven (5 L works) over medium heat until just melted and shimmering, not yet smoking. Add the pork chunks in a single layer; the lard should come about three-quarters of the way up the pork (add more melted lard if needed).
  2. 2
    Add the aromatics. Squeeze the juice from the 200 g orange into the pot; add the spent orange peels whole. Add the 250 g milk, 200 g quartered onion, 40 g halved garlic head, 3 bay leaves, 5 g canela stick, and 4 g Mexican oregano. Bring to a bare simmer (small bubbles at the surface; the lard should be at about 95 to 100°C / 200 to 212°F, not boiling). Reduce heat to low or medium-low; partially cover with the lid askew (a tight lid produces steam that emulsifies the lard and prevents the final crisp).
  3. 3
    Simmer 2 to 2.5 hours undisturbed. Check the temperature every 30 minutes; adjust burner to maintain the bare simmer. The lard slowly cooks the pork by fat conduction; the meat reads firm at the 90-minute mark and starts to become fork-tender at the 2-hour mark. The structural test for doneness: a piece of pork shreds when pressed with a wooden spoon but holds its overall shape when lifted with tongs. Do not let the lard boil; boiling produces tough stringy meat rather than tender carnitas.
  4. 4
    Phase 2, crisp the surface. Remove the spent aromatics from the pot (orange peels, onion quarters, garlic, bay leaves, canela stick) with tongs and discard. Increase heat to medium-high; the lard will start to bubble more aggressively as the milk lactose caramelizes. Continue cooking 15 to 20 minutes, stirring gently every 3 to 4 minutes, until the pork develops deep brown caramelized edges and the surface crisps against the now-hotter lard. The pork should still be tender inside; the goal is structural surface browning, not deep-frying. Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Drain and shred. Lift the pork pieces out of the lard with a slotted spoon onto a sheet pan or large plate; let drain 2 minutes. Discard the bone (the meat falls off easily) and any large fat caps. Shred the pork with two forks into ragged irregular pieces; do not over-shred or chop into uniform cubes (the structural carnitas texture is ragged with mixed crisp edges and tender interior). Strain the lard through a fine-mesh sieve into a heatproof jar; refrigerate for reuse.
  6. 6
    Phase 3, warm tortillas and assemble. Warm the 360 g corn tortillas on a dry comal or skillet over medium-high heat 15 to 20 seconds per side; stack and cover with a clean kitchen towel. Set out the 30 g chopped cilantro, 120 g diced raw onion, 90 g lime wedges, and 200 g salsa in small bowls. Build each taco: warm tortilla flat, mound about 50 g shredded carnitas in the center, scatter cilantro and onion over, spoon a teaspoon of salsa, finish with a squeeze of lime. Serve immediately. The diner builds each taco; do not pre-assemble.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion cuisine pivot→as carnitas in a slow cooker (the modern weekday version)

    Reduce the lard to 250 g (the slow cooker keeps the moisture in better than a Dutch oven and the meat self-bastes). Combine all the confit ingredients in a 6 L slow cooker; cook on low 8 hours or high 4 hours. Pull the pork and the aromatics out; spread the pork on a sheet pan and broil 4 to 6 minutes until the surface crisps. This shortcut produces a structurally similar carnitas with less hands-on time; the surface crisp from the broiler is reasonable, slightly different from the lard-crisp of the canonical version.

  • Occasion as a tortilla-free dish→over rice with refried beans (the plate version)

    Serve the carnitas over Mexican-style rice (white rice cooked in tomato puree with garlic) alongside refried black or pinto beans, with the same cilantro, onion, salsa, and lime garnish. The plate version is the canonical Sunday afternoon Michoacán family meal and is dramatically more substantial than the taco format.

  • Occasion as carnitas torta (the sandwich version)→on a torta roll with refried beans, avocado, pickled jalapeño

    Split a torta roll (the soft Mexican sandwich bread; substitute a sturdy hoagie roll if unavailable). Spread the cut sides with refried beans; top one side with about 100 g shredded carnitas, the other with sliced avocado, sliced tomato, pickled jalapeños, and a smear of mayonnaise. Close and press in a hot skillet 2 minutes per side. The torta version is the canonical Mexico City lunch and travels well as a packed meal.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • lard (manteca) → neutral oil (grapeseed, sunflower, or canola) Lard is canonical and structural; the rendered pork fat that comes off the shoulder during the cook integrates back into the lard and produces a deeper flavor than neutral oil. Neutral oil is the standard substitute when lard is unavailable; the dish reads slightly less rich but is mechanically functional. Use the same weight.
  • whole milk → evaporated milk (75 g, undiluted) Evaporated milk has higher solids and contributes more lactose for caramelization; some Mexican home cooks prefer it. Use 75 g instead of 250 g whole milk. The dish reads slightly sweeter and slightly more caramelized.
  • pork shoulder (Boston butt), bone-in → boneless pork shoulder or pork butt Boneless is more convenient and works mechanically; the bone-in version contributes more gelatin to the lard and produces a slightly richer flavor. The difference is modest; use boneless when convenience matters.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → salsa verde or salsa roja make it yourselfstub A fresh raw tomatillo salsa or a charred-tomato roja takes 10 minutes and reads dramatically brighter than jarred. For carnitas specifically, where the salsa cuts the rich pork fat, the brightness of homemade is the structural finish that makes the taco sing.
  • → corn tortillas (4 inch / 10 cm) make it yourself Homemade corn tortillas pressed from fresh masa are dramatically better than supermarket tortillas: more pliable, more flavorful, and structurally able to hold the carnitas without cracking. For any taco dish where the tortilla is half the experience, the homemade version is worth the time.

Notes

Carnitas is the most forgiving of the long Mexican pork projects and the easiest entry point for a home cook learning the structural techniques of Mexican slow cooking. The cook is unattended for 2 hours, the ingredient list is short, the technique tolerates moderate temperature inattention (the lard buffers the heat and prevents the meat from overcooking), and the resulting pork is dramatically better than any quick-cooked alternative. Master carnitas first; the techniques transfer to barbacoa, cochinita pibil, and most of the long-cooked Mexican braised meats.

The confit technique is structural. Cooking the pork submerged (or nearly submerged) in lard at a gentle 95 to 100°C / 200 to 212°F is structurally different from braising in water or stock; the fat conducts heat more efficiently than water at the same temperature, the meat does not lose moisture to the cooking medium (since the lard is hydrophobic), and the surface stays in contact with the fat-and-Maillard-products rather than with the steam that comes off a wet braise. The result is meat that is tender through, retains its own moisture, and develops a structural crisp at the surface during the final high-heat phase that water-based braising cannot produce.

The milk addition is structural and frequently misunderstood. The lactose in the milk caramelizes during the long cook, contributing to the structural surface browning that distinguishes carnitas from generic confit pork. The milk also coagulates into a soft curd that settles at the bottom of the pot and adds a structural dairy depth to the lard; this is a feature, not a problem. Some Mexican cooks use evaporated milk or sweetened condensed milk for additional caramelization; both are acceptable variations.

The orange peels are structural. The orange juice contributes the structural bright acid; the orange peels contribute the structural bitter-aromatic note from the d-limonene in the peel oil. Both work together to balance the rich fat of the lard and the pork. Skipping the peel and using only juice produces a flat carnitas; using only the peel and no juice produces a bitter carnitas. Use both, in the canonical ratio.

The crisp-at-the-end phase is the structural difference between carnitas and pulled pork. The 15-to-20-minute high-heat phase at the end of the cook is what produces the ragged crisp-edged texture that defines carnitas; without it, the dish reads as tender shredded pork in lard, which is good but not the same dish. The lard temperature rises from the confit window (95 to 100°C) to a frying window (170 to 180°C) as the milk evaporates; the pork surface caramelizes from this elevated temperature. Stir gently to crisp multiple surfaces without breaking the pork into uniform fragments.

The shred-with-forks step is the third structural element. Carnitas should be ragged and irregular, with mixed crisp edges and tender interior, not uniform cubes. Pull the pork apart with two forks (or by hand once cool enough to handle) into pieces of varying sizes; the structural texture difference between bites is the point. Chop with a knife only if specifically targeting the al pastor-style uniform dice for tacos; for plate service or sandwich service, leave it ragged.

Versatility. Carnitas travels well beyond tacos. Use it for: carnitas tortas (sandwich format), carnitas plates over rice with beans, carnitas burritos (rolled in a flour tortilla with rice and beans), carnitas tamales (mixed with masa and steamed in banana leaves), carnitas-stuffed empanadas, carnitas tacos dorados (filled and fried crisp), carnitas chilaquiles (over a bed of tortilla chips in salsa), and as the structural protein in any Mexican composed dish where shredded pork is appropriate.

Storage. Cooked carnitas keeps 5 days refrigerated; freeze in 250 g portions for 3 months. Reheat in a hot dry skillet 3 to 4 minutes (the carnitas re-crisps with the residual lard); do not microwave, the texture turns rubbery. The strained lard keeps 2 months refrigerated and is the structurally most-flavorful lard for future cooking; use it for refried beans, tamale masa, the next carnitas batch, or any savory application where lard is structural.

Sources
  • The Michoacán canon. Carnitas (literally little meats) is the structural pork dish of the state of Michoacán in western Mexico; large copper pots (cazos de cobre) of lard simmer whole pig parts (head, shoulder, ribs, belly) at Sunday markets and the carnitas master chops the cooked pork to order. The technique transfers to home cooking through pork shoulder and a heavy Dutch oven. — The canonical Michoacán carnitas uses every part of the pig; modern home carnitas focuses on shoulder (the most forgiving cut). The milk and citrus additions are structural: milk lactose caramelizes during the long cook and contributes to the surface browning; orange juice and peel add a structural bright-bitter note that distinguishes carnitas from generic confit pork. The Coca-Cola addition that some recipes call for is a modern Mexican home shortcut that works by adding sugar and acid; the canonical Michoacán version uses milk and orange.
