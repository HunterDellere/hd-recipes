Sauerkraut

Shredded green cabbage salted at 2 to 2.5%, pounded until the cell walls release enough water to form a self-brine, weighted under the brine, and lacto-fermented 1 to 3 weeks at room temperature for a fizzy, deeply sour cabbage ferment.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a 7 to 21 day project from cabbage to ready-to-eat sauerkraut. Active hands-on time is about 35 minutes; the rest is patience and the daily press-down to keep the cabbage under brine.
  • Equipment: a large non-reactive bowl (4 L+) for the salt-and-pound, a 2 L wide-mouth glass jar with an airlock lid OR a traditional open-crock with a weight (a glass fermentation weight, a clean small ceramic dish, or a zip-top bag filled with brine works for the weight). Avoid aluminum, untreated cast iron, and copper, which react with the salt-and-acid brine.
  • Salt must be non-iodized. Iodine inhibits lacto-fermentation. Kosher salt, fine sea salt, or pickling salt all work; avoid Morton iodized table salt. Salt percentage matters: 2 to 2.5% by weight of the cabbage is the canonical sauerkraut range, lower than kimchi but firmly in the lacto-fermentation window.
  • Water requirement: typically none. The salt draws enough liquid from the cabbage to form a self-brine within 30 to 60 minutes. If the cabbage is unusually dry (out of season, stored too long) and the self-brine does not cover the cabbage after 24 hours, top up with a 2% brine (10 g salt per 500 g non-chlorinated water).
  • Hold the ferment at 18 to 22 °C (room temperature) for 1 to 3 weeks. Cooler than 16 °C stalls the lactobacillus; warmer than 24 °C runs too fast and produces a soft, less complex sauerkraut. Cooler ferments (18 to 20 °C) over 2 to 3 weeks produce the most complex, deeply sour result.
  • Submersion under brine is non-negotiable. Sauerkraut above the liquid line grows kahm yeast (a white film, harmless but off-flavored) within 3 days and mold within a week. Either weigh down with a glass fermentation weight or use an open-crock with a properly-sized weight stone.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 3 items ▾
  1. 2000 g green cabbage (firm, heavy heads, not loose-leaf), outer 2 to 3 wilted or dirty leaves removed and reserved; head halved and cored; cored halves shredded into 3 mm ribbons by hand with a chef's knife or with a mandoline
  2. 45 g coarse non-iodized sea salt or kosher salt
  3. 5 g caraway seeds optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Trim and shred the cabbage. Remove the outer 2 to 3 wilted or dirty leaves from the 2000 g cabbage; reserve 2 of the cleanest large outer leaves intact for capping the jar at the end (they hold the shredded cabbage down under the brine). Halve the head through the core, cut out the white triangular core from each half, and shred the cabbage into 3 mm ribbons by hand with a sharp chef's knife (the canonical method, producing slightly uneven ribbons that ferment well) or by mandoline (faster but produces more uniform ribbons that pack tighter). Transfer the shredded cabbage to a large non-reactive bowl.
  2. 2
    Salt and pound. Sprinkle the 45 g / 3 tbsp salt evenly over the shredded cabbage. Add the 5 g / 1 tsp caraway seeds if using. With clean hands (or a wooden kraut pounder if you have one), squeeze, pound, and massage the cabbage aggressively for 8 to 12 minutes. The cabbage will go through three phases: first it stays dry and stiff (the first 2 to 3 minutes), then it begins to soften visibly and release surface liquid (minutes 3 to 6), and finally it collapses to roughly half its starting volume in a meaningful pool of its own brine (minutes 6 to 12). The end-point cue: when you squeeze a handful of cabbage, brine drips freely between your fingers; the cabbage in the bottom of the bowl should be visibly submerged in liquid.
  3. 3
    Rest 30 minutes. Let the salted cabbage sit in the bowl at room temperature for 30 minutes to allow the salt to continue drawing water through osmosis; you should have noticeably more brine pooled at the bottom by the end of the rest. If the cabbage looks dry and there is no pooled brine after the rest, give it another 5 minutes of pounding.
  4. 4
    Pack the jar. Transfer the cabbage and all the released brine to a clean 2 L wide-mouth glass jar (a Weck jar with a glass lid is ideal; a standard mason jar with an airlock lid works equally). Pack the cabbage down firmly with a wooden spoon or your fist after every cup or so to eliminate air pockets and bring the brine to the surface. The cabbage should compact significantly under pressure; what looked like a full jar after the first dump should collapse to about 70% capacity after thorough packing. Continue packing until the brine fully covers the cabbage; if the brine sits below the cabbage level, press harder or wait another 15 minutes for the salt to continue drawing water.
  5. 5
    Cap the cabbage with the reserved whole outer leaves. Take the 2 reserved whole cabbage leaves, fold them to fit the jar diameter, and lay them flat on top of the packed shredded cabbage. The whole leaves act as a barrier that keeps loose shreds from floating above the brine line; tuck the leaf edges down around the jar interior with a wooden spoon. Place a glass fermentation weight on top of the cabbage leaves (a clean small ceramic dish, a zip-top bag filled with 2% brine, or a smaller glass jar full of water all work as improvised weights); the weight holds the cabbage and leaves submerged.
  6. 6
    Fit the lid. If using an airlock lid (a mason jar lid with a one-way valve, like the Pickle Pipe or Easy Fermenter), screw it on snugly; the airlock allows CO2 from the active ferment to escape while keeping outside air and contaminants out. If using a standard mason jar lid, fit it loosely (a quarter-turn back from sealed) so CO2 can escape; a fully sealed jar will build pressure and can pop. If using a traditional open crock, drape a clean cloth over the top to keep out fruit flies and air-borne contaminants.
  7. 7
    Ferment at 18 to 22 °C for 7 to 21 days. Place the jar on a small tray to catch any brine that bubbles up during active fermentation (this peaks at days 3 to 7 and is normal; if the brine bubbles out, top up with 2% brine to keep the cabbage submerged). Active fermentation: you will see fine bubbles rising through the brine, a slight cloudiness develops, the brine starts smelling sour-fizzy by day 3, and the cabbage begins to lose its bright-green color and turn pale yellow-green. Press the cabbage back down under the brine once daily with a clean spoon.
  8. 8
    Taste starting on day 7. Pull a small piece of cabbage from beneath the brine with a clean spoon and taste. Early sauerkraut (week 1) reads salty-tangy and faintly fizzy with a clear residual raw-cabbage character; properly fermented sauerkraut (weeks 2 to 3) reads aggressively sour, complex, with a back-of-throat tang and a clean cabbage-fermented depth that no jarred commercial sauerkraut matches. Most home cooks like the 14-day stage. The longer you ferment, the more complex and the more sour; over-fermented sauerkraut (4+ weeks at room temperature) becomes very soft and very sour and is the right base for sauerkraut soup.
  9. 9
    Cold-store the sauerkraut. Once the sauerkraut has reached the desired sourness, transfer the jar (with the weight removed and the lid sealed tightly) to the refrigerator (2 to 4 °C). Fermentation slows to a crawl but continues; the sauerkraut continues to develop complexity for the next 2 to 6 months and remains safe to eat indefinitely (the high acid and salt make it hostile to pathogens). Keeps 6 to 12 months refrigerated.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cabbage variety→red sauerkraut (Rotkraut)

    Substitute 2000 g red cabbage. The fermentation proceeds identically; the cabbage anthocyanins turn the brine and the cabbage a deep magenta-purple. Add 5 g / 1 tsp juniper berries and 1 small bay leaf to the salt-and-pound for the canonical German Rotkraut character. The finished product is sweeter than green sauerkraut and traditionally pairs with pork and goose.

  • Regional aromatic→Alsatian choucroute (caraway and juniper)

    Add 5 g caraway seeds plus 5 g / 1 tbsp juniper berries to the salt-and-pound, and add 2 bay leaves to the jar. The result is the choucroute base used in choucroute garnie (the Alsatian preparation with sausages, smoked pork, and white wine). Ferment for the full 21 days for the most developed character.

  • Regional fermentation duration→fresh-style cabbage slaw (curtido)

    Reduce the salt to 30 g (1.5%), add 100 g grated carrot and 50 g sliced white onion to the cabbage, and pack as in the main recipe. Ferment only 2 to 4 days at room temperature, then refrigerate. The result is the Salvadoran curtido, a quick lacto-fermented slaw served alongside pupusas. Significantly less sour and softer than full sauerkraut.

  • Regional aromatics→kvashenaya kapusta (Russian sauerkraut with apple and carrot)

    Add 100 g grated carrot and 1 grated apple (about 150 g) to the cabbage at the salt-and-pound stage. The apple sugars accelerate the initial fermentation and the carrot contributes color and a mild sweetness. The Russian-Polish style; serves cold alongside roasted meats and as a salad with sunflower oil.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • green cabbage → savoy cabbage, or Napa cabbage Savoy works at 1:1 with a slightly more delicate finished texture; the crinkled leaves ferment less compactly and produce a fluffier sauerkraut. Napa works but is structurally closer to kimchi cabbage; the result reads as a hybrid between sauerkraut and kimchi (without the chili).
  • kosher salt → fine sea salt or pickling salt Any non-iodized salt works at the same weight. Fine sea salt dissolves faster during the pound; pickling salt is essentially fine sea salt sold under a different name.
  • caraway seeds → fennel seeds, dill seeds, or juniper berries Each shifts the regional character: fennel seeds toward Italian, dill toward Eastern European, juniper toward Alsatian. All substitute at the same weight (5 g / 1 tsp).

Notes

The lacto-fermentation chemistry. Sauerkraut fermentation is driven by a three-stage succession of lactobacillus species native to the cabbage surface. Stage 1 (days 1 to 3) is dominated by Leuconostoc mesenteroides, a salt- and acid-sensitive species that consumes the easiest sugars and produces lactic acid, acetic acid, ethanol, and CO2 (the early-stage fizzy character). Stage 2 (days 3 to 10) is dominated by Lactobacillus plantarum, which is more salt- and acid-tolerant and pushes the pH steadily lower as the lactic acid accumulates. Stage 3 (week 2 onward) is dominated by Lactobacillus brevis and other late-stage species, which consume the residual sugars and develop the complex deeply-sour character of fully mature sauerkraut. The 2 to 2.5% salt range is the canonical sauerkraut window because it inhibits aerobic spoilage organisms while permitting the full lactobacillus succession.

Why the salt percentage matters. At less than 1.5% salt, the lacto-fermentation runs but aerobic spoilage bacteria (which die at salt levels above 2%) compete for the early-stage sugars and the kraut can develop off-flavors, sliminess, or mold. At more than 3% salt, the lactobacillus succession stalls in stage 1 and the kraut never develops past the early-fizzy stage; the finished product reads salty-and-flat. The 2 to 2.5% sweet spot is the result of generations of empirical European refinement.

The pound technique. Aggressive pounding/massaging the salted cabbage is the structural step that distinguishes proper sauerkraut from a quick salt-pickle. The pounding bursts the cell walls of the cabbage shreds, releasing the bound water from the vacuoles into the surrounding salt, which dissolves into a brine within 10 to 30 minutes. Insufficient pounding produces cabbage that does not release enough water to self-brine, requiring added water and producing a less concentrated, less complex sauerkraut. The 8 to 12 minute pound is the canonical home-scale technique; a traditional German Krautstampfer (wooden kraut-pounder) is the right tool but a clean fist works.

Temperature window. The lactobacillus succession is temperature-sensitive. At 18 to 20 °C the ferment runs slowly and develops the most complex character (the cooler temperature favors L. plantarum and the late-stage species); this is the ideal range. At 22 to 24 °C the ferment runs faster (1 week to drinkable, 2 weeks to fully sour) but with slightly less complexity. Above 25 °C the kraut can over-ferment and produce a soft, less complex sauerkraut with a slight off-character; below 16 °C the succession stalls in stage 1 and the kraut reads salty-fizzy rather than properly sour.

The "open crock vs sealed jar" decision. Traditional German and Eastern European sauerkraut is made in large open ceramic crocks, weighted with a stone slab, with a layer of brine on top that excludes air from the cabbage below. This is the classical method and produces excellent sauerkraut at scale (5 to 50 L batches). The home-scale modern equivalent is a glass jar with an airlock lid; this is more compact, more visually pleasing, easier to manage, and produces sauerkraut indistinguishable from the open-crock method at small scale. The open-crock method requires more attention (skimming any kahm yeast or surface mold that forms on the exposed brine surface) but rewards the patience with a slightly more complex, more "alive" sauerkraut.

Failure modes. White film on the brine (kahm yeast): harmless but off-flavored; skim immediately, press the cabbage back down, and refresh the airlock or cloth cover. Mold (fuzzy growth, any color): the cabbage was not fully submerged; if minor, skim and discard the top inch and continue, if extensive discard the batch. Soft, mushy sauerkraut: the ferment ran too warm, too long, or the cabbage was old; still safe to eat, use for sauerkraut soup or in cooked applications. No fizz or bubbles after 5 days: the room is too cold, the salt was iodized, or the water was chlorinated; check the variables and start over. Pink or red discoloration (in green sauerkraut): a sign of yeast contamination; discard.

Service and uses. Sauerkraut is a foundational Central European pantry item. Canonical uses: alongside roasted pork (Schweinebraten and the Bavarian classic Schweinshaxe), in choucroute garnie (the Alsatian feast with sausages and smoked pork), on Reuben sandwiches, with hot dogs and bratwurst, mixed into kielbasa-and-potato stews, in pierogi fillings, and as a side dish in its own right. The high probiotic content and the bright acid make it a digestive aid for rich, fatty meals; the Eastern European tradition of eating a small forkful of sauerkraut with every bite of pork or sausage is the canonical pairing.

Cooked vs raw. Raw sauerkraut retains the live lactobacillus cultures and the maximum acid character; cooking it (which most German preparations do, simmering in white wine and pork fat for the choucroute or in apple juice for the Rotkraut variant) kills the cultures but softens the texture and rounds the acid. Both have their place. For probiotic benefit and the brightest acid, eat raw or barely warmed; for traditional service alongside roasted meats, cook the sauerkraut in stock or wine for 30 to 60 minutes.

Storage. Refrigerated sauerkraut keeps 6 to 12 months easily and remains safe to eat for years (the acid and salt are hostile to pathogens). The texture stays crisp through about 6 months, then softens; the flavor continues to develop complexity for the first 2 to 3 months, then stabilizes.
