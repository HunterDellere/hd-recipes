Harira

The Moroccan iftar soup: a deeply spiced lamb-tomato base bolstered with chickpeas, brown lentils, and the canonical Moroccan thickener (tedouira, a slurry of flour-and-water that takes the soup from broth to silky-bodied without obscuring the spice work), finished with fresh cilantro and parsley and a generous squeeze of lemon. Served traditionally to break the Ramadan fast at sunset, alongside dates, chebakia, and hard-boiled eggs. The structural defining technique is the flour slurry; the structural defining flavor is the layered spice work plus the bright citrus finish.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Soak the chickpeas overnight (or quick-soak: cover with water, boil 2 minutes, off heat 1 hour). Unsoaked chickpeas never fully soften in the 90-minute cook and the soup reads chalky. Canned chickpeas work as a fallback (add at the lentil stage).
  • Brown lentils cook directly in the soup; no soaking required. They will be tender in 30 to 35 minutes.
  • Serve with lemon wedges, dates, and warm flatbread on the table. Harira is structurally a one-bowl meal but the iftar tradition is the soup-plus-dates-plus-bread trio.
  • Bloom the saffron 15 minutes before adding to the soup. Grind 0.3 g threads with a pinch of sugar, steep in 50 g hot water.

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, the soaks and saffron bloom

  1. 150 g dried chickpeas, soaked overnight in 1 L cold water, drained
  2. 0.3 g saffron threads, ground with a pinch of sugar in a mortar — (a generous pinch)
  3. 50 g hot water (not boiling), for saffron

Phase 2, the meat and aromatic base

  1. 400 g lamb shoulder or lamb stew meat, bone-in pieces, cut into 3 cm cubes (or use bone-in shanks cut into 2 to 3 pieces by the butcher; the bone enriches the broth)
  2. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  3. 250 g yellow onion, small dice (about 5 mm) — (1 large onion)
  4. 100 g celery stalks (with leaves), small dice (about 5 mm); reserve the leaves for the herb finish — (2 stalks; the celery is structural for the harira aromatic base, distinct from many Moroccan soups)
  5. 16 g garlic clove, peeled, finely minced — (4 cloves)
  6. 20 g fresh ginger, peeled and finely grated — (a 4 cm knob)
  7. 4 g ground turmeric
  8. 4 g ground cumin
  9. 3 g ground coriander
  10. 1 g ground cinnamon
  11. 2 g sweet paprika
  12. 3 g black pepper, freshly cracked
  13. 14 g kosher salt

Phase 3, the tomato base and braising liquid

  1. 1 × 28 oz / 794 g can canned crushed tomatoes (or 500 g fresh ripe, grated) — (1 standard can; structural for the soup body and color)
  2. 1 × 6 oz / 170 g can tomato paste
  3. 2500 g water (for the braise)
  4. 100 g brown or green lentils, rinsed and picked over for stones

Phase 4, the tedouira (flour slurry)

  1. 50 g all-purpose flour
  2. 200 g cold water (for the slurry)

Phase 5, the herb finish

  1. 30 g fresh cilantro, leaves and tender stems, finely chopped (small bunch) — (half goes into the soup at the end, half on top of the bowl at service)
  2. 20 g flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped — (joins the cilantro)
  3. 200 g fresh lemon, cut into wedges — (2 large lemons; the structural finishing acid; squeezed generously over each bowl at the table)

Execution

  1. 1
    Cook the soaked 150 g chickpeas: place in a pot with 1 L fresh water, bring to a simmer, cook 45 to 60 minutes until tender (chickpea bites cleanly without resistance). Drain and reserve. This pre-cook is structural; raw chickpeas added to the soup do not soften enough in the braise time.
  2. 2
    Bloom the saffron: grind 0.3 g saffron with a pinch of sugar; add 50 g hot water; steep 15 minutes.
  3. 3
    Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil in a large heavy pot (8-quart Dutch oven or stockpot) over medium-high heat. Add the 400 g lamb cubes; brown deeply on 2 sides, 6 to 8 minutes total. Reduce heat to medium.
  4. 4
    Add the 250 g / 1 onion (diced) and 100 g / 2 stalks celery (diced); sweat 8 to 10 minutes until softened. Add the 16 g / 4 garlic cloves and 20 g grated ginger; cook 60 seconds. Add the 4 g / 2 tsp turmeric, 4 g / 2 tsp cumin, 3 g / 1½ tsp coriander, 1 g / ½ tsp cinnamon, 2 g / 1 tsp paprika, and 3 g / 1½ tsp black pepper. Stir 45 seconds to bloom.
  5. 5
    Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp tomato paste; cook 90 seconds, stirring, until darkened. Add the 400 g crushed tomatoes, the bloomed saffron liquid, 8 g / 2 tsp of the kosher salt, and the 2500 g / 10 cups water. Bring to a steady simmer; cover partially.
  6. 6
    Simmer covered 60 minutes, stirring every 15 minutes, until the lamb is fork-tender and the soup has integrated.
  7. 7
    Add the 100 g lentils and the cooked chickpeas. Continue simmering covered 25 to 30 minutes until the lentils are tender (no chalk in the bite). Add the remaining 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt. Taste; the soup should read deeply spiced, lamb-savory, with tomato body and a faint warming cinnamon-ginger note. Adjust salt and pepper.
  8. 8
    Make the tedouira: in a small bowl, whisk the 50 g flour with the 200 g cold water until completely smooth (no lumps; the slurry should look like thin pancake batter). Whisking is structural; lumps in the slurry produce lumps in the soup.
  9. 9
    With the soup at a steady simmer, pour the slurry into the pot in a slow, steady stream while stirring constantly with a wooden spoon. The soup will thicken visibly within 90 seconds. Continue to stir for 3 to 5 minutes after the slurry is fully incorporated; the flour needs to cook through (taste; the slurry-cooked soup should not have any raw-flour note). The body should now be silky and coat the back of a spoon, not just water with chunks.
  10. 10
    Off heat, stir in half of the chopped 30 g cilantro and the 20 g parsley. Cover and let stand 5 minutes for the herbs to perfume the soup.
  11. 11
    Ladle into deep bowls. Top each bowl with a generous pinch of the remaining cilantro and parsley. Serve immediately with the 200 g lemon wedges (each diner squeezes generously over their bowl; the lemon is structural, not a garnish). For the canonical iftar service, accompany with dates, chebakia (sesame-honey pastry), hard-boiled eggs, and warm khobz or pita.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional vermicelli (zaaer) version→with broken vermicelli or rice instead of tedouira

    Skip the flour slurry entirely. In the last 15 minutes of cooking (after the lentils are tender), add 80 g of broken vermicelli noodles (or 60 g of rinsed long-grain rice). Cook until the noodles or rice are tender. The soup will thicken slightly from the starch released but stays chunky rather than silky. This is the southern Moroccan zaaer version; both forms are correct.

  • Dietary vegetarian variant→as vegetarian harira

    Skip the lamb entirely. Double the chickpeas (to 300 g dried, soaked and pre-cooked) and add 150 g of diced butternut squash with the lentils for body. Use vegetable stock in place of half the water for depth. The dish is structurally correct without the meat (harira is often vegetarian during Ramadan in households that observe stricter dietary practices) and reads cleaner and more legume-forward.

  • Protein with beef shank instead of lamb→with beef shank or short rib

    Substitute 400 g bone-in beef shank for the lamb. Extend the initial simmer to 90 minutes before adding the lentils; beef shank takes longer to break down. The dish reads heavier and slightly less aromatic than the lamb version; common in some northern Moroccan home cooking.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • lamb shoulder or lamb stew meat, bone-in pieces → boneless chicken thighs Substitute 500 g boneless skinless chicken thighs (cut into 3 cm pieces). Reduce the initial simmer to 30 minutes before adding the lentils. The dish reads lighter and less rich than the lamb version; an acceptable everyday compromise.
  • dried chickpeas → 1 drained 400 g can chickpeas Canned chickpeas work as a fallback; add at the lentil stage (skip the overnight soak and pre-cook). The dish reads slightly less rich (the chickpea cooking liquid in the dried-version pot contributes body) but is structurally correct.

Notes

The tedouira is structural. The flour slurry added at the end is what distinguishes harira from a generic Moroccan lamb-and-legume soup. The cold-water slurry, whisked into the simmering soup, gelatinizes the flour starches and creates a silky body that suspends the small particles (legume bits, herb pieces, micro-onion) so they no longer settle to the bottom of the bowl. The result is a soup with a unified, almost creamy mouthfeel without any dairy; the flour body carries the spice and acid in a way that thin water-based soups cannot. The whisk-smooth-into-cold-water step is critical; lumpy slurry produces lumpy soup, and the flour needs at least 3 to 5 minutes of cooking after addition to lose its raw-grain note.

Why the lemon is non-negotiable. Harira is built around a deeply spiced, slightly rich base; the bright citrus squeeze at the table is the structural counterpoint that lifts the soup from heavy to balanced. Without the lemon, the soup reads as a hearty winter stew; with the lemon, it reads as the carefully calibrated iftar dish it is. Always serve with extra wedges and instruct first-time diners to squeeze generously; the dish is built for it.

Versatility. The harira base (lamb-chickpea-lentil-tomato-spice) is the structural template for many North African legume soups. Adjust by region: add ras el hanout for a more aromatic version; add harissa for a Tunisian-leaning hot variant; swap the lamb for fish and add fennel for a coastal Moroccan version. The tedouira thickening technique transfers cleanly to any soup that wants silky body without dairy.

Pairs across Moroccan (canonical, especially Ramadan iftar), broader Maghreb (similar legume soups exist in Algeria and Tunisia under different names), and contemporary Moroccan diaspora cookery. The dish is also a common winter offering in French bistros where Moroccan technique has been absorbed.

Storage. Harira improves dramatically with a day in the refrigerator; the flavors integrate and the spice deepens. Keeps 5 days refrigerated and freezes 2 months. The flour body may break slightly on freezing; whisk back together when reheating. Reheat gently with a splash of water to loosen; the soup thickens significantly cold. The herb finish should be added fresh at each service, not stirred into the storage container (the herbs blacken and lose their bright character).

  • The Moroccan home canon. Harira is documented across Moroccan cookery and is the iconic dish for breaking the Ramadan fast at sunset (iftar); also served year-round for special occasions, family gatherings, and as a substantial weekday dinner. Recipes vary by family but the structural elements (lamb, chickpeas, lentils, tomato, ginger, saffron, flour slurry, herbs) are consistent. — Two structural decisions split harira recipes: (1) tedouira (flour slurry) vs zaaer (rice or vermicelli), the two thickening methods. Tedouira (flour slurry) is the canonical northern Moroccan choice and produces a silky-bodied soup; zaaer (broken vermicelli or rice added directly to the soup) is more common in the south and produces a chunkier-bodied soup. The version here uses tedouira. (2) The protein: bone-in lamb is the canonical Moroccan choice; chicken is acceptable; beef is uncommon. Many vegetarian harira variants exist (skip the meat, double the legumes).
