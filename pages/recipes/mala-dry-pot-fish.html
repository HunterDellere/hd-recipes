Mala dry-pot fish (gan guo yu)

Sichuan dry-pot. White fish fillet seared, then tossed in red oil rendered from doubanjiang and dried chilies, with lotus root, cauliflower, wood ear, firm tofu, tofu skin, bell pepper, scallion, and cilantro. No broth, no sauce: the rendered oil is the medium.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Rice is the partner and must start FIRST. Set jasmine or other long-grain rice to cook so it finishes when the wok toss does, dry-pot has no broth and the rice is the only liquid foil on the table.
  • Rehydrate the dried wood ear and tofu skin sticks BEFORE you start prep: 4 to 6 hours in cold water (or 30 minutes in hot water for wood ear, 1 hour for tofu skin). Half-rehydrated wood ear is rubbery and tofu skin will not absorb the red oil.
  • Press the firm tofu under a 1 kg weight for 30 minutes during prep so it holds cube shape through the toss. Start the press the moment you begin slicing vegetables.
  • The fish coat (velveting with cornstarch and egg white) needs 15 minutes to hydrate before the sear. Mix it early in prep so it rests while you par-cook the lotus root and cauliflower.

Execution

  1. 1
    Velvet the fish. Combine the 500 g fish chunks with 30 ml / 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine, 15 g smashed ginger, 4 g / ¾ tsp salt, and 1 egg white in a bowl. Massage gently for 30 seconds. Add 20 g / 2½ tbsp cornstarch and turn until each piece is evenly coated. Let stand 15 minutes; this velveting step is what keeps the fish from breaking apart in the high-heat toss at the end. Velveting
  2. 2
    Prep the vegetables. Drain the soaked lotus root from acid water. Rehydrate the 15 g wood ear mushrooms (4-hour cold-water soak or 30-min hot-water soak ahead of time) and tear into bite-size pieces. Drain the rehydrated 30 g tofu skin sticks and cut into 4 cm angled pieces. Press the firm tofu under a board with a 1 kg weight for 30 minutes if you have time; this helps it hold cube shape during the toss.
  3. 3
    Par-cook the lotus root and cauliflower. Bring a pot of salted water to a boil. Add the 250 g lotus root slices and cook 90 seconds; lift out with a spider and shock in ice water 30 seconds. Repeat with the 250 g cauliflower florets, cooking 2 minutes. Both should be just tender, still firm; the wok finish does the rest. Ice bath shock
  4. 4
    Sear the fish. Heat a wok or wide skillet with 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil over medium-high. Once the oil shimmers, add the velveted fish in a single layer. Sear 90 seconds without moving (the cornstarch coat will set), then flip each piece and sear 60 seconds on the second side. Lift out with a spider onto a plate; the fish is 80% cooked, the wok finish will set it through. Pour off any remaining oil and wipe the wok clean.
  5. 5
    Render the mala oil. Add the remaining 50 ml / ~3 tbsp neutral oil to the dry wok over medium heat (140 to 160°C / 285 to 320°F). Add the 40 g doubanjiang and cook, breaking up the paste with a spatula, for 90 seconds; the oil will turn brilliant brick-red and the kitchen will smell distinctly of chili-bean paste. Add the 20 g broken dried chilies and 5 g toasted Sichuan peppercorns, and cook 30 seconds until aromatic. Add the 15 g minced ginger and 20 g chopped garlic, and cook 30 seconds. Do not exceed medium heat; above 175°C the chili pigments scorch and the oil goes brown rather than red. Red oil rendering
  6. 6
    Build the dry-pot. Add the firm tofu cubes (250 g) and toss for 60 seconds in the red oil; the tofu absorbs the color and aroma. Add the par-cooked lotus root and cauliflower, then the 120 g rehydrated wood ear, the 120 g rehydrated tofu skin, and the 60 g scallion whites. Toss vigorously for 90 seconds over high heat; every piece should be glossed in the red oil.
  7. 7
    Season and add the bell pepper. Add the 15 ml / 1 tbsp light soy and the 6 g / ½ tbsp sugar, tossing to distribute. Add the 150 g bell pepper pieces and toss 60 seconds; the pepper should soften slightly but stay crisp. Return the seared fish to the wok with any juices from the plate, and toss gently 30 to 45 seconds, lifting from underneath rather than stirring aggressively to avoid breaking the fish. Add the 10 ml / 2 tsp Chinkiang vinegar around the rim of the wok and toss once.
  8. 8
    Finish and plate. Pull off heat. Add the 30 g cilantro and 20 g reserved scallion greens, drizzle the 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil over, and toss once. Transfer to a wide platter or to a wok-shaped serving dish set on a portable burner if you have one (the dry-pot tradition keeps the dish hot at the table through the meal). Serve immediately with steamed jasmine rice. Eat with chopsticks, picking through the components; the rendered oil at the bottom of the bowl is the deeply-flavored finish that the rice should be tossed with.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→shrimp or scallop

    Use 500 g peeled large shrimp (16/20 size) or sliced scallops in place of the fish. Velvet identically (Shaoxing, salt, egg white, cornstarch); sear 60 seconds per side instead of 90. The dish reads cleaner and slightly sweeter; the lotus-root-and-tofu structure is unchanged.

  • Protein protein→sliced beef or lamb

    Use 500 g flank steak or lamb shoulder, sliced 3 mm against the grain. Velvet 20 minutes (cornstarch + Shaoxing + soy + a pinch of baking soda for tenderness). Sear 90 seconds per side, slightly undercooked, finish in the toss. The dish becomes gan guo niu rou or gan guo yang rou; doubanjiang quantity often increases by 25% to balance the heavier protein.

  • Heat heat level→milder for non-Sichuan-eaters

    Halve the chao tian jiao (drop to 2.5 g) while keeping the erjingtiao constant at 15 g. Reduce Sichuan peppercorns to 3 g. The dish stays Sichuan-aromatic but the heat is moderate rather than aggressive. Do not reduce the doubanjiang itself; it is the structural flavor, not the primary heat.

  • Occasion vegetable mix→seasonal substitutions

    The lotus-root, cauliflower, wood-ear, bell-pepper combination is canonical but flexible. Acceptable substitutions: Chinese yam (shan yao) or daikon for lotus root; broccoli or yu choy for cauliflower; shimeji or oyster mushrooms for wood ear; celery or chinese celery for bell pepper. Keep the total vegetable weight to about 750 g and the fish:vegetable ratio at roughly 1:1.5.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • firm white fish → cod or halibut, premium options Cod is the supermarket default and works well. Halibut is firmer and reads slightly sweeter. Both hold up to the toss.
  • firm white fish → tilapia or basa, budget options Acceptable substitutes; the texture is softer and the fish breaks more easily. Sear with extra care and toss gently.
  • Pixian doubanjiang → no substitute See ingredients/doubanjiang. The dish loses its Sichuan identity without authentic Pixian doubanjiang.
  • Sichuan peppercorns → no good substitute The numbing-tingling quality (ma) is the entire reason "mala" exists as a category. Black peppercorns do not approximate it. If unavailable, the dish becomes a non-mala dry-pot, which is a different dish.
  • Chinkiang vinegar → rice vinegar + a few drops dark soy Chinkiang's aged-malty character is preferred; rice vinegar reads sharper. The dark soy adds back some of the depth.

Notes

Why dry-pot is structurally distinct from hot-pot. Hot-pot (huo guo) is broth-cooked at the table: a simmering pot of broth (often half plain, half mala) sits on a portable burner, and diners drop raw ingredients in to cook them as they eat. Dry-pot (gan guo) is wok-finished in the kitchen and arrives at the table fully cooked. The "dry" is literal: no broth, no sauce, the rendered red oil at the bottom of the wok is the entire liquid component. The portable burner that often accompanies dry-pot is to keep the dish hot through the meal, not to continue cooking. Mistaking dry-pot for hot-pot ruins both: putting raw ingredients into dry-pot at the table over-cooks them, and sealing dry-pot in a covered serving dish steam-cooks the components into mush.

The vegetable list is canonical, not arbitrary. Lotus root contributes structural starch and crisp bite; cauliflower contributes brassica sweetness and texture; wood ear contributes chewy mushroom umami without aggressive flavor; firm tofu contributes protein and absorbs the red oil; tofu skin sticks contribute sauce-absorbing chewiness; bell pepper contributes sweetness and color contrast; scallion and cilantro contribute the green-aromatic finish. Any one omission tilts the balance, but the recipe is forgiving enough that you can swap (broccoli for cauliflower, shimeji for wood ear) without losing the dish.

Why the mala-oil rendering happens after the fish sears. The seared fish needs a clean wok or it picks up bitter notes from the searing oil residue, and the doubanjiang rendering needs the wok at controlled medium heat. Searing first, wiping the wok, then rendering is the right sequence; rendering first and adding the fish later would put the velveted cornstarch coat directly into the chili-paste oil and the coat would peel off into the oil, breaking both the fish and the rendered oil.

The cilantro is non-optional. Sichuan dry-pot without cilantro reads heavy and one-dimensional; the cilantro's volatile citrus-grass aromatics cut the rendered oil and provide the fresh top-note that the dish requires. Add it off-heat at the very end. For a soap-taster diner, see ingredients/cilantro for substitution guidance, but be aware the dish reads less authentic without it.

Eating mechanics. Dry-pot is eaten with steamed white rice, picked component by component with chopsticks. The rice is essential: it carries the rendered oil and balances the heat. Each diner gets a rice bowl, transfers individual pieces from the dry-pot platter onto the rice, and eats from the bowl. Pour the residual oil at the bottom of the dry-pot over the rice when the components are mostly gone; it is the deepest-flavored part of the dish. A non-spicy side dish (a stir-fried green vegetable, a cold smashed cucumber salad) and a clear soup (egg drop, daikon-and-pork-bone) round out the meal.
