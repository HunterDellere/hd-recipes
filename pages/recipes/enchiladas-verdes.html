Recipe

Enchiladas verdes (green-tomatillo enchiladas with chicken)

Corn tortillas briefly pan-fried to soft pliability, dipped in a charred-tomatillo salsa verde, filled with shredded poached chicken, rolled tight, topped with more salsa verde, crema, and crumbled queso fresco, finished under the broiler for 90 seconds. The everyday Mexican central-state enchilada, structurally distinct from the red mole-or-chili-paste enchiladas rojas, and arguably the most accessible Mexican dinner to make at home.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Char the tomatillos and chiles FIRST. The structural premise of the dish is the charred-tomatillo salsa verde; raw or boiled tomatillos produce a flatter sauce. The char takes 10 minutes under the broiler and is the structural step.
  • Pre-fry the tortillas in hot oil for 8 to 10 seconds per side. This is non-negotiable; raw corn tortillas tear and fall apart when dipped in hot sauce. The pre-fry softens them to pliable and seals the starch so they can hold the filling without dissolving.

Phase 1, the chicken

  1. 600 g boneless skinless chicken thighs (or breasts)
  2. 1500 ml water (for poaching)
  3. 100 g yellow onion, halved — (½ medium; for the poaching liquid)
  4. 15 g garlic cloves, smashed — (4 cloves; for the poaching liquid)
  5. 12 g kosher salt
  6. 2 each bay leaf

Phase 2, the salsa verde

  1. 700 g fresh tomatillos, husked and rinsed — (about 12 medium; the structural fruit; canned tomatillos are an acceptable shortcut, see substitutions)
  2. 50 g jalapeño peppers — (2 jalapeños; seeded for less heat, or kept whole for canonical Mexican heat level)
  3. 10 g serrano peppers (for more heat, optional) — (1 chile; can be omitted for milder sauce)
  4. 100 g white onion, halved — (½ medium)
  5. 15 g garlic cloves, peeled — (4 cloves)
  6. 30 g fresh cilantro (stems and leaves) — (1 small bunch; the structural Mexican aromatic)
  7. 200 ml chicken poaching liquid
  8. 4 g kosher salt
  9. 30 g neutral oil (for cooking the salsa)

Phase 3, assembling and finishing

  1. 12 each corn tortillas (5 to 6 inch) — (12 tortillas; fresh from a Mexican grocery or homemade; supermarket "soft taco" flour tortillas are NOT a substitute)
  2. 100 ml neutral oil (for pre-frying the tortillas)
  3. 150 g Mexican crema (or sour cream thinned with 2 tbsp milk)
  4. 100 g queso fresco, crumbled
  5. 60 g white onion, finely diced — (¼ medium; for finishing)
  6. 15 g fresh cilantro, chopped — (small handful)

Execution

  1. 1
    Poach the chicken. Combine the 600 g chicken, 1500 ml water, 100 g halved onion, 15 g smashed garlic, 12 g salt, and 2 bay leaves in a large saucepan. Bring to a simmer over medium-high heat; reduce to medium-low, cover, simmer 18 to 20 minutes until the chicken reaches 75°C / 165°F internal. Lift the chicken out; let cool 5 minutes. Reserve 200 ml of the poaching liquid for the salsa; discard the aromatics. Shred the chicken with two forks into bite-sized strips.
  2. 2
    Char the tomatillos and chiles. Arrange the 700 g husked tomatillos, 50 g jalapeños, and optional 10 g serrano on a sheet pan in a single layer. Broil on the top rack for 6 to 8 minutes, until the tomatillos are blistered, blackened in spots, and have collapsed slightly, and the chiles are charred on the upper side. Flip everything; broil another 3 to 4 minutes until the second side is similarly charred. The char is the structural step that distinguishes salsa verde asada from the raw or boiled versions. Maillard sear
  3. 3
    Blend the salsa. Transfer the charred tomatillos and chiles (with any juices from the pan) to a blender. Add the 100 g raw halved onion, 15 g raw garlic, 30 g cilantro, 200 ml reserved chicken poaching liquid, and 4 g salt. Blend on high for 60 seconds until smooth but with some texture remaining (a sauce, not a puree).
  4. 4
    Fry the salsa. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide saucepan over medium-high heat until shimmering. Pour the blended salsa into the hot oil (it will spit aggressively; this is the canonical Mexican "freír la salsa" technique that concentrates the salsa and develops Maillard depth). Cook 5 to 6 minutes, stirring, until the salsa has darkened in color from bright green to deeper green-olive, has thickened slightly, and the kitchen smells deeply of tomatillo. Taste; adjust salt. Reserve warm. Red oil rendering
  5. 5
    Mix the filling. In a bowl, combine the shredded chicken with about 100 ml of the warm salsa verde (just enough to moisten and flavor the chicken; the chicken should be lightly coated, not swimming). Taste; adjust salt if needed.
  6. 6
    Heat the tortilla-frying oil. In a small skillet, heat the 100 ml / ½ cup neutral oil to 175°C / 350°F (a small piece of tortilla dropped in should sizzle vigorously and float).
  7. 7
    Pre-fry the tortillas. Working one at a time, slide a corn tortilla into the hot oil; fry 8 to 10 seconds per side, just until the tortilla goes pliable and lightly translucent (do NOT fry to crisp; the goal is soft pliable tortilla, not chips). Lift with tongs; drain briefly on paper towels. Repeat for all 12 tortillas. Stack the pre-fried tortillas under a kitchen towel to keep warm and pliable.
  8. 8
    Dip and roll the enchiladas. Working one at a time, dip a pre-fried tortilla into the warm salsa verde in the saucepan, coating both sides (use tongs; the sauce will be hot). Lay the salsa-dipped tortilla on a plate or work surface; spoon about 30 g of the chicken filling in a line along the center; roll tightly into a cigar shape. Transfer seam-side down to a serving plate or baking dish. Repeat for all 12 tortillas, arranging 3 per plate (or 12 across a single serving dish).
  9. 9
    Finish and serve. Spoon the remaining warm salsa verde generously over the rolled enchiladas (the rolls should be fully bathed, not just topped). Drizzle the 150 g Mexican crema in zigzag lines across the salsa. Scatter the 100 g crumbled queso fresco, 60 g finely diced white onion, and 15 g chopped cilantro across the top. Serve immediately. The enchiladas should be hot, the salsa thick and vibrant, the crema and cheese providing cooling contrast against the green-chile heat.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional protein→enchiladas suizas (with cream sauce, baked, the Mexico City version)

    After rolling the enchiladas, place them in a baking dish. Top with the salsa verde mixed with 200 g additional Mexican crema (or heavy cream) and 200 g shredded Monterey Jack or Oaxaca cheese. Bake at 200°C / 400°F for 18 minutes until bubbling and lightly browned on top. The dish becomes enchiladas suizas, a richer creamier version invented at a Mexico City restaurant called Sanborns in the 1940s.

  • Protein protein→with carnitas instead of poached chicken

    Replace the poached chicken filling with 500 g of carnitas (slow-braised pork shoulder, shredded). Use vegetable stock or water as the salsa verde liquid (since you will not have chicken poaching liquid). The dish reads richer and more aggressively flavored.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→enchiladas rojas (with red-chili sauce instead of salsa verde)

    Replace the salsa verde with a red-chili sauce: 50 g dried guajillo and 20 g dried ancho chiles (toasted, soaked, blended with onion, garlic, cumin, oregano, and stock). Fry the sauce the same way. The dish becomes enchiladas rojas; structurally distinct in flavor profile but mechanically the same technique.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • fresh tomatillos → canned tomatillos, drained (use the same weight) See ingredients/tomatillo. Canned tomatillos are the standard pantry backup; they cannot be charred (already cooked through canning), so the resulting salsa is salsa verde cocida rather than salsa verde asada and reads slightly flatter. Acceptable when fresh tomatillos are unavailable.
  • Mexican crema → sour cream thinned with 2 tbsp whole milk + a pinch of salt Mexican crema is structurally thinner and slightly tangier than American sour cream; the thinning approximates the consistency. The dish reads recognizably similar.
  • corn tortillas (5 to 6 inch) → corn tortillas (homemade from masa harina) See recipes/corn-tortillas-from-masa. Fresh homemade tortillas are dramatically better and are the structural canonical choice; well-made fresh tortillas have flexibility that supermarket tortillas lack, often without the pre-fry step needed.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → corn tortillas (5 to 6 inch) make it yourself Fresh corn tortillas made from masa are dramatically more flavorful and more flexible than supermarket tortillas; the difference is significant in any dish where the tortilla is the structural carrier of sauce.
  • → chicken poaching liquid make it yourself If poaching chicken specifically for this recipe is too much work, a quality homemade chicken stock makes a fine substitute for the poaching liquid that flavors the salsa.

Notes

Charred vs raw vs boiled tomatillos. Salsa verde has three canonical preparations distinguished by how the tomatillos are treated: cruda (raw, blended raw with the chiles and aromatics, the brightest and most acidic version), cocida (boiled 5 to 8 minutes, then blended, the gentlest and most everyday version), and asada (charred under a broiler or in a comal, then blended, the canonical version with the most depth). This recipe uses asada because the char-and-fry sequence develops Maillard products that the boiled or raw versions cannot match. The asada version is structurally what defines an excellent enchilada verde from a merely correct one.

Freír la salsa is structural. The Mexican technique of frying the blended salsa in hot oil (freír la salsa) before using it is non-negotiable. The hot-oil contact develops new Maillard products in the salsa, concentrates the flavor by driving off water, and changes the color from bright green to deeper green-olive. Salsa verde used without the fry is structurally a different sauce; it reads brighter but flatter, with no depth. Cooks who skip this step are making a salsa for chips, not a sauce for enchiladas.

Why pre-fry the tortillas. The brief 8 to 10 second oil dip is what allows the tortilla to hold its shape when dipped in hot sauce. Raw corn tortillas have surface starches that dissolve immediately in hot liquid, causing the tortilla to tear and fall apart. The brief oil pre-fry seals the starches and softens the tortilla into a pliable wrap that can be rolled tight and survives the salsa bath without disintegrating. The technique is canonical across Mexican enchilada preparations.

Fresh tortillas vs supermarket. Real Mexican corn tortillas (from a Mexican grocery, ideally made same-day) have the structural flexibility to roll without cracking after a brief pre-fry. Supermarket "taco" tortillas (especially the larger 6 to 8 inch ones) are often stale and crack when rolled even after pre-frying. The accessible solution is to buy small 5 to 6 inch tortillas from a Mexican grocery, where turnover is fast and the tortillas are fresh. Flour tortillas are NOT a substitute; the dish becomes a burrito, structurally distinct from an enchilada.

Storage. Best served immediately; the salsa-soaked tortillas soften progressively over 30 minutes. Components can be made ahead: salsa verde keeps 4 days refrigerated, shredded chicken keeps 3 days refrigerated, tortillas keep 1 week refrigerated. Assemble and serve fresh. Do not assemble in advance; the dish becomes a soggy casserole.
