Recipe

Stuffed lotus root with pork

Lotus root rings with seasoned ground pork pressed into the air channels, pan-seared to set, then braised in soy-aromatic broth until the lotus softens and the pork has bound the rings together. The Cantonese braised version; Hunan deep-fries the same construction.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Steamed jasmine rice is the partner and must be on FIRST. Rinse 360 g / 1¾ cups rice three times, start in the cooker (or stovetop covered at a bare simmer 18 minutes, then 10 minute rest off heat). Total rice time of about 45 minutes overlaps the entire stuff-sear-braise sequence so both arrive together.
  • Mix the vinegar water (1500 ml water plus 15 ml white vinegar) before you start peeling lotus root. Cut lotus oxidizes to brown within 2 to 3 minutes of exposure; each round must hit the acid water the moment it leaves the knife.
  • Plan for 35 to 40 minutes of passive braise time after the active stuff-and-sear. The total active window is about 40 minutes, but the braise is hands-off; this is the gap to set the table, make a non-meat side, or hold dinner if guests are running late.
  • Stir the pork filling vigorously in one direction for 60 to 90 seconds. The single-direction stir develops the meat protein into a cohesive bind; alternating directions or under-mixing produces filling that falls out of the holes during the sear.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, lotus root prep

  1. 600 g lotus root (soup-grade, starchier segments) — (2 medium tubes; ideally the middle and base segments rather than the tip)
  2. 1500 ml cold water
  3. 15 ml white vinegar

Phase 2, pork filling

  1. 350 g ground pork (80/20 fat ratio)
  2. 12 g fresh ginger, finely minced — (1 inch knob)
  3. 30 g scallion whites, finely chopped — (2-3 scallions; reserve greens for finish)
  4. 15 ml Shaoxing wine
  5. 15 ml light soy sauce
  6. 4 g kosher salt
  7. 4 g granulated sugar
  8. 1 each large egg, beaten
  9. 12 g cornstarch
  10. 5 ml toasted sesame oil
  11. 0.5 g white pepper, freshly ground

Phase 3, sear and braise

  1. 30 ml neutral oil (rice bran, peanut, or canola)
  2. 15 g fresh ginger, sliced — (1 inch knob, sliced thin)
  3. 20 g scallion whites, cut into 4 cm lengths — (2 scallions)
  4. 1 each star anise
  5. 15 g rock sugar
  6. 30 ml light soy sauce
  7. 8 ml dark soy sauce
  8. 30 ml Shaoxing wine
  9. 500 ml water or unsalted chicken stock

Phase 4, finish

  1. 15 g reserved scallion greens, sliced — (reserved from Phase 2)
  2. 8 g cornstarch, mixed with 30 ml / 2 tbsp water

Execution

  1. 1
    Prep the lotus root. Combine the 1500 ml / 6 cups water and 15 ml / 1 tbsp white vinegar in a large bowl. Peel the 600 g lotus root with a vegetable peeler, removing the dark skin and any blemishes. Trim the bumpy ends square. Slice each tube into 1 cm thick rounds (you should get about 24 to 30 rounds across the two tubes). Drop the rounds into the acid water as you cut to prevent oxidation; lotus root browns within minutes of being cut.
  2. 2
    Mix the pork filling. In a medium bowl, combine the 350 g ground pork, 12 g minced ginger, 30 g chopped scallion whites, 15 ml / 1 tbsp Shaoxing, 15 ml / 1 tbsp light soy, 4 g / ¾ tsp salt, 4 g / 1 tsp sugar, 1 beaten egg, 12 g / 1½ tbsp cornstarch, 5 ml / 1 tsp sesame oil, and 0.5 g / ¼ tsp white pepper. Stir vigorously in one direction with chopsticks or a rubber spatula for 60 to 90 seconds; the filling should pull together into a sticky, paste-like mass. The repeated single-direction stirring is what develops the meat protein into the cohesive bind that holds the stuffing in the lotus root through the braise.
  3. 3
    Stuff the lotus rounds. Drain the lotus rounds and pat dry with a kitchen towel. Working with one round at a time, press the pork filling into each air channel with a small spoon or your fingertip, forcing the filling all the way through and slightly overflowing on both faces of the round. Smear the excess filling across both faces; this is what binds the pork-and-lotus into a sealed unit during the sear. Continue with all rounds; you should use about 280 to 320 g of filling, depending on hole-size on your specific lotus root.
  4. 4
    Sear. Heat a wide skillet or wok over medium-high heat with the 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil. Sear the stuffed lotus rounds in batches (do not crowd) for 2 minutes per side, until the pork-faces are golden brown and the lotus shows light browning at the edges. The sear is structural: the egg-and-cornstarch binder sets, locking the pork into the rings. Pull each round to a plate as it finishes.
  5. 5
    Build the braise. In the same skillet, add the 15 g sliced ginger, 20 g scallion whites, and 1 star anise. Cook 30 seconds until aromatic. Add the 15 g rock sugar and stir until it begins to dissolve and lightly caramelizes (60 seconds). Add the 30 ml / 2 tbsp light soy, 8 ml / ½ tbsp dark soy, 30 ml / 2 tbsp Shaoxing, and 500 ml / 2 cups water or stock. Bring to a simmer.
  6. 6
    Braise. Return the seared lotus rounds to the skillet, arranging them in a single layer (use a wide pan; if necessary braise in two batches and combine). The liquid should come 2/3 up the sides of the rounds; if not, add more water. Cover, reduce heat to maintain a bare simmer, and cook 35 to 40 minutes. The 600 g lotus root will turn from pale ivory to a deep tan-brown as it absorbs the braising liquid; the pork will be fully cooked through; the rounds will be tender enough to pierce easily with a chopstick.
  7. 7
    Finish. Uncover and turn each round once carefully (use a spatula or spider, not tongs, which crush the structure). Increase heat to medium-high and reduce the braising liquid by half, about 4 to 6 minutes; the sauce should coat the back of a spoon. Whisk the 8 g cornstarch with 30 ml / 2 tbsp water and add to the pan, swirling continuously; the sauce will thicken to a glossy glaze coating each round.
  8. 8
    Plate. Transfer the lotus rounds to a serving platter, spoon the glaze over, sprinkle with the 15 g reserved scallion greens. Serve hot with steamed jasmine rice. Each diner takes a few rounds onto their rice; pour any extra sauce over the rice.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional regional style→Hunan deep-fried (zha ou jia)

    After stuffing the rounds, dip each in a thin batter (60 g flour + 60 g cornstarch + 1 egg + 100 ml cold water + a pinch of salt, whisked smooth). Deep-fry at 175°C / 350°F for 5 to 6 minutes per round until deeply golden. Skip the braise entirely. Serve with a Sichuan-style dipping sauce (3 tbsp Chinkiang vinegar + 1 tbsp light soy + 1 tsp sugar + 1 tsp chili oil). The Hunan version reads crispy-outside, juicy-inside, no glaze.

  • Regional regional style→Sichuan numbing-spicy braise

    After the sear, render 25 g doubanjiang in 2 tbsp oil before adding aromatics; render until oil is brilliant red (90 seconds). Add 8 g dried chilies (broken) and 3 g toasted Sichuan peppercorns to the aromatic base. Drop the rock sugar to 8 g (the doubanjiang already contributes savor). Otherwise braise as written. The dish reads mala-leaning rather than soy-aromatic.

  • Protein protein→mixed pork-and-shrimp filling

    Use 250 g ground pork plus 100 g finely chopped shrimp (chopped to a coarse paste). Add 5 ml / 1 tsp fish sauce to the filling. The shrimp contributes sweetness and binds even more aggressively than pork alone; the dish becomes the Cantonese seafood-version stuffed lotus.

  • Occasion occasion→cold appetizer (liang cai)

    Braise as written. Cool the lotus rounds completely in the braising liquid, then refrigerate overnight in the liquid. Slice each round in half before serving so the pork center shows. Serve cold with a drizzle of the cooled braising liquid (now thickened to almost a glaze by gelatin from the pork). The cold-set version reads denser and more like Cantonese soy-eggs in flavor profile.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • lotus root (soup-grade) → lotus root (any grade available) The starchier soup-grade absorbs braising liquid better. Crisp-grade works but the lotus reads firmer at the end of the braise. If only one type is available, use what you have and adjust braise time: starchier needs 35-40 min, crisper needs 45 min for the same texture.
  • ground pork (80/20) → ground pork (regular, ~70/30) Slightly fattier; the filling binds easier but the dish reads richer. Acceptable; reduce sesame oil to 3 ml.
  • rock sugar → granulated sugar Use 12 g granulated. Rock sugar produces a cleaner sweetness and a glossier braise; granulated works but the dish reads slightly more sugary.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → water or unsalted chicken stock make it yourself Chicken stock instead of water deepens the braise audibly. Homemade chicken stock is a 4-hour project but the end result is dramatically better than store-bought boxed stock.

Notes

Why the single-direction stir matters. Mixing the pork filling by repeatedly stirring in one direction (clockwise or counterclockwise, but not both) develops the myosin proteins in the meat into long aligned strands that bind the filling into a cohesive paste. Mixing in alternating directions or stirring randomly produces a looser filling that falls out of the lotus during searing and braising. This is the same principle behind Chinese meat dumplings, fish balls, and meatball-style preparations across Asian cooking; it is the technique that distinguishes restaurant-quality fillings from home-cook attempts.

Why the soup-grade lotus. The middle and base segments of a lotus root tube are higher in starch and softer-cooking; they soak braising liquid more deeply and produce a more harmonious bite where the lotus and pork have integrated. The crisp-grade tip segments hold their crunch through a 35-minute braise and produce a textural mismatch (crunchy lotus, soft pork). Either works; the soup-grade is the canonical choice.

Eating mechanics. The dish is rich and structured; serve it as one component of a multi-dish meal rather than as a standalone. Pair with a stir-fried green vegetable (bok choy with garlic, gai lan with oyster sauce), a clear soup (winter melon and pork-bone, or daikon and pork-rib), and steamed rice. Each diner takes 2 to 3 rounds onto their rice; the sauce that pools at the bottom of the rice bowl is part of the dish. Reheats well the next day; in fact often improves overnight as the lotus continues absorbing the braising liquid.
