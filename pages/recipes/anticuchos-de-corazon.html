Anticuchos de corazón

The Peruvian beef heart skewer: trimmed cubed heart marinated overnight in ají panca, garlic, vinegar, cumin, and oregano, then skewered and grilled hot and fast over coals until the surface chars and the interior reads tender. The defining street food of Lima and one of the great offal preparations of the world.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The beef heart needs an overnight marinade for the ají panca to penetrate the dense muscle. Plan the day before.
  • Beef heart needs aggressive trimming. A whole heart is about 60% usable lean muscle; the remaining 40% is silver-skin, large blood vessels, valves, and fat caps that must be removed. Allow 15 minutes of trim time.
  • If using bamboo skewers, soak in cold water 30 minutes before threading (prevents burning on the grill).
  • The grill must be very hot. Anticuchos are cooked fast (90 seconds per side) over high heat to char the surface while keeping the interior tender. A hot-charcoal grill is structural; a gas grill on high also works.

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, trim and cube the heart

  1. 1000 g beef heart, whole or pre-trimmed, trim away silver-skin, blood vessels, valves, fat caps; cube the lean muscle into 3 cm pieces (the trimmed yield should be about 600 g)

Phase 2, the marinade

  1. 80 g ají panca paste
  2. 30 g garlic cloves, peeled, roughly chopped — (7 to 8 cloves; aggressive garlic load is structural)
  3. 60 g red wine vinegar
  4. 45 g neutral oil
  5. 4 g ground cumin
  6. 3 g dried oregano
  7. 9 g fine sea salt
  8. 3 g black pepper, freshly ground

Phase 3, the skewers

  1. 400 g small waxy potatoes (Peruvian yellow or new potatoes), boiled whole in salted water 15 minutes until tender, then halved (or quartered if larger) — (about 8 small potatoes, halved gives 16 pieces, 2 per skewer; the structural Peruvian companion on the skewer)
  2. 200 g choclo (Peruvian large-kernel corn) or sweet corn, cooked corn on the cob, cut into 3 cm rounds (about 8 rounds total) — (2 medium ears cooked, cut; 1 round per skewer; canonical Peruvian addition; canned choclo also works)
  3. 8 each bamboo or metal skewers — (8 skewers, about 25 cm long; soak bamboo skewers 30 minutes before use)

Phase 4, the ají sauce (the canonical anticucho dip)

  1. 30 g ají amarillo paste (for the sauce)
  2. 80 g mayonnaise
  3. 15 g fresh lime juice
  4. 6 g fresh cilantro, chopped (for the sauce)
  5. 4 g garlic clove (for the sauce), finely grated on a microplane — (1 small clove)

Phase 5, the finish

  1. 60 g lime, cut into wedges — (2 limes, 8 wedges)

Execution

  1. 1
    Phase 1, trim the heart. On a clean cutting board, trim the 1000 g whole beef heart aggressively: remove the silver-skin fascia (the iridescent connective tissue), the large blood vessels (cylindrical tubes running through the muscle), the valves (firm flap structures), and the fat caps (yellow-white deposits). The remaining lean muscle should be deep red and uniform; the trimmed yield should be about 600 g. Cube the lean muscle into 3 cm pieces, cutting across the grain (the structural prep that produces tender bites; cutting with the grain produces tough fibrous bites).
  2. 2
    Phase 2, mix the marinade and marinate. In a blender, combine the 80 g ají panca paste, 30 g garlic, 60 g red wine vinegar, 45 g neutral oil, 4 g cumin, 3 g dried oregano, 9 g salt, and 3 g black pepper. Blend 60 seconds until smooth and aromatic. Place the cubed heart in a bowl or zip-top bag; pour the marinade over; mix to coat each piece. Refrigerate 12 to 24 hours; the long marinade is the structural step that penetrates the dense heart muscle.12 h
  3. 3
    Phase 3, prepare the accompaniments. While the heart marinates, boil the 400 g small potatoes whole in salted water 15 minutes until tender; drain, cool, and halve. Cook the 200 g corn on the cob in salted boiling water 5 minutes; drain, cool, and cut into 3 cm rounds. Both the potato and corn are pre-cooked because the high-heat grill cooks the heart in 90 seconds per side, not enough time to cook raw potato or corn.
  4. 4
    Mix the ají sauce. In a small bowl, whisk together the 30 g ají amarillo paste, 80 g mayonnaise, 15 g lime juice, 6 g chopped cilantro, and 4 g grated garlic. Taste and adjust salt if needed; the sauce should read bright, creamy, and chili-spicy. Refrigerate until service.
  5. 5
    Phase 4, skewer the components. Pull the marinated heart from the refrigerator; thread onto skewers, alternating with potato halves and corn rounds. The canonical Peruvian skewer pattern: 1 potato half, 3 to 4 heart cubes, 1 corn round, 3 to 4 more heart cubes, 1 potato half. Reserve about 50 g of the marinade for basting (do not reuse marinade that touched the raw heart for any other purpose).
  6. 6
    Phase 5, grill the skewers. Heat a charcoal or gas grill to very high heat (250 to 290°C / 480 to 550°F at the grate). Brush the grates lightly with neutral oil. Place the skewers on the grill perpendicular to the bars; grill 90 seconds undisturbed until the surface chars and grill marks form. Flip; brush the seared side with reserved marinade; grill 90 seconds on the second side. Turn 90 degrees; grill 60 seconds on the third side; flip and grill 60 seconds on the fourth side. The total grill time is 4.5 to 5 minutes; the heart should read tender (just past the rare stage, similar to a medium-rare steak) and the surface should be deeply charred. Do not overcook; beef heart turns tough quickly past the medium mark.
  7. 7
    Rest and serve. Transfer the skewers to a plate; rest 3 minutes (the heart juices redistribute and the texture firms slightly). Serve with the ají sauce in a small bowl on the side for dipping; the 60 g lime wedges go on each plate for squeezing over the skewers. The canonical Peruvian anticucho service includes a Pisco sour or a tall glass of chicha morada (Peruvian purple corn drink) on the side.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→as anticuchos de carne (with beef sirloin instead of heart)

    Replace the beef heart with 600 g beef sirloin or flat-iron steak (cubed the same 3 cm). Marinate 4 to 6 hours (skeletal muscle absorbs marinade faster than dense heart and over-marinates beyond that window). Grill the same way; the steak version reads as a more accessible Peruvian skewer for cooks new to offal. Canonical and acceptable adaptation; the dish is no longer anticuchos de corazón but is structurally still anticuchos.

  • Protein protein→as anticuchos de pollo (chicken thigh version)

    Replace the heart with 600 g boneless skinless chicken thighs (cubed). Marinate 4 hours. Grill 2 minutes per side until the internal temperature reads 74°C / 165°F. The chicken version reads as a lighter Peruvian skewer and is a common adaptation in restaurants serving the canonical and the accessible alongside.

  • Occasion cuisine pivot→as anticuchos with a peanut sauce (the modern restaurant adaptation)

    Replace the ají sauce dip with a peanut sauce: blend 100 g toasted peanuts, 30 g ají amarillo paste, 60 g chicken stock, 30 g cilantro, 15 g lime juice, 3 g salt to a smooth thick sauce. The peanut version is a modern restaurant adaptation that has become canonical at upscale Peruvian establishments; structurally complete and pairs especially well with the heart.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • beef heart, whole or pre-trimmed → beef sirloin or flat-iron steak Beef heart is structural to canonical anticuchos and is the canonical Peruvian preparation. Sirloin or flat-iron steak is the standard substitute for cooks or guests uncomfortable with offal; marinate 4 to 6 hours (less than the heart) and grill the same way. The dish reads recognizably similar but with a different protein character.
  • ají panca paste → a 50/50 blend of ancho chili paste and chipotle in adobo Ají panca is structural to canonical anticuchos; the ancho-chipotle blend approximates the smoky-fruity character with a slightly more aggressive smoke profile. Acceptable substitute when ají panca is unavailable.

Notes

Anticuchos de corazón are the structural Lima street food and one of the great offal preparations of the world. The dish elevates beef heart from a humble offal cut to a celebrated protein through three structural techniques: aggressive trim (removing the silver-skin and connective tissue), long marinade (12 to 24 hours in ají panca and acid to flavor and tenderize the dense muscle), and very-hot fast grill (90 seconds per side to char the surface without overcooking the interior). Master anticuchos and the structural offal cookery technique transfers to chicken hearts, lamb hearts, kidney, sweetbreads, and other dense muscle organs.

The trim is structural. A whole beef heart is about 60% usable lean muscle; the remaining 40% is silver-skin (the iridescent connective tissue that does not soften with cooking), large blood vessels (cylindrical tubes running through the muscle that read rubbery), valves (firm flap structures), and fat caps (yellow-white deposits). Failure to trim aggressively produces tough chewy anticuchos that no marinade can rescue. Allow 15 minutes of trim time; ask a Latin American butcher to do the trim if you are uncomfortable with the technique.

The 24-hour marinade is structural. The dense heart muscle is structurally more compact than skeletal muscle and absorbs marinade more slowly; the 12-hour minimum is required for the ají panca to penetrate, and the 24-hour marinade is the canonical Peruvian preparation that produces the deepest flavor. The acid in the marinade (red wine vinegar) starts to denature the surface protein and tenderize the muscle; the cumin and oregano contribute structural Peruvian flavor; the ají panca brings the smoky-fruity character that defines the dish.

The very-hot fast grill is structural. Beef heart cooks fast at high heat and turns tough quickly past the medium mark. The structural cook is 90 seconds per side over very high heat (250 to 290°C / 480 to 550°F); this chars the surface (Maillard browning) while keeping the interior tender (just past rare, similar to medium-rare steak). Slower cooking at lower heat produces dry tough anticuchos; the high-heat fast cook is non-negotiable for the structural anticucho texture.

The potato-and-corn-on-the-skewer is structural to the Peruvian preparation. Lima street vendors thread small boiled potatoes and corn rounds onto the skewer with the heart; the structural Peruvian skewer pattern alternates the protein with the starch, and the diner eats them together as a structurally complete street meal. Skewers without the potato and corn read as offal-only and miss the structural Peruvian completeness. Pre-cook both (the high-heat fast grill does not have time to cook raw potato or corn).

The ají sauce dip is structural. Lima anticucho vendors serve a small dish of creamy ají amarillo sauce on the side; the diner dips each piece of grilled heart before eating. The sauce adds structural fresh heat and creamy contrast to the smoky grilled heart and is canonical to the street service. Skipping the sauce reads as structurally incomplete; the canonical Peruvian eats the anticuchos with the sauce.

Versatility. The anticucho marinade architecture (ají panca, garlic, vinegar, cumin, oregano) transfers to: chicken hearts (anticuchos de pollo, smaller skewers, faster grill), lamb hearts (excellent and slightly milder than beef), beef sirloin (anticuchos de carne), and even firm tofu (vegetarian anticuchos, marinate 6 hours, grill hot and fast). The grill technique transfers to most dense muscle cuts that benefit from char-and-quick-cook. Beyond anticuchos, the ají panca marinade works as a structural base for grilled chicken thighs, pork shoulder for slow cooking (anticucho-spiced pulled pork), and as a glaze for grilled salmon.

Storage. Marinated raw heart keeps 36 hours refrigerated; do not exceed 48 hours (the vinegar denatures the surface too aggressively and produces mealy texture). Cooked anticuchos keep 3 days refrigerated; reheat briefly in a hot skillet or on the grill to avoid drying out. The dish is best fresh; the long marinade is the make-ahead step, the grill is the day-of step.

Sources
  • The Peruvian canon. Anticuchos have pre-Columbian roots (the Quechua word anti-uchu means dish from the Andes); the modern stabilized form (marinated beef heart skewers grilled over coals) hardened in the colonial era when Spanish settlers introduced beef to Peru and African slaves popularized organ-meat skewer preparations. Anticuchos became the structural Lima street food in the 19th century and are now considered a Peruvian national treasure, served from sidewalk grills, in fine-dining restaurants, and at family barbecues. — Two structural versions: the canonical beef heart (anticuchos de corazón, this recipe) and the modern adaptations (chicken hearts, beef sirloin, even tofu). The beef heart version is structurally Peruvian and is the dish most associated with the anticucho tradition; the substitutes are honest modern adaptations for cooks or guests uncomfortable with the canonical offal. The traditional Peruvian street service includes a boiled small potato and a piece of choclo (Peruvian corn) on each skewer; the skewer is served with a small dish of ají sauce on the side.
