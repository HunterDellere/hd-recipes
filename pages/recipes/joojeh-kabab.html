Recipe

Joojeh kabab

The Persian saffron-yogurt chicken skewer: boneless thigh meat marinated 4 to 24 hours in grated onion, bloomed saffron, yogurt, lemon, and olive oil, then threaded on flat skewers and grilled over high heat until the surface is amber-mahogany and the interior is just set. The yogurt enzymes pre-tenderize the protein; the bloomed saffron carries the color and aroma; the onion juice deepens the savory base. The structural lighter-meat companion to the heavier koobideh and a defining dish of Iranian kabab cookery.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The marinade is a minimum 4-hour, ideally overnight, soak. Yogurt enzymes (lactic-acid bacteria and the residual rennet) tenderize the chicken protein over time; under 4 hours the surface flavors but the interior reads tough. Plan back from when you want to grill.
  • Bloom the saffron the night before with the marinade. Grind 0.4 g threads with a pinch of sugar, steep in 30 g hot water; the color and aroma develop over hours, not minutes. Saffron added dry to the marinade barely participates.
  • If serving with tahdig (the canonical Persian pairing), start the rice soak when you start the marinade and the rice parboil 90 minutes before grilling. The rice steam phase runs in parallel with the marinade rest and the grill setup so everything lands at the same time.
  • Flat metal skewers are structural; round skewers spin and the chicken turns with them, never sitting in contact with the grill. If you only have round bamboo skewers, soak 30 minutes and use two per kabab to prevent rotation.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the marinade

  1. 800 g boneless skinless chicken thigh, trimmed of excess fat, cut into 4 cm cubes (about 25 to 30 cubes total)
  2. 150 g yellow onion, grated on the large holes of a box grater, then squeezed through a fine sieve; reserve 60 g of juice and discard the pulp (the pulp burns on the grill and tastes bitter) — (1 medium onion; the squeezed juice is the structural carrier, not the pulp)
  3. 150 g whole-milk plain yogurt
  4. 45 g extra-virgin olive oil
  5. 30 g fresh lemon juice
  6. 12 g kosher salt
  7. 2 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Phase 2, the saffron bloom

  1. 0.4 g saffron threads, ground with the sugar in a mortar to a fine red powder — (a large pinch; the color carrier and the structural aroma of the kabab)
  2. 1 g granulated sugar — (a pinch; helps the mortar grind)
  3. 30 g hot water (not boiling)

Phase 3, the basting butter

  1. 45 g unsalted butter, melted
  2. 3 g sumac, for finishing

Execution

  1. 1
    Bloom the saffron: grind the 0.4 g saffron with 1 g sugar in a mortar to a fine powder; add the 30 g / 2 tbsp hot water, cover with a saucer, and steep 20 minutes. The liquid will turn deep amber-orange.
  2. 2
    Grate the 150 g / 1 onion on the large holes of a box grater into a fine-mesh sieve set over a bowl; press hard with a spoon to extract the juice. Reserve 60 g of onion juice; discard the pulp.
  3. 3
    In a large bowl, combine the 60 g onion juice, 150 g / ⅔ cup yogurt, 45 g / 3 tbsp olive oil, 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, 12 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt, 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper, and two-thirds of the bloomed saffron liquid (reserve the remaining third for the basting butter). Whisk until uniform; the marinade should look pale gold-orange and smell of onion and saffron.
  4. 4
    Add the 800 g chicken cubes and turn to coat every piece. Cover and refrigerate 4 to 24 hours (8 to 12 is the structural sweet spot). The yogurt enzymes work over hours; the surface color develops as the saffron penetrates.
  5. 5
    Set up the grill: charcoal at high heat (coals fully ashed and glowing, hand-test reads 3 seconds at grate height) or gas at 250 to 290°C / 480 to 550°F. Thread the chicken onto flat metal skewers, pressing 5 to 6 cubes per skewer tightly together (the contact between cubes is structural; loose cubes overcook on the exposed edges).
  6. 6
    Melt the 45 g / 3 tbsp butter and whisk in the reserved third of the saffron liquid; this is the basting butter, which will glaze the kababs in the last grilling phase.
  7. 7
    Grill the skewers directly over high heat, lid up. Cook 4 to 5 minutes on the first side until the underside is amber with darker char marks (no need to flip early; the first side establishes the structural crust). Flip with tongs; brush the seared top with the saffron-butter. Cook 3 to 4 minutes on the second side. The interior is done at 71°C / 160°F at the thickest point; the surface should be deep mahogany-amber with crisp edges.
  8. 8
    In the last minute, lift the skewers off the grate and brush all over with the remaining saffron-butter; return to the grill for 30 seconds to set the glaze. Pull and rest on a warm platter 3 minutes covered loosely with foil.
  9. 9
    Sprinkle the rested kababs with the 3 g / 1 tsp sumac. Serve immediately on the skewers, or slide the chicken off onto a bed of saffron rice. Sumac, fresh lemon wedges, grilled tomato (an empty skewer of small tomatoes grilled alongside, traditional), and a small bowl of mast-o-khiar or plain yogurt are the canonical sides.
Safety
  • Pulling chicken at 71°C / 160°F internal (just past the carryover threshold to a final 165°F) Pulled at 160°F internal, then rested for 3 minutes covered, the meat carries to roughly 165°F (USDA published-safe instantaneous target). Resting under foil drives the carryover; pulling at 165°F internal directly produces dry, chalky chicken. Vulnerable diners (pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, very old) should be served chicken cooked to 165°F internal with no carryover credit. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional oil-only marinade→as the commercial kabab-house version

    Skip the yogurt entirely. Increase the olive oil to 90 g / 6 tbsp and the lemon juice to 45 g / 3 tbsp. The marinade reads lighter and the chicken surface caramelizes more aggressively without the dairy buffer; the interior reads slightly less tender. Useful when serving the kabab over heavier rice dishes where the dairy yogurt-rice interaction would be redundant.

  • Protein lamb chunks (joojeh format with shishlik logic)→with cubed lamb leg or sirloin

    Substitute 800 g cubed lamb leg or sirloin for the chicken; double the marinade time to 12 to 24 hours; grill 5 minutes per side at the same heat. The yogurt marinade tenderizes the lamb beautifully; the saffron-butter glaze carries identically. The dish becomes adjacent to shishlik, the Persian cubed-meat kabab.

  • Texture bone-in version→with bone-in chicken thighs and drumsticks instead of cubed boneless

    Use 1.2 kg bone-in chicken thighs and drumsticks; slash each piece 2 to 3 times to the bone. Marinate 12 to 24 hours. Grill skin-side down 8 to 10 minutes, flip, and grill 8 to 10 minutes more, basting with the saffron-butter; finish over indirect heat or in a 200°C oven for 5 minutes to render through to the bone. Reads more substantial and more aromatic; the bones carry flavor that the boneless cubes do not.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole-milk plain yogurt → whole-milk Greek yogurt thinned with 30 g water Greek yogurt is too thick; thin to approximate the body of regular plain yogurt before marinating. The body of the marinade matters because the chicken needs to be coated, not clumped.
  • boneless skinless chicken thigh → bone-in skin-on chicken thighs, deboned, skin retained on the cubes Slightly more work but yields the most aromatic kabab; the skin renders into a crisp surface layer over the grill. Use the same 800 g target weight after deboning.
  • saffron threads → turmeric (1 g / ½ tsp) plus a pinch of paprika for color A fallback only. Saffron is structural for joojeh kabab; the turmeric version reads as a generic yogurt-marinated chicken without the floral, slightly bitter, slightly metallic saffron note that defines the dish. Worth seeking out real saffron for this recipe.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → whole-milk plain yogurt make it yourselfstub Home-cultured yogurt has more active lactic-acid bacteria and reads brighter; the tenderizing effect on the chicken is more pronounced and the marinade carries a cleaner tang.

Notes

Why thigh, not breast. Boneless thigh has roughly twice the intramuscular fat of breast and significantly more collagen-rich connective tissue; both buffer the chicken against the high grill heat that joojeh kabab requires. Breast meat hits the dry-and-chalky window at the same grill duration that yields perfectly juicy thigh. The kabab tradition uses thigh almost universally for this reason; the dish was developed on the meat that survived the cook.

The onion-juice technique. Grating the onion and squeezing through a sieve to extract only the juice is structural. The juice carries the enzymatic activity (allinase, which breaks down protein) and the structural sweetness without the bulk of the onion pulp; the pulp, left in the marinade, burns to bitter carbon on the grill. The squeeze-and-discard technique is universal across Persian kabab marinades; it is not optional.

The saffron-butter glaze. The final basting step is what gives joojeh its signature finish: deep saffron-orange color, slight crisp edges from the butter solids, and a glossy lacquered surface. Applied too early the butter burns before the kabab is done; applied too late the kabab is dry before the glaze sets. The 30-second post-baste hold over the grill is the sweet spot.

Versatility. The marinade transfers cleanly to: bone-in chicken pieces for an oven or air-fryer joojeh (roast at 220°C for 30 to 35 minutes), butterflied whole chicken for grilling spatchcocked, cubed lamb for shishlik, large flat-cut chicken breasts pressed under a board for tabaka-style flat-grilled chicken, and skewered shrimp (cut marinade time to 30 minutes).

Pairs across Persian (canonical), broader Iranian diaspora, Afghan and Central Asian (where similar saffron-yogurt marinades produce chicken kebab), Mediterranean, and contemporary grill cookery globally.

Storage. Marinated raw chicken keeps 48 hours refrigerated; do not exceed 24 hours of marinade contact or the yogurt enzymes over-tenderize and the surface reads mushy. Cooked kababs keep 3 days refrigerated; reheat in a 180°C oven for 8 minutes to restore the surface crust, or eat cold sliced over salad. The marinade itself does not freeze well; the yogurt breaks.

  • The Iranian kabab canon. Joojeh (chicken) kabab is the lighter companion to koobideh (ground lamb) in the kabab house tradition; documented across Persian cookery since at least the Qajar period. — Two structural marinade schools: the yogurt-based marinade (here, deeper tenderization, more aromatic carry from the saffron-blooming dairy interaction) and the oil-only marinade (lighter, faster, less complex). The yogurt version is the home-cook standard; the oil-only is more common in commercial kabab houses where the marinade turnover is faster.
