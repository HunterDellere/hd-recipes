Jungle curry paste (prik gaeng pa)

A pounded-fresh Central Thai jungle curry paste built on soaked dried red chilies, lemongrass, galangal, and the two aromatics that define the style: fingerroot (krachai) and a backbone of white pepper. No roasted seed spice and no sweetness, so the paste reads fierce, herbaceous, and clean rather than rounded like a red curry paste. The base for a coconut-free forest curry.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • A heavy granite mortar and pestle (krok hin) is the canonical tool and the reason this paste outperforms a food processor version. The mortar shears cell walls and releases the lipids bound inside the lemongrass, galangal, and krachai; a processor cuts rather than shears and produces a wetter, weeping paste. With a processor you reach about 70 percent of the way.
  • Soak the dried chilies in hot water for 20 minutes before any pounding begins; this passive window is when you toast and cool the shrimp paste and white pepper, so the two tasks overlap exactly.
  • Plan for 15 to 20 minutes of steady pounding once the mortar work starts. Fibrous aromatics first and soft alliums last is the only order that yields a smooth paste; rushing leaves a stringy paste that reads of lemongrass texture instead of lemongrass oil.
  • The paste keeps one week refrigerated and three months frozen in an ice-cube tray. Doubling the batch costs no extra mortar time and pre-portions twice the curries, so there is no reason to pound a single-curry amount.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Chilies

  1. 40 g dried long red chilies (prik chee fah / Thai guajillo type), seeded, stems and most seeds removed, soaked in hot water 20 minutes, drained — (about 12 large dried chilies; the color and bulk of the paste, mild to moderate heat)
  2. 8 g dried Thai bird chilies, soaked with the long chilies, drained — (about 10 small dried chilies; the sharp heat, adjust up or down)

Fibrous aromatics

  1. 45 g lemongrass, white part only, sliced thin — (3 stalks)
  2. 20 g fresh galangal, peeled and sliced thin — (1.5-inch knob)
  3. 25 g fingerroot (krachai), peeled and sliced thin — (a small cluster of fingers; the signature jungle-curry aromatic)
  4. 4 g makrut lime zest
  5. 10 g fresh coriander root (or lower cilantro stems)

Alliums, spice & funk

  1. 30 g garlic cloves, peeled — (8 cloves)
  2. 50 g Thai shallots (or French), peeled — (4 small Thai or 2 French)
  3. 3 g white peppercorns
  4. 12 g shrimp paste (kapi), toasted
  5. 4 g fine sea salt

Execution

  1. 1
    Stem the dried chilies and shake out most of the seeds (leaving a few in the bird chilies for heat). Soak all 48 g of dried chilies (40 g long red plus 8 g bird) in hot water for 20 minutes until pliable, then drain hard and squeeze out the water. Wet chilies pound into a far smoother paste than dry, and removing the seeds keeps the texture clean rather than gritty.
  2. 2
    While the chilies soak, toast the aromatics that need it. Dry-toast the 3 g / 1.5 tsp white peppercorns in a skillet over medium heat for about 1 minute until fragrant, shaking constantly so they do not scorch, then cool and grind to a powder. Toast the 12 g / 2 tsp shrimp paste separately: wrap it in foil and hold it over a flame, turning, for 30 to 45 seconds per side until it smells smoky and deep, or dry-fry it pressed against the pan. Raw kapi tastes harshly marine; toasting converts the sulfurous top notes into something rounder and more savory.
  3. 3
    Add the 4 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt to a heavy granite mortar, then pound the fibrous aromatics in order of toughness: 45 g lemongrass first, then 20 g galangal, 25 g fingerroot, 4 g / 2 tsp makrut lime zest, and 10 g coriander root. Pound steadily for 6 to 8 minutes until the fibers break down into a homogeneous mass. The salt acts as an abrasive. This order is structural, not preference: lemongrass, galangal, and krachai have cell walls that resist bruising, and if softer ingredients go in first the hard fibers never grind fine and the paste reads stringy.
  4. 4
    Add the drained soaked chilies and pound until they break down and the paste turns an even brick red, about 4 minutes. Then add the 30 g / 8 cloves garlic and 50 g / 4 shallots and pound until smooth; these go last because they are soft and release liquid quickly, and added early they turn the paste wet and stop the fibrous aromatics from grinding fine.
  5. 5
    Add the 12 g / 2 tsp toasted shrimp paste and the ground 3 g / 1.5 tsp white pepper. Pound and grind, alternating downward strikes with a lateral smear against the mortar wall, until the paste is uniform and a faint oil sheen surfaces on top. That sheen is the signal that enough cell walls have ruptured to release the bound lipids; it is the difference between a paste that blooms cleanly in hot oil and one that weeps water into the pan.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • fingerroot (krachai) → frozen or brined krachai Frozen krachai is a true substitute and the practical default outside Thailand; brined julienned krachai, drained and rinsed, also works. Do not substitute ginger, which removes the medicinal-peppery signature that defines a jungle curry.
  • dried long red chilies → dried guajillo or New Mexico chilies Mexican dried chilies of the same mild-to-moderate range give comparable color and bulk. Keep the bird chilies for the actual heat, since guajillo and New Mexico are gentle.
  • fresh coriander root → lower stems of cilantro bunches Acceptable outside Thailand. Use the bottom 2 inches where the stems thicken, which carry most of the root flavor.
  • shrimp paste (vegetarian) → dark miso plus a pinch of kombu powder Loses the marine funk that anchors the paste; 1 tbsp dark miso with a pinch of ground kombu supplies an adjacent savory depth.

Notes

How this differs from red curry paste. Prik gaeng pa is essentially a red curry paste stripped of its rounding agents and pushed harder on the fresh, fierce aromatics. It carries no roasted coriander or cumin seed (which give red and panang pastes their warm, almost sweet backbone) and no palm sugar. Instead it leans on fingerroot and white pepper, which read peppery and herbaceous rather than warm and rounded. The result is a paste built for a brothy, coconut-free curry that gets its body from stock and its richness from nothing but the bloomed paste itself.

Yield and storage: this batch is about 240 g, enough for 3 to 4 curries at 3 tablespoons per. Pack into a small jar, press a thin film of neutral oil across the surface to slow oxidation, and refrigerate up to a week. Freeze in a single-layer ice-cube tray, then bag the cubes for pre-portioned amounts that keep three months.

Calibrating heat. The long red chilies are for color and bulk and the bird chilies are for heat, so dial the heat with the bird-chili count alone and leave the long chilies fixed to hold the color and volume. Jungle curry is traditionally one of the hotter Thai curries; if you are building toward that, push the bird chilies up rather than overloading the long reds.

Calibrating to jarred paste. When this replaces store-bought paste in a recipe, start with about two-thirds the listed jarred amount and taste, since a fresh-pounded paste is more concentrated and far more aromatic than a jar.
