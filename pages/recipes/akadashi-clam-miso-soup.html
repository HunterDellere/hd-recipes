Akadashi clam miso soup (asari no akadashi)

Nagoya and Osaka kansai-style miso soup built on red miso (hatcho or aka) and littleneck or asari clams. The clams release their own succinic-acid umami into the dashi during a brief simmer, producing a soup where three umami nucleotides stack: glutamate from the kombu, inosinate from the katsuobushi, and succinate from the clams. The red miso supplies the dark, fermented, robust character that defines the Nagoya akadashi tradition, and the broth eats fuller, more savory, and more bracing than the everyday white-miso washoku bowl.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The clams need a 30 to 35 minute saltwater purge BEFORE any cooking starts, or sand and grit will end up in the broth. Submerge the 600 g clams in 1.5 L cold water with 45 g kosher salt (3% brine), set in a dark, quiet spot, and time everything else around this window.
  • Cold-soak the kombu in the dashi water during the same 30 minutes the clams are purging. Glutamate extracts at room temperature; running both passive infusions in parallel collapses 30 minutes of dead time into one.
  • Never boil miso once it is whisked in. Pull the dashi off heat, dissolve the 75 g red miso through a strainer or whisk, and serve. Boiling drives off the fermented-aroma volatiles and shifts the texture grainy.

Execution

  1. 1
    Purge the clams. In a wide bowl or container, dissolve the 45 g / 3 tbsp kosher salt in the 1500 ml / 6 cups cold water; the resulting 3% brine matches the salinity of seawater. Add the 600 g clams in a single layer (not piled; the clams need contact with the brine to filter-feed). Cover loosely with a kitchen towel (the clams will spit water as they purge, which is the desired behavior). Set aside in a dark cool spot (under a counter, in a corner of the kitchen away from direct light) for 30 to 45 minutes. The clams will release the grit and sand from inside their shells into the brine; you can sometimes see them visibly opening and closing during this time. After the purge, lift the clams out (do not pour through a strainer; the grit will recontaminate them), rinse under cold running water, and discard any clams that are open and do not close when tapped (those are dead and should not be cooked).
  2. 2
    Cold-soak the kombu while the clams purge. In a separate 2-quart saucepan, place the 10 g kombu square and pour over the 900 ml / 3¾ cups cold water. Let stand at room temperature for the 30 to 45 minutes that the clams are purging. The kombu hydrates and releases its glutamate slowly into the cold water; a hot start would extract the kombu but also release the slimy alginate compounds, dulling the dashi. This is the canonical Japanese cold-start kombu method.
  3. 3
    Build the dashi (ichiban). After the kombu cold-soak, place the saucepan over medium heat and bring slowly to just under boiling, about 8 to 10 minutes. The water should reach about 80 to 85°C with small bubbles forming at the pot bottom but no rolling boil. Just before the water boils, lift out the kombu with chopsticks or tongs; the kombu has done its work and continuing to heat it will release bitter compounds. Reserve the spent kombu (it can be cut into strips and added back to the soup as a vegetable component, or saved for niban dashi). Increase the heat slightly to bring the water to 95°C, then sprinkle the 15 g katsuobushi over the surface; the flakes will sink within 30 to 60 seconds. Pull off heat immediately. Let stand 3 to 4 minutes for the katsuobushi to release its inosinate into the dashi (a longer steep produces a stronger and slightly more bitter dashi; this short steep is the ichiban style). Strain through a fine-mesh sieve lined with cheesecloth into a clean pot or large measuring cup. You should have about 750 to 800 ml of clear amber dashi. Dashi (kombu and katsuobushi extraction)
  4. 4
    Cook the clams in the dashi. Return the strained dashi to the saucepan and bring to a gentle simmer over medium heat (about 90°C, small steady bubbles, not a rolling boil). Add the purged clams in a single layer. Cover the pot with a lid. Simmer 3 to 5 minutes, gently shaking the pot once or twice to redistribute the clams. The clams will open as they cook; once most clams are open (about 4 minutes into the cook), pull from heat. Discard any clams that have not opened (they were already dead and are not safe to eat). The clams have released their succinate and their internal liquor into the dashi during this brief cook; the broth is now a layered umami of glutamate (kombu), inosinate (katsuobushi), and succinate (clam) plus the natural saltiness of the clam liquor. Taste the broth before adding miso, it should already read quite savory; the miso will primarily add miso character rather than additional saltiness.
  5. 5
    Add the miso off-heat. Pull the saucepan completely off the burner. Place a small fine-mesh sieve into the broth (a tea strainer works, or any small sieve) and add the 75 g red miso into the sieve. With the back of a small spoon or whisk, press the miso through the sieve while moving the sieve in slow circles through the broth; the sieve breaks up clumps and dissolves the miso evenly. Alternatively, ladle 80 ml of hot broth into a small bowl, whisk the miso into the bowl until smooth, then pour back into the pot. The red miso dissolves more slowly than white miso (the longer-aged paste is denser and contains more whole soybean particles), so this step may take 60 to 90 seconds of patient pressing. The broth transitions from clear amber to a deep red-brown with the cloudy miso fully integrated. Do not return the pot to heat. Red miso's volatile esters are more heat-sensitive than white miso's, and a single boil dramatically dulls the akadashi character.
  6. 6
    Plate and serve. Ladle the soup into 4 small bowls (about 220 ml each, with 4 to 6 clams per bowl, distributing the broth evenly). Scatter a few mitsuba leaves or a thin slice of scallion green over each bowl. Add a tiny pinch (less than ⅛ tsp) of freshly ground sansho pepper per bowl if using. Serve immediately as part of a washoku-style meal, the canonical accompaniment is a bowl of plain Japanese rice and one or two banchan, often a piece of grilled fish or a quick-pickled vegetable. The clams are eaten directly from the shell with chopsticks (a clam in one hand to grip the shell, chopsticks in the other to extract the meat); the broth is sipped from the bowl rather than spoon-eaten in the everyday Japanese style. Reheating to a boil ruins the miso flavor, so serve straight from the pot.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein shellfish variation→shijimi (freshwater clam) akadashi

    Substitute 400 g shijimi clams (small freshwater clams, available frozen at Japanese groceries) for the 600 g littlenecks. Shijimi are typically sold pre-purged; if frozen, thaw at room temperature for 30 minutes before cooking. Cook in the dashi for 4 to 5 minutes until shells open. The dish is the Nagoya canonical akadashi (shijimi no akadashi) and is reputed to be a hangover cure in Japan due to the ornithine content of the clam liquor.

  • Regional miso ratio (for tougher hatcho miso fans)→pure hatcho miso (no aka miso blend)

    Some kansai cooks insist on 100% hatcho miso for the canonical Nagoya akadashi, where most home cooks use a blend of hatcho and lighter aka miso to soften the dark register. For the strongest, most traditional bowl, use 75 g pure hatcho miso; for a slightly more accessible bowl, use 50 g hatcho miso plus 30 g aka miso. The pure-hatcho version reads more aggressively fermented and chocolate-savory; new diners often prefer the blend.

  • Occasion season (autumn variation)→shimeji and root vegetable akadashi

    Skip the clams. In place of the clam phase, add 60 g shimeji mushrooms (separated into individual stems) and 80 g diced daikon (1 cm cubes) to the dashi. Simmer 8 to 10 minutes until the daikon is tender. Proceed with the miso addition as in phase 5. The dish is an autumn-winter mushroom akadashi, dispensing with the succinate of the clams but compensating with the glutamate and earthy character of the shimeji.

  • Occasion occasion (special / banquet)→akadashi with grated ginger and a touch of sansho oil

    Finish each bowl with ¼ tsp freshly grated ginger and 2 drops of sansho-infused sesame oil (or a pinch of fresh-ground sansho pepper). The ginger lifts the dish into a more brace-and-warm register suitable for cold weather and reads as a kaiseki-restaurant flourish. Some Nagoya kaiseki kitchens also add a small piece of fresh yuba (tofu skin) for visual contrast.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • littleneck or asari clams manila clams, cherrystones (cut to size), mussels

    Manila clams are the direct cousin of asari and the closest American substitute. Cherrystones (the larger version of littlenecks) work but are too large for the elegant Japanese bowl presentation; if using, cut the clam meat out of the shell and use the meat alone. Mussels produce a different but acceptable dish; the dark-shell visual is non-canonical for Japanese serving, but the umami stacks similarly. Avoid quahogs (chowder clams), they are too large and the flavor is too strong for the akadashi register.

  • kombu-katsuobushi ichiban dashi niban dashi, hondashi (instant), or vegetarian shiitake-kombu dashi

    Niban dashi (second-extraction from the spent kombu and katsuobushi) is acceptable and is what most Japanese home kitchens use for everyday miso soup. Hondashi (instant dashi powder, 1 tsp per 800 ml hot water) is the weeknight shortcut, workable but flatter than fresh dashi. Vegetarian kombu-shiitake dashi makes the dish pescatarian-only but Buddhist-cuisine-style; the inosinate-umami is replaced with guanylate from the shiitake, the dish reads cleaner and more vegetable-forward.

  • red miso (hatcho or aka) awase miso (50/50 white-and-red), pure white miso (for milder)

    Awase miso is the everyday Japanese miso and works at 1:1; the resulting dish reads as a darker-than-usual everyday miso soup rather than as akadashi. Pure white miso (shiro miso) produces a meaningfully different dish, lighter and sweeter, and would not be called akadashi by a Japanese cook; the dish is fine but is structurally a different soup.

  • mitsuba scallion greens, flat-leaf parsley, or omit

    Scallion greens are the practical American substitute. Flat-leaf parsley is the closest Western herb analog (mitsuba is in the same family); use 1 small sprig per bowl. Omitting entirely is acceptable; the dish is fine without the herb garnish.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → kombu-katsuobushi ichiban dashi make it yourselfstub Homemade ichiban dashi takes 20 minutes from cold water to strained dashi and is dramatically better than any instant or store-bought version. The cold-water kombu start, the lift-before-boil, the brief katsuobushi steep, and the strain-through-cheesecloth produce a dashi with clarity, depth, and balanced umami that no instant product approaches. Make a 1-liter batch in 20 minutes for the akadashi above plus the next 3 days of cooking.

Notes

Why three umami nucleotides matter. The defining technical feature of akadashi-no-asari is the stacking of three umami nucleotides in the broth: glutamate (the free amino acid from the kombu), inosinate (the nucleotide from the dried bonito), and succinate (the nucleotide that gives shellfish, especially clams and mussels, their distinctive umami flavor). Each of these compounds activates the umami receptor on the tongue, and they exhibit synergy: the perceived umami intensity from glutamate plus inosinate is about 8 times the intensity of either alone, and the addition of succinate adds another distinct layer because succinate binds to a slightly different receptor site. The end-result broth reads more "complete" and more deeply savory than a dashi-and-miso bowl alone, with the clam contribution adding a marine-mineral register that the dashi alone cannot supply. This three-nucleotide stacking is the structural reason akadashi-no-asari is considered a refined kansai preparation rather than just a clam miso soup.

Why red miso, not white. The dark color and deeper flavor of red miso is the result of long fermentation (typically 12 to 24 months for hatcho miso, vs 3 to 6 months for white miso) and a higher proportion of soybean to rice or barley koji. The longer Maillard reaction during fermentation produces melanoidins (the brown pigment) and a complex chocolate-meat-savory flavor that white miso does not have. For the clam preparation specifically, the dark miso is the right pairing because it stands up to the assertive marine flavor of the clams; white miso would be overpowered. Conversely, white miso is the right pairing for delicate preparations (tofu and wakame, the everyday Japanese bowl) where red miso would dominate. The kansai (Nagoya, Osaka, Kyoto) culinary tradition is built around red miso preferences and the akadashi concept; the broader Japanese household tradition leans toward awase or white miso. Both are legitimate; akadashi is the regional specialty, not the universal practice.

The 35-minute purge is non-negotiable. Wild and farmed clams retain grit, sand, and silt inside their shells, and a single grain of sand in a bowl of miso soup is the kind of mistake that elevates an otherwise good cook to a kitchen-bond-villain. The 3% saltwater purge (matching seawater salinity) triggers the clams to behave as if they''re still in the ocean and filter-feed normally; over 30 to 45 minutes they expel most of their internal grit into the surrounding brine. Some Japanese cooks add a small amount of nuka (rice bran) to the purging water to slightly cloud it and reportedly improve the purge; this is regional and not strictly necessary. Skipping the purge produces a gritty, unpleasant soup; reducing the purge time to under 20 minutes produces a partial purge that still leaves some grit in the broth. The full 30 to 45 minutes is structural.

Lift the kombu before boiling. The canonical Japanese kombu preparation pulls the kombu out of the water just before it boils, around 85 to 90°C. Continuing to heat kombu in the water after this point releases alginate (slimy texture-affecting compounds) and bitter polyphenols that dull the dashi. This is one of the few absolute rules of dashi preparation, and most American recipes either get it wrong or fail to mention it. The kombu has fully released its glutamate at the temperature range of 60 to 85°C; longer or hotter extraction is counterproductive. The "just below the boil" lift is the right cue.

Why the strain-through-cheesecloth. Strained dashi looks visually different from unstrained dashi: clear and amber rather than slightly cloudy with floating particles. The clarity matters for the akadashi specifically because the red miso will cloud the broth significantly, and starting from a clear base produces a more attractive finished bowl. Cheesecloth-lined sieve removes the fine katsuobushi particles that a coarse sieve alone would let through. Some kaiseki kitchens use a paper coffee filter for the absolute clearest dashi; this is overkill for home cooking but is the gold standard.

Off-heat miso addition is non-negotiable. This is the same rule that applies to every miso preparation: do not boil miso. Red miso is even more heat-sensitive than white miso because its longer fermentation has built up more volatile aromatic compounds that flash off at boiling temperatures. A single boil after the miso is added drops the perceived flavor by 30 to 50%, producing a flat, salty-only bowl that bears little resemblance to a well-made akadashi. The off-heat rule extends to reheating: cold leftover miso soup should be re-warmed gently to no more than 80°C, never re-boiled. For this reason, miso soup is best made just before serving, in the volume that will be consumed at the meal.

The mitsuba question. Mitsuba (Japanese trefoil or Japanese parsley) is the canonical herb for clam miso soup, particularly in kansai cooking. The herb has a faint celery-and-anise aroma that pairs specifically well with shellfish; it grows wild across Japan and is in the same Apiaceae family as parsley and culantro. American Japanese groceries (especially the larger Tokyo Central, Mitsuwa, and H Mart-Asian sections) usually carry mitsuba seasonally; the herb is hard to grow at home but worth seeking out. Scallion greens are the practical substitute and most American home cooks will use scallion without complaint; mitsuba is the upgrade for the cook chasing the kansai canonical version.

Why this is a soup, not a stew or pasta sauce. The bowl size (about 220 ml per serving, smaller than a Western soup portion) and the protein-to-broth ratio (4 to 6 clams per bowl in a 220 ml broth) are calibrated for the Japanese eating context: miso soup is one of many small dishes at a washoku meal, sipped between bites of rice and other dishes, not eaten as a standalone main course. Western diners sometimes scale up the broth volume and the clam count to make it a full-meal soup; the dish then reads less like Japanese miso soup and more like a Western seafood broth, which is fine but is structurally a different dish. The kansai akadashi is by design a refined, intense, small-portion soup that punches above its volume in flavor density.

Make-ahead and leftovers. The dashi can be made 24 hours in advance and refrigerated; the clams must be purged and cooked the same day they''re served. The completed soup with miso does not hold well, the miso''s volatile aromatics decay within hours, and reheating ruins the flavor. The structural compromise for make-ahead service: prepare the dashi, purge the clams, and cook the clams in the dashi up to 4 hours ahead. Hold the soup off-heat at room temperature (the clam meat is safe at this point because it was cooked). Add the miso at the last moment before serving, after a brief gentle reheat to about 75°C. This preserves about 85% of the fresh-cooked flavor and works for entertaining where last-minute assembly is impractical.

  • Adapted from the Nagoya and broader kansai akadashi tradition — Akadashi (赤だし) refers specifically to a miso soup built on red miso (aka miso), as distinct from the everyday awase-or-shiro miso soup of much of Japan.
