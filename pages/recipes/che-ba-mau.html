Chè ba màu (three-color Vietnamese sweet bean dessert)

Stratified parfait of sweetened mung bean, red bean, and pandan jelly under a coconut cream pour. The three colors hold cleanly because each layer is built at the right density and chilled before the next goes in.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Soak the adzuki beans 8 hours or overnight in cold water before starting. Without the soak the cook time stretches from 1 hour to over 2 and the skins remain tough. If you forgot, quick-soak: pour boiling water over the beans and let stand 1 hour, works but loses some texture.
  • All three layers must be FULLY CHILLED before assembly. Total elapsed time is about 3 hours but most of it is passive cooling. Build the components in this order so cooling overlaps with active work: pandan jelly first (longest set), then adzuki, then mung bean, then coconut sauce last.
  • The dessert is assembled à la minute. The chilled layers go into glasses in stacked order, then ice, then coconut sauce, pour ice and sauce only when you are ready to serve. Pre-assembled glasses turn slumpy and the colors muddy as the ice melts.

Execution

  1. 1
    Layer 1, mung bean. Rinse the 120 g / ⅔ cup split mung beans until water runs clear. Combine with 360 ml / 1½ cups water in a small saucepan. Bring to a boil, drop to low, cover, and simmer 22 minutes until the beans are fully soft and the water has mostly absorbed. Stir in the 25 g / 2 tbsp sugar and 1 g pinch salt. Mash about half the beans with a fork, half whole, half paste gives the layer body without sliminess. Cool to room temperature, then refrigerate.
  2. 2
    Layer 2, adzuki. Drain the soaked 100 g / ½ cup adzuki beans. Combine with 600 ml / 2½ cups fresh water in a saucepan. Bring to a boil, then simmer covered 45 to 60 minutes until the beans yield completely to a spoon but still hold individual shape (not mashed). Drain off most of the cooking liquid, leaving just enough to keep the beans glossy. Stir in the 40 g / 3 tbsp sugar and the 1 g pinch salt while the beans are still hot so the sugar dissolves. Cool, then refrigerate.
  3. 3
    Layer 3, pandan jelly. Combine the 480 ml / 2 cups water and the 4 bruised knotted pandan leaves in a saucepan. Bring to a gentle simmer, hold 5 minutes off the heat covered to steep, then return to a simmer. Whisk in the 4 g / 1 tsp agar-agar powder until fully dissolved. Bring to a hard boil for 1 minute (agar needs the rolling boil to activate). Stir in the 30 g / 2½ tbsp sugar. Pull the pandan leaves out. Pour into a parchment-lined shallow pan or 8x8 baking dish to a depth of about 1 cm / ½ inch. Let set 20 minutes at room temperature, then refrigerate 1 hour until fully firm. Slice into small 5 mm / ¼ inch cubes.
  4. 4
    Coconut cream finish. In a small saucepan combine the 240 ml / 1 cup coconut cream, the 15 g / 1 tbsp sugar, and the 1 g pinch salt. Warm over low heat just until the sugar dissolves. Whisk in the 5 g tapioca starch slurry; stir gently for 1 minute as it thickens to a pourable cream consistency. Cool to room temperature. Do not boil; the coconut emulsion breaks above 90°C.
  5. 5
    Assembly. In each of 4 tall glasses, spoon 3 tbsp of the chilled mung bean as the bottom layer. Spoon 3 tbsp of the chilled adzuki bean as the middle layer. Scatter 3 tbsp of the pandan jelly cubes as the top layer. Add a small handful of crushed ice. Pour 60 ml / ¼ cup of the coconut cream over the top.
  6. 6
    Serve immediately with a long spoon. The eater stirs everything together at the table, the colors are display, the dessert is the unified mixture once spooned through.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion topping additions→add longan, lychee, or jackfruit

    Scatter 2 tbsp drained tinned longan, lychee, or jackfruit pieces over the jelly layer before adding ice. Common Saigon street stalls add 5 to 7 toppings; the base three colors plus fruit is the standard premium build.

  • Dietary dietary→reduced-sugar chè ba màu

    Cut all sugar by 30 percent. The dish reads better when the beans taste like beans and the coconut tastes like coconut; commercial versions over-sugar to mask cheap ingredients.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • agar-agar powder → agar flakes, double the weight (8 g / 2 tbsp) Agar flakes are roughly half the strength of powder by weight. Soak in cold water 5 minutes before bringing to the boil.
  • fresh pandan leaves → frozen pandan leaves (1:1) or 1 tsp pandan extract Frozen retains 80 percent of aroma. Pandan extract is mostly synthetic and gives color without depth; use as a last resort with vanilla added to compensate.

Notes

The three colors are visual, but the dessert is one mixed bite. Stratifying is the technique; eating is the dissolution. Do not over-engineer the layers to hold separately on the spoon, they are meant to merge as the ice melts.

Bean texture is the dish's soul. Adzuki must be tender enough to crush against the roof of the mouth but still hold shape; mung must be soft enough to spread but still grainy on the tongue. Both go pasty if overcooked. Pull the adzuki 5 minutes earlier than feels right and salt them while hot; the salt firms the skins as they cool.

The coconut cream thickening with tapioca starch (rather than just using coconut cream straight) is non-traditional but produces a noticeably better mouthfeel and pourability. Most Vietnamese home cooks now use this trick; older recipes call for coconut cream alone, which separates when it meets ice.
