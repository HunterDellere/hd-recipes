Lahpet thoke (tossed market style)

The pre-mixed, hand-tossed form of Burmese tea-leaf salad: fermented tea, fried bean crunch, peanuts, fried garlic, cabbage, tomato, and dressing all kneaded together in the bowl until the flavors marry, then eaten right away. Heartier and more integrated than the composed platter, this is the everyday teashop and street version.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Like the platter version, this leans on three made-ahead components: fermented tea (lahpet), the fried bean-and-seed crunch (pè kyaw), and crispy fried garlic with its oil. Have all three ready (recipes linked below) and the salad comes together in 15 minutes.
  • This version is tossed immediately and eaten immediately. Because everything is mixed at once, the crunch begins softening from the moment it is dressed; toss right before you eat, and do not let it sit. If you want a salad that holds on a plate, make the composed platter version instead.
  • Knead, do not just stir. The signature of the tossed style is hand-mixing the salad firmly so the fermented tea coats everything and the dressing works into the cabbage; a gentle toss leaves it disjointed.

The salad

  1. 50 g lahpet (fermented tea leaves), drained, squeezed, stems pinched out, roughly chopped
  2. 120 g green cabbage, very finely shredded
  3. 100 g firm tomato, ripe, cored and diced small — (1 small; sweet-acid pop)
  4. 70 g pè kyaw (fried bean and seed crunch)
  5. 25 g roasted peanuts, extra, roughly chopped
  6. 15 g crispy fried garlic
  7. 8 g serrano or Thai green chili, thin-sliced optional — (1, to taste)

The dressing

  1. 25 g garlic oil (from the fried garlic)
  2. 12 g fish sauce
  3. 20 g lime juice, fresh — (from 1 lime; finishing acid)

Execution

  1. 1
    Prep the tea and vegetables into one bowl. Drain and squeeze the 50 g / 3 tbsp lahpet, pinch out tough stems, chop it roughly, and drop it into a wide mixing bowl. Add the 120 g / 2 cups finely shredded green cabbage and the 100 g / 1 small diced tomato. Shred the cabbage as finely as you can, fine shreds take the dressing and mix evenly; coarse cabbage stays raw-clumpy.
  2. 2
    Add the crunch and dressing. Scatter in the 70 g / ½ cup pè kyaw bean-and-seed crunch, the 25 g / 3 tbsp extra chopped peanuts, the 15 g / 2 tbsp crispy fried garlic, and the 8 g / 1 sliced chili if using. Spoon over the 25 g / scant 2 tbsp garlic oil, the 12 g / 2 tsp fish sauce, and the 20 g / juice of 1 lime.
  3. 3
    Knead it together. With clean hands (or two spoons), mix and gently knead the whole bowl for 30 to 45 seconds, pressing the fermented tea and dressing into the cabbage so everything is evenly coated and the flavors marry. This firm hand-mixing is the defining move of the tossed style; the tea should streak through the cabbage and the dressing should reach every shred.
  4. 4
    Taste, balance, and eat now. Taste and adjust: a squeeze more lime if it is flat, another 5 g / 1 tsp fish sauce if it needs salt, a pinch more chili for heat. It should read intensely savory-sour with a tea bitterness underneath and a heavy crunch throughout. Serve immediately, while the beans and garlic are still crisp, ideally piled onto plates straight from the bowl.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Dietary fully vegan→fish-sauce-free dressing

    Swap the 12 g / 2 tsp fish sauce for 2 tsp light soy sauce plus a small pinch of MSG or ½ tsp white miso. This keeps the salt and the deep umami the fermented tea needs to taste savory rather than just sour.

  • Occasion a more substantial meal→lahpet thoke with rice or as a rice topping

    Serve the tossed salad over or alongside a bowl of warm jasmine or short-grain rice. The fermented tea and crunch make a complete light meal against plain rice; in Myanmar lahpet is eaten exactly this way, as a savory-sour foil to a starch.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • green cabbage → romaine, napa cabbage, or shredded lettuce Any crisp leaf works as the bulk; cabbage holds longest, romaine adds sweetness, napa is more tender. Shred whatever you use finely.
  • serrano or Thai green chili → jalapeño or a pinch of dried chili flakes For milder heat use jalapeño; for convenience a pinch of chili flakes. Or omit, though a little fresh heat suits the salad.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → lahpet (fermented tea leaves) make it yourself The fermented tea defines the dish; the from-scratch ferment of steamed green leaf has a freshness and depth that bagged lahpet seldom matches. It is the one component most worth the three-week project.
  • → pè kyaw (fried bean and seed crunch) make it yourself Home-fried split peas and beans crunch harder and taste fresher than the bagged fried-bean mixes, and you control the salt and toast.
  • → crispy fried garlic make it yourself One slow fry gives you both the crisp garlic topping and the garlic oil that dresses the salad, fresher and far less bitter than store-bought.

Notes

Tossed versus platter. This is the same dish as the composed Mandalay platter, built from the same components, but assembled the everyday way: everything goes into one bowl and gets hand-kneaded together so the flavors integrate and the fermented tea coats every shred of cabbage. It is heartier, faster, and less ceremonial, the form you meet in a Burmese teashop or eaten at home as a snack, where the platter version is the one brought out for guests. The trade-off is time: because it is mixed all at once, it must be eaten right away, before the crunch goes soft.

Why knead rather than toss. Gently tossing leaves the elements sitting side by side; firmly hand-mixing presses the sticky fermented tea and the dressing into the cabbage so the whole bowl reads as one married flavor rather than a pile of separate ingredients. The cabbage softens very slightly under the salt and acid as you work it, which is exactly right here, it goes from raw-crisp to crisp-tender and takes on the dressing.

The balance to chase. Lahpet thoke should hit every axis at once: salt and umami from the fish sauce, sourness from both the fermented tea and the fresh lime, bitterness from the tea leaf itself, heat from the chili, sweetness from the tomato, and a heavy, varied crunch from the beans, peanuts, sesame, and garlic against the soft tea and crisp cabbage. Taste and rebalance at the end the way you would a Thai or Vietnamese salad; the fix for a flat salad is almost always more lime, and the fix for a thin one is more fish sauce.
