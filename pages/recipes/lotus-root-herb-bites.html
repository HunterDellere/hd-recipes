Recipe

Lotus root and herb small bites

Thin lotus root rounds blanched briefly to set the wagon-wheel structure, dressed with herb-oil, and topped per portion with one of: grated cured egg yolk, chili crisp and scallion, sesame paste and black vinegar, or soft soy-cured roe. The format is small-plate appetizer.

Execution

  1. 1
    Prep the lotus root. Combine the 1500 ml / 6 cups water and 15 ml / 1 tbsp white vinegar in a large bowl for the acid bath. Peel the 400 g lotus root with a vegetable peeler. Trim the bumpy ends square. Slice the tube into 3 to 4 mm thick rounds (thin enough to be crisp-tender, thick enough to carry a topping without bending). Drop each round into the acid water as you cut.
  2. 2
    Blanch and shock. Bring a 2-liter pot of water to a boil with the 15 g / 1 tbsp salt. Drop the lotus rounds in and blanch 90 seconds; the rounds should still be firm and crisp at the center but the surface starches should set. Lift out with a spider and shock immediately in a bowl of ice water for 60 seconds. Drain and pat completely dry on a clean towel. Ice bath shock
  3. 3
    Make the herb oil. In a small heatproof bowl, combine the 30 g chopped cilantro, 30 g chopped scallions, and 8 g minced ginger. Heat the 60 ml / ¼ cup neutral oil in a small saucepan until shimmering hot (about 175°C / 350°F; a piece of scallion dropped in should sizzle aggressively). Pour the hot oil over the herbs in one motion; the herbs will hiss and fry briefly. Stir in the 3 g / ½ tsp salt. Cool 5 minutes.
  4. 4
    Assemble the base dressing. In a separate small bowl, whisk the 15 ml / 1 tbsp light soy, 8 ml / ½ tbsp rice vinegar, 4 g / 1 tsp sugar, and 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil until the sugar dissolves.
  5. 5
    Dress the rounds. Arrange the dried lotus rounds on a wide platter in a single layer (or slightly overlapping if needed). Spoon the herb oil evenly over them; each round should get a small dollop of herb mixture and the oil. Then drizzle the soy-vinegar dressing over; the dressing pools in the air channels of each round, which is the visual you want.
  6. 6
    Top per option. Either pick one topping for the whole platter, or quarter the platter and top each section differently for a four-way variant. (Option 1) Grate the cured egg yolk through a microplane over half the platter, producing a fine yellow snow that coats each round. (Option 2) Spoon the chili crisp over, distributing oil and bits evenly. (Option 3) Drizzle the 30 g thinned sesame paste over and finish with a 5 g / 1 tsp sprinkle of toasted sesame seeds. (Option 4) Spoon small dots of soy-cured roe over the rounds, one or two pearls per round. Serve immediately at room temperature.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture preparation style→fully raw (no blanch)

    Skip the blanch entirely. Slice the lotus root extra-thin (2 mm) on a mandoline, soak in acid water 15 minutes, drain and pat dry. The texture is crunchier and more aggressive; suits cool-weather appetizer formats and reads as a banchan-adjacent cold dish. Salt the dressing slightly more (raw lotus root is less seasoned by the blanch).

  • Protein occasion→with seared scallop or shrimp

    Sear 4 to 6 small (size U-10) sea scallops or 8 large shrimp briefly (60 seconds per side, salt only) and place one on top of every 4 to 5 lotus rounds. The dish becomes a more substantial appetizer or small main; the seafood-and-lotus-and-herb-oil combination is a Cantonese banquet appetizer template. Skip the topping options or use the soy-roe one (which complements seafood best).

  • Regional aromatic profile→Sichuan ma-la dressing

    Replace the rice vinegar with 8 ml / ½ tbsp Chinkiang vinegar, add 2 g / 1 tsp ground toasted Sichuan peppercorns to the dressing, and use the chili crisp topping (option 2). The dish becomes a Sichuan ma-la lotus root appetizer, hotter and more aromatic.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • lotus root (crisp-grade tip segments) → lotus root (any grade) Soup-grade middle/base segments work but read softer after blanching; reduce blanch time to 60 seconds. The wagon-wheel visual is identical.
  • fresh cilantro → flat-leaf parsley For soap-tasting cilantro-averse diners. The herb oil reads less Chinese but the structure works.
  • cured egg yolk topping → finely grated dry-aged Parmesan or Pecorino Different cuisine, similar role: salty, savory, fine-grated finishing. The dish reads fusion rather than Chinese; defensible at a cross-cultural appetizer table.

Notes

Format flexibility. The base of this dish, blanched lotus rounds, herb oil, soy-vinegar dressing, is the canonical Chinese liang cai opener and works as-is without any topping. Adding the topping options pushes the dish toward different culinary registers: cured egg yolk reads upmarket-modern (Italianate, fine-dining-Chinese fusion); chili crisp reads contemporary-comfortable; sesame paste reads classical Northern Chinese; soy-cured roe reads Japanese-inflected modern Chinese. The same base supports all four because the lotus-and-herb-oil foundation is structurally neutral.

The wagon-wheel matters. The visual identity of the dish is the lotus root cross-section; drape the topping across the rounds in a way that lets the air channels show through. Burying the rounds under sauce defeats the entire format. Restraint with the topping is the rule: 1 tsp per round is plenty for any of the options.

Why the herb oil over plain dressing. The hot-oil-over-herbs technique briefly fries the herbs in the oil, releasing aromatics into the fat and softening the herbs from chewy-fibrous to tender-aromatic. The technique borrows from the Chinese ginger-scallion oil approach (see recipes/ginger-scallion-oil). The result is a dressed lotus that reads more aromatic and less raw-herbal than a simple scatter of chopped herbs would produce.

Eating mechanics. Cold-platter format. Each diner picks rounds with chopsticks and eats them one or two at a time. The dish is light and aromatic; it pairs well with stronger flavors elsewhere on the table, a stir-fry, a braise, a soup. Reheats poorly (the herb oil flattens, the lotus loses crunch); make and serve.
