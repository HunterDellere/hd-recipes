Ginger-scallion seared tuna

Sashimi-grade tuna seared bare and fast, sliced across the grain, and finished Cantonese-style with a fresh ginger-scallion relish bloomed by smoking-hot oil poured over it at the table. The technique is borrowed wholesale from steamed Cantonese fish: raw minced ginger and scallion sit on the fish, and a ladle of smoking neutral oil hits them to release their aromatics in a sizzle, the gentlest, cleanest way to dress a delicate protein. Here it lands on seared-rare tuna, with a light soy drizzle underneath.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The tuna is served rare, so it must be sashimi-grade or commercially flash-frozen for parasite kill (a home freezer cannot do this). Ask the counter "was this commercially flash-frozen." If you cannot source it, sear the block through to a cooked tuna-steak doneness, see the modification.
  • Two pans, two heats, running close together. The tuna sears in one hot pan; the finishing oil heats in a small separate pan or pot to smoking right as the sliced fish is plated. Have the ginger and scallion minced and piled on the fish before the oil goes on to heat, the oil must hit the aromatics the moment it smokes, or it cools and the bloom falls flat.
  • Sear from cold. A fridge-cold block holds a wider raw center through the sear; do not temper the fish.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the ginger-scallion relish

  1. 20 g fresh ginger, fine-minced, peeled and minced to 1 to 2 mm, or microplaned then squeezed of excess juice
  2. 30 g scallion, fine-minced, white and pale-green parts minced, dark-green tops sliced thin and reserved for garnish — (3 to 4 scallions)
  3. 1 g kosher salt
  4. 30 g neutral oil (high smoke point: refined avocado, rice bran, or grapeseed)

Phase 2, the soy drizzle

  1. 20 g light soy sauce
  2. 3 g rock sugar or granulated sugar, dissolved into the soy with a splash of hot water
  3. 2 g toasted sesame oil optional

Phase 3, the tuna

  1. 350 g sashimi-grade tuna loin, in two blocks, two rectangular blocks about 4 to 5 cm thick, patted completely dry; yellowfin or bigeye
  2. 3 g kosher salt
  3. 15 g neutral oil (for the sear)

Phase 4, garnish

  1. 5 g fresh cilantro leaves optional — (a small handful, picked; the classic Cantonese fish herb)

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the relish base. Pile the 20 g / 2 tbsp minced ginger and the 30 g / scallion whites and pale greens in a small heatproof bowl, toss with the 1 g / ¼ tsp kosher salt, and let sit 10 minutes. The salt draws out a little moisture and softens the raw bite so the hot-oil bloom lands evenly. Slice the dark-green scallion tops thin and set aside for garnish. Blooming aromatics
  2. 2
    Mix the soy drizzle. Dissolve the 3 g / ½ tsp rock sugar in the 20 g / 4 tsp light soy sauce with a teaspoon of hot water (rock sugar needs the heat to dissolve; granulated sugar dissolves cold). Stir in the optional 2 g / ¼ tsp toasted sesame oil. Set aside.
  3. 3
    Sear the tuna fast. Pat both 350 g / 12 oz blocks bone-dry and season all faces with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt. Heat a heavy skillet over high for 3 to 4 minutes until smoking, add the 15 g / 1 tbsp sear-oil, and swirl. Lay the first block in and sear 30 to 45 seconds per face, rolling around all four long sides plus a 15-second touch on each end, total about 3 minutes. The cooked-opaque band should creep in only 2 to 3 mm, leaving a wide cool ruby center. Sear the second block. Pull both to a board. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Slice and plate, with the soy underneath. Rest the blocks 60 seconds to halt carryover, then slice each across the grain into 1 cm slabs. Spoon the soy drizzle across a serving plate as a thin pool, lay the tuna slices over it cut-face up (soy under the fish, not over it, so the slices do not go soggy and the ruby center shows). Pile the salted ginger-scallion relish in a neat mound on top of the slices.
  5. 5
    Bloom the relish with smoking oil. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp bloom-oil in a small pan or pot over high heat until it is genuinely smoking and shimmering hard, 1 to 2 minutes, it must be hotter than the sear oil, hot enough to sizzle violently on contact. Pour it directly and evenly over the ginger-scallion mound; it will hiss and foam and the kitchen will fill with the smell of bloomed ginger and allium. This is the moment the dish comes together: the raw aromatics cook just enough to lose their sharp edge and release their fragrant oils onto the fish. Hot-oil finish (smoking-oil bloom over raw aromatics)
  6. 6
    Garnish and serve immediately. Scatter the reserved sliced scallion greens and the optional 5 g cilantro over the top. Serve right away, while the bloomed oil is still fragrant and the tuna center is cool; spoon some of the now-aromatic oil and soy from the bottom of the plate over each portion as you serve.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein no sashimi-grade fish available→fully cooked tuna steak, or a whole steamed fish

    Do not serve rare from a regular counter. Either sear the salted block through (about 90 seconds per face, pull at 55 to 60 °C internal), then apply the relish and hot-oil bloom as written; or run the technique on its home turf and steam a whole white fish (sea bass, snapper) 8 to 10 minutes, then top with the relish and bloom. The hot-oil finish works identically on cooked fish.

  • Heat aromatic direction→add chili and Sichuan pepper to the bloom

    Drop 2 g / 1 tsp thinly sliced fresh red chili and a pinch of crushed Sichuan peppercorn into the relish mound before the oil pour. The smoking oil blooms the chili and the huajiao along with the ginger and scallion, pivoting the dish from clean Cantonese toward a numbing-warm Sichuan register. Keep the soy drizzle as is.

  • Occasion service as a rice bowl→ginger-scallion tuna over rice

    Lay the seared sliced tuna over a bowl of hot jasmine rice, spoon the soy and bloomed ginger-scallion oil over the top so it runs down into the rice, and finish with the scallion greens and cilantro. The rice soaks up the aromatic oil and soy and the dish becomes a complete one-bowl meal.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • sashimi-grade tuna loin → sashimi-grade salmon, or a whole steamed white fish Salmon works seared the same way (sear slightly less). For the most traditional expression of the technique, skip searing and steam a whole fish, the ginger-scallion-and-hot-oil finish was built for it.
  • light soy sauce → a Cantonese steamed-fish soy (seasoned soy), or light soy plus a pinch more sugar Bottled "seasoned soy for seafood" (zheng yu chi you) is purpose-made for this dress and the most authentic pick. Plain light soy plus the rock sugar as written is the from-scratch equivalent.
  • rock sugar → granulated sugar or a few drops of honey Rock sugar gives the cleanest sweetness and a faint gloss; granulated sugar (dissolved cold) is a fine one-for-one. Honey reads slightly floral and works in a pinch.
  • fresh ginger → no good substitute Fresh ginger is structural here; dried ground ginger is a completely different, flat-and-dusty flavor and will not bloom in oil. If you have no fresh ginger, make a different seared-tuna preparation.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → the ginger-scallion relish (ginger, scallion, oil, salt) make it yourself The full ginger-scallion oil recipe on this site is the same relish made in larger batch and kept in the fridge, ready to spoon over fish, rice, congee, or noodles any night. Making a jar of it once means this dish (and a dozen others) is a two-pan, ten-minute job thereafter; the from-scratch version also teaches the salt-cure-then-bloom logic that makes the raw aromatics mellow correctly.
  • → light soy sauce make it yourselfstub On a dress this lean, where soy is one of only two seasonings, a fresher house light soy reads cleaner and less flatly salty than bottled. It is a long ferment but a single batch seasons dozens of dishes.

Notes

Why the hot-oil bloom, and why it has to smoke. Raw ginger and scallion carry sharp, volatile, slightly harsh compounds (gingerol, the allium thiosulfinates) that read aggressive on delicate fish. Pouring smoking-hot oil over them does in two seconds what gentle cooking does in minutes: it flash-heats the surface of the minced aromatics, driving off the harshest top-notes and releasing the rounder, fragrant oils into the pooling oil that then dresses the fish. The oil has to be genuinely smoking (well above 200 °C) for this to work, a merely-warm oil soaks into the aromatics without blooming them and the relish stays raw and sharp. This is the exact technique Cantonese kitchens use to finish a steamed whole fish, and it is the lightest, cleanest way to dress any delicate protein.

Soy under, oil over. The plating order is deliberate. The soy goes down first as a thin pool, the fish sits on it, and the bloomed oil goes over the top. Soy poured over the slices would soak in and the cut faces would go soggy and dark, hiding the ruby center; pooled underneath, it seasons each bite from below as you lift it without drowning the fish. The bloomed aromatic oil over the top is where the fragrance lives, so it belongs on the surface where the nose meets it first.

Restraint is the register. This is the lightest of the three seared-tuna preparations on this site: no crust, no glaze, just aromatics, hot oil, and a touch of sweetened soy. The whole point is to taste the fish, with the ginger-scallion as a clean fragrant frame. Resist the urge to add more, a heavier sauce would bury the delicate seared tuna that the technique exists to showcase.

Source still gates the dish. As with all seared-rare tuna, the center is functionally raw; the fish must be sashimi-grade or commercially flash-frozen for parasite kill, which a home freezer cannot achieve. If you cannot confirm flash-freezing with the counter, cook the block through or steam a whole fish instead (the modification above), both carry the ginger-scallion finish just as well.
