Som tum thai (green papaya salad)

Shredded green papaya bruised in a clay mortar with garlic, Thai bird chili, palm sugar, fish sauce, and lime, finished with dried shrimp, peanuts, yardlong bean, and cherry tomato. The dressing is built in the mortar as the papaya is pounded; the bruise is the technique.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Sticky rice is the canonical partner. Soak 200 g / 1 cup glutinous rice in cold water at least 4 hours ahead (overnight is better); when you begin the salad, drain and put it on to steam (recipe thai-sticky-rice). A 30 minute steam runs in parallel with the papaya shred, mortar work, and final toss.
  • Shred the papaya into the ice water and soak the shreds 10 minutes BEFORE building the mortar dressing. Cold water firms the cell walls so the shreds bend without breaking under the pestle; without the soak the papaya pulps to mush in the final pound.
  • Use a clay mortar (krok din) with a wooden pestle, not a granite mortar. Granite mortars are designed for hard curry pastes and will crush som tum to a paste; the clay-and-wood combination bruises rather than pulverizes. If you only have granite, lighten every strike noticeably.
  • The salad does not hold. Have diners at the table and rice ready to portion before the papaya hits the mortar; the dish begins to weep within 15 to 20 minutes and the textural contrast collapses.

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, papaya prep

  1. 500 g green (unripe) papaya, peeled, seed cavity scraped, shredded into 2 mm × 5 cm matchsticks — (about 1 medium green papaya; flesh should be firm, white-green, no orange ripening)

Phase 2, mortar base

  1. 12 g garlic, peeled — (3 medium cloves)
  2. 4 g thai bird chili, fresh, stems removed, halved
  3. 25 g palm sugar, shaved or finely chopped from the disc
  4. 15 g dried shrimp, small, rinsed in warm water 1 minute, drained

Phase 3, dressing liquids

  1. 25 g fish sauce (premium, 30 to 40°N)
  2. 35 g lime juice, fresh
  3. 2 each spent lime shells — (2 halves from the limes above; bruise into the dressing for citrus-peel oils)
  4. 10 g tamarind paste (concentrate) optional

Phase 4, structural

  1. 80 g yardlong bean (long bean), cut into 4 cm / 1.5-inch lengths — (4 to 5 beans; if unavailable, haricots verts work but bring less sweetness)
  2. 150 g cherry tomatoes, halved

Phase 5, finish

  1. 30 g roasted peanuts, skin-on or blanched, lightly crushed under the side of a knife, not powdered

Execution

  1. 1
    Peel the 500 g green papaya with a Y-peeler, then shred it into 2 mm × 5 cm matchsticks. The traditional method is to hold the peeled papaya in one hand and chop into the surface with a heavy cleaver in fast parallel cuts at 2 mm spacing, rotating the fruit slightly between strikes, then shave a thin layer of those slits off as julienne strips. A box grater on the largest tooth is the home substitute and produces a slightly more uniform but less ragged shred. Soak the shreds in ice water for 10 minutes while you build the dressing, cold water firms the cell walls so the shreds bend without breaking under the pestle. Mortar pounding (clay vs granite)
  2. 2
    In a clay mortar (krok din), pound the 12 g / 3 cloves garlic and 4 g Thai chilies to a coarse paste, 8 to 10 strikes, no more. The garlic should be bruised and broken, not creamed; chili seeds should be cracked but recognizable. A clay mortar with a wooden pestle is the correct tool, granite mortars (krok hin) are designed for hard curry pastes and crush som tum to mush. Mortar pounding (clay vs granite)
  3. 3
    Add the 25 g / 2 tbsp shaved palm sugar and 15 g / 2 tbsp rinsed dried shrimp. Pound 10 to 15 strikes until the palm sugar is broken down into the chili-garlic paste and the dried shrimp are torn into rough fibers. The sugar dissolves into the chili oil released from pounding; the dried shrimp release a glutamate-rich fishy depth that compounds with the fish sauce later. Do not pound to a smooth paste, texture is the point. Mortar pounding (clay vs granite)
  4. 4
    Add the 25 g / 1½ tbsp fish sauce, 35 g / 2½ tbsp lime juice, and the 2 spent lime shells. If using tamarind, add 10 g / 2 tsp now. Stir with the pestle to dissolve the sugar fully into the liquid, then pound the lime shells lightly 5 to 6 times to express the citrus-peel oils into the dressing. Taste from the pestle: the dressing should read salty (fish sauce), sweet (palm sugar), sour (lime, tamarind), and hot (chili) all in audible range, none dominant. This is the four-flavor balance, adjust now, before vegetables go in. The papaya will dilute everything.
  5. 5
    Drain the 500 g papaya shreds and shake dry. Add the 80 g yardlong bean (4 cm lengths) to the mortar first and pound 4 to 6 strikes, long beans need to be bruised before the papaya goes in, or they will sit in the dressing without absorbing it. The dull thud of the pestle on bean is the cue; you should see the bean walls fracture and exude faint juice. Mortar pounding (clay vs granite)
  6. 6
    Add the 150 g halved cherry tomatoes. Pound 6 to 8 strikes, rotating the mortar with one hand and pestling with the other, the tomatoes should split open and release their gel into the dressing without being completely crushed. The tomato gel adds glutamate and pectin, which thickens the dressing into something that will cling to papaya rather than running off.
  7. 7
    Add the drained papaya in two batches. With each batch, pound and toss simultaneously: hold a long spoon in one hand and the pestle in the other, pestling the papaya from above while the spoon pulls dressing-soaked shreds up from the bottom. Forty to fifty strikes total, alternating pound and toss, until every shred is glossy with dressing and the papaya has slumped from rigid white shred to pliable pale-orange. The bruise is the technique, it ruptures the papaya cells and pulls dressing into the matrix instead of leaving it pooled at the bottom. Mortar pounding (clay vs granite)
  8. 8
    Lift the spent lime shells out and discard. Taste from the mortar one more time. The salad should read aggressively seasoned but balanced: chili heat in the throat, lime-and-fish-sauce on the front of the tongue, palm-sugar caramel on the back, papaya texture between crunchy and pliable. If flat, add 5 g / 1 tsp more fish sauce; if too sweet, 5 g / 1 tsp more lime juice; if not hot enough, pound in another half-chili. The mortar lets you adjust without dirtying another bowl.
  9. 9
    Pile the salad onto a plate. Scatter the 30 g / ¼ cup crushed peanuts over the top, peanuts are added at the plate, not the mortar, because the pounding crushes them to powder and erases the textural contrast that makes the dish read three-dimensional. Eat immediately at room temperature with sticky rice and grilled chicken, or alongside larb. The papaya begins to weep within 15 to 20 minutes; the dish does not hold.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→som tum lao / som tum pla ra (Isaan-style)

    Replace half the fish sauce (15 g / 1 tbsp) with pla ra, fermented fish sauce, opaque and aggressively funky, sold in jars at Thai markets. Add 1 to 2 small salted black field crabs (pu khem), pounded with the garlic in step 2, in their shells. Drop the dried shrimp and the peanuts. Heat goes up by 50%, Isaan eaters take 8 to 10 chilies. The result is funkier, more savory-fermented, and less Bangkok-pretty.

  • Protein cuisine→som tum poo (with raw salted blue crab)

    Add 1 small whole salted/fermented blue crab (pu khem) to the mortar with the garlic and chili in step 2; pound 6 to 8 strikes to crack the shell into the dressing. Eat the cracked shell pieces directly, the crab roe and cure-funk lift the dish. Standard practice in Bangkok and Isaan but raises raw-shellfish risk if not properly sourced; buy from a Thai market with fast turnover or skip.

  • Heat spice level→mild (farang) version

    Drop chilies to 1 g / 1 chili and remove the seeds before pounding. The dish loses some structure, Thai diners taste through the heat to the four-flavor balance, and at low heat the salt-sweet-sour reads less integrated, but is approachable for first-time eaters.

  • Occasion protein→som tum with grilled chicken / pork (full meal)

    Serve the salad alongside grilled chicken thigh marinated in fish sauce, garlic, white pepper, and palm sugar (gai yang) and a basket of warm sticky rice. The salad is rarely a standalone dish in Thailand, it lives at the center of a larger meal. Build the chicken in the morning, marinate 2 hours, grill while you pound.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • green papaya → green mango (unripe), shredded the same way Slightly more acidic, more crisp. The Vietnamese cousin (gỏi đu đủ becomes gỏi xoài). Reduce lime by 25% to compensate for the mango acid.
  • green papaya → firm jicama or kohlrabi, julienned Western produce-aisle substitute. Loses the slight floral papaya aroma and the tropical-fruit pectin character; the texture is closer but the flavor is generic. Acceptable but not equivalent.
  • thai bird chili → serrano or fresno chili Use 2 to 3× the gram weight; serranos and fresnos carry roughly half the capsaicin of Thai birds. The flavor reads slightly more vegetal-green and less floral-hot.
  • palm sugar → light brown sugar or coconut sugar Brown sugar is the closest commercial substitute but lacks the butterscotch-floral notes of real palm sugar. Use 90% by weight (palm sugar is slightly less sweet per gram). Coconut sugar is a closer flavor match if available.
  • tamarind paste (concentrate) → omit, or use 2 g / 1 tsp pomegranate molasses Tamarind is optional in som tum thai; many recipes skip it entirely. Pomegranate molasses brings sour-sweet but reads Levantine; use sparingly.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → fish sauce (premium, 30 to 40°N) make it yourselfstub Twelve-month enzymatic fermentation of fresh anchovies and salt produces a fish sauce with brighter top notes and less industrial salinity than commercial bottles. A genuine project, not a weeknight swap.
  • → roasted peanuts, skin-on or blanched make it yourself Dry-roasting raw peanuts at home (12 to 15 minutes at 175°C) yields a fresher, more aromatic kernel than any pre-roasted bag, and the toast level is tunable to the dish.

Notes

The thesis is bruise-driven sauce uptake. Som tum is not tossed; it is pounded. Pounding ruptures cell walls in the papaya, the long bean, and the tomato simultaneously, which lets the dressing migrate into the vegetable matrix instead of beading on intact cell surfaces. A som tum built without a mortar, papaya and dressing tossed in a bowl, is structurally a different dish: dressing pools at the bottom, papaya stays sharp-crisp without absorbing, the four flavors sit next to each other instead of integrating.

Why a clay mortar (krok din), not a granite mortar (krok hin). Granite mortars are designed for crushing hard dry ingredients into pastes, Thai curry pastes, spice blends. They are too aggressive for vegetables; a granite-pounded som tum is mush. Clay mortars are softer-walled and slightly larger, with a wooden pestle, and the bruise they deliver fractures cell walls without grinding. If you only own a granite mortar, use a wooden spoon-pestle (a long flat-ended wooden spatula) and reduce strike force; or build the dressing in the granite mortar and transfer to a wide bowl for the vegetable pounding phase using the back of a heavy spoon.

Four-flavor balance, in numbers. The dressing as written (25 g fish sauce, 35 g lime juice, 25 g palm sugar, 4 g chili) is calibrated to the central-Thai/Bangkok som tum thai profile: salty-front, sour-prominent, sweet-warm, hot-but-not-aggressive. Isaan versions push the salt-funk axis (more pla ra, more chili, less sugar) and the sour axis (more lime). If your home version tastes one-dimensional, the failure is almost always under-seasoning rather than imbalance: double everything proportionally and re-taste before pulling individual levers.

Salting the lime shells in. Spent lime halves carry citrus oil in the rind, limonene, geranial, and other terpenes that the juice itself does not contain. Bruising the shells in the mortar with the dressing extracts those oils into the fish-sauce-palm-sugar-lime liquid, adding a perfume layer that distinguishes pounded som tum from a stirred dressing. Lift the shells out before adding papaya so the bitterness of pith does not bleed in.

Why peanuts at the plate. Pounded peanuts in the mortar break to oily powder and disappear into the dressing as fat. Peanuts at the plate retain their shatter and provide the textural counterpoint that separates som tum thai from the peanut-less Isaan versions. The dish is a textural three-way: pliable papaya, juicy split tomato, crunchy peanut.

Eat immediately. The salt and bruising together pull water out of the papaya progressively; within 15 to 20 minutes the dish weeps a watery liquid into the bowl and the shreds slump from pliable to limp. Som tum is a la-minute food.
