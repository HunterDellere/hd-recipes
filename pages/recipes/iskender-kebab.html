Recipe

İskender kebab

The Bursa invention: sliced döner-style lamb (or beef) reformatted from the vertical spit to the home skillet, layered over bite-sized pieces of toasted pide bread, drenched in a tomato sauce thinned with broth, crowned with hot foaming brown butter poured tableside, and served with a side of thick Turkish yogurt. The structural genius of the dish is its layered architecture: the bread soaks the tomato sauce from below, the meat sits in the middle, the brown butter coats the meat from above, and the cold yogurt at the side keeps each bite balanced. The unofficial national dish of Bursa and one of the most engineered plates in Turkish cookery.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Home cooks cannot replicate a vertical döner spit. The work-around: marinate thin-sliced lamb leg or shoulder in yogurt and aromatics for 4 to 12 hours, then sear in a smoking hot pan in single-layer batches. The result is structurally adjacent to spit-shaved meat; not identical, but the closest the home stove offers.
  • Stale or day-old pide bread is better than fresh; the dried crumb absorbs the tomato sauce without disintegrating. If using fresh, toast aggressively before cutting and assembling.
  • The tableside butter pour is the showpiece. Have the butter foaming and brown in a small saucepan when you bring the plates to the table; pour it over the meat right in front of the diners. Cold butter ladled on at the kitchen ruins the theater and the structural temperature contrast.

Phase 1, the meat marinade (4 to 12 hours ahead)

  1. 600 g lamb leg (or shoulder, or beef sirloin), sliced as thinly as possible against the grain (3 to 4 mm; freezing for 30 minutes first makes this easier)
  2. 100 g whole-milk plain yogurt
  3. 100 g yellow onion, grated on the small holes of a box grater and squeezed through a sieve; reserve 50 g juice, discard pulp — (½ medium onion; juice carries the enzymes)
  4. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  5. 3 g ground cumin
  6. 3 g pul biber or Aleppo pepper
  7. 8 g kosher salt
  8. 2 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Phase 2, the tomato sauce

  1. 30 g unsalted butter
  2. 1 × 6 oz / 170 g can tomato paste
  3. 1 × 28 oz / 794 g can canned crushed tomatoes (or 250 g fresh, peeled and chopped)
  4. 250 g chicken or beef broth
  5. 3 g fine sea salt
  6. 2 g granulated sugar

Phase 3, the assembly

  1. 300 g Turkish pide bread (or Lebanese pita, or country sourdough), cut into 2 cm cubes; if fresh, toast in a 200°C oven 5 minutes until dry on the surface but still soft inside — (2 pide, day-old preferred; the bread absorbs the tomato sauce as the base layer)
  2. 15 g neutral oil

Phase 4, the tableside brown butter

  1. 90 g unsalted butter

Phase 5, the side yogurt

  1. 300 g whole-milk Greek yogurt (or thick plain yogurt)

Execution

  1. 1
    Marinate the meat (4 to 12 hours ahead): in a bowl, combine the 600 g sliced lamb, 100 g / scant ½ cup yogurt, 50 g onion juice, 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil, 3 g / 1½ tsp cumin, 3 g / 1½ tsp pul biber, 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt, and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper. Turn to coat every slice. Cover and refrigerate 4 to 12 hours.
  2. 2
    Make the tomato sauce: melt the 30 g / 2 tbsp butter in a small saucepan over medium heat. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp tomato paste; cook 90 seconds, stirring, until the paste deepens in color from bright red to brick-red and smells caramelized. Add the 200 g / ¾ cup crushed tomatoes, 250 g / 1 cup broth, 3 g / ½ tsp salt, and 2 g / ½ tsp sugar. Bring to a simmer; cook 8 to 10 minutes until slightly thickened (the sauce should be loose enough to ladle and pool but not watery; coats the back of a spoon and pours in a steady stream). Adjust salt. Keep warm.
  3. 3
    Toast the bread: if using fresh pide, cut into 2 cm cubes and toast on a sheet pan at 200°C / 400°F for 5 minutes until the surface is dry and crisp. Day-old pide can be cubed and used directly.
  4. 4
    Sear the meat: heat a large heavy skillet (cast iron or carbon steel) over high heat until smoking, 3 to 4 minutes. Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil. Working in two or three batches (do not crowd the pan; crowded meat steams instead of searing), add a single layer of marinated lamb slices and cook 60 to 90 seconds per side until deeply browned with crisp edges. Transfer each batch to a warm plate; deglaze any heavy fond between batches with 30 g water and scrape it back over the cooked meat (this is the structural meat juice for the dish).
  5. 5
    Assemble the plates: divide the 300 g bread cubes among 4 wide shallow plates as the base layer. Ladle one-quarter of the warm tomato sauce over the bread of each plate (about 100 g per plate); the bread should be visibly soaked but not drowning. Mound one-quarter of the seared meat on top of the soaked bread on each plate.
  6. 6
    Bring the plates to the table. In a small saucepan over medium-high heat, melt the 90 g / 6 tbsp butter and cook past the foam stage 2 to 3 minutes until the milk solids turn deep amber and the butter smells of toasted hazelnut. Carry the foaming hot brown butter to the table and pour about 22 g (1½ tbsp) over the meat of each plate; the butter will sizzle on contact with the warm meat and tomato sauce. The pour is the structural showpiece of the dish. Brown butter (beurre noisette)
  7. 7
    Serve immediately with a small bowl of the 300 g Greek yogurt (about 75 g per person) on the side. The diner alternates bites of meat-and-bread with cool yogurt; the contrast of hot brown butter and cold yogurt is the structural rhythm of the dish. A simple side salad of sliced tomato, cucumber, and red onion dressed with lemon and olive oil is the canonical Bursa accompaniment.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein with beef sirloin (the modern Bursa standard)→with thin-sliced beef sirloin instead of lamb

    Substitute 600 g beef sirloin, sliced 3 mm against the grain, for the lamb. Most contemporary Bursa kebab houses serve beef rather than lamb; the dish reads slightly less aromatic but is structurally correct. The sear time stays the same (60 to 90 seconds per side).

  • Protein with chicken (a modern lighter variant)→as tavuk iskender (chicken iskender)

    Substitute 600 g chicken thigh meat, sliced 5 mm thick, for the lamb. Add 15 g / 1 tbsp of the saffron-and-paprika bloomed oil to the marinade for the canonical color. Cook the chicken slightly longer (90 seconds per side) and slightly cooler heat (medium-high vs maximum) so the surface caramelizes without the inside drying. A common modern lighter variant in Turkish kebab houses.

  • Occasion served on a single platter for sharing→as a family-style assemble-at-the-table iskender

    Use a single wide shallow platter; spread the bread cubes across the platter, ladle the tomato sauce evenly, mound the meat down the center, and pour the brown butter over the entire platter at the table. Serve the yogurt in a single bowl and bring forks for each diner. The platter version reads more communal and is common at family meals and casual dining.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • lamb leg (or shoulder, or beef sirloin) → beef sirloin or skirt steak Most modern Bursa kebab houses serve beef sirloin; the dish reads cleaner and less aromatic than the lamb version. Skirt steak (sliced very thin against the grain) is a workable home substitute with similar texture.
  • Turkish pide bread → Lebanese pita, country sourdough, or any sturdy flatbread The structural requirement is a bread that absorbs the tomato sauce without disintegrating; any sturdy flatbread or country loaf works. Avoid soft sandwich bread (turns to mush) and very thin lavash (too thin to hold the sauce).

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → Turkish pide bread (or Lebanese pita, or country sourdough) make it yourself Home-baked pide that has dried out 24 hours is the canonical bread base; the day-old crumb absorbs the tomato sauce ideally and the flavor is dramatically better than commercial flatbread.
  • → chicken or beef broth make it yourself Home-simmered stock has gelatin and depth that boxed broth lacks; the difference shows in the body of the tomato sauce.

Notes

Why the brown butter is structural. The tableside pour of foaming brown butter is not theater; it is the structural flavor-and-temperature climax of the dish. The brown butter contributes three things simultaneously: (1) the Maillard products in the browned milk solids (the toasted hazelnut character that defines beurre noisette), (2) the high pour temperature that re-heats the meat and renders some of the lamb fat into the tomato sauce, and (3) the visual and aromatic moment of the pour itself. Skipping the butter, using unbrowned butter, or pouring before the bake produces a dish that is recognizably iskender-adjacent but structurally incomplete.

Why the bread layer matters. The pide cubes underneath the meat are not a garnish; they are the structural carrier of the tomato sauce. As the diner works through the plate, each bite combines meat (the protein), bread (the starch), sauce (the moisture and acid), brown butter (the fat and aromatic), and yogurt (the cooling counterpoint). This five-element architecture in one bite is what makes the dish more than the sum of its parts; remove any one element and the structural balance collapses. The bread layer is also why iskender keeps the dish satisfying with relatively little meat per portion (150 g per person reads as a full meal).

Versatility. The marinated lamb travels onto: pita wraps for a quick döner, over rice for a different kebab plate, in lavash rolls with vegetables and tahini. The tomato sauce is structurally the Turkish menemen sauce minus the eggs and works on grilled chicken, poached fish, and as a topping for grilled bread.

Pairs across Turkish (canonical, especially Bursa), broader Anatolian, Cypriot, and modern Turkish diaspora cookery. The dish is also widely served in German-Turkish restaurants where döner culture is deeply rooted; the iskender format is one of the most exported Turkish dishes.

Storage. The components keep separately: marinated raw meat 48 hours refrigerated, cooked meat 3 days refrigerated, tomato sauce 4 days and freezes 2 months. Reassemble at service; iskender does not reheat well as an assembled dish (the bread becomes mush and the brown butter cannot be re-poured hot). The yogurt keeps a week.

Sources
  • İskenderoğlu of Bursa, late 19th century. The dish was invented by İskender Efendi who rotated the lamb spit from horizontal (the older kebab tradition) to vertical, creating what is now known globally as döner kebab; the plated form with bread, tomato sauce, and brown butter is the original Bursa presentation that bears his name. The Bursa kebab house Kebapçı İskender, run by his descendants, still serves the canonical version. — The vertical spit is structural for the canonical version; thin-shaved layered marinated lamb compressed and roasted vertically against a heat source produces a meat with distinctive texture (crispy outer slices, juicy interior slices, the fat constantly basting). The home approximation here uses thin-sliced lamb seared in batches in a hot pan; the texture is structurally adjacent but not identical. Beef sirloin is the most common modern substitute and what most Bursa kebab houses serve today.
