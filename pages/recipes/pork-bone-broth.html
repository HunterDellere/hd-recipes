Pork bone broth (zhu gu tang)

Pork bones blanched, then long-simmered for a clear, gelatin-rich Chinese-style broth. The base of Cantonese soups, congee, dumpling fillings, and noodle bowls. Modifications cover the rolling-boil milky version (tonkotsu-style for ramen) and the herbal Cantonese long-soup tradition.

Execution

  1. 1
    Blanch the bones. Combine the 1500 g pork bones, 3000 ml / 12 cups cold water, 20 g smashed ginger, and 30 ml / 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine in an 8-quart stockpot. Bring to a boil over high heat; brown-gray scum will rise aggressively. Boil hard for 5 minutes, this purges blood and surface impurities that would cloud the stock. Drain into a colander, discard the blanching liquid, and rinse the bones briefly under cold water to remove any clinging scum.
  2. 2
    Start the simmer. Return the rinsed bones to the cleaned pot. Add the 3500 ml / 14 cups fresh cold water, 25 g smashed ginger, 4 scallion whites, and the optional 1 onion and 4 jujube. Bring slowly to the threshold of a simmer over medium heat (about 90°C / 195°F).
  3. 3
    Skim. Once at simmer-threshold, gray foam will appear briefly, much less than the blanch produced, and a clear fat layer will develop. Skim the foam with a flat spoon for 5 to 10 minutes; do not skim the clear fat. Reduce heat to maintain a bare tremor of a simmer (just below 90°C, no active bubbles). The blanch step did the heavy purification work; this skim is fine-tuning.
  4. 4
    Simmer 4 to 6 hours. The broth will reduce by 25 to 30%, deepen from clear to pale amber, and develop noticeable body from gelatin extraction. Cover loosely with the lid askew. Do not boil, the entire point of the clear-broth tradition is keeping the heat below boiling so the broth stays glassy.5 h
  5. 5
    Strain. Lift bones and aromatics out with tongs. Strain the broth through a fine-mesh sieve into a second pot, then a second time through cheesecloth set inside the sieve for absolute clarity.
  6. 6
    Cool quickly. Divide between wide shallow pans set in an ice bath, stirring every 5 minutes. Refrigerate. Once cold, the fat layer that solidifies on top can be lifted off (rendered pork fat is the foundation of much southern Chinese stir-fry; reserve in a separate container) or left in for richer downstream uses.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional preparation style→tonkotsu-style milky broth (Japanese ramen base)

    Skip the blanch step entirely (or do a 60-second blanch only). Use 2.5 kg pork bones (heavily skewed to trotters and knuckle for collagen). Cover with 4 L cold water. Bring to a rolling boil and maintain a hard rolling boil for the entire cook, 8 to 12 hours, topping up water as it evaporates. The aggressive boil emulsifies fat and collagen into the water, producing the opaque cream-white broth that defines tonkotsu ramen. The Chinese clear-broth tradition and the Japanese ramen tradition use almost identical bones with opposite technique to produce structurally different broths.

  • Regional preparation style→Cantonese herbal soup (long-soup tradition)

    Add Chinese herbal-soup ingredients in the simmer phase: 30 g dried Chinese yam (huai shan), 20 g dried lotus seeds, 10 g dried Chinese wolfberries (gou qi zi), 4 dried jujube, and 200 g lean pork (cut into chunks, blanched separately). Simmer 3 to 4 hours. The result is a Cantonese herbal soup, served as the soup course of a banquet meal, where the broth and the simmered herbs are eaten together rather than the broth being a base ingredient.

  • Occasion time-shortcut→pressure-cooker version

    Use the blanch step as written. Then transfer to an Instant Pot or pressure cooker with the same fresh aromatics and water. High pressure 2 hours, natural release. The broth is somewhat cloudy from the boiling-under-pressure but the gelatin and flavor extraction is excellent.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • mixed pork bones → pork neck bones (most common at supermarket butchers) Acceptable substitute for the routine clear broth. Pork necks have moderate gelatin and flavor; trotters and knuckles contribute more body and are worth seeking out at Asian groceries or farmers' markets.
  • Shaoxing wine (blanch step) → dry sherry 1:1 substitute. The wine is doing antimicrobial and flavor-cleaning work in the blanch.

Notes

Why blanch first. Pork bones carry significantly more blood and surface impurities than chicken bones; without the aggressive blanch step, the resulting broth has a muddy, overly-porky character. The blanch sacrifices the first 30 minutes of cooking time but pays back in clarity and flavor cleanliness. Skip the blanch only for the tonkotsu-style milky broth where clarity is not the goal.

Why no Western mirepoix. Pork bone broth in the Chinese tradition uses ginger, scallion, sometimes onion or jujube, but never carrots or celery. The Western mirepoix flavor profile (sweet carrot, herbal celery) is wrong for Chinese cooking; it tastes "mirepoix" rather than "Chinese broth" downstream. The aromatic minimalism of the Chinese version is deliberate; the bones contribute most of the flavor and the broth is meant to be neutral enough to take on whatever soup, congee, or braise is built on it.

Use cases. Cantonese rice porridge (congee) cooked with pork broth instead of water is the foundational application; the broth deepens the flavor of plain congee dramatically without being explicit. Wonton soup, Chinese cabbage and tofu soup, daikon and pork rib soup. Braised dishes (red-braised pork belly, Cantonese braised tofu) that need a deep base. As the simmer liquid for stuffed lotus root, stuffed bean curd, and other multi-component braises. Frozen in 500 ml containers, the broth keeps 6 months and is the foundational pantry asset for any home cook regularly making southern Chinese food.

Storage. Refrigerated, defatted: 5 days. Refrigerated with fat cap: 7 days. Frozen: 6 months. Cold-set jellied broth (the gelatin-rich version that goes solid in the fridge) is normal and indicates excellent extraction; reheats to liquid in seconds.
