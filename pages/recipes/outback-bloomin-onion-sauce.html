Outback bloomin onion sauce (homemade copycat)

The horseradish-mayo-ketchup-cayenne dipping sauce that comes with Outback Steakhouse's deep-fried onion. Creamy, tangy, hot, and pale pink-orange; the horseradish is the load-bearing aromatic, the cayenne provides slow back-of-tongue heat, and the ketchup contributes color and a faint sweet base. Reads as a remoulade crossed with a cocktail sauce.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Whisk-and-rest, not whisk-and-serve. The sauce needs at least 60 minutes refrigerated before service. Prepared horseradish is volatile when fresh-mixed; the allyl isothiocyanate (the sinus-burn compound) is at its sharpest and reads as a one-dimensional hot punch. After 60 minutes in the cold the horseradish settles into the matrix and reads as integrated heat with the cayenne providing the slow back-of-tongue burn underneath.
  • For best result, make it 4+ hours ahead. The franchise calibration is a horseradish that registers as warmth and complexity rather than as nose-burn; that integration takes time the cook cannot shortcut.

Sauce

  1. 120 g mayonnaise (full-fat, real-egg, ideally homemade)
  2. 30 g ketchup
  3. 25 g prepared horseradish (white, refrigerated, drained)
  4. 1 g cayenne pepper
  5. 0.5 g dried oregano
  6. 1 g paprika (sweet, not smoked)
  7. 0.5 g garlic powder
  8. 0.5 g fine sea salt — (pinch; the horseradish brings salt)
  9. 0.5 g freshly cracked black pepper

Execution

  1. 1
    Drain the 25 g / 1½ tbsp prepared horseradish in a fine-mesh sieve for 30 seconds before measuring; the liquid in commercial horseradish jars varies and undrained horseradish will thin the sauce and dilute the heat. Press gently with a spoon to push off excess brine.
  2. 2
    In a small bowl combine the 120 g / ½ cup mayonnaise, 30 g / 2 tbsp ketchup, the 25 g / 1½ tbsp drained horseradish, 1 g / ½ tsp cayenne, 1 g / ½ tsp paprika, 0.5 g / ¼ tsp garlic powder, 0.5 g / ¼ tsp dried oregano, 0.5 g pinch salt, and 0.5 g / ¼ tsp black pepper.
  3. 3
    Whisk vigorously for 30 seconds until completely smooth and uniformly pale pink-orange with visible horseradish flecks. The cayenne and paprika should fully disperse into the fat phase; if red specks remain on the surface, push them through with the back of the spoon until the color is even.
  4. 4
    Taste a small spoonful with the understanding that the heat will read sharper now than after the rest. Adjust only the salt at this point. The horseradish and cayenne calibration is what it is; under-doses can be added after the rest, over-doses cannot be removed.
  5. 5
    Cover and refrigerate at least 1 hour before serving, 4 hours is markedly better. The horseradish needs to settle from sharp-nasal-burn to integrated background heat; this is the whole point of the rest. Holds 1 week refrigerated in a sealed jar; the heat slowly fades after day 4 as the horseradish volatiles dissipate.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional flavor→remoulade-leaning version

    Add 1 tsp Dijon mustard, 1 tsp finely minced capers, and 5 g / 1 tsp finely minced shallot. Pushes the sauce toward a proper remoulade, which pairs well with cold seafood and fried fish in addition to the onion.

  • Heat heat→extra-hot version

    Double the cayenne to 2 g / 1 tsp and add 5 ml / 1 tsp Tabasco. The horseradish nasal heat layers with the cayenne capsaicin burn and the Tabasco vinegar bite; produces a sauce that reads distinctly hot rather than as warm.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • prepared horseradish → wasabi paste (5 g / 1 tsp) + 20 g extra mayo Wasabi paste is most often horseradish-based anyway and provides the same nasal-heat compound. Reduce dose substantially, wasabi paste is more concentrated than prepared horseradish.
  • cayenne pepper → gochugaru Korean coarse red pepper, milder and fruitier than cayenne. Use double the dose (2 g / 1 tsp). Produces a different heat profile that some prefer.
  • mayonnaise → half mayo + half sour cream Tangier and lighter. Loses some of the body but adds a yogurt-like tang that pairs well with the horseradish.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → mayonnaise (full-fat, real-egg, ideally homemade) make it yourselfstub Real-egg mayo makes a meaningfully cleaner bloomin onion sauce; cleaner egg flavor, no preservatives, and the horseradish reads more crisply against fresh mayo.
  • → ketchup make it yourself Homemade ketchup made with canned tomatoes is meaningfully better than commercial; the sauce takes on a deeper tomato character that complements the horseradish.

Notes

The Outback bloomin onion sauce is a horseradish-forward riff on the broader cocktail-sauce family. Outback has never published the recipe but the chain has been operating since 1988 and the major copycat clones have converged on this ingredient set, with horseradish dose being the only meaningful variable. The 25 g / 1½ tbsp here is moderately heavy; reduce to 15 g if you want a milder version that lets the cayenne lead.

Prepared horseradish from the refrigerated section of the supermarket is non-negotiable. Shelf-stable horseradish in the condiment aisle has lost most of its volatile heat compounds during pasteurization and storage; it tastes flat and woody and will produce a sauce that reads as a dull mayo with red specks. The refrigerated section product, drained before measuring, has the bite needed.

The 60-minute minimum rest is the difference between a sauce that reads as horseradish-on-top-of-mayo and one that reads as the unified franchise spread. There is no shortcut; the rest is a passive chemistry process and cannot be accelerated by additional whisking.
