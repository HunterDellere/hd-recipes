Recipe

Roasted brussels sprouts with balsamic and parmesan

Halved sprouts roasted cut-face down at 230 °C until the flat side builds a deep mahogany Maillard crust and the loose outer leaves char to chips, then tossed off the heat with a reduced balsamic glaze and a shower of microplaned parmigiano. The dish is a single sweet-sour-bitter-umami chord: caramelized brassica char, the cooked-fruit sweetness and acid of reduced balsamic, and the glutamate-and-salt of aged hard cheese.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Reduce the balsamic first and let it sit. It takes 5 to 8 minutes to simmer down to a glaze and then thickens further as it cools; making it while the sprouts roast means it is ready and pourable the moment they come out, rather than either scorching from neglect or setting to a hard lacquer in the pan.
  • Preheat the sheet pan empty inside the oven. Sprouts laid on a cold pan steam in their own surface moisture before the cut face ever browns; a pan preheated to 230 °C sears the cut face on contact and is the single biggest lever on crust quality.

Phase 1, the roast

  1. 680 g brussels sprouts, trimmed at the stem, any loose or yellowed outer leaves removed, halved through the core (quartered if larger than a golf ball)
  2. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  3. 4 g kosher salt
  4. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Phase 2, the balsamic glaze

  1. 120 g balsamic vinegar (mid-grade IGP)
  2. 10 g honey optional

Phase 3, the finish

  1. 30 g parmigiano reggiano, microplaned to a fine fluff
  2. 1 g flaky finishing salt — (a pinch, for the table)

Execution

  1. 1
    Put a heavy rimmed sheet pan in the oven and preheat both to 230 °C / 450 °F. Give the pan a full 10 minutes at temperature so it is genuinely hot, not just warm; the cut face has to sear on contact.
  2. 2
    While the oven heats, reduce the balsamic. Pour the 120 g / ½ cup balsamic vinegar and the optional 10 g / 1½ tsp honey into a small saucepan and simmer over medium heat until it has reduced to roughly a third of its volume and coats the back of a spoon with a drag-line, 5 to 8 minutes. Pull it off the heat the moment it flows like warm syrup; it thickens substantially as it cools, and an over-reduced glaze sets to a hard lacquer. Set aside.
  3. 3
    Toss the 680 g / 1.5 lb halved sprouts in a bowl with the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil, the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper until every cut face is slicked with oil. Pull the hot pan out, and lay the sprouts cut-face down in a single uncrowded layer; they should sizzle faintly on contact. Spacing matters: crowded sprouts steam and never crisp, so use two pans if 1.5 lb does not fit in one clean layer. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Roast undisturbed for 20 minutes. Do not stir or flip early; the cut face needs uninterrupted contact with the hot pan to build the deep mahogany crust, and lifting the sprouts to peek resets that clock. The loose outer leaves scattered on the pan will char to crisp chips, which is the best part of the batch.
  5. 5
    Check at 20 minutes. The cut faces should be deep brown to nearly black at the edges, and a sprout should give easily when pressed with a spatula. If they are browned but the cores still feel firm, give them another 3 to 5 minutes. When they are done, tip them into a serving bowl while they are screaming hot.
  6. 6
    Finish off the heat. Drizzle the balsamic glaze over the hot sprouts and toss so it coats and clings (the residual heat loosens the glaze enough to spread). Taste a sprout: it should read sweet-sour against the char. Shower the 30 g / ½ cup microplaned parmigiano over the top so it half-melts into a savory dusting, scatter the pinch of flaky salt, and serve immediately while the leaves are still crisp.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein, to make it a main→add rendered bacon or pancetta

    Dice 110 g / 4 oz bacon or pancetta and render it in the cold oven-safe pan on the stovetop until crisp, then lift out the pork and toss the halved sprouts in the rendered fat (skip or halve the olive oil) before roasting in that same fat. Scatter the crisp pork back over at the finish with the cheese. The pork fat carries its own Maillard depth and salt, and the dish reads as a full plate rather than a side.

  • Heat heat→chili-balsamic

    Add 1 g / ½ tsp red pepper flakes to the balsamic as it reduces, so the capsaicin infuses the oil-soluble fraction of the glaze. The heat builds slowly against the sweet-sour glaze and the brassica char rather than spiking; calibrate up to 1 tsp for a genuinely hot version.

  • Regional cheese direction→pecorino and lemon

    Swap the parmigiano for the same weight of microplaned pecorino romano and add the zest of half a lemon plus a squeeze of juice at the finish in place of (or alongside) the balsamic. The dish pivots from sweet-sour-Modena toward sharp-salty-bright Roman, leaner and more aggressive on the palate.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • balsamic vinegar (mid-grade IGP) → red wine vinegar plus brown sugar, reduced together Simmer red wine vinegar with a teaspoon of brown sugar to rebuild the sweet-sour-syrupy profile. Pomegranate molasses is an even closer one-for-one, already syrupy and sweet-tart, no reduction needed, just drizzle.
  • parmigiano reggiano → pecorino romano or grana padano Pecorino is sharper and saltier (sheep's milk), so use a touch less; grana padano is milder and cheaper and works as a near-equal stand-in. Avoid pre-grated tubbed cheese, the anti-caking cellulose keeps it from melting into the sprouts.
  • extra-virgin olive oil → neutral high-smoke-point oil (avocado, grapeseed) At 230 °C a robust extra-virgin oil can edge toward bitter at the pan-contact points. A neutral oil with a higher smoke point browns cleaner if your oven runs hot; finish with a drizzle of good raw olive oil for flavor if you make the swap.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → balsamic vinegar (mid-grade IGP) make it yourself A from-scratch balsamic glaze lets you control the sweetness and viscosity exactly to the dish, and a slow reduction of a decent IGP with a touch of brown sugar or honey makes a glossier, more clinging syrup than the thickener-laden bottled "glaze" sold pre-reduced. It keeps for weeks refrigerated and turns this into a five-minute side thereafter.

Notes

Why cut-face down and why a preheated pan. A halved sprout presents a flat starchy cut face that is the ideal surface for Maillard browning, but only if it makes direct contact with metal hot enough to drive the reaction before the sprout''s own moisture turns to steam. A cold pan, or sprouts piled two deep, traps that steam and the sprouts boil-roast to a drab, soft, faintly sulfurous result. A pan preheated to full temperature sears the cut face on contact, locks in the crust, and the interior steams gently inside its own skin to tender. This single move is the difference between a side people pick at and one they fight over.

Why the balsamic goes on after, not before. Balsamic carries dissolved sugar from its cooked-grape-must base. Tossed onto raw sprouts before a 230 °C roast, that sugar scorches to acrid black carbon long before the 20-minute mark. Reduced separately to a glaze and added to the hot finished sprouts, the same sugar reads as sweet-sour caramel clinging to the char. The reduction also concentrates the acid, so the glaze stays sharp enough to cut the richness of the cheese rather than reading one-note sweet.

The leaf chips are a feature, not a loss. The loose outer leaves that fall off during tossing will scatter across the pan and char into crisp, dark, intensely savory chips. Do not pick them out or pile the sprouts to protect them; spread everything in one layer and let the strays crisp. They are the most concentrated bite in the dish.

Sizing and timing. Uniform size is what lets a whole pan finish at once. If your bag has a wide size spread, quarter the large sprouts and halve the small ones so every piece has roughly the same mass; otherwise the small ones burn while the large cores stay grassy. Twenty minutes is right for a halved golf-ball sprout on a properly preheated pan; check rather than trust the clock the first time you run it in your oven.
