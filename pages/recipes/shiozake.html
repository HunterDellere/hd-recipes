Shiozake (salt-cured broiled salmon)

Salmon fillet dry-cured in coarse salt for 24 hours to firm the flesh and concentrate flavor, then broiled until the skin is crisp and the interior just opaque. The canonical onigiri filling, breakfast fish, and bento staple. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 3 items ▾

Meat & seafood 1

  1. 500 g skin-on salmon fillet (sockeye, king, or coho)

Spices & dried herbs 1

  1. 25 g coarse sea salt (or kosher salt, Diamond Crystal)

Pantry 1

  1. 30 ml sake (for surface rinse) optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: pat the salmon dry. Optional sake rinse: brush both sides with sake, pat dry again. Sprinkle the salt evenly on both sides of each portion.
  2. 2
    Stub: place the salted fillets on a wire rack over a parchment-lined sheet pan, uncovered, in the refrigerator for 24 hours. The salt will draw moisture out of the flesh, the surface will firm, and the proteins will rearrange into the slightly translucent texture characteristic of Japanese cured fish.1 d 0 h
  3. 3
    Stub: pat the fillets dry once more (no rinsing; the surface salt is part of the seasoning). Broil skin-side-up for 6 to 8 minutes at high broil, 4 inches from the flame, until the skin is crisp and the thickest part of the flesh just turns opaque (52 to 55°C / 125 to 130°F internal). The traditional Japanese doneness leans rarer than Western preferences.
  4. 4
    Stub: rest 2 minutes. Serve hot as a breakfast portion with rice and miso soup; flake when cool for onigiri filling, bento boxes, or chazuke (rice with hot tea poured over).

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the salt-curing chemistry (osmotic dehydration of the surface, gentle protein denaturation that firms the texture without cooking, the concentration of free amino acids that produces the characteristic shiozake flavor), the salt-percentage spectrum (3% mild "amakuchi-jio" cure, 5% standard, 8 to 10% traditional shio-zake-jio that is meant to be soaked in water before cooking), the salmon-species spectrum (sockeye is the closest U.S. analog to Japanese ginzake/silver salmon; king is fattier and produces a richer shiozake; farmed Atlantic is acceptable but less flavorful), the broiling vs. grilling vs. pan-searing options (broiling is the home-kitchen standard, grilling over charcoal is the gold standard at restaurants, pan-searing in a dry skillet is acceptable for rushed mornings), the doneness tradition (Japanese cured salmon is intentionally cooked rarer than Western salmon, leaving a translucent center), the storage logic (cure for 24 hours minimum but up to 3 days; broiled portions keep 3 days refrigerated and freeze 1 month for onigiri prep), and the chazuke application (broken-up shiozake plus rice plus a splash of hot dashi or sencha is the canonical "Japanese pour-over comfort food").
