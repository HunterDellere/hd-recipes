Gochujang (homemade Korean fermented chili paste)

Glutinous rice cooked into porridge, blended with meju powder, gochugaru, and salt, fermented in earthen jars or sealed containers for 6 months minimum (ideally 1 to 3 years) until the rice starches convert to sugars and the flavor deepens into the signature sweet-spicy-funky-savory paste. Stub — a multi-month commitment.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 6 items ▾
  1. 500 g glutinous (sweet) rice flour, fine
  2. 2000 ml water (for rice porridge)
  3. 200 g barley malt powder (yeotgireum garu)
  4. 250 g meju powder (fermented soybean meal)
  5. 400 g gochugaru (Korean chili flakes, fine)
  6. 250 g unrefined sea salt (no iodine)

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: cook the rice porridge. Whisk the 500 g glutinous rice flour with the 2000 ml water in a heavy pot. Bring to a low simmer over medium heat, stirring constantly to prevent scorching, until the porridge thickens to a smooth, viscous gloop (about 20 minutes). Pull off heat.
  2. 2
    Stub: add the 200 g barley malt powder while the porridge is still warm (around 60°C / 140°F — too hot deactivates the enzymes, too cool slows them). Stir thoroughly. Let stand 1 hour at warm room temperature; the diastase enzymes will convert the rice starches to sugars and the porridge will thin out and turn sweet.
  3. 3
    Stub: when the porridge has cooled to room temperature (around 25 to 30°C), stir in the 250 g meju powder, 400 g gochugaru, and 250 g sea salt. Mix vigorously until uniform and no dry pockets remain. The mixture will be a thick deep-red paste.
  4. 4
    Stub: pack into a sterilized glass or earthen jar (or a Korean onggi if you have one), leaving 5 cm headspace. Cover the surface with a layer of plastic wrap pressed directly onto the paste (prevents oxidation), then seal with a lid that allows gas escape (a fermentation airlock works, or a loose lid checked weekly). Place in a sunny warm spot during day and a cool spot at night for the first month (this temperature fluctuation drives the early fermentation), then transfer to a cool dark spot for the long age.
  5. 5
    Stub: ferment 6 months minimum, ideally 1 to 3 years. Check monthly: scrape any white surface mold (kahm yeast, harmless) into the paste and re-cover; black or pink mold is contamination, scrape off the affected area generously and use the rest. The paste will deepen in color (red-brown to deep brown), develop a complex savory-funky-sweet aroma, and reduce slightly in volume as moisture evaporates. When ready, transfer to smaller jars and refrigerate for daily use.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the meju-sourcing question (Korean groceries carry it; making meju from scratch is its own project), the temperature management for the first month (why the day-night fluctuation matters — it drives the lactic acid bacteria and yeast cycles that produce the signature flavor), the difference between traditional onggi-fermentation and modern sealed-jar fermentation, the gochugaru selection (sun-dried Korean gochugaru produces the right color and flavor; commercial paprika does not work), the salt level (the 250 g salt for this batch is roughly 13% of total weight, which is the structural minimum for safe long-ferment without spoilage), the safety considerations (the salt and the starter culture from meju prevent pathogen growth — do not reduce salt below 12% or the ferment becomes unsafe), and the practical note that quality commercial gochujang (Mother-In-Law's, Chung Jung One Sunchang) is excellent and the homemade project is more about the experience and the connection to a centuries-old Korean tradition than the kitchen need. The stub above is correct in outline; the deeper drafting pass should add the chemistry and the safety and the cultural context.
