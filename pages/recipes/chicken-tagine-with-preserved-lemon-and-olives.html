Chicken tagine with preserved lemon and olives

The canonical Moroccan tagine: chicken thighs and legs slow-braised over a bed of sliced onion in a pool of olive oil, saffron-bloomed water, ginger, turmeric, cumin, garlic, and cilantro, with finely chopped preserved-lemon rind and meaty green olives added in the last twenty minutes so the lemon stays bright and the olives stay firm. The conical tagine lid (or a tight Dutch oven lid) is structural; the dome condenses steam and returns it to the dish, self-basting and concentrating flavor with very little added liquid. The defining Moroccan plate worldwide and the most reliable introduction to tagine technique.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Preserved lemons are structural for this dish; the rind is what is used (finely chopped), not the flesh. Quality preserved lemons (Moroccan-style, salt-cured 3 to 4 weeks) come in jars at Middle Eastern groceries or can be made at home. Sliced lemon in salt for a few days is not the same; the full ferment is what produces the floral, sour, saline-savory character.
  • Bloom the saffron 15 minutes before the braise starts. Grind 0.3 g threads with a pinch of sugar, steep in 50 g hot water; the bloomed liquid goes into the braise as the structural color and aroma carrier.
  • Serve with warm Moroccan flatbread (khobz, msemen, or pita) for sopping the sauce, or over steamed couscous. The dish is built around bread or grain to absorb the sauce.
  • A proper Moroccan tagine pot (conical clay lid over a shallow flared base) is ideal; a heavy Dutch oven with a tight lid produces a structurally adjacent result. The conical shape promotes more aggressive self-basting (the steam condenses on the high cone and runs down the sides into the dish); a flat Dutch oven lid does the same job less dramatically. Either works.

Execution

  1. 1
    Bloom the saffron: grind the 0.3 g saffron with 1 g sugar in a mortar; add the 50 g hot water; cover and steep 15 to 20 minutes. The liquid will turn deep amber.
  2. 2
    Season the 1200 g chicken pieces with 6 g / 1½ tsp of the kosher salt; rub the salt into the scored skin and meat. Set aside while you start the onion base.
  3. 3
    Heat the 45 g / 3 tbsp olive oil in a heavy 5-quart Dutch oven (or in the base of a tagine) over medium heat. Add the 300 g / 2 onions (sliced); sweat 10 to 12 minutes, stirring occasionally, until fully softened and translucent but not browned. The onions should look melted and golden, not caramelized; for tagine they cook into a soft bed under the chicken.
  4. 4
    Add the 16 g / 4 garlic cloves and 20 g grated ginger; cook 90 seconds, stirring, until fragrant. Add the 4 g / 2 tsp turmeric, 3 g / 1½ tsp cumin, 2 g / 1 tsp coriander, 2 g / 1 tsp paprika, 1 g / ½ tsp cinnamon, and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper; stir 30 seconds to bloom the spices in the onion fat (the oil will turn deep yellow-orange).
  5. 5
    Pour the bloomed saffron liquid into the pot along with the 200 g / ¾ cup water. Add half the chopped 30 g cilantro and the 15 g parsley. Nestle the chicken pieces into the onion bed, skin-side up, in a single layer; press gently so the bottoms of the pieces are submerged but the skin is exposed. The skin out of the liquid will stay crisp; the meat below braises in the steam.
  6. 6
    Bring to a steady simmer; cover (with the conical tagine lid or the Dutch oven lid). Reduce heat to low; braise 45 minutes, basting the exposed chicken skin with the pot liquid every 15 minutes. The pot should hold a steady tremor, not a rolling boil; if you hear aggressive bubbling reduce the heat further.
  7. 7
    After 45 minutes, add the 60 g preserved lemon strips and the 150 g rinsed olives, scattering around the chicken (not directly on the skin tops). Add the 4 g / 1 tsp remaining salt. Cover and simmer 15 to 20 more minutes until the chicken is fork-tender (the meat pulls easily from the bone) and the sauce has reduced into a glossy, deeply golden gravy that pools at the bottom of the pot.
  8. 8
    Taste the sauce. It should read of bright preserved lemon, savory olive brine, deep saffron-and-ginger spice base, and chicken juices integrated. Adjust with the 15 g / 1 tbsp fresh lemon juice for brightness if the sauce reads soft; salt is usually unnecessary at this point because the preserved lemon and olives carry it.
  9. 9
    Scatter the remaining chopped cilantro over the top. Serve directly from the tagine or Dutch oven at the table: place a piece of chicken on each plate, ladle sauce, olives, and lemon strips around, with warm flatbread (khobz, msemen, or pita) on the side for sopping, or over steamed couscous.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional with green pepper and tomato (a southern Moroccan variant)→with sliced green pepper and quartered tomato added

    In step 5, add 1 sliced green bell pepper (180 g) and 2 quartered tomatoes (250 g) along with the chicken. The dish reads more vegetable-forward and slightly sweeter from the tomato; common in southern Moroccan home cooking.

  • Regional with chickpeas (a heartier variant)→with cooked chickpeas added in the last 15 minutes

    Add 200 g cooked chickpeas (or 1 drained 400 g can) at step 7 along with the lemon and olives. The chickpeas absorb the sauce and make the dish more substantial; common in Marrakech home cooking.

  • Protein as a lamb tagine variant→with lamb shoulder instead of chicken

    Substitute 800 g cubed lamb shoulder (4 cm cubes) for the chicken. Brown the lamb in 30 g olive oil before the onions (8 minutes); proceed with the recipe. Extend the initial braise to 90 minutes before adding the lemon and olives; total braise time increases to about 2 hours. The dish is structurally adjacent and is the canonical "lamb tagine with preserved lemon and olives" served interchangeably across Morocco.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • preserved lemon, rind only → the zest of 2 fresh lemons plus 1 tbsp brine from a jar of olives A fallback only; fresh lemon zest is sharper, brighter, and lacks the fermented complexity that preserved lemon contributes. Worth seeking out preserved lemon for this dish; the rind is the structural ingredient.
  • cracked green Moroccan olives → castelvetrano or picholine green olives Castelvetrano (Italian) and picholine (French) are widely available and structurally correct; both are firm, meaty, mildly bitter green olives that hold up to the braise. Avoid kalamata or any black olive; they read wrong in this dish.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → preserved lemon, rind only make it yourself Home-made preserved lemons salt-cured for 3 to 4 weeks have a brighter, more floral character than jarred; the lemon is structural for tagine and worth making in advance. The technique is simple and the jars keep for a year.

Notes

Why the tagine cooks with so little liquid. The Moroccan tagine pot (and a tight-lidded Dutch oven that approximates it) is designed for low-liquid braising. The conical lid (or domed lid) collects the steam that rises from the simmering food, condenses it on the cool surface, and returns it as droplets that run down the inside of the dome back into the dish. The result is a self-basting environment that concentrates flavor with very little added water; tagine recipes typically call for 100 to 250 g of liquid where a Western braise would use 500 to 1000 g, because the system retains and recirculates moisture rather than evaporating it. Adding too much liquid produces a soup, not a tagine; trust the small amount and trust the lid.

Why the lemon goes in late. Preserved lemon rind has a delicate floral-sour-saline character that turns muddy and bitter under prolonged heat. Adding the rind in the last 15 to 20 minutes of the braise (with the olives) preserves the brightness and the fermented complexity; the rind softens but does not break down, and the flavor stays distinct. The olives also benefit from late addition: they release some of their brine into the sauce but stay firm and meaty rather than collapsing. Both ingredients are structural for the dish and both depend on this late-addition technique.

Versatility. The base technique (saffron-bloomed onion-and-spice base, slow braise with bone-in chicken or cubed lamb, late-added bright ingredients) is the universal Moroccan tagine template. Swap the late additions: dried apricots and almonds for a sweet-savory chicken tagine; quince and honey for a deeply autumnal version; artichoke hearts and peas for a spring variant; sweet potato and chickpeas for a hearty vegetable-forward version. The spice base stays the same; the personality changes with the late additions.

Pairs across Moroccan (canonical), broader Maghreb (Tunisia, Algeria, Libya have related but distinct tagine traditions), and contemporary North African diaspora cookery. The dish travels especially well in French bistro contexts where Moroccan technique has been absorbed into the broader French repertoire.

Storage. Tagine improves dramatically with a day in the refrigerator; the flavors integrate and the chicken meat absorbs the sauce. Keeps 4 days refrigerated and freezes 2 months. Reheat gently covered on the stovetop with a splash of water to loosen; do not boil aggressively, the chicken collapses and the sauce reads greasy. Many Moroccan cooks consider day-two tagine the structural ideal.
