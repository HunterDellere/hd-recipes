Corn tortillas from masa

The structural bread of Mexican cooking, made from nixtamalized corn flour (masa harina) hydrated with water and salt, pressed thin, and griddled on a hot comal until the surface puffs from internal steam. Soft, pliable, deeply corn-flavored, and dramatically better than any supermarket tortilla.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Heat the comal or skillet to medium-high for 5 minutes before starting; the surface needs to be at a steady 230 to 250°C / 450 to 480°F for the tortilla to puff. Test with a few drops of water; they should sizzle and evaporate in 2 seconds.
  • Have a clean kitchen towel and a tortilla warmer (or two stacked plates) ready before the first tortilla goes on the comal. Tortillas need to steam in a covered warm container immediately after cooking; air-cooled tortillas dry out and crack.
  • A tortilla press is the structural tool but is not strictly required. Two pieces of plastic (a quart-size zip-top bag cut down both sides works) plus a flat heavy skillet or cookbook produces equivalent results with a bit more effort.

The dough

  1. 240 g masa harina (Maseca or comparable)
  2. 3 g fine sea salt
  3. 270 g warm water (about 38°C / 100°F)

Execution

  1. 1
    Mix the dough. In a medium bowl, whisk together the 240 g masa harina and 3 g fine sea salt. Pour in the 270 g warm water; mix with a wooden spoon until a shaggy dough forms. Knead by hand 2 to 3 minutes inside the bowl until the dough is smooth, uniform, and just slightly tacky. Test by squeezing a small piece; it should hold together cleanly without crumbling and without sticking to your fingers. If too dry (crumbles at the edges), add water 5 g at a time; if too wet (sticks to the bowl), add masa harina 5 g at a time.
  2. 2
    Rest the dough. Cover the bowl with a damp kitchen towel; rest 15 minutes (do this while the comal heats). The rest hydrates the masa particles fully and produces a more pliable dough that presses thinner without tearing. Skipping the rest produces tortillas that crack at the edges and tear during handling.
  3. 3
    Heat the comal. While the dough rests, heat a comal or heavy dry skillet (cast iron, carbon steel, or stainless) over medium-high heat 5 minutes until the surface reads 230 to 250°C / 450 to 480°F. Test with a few drops of water; they should sizzle and evaporate within 2 seconds. The comal must be hot enough to set the tortilla surface immediately on contact (which is what produces the puff) and not so hot that the surface burns before the interior cooks.
  4. 4
    Divide and press. Divide the dough into 12 equal portions (about 40 g each); roll each into a smooth ball, returning the balls to the covered bowl as you work so they do not dry out. Working one at a time: place a ball between two pieces of plastic (cut zip-top bag or wax paper) on a tortilla press or under a flat heavy skillet. Press firmly to a 12 cm / 5 inch round about 2 mm thick. Peel off the top plastic, flip the tortilla onto your open palm, and peel off the second plastic.
  5. 5
    Griddle the tortilla. Slide the pressed tortilla onto the hot comal in one smooth motion. Cook 20 to 30 seconds (the edges should just start to lift and dry); flip with a thin spatula. Cook 45 to 60 seconds (the tortilla should start to puff; press the center gently with a clean folded towel or a fish spatula to encourage the steam to expand the tortilla into a full puff). Flip a third time; cook 15 to 20 seconds. The completed tortilla should be flexible, with light brown spots on both sides and a slight puff that collapses on cooling. Comal charring (vegetables, chilies, tortillas)
  6. 6
    Warm and steam. Transfer the cooked tortilla immediately to a tortilla warmer (or wrap in a clean kitchen towel inside a covered bowl). The steaming step is structural; air-cooled tortillas dry out in minutes and crack. Repeat with the remaining dough balls; the tortillas can stack in the warmer with the towel between them. Serve warm. The tortillas are best within 30 minutes of cooking; they hold acceptably for 2 hours in the warmer.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as blue corn tortillas (the Oaxacan version)

    Substitute the masa harina with blue corn masa harina (Masienda and Bob's Red Mill both sell blue corn masa harina; specialty Mexican groceries carry it). The dough is structurally identical and the technique unchanged; the result is a deeply purple-blue tortilla with a slightly nuttier flavor than yellow corn. Canonical in Oaxaca and across much of southern Mexico; visually striking and pairs especially well with meat fillings.

  • Occasion as sopes (thick masa rounds with pinched edges)→thicker masa cakes with raised edges, fried, topped with beans and meat

    Increase the dough portion to 60 g per piece; press to a 10 cm round about 5 mm thick. Griddle 60 seconds per side until set but not fully cooked. Immediately after pulling off the comal, pinch the edges up to form a low wall (the structural feature of a sope); pinch quickly while the tortilla is still hot. Pan-fry the sopes in 5 mm of lard or oil 60 seconds per side until crisp. Top with refried beans, shredded meat, salsa, crumbled queso fresco, lettuce. The sope is the structural Mexican antojito and uses the same masa dough.

  • Occasion as quesadillas (the most common Mexican lunch)→tortillas folded around cheese and griddled

    Press the tortilla as written; place on the hot comal. After 20 seconds (the first side has just set), sprinkle 30 g shredded Oaxaca cheese (or low-moisture mozzarella) over half the tortilla; add other fillings (cooked chicken, mushrooms, squash blossoms, huitlacoche). Fold the tortilla over the filling; press gently with a spatula. Cook 60 to 90 seconds per side until the cheese melts and the tortilla browns lightly. The quesadilla is the canonical Mexican lunch dish and uses the fresh tortilla as its structural base.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • masa harina (Maseca or comparable) → fresh masa from a tortillería (260 g fresh masa per 240 g masa harina; do not add water) Fresh masa is the canonical Mexican daily-cooking material and produces dramatically better tortillas than reconstituted masa harina. The texture is more pliable, the corn flavor is deeper, and the tortillas puff more reliably. Available at tortillerías in Mexican-American neighborhoods. Use within 24 hours of purchase; fresh masa is structurally a perishable wet dough.
  • masa harina (Maseca or comparable) → no acceptable substitute Cornmeal, corn flour, and wheat flour are all structurally different ingredients and will not produce tortillas. If masa harina is unavailable, prepare a different dish (rice, wheat flatbread, polenta) rather than attempting to substitute; the failure is dramatic. Order masa harina online (Maseca and Bob's Red Mill ship widely) for any future Mexican cooking project.

Notes

Corn tortillas from masa harina are one of the highest-leverage projects in home cooking: a 3-ingredient, 22-minute recipe that produces results dramatically better than the best supermarket alternative. Once the cook has the technique down (which takes one practice round), the time-and-cost ratio is overwhelming, and the structural quality difference between fresh tortillas and packaged tortillas reframes every Mexican dish that depends on them.

The puff is the structural test of a good tortilla. When the dough hydration is correct, the comal temperature is right, and the timing is right, the tortilla puffs up into a full balloon on the second flip; the puff indicates that the interior moisture has converted to steam, the dough has set into two layers (a top and a bottom) that separate cleanly when the tortilla cools, and the structure has reached its proper hydration. A tortilla that does not puff is still edible but indicates the comal is too cool, the dough is too dry, or the dough was pressed too thick. Work on the puff; it is the marker of mastery.

The dough hydration is the most variable. Different brands of masa harina absorb water differently (Maseca needs slightly more water than Bob''s Red Mill); ambient humidity affects the absorption (drier days need more water); and the storage age of the flour matters (older flour absorbs less). Start at 1.1 times the masa weight in water (270 g water per 240 g masa) and adjust by feel; the dough should hold together when squeezed without crumbling and without sticking to your fingers. Too dry produces cracking edges; too wet produces dough that sticks to the press and tortillas that turn gummy on the comal.

The pressing technique matters. Press firmly enough to produce a 2 mm thick tortilla; thinner is more challenging to handle and tears easily, thicker does not puff. The plastic between the dough and the press surfaces is structural; without it, the masa sticks to the press and tears on lifting. Cut a quart-size zip-top bag down both sides to produce two squares; these are reusable and ideal. Wax paper works but tears easier; parchment is too thick and produces uneven pressing.

The comal temperature is the second structural variable. Too cool (under 200°C) and the tortilla sticks to the surface, fails to develop the structural surface set that allows the puff, and reads gummy. Too hot (over 270°C) and the surface burns before the interior cooks, producing a dry brittle tortilla. The 230 to 250°C window is correct; the water-drop test (drops should sizzle and evaporate in 2 seconds) is a reliable manual check.

The post-cook steam is structural. Tortillas pulled directly from the comal onto a plate dry out and crack within 2 minutes; the moisture in the freshly cooked tortilla evaporates rapidly to the air. Transfer immediately to a tortilla warmer or wrap in a clean kitchen towel inside a covered bowl; the steaming step keeps the tortillas pliable and is non-negotiable. A 12-tortilla batch can stack in the same warmer with the towel between layers.

Versatility. Fresh corn tortillas are the structural base of an enormous range of Mexican dishes: tacos (all kinds), quesadillas, enchiladas, chilaquiles, tostadas (when fried crisp), sopes (thicker version, pinched edges), gorditas (split and stuffed), huaraches (oval, larger), tlacoyos, panuchos, and many more. Master the basic tortilla and the masa dough scales up to all of these. A double batch (480 g masa harina, 24 tortillas) is a reasonable Sunday cooking project; the tortillas freeze well between sheets of parchment in a zip-top bag and reheat from frozen on a hot comal in 30 seconds per side.

Storage. Best within 30 minutes of cooking; hold acceptably 2 hours in a warmer or wrapped in a clean towel inside a covered bowl. Refrigerated in a sealed bag: 3 days, requires reheating on a hot comal 20 seconds per side. Frozen in a sealed bag with parchment between tortillas: 3 months, reheat from frozen 30 seconds per side on a hot comal (do not thaw first; thawed and then reheated tortillas turn gummy). The masa dough itself (uncooked) keeps 4 days refrigerated tightly covered with a damp towel; bring to room temperature before pressing.

Sources
  • The Mesoamerican canon. The corn tortilla is one of the oldest foods continuously prepared in the Americas, with nixtamalization documented to at least 1500 BCE in Mesoamerican archaeological sites. The masa harina shortcut (introduced by Maseca in 1949) allows home cooks outside Mexico to produce tortillas without the multi-day nixtamalization process; the canonical Mexican daily-cooking material is fresh masa from a tortillería. — The dough hydration ratio (1.1 to 1.2 parts water by weight per 1 part masa harina) varies by brand and by humidity; start at 1.1 and adjust by feel. The press-and-griddle technique is unchanged from the pre-Columbian original; the variables are the dough hydration, the comal temperature, and the pressing pressure. Fresh masa from a tortillería produces meaningfully better tortillas than masa harina and is the canonical material when available; the masa harina version is the realistic home option outside Mexican neighborhoods.
