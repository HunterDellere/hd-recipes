Recipe

Charred broccoli with garlic-anchovy crunch

Broccoli seared hard in a dry pan until the florets catch deep char, steam-finished briefly under a lid, then dressed with a crumb of panko fried in olive oil with sliced garlic, anchovy, and chili. The broccoli supplies sweet-bitter brassica char; the crumb supplies the savory, crunchy, glutamate-heavy counterpoint that turns a plain green vegetable into a side with structure. The whole dish runs on dry heat: no boiling, no steaming until the very end, so the broccoli browns instead of going gray and sulfurous.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Fry the crumb first and set it aside. It takes 4 to 5 minutes and stays crisp at room temperature; making it before the broccoli means it is ready to scatter the moment the broccoli comes off the heat, rather than going soggy waiting in the pan or burning while you chase the broccoli.
  • Dry the broccoli well after washing. Surface water in a hot dry pan turns to steam and the florets gray and soften instead of charring; spin or towel them properly dry before they hit the pan.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the garlic-anchovy crumb

  1. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  2. 12 g garlic, sliced thin, peeled and sliced into thin coins — (3 cloves; fried to amber for the crunch and sweet Maillard)
  3. 8 g anchovy fillets in oil, patted of excess oil, chopped — (2 fillets; the glutamate source, dissolves into the oil and reads savory, not fishy)
  4. 30 g panko breadcrumbs
  5. 1 g red pepper flakes

Phase 2, the broccoli

  1. 600 g broccoli, cut into long florets with a flat side, stems peeled and sliced on the bias, washed and dried well — (2 medium crowns; long florets give more flat surface to char)
  2. 15 g neutral oil (high smoke point)
  3. 4 g kosher salt
  4. 30 ml water, for the steam-finish

Phase 3, the finish

  1. 0.5 each lemon, cut into wedges, for squeezing at the table — (½ lemon; the finishing acid)

Execution

  1. 1
    Fry the crumb first. Warm the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil in a skillet over medium heat, add the 12 g / 3 cloves sliced garlic, and fry gently, stirring, until the garlic is pale gold, 2 to 3 minutes. Add the 8 g / 2 chopped anchovy fillets and the 1 g / ½ tsp red pepper flakes and stir 30 seconds until the anchovy dissolves into the oil. Add the 30 g / ½ cup panko and stir constantly until the crumb turns deep golden and crisp and the garlic is amber, 2 to 3 minutes. Pull off-heat the moment the garlic is amber, it carries over fast and five seconds late means bitter. Tip the crumb onto a plate to stop the cooking and stay crisp. Garlic crunch
  2. 2
    Heat the broccoli pan dry. Wipe the skillet, return it to high heat, and let it get genuinely hot, 2 to 3 minutes. Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil and swirl; it should shimmer and just begin to smoke.
  3. 3
    Char the broccoli hard. Add the 600 g / 2 crowns of dried broccoli florets and stem slices in as close to a single layer as the pan allows, season with 3 g / ¾ tsp of the kosher salt, and press the flat faces against the metal. Leave them undisturbed 2 to 3 minutes to catch a deep char on the down-faces, then toss and char a second face for another 2 minutes. Crowding steams them, so work in two batches if the pan is small; the goal is brown-black char spots, not even green. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Steam-finish under a lid. Add the 30 ml / 2 tbsp water and immediately clamp on a lid; it will hiss and steam hard. Leave it 60 to 90 seconds, just long enough for the steam to finish the cores tender while the char stays on the outside. Pull the lid, let any remaining water cook off over the heat for 15 seconds, and season with the last 1 g / ¼ tsp kosher salt. The broccoli should be char-spotted outside and crisp-tender, not soft, within. Vapor steaming (high-vapor wet-heat cookery)
  5. 5
    Finish and serve. Tip the hot florets into a serving dish and scatter the fried crumb generously over the top so it stays on the surface and crisp rather than mixed in and softening. Serve with 4 lemon wedges (cut from the ½ lemon) to squeeze at the table; the acid cuts the savory crumb and the brassica char. Eat hot, while the crumb still crackles.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Dietary vegan / no anchovy→caper and olive crumb

    Drop the anchovy and add 10 g / 1 tbsp chopped capers and 6 chopped olives to the crumb with the garlic, plus a pinch of nutritional yeast or a few drops of soy for the missing glutamate. The crumb stays savory and briny without the fish; the dish becomes fully vegan.

  • Regional cheese finish→pecorino and lemon zest

    Stir 20 g / ¼ cup microplaned pecorino romano and the zest of half a lemon into the crumb off-heat after frying. The cheese adds a sharp salty-glutamate layer and the zest lifts it; the dish stops being dairy-free but reads richer and more Roman.

  • Regional vegetable swap→broccoli rabe or cauliflower

    Broccoli rabe (rapini) is the most traditional partner for this crumb, more bitter and leafy; blanch it 60 seconds first to tame the bitterness, then char and crumb as written. Cauliflower works cut into 3 to 4 cm florets, char it 4 to 5 minutes per face since it is denser.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • anchovy fillets in oil → colatura or fish sauce (a few drops), or capers for a non-fish version Colatura (Italian anchovy extract) or a few drops of fish sauce give the same glutamate depth without the fillets to chop. Capers (see the vegan modification) replace the savory-briny role for a fish-free crumb.
  • panko breadcrumbs → torn stale sourdough or coarse dried breadcrumbs Torn stale bread fried in the oil makes a more rustic, irregular crumb (pangrattato) that some prefer; coarse dried breadcrumbs work but go soggy faster than panko, so add them to the hot broccoli at the last second.
  • broccoli → broccolini, broccoli rabe, or romanesco Broccolini is sweeter and faster (char 90 seconds per face); broccoli rabe is the traditional bitter partner (blanch first); romanesco holds its fractal shape and chars beautifully.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → panko breadcrumbs make it yourselfstub House panko, made from crustless white bread dried and coarsely grated, has a jaggeder, airier flake than the dense commercial crumb and fries up dramatically crispier and lighter, which is exactly what a topping crumb wants. A batch dries in an afternoon, keeps for weeks, and makes every fried-crumb and cutlet dish better.

Notes

Why dry char, then a brief steam, never a boil. Broccoli's flavor lives in its glucosinolate-myrosinase system: cut the head, the enzyme reacts, and you get sulforaphane and the pleasant nutty-brassica volatiles. Cook it gently and dry and those read well; boil it or simmer it long and the same precursors break down into hydrogen sulfide and dimethyl sulfide, the cafeteria-broccoli smell. So the cook is fast and dry: a screaming pan to char the florets, then just 60 to 90 seconds of steam under a lid to finish the cores, and no longer. The char gives the sweet-bitter Maillard note, the brief steam gives crisp-tender (not soft) interiors, and the sulfur compounds never get the long wet window they need to dominate.

Why the crumb is fried separately and added last. Panko fried in the garlic-anchovy oil is the dish's whole textural and savory payoff, and it only works crisp. Mixed into the broccoli during cooking, or scattered on too early, it soaks up moisture and goes soft within a minute. Fry it on its own, drain it onto a plate to stop the carry-over, and scatter it over the broccoli at the very end so it sits on the surface and stays crunchy. The same logic governs the garlic: pull it at amber, because it carries over to bitter-brown in the residual pan heat in seconds.

The anchovy is umami, not fish. Two fillets dissolved into hot oil contribute free glutamate and a deep savory backbone; what you taste in the finished dish is savoriness, not anchovy. This is the same mechanism behind Caesar dressing and puttanesca, the anchovy is a seasoning that disappears into the dish and makes everything around it taste more like itself. Cooks who think they dislike anchovy almost never identify it here.

Stems are not waste. Peel the fibrous outer layer off the broccoli stems and slice the sweet, dense core on the bias; treat it as part of the dish. It chars well, holds its texture, and is arguably better eating than the florets. Discarding it throws away a third of the vegetable.
