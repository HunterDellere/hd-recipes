Light soy sauce (生抽)

Soybeans and wheat fermented with Aspergillus mold for 6 to 18 months, pressed and pasteurized into the lighter, saltier seasoning soy of Chinese cooking. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units

Produce 1

  1. 750 g whole wheat berries

Spices & dried herbs 1

  1. 600 g kosher salt

Other 3

  1. 3000 ml filtered water
  2. 5 g koji starter (Aspergillus oryzae spores)
  3. 1500 g whole soybeans, dried

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: prepare the koji-cultured grain. Soak 1500 g / 3.3 lb soybeans 12 hours, drain, pressure-cook 60 minutes until the beans crush easily between fingers. Toast 750 g / 1.65 lb wheat berries in a dry skillet 5 minutes until fragrant; pulse-grind in a food processor to a coarse cracked-grain texture. Combine cooled cooked soybeans and cracked wheat at body temperature (35 to 37 °C); inoculate with 5 g / 1 tsp koji starter spores. Spread on shallow trays and incubate at 30 °C / 86 °F with 80 to 90 percent humidity for 48 to 72 hours; stir twice daily. The substrate develops a white-to-pale-green mold coverage and smells distinctly of fresh-mushroom and chestnut.3 d 0 h
  2. 2
    Stub: prepare the moromi (the brine fermentation). Combine the koji-cultured grain with 600 g / 2½ cups kosher salt and 3000 ml / 12 cups filtered water in a wide-mouth fermentation crock or food-grade plastic bucket; the salt-to-water ratio is approximately 18 to 20 percent salinity. Stir vigorously to break up clumps. Cover with a clean cloth and a fitted lid that allows gas escape (a fermentation airlock or a loose lid weighted with a clean weight). Place in a stable 25 to 30 °C location.
  3. 3
    Stub: ferment for 6 to 18 months. Stir vigorously once weekly for the first month, then once monthly for the duration. The moromi develops a complex aromatic profile through the fermentation: month 1 to 3 reads as raw-koji and harshly salty; month 6 reads as recognizably soy-sauce-like; month 12 reads as deep and complex. The longer the ferment, the deeper and more nuanced the finished sauce.178 d 12 h
  4. 4
    Stub: press and pasteurize. After the desired ferment time, transfer the moromi to a press cloth (cheesecloth-lined colander suspended over a stockpot, or a dedicated fruit press) and squeeze out all liquid. The press yield is approximately 1500 to 1800 ml of raw soy sauce. Pasteurize at 70 to 80 °C / 160 to 175 °F for 30 minutes (this halts the fermentation, kills any pathogenic microorganisms, and develops the soy sauce's final aromatic profile through Maillard reactions during the heat treatment). Bottle in clean glass; the pasteurized sauce is shelf-stable indefinitely refrigerated.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the Aspergillus oryzae chemistry (the koji mold produces protease and amylase enzymes that break down the soybean and wheat proteins and starches into amino acids and sugars during the moromi ferment, which is what makes soy sauce''s complex umami and slight sweetness possible), the safety considerations (the high salt concentration (18 to 20 percent) prevents pathogenic bacteria from growing during the long fermentation; lower salt concentrations are unsafe for unattended ferments at room temperature), the difference between light soy (生抽, sheng chou; the first press of the moromi, lighter color, saltier, used for seasoning) and dark soy (老抽, lao chou; aged longer, sometimes with caramel added, darker color, deeper flavor, used for color and depth in red-cooked dishes), the East Asian variants (Japanese shoyu uses the same Aspergillus inoculation but typically with equal soybean-and-wheat ratios and a faster ferment; Korean ganjang uses meju cakes of dried soybeans and a different Bacillus-and-Aspergillus mixed culture), the comparison to commercial light soy (homemade tastes more complex and less narrowly salty; the supermarket Pearl River Bridge or Lee Kum Kee light soy is a workmanlike commercial product but lacks the depth of a long-aged batch), the koji-starter sourcing (GEM Cultures, Cultures for Health, sake-supply shops; the spores ship dried and stay viable for years), the failure modes (kahm yeast forming a white film on the moromi surface, which is harmless but indicates oxygen exposure; pink or black mold colonies, which mean contamination and the batch should be discarded), the storage (the finished pasteurized sauce keeps indefinitely refrigerated; unpasteurized "live" soy sauce continues to develop in the bottle), the ratio variations (the 2:1 soybean-to-wheat ratio is the Chinese light-soy default; Japanese shoyu uses 1:1; Korean ganjang uses pure soybean with no wheat).
