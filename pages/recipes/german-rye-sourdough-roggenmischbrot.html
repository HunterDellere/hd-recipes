German rye sourdough (Roggenmischbrot)

A 70% rye, 30% wheat mixed sourdough built on a three-stage rye sour. Rye carries almost no functional gluten, so structure comes from pentosan gums and the acid that keeps starch-degrading enzymes in check; the dough is a stiff paste rather than a kneadable mass, smeared into a loaf tin and proofed short, then baked dense, moist, and deeply sour with toasted caraway. This is the everyday bread of German bakeries, structurally and texturally a different object from a wheat country loaf.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a two-day cycle. The three-stage rye sour runs across roughly 16 to 18 hours of unattended fermentation; the final dough mix, short proof, and bake take about 3 hours the next day. Active hands-on time is about 45 minutes total. Start the sour the evening before you want to bake.
  • You need a mature rye starter (an active rye Sauerteig, or a wheat starter converted to rye over two or three feeds) as the seed for stage one. A reliably doubling, pleasantly sour rye starter is the prerequisite; building one from scratch is a separate 7 to 10 day project.
  • Rye dough is a stiff paste, not a kneadable elastic ball. Do not expect a smooth windowpane; do not try to develop gluten. The dough is mixed until uniform and then handled wet, with a wet spatula and wet hands. Stage the loaf tin, oil, and a bowl of water before mixing, because once the final dough is together you work fast and everything sticks.
  • Bake in a standard 900 g / 2 lb loaf tin (about 25 by 13 cm). Rye has no structure to hold a free-standing boule shape during proof; the tin is the structural support, not a stylistic choice.

Execution

  1. 1
    Stage 1, the fresh sour (evening, day one). In a medium bowl or jar, stir the 10 g rye starter, 50 g whole rye flour, and 40 g warm water into a stiff paste. It will be claggy and dense, that is correct for this stage. Cover loosely and hold warm, 26 to 28°C / 79 to 82°F, for about 4 hours. This stiff, cooler stage favors the acetic-acid bacteria that build the sharp aromatic top notes of the sour. A turned-off oven with the light on, or a warm spot near the stove, holds the temperature.
  2. 2
    Stage 2, the basic sour. Fold all of stage 1 together with 100 g whole rye flour and 100 g warm water. This stage is looser, 100% hydration, and runs warmer, 28 to 30°C / 82 to 86°F, for about 3 to 4 hours. The wetter, warmer environment shifts the balance toward yeast and lactic-acid bacteria, building the leavening power that the final dough will rely on. The mixture should become visibly bubbly and smell distinctly sour by the end.
  3. 3
    Stage 3, the full sour (overnight). Fold all of stage 2 together with 150 g whole rye flour and 150 g warm water to build the mature sour, about 440 g total. Cover and let it mature at cool room temperature, 18 to 22°C / 64 to 72°F, for 8 to 12 hours overnight. This long, cool final maturation rounds the acetic sharpness into a fuller, more balanced sourness and brings the pH down to roughly 4.2 to 4.3, the acidity that will keep rye amylase in check during the bake. By morning the sour should be bubbly, slightly domed, and smell sharply tangy with a faint alcohol edge.11 h
  4. 4
    Toast the caraway and build the final dough (morning, day two). Toast the 8 g caraway seed in a dry skillet over medium heat, shaking, for 60 to 90 seconds until fragrant and a shade darker; tip out and crack coarse in a mortar, reserving a small pinch for the crust. Scrape 10 g of the mature sour back into your starter jar and feed it for next time. In a large bowl combine the remaining 430 g rye sour, 300 g whole rye flour, 200 g bread flour, 280 g warm water, 14 g fine sea salt, the toasted cracked caraway, and the 12 g barley malt syrup.
  5. 5
    Mix to a uniform paste. With a stiff wooden spoon or a wet hand, mix vigorously for 3 to 5 minutes until no dry flour remains and the dough is a thick, sticky, uniform paste with no streaks. Do not knead and do not look for gluten development or a smooth ball; rye has almost no functional gluten, and the structure here comes from the pentosan gums hydrating, not from a gluten network. The dough should be the texture of very stiff mortar, holding the spoon upright for a moment before slumping. If it is dry and crumbly rather than paste-like, work in another 10 to 20 g water.
  6. 6
    Bulk rest. Cover the bowl with a damp kitchen towel and let the dough rest 30 to 45 minutes at warm room temperature. There is no stretch-and-fold here, the paste cannot be stretched; this short rest simply lets the flour finish drinking water and the sour begin lifting the dough.
  7. 7
    Pan the loaf. Grease a 900 g / 2 lb loaf tin thoroughly with the 8 g neutral oil, including the corners. Wet your hands and a spatula, scrape the dough out of the bowl, and pack it into the tin, pressing into the corners and smoothing the top with a wet spatula or wet fingers; aim for an even, slightly domed surface with no air pockets. The wet tools are the whole technique for handling rye paste, dry hands weld to it instantly.
  8. 8
    Dust and proof. Sift the 10 g rye flour over the top of the loaf in an even layer and scatter the reserved pinch of caraway. Cover loosely with the damp towel and proof at warm room temperature, 26 to 28°C / 79 to 82°F, for 45 to 75 minutes. Rye proofs short and is easy to overproof; the cue is that the floured surface develops a network of fine cracks as the dough rises beneath it, and the dough has risen by roughly 50% and sits domed at or just above the tin rim. Crack pattern fully open and a domed top that has started to flatten or sink means you have proofed too far, bake immediately.
  9. 9
    Preheat with steam. While the loaf proofs, preheat the oven to 250°C / 480°F with a cast-iron skillet or a metal tray on the bottom rack to heat for steam. Rye needs a hot, steamy start, the high initial heat sets the crust and drives the oven rise, and steam keeps the crust supple long enough for that rise to happen before the surface sets hard.
  10. 10
    Load and steam. Put the tin in the oven on the middle rack. Pour about 100 g / scant ½ cup boiling water into the preheated skillet below and immediately close the door to trap the steam. Bake at 250°C / 480°F for 15 minutes. The blast of high heat and steam gives the loaf its initial rise and begins setting the crust; the crazed floured top opens fully into its characteristic rustic pattern in this phase. Maillard sear
  11. 11
    Drop the heat and bake through. Reduce the oven to 200°C / 390°F and bake another 40 to 50 minutes. If you have a way to vent the residual steam after the first 15 minutes, do so, the long second phase needs dry heat to build a thick crust. The loaf is done when the crust is deep brown and firm, the loaf sounds hollow tapped on the bottom (turn it out of the tin to check), and the internal temperature reads 96 to 98°C / 205 to 208°F on an instant-read thermometer. Rye runs slightly higher and slower than wheat; underbaking leaves the dense crumb gummy, so do not pull it early on color alone.
  12. 12
    Cool fully, then wait a day. Turn the loaf out of the tin onto a wire rack and cool completely, at least 3 to 4 hours. This is non-negotiable for rye and stricter than for wheat: the crumb is still setting from residual heat and enzyme activity for hours, and a rye loaf cut warm is gummy and pasty. Better still, wrap the fully cooled loaf in a clean kitchen towel and let it rest a full 12 to 24 hours before the first slice. The crumb tightens, the moisture redistributes evenly, and the sour flavor matures and deepens overnight; a one-day-old rye slices clean and tastes markedly better than a same-day one.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture rye fraction→pure rye loaf (Roggenbrot, 90 to 100% rye)

    Replace the 200 g bread flour with 200 g additional whole rye flour, bringing the loaf to roughly 100% rye. Bump the final-dough water by 30 to 40 g, since rye drinks more; the paste gets even stickier and the crumb bakes denser and moister. Lower the second bake to 190°C / 375°F and extend it 10 to 15 minutes. With essentially no gluten, the loaf relies entirely on pentosan structure and a fully acidified sour; do not skimp on the three-stage build.

  • Regional flavor and color→Berliner Landbrot direction, with cocoa and coffee for a near-black crumb

    Add 10 g / 2 tsp Dutch-process cocoa and 30 g / 2 tbsp strong brewed coffee (subtract the coffee from the dough water) to the final dough. Neither reads as cocoa or coffee in the loaf; together they deepen the crumb toward the dark, malty register of a north-German Landbrot and add a faint bitter complexity behind the sour. Increase the malt syrup to 18 g.

  • Texture seeds→sunflower-rye (Sonnenblumenbrot)

    Soak 100 g raw shelled sunflower seeds in 100 g water for 2 hours, then fold the drained seeds (and any clinging water, subtracted from the dough water) into the final dough at the mixing stage. The seeds add fat, structure, and a toasted savory note that pairs naturally with rye sour; a near-ubiquitous German bakery variation. Pumpkin seeds or a flax-and-sesame mix work the same way.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole rye flour → light or medium rye flour (Type 997 / 1150), or up to half spelt Whole (dark) rye carries the most bran, flavor, and pentosan, and ferments most actively, which is why it is the default here. Lighter sifted ryes make a milder, slightly less dense loaf; reduce the final-dough water by 20 to 30 g, since less bran means less water uptake. Spelt can stand in for up to half the rye for a softer, nuttier loaf, but it is not a true rye substitute and shifts the dough toward wheat behavior.
  • bread flour → all-purpose flour or strong white wheat flour The wheat fraction exists only to add the gluten that rye lacks. Bread flour (11 to 12% protein) gives the best lift; all-purpose works with slightly less rise. Whole wheat can replace it for a darker, denser loaf, but it brings no extra gluten and pushes the crumb tighter.
  • barley malt syrup or dark molasses → honey, or omit The malt syrup adds color, a faint malt-bitter depth, and a small sugar dose for the yeast. Honey is a workable swap for color and a hint of sweetness, though it lacks the malt note. Omitting it entirely is fine; the loaf bakes a shade lighter and slightly less complex but is still correct.
  • caraway seed → fennel seed, or a caraway-fennel-anise blend, or omit Caraway is the canonical German rye aromatic, but plenty of regional ryes use little or none. Fennel seed reads in an adjacent anise register without the warm-bitter undertone; some bakers use a fennel, anise, and coriander blend. A plain caraway-free loaf is a legitimate everyday style if the licorice-warm note is not to taste.

Notes

Why rye is governed by acid, not gluten. Rye contains glutenin and gliadin proteins, but its high pentosan content (soluble arabinoxylan gums, far higher than in wheat) physically interferes with gluten-network formation. The result is a flour that cannot build the elastic, gas-trapping web that wheat does. Structure in a rye loaf comes instead from those same pentosans, which absorb several times their weight in water and set into a gel that holds the crumb together. This is why rye dough is a paste, not a ball, why it is mixed rather than kneaded, and why the crumb is dense and moist rather than open and airy. The other half of the story is enzymatic: rye is rich in alpha-amylase that stays active to roughly 65 to 70°C, well into the bake, far past where wheat amylase has denatured. Left unchecked, that enzyme keeps cleaving the gelatinizing starch into sugars during baking, and the crumb collapses into a sticky, gummy paste (the classic rye failure). The fix is acid: at a dough pH around 4.3 or below, rye amylase activity is sharply suppressed and the starch sets properly. The sourdough is therefore not a flavor choice; it is the only practical home method to acidify rye enough to make it bake into sliceable bread. Commercial bakeries that use yeast-leavened rye add lactic acid or acidified additives to do the same job.

The three-stage Detmolder sour, and why three stages. Each stage is tuned to favor a different part of the microbial population by manipulating temperature, hydration, and time. Stage one (Anfrischsauer) is stiff and cool, conditions that favor heterofermentative acetic-acid bacteria, which build the sharp, aromatic top notes. Stage two (Grundsauer) is wetter and warmer, shifting toward yeast and lactic-acid bacteria to build leavening power. Stage three (Vollsauer) matures long and cool to round the acetic sharpness into a full, balanced sourness and drop the pH to the target window. A single-stage sour can leaven a rye loaf, and many home bakers use one; the three-stage build is the classical professional method because it gives the most control over the acid balance and the most reliable rise. If you want to shortcut, a single overnight rye sour (200 g rye flour, 200 g water, 20 g starter, 12 to 16 hours at cool room temperature) will work, with a slightly flatter, less nuanced sourness.

Handling the paste. Everything about working rye is wet. Wet the spoon, wet the spatula, wet your hands, wet the bench scraper. Rye paste is one of the stickiest doughs in baking, and the only defense is a film of water between the dough and whatever touches it. There is no shaping in the wheat sense; you scrape the paste into a greased tin and smooth the top. The floured top is functional as well as rustic: as the loaf rises in the proof and the early bake, the flour layer cracks into a crazed network, which is both the visual signature of a tin rye and a reliable proof gauge, fine cracks mean ready, fully blown-open cracks with a sinking dome mean overproofed.

Hydration and why rye drinks so much. The pentosan gums and the bran in whole rye absorb dramatically more water than wheat starch and gluten do. A rye dough at what looks like a very high hydration on paper still presents as a stiff paste, because so much of that water is bound up in the gums rather than free. Do not try to dry the dough out to a wheat-like consistency; a rye dough mixed stiff enough to feel like bread dough is badly underhydrated and will bake dense and dry. The target is a thick, sticky, spreadable paste.

Why the day-of-rest matters more for rye. A wheat loaf is best within hours of cooling. A rye loaf genuinely improves over the first 24 hours. The dense, moist, pentosan-bound crumb continues to set and equilibrate well after the loaf is cool to the touch, and cutting too early gives a gummy, pasty crumb even when the bake was correct. The sour flavor also matures, the sharp edges settle into a deeper, rounder sourness. Wrap the cooled loaf in a kitchen towel (or a beeswax wrap) and wait at least overnight; this is standard practice in German bakeries, where rye is sold the day after baking.

The umami-and-acid frame, in bread terms. The sour itself is the dish's acid, a built-in lactic and acetic tang that is the entire point of a German rye. The glutamate side is carried by the long fermentation and the deep Maillard crust: the three-stage sour and the dark bake generate savory, malty, faintly meaty browning compounds, reinforced by the barley malt. A correctly fermented and well-baked rye reads savory and complex, not merely sour; if it tastes flat or one-dimensionally acidic, the bake was too pale or the sour underdeveloped.

Storage. A whole, uncut rye loaf keeps remarkably well, 5 to 7 days at room temperature wrapped in a towel or kept cut-face-down on a board, far longer than a wheat loaf, because the acid suppresses mold and the moist crumb stays moist. Do not refrigerate, which stales bread fastest. For longer storage, slice the cooled loaf, wrap portions in parchment, and freeze up to 3 months; toast slices directly from frozen. The acid and density that make rye slow to stale are the same properties that make it the classic keeping bread of long Central European winters.
