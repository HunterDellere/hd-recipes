Recipe

Red-braised yellow croaker (hong shao huang yu)

Whole yellow croaker salted, dried, and shallow-fried to set and seal the thin skin, then braised in a glossy red sauce of Shaoxing wine, light and dark soy, and a caramel of rock sugar with ginger, garlic, scallion, and dried chili. The braising liquid is spooned over and reduced until it clings to the fish as a mahogany glaze, finished with scallion and a touch of Chinkiang vinegar.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Salt the fish and let it rest 15 to 20 minutes, then pat it bone-dry just before frying. The fry is not for crispness here the way it is in a pan-fried fish; it is to set and seal the thin, low-collagen skin so it survives the braise without sloughing off. A wet fish steams and sticks, tears on the turn, and disintegrates in the sauce. Do not skip the second pat-dry.
  • Start jasmine rice FIRST as a parallel track. The braise runs about 12 minutes once the sauce goes in and the fish is best the moment the glaze is at its glossiest; the rice (a roughly 12 minute simmer plus 10 minute rest) should be finished and resting before the fish goes into the oil. Hong shao fish is a rice dish, and the sauce is engineered to be spooned over rice.
  • Crush the rock sugar and have every aromatic and the full sauce mix measured and within reach before the fish hits the pan. Once the caramel starts it moves fast, and a scorched caramel is bitter and unrecoverable; you cannot stop to mince garlic mid-caramel.

Execution

  1. 1
    Prep the fish. The grocer should have gutted, scaled, and gilled the 450 g / 2 to 3 whole croakers; verify all three. Rinse each fish inside and out and scrape any dark blood line from the spine cavity with the back of a small spoon, the blood line carries the strongest fishy note. With a sharp knife cut three diagonal slashes 1.5 cm deep into each side through the skin to the bone; the slashes let the salt and the braise reach the spine and give the thin skin somewhere to release tension instead of curling and tearing in the pan. Pat the fish completely dry, inside and out.
  2. 2
    Salt-and-wine cure. Rub the 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt evenly over the skin, into the slashes, and inside the cavity of every fish, then splash the 15 g / 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine over the same surfaces and rub it in. Rest the fish on a rack, uncovered, 15 to 20 minutes. The salt firms the surface flesh and pulls water to the skin; the wine esters perfume the fish and the brief denaturation helps the thin skin hold together through the fry. Meanwhile, with the rice already started, prep and measure all the aromatics and the braise sauce so they are within reach.
  3. 3
    Build the sauce mix. In a small bowl combine the 30 g / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, the 10 g / 2 tsp dark soy sauce, the 30 g / 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine, and the 240 g / 1 cup hot water or stock; stir to blend. Keep the 20 g / 1½ tbsp crushed rock sugar separate for the caramel. Having the liquid premixed means you can deglaze the caramel in one fast pour before it scorches.
  4. 4
    Dry and dust the fish. After the cure, pat the fish bone-dry a second time, blotting the beaded moisture off the skin and out of the slashes; residual water makes the skin steam, stick, and tear. Dust each fish all over with the 12 g / 1½ tbsp cornstarch for a thin, even film, then lift and tap off all excess. The starch is a sheer coat, not a crust; it gives the thin croaker skin a sturdier surface that sets in the fry and resists sloughing in the braise.
  5. 5
    Sear the fish to set the skin. Heat the 40 g / 3 tbsp neutral oil in a wide skillet or wok over medium-high until it shimmers and a pinch of cornstarch sizzles on contact, about 180 °C. Lay the fish in away from you and fry undisturbed 3 to 4 minutes per side, turning once with a thin spatula slid fully underneath, until the skin is set, golden, and sealed. This is a setting sear, not a fry to doneness; the interior finishes in the braise. The fish should release freely before you turn it, one confident flip protects the skin. Lift the fish out to a plate and pour off all but about 15 g / 1 tbsp of the oil, leaving the fond.
  6. 6
    Bloom the aromatics. Return the pan to medium heat with its 15 g / 1 tbsp reserved oil. Add the 20 g / ginger coins and matchsticks, the 15 g / 4 smashed garlic cloves, the 30 g / 2 scallion batons, and the 3 g / 3 to 4 dried chilies. Stir-fry 60 to 90 seconds until the aromatics are fragrant and the garlic just turns pale gold; do not let them brown hard or the braise turns acrid. Push them to the side of the pan.
  7. 7
    Make the caramel. Add the 20 g / 1½ tbsp crushed rock sugar to the cleared center of the pan and let it melt over medium heat, swirling gently, until it dissolves into a smooth amber syrup and just begins to smell of caramel, about 2 minutes. Watch it closely: rock sugar melts unevenly and goes from amber to acrid-black fast, and a burnt caramel is bitter and must be discarded. The caramel is what gives hong shao its red gloss and rounded sweetness that plain soy cannot supply.
  8. 8
    Deglaze and build the braise. The moment the caramel is amber, pour in the premixed sauce, the 30 g / 2 tbsp light plus 10 g / 2 tsp dark soy, 30 g / 2 tbsp Shaoxing, and 240 g / 1 cup hot water or stock, all at once; it will bubble up violently and the caramel may seize into shards, this is normal. Stir over the chilies and aromatics until any seized caramel redissolves and the liquid is a uniform glossy red-brown, about 1 minute. Bring to a brisk simmer.
  9. 9
    Braise the fish. Slide the seared fish back into the pan in a single layer; the liquid should come about halfway up the fish, not submerge it. Simmer uncovered 10 to 12 minutes, basting constantly: tilt the pan and spoon the braising liquid over the exposed top of the fish every minute or so. Spooning rather than submerging is the technique that keeps the thin-skinned fish intact while the sauce works into the slashes and seasons the flesh. Halfway through, gently slide a spatula under each fish and tilt it to loosen, but turn the fish only once if at all, the more you move it the more the skin frays.
  10. 10
    Reduce to a glaze. When the fish is opaque to the bone in the deepest slash and the sauce has reduced by roughly half to a syrupy, spoon-coating consistency, lift the fish to a warm serving platter. Raise the heat under the remaining sauce and reduce another 30 to 60 seconds until it is glossy and clings to the back of a spoon; it should look like loose lacquer, not soup. If it goes too thick, splash in a tablespoon of hot water; if too thin, reduce harder.
  11. 11
    Finish and serve. Pull the sauce off the heat and swirl in the 8 g / ½ tbsp Chinkiang vinegar; off-heat keeps the volatile acetic and aromatic notes bright and stops the vinegar from cooking flat. Taste the sauce: it should read deep, glossy, savory-sweet, with the vinegar lifting the back end so it does not cloy. Spoon the glaze over the fish, fishing out and discarding the spent chilies if you like, then scatter the reserved 15 g / 2 stalks bias-sliced scallion over the top. Serve immediately with the cooked 300 g / 2 cups jasmine rice; spoon the extra glaze over the rice as you eat.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Heat heat profile→Sichuan doubanjiang red braise

    After blooming the aromatics and before the caramel, fry 20 g / 1 tbsp doubanjiang (fermented chili-bean paste) in the oil for 60 seconds until the oil stains red and smells deeply savory, then proceed with a smaller 12 g / 1 tbsp rock sugar caramel. The braise shifts from gently sweet Jiangnan red-cook toward a spicier, funkier Sichuan register; cut the dried chilies to 2 g so the doubanjiang carries the heat.

  • Regional aromatic profile→with star anise and a five-spice lean

    Add 1 whole star anise pod and a 2 cm cinnamon shard to the aromatics before the caramel, and braise as written. The dish gains the warm licorice-and-bark backbone of a classic northern hong shao; pull the star anise before serving so it does not overpower. Best with the larger 200 g+ croakers, whose firmer flesh stands up to the bigger spice.

  • Protein cut→with thick fish steaks instead of whole fish

    Replace the whole croaker with 2 cross-cut steaks of a firmer white fish (carp, snapper, or sea bass, each ~200 g, 3 cm thick). Sear and braise as written but extend the braise to 14 to 16 minutes by thickness, basting throughout. The dish loses the whole-fish presentation but works for households without access to small whole fish, and the thicker cut is more forgiving in the braise.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole yellow croaker → small whole snapper, porgy, or tilapia For a soy braise the fish needs to hold its shape through simmering rather than crisp, so a slightly firmer white fish works well. Small snapper is the best match for sweetness and structure; porgy is reliable and well-priced. Tilapia is the budget option, structurally fine but blander, so push the ginger, chili, and the Chinkiang finish harder to compensate.
  • rock sugar → granulated sugar Granulated sugar caramelizes faster and more evenly, so it is actually easier to control, but it lacks the gentle clean roundness of rock sugar and the glaze reads a touch sharper. Use the same 20 g weight and watch the caramel even more closely, as granulated tips from amber to burnt sooner.
  • Chinkiang (black) vinegar → rice vinegar with a few drops of dark soy, or balsamic in a pinch Chinkiang has a malty, almost smoky depth that rice vinegar lacks; rice vinegar plus a drop of dark soy approximates the color and roundness. A few drops of good balsamic can stand in for the maltiness in a pinch, but use less, as it is sweeter and more aggressive.
  • light and dark soy sauce → gluten-free tamari (light) plus a touch of molasses for color To make the dish gluten-free, swap both soys for tamari and replace the dark soy color with a few drops of molasses or a longer caramel; also swap the Shaoxing wine for a gluten-free dry sherry or sake. The braise reads slightly less complex but the technique is unchanged.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → light soy sauce make it yourselfstub Soybean and wheat fermented for months yield a markedly more aromatic, layered soy than the mass-produced bottle, and soy is the backbone of this braise, so the upgrade is tasted directly. One batch covers a year of cooking.
  • → Shaoxing wine make it yourselfstub Glutinous rice and wheat fermented with a qu starter for months give a true Shaoxing depth that the diluted, salted supermarket cooking wine lacks, and the braise leans on the wine for aroma. A long but mostly passive weekend ferment.

Notes

Why fry before you braise. The yellow croaker has a thin, low-collagen skin that is its glory pan-fried but its liability in a braise, where it will slough off and disintegrate if the fish goes into the liquid raw. The shallow sear sets and seals the surface proteins, the cornstarch dust reinforces it, and the result is a fish that holds its shape and its skin through twelve minutes of simmering and basting. This is a setting sear, not a fry to doneness or even to deep crispness; the interior cooks in the braise and the skin softens slightly under the glaze, which is correct for hong shao. If you want shattering crisp skin, that is the pan-fried jogi-gui preparation, a different dish.

The caramel is the soul of red-braising. Hong shao, literally red-cooking, gets its name and its mahogany gloss from sugar caramelized before the braising liquid goes in, not from soy alone. The rock-sugar caramel contributes three things pure soy cannot: a red-brown color with depth and shine, a rounded bittersweet complexity from the caramelization, and a glaze body that lets the reduced sauce cling to the fish and the rice. Dark soy supplements the color but is used sparingly, only 10 g, because it turns bitter and muddy in quantity; the caramel does the real color work. The single highest-risk moment in the recipe is the caramel, which moves from amber to acrid in seconds, so have the premixed liquid in hand to deglaze the instant it is ready.

Umami and acid. The glutamate source is the light soy sauce, which anchors the braise with salt and savory depth, supported by the fish's own glutamate released into the sauce as it cooks. The finishing acid is the Chinkiang black vinegar, swirled in off-heat at the very end: it cuts the sweet-salt richness of the soy-caramel glaze and lifts the whole dish so it reads deep rather than cloying. Added off-heat, the vinegar keeps its malty aromatic top notes; cooked into the braise it would flatten into a dull sourness. The vinegar is a quiet move, half a tablespoon, but the braise tastes heavy and one-note without it.

Spoon, do not submerge. The braising liquid is kept deliberately shallow, about halfway up the fish, and the cook bastes constantly by spooning the sauce over the exposed top rather than flooding the pan and boiling the fish under liquid. This protects the thin skin from the agitation of a rolling submersion, concentrates the sauce as it reduces, and works the glaze into the slashes so the flesh is seasoned all the way to the bone. Turn the fish once at most; every extra move frays the skin. The payoff is a fish that arrives at the table whole and intact under a clinging lacquer, the hallmark of a well-executed hong shao yu.

Sourcing and the dye caution. Buy whole croaker at H Mart, 99 Ranch, or any Chinese or Korean grocer from the iced case. A genuine fish has a soft, natural pale-gold blush along the belly that fades to silver on the back, clear domed eyes, bright red gills, and firm springy flesh. Be wary of a fish dyed an even, saturated, vivid yellow to fake freshness; if the color rubs off on a wet finger or stains the ice, choose another. The larger 200 g and up croakers (the Chinese braising grade) suit this preparation better than the smallest Korean grade, since the firmer flesh stands up to the simmer, but any whole croaker that fits the pan works. Frozen-at-sea fish thaw overnight in the fridge with no loss for a braise.

Sources
  • Adapted from the Chinese hong shao (red-braise) whole-fish canon, the soy-and-sugar braise that turns a fried fish into a glossy mahogany centerpiece across Jiangnan and northern home kitchens. — The dish turns on a sequence the cook cannot reorder: salt and dry the fish so the skin can take a shallow fry that sets it against the braise, build a rock-sugar caramel for the red color and gloss that pure soy cannot give, then braise short and spoon the liquid over rather than submerge so the thin-skinned fish holds its shape while the sauce reduces to a clinging glaze. Light soy carries the salt and glutamate, dark soy and the caramel carry the color, and a final touch of Chinkiang vinegar cuts the sweet-salt richness without reading as sour.
