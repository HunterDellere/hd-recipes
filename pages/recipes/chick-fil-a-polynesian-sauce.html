Chick-fil-A Polynesian sauce (homemade copycat)

The sweet-sour orange-tinted dipping sauce. Ketchup, vinegar, sugar, mustard, and a small pinch of warm spice; tastes like a Catalina dressing crossed with a sweet-and-sour glaze. Pure pantry-and-ten-minutes build.

Mise en place by phase 1 phase ▾

Sauce

  1. 80 g ketchup
  2. 40 g granulated sugar
  3. 30 ml apple cider vinegar
  4. 15 g neutral oil (canola or avocado)
  5. 30 ml water
  6. 4 g yellow mustard (American)
  7. 0.5 g smoked paprika
  8. 0.5 g garlic powder
  9. 0.5 g onion powder
  10. 0.5 g fine sea salt — (pinch)

Execution

  1. 1
    In a small saucepan combine the 80 g / ⅓ cup ketchup, 40 g / 3 tbsp sugar, 30 ml / 2 tbsp apple cider vinegar, 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil, and 30 ml / 2 tbsp water. Whisk to a uniform liquid.
  2. 2
    Warm over medium-low heat for 3 to 4 minutes, whisking continuously, just until the sugar fully dissolves and the sauce looks glossy. Do not boil, sustained high heat caramelizes the sugar and the sauce takes on a burnt edge.
  3. 3
    Pull off heat. Whisk in the 4 g / ¾ tsp yellow mustard, 0.5 g smoked paprika, 0.5 g garlic powder, 0.5 g onion powder, and 0.5 g pinch salt. The smoked paprika should dissolve fully; if it leaves orange specks, push them with the back of a spoon.
  4. 4
    Cool to room temperature, then transfer to a sealed jar. Refrigerate at least 30 minutes before serving, the flavors integrate substantially with a short rest. Holds 2 weeks refrigerated.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional flavor→tropical Polynesian (with pineapple)

    Replace the water with 30 ml pineapple juice. Add ½ tsp grated fresh ginger. Closer to an actual Polynesian-island sweet-and-sour rather than a Georgia chain interpretation.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • ketchup → tomato paste + 1 tbsp brown sugar + 1 tsp vinegar If no ketchup on hand. Build from concentrate: 60 g tomato paste, 1 tbsp brown sugar, 1 tsp white vinegar dissolves into a workable ketchup-substitute base.
  • apple cider vinegar → distilled white vinegar The franchise version uses distilled white. Apple cider gives a softer fruit-edge that I prefer; both are legitimate.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → ketchup make it yourself Homemade ketchup made with real canned tomatoes is meaningfully better than commercial; when the ketchup is half the sauce, the upgrade is visible.

Notes

The franchise Polynesian sauce is essentially a French dressing crossed with a sweet-and-sour Chinese restaurant pour sauce. The orange color comes from the ketchup base plus the smoked paprika. Most copycat recipes online add a drop of orange food coloring; this version skips it because the natural color is already close.

Warm-then-cool method matters because the sugar dose is high. Cold mixing leaves undissolved sugar crystals that taste gritty; sustained boiling caramelizes the sugar to a darker flavor. The brief warm-and-whisk dissolves cleanly without color change.

The sauce is meant to read sweet. The franchise calibration is sugar-forward; if you find it cloying, drop the sugar to 25 g / 2 tbsp and add an extra teaspoon of vinegar to compensate. Bear in mind that as a dipping sauce for nuggets the salt and protein of the chicken counterbalances; tasted alone from the spoon, it will always read sweeter than it does in service.
