Peanut soup (Senegalese mafé / Ghanaian groundnut stew)

Beef or chicken slow-braised in a smooth peanut-and-tomato base with scotch bonnet, ginger, garlic, and a long reduction that pulls the natural peanut oil out and gives the stew its characteristic glossy red-brown sheen. Eaten over rice or with fufu; the canonical West African peanut stew, called mafé in Senegal and groundnut stew or domoda elsewhere along the coast.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Use natural unsalted unsweetened peanut butter (the only ingredient is peanuts, sometimes with salt). American commercial peanut butter (Jif, Skippy) contains added sugar and stabilizers that throw the dish toward dessert; if you only have commercial, reduce or omit the small honey addition and accept the slightly off-balance result.
  • The peanut paste needs to be thinned with broth before going into the pot; adding it dry to hot liquid clumps the proteins. Whisk the paste with 250 ml of warm broth in a separate bowl first, then pour the thinned mixture in.
  • The 60 to 75 minute braise is what lets the natural peanut oil render out and pool on the surface; this glossy red-brown sheen is the structural visual signature of the dish. Cooks who shorten the braise produce a peanut stew that reads pasty rather than glossy.

Execution

  1. 1
    Pat the 800 g / chuck or chicken pieces dry with paper towels and season all sides with the 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt and the 2 g / ¾ tsp black pepper. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy 5 to 6 quart Dutch oven over medium-high heat until shimmering, about 1 minute. Sear the meat in two batches, 3 minutes per side, until deeply browned. Transfer to a plate. The Maillard crust on the meat is the structural flavor anchor; do not crowd the pot or the meat steams instead of searing. Maillard sear
  2. 2
    Drop the heat to medium. Add the 250 g / 1 large diced yellow onion to the hot pot (with the rendered meat fat still in it) and cook 6 to 8 minutes, stirring every minute, until the onion is deep golden brown.
  3. 3
    Add the 20 g / 5 cloves microplaned garlic, the 20 g / 4 cm knob microplaned ginger, and the 8 g / 1 to 2 scotch bonnets minced. Cook 1 minute, stirring constantly, until fragrant.
  4. 4
    Push the aromatics to one side. Add the 60 g / 4 tbsp tomato paste to the empty side and fry 3 minutes in the residual oil, stirring constantly, until it darkens from bright red to brick red and the oil takes on its color. Stir the aromatics back through.
  5. 5
    In a separate bowl, whisk the 300 g / 1¼ cups natural peanut butter with 250 ml / 1 cup of the chicken or beef stock until completely smooth, no lumps. This pre-thinning is critical; adding dry peanut butter to hot liquid causes the proteins to clump.
  6. 6
    Pour the thinned peanut mixture into the pot and stir to combine with the aromatic base. Add the remaining 750 ml / 3 cups stock, the 400 g / 1 can crushed whole tomatoes (broken up by hand), the 4 g / 1 Maggi cube crumbled in, the 3 g / 1 tsp fresh thyme, and the 2 bay leaves. Stir well; the mixture should be the consistency of thick gravy. Return the seared meat (and any juices on the plate) to the pot, nestling the pieces into the liquid so they are mostly submerged.
  7. 7
    Bring to a vigorous simmer, then drop to low and cover with the lid slightly ajar (1 cm gap to let steam escape). Simmer for 60 minutes (chicken) or 90 minutes (beef chuck), stirring gently every 20 minutes so the peanut base does not catch on the bottom. The stew should look glossy and red-brown by the end with a visible sheen of natural peanut oil pooling on the surface; this oil rendering out of the peanut paste is the structural signature of a properly cooked peanut stew.
  8. 8
    If using the 300 g / 1 medium sweet potato, add it for the final 25 to 30 minutes of the braise (drop it in when the meat has 25 minutes left to go). The sweet potato softens to fork-tender and absorbs the peanut sauce; it makes the stew more substantial as a one-pot meal.
  9. 9
    Uncover and taste the stew. It should read deeply nutty, faintly tangy from the tomato, warm from the scotch bonnet. If too thick, splash in 100 ml water; if too thin, simmer uncovered another 10 minutes. Adjust salt; the Maggi contributes salt, so taste before adding more.
  10. 10
    Pull from heat. Stir in the 15 g / 1 tbsp fresh lemon juice off heat. Discard the bay leaves. Rest covered for 5 minutes off heat so the flavors integrate.
  11. 11
    Serve in wide shallow bowls over steamed long-grain rice (the canonical Senegalese service), or with fufu or pounded yam (the Ghanaian service), garnished with the 30 g / ¼ cup chopped roasted peanuts and the 10 g / ¼ cup torn cilantro if using.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→with bone-in lamb shoulder

    Replace the beef chuck with 1000 g bone-in lamb shoulder, cut into 5 cm chunks. Extend the braise to 120 minutes; lamb collagen needs longer to break down. The dish becomes lamb mafé, which is the more traditional Senegalese version (lamb is the more common meat in West African Muslim cooking).

  • Dietary protein→vegan, with mushrooms and chickpeas

    Skip the meat entirely. Replace with 400 g cremini or oyster mushrooms (quartered, seared in the oil for 4 minutes per side until deeply browned) and 400 g cooked chickpeas (drained from 2 standard cans). Add both at the point where you would return the meat to the pot. Use vegetable stock instead of meat stock. The dish loses the meat collagen depth but the peanut-and-tomato base carries the flavor.

  • Regional cuisine→Ghanaian groundnut stew (with okra and sweet potato)

    Add 300 g sweet potato (as the optional addition) and 200 g whole small okra pods (added in the final 15 minutes). Reduce the tomato to half (200 g) and skip the lemon finish. The dish becomes the Ghanaian version, which leans starchy-vegetal rather than the Senegalese meat-and-peanut focus.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • natural unsalted unsweetened peanut butter → commercial peanut butter (Jif, Skippy), with adjustments If commercial peanut butter is the only option, skip any sweet addition (some recipes call for honey, this one does not), reduce the salt by 2 g, and accept that the finished dish reads slightly off-balance. The added sugar and stabilizers in commercial peanut butter push the stew toward dessert. Far better to source natural peanut butter from any grocery (Smucker's Natural, Whole Foods 365, or similar).
  • scotch bonnet pepper → habanero (1:1), or fresno chili (3 peppers for similar heat) Habanero is the closest substitute and the same C. chinense species. Fresno reads cleaner and milder, useful for diners who do not handle aggressive heat.
  • Maggi seasoning cube → better than bouillon, 1 tsp Marmite, or 1 tbsp soy sauce Maggi is genuine West African pantry. Substitutes work but lose cuisine-specific signature.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → chicken stock or beef stock (matching the protein) make it yourself A bone-rich homemade stock deepens the braise dramatically; the gelatin from the stock plus the gelatin from the bone-in meat plus the peanut fat produce a glossier, more luxurious finished stew.

Notes

Why natural peanut butter. Commercial American peanut butter contains added sugar (4 to 8 g per 2 tbsp serving) and partially hydrogenated oils for stability. Both wreck a peanut stew: the sugar pushes the dish toward dessert and the stabilizers prevent the natural peanut oil from rendering out during the long braise. Natural unsweetened peanut butter (only ingredient: peanuts, sometimes with salt) is what every West African kitchen uses and what this dish needs. Smucker''s Natural, Whole Foods 365, Trader Joe''s, and most natural-food brands all make working versions.

Why the long braise matters. The 60 to 90 minute simmer does two things: it tenderizes the meat (collagen converts to gelatin) and it renders the natural peanut oil out of the peanut paste, where it pools on the surface as a visible glossy sheen. A short cook leaves the peanut base reading pasty and dull; the long cook produces the glossy red-brown stew that is the structural visual signature of a real West African peanut stew.

The finishing lemon. The acid lift at the end is structural; the rich peanut-and-tomato base reads heavy without an acid finish. The Senegalese tradition uses lime; lemon is the closer Western equivalent. Vinegar works in a pinch (1 tsp white wine vinegar).

The swallow. Senegalese mafé is traditionally served over long-grain rice; Ghanaian groundnut stew is more often served with fufu or pounded yam. Both work; rice is the accessible default for Western kitchens.

Make-ahead and reheat. Peanut stew improves dramatically overnight; the spices integrate, the peanut deepens, and the sauce thickens further. Reheat gently with a splash of water to loosen, covered, over low heat for 15 minutes. Holds 4 days refrigerated, 3 months frozen.
