Miso-butter shrimp with delicata, shimeji, and charred broccoli

A one-pan washoku-leaning sheet dish. Delicata roasts to chestnut-sweet edges, broccoli and shimeji char dry, shrimp hit the pan last and the whole thing gets glossed with a miso-mirin-butter sauce finished with rice vinegar.

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the oven to 230°C / 450°F with a heavy rimmed sheet pan on the middle rack. The pan must be ripping hot before the squash hits it; a cold pan steams the squash; a 230°C pan gets you Maillard browning in 90 seconds.
  2. 2
    Brine the shrimp. Toss 680 g / 1.5 lb shrimp with 4 g / ¾ tsp baking soda and 4 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt in a bowl. Let sit at room temperature while you prep the vegetables. The alkaline-saline cure shifts surface protein structure and locks in water; the shrimp will read snappier and juicier with no extra effort.
  3. 3
    In a large bowl, toss 600 g / 1.3 lb delicata half-moons with 15 g / 1 tbsp avocado oil and 3 g / ½ tsp of the kosher salt. Spread on the preheated sheet pan in a single layer. Roast 12 minutes; do not stir. The flat sides need full pan contact to char; flipping early means pale, soft squash.
  4. 4
    While the squash roasts, build the glaze. In a small bowl, whisk 45 g / 3 tbsp white miso, 30 g / 2 tbsp mirin, 30 g / 2 tbsp sake, 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy sauce, 12 g / 1 tbsp grated ginger, and 12 g / 3 cloves grated garlic. The miso will fight you for ten seconds, then loosen; keep whisking. Set aside.
  5. 5
    In the same vegetable bowl, toss 450 g / 1 lb broccoli (florets and bias-cut stems) and 200 g / 2 packs separated shimeji with the remaining 15 g / 1 tbsp avocado oil and 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. Do not crowd them; if the bowl looks soggy, blot dry with a towel before they hit the pan.
  6. 6
    Pull the squash. Push it to one half of the sheet pan, scatter the 450 g / 1 lb broccoli and 200 g / 7 oz shimeji in the open half, separating the shimeji into a single layer. The shimeji must touch the pan directly; they are dry-searing first, picking up oil from the pan film, not sitting in oil. Roast 10 minutes. The broccoli florets should char at the tips; the shimeji should crisp at the cap edges and stop reading bitter.
  7. 7
    Melt 60 g / 4 tbsp butter in a small saucepan over medium-low. Cook past the foaming stage until the milk solids smell faintly toasted; about 90 seconds, stop before deep-amber browning (we want some Maillard depth, not full beurre noisette here, because the miso has its own toasted backbone). Brown butter (beurre noisette)
  8. 8
    Off heat, whisk the miso mixture into the warm 60 g / 4 tbsp browned butter. The glaze will look glossy and slightly broken; this is fine; the emulsion reforms when it hits the hot vegetables.
  9. 9
    Pull the sheet pan. Push everything to one side. Pat the brined shrimp dry; surface moisture is the enemy of a sear; and spread them in a single layer on the empty half of the pan. Brush the shrimp with about a third of the glaze (~50 g). Return to the oven, switch to broil on high (top rack about 15 cm from the element), and broil 3–4 minutes until the shrimp curl into Cs (not Os; Os mean overcooked) and the tails turn deep coral with charred edges. Pull immediately at first sign of color set; carryover does the rest.
  10. 10
    Drizzle the remaining glaze over the vegetables on the pan, then drizzle 10 g / 2 tsp rice vinegar across the entire pan. Toss with a spatula directly on the sheet pan; the residual heat tightens the glaze into a coat instead of a puddle. The vinegar cuts the salt-sweet weight of the miso-butter and pulls the dish together; without it the plate reads heavy and one-note.
  11. 11
    Finish with 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil drizzled across, then scatter 30 g / 3 sliced scallion greens and 6 g / 2 tsp toasted sesame seeds. Serve directly from the sheet pan or transfer to a warmed platter. Pair with short-grain rice.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→sea scallops (U/10 dry-pack)

    Skip the brine; scallops dry-pack are already at peak texture and salting them pulls water. Pat aggressively dry. Sear scallops separately in a screaming-hot stainless pan with neutral oil for 90 seconds per side until deeply browned, then add to the sheet pan at the very end and glaze. Don't broil scallops; the dry-heat sear is what they want.

  • Regional cuisine→Korean-leaning (gochujang-butter)

    Replace the white miso with 30 g / 2 tbsp gochujang and 15 g / 1 tbsp doenjang. Sake stays. Add 2 g / 1 tsp gochugaru to the vegetable toss. Finish with perilla oil instead of sesame, and a squeeze of yuzu or lime instead of rice vinegar. The dish moves from washoku-restraint to fermented-funk-and-fruit-heat.

  • Occasion season→spring/summer (no winter squash)

    Swap delicata for 600 g / 1.3 lb baby zucchini, halved lengthwise, and add 200 g / 7 oz sugar snap peas in the last 4 minutes. Drop the initial roast time to 6 minutes; zucchini chars fast and overcooks faster.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • shrimp → sea scallops or firm white fish See modifications for scallop technique. For fish: cube cod or halibut to 2 cm, brine the same way, broil 3 minutes only.
  • delicata squash → kabocha squash, peeled, sliced 1 cm thick Kabocha skin is too tough to leave on. Closest flavor profile; roast 2 extra minutes to compensate for higher moisture.
  • shimeji → maitake (hen of the woods), pulled into clusters Crisps even better. Read the cap-edge browning the same way.
  • hon-mirin → sake + sugar (3 parts sake : 1 part sugar by weight) For 30 g mirin: 22 g sake + 8 g sugar. Loses koji-derived complexity but covers the alcohol-sweetener role.

Notes

This recipe is engineered around two heat windows and a finishing acid.

**The vegetable window** is dry, hard, and uninterrupted. Delicata gets a head start because winter squash is dense and needs the longest. Broccoli and shimeji come in at minute 12 because they're fragile; broccoli florets char in 8 minutes, shimeji crisp in 6, and either of them on the pan from the beginning would turn to mush. The shimeji specifically need single-layer pan contact for the bitterness compounds (fasciculol) to break down; pile them up and they'll read raw and acrid in the finished dish.

**The shrimp window** is short and aggressive. Brining (alkaline + salt) at 0.6% baking soda by weight changes the surface charge of the shrimp protein, locking water in and producing a noticeably snappier bite. Broiling under high heat for 3–4 minutes lets the surface caramelize via the miso glaze without overcooking the interior; shrimp internal target is 60–63°C and broiling overshoots fast. Pull the shrimp at C-shape, not O-shape; carryover finishes them.

**The acid is non-negotiable.** Miso, butter, mirin, and soy stack heavy savory weight. The 2 tsp of rice vinegar at the end, drizzled on the hot pan, is what makes the dish read clean instead of muddy; it cuts through the fat and brightens the squash sweetness. If you taste at the end and it reads "good but heavy," more vinegar is your move, not more salt.

**MSG is welcome but not required.** A pinch (~1 g) added to the glaze during whisking deepens the umami without adding salt. Optional, but in line with the broader washoku flavor architecture.
