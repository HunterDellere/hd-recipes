Individual fluffy chocolate cakes

Two 4-inch tall, light-crumbed chocolate cakes built on creamed butter, bloomed Dutch cocoa, and sour cream for tenderness. Designed to be served warm with a scoop of ice cream melting into the open crumb.

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat oven to 350°F (175°C) with a rack in the middle. Butter two 4-inch round pans heavily with about 10 g / 2 tsp soft butter total, line each base with a parchment circle, butter again, and dust with cocoa, knocking out the excess. Set the pans on a sheet tray.
  2. 2
    Whisk the 25 g / ¼ cup Dutch-process cocoa with the 75 ml / ⅓ cup hot coffee in a small bowl until it forms a glossy paste with no dry pockets. Let it sit while you cream the butter; the rest hydrates the starch granules and pulls aromatics into solution. The bowl should be barely warm, not hot, by the time you use it. Blooming cocoa
  3. 3
    In a stand mixer with the paddle (or with a hand mixer), cream the 85 g / 6 tbsp butter with the 100 g / ½ cup granulated sugar and 30 g / 2 tbsp light brown sugar on medium for 3 to 4 minutes, until pale, fluffy, and falling in a thick ribbon off the paddle. The mechanical aeration here is the entire lift mechanism; the chemical leaveners only expand bubbles you already created. Cutting this short produces a dense crumb regardless of leavener quantity. Creaming butter and sugar
  4. 4
    On low speed, add the 1 whole egg, then the 1 yolk, beating each until just incorporated, then 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste. Scrape the bowl. Add the 70 g / ¼ cup + 1 tbsp sour cream and 30 ml / 2 tbsp whole milk and mix on low until uniform; the mixture will look slightly broken or curdled, which is fine, the flour pulls it back together.
  5. 5
    In a small bowl, whisk the 110 g / ¾ cup + 2 tbsp all-purpose flour, 10 g / 1 tbsp cornstarch, 3 g / ¾ tsp baking powder, 1.5 g / ¼ tsp baking soda, and 2 g / ⅓ tsp fine sea salt. Add to the wet bowl in two additions, alternating with the bloomed cocoa from step 2 (cocoa, dry, cocoa, dry, dry), mixing on low between each just until incorporated. Stop the moment the last streaks disappear: this batter is light and overmixing knocks out the air you cultivated in the cream.
  6. 6
    Divide between the two pans (about 250 g per pan). Smooth the surfaces gently with a spoon: the batter is thick and should level evenly without prodding. Tap each pan once on the counter to release any oversized bubbles, but do not bang aggressively, you do not want to deflate the structure.
  7. 7
    Bake 22 to 26 minutes. Pull when the tops have domed and sprung back fully under a finger press, the cakes have just started to pull from the sides of the pan, and a toothpick inserted in the center comes out with a few moist crumbs (not wet batter). Underbake by even 2 minutes here and the centers sink as they cool; this is a bouncy crumb, not a fudge crumb, and it needs to be fully set.
  8. 8
    Cool in the pans on a rack 8 minutes, no longer; the cakes need to release while still slightly warm so the steam in the crumb doesn't condense and turn the bottom gummy. Run a thin knife around each edge, invert onto a plate, peel parchment, flip back so the domed tops are up.
  9. 9
    Serve warm. Set each cake on a small plate, place a generous scoop of vanilla ice cream (90 to 120 g) directly on top, and serve immediately. The open crumb absorbs the first wave of melting cream into the cake interior; subsequent melt pools around the base and the cake stays distinct rather than going soggy. The cake is engineered for this moment.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional flavor accent→malted

    Add 15 g / 2 tbsp diastatic or non-diastatic malted milk powder to the dry ingredients. Pairs with the warm-cake-and-ice-cream format to read as a malt shake in cake form. Reduce flour by 10 g to compensate.

  • Occasion pan format→one 6-inch round

    Bake in a single 6-inch round pan at 350°F for 32 to 36 minutes. The crackle and edge contrast diminish but the crumb structure holds. Lose the individual format and the cake-plus-scoop visual identity weakens; this is a fallback, not a preference.

  • Occasion pairing→with chocolate ganache pour

    Skip the ice cream. Pour 40 to 60 g warm chocolate ganache (1:1 dark chocolate to cream) over each warm cake just before serving. The fluffy crumb absorbs ganache differently than the dense decadent version: the cake stays distinct, the ganache pools rather than soaks. Crosses into proper layer-cake territory without the layering work.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • sour cream → full-fat Greek yogurt Same low-pH tenderizing function and similar fat content. The yogurt's tang reads slightly cleaner; sour cream contributes more roundness. Either works; do not substitute non-fat versions of either, the fat is doing structural work.
  • cake flour subbed via AP + cornstarch → true cake flour, 120 g / 1 cup minus 1 tbsp Direct equivalent. The AP-plus-cornstarch trick lowers effective protein from ~10.5% to ~8% and matches the tenderness of cake flour without keeping a separate bag. Use whichever is on hand.
  • Dutch-process cocoa powder → natural cocoa powder The leavener balance is set for Dutched cocoa's neutral pH. With natural cocoa, increase baking soda to 3 g / ½ tsp and drop baking powder to 1 g / ¼ tsp; the soda neutralizes the natural acidity and provides equivalent total lift.
  • hot brewed coffee → hot water + 2 g / 1 tsp instant espresso powder Same chocolate-amplification function. Plain hot water alone (no coffee element) loses the bridge bitterness; the cake will read more milk-chocolate sweet, which is correct if you want a kid-friendlier version.

Notes

This is the counterpart to individual-decadent-chocolate-cakes, not a replacement. Same format, opposite texture target. The decadent version is built on a chocolate-butter emulsion with melted chocolate and a fudge-textured crumb that carries its own fat payload; this version is built on creamed butter with cocoa-only chocolate, tall lift, and an open crumb that pairs cleanly with cold dairy on top.

Why no melted chocolate. Melted chocolate dropped into a creamed-butter batter dampens the air pockets the cream just built, deflates the lift, and tightens the crumb into the dense territory we want to avoid. Cocoa-only delivers chocolate flavor without the structural cost; the bloom step extracts enough aromatic depth that the cake reads richly chocolatey despite the cocoa-only choice.

Why the extra yolk. One whole egg gives lift via the white; the second yolk adds fat and emulsifying lecithin without more white that would dry the crumb. The combination produces a tender, open crumb that holds its shape under a scoop of ice cream.

Why sour cream and milk together. Sour cream is structural fat with a low pH that softens gluten; milk is liquid hydration without the fat. The 70 g / 30 ml split hits the moisture target the dome and crumb need without making the batter so wet it cannot hold air through the bake.

The ice cream pairing is the engineering target. The serving direction is not a suggestion; the recipe was tuned around it. A scoop of vanilla ice cream (or coffee, or salted caramel, or burnt-honey) on top of each warm cake is the intended terminal state, and the crumb porosity, sugar level, and cocoa intensity are calibrated for it. Eaten plain, the cake is good; eaten as designed, it earns the format.
