Homemade panko breadcrumbs

Crustless white bread, dried low and slow, then ground coarse. The result is the long, splintery shard of real panko, not the grainy dust of the supermarket can.

The bread

  1. 250 g white sandwich bread, crusts removed — (8–10 slices, plain white pullman or shokupan; day-old is ideal but fresh works)

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat oven to 120°C (250°F). Tear the crustless bread into rough 4cm chunks; do not cube neatly. The irregular edges become the splintery shards. The crusts can be saved for fine breadcrumbs (see notes); the panko itself is from the soft interior only.
  2. 2
    Spread the chunks in a single layer on a parchment-lined sheet pan. Bake 30–40 minutes, tossing once at the 20-minute mark. The bread should dry through but stay pale, straw-colored at most. If it browns at all you have toast, not panko.
  3. 3
    Cool completely on the pan, 10 minutes. The chunks must be brittle through to the center; squeeze one: it should crumble dry, not bend.
  4. 4
    Pulse in a food processor in three or four short bursts (1–2 seconds each) until the largest pieces are the size of a sesame seed and the smallest are still crumb, not dust. Stop early; the visual signal is "uneven shards" not "uniform sand."
  5. 5
    Sift through a wide-mesh sieve (4mm); anything that drops through is fine breadcrumb (save for meatballs and gratins), and what stays on top is panko. The yield split is roughly 80% panko / 20% fine.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • white sandwich bread → sourdough or country loaf Acceptable but skews darker and the crust-to-crumb ratio is wrong; you lose half to crusts. Pullman or shokupan stays white and gives long shards.

Notes

Real panko is structural, not textural. The splintery long-shard shape is what makes it stay crisp under sauce — it traps air in the gaps so steam can escape rather than soaking the breading. Round breadcrumbs absorb; panko shards deflect.

Why low temp: at 250°F the bread loses water before any Maillard reaction starts on the cut surfaces. Industrial panko avoids browning entirely (the crumbs are baked in an electric field, no contact with hot metal); home oven gets close enough that the difference is invisible in a fried application.

Crusts make excellent fine breadcrumbs: same drying step, food-process to powder, season with salt and parmesan or za'atar for a meatball mix or gratin topping. Don't throw them out.

Storage: airtight at room temperature, 2 weeks. Freezer 3 months. The crumbs absorb humidity from the air and turn flexible if left out; if that happens, 5 minutes back in a 250°F oven re-crisps them.

The substitution test: if your "panko" is dust-fine and uniformly tan, it's regular breadcrumb in a bigger jar.
