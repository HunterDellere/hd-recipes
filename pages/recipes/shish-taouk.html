Shish taouk

The Levantine garlic-and-lemon chicken skewer: boneless chicken marinated in yogurt, a heavy hand of garlic, lemon, olive oil, tomato paste, and seven-spice, then grilled hard so the surface chars while the yogurt-tenderized interior stays juicy. Where Persian joojeh leans on saffron, shish taouk is built on raw garlic and citrus, and it lives beside a pot of toum, the fierce whipped garlic sauce that is its inseparable partner. Grilled across Lebanon, Syria, and the wider Levant, wrapped in saj bread with pickles and toum, or plated with rice and grilled vegetables.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Marinate the chicken at least 4 hours, ideally overnight. The yogurt and lemon acid tenderize the meat and the garlic needs hours to penetrate; a short marinade only seasons the surface. Do not exceed 24 hours, past which the acid begins to break the surface down to a mealy texture.
  • Have the toum (garlic sauce) made ahead. It is the inseparable partner to shish taouk, not an optional dip, and it needs time to set up cold. Make it while the chicken marinates, or buy a good one.
  • Skewer the chicken and any vegetables separately; they cook at different rates. Grill the chicken over a hot direct fire; charcoal is ideal but a grill pan or broiler works.

Execution

  1. 1
    Marinate the chicken (4 to 24 hours ahead). Crush the 20 g garlic to a paste with a pinch of the salt. In a bowl, whisk the 120 g / ½ cup yogurt, the garlic paste, 45 ml / 3 tbsp lemon juice, 40 g / 3 tbsp olive oil, 20 g / 1 tbsp tomato paste, 3 g / 1½ tsp coriander, 3 g / 1½ tsp cumin, 3 g / 1½ tsp paprika, 1.5 g / ¾ tsp allspice, 1 g / ½ tsp cinnamon, 1.5 g / ¾ tsp white pepper, and the 9 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt into a smooth rust-red marinade. Add the 800 g chicken and turn to coat every piece. Cover and refrigerate 4 to 24 hours. Pull from the fridge 40 minutes before grilling.
  2. 2
    Build the fire. Light a chimney of charcoal; when the coals are ashed over, spread them for a hot direct zone and a cooler zone. Oil the grate with a rag dipped in the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil. The grate should be hot enough that a hand 10 cm above forces you back within 2 to 3 seconds.
  3. 3
    Skewer. Thread the marinated chicken snugly onto skewers, 5 to 6 pieces each, wiping off excess marinade so it chars rather than steams. On separate skewers, thread the 150 g red onion and 150 g green pepper squares; brush them with any oil left in the marinade bowl.
  4. 4
    Grill. Lay the chicken skewers over the hot zone. Grill 12 to 15 minutes total, turning every 3 minutes, until the surface is charred amber-mahogany and the interior reaches 74°C / 165°F. The marinade colors the surface fast, so watch for scorching and slide to the cooler zone if flare-ups blacken the meat. Grill the onion and pepper skewers alongside for 8 to 10 minutes until charred and tender. Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Rest. Slide the skewers onto a platter and rest 5 minutes so the juices redistribute; chicken pulled straight off the fire sheds its moisture onto the plate.
  6. 6
    Serve. Warm the 4 flatbreads briefly at the edge of the fire. Slide the chicken off the skewers, pile the grilled onion and pepper alongside, and serve with the 150 g toum, the warm bread, pickles, and the lemon wedges. Eat wrapped in bread with a smear of toum and a pickle, or plated with the toum on the side.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture no charcoal grill→grill pan or broiler shish taouk

    Preheat a cast-iron grill pan over high heat until smoking, or set an oven rack 12 cm below the broiler on high. Cook the skewers 3 to 4 minutes per side to 74°C internal. You lose the charcoal smoke but keep the garlicky char; a pinch of smoked paprika in the marinade compensates a little for the missing wood smoke.

  • Texture the traditional leaner cut→shish taouk with chicken breast

    Use 800 g boneless chicken breast in 3.5 cm pieces, the classic cut for shish taouk. Keep the full marinade acid and yogurt, and pull at 71°C to catch carryover; breast dries fast past 74°C. The result is firmer and leaner than the thigh version, and closer to what most Levantine grills serve.

  • Occasion a wrap for the street-food version→shish taouk wrap (sandwich)

    Slice the grilled chicken, roll it tightly in saj or thin markook bread with a heavy smear of toum, pickled cucumbers, and a few French fries (the Lebanese way), then toast the wrap on a flat griddle until crisp. The ubiquitous Lebanese takeout format.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • boneless, skinless chicken thighs → boneless chicken breast Breast is the more traditional cut for shish taouk and gives a firmer, leaner bite; thigh is more forgiving and juicier over a hot fire. Both are correct. Pull breast at 71°C to avoid drying.
  • sweet paprika and the ground spices → a ready Lebanese seven-spice (sabaa baharat), about 8 g / 4 tsp total The individual spices here reconstruct a seven-spice blend. A good jarred Lebanese seven-spice replaces the coriander, cumin, allspice, cinnamon, and white pepper in one measure; keep the separate paprika for color.
  • pita or saj flatbread → any thin pliable flatbread, or serve over rice Shish taouk plates equally well over vermicelli rice (roz bi shaghria) with grilled tomato. The bread is for the wrap format; the rice format is the sit-down restaurant plate.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → toum (whipped garlic sauce) make it yourself Toum is the soul of shish taouk, a fierce emulsion of raw garlic, oil, lemon, and salt that no jarred sauce matches; whipped fresh it is airy, pungent, and structurally the whole point of the dish.

Notes

Why yogurt and lemon together. The marinade does two jobs: the yogurt's lactic acid and calcium gently denature surface proteins and hold moisture (a slow, forgiving tenderizer), while the lemon's stronger acid brightens and penetrates. Together they tenderize without turning the chicken mealy, provided you stay inside the 24-hour window. Past that, the combined acid load breaks the surface down too far and the texture goes soft and pasty. The tomato paste is not just color: it is the glutamate anchor that gives the marinade savory depth beneath the garlic and citrus.

Garlic is the identity, and toum is not optional. Shish taouk is a garlic dish. There is raw garlic crushed into the marinade, and then there is toum, the whipped emulsion of raw garlic, oil, lemon, and salt, served alongside. The two are different expressions of the same allium, and a shish taouk plate without toum is missing its defining note. This is the clearest line between shish taouk and Persian joojeh, which is built on saffron and onion and served with saffron rice rather than garlic sauce.

White pepper, not black. Levantine seven-spice traditionally uses white pepper, which brings heat without the black-pepper fruitiness and keeps the marinade's color clean. It is a small detail that reads as authentic; black pepper works if it is what you have.

Umami and acid, built in. The tomato paste and yogurt supply the savory-umami base, and the acid is layered: lemon in the marinade, lemon at the table, and the sharp lemon-and-garlic hit of the toum. This garlic-citrus brightness is exactly what a rich grilled-chicken plate needs to stay lively.

Pairs across Lebanese and broader Levantine cooking. Serve with toum, warm pita or saj, pickled turnips and cucumbers, a fattoush or tabbouleh, and grilled tomatoes. Vermicelli rice and a bowl of hummus round it into a full plate. The marinated chicken keeps raw 2 days refrigerated and freezes well in the marinade for a month; thaw fully before grilling.

Sources
  • Levantine (Lebanese and Syrian) grill tradition — Shish taouk (شيش طاووق, from Turkish şiş tavuk, skewered chicken) is the Levantine chicken kebab, ubiquitous across Lebanon, Syria, Jordan, and Palestine and throughout the diaspora. It is defined by a garlic-forward, lemon-bright, yogurt-tenderized marinade seasoned with seven-spice (a Lebanese baharat) and tomato paste for color and depth, and it is always served with toum, the emulsified raw-garlic sauce. It differs from Persian joojeh (which is saffron-and-onion driven) and from Turkish şiş (whole lamb cubes) in its garlic-and-citrus character.
