Recipe

Crispy-skin black sea bass with beurre blanc

Two black sea bass fillets scored, salted, and pressed skin-side-down in a hot pan until the skin renders flat and shatter-crisp, flipped for a brief set, and pulled at just-cooked while the flesh stays moist. The sauce is a classic beurre blanc: a white-wine, shallot, and white-wine-vinegar reduction mounted with cold cubed butter off the heat into a glossy emulsion, finished with lemon and chives. The firm, structured flake and hard-rendering skin of black sea bass are exactly what this preparation is built for.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Dry and score the skin well ahead, this is the whole game. Pat both fillets bone-dry on the skin side with paper towels, then set them skin-up, uncovered, on a rack in the fridge for at least 30 minutes (up to a few hours) so the surface dehydrates further; score the skin in shallow 1 cm-apart diagonal lines just before salting. Damp skin steams and goes leathery instead of crisping, so the dryness of the skin at the moment it hits the pan determines the entire result.
  • Stage the beurre blanc mise completely before the fish goes in the pan. The butter mount happens off heat and moves fast, so have the reduction made and the 110 g cold cubed butter measured and waiting in the fridge until the moment you whisk it in. A beurre blanc breaks when it overheats, so it is built last, after the fish is seared and resting warm.
  • Keep the fillets warm while you finish the sauce. Have a warm plate ready and rest the seared fish skin-side-up (never skin-down, which re-steams the crust) on the rack or a warm plate while you mount the butter, so the skin stays crisp and the flesh coasts to its final temperature.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the fish

  1. 340 g black sea bass fillets, skin on, pin-boned, two fillets of ~170 g each, scaled, pin bones pulled, skin patted bone-dry and air-dried on a rack, skin scored in shallow 1 cm-apart diagonal lines
  2. 4 g kosher salt
  3. 1 g black pepper, freshly ground
  4. 14 g neutral oil (grapeseed, canola, or rice bran)

Phase 2, beurre blanc reduction

  1. 30 g shallot, minced fine, minced as fine as possible so it dissolves into the sauce — (1 medium shallot; the aromatic and a textural body for the emulsion)
  2. 120 g dry white wine (Sauvignon Blanc or Muscadet)
  3. 30 g white wine vinegar
  4. 30 g heavy cream

Phase 3, the mount and finish

  1. 110 g unsalted butter, cold, cubed, cut into 1 cm cubes and kept refrigerator-cold until the moment of mounting
  2. 8 g lemon, juiced, plus zest if desired
  3. 6 g chives, sliced thin, sliced into fine rings
  4. 1 g fine sea salt

Execution

  1. 1
    Build the reduction first, before the fish, since it can sit while you sear. In a small saucepan combine the 30 g / 1 minced shallot, the 120 g / ½ cup dry white wine, and the 30 g / 2 tbsp white wine vinegar. Simmer over medium heat until the liquid reduces to about 2 tbsp of syrupy, intensely sour-aromatic liquid with the shallot suspended in it, 6 to 8 minutes. If using the 30 g / 2 tbsp heavy cream, stir it in now and simmer 1 minute more; the cream is a stabilizer that makes the butter mount far more forgiving. Pull the pan off the heat and let it cool to barely warm, the temperature of the reduction governs the emulsion, and a reduction too hot will break the butter into oil.
  2. 2
    Salt the fish. Just before cooking, blot the scored skin one more time, then season the skin side of both 340 g / 2 fillets evenly with about half the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt, pressing it into the scores. Salt at the last moment on the skin side: salt applied early draws moisture to the surface and defeats the crisping. Leave the flesh side unseasoned for now.
  3. 3
    Heat the 14 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy stainless or cast-iron skillet over medium-high until it shimmers and just begins to wisp smoke. Lay the fillets in skin-side-down, away from you, and immediately press each one flat with a fish spatula or a flat lid for the first 20 to 30 seconds. Fillets curl the instant the skin hits heat as the skin-side collagen contracts faster than the flesh; the press holds the skin in full flat contact so it renders evenly instead of buckling and crisping only at the high points. Once they lie flat on their own, ease the pressure but keep them moving as little as possible. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Render the skin. Hold over medium-high and let the skin cook undisturbed for 4 to 6 minutes, adjusting the heat so it sizzles steadily without scorching. You are driving off the subcutaneous moisture and setting the skin collagen into a rigid sheet; resist the urge to peek or lift, since the skin releases from the pan on its own only when it is properly crisp. The edges of the flesh will turn opaque and creep up the sides as the heat conducts through, this visible opacity line is your doneness gauge. The skin is ready when it is deep golden, rigid, and lifts cleanly without sticking.
  5. 5
    Flip and finish. Season the now up-facing flesh with the remaining kosher salt and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper, then flip each fillet onto its flesh side for just 30 to 60 seconds, only long enough to set the surface; the residual pan heat carries the lean flake to a moist, just-set 52 to 55°C / 126 to 131°F at the thickest point. Black sea bass is lean and overshoots into chalkiness fast above 65°C, so pull it the moment the flesh is opaque but still glistening. Transfer to a warm plate or rack skin-side-up so the crust stays crisp, and let it rest while you mount the sauce.
  6. 6
    Mount the beurre blanc off heat. With the reduction barely warm and off the burner, whisk in the 110 g / 1 stick cold cubed butter a few cubes at a time, adding more only once the last has emulsified into a glossy, opaque, pale sauce. Work fast and keep the pan off direct heat; if it ever stops being warm enough to melt the butter, return it to the lowest heat for a few seconds, no hotter than you can hold a finger to. Above roughly 58°C the emulsion breaks into a greasy split of butterfat and watery reduction; the cold butter going in is what keeps the whole mass in the narrow window where the milk-solid emulsifiers and wine acids hold the fat in suspension. Pan emulsion (mantecare)
  7. 7
    Finish and plate. Off heat, stir the 8 g / 2 tsp lemon juice and most of the 6 g / 2 tbsp chives into the finished sauce, then season to taste with the 1 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt; the lemon is the bright finishing acid over the rounder vinegar in the reduction, and the salt dissolves cleanly into the warm emulsion. Spoon a pool of beurre blanc onto each warm plate, set a fillet skin-side-up on top so the crust never touches the sauce and stays shatter-crisp, and scatter the reserved chives over the skin. Serve immediately, the sauce does not hold long and the skin softens the longer it sits.
Safety
  • black sea bass at 52 to 55°C / 126 to 131°F internal (below the USDA published-safe 145°F for fish) Pulling the fillet at just-set keeps the lean flake moist rather than chalky, but 52 to 55°C is a texture-driven target below the USDA published-safe 145°F (63°C) for fin fish. It depends on fresh, well-handled fish from a trusted counter. Cooks who are pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, or elderly, or anyone working with fish of uncertain freshness, should cook to an opaque, fully flaking 63°C / 145°F at the thickest point. Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein fish→with another firm skin-on fillet

    Swap the black sea bass for skin-on branzino, snapper, striped bass, or rockfish at the same ~170 g per-fillet size. The crisp-skin method is identical; thinner branzino fillets need 30 to 60 seconds less skin-render time, and firmer striped bass or rockfish tolerate a slightly longer sear. Avoid soft, thin fish like sole or tilapia whose flesh collapses before the skin crisps.

  • Regional sauce→beurre rouge

    Build the reduction on a dry red wine and red wine vinegar in place of the white, reducing the same way and mounting with the same 110 g cold butter. The sauce turns a deep rose-violet with a more tannic, fruit-forward edge; it suits the fish in autumn and pairs well with a glass of the same red.

  • Texture aromatics→with tarragon and a herb-forward finish

    Add 1 g / 1 tsp chopped fresh tarragon to the reduction as it cools and finish the mounted sauce with tarragon alongside the chives, nudging the dish toward a béarnaise-adjacent register. The anise note of tarragon is the classic French partner to white fish and lifts the butter without competing with the lemon.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dry white wine → unoaked Chardonnay, Pinot Grigio, or dry vermouth Any crisp, dry, unoaked white works; dry vermouth is an excellent and longer-keeping substitute since it is already aromatized and acidic. Avoid sweet wines (Riesling, Moscato) and oaked wines, which throw the reduction out of balance.
  • white wine vinegar → champagne vinegar or lemon juice Champagne vinegar is the closest match, slightly softer. In a pinch, an extra 15 g / 1 tbsp lemon juice carries the acid, though the sauce reads brighter and less rounded without the vinegar depth.
  • heavy cream → omit entirely The cream is an optional insurance policy that stabilizes the emulsion. A classic beurre blanc has none; omit it for a purer, more delicate sauce, but mount the butter more carefully since the margin for breaking is narrower.
  • chives → fresh parsley, chervil, or tarragon Any soft fresh herb works as the green finish; flat-leaf parsley is the neutral default, chervil the most classic, and tarragon nudges the dish toward béarnaise. Add at the end so the heat does not dull the color.

Notes

Why dry skin is the whole game. Fish skin crisps the same way pork crackling does: the water bound in and just beneath the skin must flash off before the collagen can set into a rigid, glassy sheet. Any surface moisture at the moment the fillet hits the pan goes to steam instead, and steam holds the skin temperature near 100°C, far below the Maillard and dehydration regime that produces crispness, so the skin turns leathery and pale. Patting dry, air-drying uncovered in the fridge, salting at the last second, and pressing the fillet flat for the first 30 seconds all serve the single goal of getting and keeping the skin in dry, full, flat contact with the hot pan. Score the skin shallowly so it does not contract and curl, but not so deep that you cut into the flesh and let its moisture weep up through the cuts.

Why press the fillet flat. A skin-on fillet curls upward the instant it hits the pan because the skin side, dense with collagen, contracts as it heats faster than the flesh does. A curled fillet renders only on its high points and stays flabby in the dimples. Thirty seconds of firm, even pressure with a fish spatula or a flat lid holds the whole skin in contact until enough of it has set rigid to stay flat on its own. After that the press comes off and the fillet renders undisturbed.

The doneness logic. Black sea bass is a lean, firm, structured-flake fish: its muscle proteins set from translucent to opaque across roughly 50 to 60°C, and above about 65°C the lean flesh expels its water and turns chalky, the failure mode of all overcooked lean fish. Nearly all the cooking happens on the skin side, slowly and from below, so the flesh climbs gently while the skin crisps; the brief flesh-side flip is only to set the surface. Pull at 52 to 55°C at the thickest point for a moist, just-set, large-flake texture, and read the visible opacity line creeping up the sides of the fillet as your gauge if you are not using a thermometer. See the safety note for the published-safe 145°C / 63°C alternative.

Why beurre blanc is built off heat. Beurre blanc is an emulsion of melted butterfat suspended in the watery wine reduction, held together by the milk-solid emulsifiers in butter (and, here, the casein and lecithin from the optional cream) and stabilized by the acid of the wine and vinegar. The emulsion is a narrow-temperature phenomenon: the butter must stay just at the edge of melting, warm enough to incorporate but never hot enough to boil. Above roughly 58°C the proteins that hold the fat in suspension fail and the sauce breaks into a greasy puddle of split butterfat and thin liquid. Mounting cold butter, a cube or two at a time, off direct heat, keeps the whole mass in that window: each cold addition pulls the temperature back down as it melts in. A reduction that is barely warm, not hot, at the start of the mount is the single most important variable. The acid is not just flavor; the low pH of the concentrated wine and vinegar is part of what keeps the emulsion stable, which is why a beurre blanc reduction is built sharp.

The flavor architecture. The umami is the seared fond on the skin (the Maillard browning of the skin proteins) and the butterfat itself, which carries the dish's richness. The finishing acid runs in two registers: the concentrated wine-and-vinegar reduction inside the sauce gives a rounded, deep sourness, and the lemon juice stirred in at the very end gives a brighter, more volatile top note that lifts the butter and keeps the sauce from reading flat or greasy. Taste the finished sauce and correct the balance: if it reads heavy, more lemon; if it reads thin or harsh, a touch more salt and a final cube of butter to round it.

Why black sea bass for this preparation. Black sea bass earns this method on two counts. Its skin is thick and collagen-dense and renders to a hard, shatter-crisp sheet under direct heat rather than tearing or going gummy the way a thinner-skinned fish does, and its lean, firm, structured flake holds its shape through the high-heat sear without collapsing, so the fillet stays intact through the flip and the transfer to the plate. A soft fish like sole or tilapia would fall apart and never crisp; black sea bass, branzino, snapper, and striped bass are the right structural class.
