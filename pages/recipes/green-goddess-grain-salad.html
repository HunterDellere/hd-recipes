Recipe

Green goddess grain salad

Cooked farro tossed cold with cucumber, scallion, and a creamy yogurt-avocado green goddess dressing that goes into the blender with a large fistful of soft herbs (parsley, basil, dill, chives) and a small punch of anchovy and lemon. The dressing is the entire dish: the herbs blend into a smooth, bright-green emulsion, the avocado contributes body without dairy heaviness, and the anchovy provides the structural glutamate that makes the salad read like a salad rather than a yogurt-coated grain pile. No-cook for the dressing, cold-assembled, batch-cookable for lunches.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Cook the farro first, it takes 20 to 25 minutes and needs to cool completely before the salad assembles. Drain it well and spread on a sheet pan to cool quickly; warm grain wilts the herbs and turns the bright-green dressing olive-brown.
  • The dressing must be blended just before serving for the brightest color. The chlorophyll oxidizes within 2 hours of blending and the vivid green turns dull. If batch-cooking, hold the dressing components separately and blend each morning.
  • All herbs need to be washed, dried, and at room temperature before blending. Wet herbs water down the dressing, cold herbs slow the blender and produce a coarser texture.
  • The anchovy is structural, not optional, for the umami foundation that elevates this from "herb yogurt" to "green goddess." Use a single anchovy fillet; it disappears completely into the dressing but contributes the glutamate that the herbs alone cannot.

Phase 1, the farro base

  1. 200 g pearled farro
  2. 1500 ml water
  3. 12 g kosher salt (for the cooking water)

Phase 2, the green goddess dressing

  1. 150 g whole-milk Greek yogurt
  2. 120 g ripe avocado, flesh only, pitted, scooped out of the skin; the flesh should yield to gentle pressure — (½ large or 1 small Hass; the avocado adds body without dairy heaviness and gives the dressing its distinctive smoothness)
  3. 25 g flat-leaf parsley, leaves and tender stems
  4. 10 g fresh basil leaves
  5. 8 g fresh dill, fronds only
  6. 10 g fresh chives, cut into 2 cm pieces; chives are easier to blend in pieces than whole
  7. 5 g anchovy fillet (oil-packed, salted) — (1 fillet; the structural umami, disappears into the dressing but is non-negotiable)
  8. 30 g lemon juice, fresh
  9. 4 g garlic clove, microplaned — (1 small clove; raw, microplaned to dissolve into the dressing)
  10. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  11. 3 g fine sea salt
  12. 30 g cold water (to thin to ribbon consistency)

Phase 3, the salad assembly

  1. 300 g English cucumber, diced, cut into 1 cm dice; English cucumber has fewer seeds and a thinner skin so it does not need peeling — (1 large; the structural crunch and cooling water content)
  2. 40 g scallions, thinly sliced on the bias — (4 scallions; the whole stalk)
  3. 100 g fresh peas (or frozen, defrosted)
  4. 15 g flat-leaf parsley, roughly chopped, for serving
  5. 80 g crumbled feta, crumbled by hand into rough 1 cm pieces

Execution

  1. 1
    Cook the farro. Bring 1500 ml / 6 cups of water to a boil with the 12 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt. Add the 200 g / 1 cup pearled farro. Simmer uncovered 20 to 25 minutes until tender with a discernible bite. Drain in a sieve and spread on a sheet pan to cool completely (8 to 10 minutes), or refrigerate to speed cooling.
  2. 2
    While the farro cooks, prep the salad vegetables. Cut the 300 g cucumber into 1 cm dice. Slice the 40 g scallions thinly on the bias. Measure out the 100 g peas. Wash and dry all herbs (for both the dressing and the garnish).
  3. 3
    Build the dressing. In a high-speed blender, combine the 150 g / ⅔ cup Greek yogurt, the 120 g / ½ avocado flesh, the 25 g parsley, the 10 g basil, the 8 g dill, the 10 g chopped chives, the 5 g / 1 anchovy fillet, the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, the 4 g / 1 clove microplaned garlic, the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil, and the 3 g / ¾ tsp fine sea salt. Blend on high for 45 to 60 seconds, scraping down the sides once, until completely smooth and vivid green. With the blender running, stream in the 30 g / 2 tbsp cold water until the dressing reaches ribbon consistency (it should pour slowly off a spoon).
  4. 4
    Taste the dressing. It should read herbaceous-forward, lemon-bright, fully salted, with a savory background you cannot quite identify (that is the anchovy). Adjust: more lemon if it reads flat, more salt if it reads vegetal, another 15 g / 1 tbsp water if it reads too thick to pour.
  5. 5
    Assemble. In a wide bowl, toss the cooled cooked farro (about 500 g / 3 cups cooked) with the 300 g diced cucumber, the 40 g sliced scallions, and the 100 g peas. Pour about half of the dressing (about 175 g / ¾ cup) over the salad and toss until every grain is coated. Add more dressing to taste; the salad should look generously dressed without pooling at the bottom of the bowl.
  6. 6
    Plate and finish. Transfer to a wide serving bowl or divide across four lunch bowls. Scatter the 80 g crumbled feta and the 15 g chopped parsley across the top. Serve with the extra dressing on the side for individual drizzling. Eats cold or at cool room temperature.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→with shredded poached chicken or canned chickpeas

    Shred 250 g cold poached chicken thigh into the salad; or add 250 g drained chickpeas (rinsed). The salad becomes a complete protein-and-grain lunch. The dressing already contains enough body to coat the additional component without watering down.

  • Texture grain→with barley, quinoa, or freekeh

    Barley cooks like farro (25 to 30 minutes pasta-style). Quinoa cooks absorption-style in 15 minutes (200 g grain + 360 g water + 4 g salt). Freekeh is a cracked-wheat alternative that cooks in 20 minutes and reads slightly smoky. All substitute 1:1 by dry weight.

  • Occasion cuisine direction→classic green goddess on butter lettuce

    Skip the grain base. Drop the dressing onto a head of butter lettuce broken into leaves (about 200 g) with the cucumber and herbs on top. The classical service of the original Palace Hotel dressing; lighter, more of a side salad than a meal.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole-milk Greek yogurt → labneh, sour cream, or mayonnaise Labneh is the closest analogue (thicker and tangier). Sour cream produces a slightly looser dressing with a milder tang. Mayonnaise (which is the original 1923 base) makes a richer, less tangy dressing; use 100 g mayo + 50 g yogurt for a hybrid that reads classical with modern lightness.
  • ripe avocado, flesh only → extra Greek yogurt (substitute 100 g additional yogurt for 120 g avocado) The avocado is doing structural body work, not flavor work. Extra yogurt substitutes structurally but the dressing reads thinner and tangier. Worth keeping the avocado in if at all possible.
  • anchovy fillet → 5 g / 1 tsp white miso or 5 g / 1 tsp soy sauce For a vegetarian or vegan version, white miso or soy sauce substitute the structural glutamate. Both work; miso is closer to the disappearing-into-the-dressing texture, soy sauce is more aggressive.
  • fresh peas → edamame, blanched fava beans, or extra cucumber Edamame substitutes 1:1 and reads similar. Blanched favas are the spring upgrade. Extra cucumber works if peas are unavailable but the salad loses some of its sweet-green range.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → whole-milk Greek yogurt make it yourselfstub Homemade strained yogurt is fresher-tasting and thicker than commercial Greek yogurt, which makes the green goddess dressing read more pronounced.

Notes

Why blend instead of finely chop. The original 1923 green goddess used finely minced herbs because there were no blenders. The modern blender takes the dressing into a different texture register: the herbs liquefy into the dressing, the chlorophyll fully releases, and the resulting emulsion is smooth and aggressively green rather than herb-flecked. Both versions are good; this recipe is designed for the blended texture, which holds the avocado-yogurt body together better than a chopped version would.

Why the anchovy matters and why one is enough. The anchovy is doing structural glutamate work, not anchovy-flavor work. A single 5 g fillet contributes about 50 mg of free glutamate (the savory-amino-acid that the human palate registers as umami). The herbs alone, even in generous quantity, do not produce that savory anchor; the dressing reads as "herb yogurt" rather than "green goddess." Add the anchovy and the dressing pulls itself together; without it, the dressing reads ingredient-list rather than dish.

The avocado is structural, not just rich. The yogurt alone gives a tangy dressing with no body. The mayonnaise of the original 1923 recipe gave body through emulsified egg yolk and oil. The avocado here gives body through plant lipid and starch, with the bonus that it stays bright green (the avocado''s natural color reinforces the herb color) and contributes monounsaturated fat that reads as creamy without dairy heaviness. Skipping the avocado and using only extra yogurt drops the dressing into thin-yogurt territory.

The chlorophyll oxidation window. Fresh-blended green goddess is at peak color for about 90 minutes. After that, the chlorophyll oxidizes (especially in the presence of the acid from lemon juice) and the color shifts from vivid green to dull olive-green to brown over 24 hours. The dressing is still edible at 24 hours but loses its visual punch. For best result, blend just before serving. For batch-cooking, hold the dressing components in separate refrigerated containers (yogurt-avocado-anchovy-garlic in one, fresh herbs picked but whole in another, lemon juice and oil in a third) and blend each morning in 60 seconds.

Why farro over rice. Farro is a heartier whole grain than rice and holds up better to the cold-tossed treatment without going gummy. Cooled rice in salads tends to clump and the grains lose their distinct bite; farro stays toothsome and absorbs the dressing without losing structure. The grain choice signals the salad''s register: with farro, it reads as a substantial cold lunch; with rice, it would read closer to a sushi-bowl format.

Make-ahead and storage. The cooked farro keeps 4 days refrigerated. The chopped vegetables (cucumber, scallion) keep 2 days; chop fresh each day for the best texture. The dressing keeps 3 days refrigerated but loses color quality after 24 hours; the assembled salad keeps 1 day refrigerated but is best fresh. The format is calibrated for Sunday-batch-cook-then-blend-each-morning, where the farro, vegetables, and dressing components are prepped on Sunday and each morning takes 90 seconds (blend dressing, toss with portioned salad, scatter feta).
