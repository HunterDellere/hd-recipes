Scallion-oil oyster mushrooms with white pepper

Oyster mushrooms torn and dry-seared until the water cooks off and the edges crisp, then dressed in a hot scallion-and-ginger oil with white pepper and a bright splash of rice vinegar. The dish runs pale, clean, and allium-forward while still carrying the mushrooms' own meaty glutamate.

Phase 2, dry-sear to render the water

  1. 5 g neutral oil

  1. 42 g neutral oil

Phase 4, season and acid finish

  1. 10 g rice vinegar
  2. 1 g toasted sesame oil

Execution

  1. 1
    Tear the 450 g / 1 lb oyster mushrooms along their grain into strips a finger wide; the torn, feathered edges crisp and hold dressing where a clean cut stays slick. Do not soak them, since they drink water and you are about to drive water out; wipe grit with a damp cloth.
  2. 2
    Slice the 60 g / 4 scallions, keeping a generous pile of the whites and pale greens in fine rings for the oil and reserving the dark greens for the finish. Have the 12 g / 1 tbsp julienned ginger ready. Mix the seasoning in a small cup: the 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy sauce, the 2 g / ½ tsp granulated sugar, the 0.5 g / ¼ tsp white pepper, and the 15 g / 1 tbsp water.
  3. 3
    Heat a wok or heavy skillet over medium-high with no oil to around 205°C / 400°F. Add the mushrooms in a single layer and let them sit; within a minute they weep water and the pan goes loud and steamy. Oyster mushrooms are roughly 90 percent water, and that moisture has to boil off before browning can start, because a wet surface stalls near 100°C / 212°F and only steams. Press them flat and toss every minute, cooking 5 to 7 minutes until they shrink by half, the pan dries, and the edges crisp and gold. Add the 5 g / 1 tsp neutral oil for the last minute to set the crisp, then transfer the mushrooms to a heatproof bowl with the scallion whites and ginger piled on top. High-heat dry roasting
  4. 4
    Wipe the pan and heat the 42 g / 3 tbsp neutral oil until it just begins to shimmer and wisp, around 190°C / 375°F. Pour it directly over the raw scallion and ginger on the mushrooms; it should sizzle hard. This hot-oil pour is the cong you move: it flash-cooks the alliums for a few seconds, pulling their fat-soluble aromatics into the oil while keeping the raw bite and color that a slow saute would cook away. Hot-oil finish (smoking-oil bloom over raw aromatics)
  5. 5
    Pour the soy-and-white-pepper mix over while everything is still hot and toss to coat. Off the heat, add the 10 g / 2 tsp rice vinegar and toss once: the bright, clean acid lifts the scallion oil and keeps the dish from reading flat and oily. Taste for salt. Scatter the reserved scallion greens and the ¼ tsp / 1 g toasted sesame oil and serve warm or at room temperature.
  6. 6
    The Adjustment. If the mushrooms come out soggy rather than crisp, the pan was crowded or you rushed the dry-sear; the water has to fully boil off before any oil, so split into two batches. If the dish reads flat and oily, more rice vinegar by the ½ tsp; the clean acid is what makes the scallion oil sing. If it reads harsh or raw-hot, the white pepper is forward; a pinch of sugar rounds it. If the scallions stewed soft and dull instead of staying bright, the oil was not hot enough when you poured it; take it to a visible wisp next time.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion temperature→cold mushroom plate (ginger-scallion sauce)

    Chill the dry-seared mushrooms, then dress with a raw ginger-scallion relish (mince 20 g / 3 tbsp scallion and 12 g / 1 tbsp ginger, salt, and finish with the hot oil poured over) for a cold-dish version that holds and travels.

  • Heat heat→peppery register

    Double the white pepper to 1 g / ½ tsp and add it to the oil as you pour, for a sharper, more pungent dish closer to a Cantonese white-pepper stir-fry.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • oyster mushrooms → king oyster, torn or planked Meatier and denser, so give it a minute longer to render and crisp; the texture turns scallop-like.
  • rice vinegar → white wine vinegar cut with a pinch of sugar A touch sharper and less rounded; the sugar softens the edge toward rice vinegar's gentler acidity.
  • white pepper → none ideal Black pepper changes the flavor toward woody and muddies the pale look; if you must, use half as much and accept the darker speckle.

Notes

The hot-oil pour is lipid-phase aromatic extraction done in seconds. Scallions and ginger carry their character in fat-soluble and volatile compounds (the sulfur molecules and gingerols that read as sharp and warm) that dissolve far better into hot oil than into water and that scatter into aroma the moment they are freed. Pouring oil at around 190°C / 375°F over the raw alliums spikes their surface temperature instantly: enough to rupture the cells and pull those compounds into the fat, but too brief to run the Maillard browning or the slow sugar breakdown that a stovetop saute would. You get the perfumed, infused oil of a long cook with the bright color and clean bite of something nearly raw, which is the whole point of cong you and the reason it stays light on the plate.

The dry-sear is the other half. Oyster mushrooms are around 90 percent water, and that water has to leave before the surface can climb past the 100°C steaming ceiling into browning territory. A bare, hot pan with the mushrooms in a single layer drives the moisture off as steam; only once the pan goes quiet and dry do the torn edges crisp and gold. Adding oil too early traps a film of water against the surface and the mushrooms stew soft instead.
