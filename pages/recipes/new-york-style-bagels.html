Recipe

New York-style bagels

A stiff, low-hydration (about 57%) high-gluten dough built on barley malt for color and a faint sweetness, bulk-fermented briefly, then shaped, cold-retarded overnight in the refrigerator, boiled in malted water, and baked on a preheated stone. The defining sequence is boil-then-bake: a 30- to 45-second boil gelatinizes the surface starch into a sealed skin that sets the dense, chewy interior and the glossy, blistered crust, the two traits that separate a New York bagel from a soft sandwich roll. The overnight cold retard develops the lactic acidity and the toasty fermentation flavor that a same-day dough never reaches.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a two-day bake. Day 1 is the mix, a short bulk ferment (60 to 90 minutes), shaping into rings, and then an overnight cold retard in the refrigerator (12 to 18 hours). Day 2 is the boil and the bake. The cold retard is structural, not optional: it develops the flavor and firms the rings so they hold their shape in the boiling water instead of slumping.
  • Use high-gluten flour if you can get it (Sir Lancelot, King Arthur Sir Galahad, or any 13.5 to 14.5% protein flour). Bread flour at 12 to 12.5% protein works and is the listed substitution, but the chew is softer; the extra gluten is what gives a true bagel its dense, elastic pull. Do not use all-purpose; at 10 to 11% protein the ring will be bready and slack.
  • Preheat the baking stone for a full hour before baking on day 2. A bagel needs a hot, retained-heat surface to set the bottom crust fast; a cold stone or a bare sheet steams the base and you lose the snap. Start the oven and stone heating before you bring the water to a boil.
  • Stage the boil before you shape the kettle station: a wide pot or deep skillet of water at least 10 cm deep, the barley malt and baking soda measured out, a slotted spoon or spider, and a wire rack or a parchment-lined tray ready to receive the boiled rings. Once the first batch goes in the water you are committed to a continuous boil-then-bake rhythm.

Execution

  1. 1
    Day 1, mix the dough. In a stand-mixer bowl, dissolve the 18 g / 1 tbsp barley malt syrup and the 4 g / 1¼ tsp instant yeast into the 285 g / 1¼ cups minus 1 tbsp lukewarm water. Add the 500 g / 4 cups high-gluten flour and the 10 g / 1¾ tsp fine sea salt. Mix on low with the dough hook until no dry flour remains, about 2 minutes. The dough will look shaggy and tight; at 57% hydration it is meant to be much stiffer than a normal bread dough.
  2. 2
    Knead to full gluten development. Run the hook on low to medium-low for 8 to 10 minutes. A stiff high-gluten dough resists the machine and may climb the hook; stop and push it down as needed. The dough is ready when it pulls fully off the bowl into a smooth, dense, slightly tacky ball that passes a windowpane test (stretch a walnut-sized piece thin enough to see light through it without tearing). Because the dough is so stiff, this takes longer than a soft dough; if kneading by hand, plan on 12 to 15 minutes of hard work.
  3. 3
    Bulk ferment, short. Round the dough into a ball, place it in a lightly oiled bowl, and cover with a damp towel. Let it ferment at room temperature for 60 to 90 minutes, until it has risen by roughly half (not doubled). The bulk is deliberately short because the long flavor development happens overnight in the cold; a full room-temperature proof now would overshoot and leave the rings slack and gassy in the kettle.
  4. 4
    Divide and pre-round. Turn the dough out and divide it into 8 equal pieces of about 113 g / 4 oz each (weigh them; uneven pieces bake unevenly). Round each piece into a tight ball under a cupped palm against the counter, building surface tension. Let the balls rest, covered with a damp towel, for 10 minutes so the gluten relaxes enough to shape without snapping back.
  5. 5
    Shape the rings (the poke-and-stretch method). Press your thumb through the center of a ball to make a hole, then hook two fingers through and rotate the dough, stretching the hole to about 4 to 5 cm wide. New York rings are shaped with a generous hole because it shrinks during the boil and bake; a small hole closes into a roll. Alternatively, roll each ball into a 20 cm rope and wrap it around your knuckles, sealing the overlap by rolling the seam against the counter. Place shaped rings on a parchment-lined tray dusted lightly with flour or semolina.
  6. 6
    Overnight cold retard. Cover the tray of shaped rings (an inverted tray or a damp towel under a shower cap works well) and refrigerate for 12 to 18 hours. This is where the flavor lives: the cold slows the yeast to a crawl while enzymes and bacteria build the lactic and acetic acidity that gives a bagel its tang and lets the crust blister. The rings firm up in the cold, which is exactly what you want for a clean boil. They should look slightly puffed but not doubled when you pull them out.15 h
  7. 7
    Day 2, preheat and test the float. At least one hour before baking, set a baking stone on a middle rack and heat the oven to 245°C / 475°F. Pull the bagels from the fridge and let them sit while the oven heats. To check that they are proofed enough to boil, drop one ring into a bowl of cool water: it should float within 10 seconds. If it sinks, the dough is underproofed; let the rings rest at room temperature for 20 to 30 minutes and test again. (Return the test bagel to the tray; it boils fine.)
  8. 8
    Bring the kettle to a boil and malt it. In a wide pot or deep skillet, bring the 3000 ml / 12 cups water to a rolling boil. Stir in the 40 g / 2 tbsp barley malt syrup and the 8 g / 1½ tsp baking soda. The malt darkens and glosses the crust; the baking soda raises the water pH toward alkaline, which accelerates Maillard browning and gives the surface a faint, clean pretzel note. Keep the water at a steady hard simmer to a low boil.
  9. 9
    Boil the bagels, the step that makes the bagel. Lower 3 or 4 rings into the water without crowding them. Boil 30 seconds, flip with a slotted spoon or spider, and boil 30 seconds more. The boil gelatinizes the surface starch into a sealed, glossy skin: this pre-cooked layer is what locks in the dense chewy interior and gives the baked crust its shine and snap. A longer boil (45 to 60 seconds per side) yields a thicker, chewier skin; a shorter boil reads softer and more bread-like. Lift each ring out, let it drain for a second, and set it on a wire rack or parchment.
  10. 10
    Wash and top. While the rings are still tacky from the boil, brush the tops with the 30 g / 1 egg white beaten with water. Immediately press the wet tops into a bowl of the 30 g / 3 tbsp sesame seeds, the 30 g / 3 tbsp poppy seeds, an everything blend, or the 6 g / 1 tsp coarse salt, or leave them plain. The boil-moistened surface and the egg wash both help the seeds adhere; topping after the boil rather than before keeps the seeds from washing off in the kettle.
  11. 11
    Bake on the stone. Transfer the boiled, topped bagels directly onto the preheated stone (slide them off a peel or the parchment). Bake at 245°C / 475°F for 18 to 22 minutes, rotating the tray or peel position halfway, until the crust is deep golden-brown and glossy and the bottoms are firm and browned. Pull a sample and tap it: the crust should sound hard and the interior should read about 96 to 99°C / 205 to 210°F on an instant-read. Underbaked bagels read pale and gummy; push the color further than you would a sandwich roll.
  12. 12
    Cool, then eat. Cool the bagels on a rack for at least 20 minutes before slicing. The crumb is still setting straight out of the oven; cutting too early drags the dense interior into a gummy paste against the knife. Eat the same day for the best crust, or freeze sliced bagels in a sealed bag and toast from frozen, which restores most of the fresh-baked crust snap.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional everything bagels→everything-bagel topping

    Combine equal parts sesame seeds, poppy seeds, dried minced garlic, dried minced onion, and coarse salt (about 1 tbsp / 8 g each for the full batch). Toast the garlic and onion lightly first so they do not scorch black in the 245°C oven. Press the boiled, egg-washed tops into the blend so it sticks in a thick crust.

  • Regional whole-grain depth→pumpernickel or whole-wheat bagels

    Replace 100 to 150 g of the high-gluten flour with dark rye or whole-wheat flour and add 10 g / 2 tsp more water to compensate for the bran absorption. The dough ferments faster and the crumb is denser and earthier; keep the boil to 30 seconds a side so the heavier dough does not turn leaden.

  • Regional sweeter Montreal-style direction→Montreal-style bagels

    Add 25 g / 2 tbsp honey and 1 egg to the dough, drop the salt to 6 g / 1 tsp, and boil in honey-sweetened water (40 g / 2 tbsp honey in place of the kettle malt) for a full 60 seconds a side. The result is smaller, denser, sweeter, and chewier than New York style, with a thinner crust; finish in a very hot oven, ideally with wood-fired smoke if you have it.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • high-gluten flour → bread flour Bread flour at 12 to 12.5% protein is the everyday substitution and bakes a good bagel; the chew is softer and less elastic than true high-gluten flour. To push it closer, add 15 g / 2 tbsp vital wheat gluten per 500 g flour, which raises the effective protein into bagel range. Do not use all-purpose; the ring will read bready, not chewy.
  • barley malt syrup → diastatic malt powder, or honey plus a pinch of malt powder Diastatic malt powder (1 tsp / 4 g in the dough) gives the same enzyme-driven browning and a cleaner mix. Honey or light brown sugar can stand in for sweetness and color (use 18 g in the dough, 40 g in the kettle), but they lack the toasted-grain malt flavor and the diastatic enzymes that feed the yeast, so the crust browns slightly less and the crumb is a touch less complex.
  • baking soda in the kettle → baked baking soda (sodium carbonate), or omit Baking the soda on a tray at 120°C / 250°F for an hour converts it to sodium carbonate, a stronger alkali that pushes the crust toward a true pretzel sheen and snap; use the same 8 g. Omitting the soda entirely still makes a fine bagel, just paler and less burnished.

Notes

Why low hydration and high gluten. A New York bagel is the densest member of the lean-bread family by design. At 57% hydration the dough is stiff enough to resist your hands, and the high-gluten flour (13.5 to 14.5% protein) builds a tight, elastic network that the stiff hydration keeps from opening into an airy crumb. The combination is the entire texture: a chewy, close, slightly resistant interior with a real pull to it. Push the hydration to 65% or drop to bread or all-purpose flour and you slide toward a soft roll, which is exactly the failure mode of most supermarket "bagels."

Why the overnight cold retard is non-negotiable. The short room-temperature bulk does the structural rise; the long cold retard does the flavor and the handling. Twelve to eighteen hours at refrigerator temperature lets the slow enzymatic and bacterial activity build lactic and acetic acidity, which is the tang you taste, and the slow gas production conditions the gluten so the crust can blister rather than bake smooth. The cold also firms the rings, which is what lets them go straight into boiling water and hold their shape instead of slumping into doughnuts. A same-day bagel is flatter in flavor and harder to handle in the kettle.

Why boil before you bake. The boil is what makes a bagel a bagel rather than a baked ring of bread. Thirty to forty-five seconds in near-boiling water gelatinizes the starch in the outer few millimeters of the dough, cooking it into a sealed, glossy skin before the oven ever touches it. That pre-set skin does two things: it traps the interior so it bakes dense and chewy instead of expanding into an open loaf, and it gives the finished crust its characteristic shine and snap. Malt in the water deepens the color and adds gloss; the baking soda raises the pH so the surface browns faster and harder, the same chemistry that gives a pretzel its mahogany skin and faint mineral note. Boil longer for a thicker, chewier crust; boil shorter for a softer, more bread-like one.

Why bake on a stone. A preheated baking stone delivers a blast of stored heat to the bottom of the boiled ring the moment it lands, setting the base crust fast and driving the early oven spring before the wet boiled surface can steam soft. A bare sheet pan or a cold stone bakes a paler, flatter-bottomed bagel. If you do not have a stone, a heavy inverted sheet pan preheated for the full hour is the closest stand-in; a steel works even better than stone because it conducts heat faster.

The umami-and-acid frame. Barley malt is the savory-sweet anchor here: its maltose and the toasted-grain flavor from the kilned barley give the crust depth that plain sugar cannot, and the diastatic enzymes break starch into the sugars the yeast and the Maillard reaction both feed on. The finishing acid is built in rather than spooned on: the overnight cold ferment generates the lactic and acetic acids that give the crumb its tang, so a properly retarded bagel tastes balanced and faintly sour without any added acid. If you skip the retard, the bagel will taste flat and one-note no matter how well it boils and bakes.

Storage and the freezer. Bagels are best within hours of baking, when the boiled-and-baked crust is at its crispest. They stale fast at room temperature because the dense crumb retrogrades quickly; by day two a counter-stored bagel is noticeably tougher. The fix is the freezer: slice the cooled bagels, freeze them in a sealed bag, and toast straight from frozen. Toasting re-gelatinizes the surface starch and restores most of the fresh-baked crust snap, which is why freeze-then-toast beats refrigerator storage, where the crumb goes stale and rubbery without the crust ever crisping back.
