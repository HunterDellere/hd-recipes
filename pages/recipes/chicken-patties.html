Chicken patties (Pakistani puff-pastry hand pies)

Spiced chicken keema (ground chicken cooked with onion, ginger-garlic, garam masala) folded into store-bought puff pastry, sealed, egg-washed, and baked until shatteringly crisp. The Karachi bakery-stall standard, the structural item at every Pakistani tea-and-snack gathering. Two filling traditions cover most of the spectrum: keema (dry) and creamy (with bechamel).

Phase 4, pastry assembly

  1. 500 g puff pastry, frozen, all-butter (Pepperidge Farm, Dufour, or store brand)

  4. 2 g nigella seeds (kalonji, optional)

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the keema filling. Heat the 30 ml oil in a wide skillet over medium heat. Add the 3 g cumin seeds; bloom 30 seconds until fragrant. Add the 150 g chopped onion; stir-fry 6 to 8 minutes until deep golden-brown. The browning is structural to flavor depth. Maillard sear
  2. 2
    Add aromatics and brown the chicken. Stir in the 25 g ginger-garlic paste and 8 g green chilies; stir-fry 60 seconds until the raw garlic note goes off. Add the 400 g ground chicken; break up with a spatula and stir-fry 5 to 6 minutes until the chicken is no longer pink, dry, and starting to brown at the edges. Use the spatula to break up any clumps; the keema texture is fine granular, like the pebbly texture of well-cooked taco meat.
  3. 3
    Add spices and tomato. Stir in the 6 g ground coriander, 4 g Kashmiri chili powder, 1 g turmeric, 6 g salt, and 2 g black pepper. Stir-fry 30 to 45 seconds until the spices coat the chicken and the kitchen fills with the aroma. Add the 100 g chopped tomato. Stir-fry 4 to 5 minutes; the tomato will release water, then break down, and the keema will become moist-cohesive (not wet, but not dry either). Pull when most of the visible liquid has cooked off. Red oil rendering
  4. 4
    Finish the filling. Stir in the 4 g garam masala, 15 g chopped cilantro, and 8 ml lemon juice. Stir 30 seconds off heat. Taste; adjust salt. Spread the keema on a wide plate to cool to room temperature, about 15 to 20 minutes. The filling must be cool before it goes into pastry; warm filling melts the butter in the puff pastry and produces wrong-textured patties.
  5. 5
    Roll out the pastry. Flour a clean countertop lightly. Unfold one sheet of thawed puff pastry (250 g; 1 sheet of the 500 g / 2-sheet package). Roll gently with a rolling pin to a 30 cm × 30 cm square (about 3 mm thick). Cut into 4 equal squares (each 15 cm × 15 cm). Repeat with the second 250 g sheet. You should have 8 squares total.
  6. 6
    Fill and seal the patties. Place 2 to 2½ tbsp (about 50 g) of cooled keema filling in the center of each square, leaving a 2 cm border around the filling. Brush the borders lightly with beaten egg wash. Fold each square diagonally to make a triangle, pressing the edges firmly to seal. Crimp the edges with a fork tine for a decorative seal that also reinforces the closure. Place on a parchment-lined baking sheet, leaving 3 cm between patties (they expand during baking).
  7. 7
    Egg wash and bake. Brush the tops of each patty with the beaten egg wash. Sprinkle with the 4 g / 2 tsp sesame seeds and 2 g / 1 tsp nigella seeds (if using). Bake in a preheated 200°C / 400°F oven for 22 to 25 minutes, until the patties are deeply golden-brown, the pastry is fully puffed, and the bottoms are crisp when lifted with a spatula. The internal temperature of the filling should reach at least 75°C / 165°F (the chicken was already cooked through in the keema phase, but the temperature check confirms food safety). Cool 5 minutes on the baking sheet, then transfer to a wire rack to finish cooling slightly.
  8. 8
    Serve. Serve warm or at room temperature. Patties are eaten by hand, often with a cup of strong masala chai or sweet milky tea. Tomato ketchup or a green chutney (mint-cilantro) is the typical accompaniment; some Pakistani households serve with sliced raw onion and a wedge of lemon. The patty should give a faint crunch on first bite, then yield to the soft spiced filling inside.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion filling style→creamy chicken (with bechamel)

    Skip the keema-style filling entirely. Make a bechamel: melt 30 g butter, whisk in 30 g flour, cook 1 minute, slowly whisk in 240 ml warm milk, cook 4 minutes until thick. Stir in 200 g shredded cooked chicken (rotisserie or boiled-and-shredded breast), 1 tsp ginger-garlic paste, ½ tsp Kashmiri chili powder, ½ tsp garam masala, 1 tbsp chopped cilantro, salt to taste. Cool completely before filling pastry. The dish becomes the creamy-chicken version popular at Pakistani-British bakery counters. Lightly less spicy, more mild-rich.

  • Occasion pastry shortcut→with crescent-roll dough

    Substitute Pillsbury crescent rolls or sheet dough. Use 2 cans (8 oz each). Unroll into rectangles, cut as written. The patty texture is denser and slightly more bread-like (not as flaky as puff pastry); acceptable when puff pastry is unavailable.

  • Protein protein→with ground beef or lamb (kheema patties)

    Substitute 400 g ground beef (80/20) or ground lamb. The fillings tradition is broader; "kheema patties" with ground beef or lamb is an established Pakistani variant. Same spice quantities; cook the meat 7 to 8 minutes for proper browning.

  • Occasion serving variant→mini patties for tea service

    Cut each puff pastry sheet into 16 squares (8 cm × 8 cm) instead of 4. Use 1 tbsp filling per mini patty. Bake 18 to 20 minutes. The dish becomes the small-bite version typical at Pakistani-Indian wedding teas and elaborate snack spreads. Yield: 32 mini patties.

  • Occasion cooking method→air fryer (faster)

    After assembling the patties, spray lightly with oil. Air fry at 190°C / 375°F for 12 to 14 minutes, flipping halfway. The pastry crisps faster than oven-baking and the result is dramatically less greasy than deep-fried versions. Common in modern Pakistani home cooking.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • frozen puff pastry → no good substitute Puff pastry is the structural component of these patties. If unavailable, switch to a different dish (samosas with spring-roll wrappers, or a savory tart) rather than substituting with non-laminated dough.
  • ground chicken (thigh) → ground chicken (breast) or ground turkey Acceptable but lean ground chicken or turkey produces drier filling. Add 1 tbsp neutral oil to compensate. The dish reads slightly less rich.
  • fresh ginger-garlic paste → jarred ginger-garlic paste Acceptable. Indian groceries carry jarred ginger-garlic paste (Patak's, Shan, Geeta's); the freshness is slightly less but the convenience is real. 1:1 substitute by weight.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → ginger-garlic paste make it yourselfstub Fresh-blended ginger-garlic paste is dramatically more aromatic than jarred. Five-minute project that lasts a week refrigerated.
  • → garam masala make it yourselfstub Fresh-toasted-and-ground garam masala is dramatically more aromatic than supermarket pre-ground; the volatile aromatics oxidize within months once ground.
  • → puff pastry make it yourselfstub Homemade rough puff pastry from cold butter cubes laminated through fold-and-rest cycles produces dramatically more dramatic puff and a fresher butter flavor than the frozen sheet. A weekend-baking project for the dedicated cook.

Notes

Why thigh meat for keema. Ground chicken thigh has more fat (around 8% vs 2% for breast) and significantly more flavor. The keema cooked with thigh is moist, savory, and self-bastes; the same recipe with ground breast produces dry, bland filling that needs additional fat to compensate. If only breast meat is available, add 30 g extra oil (or better, 30 g ghee) to the cooking; the dish reads slightly different but works.

The pastry-handling rule. Puff pastry is structurally fragile when warm: the butter layers melt at room temperature and the laminated structure collapses. Work cold: thaw pastry in the fridge overnight, work on a cool counter, fill and seal quickly, refrigerate the assembled patties 10 minutes before baking if the kitchen is warm. The result is patties that puff dramatically in the oven; pastry that has gone too warm produces patties that puff sluggishly and have visibly less layered structure.

Why the cool-the-filling rule is non-negotiable. Warm keema filling placed into puff pastry melts the butter layers from the inside, producing a soggy bottom and underwhelming puff. Cool the filling completely (room temperature, 20+ minutes on a wide plate) before filling the patties. If pressed for time, spread the keema thin on a baking sheet and refrigerate 10 minutes; this works to bring the temperature down quickly.

Eating mechanics. Patties are tea-snack food in Pakistani culture: served with strong masala chai, often as part of an afternoon spread that includes samosas, pakoras, mixed nuts, and biscuits. School lunchboxes; office tea breaks; family gatherings. The patties keep at room temperature 2 to 3 hours after baking; refrigerate any leftovers up to 3 days. Reheat in a 175°C / 350°F oven for 8 to 10 minutes (microwave reheating produces soggy pastry — avoid). Freeze unbaked assembled patties for up to 1 month; bake from frozen at 200°C / 400°F for 28 to 32 minutes.

Sources
  • Pakistani / North Indian bakery tradition — Chicken patties are a Karachi bakery and tea-stall signature, descended from British Empire-era hand pies but completely transformed by South Asian spice-and-aromatic traditions. The dish is a fixture at Pakistani family tea gatherings, school lunchboxes, and corner bakery counters. Two filling styles dominate: dry keema (spiced ground chicken) and creamy chicken (shredded with bechamel-style white sauce); both are valid and historically established.
