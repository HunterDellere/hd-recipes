Homemade mayonnaise

Egg yolk, neutral oil, Dijon mustard, lemon juice, and salt emulsified into a thick glossy sauce. The 90-second immersion-blender method (whole egg + oil + acid in a tall vessel, plunge to bottom, slow lift) is the modern home-cook revolution; the 5-minute classical hand-whisk method (yolk first, oil drop-by-drop, slow stream) is the technique-builder. Either way, fresh mayo is structurally distinct from commercial: thicker, more eggy, more aggressively lemon-mustard, with the pure neutral oil background carrying the egg flavor cleanly.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Every ingredient at room temperature. Cold eggs and cold oil refuse to emulsify; the lecithin in the egg yolk cannot bridge oil and water phases properly at low temperatures. If the eggs are coming from the refrigerator, set them on the counter 30 minutes before starting (or warm briefly in a bowl of warm tap water for 5 minutes).
  • Drizzle the oil slowly for the first 30 ml of the hand-whisk method. The emulsion has to form before the bulk of the oil goes in; trying to pour the oil too fast causes the emulsion to break, and breaking on the first pour means starting over. Once the first 30 ml is integrated, the remaining oil can be added in a steady stream.

Phase 1, the base (for both methods)

  1. 1 each large eggs (whole egg for immersion blender, yolks only for hand-whisk) — (1 whole egg for the immersion-blender method, OR 2 large egg yolks for the hand-whisk method; the whole egg has more water and protein, which adds emulsion stability for the immersion-blender method but produces a slightly thinner mayo)
  2. 7 g Dijon mustard
  3. 15 ml fresh lemon juice (or white wine vinegar)
  4. 3 g fine sea salt

Phase 2, the oil (the structural body)

  1. 250 ml neutral oil (avocado, grapeseed, or refined sunflower)

Phase 3, optional flavoring

  1. 0.5 g fresh-ground black pepper

Execution

  1. 1
    Immersion blender method (90 seconds, the practical default). Combine the 1 whole egg, 7 g Dijon, 15 ml lemon juice, and 3 g fine sea salt in the bottom of a tall narrow vessel (a 2-cup measuring cup or the cup that came with the immersion blender). Pour the 250 ml neutral oil on top of the egg mixture; let everything settle for 10 seconds (the oil floats on top, the egg mixture sits on the bottom, this layering is structural).
  2. 2
    Plunge the immersion blender head all the way to the bottom of the vessel, fully in contact with the egg mixture. Turn on the blender at full speed; hold steady at the bottom for 10 to 15 seconds. The mayo will form at the base, climbing up the sides of the blender shaft as the emulsion builds. Slowly lift the blender as the emulsion incorporates the oil from above, taking 60 to 75 seconds total to lift the full length and fully incorporate all the oil. The mayo should be thick, glossy, pale yellow, and stand in soft peaks.
  3. 3
    Hand-whisk method (5 to 7 minutes, the classical French technique). Combine the 2 egg yolks, 7 g Dijon, 5 ml of the lemon juice (reserve the rest), and 3 g fine sea salt in a medium bowl. Set the bowl on a damp tea towel folded into a ring (the towel keeps the bowl from sliding while you whisk one-handed). Whisk briefly to combine the egg-yolk-mustard mixture into a uniform paste.
  4. 4
    Build the emulsion. Begin drizzling the 250 ml neutral oil drop-by-drop into the bowl with one hand while whisking vigorously with the other. The first 30 ml of oil is the structural critical phase; the emulsion must form here. Whisk constantly, adding oil in literal drops (not streams) until the bowl mixture has visibly thickened and lightened in color (about 2 minutes of slow drizzling). The emulsion has formed when the mixture goes from translucent to opaque and from runny to thick.
  5. 5
    Stream in the remaining oil. Once the emulsion is established (after the first 30 ml), the remaining oil can be added in a slow steady stream, still whisking constantly. Take 3 to 4 minutes to incorporate the full 250 ml; the mayo will become progressively thicker and lighter in color. Whisk in the remaining 10 ml of lemon juice at the end to brighten and thin slightly.
  6. 6
    Taste and adjust (both methods). The mayo should taste eggy, lemony, mustardy, salty, with a clean neutral oil background. If flat: add another 1 g of salt. If too thick: whisk in 5 to 10 ml of warm water to loosen. If too thin: add another half egg yolk while whisking. If broken (separated and runny): start a new yolk in a new bowl, whisk briefly, then drizzle the broken mayo into the new yolk while whisking, the new yolk pulls the broken emulsion back into a stable mayo (the broken-mayo rescue is structural and reliable).
  7. 7
    Rest and serve. Refrigerate at least 15 minutes before serving; the flavors integrate during the rest, the mustard mellows slightly, and the lemon backs off from being aggressive. Use within 3 days for peak quality, 5 days at the outer limit. The mayo will continue to thicken slightly as it chills.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→aioli (Provençal garlic mayo) or alioli (Catalan)

    Add 1 large clove (4 g) of finely grated garlic to the egg base before emulsifying. Replace half the neutral oil with extra-virgin olive oil. The result is aioli, structurally a different sauce despite the mechanical similarity; serve with grilled fish, roasted vegetables, and bread. The Catalan alioli traditionally uses only garlic and olive oil with no egg, pounded in a mortar; this version is the modern egg-enriched preparation.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→Japanese Kewpie-style mayo (the umami mayo)

    Replace the lemon juice with 15 ml of rice vinegar. Add 2 g of MSG (or 1 g of dashi powder) to the egg base. Add a pinch of white pepper. The result reads dramatically umami-forward and is the structural Japanese sandwich mayo; significantly different from the European preparation.

  • Occasion flavor→sriracha mayo, chipotle mayo, herb mayo

    After the mayo is fully emulsified, stir in 15 to 30 g of: sriracha (for sriracha mayo), pureed chipotle in adobo (for chipotle mayo), or finely chopped herbs (basil, dill, tarragon, chives) for herb mayo. The flavored mayos are the structural condiments for sandwiches and crudités.

  • Dietary dietary→vegan mayo (with aquafaba instead of egg)

    Replace the 1 egg with 60 ml of aquafaba (the liquid from a can of chickpeas). Use the immersion-blender method; the aquafaba contains enough plant proteins and starches to form a stable emulsion. The result reads recognizably like mayonnaise and is structurally vegan; common in plant-based cooking.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • neutral oil (avocado, grapeseed, or refined sunflower) → a 50/50 blend of neutral oil and extra-virgin olive oil Pure olive oil mayonnaise reads aggressive and bitter (the olive polyphenols dominate); the 50/50 blend is the traditional Spanish preparation and produces a more flavorful mayo with the olive character present but balanced. Acceptable variation.
  • fresh lemon juice → white wine vinegar or apple cider vinegar (use 12 ml instead of 15 ml lemon) Vinegar is the classical French acid choice; lemon juice is the everyday home choice. The dish reads slightly less bright with vinegar but is structurally fully recognizable.
  • Dijon mustard → whole-grain Dijon (moutarde à l'ancienne) Whole-grain Dijon adds visible mustard seeds to the finished mayo, structurally suitable for charcuterie and crudités. Smooth Dijon is the default for sandwich mayo; whole-grain is the structural alternative for visible texture.

Notes

The lecithin chemistry. Mayonnaise is an oil-in-water emulsion: tiny droplets of oil suspended in a water-based continuous phase (the egg yolk, the lemon juice, the salt). The oil and water cannot mix without an emulsifier; the emulsifier in mayonnaise is lecithin, a phospholipid found in egg yolk. Lecithin has a hydrophilic (water-loving) head and a hydrophobic (oil-loving) tail; the molecules arrange themselves at every oil-water interface with their tails into the oil and their heads into the water, geometrically bridging the two phases. The smaller the oil droplets (achieved by vigorous whisking or high-speed immersion blender), the more lecithin molecules at the interfaces, and the more stable the emulsion. A fully emulsified mayo can hold 4 to 5 times its weight in oil per egg yolk; the structural limit is the lecithin coverage capacity.

Why room-temperature ingredients. The lecithin molecule has limited mobility at low temperatures; cold lecithin cannot bridge the oil-water interfaces fast enough to form fine droplets, and the emulsion either fails to form or breaks within minutes of forming. Room-temperature egg yolk has lecithin in active fluid arrangement, ready to coat oil droplets immediately. The temperature-of-ingredients rule is structural, not optional.

The whole-egg vs egg-yolk choice. The immersion-blender method uses a whole egg because the egg white provides additional water phase and protein structure that stabilizes the high-speed emulsion. The hand-whisk method uses egg yolks only because the lower-speed whisking does not need the extra stabilization and the all-yolk version produces a richer, thicker mayo. Both are correct; choose by method, not by preference.

The salmonella discussion. Commercial American eggs have approximately 1 in 20,000 contamination with Salmonella Enteritidis (FDA estimate, 2010s data). For raw-egg preparations like mayonnaise, the risk is real but small. Mitigations: the acid (lemon juice or vinegar) creates an environment unfavorable to Salmonella growth; refrigeration below 4°C / 40°F prevents proliferation; consuming within 3 to 5 days stays within the safety window. For pregnant women, immunocompromised diners, or anyone risk-averse, use pasteurized eggs (sold in cartons at most US supermarkets, the eggs are heated to 60°C / 140°F for 3 minutes before packaging, which kills Salmonella without cooking the eggs). The risk of homemade mayo from commercial eggs is comparable to the risk of eating undercooked eggs in restaurant breakfast preparations.

The broken-mayo rescue. Mayonnaise that breaks (separates back into oil and a thin egg-water layer) can almost always be saved. The technique: start a new egg yolk (or 30 ml of warm water if no extra yolk is available) in a new clean bowl, whisk briefly, then slowly drizzle the broken mayo into the new base while whisking vigorously. The new lecithin grabs onto the broken oil droplets and pulls them back into a stable emulsion. The rescue produces a slightly thinner final mayo (because of the extra water from the new yolk or warm water) but is fully recoverable. The rescue is structural and reliable; do not throw away broken mayo, fix it.

Storage. Refrigerated in a sealed jar, 3 to 5 days at peak quality. Do not freeze; the emulsion breaks irreversibly on thaw. Do not leave at room temperature for more than 2 hours; Salmonella proliferates at room temperature within that window. The mayo will thicken slightly during chilling; if too thick to use, whisk in a tablespoon of warm water to loosen.

When homemade is structural vs when commercial is fine. Homemade mayo is dramatically better for finishing applications and aioli (where the mayo is the dish): crudités, grilled vegetables, fish, sandwich finishing. Commercial mayo (Hellmann''s, Duke''s, Kewpie) is structurally fine for sandwich binders, salad dressings (chicken salad, potato salad, tuna salad), and applications where the mayo is one of many flavors. Knowing when to make and when to buy is part of cooking efficiently; do not over-commit to homemade for applications where it makes no perceptible difference.

Sources
  • Classical French and Spanish tradition — Mayonnaise is a 19th-century French sauce, debated origin (Mahón in Menorca or Bayonne in southwest France). The classical preparation is hand-whisked, drop-by-drop addition of oil to egg yolk; the immersion-blender route is a 21st-century home innovation that produces a comparable emulsion in 2 percent of the time. Both methods are correct; the immersion-blender method is the practical default for everyday cooking, the hand-whisk method is the technique to know for understanding the chemistry.
