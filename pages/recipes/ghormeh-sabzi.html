Recipe

Ghormeh sabzi

The Persian herb stew: enormous quantities of parsley, cilantro, fenugreek leaves, and scallion sautéed in oil until the chlorophyll deepens to near-black, then braised with cubed lamb shoulder, soaked red kidney beans, deeply browned onions, turmeric, and whole pierced dried limes for 2 to 3 hours until the lamb collapses and the broth turns viscous and dark. The herb-darkening and the dried-lime sourness are the two structural signatures; the dish is the most iconic khoresh in the Persian canon and the national dish of Iran by popular vote.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Soak the red kidney beans overnight (or quick-soak: cover with water, bring to a boil, off heat 1 hour). Unsoaked beans never fully soften in the 2 to 3 hour braise and the stew reads chalky.
  • Wash the fresh herbs (1.5 kg total raw) in two changes of water, spin or pat very dry, and chop fine. Wet herbs steam instead of sautéeing in step 1 and the herb-darkening phase fails; the dish reads green and grassy instead of deep and complex.
  • Serve over chelow or tahdig (the canonical Persian pairing). Start the rice soak when you start the bean soak and the rice parboil 90 minutes before the stew finishes.
  • Dried Persian limes (limoo amani) are structural. They are sold whole, dried hard like wooden balls, in Middle Eastern groceries; pierce each one twice with a paring knife before adding so the interior fermentation can release. Fresh lime juice substitutes the citric acid but not the fermented depth.

  1. 500 g flat-leaf parsley, thick stems removed, washed, fully dried, finely chopped (about 4 large bunches)
  2. 400 g cilantro, thick stems removed, washed, fully dried, finely chopped (about 3 bunches)
  3. 250 g scallion (greens and pale parts), roots trimmed, washed, finely chopped (about 2 bunches) — (replaces leek tops, the more canonical Iranian choice when available)
  4. 120 g fresh fenugreek leaves (or 30 g dried fenugreek leaves / kasoori methi), leaves only, washed, fully dried, finely chopped — (1 small bunch fresh; the structural defining herb of ghormeh sabzi; if using dried, no chopping needed, add directly with the other herbs)

Phase 3, the braise base

  1. 60 g neutral oil
  2. 300 g yellow onion, small dice (about 5 mm), 2 medium onions — (structural for the deep-brown fond; do not skip the long brown)
  3. 800 g lamb shoulder, boneless, trimmed of excess fat, cut into 3 cm cubes
  4. 6 g ground turmeric
  5. 14 g kosher salt
  6. 2 g black pepper, freshly cracked
  7. 1200 ml water
  8. 15 g fresh lemon juice (or 1 extra dried lime), for finishing

Execution

  1. 1
    Drain the soaked 200 g kidney beans; place in a pot with 1 L fresh water, bring to a boil, reduce to a simmer, and cook 30 to 45 minutes until just tender (still holding shape, slight resistance at the center). Drain and reserve. This pre-cook is structural; raw beans added to the stew do not soften enough in the braise time.
  2. 2
    Heat 30 g / 2 tbsp of the neutral oil in a wide skillet over medium heat. Add all the chopped herbs (parsley, cilantro, scallion, fenugreek). The pan will look overstuffed; the herbs collapse dramatically as they cook. Sauté 25 to 35 minutes, stirring frequently and reducing heat slightly as the herbs darken, until the green color deepens from bright spring-green to deep forest-green to near-black, the volume reduces by about 70%, and the herbs smell deeply roasted with no raw grassy note. This long sauté is the structural defining technique of ghormeh sabzi; rushed herbs taste of raw chlorophyll and the stew never integrates.
  3. 3
    Meanwhile, in a heavy 5-quart Dutch oven, heat the remaining 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil over medium heat. Add the 300 g / 2 onions (diced) and cook 15 to 20 minutes, stirring frequently, until deep golden brown with crispy edges (much deeper than translucent; the structural fond color is dark amber-mahogany, not light blonde). Reduce heat if the onions threaten to burn.
  4. 4
    Add the 800 g lamb cubes to the browned onions; raise heat to medium-high. Brown the lamb on all sides, 6 to 8 minutes total. Add the 6 g / 1 tbsp turmeric, 8 g of the kosher salt (about 2 tsp), and 2 g / 1 tsp black pepper; stir 30 seconds to bloom the turmeric in the lamb fat. The pot bottom should be deeply browned with a layer of fond.
  5. 5
    Add the darkened herb mixture to the lamb pot; stir to combine. The herbs will smell intensely fragrant, almost meaty-vegetal at this point. Add the 1200 ml / 5 cups water; scrape the bottom to lift the fond. Add the pierced 12 g / 4 to 6 dried limes. Bring to a steady simmer, cover, and reduce heat to low.
  6. 6
    Simmer covered 90 minutes, stirring every 30 minutes. The stew should hold a tremor, not a rolling boil; aggressive boil emulsifies the lamb fat into a greasy slurry instead of letting it float on top.
  7. 7
    Add the cooked beans and the remaining 6 g salt (about 1½ tsp). Continue simmering covered 30 to 45 minutes more until the lamb is fork-tender (collapses under gentle pressure) and the stew has reduced and darkened, the surface oils separating slightly. The total braise time is structurally 2 to 2.5 hours; longer is fine and improves the dish.
  8. 8
    Taste. The stew should read deeply herbal, sour from the dried lime, savory from the long-browned onion and lamb, and faintly bitter from the fenugreek. If the sour reads soft, fish out one dried lime, crush against the side of the pot with a spoon to release more interior, and stir back in; or add the 15 g / 1 tbsp fresh lemon juice. If the salt reads low, add in 2 g increments. Pull the dried limes before serving (they soften to mush and read bitter if bitten into).
  9. 9
    Serve over chelow (plain steamed Persian rice) or alongside tahdig in a shallow bowl. The canonical service is the rice and the stew side-by-side, not mixed, so each bite can be composed by the diner.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein pivot→with beef chuck instead of lamb

    Substitute 800 g beef chuck cubes for the lamb. Extend the initial braise to 2 hours before adding beans. The dish reads slightly less complex (lamb has more aromatic fat) but is structurally correct; common in Iranian home cooking when lamb is unavailable or expensive.

  • Dietary vegetarian variant→as vegetarian ghormeh sabzi

    Skip the lamb entirely. Double the kidney beans to 400 g dried (cook fully before adding). Add 300 g cubed potato or 200 g chopped mushroom with the beans for body. Use 1 L vegetable stock in place of the water. The dish reads lighter and herb-forward; the dried-lime sour and the herb-darkening still carry the structural signature. Common in modern vegetarian-Persian cookery and acceptable in the tradition.

  • Regional fenugreek emphasis→as a fenugreek-forward ghormeh sabzi

    Double the fenugreek leaves (240 g fresh or 60 g dried kasoori methi). The dish reads more bitter and more aromatic; some Iranian families consider this the proper version, while others find it overpowering. Worth trying at least once to calibrate personal preference.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dried Persian limes (limoo amani) → 60 g fresh lime juice plus 1 strip of fresh lime zest, added with the lemon juice in step 8 A fallback only; the dried lime contributes a fermented-citrus complexity that fresh lime juice cannot replicate. If you can find dried limes (Middle Eastern groceries; sold in mesh bags), use them. The dried form is the structural difference between ghormeh sabzi and a generic lamb-and-herb stew.
  • fresh fenugreek leaves → 30 g dried fenugreek leaves (kasoori methi, from Indian groceries) Dried fenugreek leaves substitute well and are widely available in Indian groceries (sold as kasoori methi). Use about one-quarter the volume of fresh; add directly with the other herbs, no chopping needed. Fenugreek seeds do not substitute; they are a different spice with a different flavor profile.
  • flat-leaf parsley → a mix of flat-leaf parsley and dried parsley (one-third of the total parsley weight as dried) Some traditional ghormeh sabzi recipes use a portion of dried parsley alongside fresh for a more concentrated herb flavor. Acceptable; the herb-darkening step still works on the mixed fresh-and-dried.

Notes

The herb-darkening phase explained. The 25 to 35 minute sauté of fresh herbs in oil is not optional and not a step you can shorten. Three chemical changes occur during the darkening: (1) the chlorophyll in the herbs degrades from bright green to olive to near-black as the magnesium ion at its center is displaced by hydrogen under prolonged heat; (2) the green volatiles (cis-3-hexenal, the grassy compound that makes raw cilantro smell soapy to some palates) cook off and deeper roasted volatiles develop; (3) the cell walls break down and the cellular water evaporates, concentrating the herb flavor. The result is a herb base that tastes deep, savory, and almost meaty rather than fresh, grassy, and vegetal. Skip or shorten this step and the stew tastes of raw herbs; do it correctly and the herbs carry the dish.

Why the dried lime is structural. Limoo amani (Persian dried limes) are limes that are boiled briefly, then sun-dried for weeks until they are hard, hollow, and dark. The drying ferments the lime interior; the resulting flavor is sour, citrusy, but also fermented, slightly bitter, and umami-savory in a way that fresh lime juice cannot replicate. Pierced limes added whole to a stew slowly release this complex sourness over the long braise; the lime acts as both an acid and a fermented depth element. The dried lime is the second structural defining ingredient of ghormeh sabzi (after the darkened herbs); without it the dish is not the dish.

Versatility. The herb-darkening technique transfers to other Persian khoresh: khoresh-e karafs (celery and herb stew with mint), khoresh-e bademjan (eggplant and herb stew), khoresh-e baghali (fava and dill stew). The deep-brown onion technique is universal across the cuisine and the home-cook foundation of any khoresh.

Pairs across Persian (canonical), broader Iranian diaspora, Afghan (similar herb stews exist as qorma sabzi), and contemporary Mediterranean-leaning herb-forward cookery.

Storage. Ghormeh sabzi improves with a day in the refrigerator; the flavors integrate and the herbs deepen further. Keeps 5 days refrigerated and freezes 3 months. Reheat gently on the stovetop; do not boil aggressively (the lamb fat will emulsify and read greasy). Many Iranian cooks consider day-two ghormeh sabzi the structural ideal; make ahead when serving for guests.

Sources
  • The Iranian khoresh canon. Ghormeh sabzi (literally "stewed greens") is documented across Persian cookery for at least 500 years and is widely considered the national dish of Iran. — Two structural schools split on the herb mix: the canonical mix is parsley, cilantro, fenugreek leaves (shanbalileh), and scallion; some regional variants add dill, mint, leek tops, or chives. The dried-lime sourness is non-negotiable; the kidney bean is non-negotiable; the deep-brown onion fond is non-negotiable. Beyond those three structural anchors, the herb proportions vary by family. Fresh fenugreek leaves are the hardest to source outside Iran; dried fenugreek leaves (kasoori methi from Indian groceries) substitute well at one-quarter the volume.
