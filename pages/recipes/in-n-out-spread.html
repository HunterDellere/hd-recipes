In-N-Out spread (animal-style copycat)

The pink-orange spread that defines In-N-Out's animal-style burger and goes on every Double-Double by default. Mayo, ketchup, sweet pickle relish, sugar, and a touch of vinegar; distinct from Big Mac sauce in that it leans markedly sweeter, uses more relish, and skips the mustard entirely. Reads as a Thousand Island simplified to its load-bearing parts.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Whisk-and-rest, not whisk-and-serve. The spread needs at least 30 minutes refrigerated before service. The sugar takes time to fully dissolve into the cold emulsion, the pickle brine has to integrate with the ketchup, and freshly mixed spread tastes harshly sweet-on-top-of-mayo rather than as the unified Thousand-Island-like sauce the chain is calibrated for.
  • For the best read, make it the day before. Spread that has rested 12+ hours reads notably smoother and the pickle character migrates evenly through the matrix.

Sauce

  1. 120 g mayonnaise (full-fat, real-egg, ideally homemade)
  2. 40 g ketchup
  3. 40 g sweet pickle relish
  4. 8 g granulated sugar
  5. 5 ml distilled white vinegar
  6. 0.5 g fine sea salt — (pinch; adjust based on mayo and relish saltiness)

Execution

  1. 1
    In a small bowl combine the 120 g / ½ cup mayonnaise, 40 g / 2½ tbsp ketchup, 40 g / 2 tbsp sweet pickle relish, 8 g / 2 tsp sugar, 5 ml / 1 tsp white vinegar, and a 0.5 g pinch salt.
  2. 2
    Whisk vigorously for 30 seconds until completely smooth and uniformly pink-orange. The sugar should fully dissolve into the wet phase rather than show as crystals at the bottom of the bowl; if you feel grit on the whisk, keep going for another 30 seconds.
  3. 3
    Taste. Adjust by quarter-teaspoon increments: more sugar if it reads sharp or vinegar-forward, more relish if it reads under-pickled, more ketchup if it reads pale. The franchise calibration is markedly sweet with the pickle texture as the dominant mouthfeel and no mustard or paprika present; aim there.
  4. 4
    Cover and refrigerate at least 30 minutes before serving. The flavors integrate substantially with even a short rest, and the spread thickens slightly as the sugar fully dissolves and the relish brine migrates through the emulsion. Holds 1 week refrigerated in a sealed jar.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional flavor→Thousand Island variant

    Add 15 g / 1 tbsp finely minced shallot, 1 tsp Worcestershire, and a hard-boiled egg yolk pressed through a sieve. The sauce shifts toward the canonical Thousand Island used on Reuben sandwiches. No longer In-N-Out spread, but in the same family.

  • Heat heat→spicy spread

    Add ½ tsp sriracha and ¼ tsp cayenne. The base sweetness carries the heat well and produces a spread that pairs especially well with carne asada fries.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • sweet pickle relish → finely minced bread-and-butter pickles + 1 tsp of their brine Bread-and-butter pickles produce a slightly cleaner, less corn-syrup result than commercial relish. Use the same total mass.
  • ketchup → homemade ketchup Recommended. When the ketchup is a quarter of the spread, the upgrade is visible. See homemade alternatives below.
  • mayonnaise → Greek yogurt for a lighter version Replaces half the mayo (60 g yogurt + 60 g mayo). Loses some body but cuts fat substantially. The franchise version is full-mayo.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → mayonnaise (full-fat, real-egg, ideally homemade) make it yourself Fresh real-egg mayo is dramatically better than jarred; the spread reads less commercial when built on it.
  • → ketchup make it yourself Homemade ketchup made with canned tomatoes is meaningfully better than commercial; the spread takes on a deeper tomato character when the ketchup is real.

Notes

The In-N-Out spread is the simplest of the major fast-food special sauces. Five ingredients plus salt, no mustard, no paprika, no garlic or onion powder. The cleanness of the ingredient list is the point: it tastes like a Thousand Island stripped down to mayo, ketchup, pickle, and sugar. Most copycat recipes online add a small dose of mustard or paprika to "round it out"; the chain version doesn''t use them and tastes flatter and cleaner for it.

The sugar dose is higher than most home cooks expect. Two teaspoons in a ½-cup batch is the calibration that makes the spread read as the chain version rather than as a generic mayo-ketchup blend. If you find it cloying, drop to 5 g / 1¼ tsp, but understand you are no longer making In-N-Out spread; you are making your preferred lower-sugar version.

Relish quality matters. Heinz or Vlasic sweet relish are the closest commercial matches. Off-brand relishes vary in pickle chunk size; the chain spread has visible, distinct pickle pieces in the mouth, not a uniform purée. If your relish is unusually finely chopped, the texture will read wrong even if the flavor is right.
