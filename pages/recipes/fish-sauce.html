Fish sauce

Whole small fish, usually anchovies, layered with sea salt at roughly a 3-to-1 ratio and left to ferment and autolyze for six months to a year, until the fish dissolve into a clear amber liquid driven by their own enzymes and salt-tolerant bacteria. The first-press liquid drawn off the top is the prized nuoc mam nhi or nam pla; what remains can be re-extracted with brine for a second-grade sauce. The salt of an entire region of cooking, built from two ingredients and time.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This takes 6 to 12 months. There is no shortcut; the amino-acid breakdown that creates the flavor is slow even in a warm climate. Start it when you have the patience to leave it alone, and label the jar with the date.
  • The salt ratio is a safety control. Use roughly 3 parts fish to 1 part salt by weight (a salt concentration around 25%). This high-salt environment is what suppresses Clostridium botulinum and spoilage organisms while the salt-tolerant enzymes do their work. Do not reduce the salt to make it less salty; under-salting a fish ferment at ambient temperature is genuinely dangerous.
  • Use the freshest whole small fish you can get, gut intact. The fish's own digestive enzymes drive much of the autolysis, so whole un-gutted anchovies, sardines, or similar small oily fish are traditional and correct here. Freshness matters: start with fish that smells of clean sea, never anything already turning.
  • Ferment somewhere warm, smell-tolerant, and out of direct sun. A garage, a covered porch, or a warm pantry corner works. The ferment is pungent for months; this is not an indoor-kitchen project.

Mise en Place

  1. 500 g Kosher salt

  1. 250 ml filtered or non-chlorinated water optional
  2. 1500 g fresh whole anchovies (or sardines, smelt, or other small oily fish)

Execution

  1. 1
    Rinse the 1500 g / 3.3 lb fresh whole anchovies briefly in cold water to wash off grit, and drain well. Leave them whole and un-gutted; their digestive enzymes are part of what breaks the protein down. Sort out anything that is not fresh, the ferment is only as clean as the fish that start it.
  2. 2
    Layer fish and salt. In a clean non-reactive vessel (glass, food-grade ceramic crock, or food-grade plastic, never bare metal), lay down a base layer of the 500 g / 2 cups coarse sea salt, then alternate layers of anchovies and salt, ending with a generous salt cap on top. Aim for the full 3-to-1 fish-to-salt ratio distributed through the layers. The salt cap on top is a barrier against surface spoilage and mold.
  3. 3
    Weight and submerge. Place a clean plate or a food-grade follower on top of the fish and weight it down (a water-filled zip bag or a scrubbed stone works) so the fish stay pressed below the liquid that will draw out over the next days. If after 24 to 48 hours the salt-and-fish liquid (the brine the salt pulls from the fish) does not cover the solids, top up with the 250 ml / 1 cup light brine. Keeping everything submerged under brine is what keeps mold and air-loving spoilage organisms out.
  4. 4
    Cover and ferment warm. Cover the vessel with a breathable cloth secured with a band, or a loose lid that lets gas escape but keeps insects out. Set it in a warm spot (ideally 25 to 35 °C / 77 to 95 °F) out of direct sunlight. Leave it to ferment and autolyze for 6 to 12 months. Over this time the fish gradually dissolve and the liquid turns from cloudy gray to a clear, deep amber-to-mahogany. Stir or check monthly: the smell should evolve from raw-fishy to deeply savory and funky-umami; persistent ammonia reek, fuzzy mold on the surface above the brine, or any pink or black slime means something went wrong (usually under-salting or fish exposed above the brine), and the batch should be discarded.
  5. 5
    Draw the first press. When the liquid is clear amber and smells savory rather than raw (6 months at the earliest, often closer to a year), ladle or siphon the clear liquid off the top without disturbing the sediment. Filter it through several layers of cheesecloth, then through a coffee filter or fine paper for clarity. This first-press liquid is nuoc mam nhi, the highest grade, clear and intensely savory. Bottle it in clean glass.
  6. 6
    Optional second press, then store. For a second-grade sauce, cover the remaining solids with a fresh light brine (about 200 g salt per liter of water), steep two to four weeks, and draw and filter again; this thinner liquid is used for cooking rather than dipping. Store all finished fish sauce in sealed glass in a cool dark pantry. Taste the first press against a trusted commercial bottle to calibrate; a good homemade nuoc mam reads clean, deeply savory, and salty, with no harsh ammonia note.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture faster, hydrolyzed cheat→a quick enzyme-assisted version

    For a 1-to-2 month version, some makers add a spoonful of koji or a pinch of a protease enzyme to speed the autolysis, fermenting at a steady warm temperature. It accelerates the amino-acid breakdown but never fully matches the depth of a year-long traditional ferment. The salt ratio stays the same; the enzyme only speeds what salt and the fish's own enzymes do slowly.

  • Regional colatura-style finishing sauce→Italian colatura di alici

    The Italian colatura tradition (from Cetara) is the same first-press anchovy liquid, fermented a shorter time and used as a fine finishing sauce, especially on pasta. Ferment a smaller batch of premium anchovies, draw the first press at around 6 months, and treat it as a high-end finishing condiment by the teaspoon rather than a cooking workhorse.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • fresh whole anchovies → sardines, smelt, herring, or other small oily fish Any small, oily, whole fish works; anchovies are traditional and give the cleanest result. The fish should be small enough to break down fully and fresh enough to start clean. Larger or leaner fish ferment less reliably.
  • coarse sea salt → any non-iodized salt without anti-caking agents Use pure salt: iodized salt and anti-caking additives can off-flavor the ferment and interfere with the microbiology. Coarse sea salt is traditional; kosher salt works as long as it is additive-free. Do not reduce the quantity; the high salt level is the safety control.

Notes

Why this is a long project and what time actually does. The savory depth of fish sauce is free glutamate and other amino acids, released as the fish protein is digested by the fish''s own enzymes and salt-tolerant bacteria. That breakdown is slow even at warm fermentation temperatures, which is why traditional nuoc mam and nam pla ferment for the better part of a year. There is no genuine shortcut to the full flavor; enzyme- or koji-assisted versions speed it but plateau below the depth of a year-long ferment. For nearly every cook, a good two-ingredient commercial bottle (anchovy and salt only, such as Red Boat) is the right call, and this recipe exists to understand and control the process, not because the homemade version is strictly necessary.

The salt ratio is not negotiable and not a flavor choice. Roughly 3 parts fish to 1 part salt, a salt concentration around 25%, is high enough to suppress Clostridium botulinum and ordinary spoilage organisms while the salt-tolerant (halophilic) enzymes and bacteria do their slow work. This is the single most important number in the recipe. Cutting the salt to make a milder sauce removes the safety margin and turns an ambient-temperature fish ferment into a real hazard. The finished sauce is meant to be very salty; you dilute and balance it in the dish (with lime, sugar, and water in nuoc cham), not in the jar.

Keep everything under the brine, and read the smell. The two common failures are fish left exposed above the liquid (which molds or rots aerobically) and under-salting. Weight the solids down so the brine covers them, cap the top with salt, and top up with brine if the level drops. The smell is your monitor: it should travel from raw-fishy through pungent to deeply savory-funky-umami over the months. A clean funk is the goal. A sharp, persistent ammonia reek, surface mold above the brine, or any pink or black slime are signals to discard the batch rather than risk it.

First press versus second press. The clear amber liquid drawn off the top without disturbing the sediment is the first press, the highest grade (nuoc mam nhi), reserved for dipping sauces and finishing where its clean depth shows. Re-brining the spent solids yields a thinner, second-grade sauce fit for cooking. Commercial grading works the same way, which is why bottles vary so much in price and intensity; the first press of a long ferment is the premium product.
