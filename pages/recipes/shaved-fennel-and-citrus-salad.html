Recipe

Shaved fennel and citrus salad

Raw fennel shaved paper-thin on a mandoline and crisped in ice water, tossed with supremed orange and ruby grapefruit, brine-cured green olives, and a finishing-oil dressing built on the citrus juices, white-wine vinegar, and a slick of anchovy for glutamate depth. The Sicilian insalata di finocchio template: the dish is a study in the anise-bitter of raw fennel, the sweet-tart of two citrus, the salt-bitter of olives, and the cooling green of fennel fronds and mint, held in balance so no single axis dominates.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Shave the fennel first and drop it into ice water for 15 to 20 minutes while you supreme the citrus and build the dressing. The cold-water soak crisps the cut leaf bases (the cells reabsorb water and turn turgid) and mellows the raw anise edge; fennel shaved and dressed straight from the cutting board reads limp and aggressively licorice. Spin it bone-dry before dressing or the water dilutes the vinaigrette.
  • Supreme the citrus over a bowl and reserve every drop of juice that runs off. That juice is the acid base of the dressing; there is no separate citrus called for in the vinaigrette. Squeeze the spent membranes hard once the segments are out.
  • This is a no-cook salad but it is not a toss-and-serve. The fennel soak runs 15 to 20 minutes in parallel with the citrus and dressing work, so read to the end before you start cutting.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the fennel

  1. 600 g fennel bulbs, with fronds attached, 2 medium bulbs; fronds picked and reserved, stalks trimmed off and saved for stock, core wedge cut out, bulb halved through the root
  2. 1000 ml ice water

Phase 2, the citrus

  1. 360 g navel or blood oranges, supremed: top and bottom cut flat, peel and pith pared away following the curve, segments cut free from the membranes over a bowl — (2 large oranges; blood oranges if in season for the deeper color and berry note)
  2. 250 g ruby red grapefruit, supremed the same way as the oranges; the bitterer, more astringent citrus axis — (1 large grapefruit; ruby or pink, not white, for sweetness against the bitterness)

Phase 3, the dressing

  1. 45 g reserved citrus juice (run-off from supreming plus squeezed membranes)
  2. 10 g white-wine vinegar
  3. 5 g oil-cured anchovy fillet, minced to a paste with the flat of a knife — (1 fillet; the glutamate anchor, dissolves into the oil and reads as savory depth, not fish)
  4. 5 g honey
  5. 60 g extra-virgin olive oil
  6. 3 g fine sea salt
  7. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Phase 4, finish

  1. 60 g green olives (Castelvetrano), pitted and torn, pitted and torn in half by hand so they read as ragged pieces, not neat slices
  2. 5 g fresh mint leaves, torn just before serving
  3. 3 g reserved fennel fronds, picked into small feathery sprigs — (a small handful; the anise-forward finishing herb from the bulb itself)
  4. 25 g pistachios, toasted and roughly chopped, toasted in a dry pan until fragrant, cooled, roughly chopped
  5. 1 g flaky finishing salt (Maldon or similar) — (a pinch, for the table; the macro-flake hits the tongue first against the dressed leaves)

Execution

  1. 1
    Shave the fennel. Trim the stalks off the 600 g / 2 fennel bulbs and pick the feathery fronds into a small bowl of cold water (reserve for the finish). Halve each bulb through the root, cut out the tough triangular core wedge at the base, and shave the halves across the fibers on a mandoline set to 1 to 2 mm, cut-guard on. You want translucent half-moons that you can almost see through; a knife cannot match a mandoline here, and fennel sliced thick reads woody and aggressively licorice raw. Drop the shaved fennel into the 1000 ml / 4 cups ice water as you go.
  2. 2
    Soak and crisp. Leave the shaved fennel in the ice bath 15 to 20 minutes. The cold water crisps the cut leaf bases (the cells reabsorb water and go turgid, the same reason cut potatoes firm in cold water) and pulls down the raw anise edge from sharp to clean-sweet. Do the citrus and the dressing while it soaks.
  3. 3
    Supreme the citrus. Set a bowl under your board to catch juice. Cut the top and bottom off each of the 360 g / 2 oranges and the 250 g / 1 grapefruit so they sit flat. Following the curve of the fruit, pare the peel and all the white pith away in strips, top to bottom, until only naked flesh remains. Hold each fruit over the bowl and cut each segment free from the membranes on both sides, letting the segments and any juice fall into the bowl. Once all segments are out, squeeze the spent membranes hard over the bowl to extract the last juice. Lift the segments out with a fork onto a plate and reserve; keep the juice in the bowl for the dressing.
  4. 4
    Build the dressing. Pour off 45 g / 3 tbsp of the reserved citrus juice into a small jar or bowl (drink or discard any excess). Add the 10 g / 2 tsp white-wine vinegar, the 5 g / 1 anchovy fillet minced to a paste, the 5 g / 1 tsp honey, the 3 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp cracked black pepper. Whisk until the salt and anchovy dissolve, then stream in the 60 g / ¼ cup extra-virgin olive oil while whisking to a loose emulsion. Taste: it should read bright, savory, and barely sweet, with the grapefruit bitterness already showing. Adjust with a few drops more vinegar if it reads flat, a touch more honey only if the grapefruit dominates.
  5. 5
    Dry the fennel. Drain the fennel and spin it bone-dry in a salad spinner, then blot any remaining water with a clean towel. This step is not optional: water clinging to the fennel dilutes the dressing and the salad turns watery on the plate within minutes.
  6. 6
    Dress and toss. Put the dried fennel in a wide bowl. Pour over about three-quarters of the dressing and toss with your hands until every shaving is coated. Add the citrus segments, the 60 g / ⅓ cup torn Castelvetrano olives, and the 5 g / 2 tbsp torn mint, and fold gently so the segments do not break apart. Taste and add the remaining dressing if it reads dry; raw fennel drinks dressing, so it usually wants most of it.
  7. 7
    Plate and finish. Pile the salad onto a wide platter, lifting the fennel up off the bottom so it sits loose rather than packed. Tuck the citrus segments where they show. Scatter the reserved fennel fronds, the 25 g / 3 tbsp toasted chopped pistachios, and a pinch of the flaky finishing salt over the top. Drizzle any dressing left in the bowl over the plate. Serve at once, while the fennel is still crisp and cold; this salad does not hold dressed for more than 15 minutes before the fennel begins to soften.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional a single, simpler citrus→all-orange (the strict Sicilian classic)

    Drop the grapefruit and use 600 g / 3 to 4 oranges total, blood oranges if you can get them. The salad loses its bitter axis and reads sweeter and softer, the original insalata di finocchio e arance. Cut the honey to half (the grapefruit bitterness it was counterweighting is gone) and add the seeds of half a small chili or a pinch of red pepper flakes for a low heat note to replace the lost bitterness.

  • Dietary a vegan, fully plant version→no-anchovy dressing

    Swap the 5 g anchovy for 5 g / 1 tsp white miso whisked into the dressing, or 5 g / 1 tsp capers minced to a paste. Miso gives the same glutamate depth; capers give a brinier, more acid-forward savor. The salad stays balanced; the umami anchor just comes from a plant source.

  • Protein a heartier, plated-course version→fennel-citrus salad with crumbled feta or burrata

    Tear 120 g / 4 oz burrata or crumble 100 g / 3.5 oz sheep's-milk feta over the plated salad before the pistachios. The dairy turns the side into a light lunch and softens the bitter-acid edge; if using feta, cut the fine sea salt in the dressing to ¼ tsp since the cheese brings its own salt. This version is no longer dairy-free.

  • Texture a winter, room-temperature version→half-roasted fennel

    Roast one of the two bulbs in wedges at 220 °C / 425 °F with olive oil and salt for 25 to 30 minutes until deeply caramelized, cool to room temperature, and fold it through the raw shaved fennel. The contrast of sweet-soft roasted against crisp-anise raw adds a second texture and a Maillard depth that reads more substantial in cold months.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • fennel bulbs → celery hearts plus a pinch of fennel seed Shave celery hearts thin on the mandoline for the same crisp-translucent structure, and bloom ¼ tsp ground fennel seed into the dressing oil for the anise note. The salad reads as a celery-citrus salad with fennel undertones, good but not equivalent; the bulb sugar and the fronds are both lost.
  • ruby red grapefruit → pomelo, cara cara orange, or more blood orange Pomelo is the gentler, less bitter cousin and supremes the same way (the segments are firmer and drier). Cara cara oranges read pink-fleshed and low-acid, sweeter than grapefruit. If you want to keep the bitter axis without grapefruit, add 2 g / 1 tsp grated orange zest to the dressing.
  • green olives (Castelvetrano) → Cerignola, Lucques, or oil-cured black olives Cerignola and Lucques are the same buttery-mild register as Castelvetrano. Oil-cured black olives read much more intense and salty; use about half the weight (30 g) so they do not overwhelm the citrus. Avoid canned California black olives, which contribute color but no flavor.
  • pistachios → toasted pine nuts, hazelnuts, or marcona almonds Pine nuts are the softer, more buttery Sicilian alternative; hazelnuts read nuttier and more autumnal; marcona almonds bring a richer, rounder fat. Toast whichever you use; raw nuts read flat against the bright citrus.

Notes

Why the mandoline is non-negotiable. The eating quality of this salad is set entirely by the thickness of the fennel. At 1 to 2 mm the cut leaf bases read tender and the anise reads as a clean sweet perfume; at the 3 to 5 mm a knife produces, the same fennel reads fibrous and the licorice note turns sharp and medicinal. There is no recovering thick-cut raw fennel with dressing or soak time. If you do not own a mandoline, slice as thin as you possibly can with a sharp knife and accept that the texture will be the limiting factor; better still, buy an inexpensive handheld mandoline, which earns its drawer space on this salad alone. Always use the cut guard; fennel is round and rolls, and a mandoline blade does not distinguish fennel from fingertip.

The supreme is what separates this from a thrown-together citrus salad. Cutting the segments free of every scrap of membrane and pith removes the bitter, chewy connective tissue and leaves naked flesh that bursts sweet-tart in the mouth. The juice that runs off during the supreme is not waste; it is the acid base of the dressing, which is why the recipe calls for no separate citrus in the vinaigrette. Supreme over a bowl, lose nothing, and squeeze the spent membranes hard at the end to get the last of it.

The four-axis balance. This salad is a deliberate study in opposed flavors held in tension: the anise-sweet of raw fennel, the sweet-tart of orange, the bitter-astringent of grapefruit, and the salt-bitter of green olives, with the fennel fronds and mint laying a cooling green over the top. The anchovy and the olives supply the glutamate, the citrus juice and the white-wine vinegar supply the finishing acid, and the honey is a single small counterweight against the grapefruit so the bitterness reads as structure rather than a flaw. Pull any one axis and the salad collapses toward whatever remains; this is why the grapefruit earns its place against the more classic all-orange version, which is lovely but reads one-dimensionally sweet by comparison.

Timing and holding. The salad is at its peak the moment it is dressed and will not hold. Raw fennel is hygroscopic; salt and acid pull water out of the cut cells within 15 minutes and the crisp goes limp, the dressing turns watery, and the bright edge dulls. You can shave the fennel and hold it in the ice bath up to 2 hours ahead, supreme the citrus and refrigerate it covered, and build the dressing ahead, but do not combine them until the moment you serve. If you must transport it, carry the components separately and toss on arrival.

Why dairy-free by default. The Sicilian original is an oil-and-citrus salad with no cheese, and the clean, bright profile is the point. The burrata or feta modification turns it into a different, richer dish, fine for a plated lunch but a departure from the crisp-acid character that makes the no-cheese version refreshing. Keep it dairy-free when you want a palate-cutting starter ahead of a rich main.
