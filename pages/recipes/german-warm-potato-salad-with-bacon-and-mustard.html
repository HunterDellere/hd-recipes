Recipe

German warm potato salad with bacon and mustard

Schwäbischer Kartoffelsalat in the Southern German tradition: thin-sliced waxy potatoes dressed warm in their own steam window with a vinegar-mustard-stock-and-bacon-fat emulsion, finished with crisp bacon lardons, sweated onion, and a deep fold of fresh chives and parsley. The dressing is built directly in the rendered bacon fat by deglazing with white wine vinegar, dissolving dijon and a measured pinch of sugar into the hot fond, then thinning with chicken stock; poured over still-warm potatoes that absorb the dressing as they cool, the salad reads tart, savory, and structurally satisfying in a way no cold mayonnaise-bound American potato salad approaches.

Execution

  1. 1
    Cold-water start the potatoes. Place the 1000 g / 8 to 10 scrubbed waxy potatoes (skin on, whole) in a wide pot. Cover with 3000 ml / 12 cups cold water and add the 30 g / 2 tbsp kosher salt and the 2 bay leaves. The water should cover the potatoes by 4 cm; the cold-water start cooks the potato evenly from outside to center, which is critical for a salad cut where every slice has to read the same texture. A hot-water drop produces a cooked outside and underdone center, which slices unevenly and falls apart.
  2. 2
    Set the pot over medium-high heat. Bring the 3000 ml pot of water to an active simmer (small surface bubbles, not a hard rolling boil; a hard boil agitates the potatoes against each other and chips the skin). Cook 18 to 25 minutes from the time the simmer establishes, depending on potato size; the potatoes are done when a paring knife slides through the largest one with no resistance at the center. The skin will be loose and slightly cracked at this point. Pull the pot from the heat.
  3. 3
    Drain the 1000 g of cooked potatoes into a colander; do not rinse with cold tap water (the cold rinse stops the residual heat that the salad depends on for absorbing the dressing). Let the potatoes steam in the colander for 8 to 10 minutes; they should be cool enough to handle but still distinctly warm (50 to 60 °C internal). While the potatoes cool, work through the bacon, onion, and dressing in parallel.
  4. 4
    Render the bacon. Place the 200 g / 6 thick-cut slices, cut into 1 cm lardons, directly in a cold heavy-bottomed 25 cm / 10 inch skillet (cast iron, carbon steel, or heavy stainless; not non-stick, the fond needs a surface to adhere to for the deglaze later). Set the heat to medium-low. The bacon will begin to ooze fat within 2 to 3 minutes and sizzle audibly around minute 4. Stir or shake every 60 to 90 seconds to expose all faces; do not stir constantly. Cook 10 to 14 minutes total until the lardons are deeply mahogany-brown on all sides, crisp on the outside with a slightly chewy center, and the rendered fat in the pan is 4 to 5 mm deep and golden-amber (not brown-black; brown-black signals scorching and the fat is unusable). Lift the lardons out with a slotted spoon onto a paper-towel-lined plate; reserve. Lardon rendering
  5. 5
    Sweat the onion in the rendered bacon fat. The pan should hold roughly 60 to 80 g of rendered fat at this point (the bacon yields ~30 to 40 percent of its weight as rendered fat); if the pan is noticeably scorched-dark at the bottom, scrape any bitter solids and pour the fat through a fine mesh into a heatproof bowl, then return the fat to the pan. Set the heat to medium-low. Add the 200 g / 1 large fine-diced yellow onion and a pinch (1 g / ¼ tsp) of the kosher salt. Sweat 6 to 8 minutes, stirring every 90 seconds, until the onion is fully translucent, edges just barely turning gold, and smells sweet rather than raw. Do not brown the onion; the dressing wants the sweated sweet-aromatic onion not caramelized.
  6. 6
    Build the dressing in the pan. Pull the pan from the heat for 30 seconds (so the next addition does not flash-evaporate). Add the 80 g / ⅓ cup white wine vinegar to the pan with the onion and rendered fat; the vinegar will hiss as it hits the hot fat and immediately deglaze any bacon fond stuck to the pan bottom. Stir with a wooden spoon to scrape the fond into the vinegar; the liquid will turn cloudy-brown as the dissolved fond goes into suspension. The pan should smell aggressively of vinegar-and-bacon at this moment. Deglazing
  7. 7
    Return the pan to medium heat. Whisk in the 30 g / 2 tbsp dijon mustard and the 15 g / 1 tbsp whole grain mustard; the mustard mucilage will immediately start to thicken the dressing and emulsify the rendered fat into the vinegar. Whisk until the mustard is fully dispersed and the dressing reads uniform brown-amber, 30 to 45 seconds. Add the 200 g / ¾ cup + 1 tbsp warm chicken stock in three additions, whisking between each so the dressing tightens into an emulsified state rather than splitting. Add the 6 g / 1½ tsp sugar and stir to dissolve. Bring the dressing to a bare simmer; it should reduce by no more than 10 percent in volume, just enough to tighten the consistency to a pourable but coating sauce. Vinaigrette emulsion
  8. 8
    Stream in the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil while whisking; this is the supplemental fat that brings the total fat in the dressing up to roughly the volume of the vinegar-and-stock combined. The dressing should now be a glossy uniform amber, slightly viscous, and ribbon off the whisk. Taste; it should read tart-savory-bacony in that order, with the mustard and onion as background. Add the remaining ~2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt if needed (the bacon and stock both contribute salt; taste before adding) and the 2 g / ¾ tsp cracked black pepper. Pull the pan off the heat and hold warm; the dressing should not be hotter than 70 °C when it hits the potatoes (boiling-hot dressing on potato slices breaks the starch surface and produces a gluey result).
  9. 9
    Slice the potatoes. The potatoes should still be distinctly warm (40 to 50 °C internal at this point, off the colander for 15 to 20 minutes). Peel the skin off the warm potatoes with your fingers (the skin lifts away in sheets if the potato is properly cooked) and slice each into 3 to 4 mm rounds with a sharp chef knife or a mandoline. The slices need to be uniform and thin enough to absorb the dressing without falling apart; thicker slices read as boiled-potato-with-dressing rather than dressed-potato-salad. Arrange the slices in a wide shallow serving bowl (not a deep mixing bowl, the salad reads better spread thin and the slices on top do not crush the slices on the bottom).
  10. 10
    Dress the warm potatoes. Pour two-thirds of the warm dressing across the potato slices while they are still distinctly warm (the dressing should hit potatoes at 30 to 40 °C). Fold gently with a wide spoon or silicone spatula, 6 to 10 turns, just enough to coat every slice without crushing them. The warm potato slices will visibly absorb the dressing as you fold; the salad will look wetter than the final state because the absorption continues over the next 5 to 10 minutes as the salad rests. Add half (about 7 g) of the 15 g sliced chives, half (about 6 g) of the 12 g chopped parsley, and half the reserved bacon lardons; fold once more to distribute.
  11. 11
    Rest and finish. Let the salad sit at room temperature for 8 to 12 minutes; the slices continue to absorb the dressing and the flavors integrate. The salad should read distinctly warm at service (not hot, not cold; the warm-temperature window is structural to the dish). Just before serving, drizzle the remaining third of the dressing across the top (the salad will look wet and shiny at this point; this is correct, the second drizzle is the dish dressing itself rather than the absorbed dressing), scatter the remaining chives, parsley, and bacon lardons across the top. Taste a slice from the surface; if flat, more cracked pepper; if undersalted, a small additional pinch (1 to 2 g / ¼ to ½ tsp kosher salt, no more, the bacon carries salt); if the dressing reads thin, fold in the rest of the dressing reserved earlier. Serve immediately or hold at room temperature for up to 1 hour; the salad declines past 90 minutes (the slices get oversaturated and read soggy).

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→Bavarian leaning

    Increase the bacon to 280 g / 9 to 10 slices, cut into 1.5 cm strips rather than 1 cm dice; slice the potatoes 6 to 8 mm rather than 3 to 4 mm; reduce the dijon to 20 g and skip the whole grain mustard. The dish becomes a richer, chunkier, less acid-forward Bavarian Beierwurst-stand register, more food-than-condiment. Both versions are recognizably Kartoffelsalat; the Bavarian skews substantial-and-meaty, the Swabian skews delicate-and-tart.

  • Regional cuisine→Rheinland leaning, with cider vinegar and apple

    Replace the white wine vinegar with 80 g / ⅓ cup apple cider vinegar; add 80 g / ½ medium tart apple (Granny Smith or Boskop), peeled and cut into 5 mm dice, folded in at the dressing-with-potato step. The dish shifts West into the Rheinland and Pennsylvania Dutch register; the apple reads against the bacon similarly to the bacon-apple pairing in Alsatian Flammkuchen, and the cider vinegar carries the apple aromatic into the dressing.

  • Protein protein→with Bratwurst or Frankfurter alongside

    Grill or pan-sear 4 to 6 Nuremberg-style Bratwurst (~80 g each) over medium heat for 8 to 10 minutes until deeply browned on all sides and 71 °C internal; serve the warm potato salad alongside with grainy German mustard (Senf) for dipping. The pairing is the canonical Southern German biergarten plate; the salad reads as the structural starch under the sausage rather than as a standalone dish.

  • Occasion season→cold-weather version with caraway and stronger smoke

    Add 2 g / 1 tsp whole caraway seeds, toasted in the rendered bacon fat after the lardons come out and before the onion goes in; substitute the bacon with 200 g of smoked German Speck (cured smoked pork belly from a German deli) or with double-smoked American thick-cut bacon. The dish reads as a winter Kartoffelsalat with a more aggressive smoke axis and the caraway-and-pork pairing that runs through Bavarian and Austrian cooking.

  • Dietary dietary→vegetarian

    Skip the bacon entirely; render 60 g / 4 tbsp unsalted butter with 8 g / 1½ tsp smoked paprika and 5 g / 1 tsp soy sauce or tamari over medium heat for 90 seconds (the paprika blooms into the butter and the soy contributes the missing salt-and-umami load from the absent bacon). Use this paprika-butter base in place of the rendered bacon fat; proceed with the rest of the recipe identically. Substitute vegetable stock for the chicken stock. The dish reads differently (less smoke-and-pork, more spiced-butter) but legibly Kartoffelsalat. Scatter 40 g / ⅓ cup pumpkin seeds, toasted, at the finish to replace the crisp-textural function of the bacon lardons.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • waxy potatoes (Yukon Gold or red new potatoes) → fingerling potatoes or La Ratte Higher-end waxy cultivars that hold their shape exceptionally well in the slice and dress. The dish reads slightly more refined; the dressing absorption is comparable. Avoid russet or other floury potatoes; they collapse on slicing and the salad reads as mashed-potato-with-dressing.
  • thick-cut bacon → German Speck or double-smoked Polish kielbasa, cut into similar lardons German Speck (cured-and-smoked pork belly, available at German delis) is the regional canonical product and reads slightly drier with more concentrated smoke; renders less fat (use the full 30 g neutral oil to compensate) and produces a more aromatic dressing. Double-smoked kielbasa is a Polish-leaning substitution; reads sausage-and-paprika rather than pure smoke-and-pork.
  • dijon mustard → German medium-hot mustard (mittelscharfer Senf, e.g. Löwensenf, Bautzner) The canonical Southern German choice is medium-hot German mustard rather than French dijon; the German mustards are sweeter, grainier, and less aromatic than dijon. Use the same mass; the dish reads slightly sweeter and less sharp, which is correct for the Bavarian register. Dijon is the cosmopolitan workhorse substitution and is what most American cooks have on hand; both are legitimate.
  • white wine vinegar → apple cider vinegar Slightly softer (5 percent acidity vs 6 to 7), distinctly more fruity-autumnal. The substitution shifts the dish into the Rheinland and Pennsylvania-Dutch register; many German-American home cooks prefer cider vinegar over white wine. The two are equally legitimate regional choices.
  • chicken stock → vegetable stock or beef stock Vegetable stock for a vegetarian-leaning version (combined with the dietary modification above for a fully vegetarian dish); beef stock for a deeper, more substantial dressing that pushes the dish toward sauerbraten territory. Chicken is the neutral middle and the most common Southern German home choice.
  • whole grain mustard → omit; increase dijon to 40 g / 2½ tbsp total The whole grain mustard provides textural crunch and a slightly different aromatic; omitting and increasing dijon to 40 g produces a smoother dressing with less visible mustard seed in the salad. Acceptable substitution; the texture difference is meaningful at side-by-side tasting but the dish reads cohesive either way.
  • fresh chives → green onion / scallion tops, very thinly sliced (1 mm rounds), green portion only Acceptable everyday substitution at 75 percent of the chive mass to compensate for the stronger onion aromatic; the dish reads slightly more onion-forward. Avoid dried chives, which have almost no aromatic.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → thick-cut bacon make it yourselfstub Pork belly cured with salt, sodium nitrite, sugar, and aromatics for 7 days, then cold-smoked over hardwood for 12 to 24 hours produces a bacon with markedly more complex flavor and the cure tuned to the cook's preference (less sweet, more peppery, more smoky); about 10 days elapsed time, 4 hours active. Weekend project.
  • → chicken stock, warmed make it yourself Cold-water start with chicken bones, slow-skim, no-boil 4-hour simmer with mirepoix and bay produces a clear, gelatin-rich stock that carries dramatically more body and clean roast-chicken aromatic than store-bought; about 5 hours elapsed time, 30 minutes active.
  • → dijon mustard make it yourself Brown mustard seeds soaked in white wine, ground with salt and acid, aged 24 to 48 hours to mellow the bitterness produces a fresher more aromatic mustard than the year-old jar from the supermarket; about 30 minutes active, 2 days passive.
  • → whole grain mustard make it yourself Yellow and brown mustard seeds soaked whole in white wine vinegar and water, lightly cracked rather than fully ground, aged briefly to develop flavor produces a coarser, more aromatic grainy mustard than commercial; about 20 minutes active, 24 hours passive.

Notes

The thesis is that a warm potato salad is not a cold potato salad served hot. The whole technique pivots on the steam window between draining and dressing: the potato slices have to be warm enough that the starch surface is still slightly tacky and can absorb the dressing (50 to 60 °C internal at slicing, 30 to 40 °C when the dressing hits), but not so hot that the dressing boils on contact and breaks. The German canon is built around this specific temperature condition; a cold-dressed German potato salad is a different dish in the same kitchen-conceptual family but does not read as Kartoffelsalat.

Why cold-water start. Potatoes dropped into already-boiling water cook the surface starches before the center has heated; the result is a slice with a translucent gelled outer ring around a still-firm white center. This unevenness is invisible to the eye but reads structurally in the bite. A cold-water start brings the whole potato to temperature together; every slice reads the same texture from edge to center. The same logic applies to hard-cooked eggs and to potatoes destined for any composed salad; the cold start is non-negotiable for uniform cook.

Why slice warm, not cold. The starch on a freshly cut warm potato surface is slightly gelatinous and sticky; it grabs the dressing on contact and pulls it into the slice. The same potato sliced cold has a sealed glassy surface and the dressing sits on top rather than absorbing in; the salad reads as undressed potato in a puddle of vinaigrette. The Swabian school is precise about this: dress within 20 minutes of draining, while the slices are still distinctly warm to the touch (test by pressing a finger to the surface; it should feel like a freshly drawn bath, not like room temperature).

Why the dressing is built in the pan. Three reasons: (1) the rendered bacon fat is the structural fat of the dressing and cannot be cleanly transferred to a separate bowl without losing the fond; (2) the deglaze of vinegar onto the bacon-and-onion fond captures the entire flavor accumulation of the cook into the dressing; (3) the warm-pan whisk produces a tighter emulsion than a cold-bowl whisk because the heat keeps the fat liquid and disperses the mustard mucilage faster. A pan-built dressing is structurally superior here; a bowl-built dressing of the same ingredients reads thinner and less integrated.

Why white wine vinegar specifically. The Swabian tradition uses white wine vinegar; the Rheinland tradition uses cider vinegar; both are legitimate German regional choices. White wine vinegar reads cleaner, brighter, and more wine-and-fruit; cider vinegar reads softer, more autumnal, and more apple-forward. American distilled white vinegar is wrong here (too harsh, no parent-fruit aromatic) and red wine vinegar pushes the dish toward an Italian register. The choice between white wine and cider is regional preference; the choice not to use distilled white or red wine is structural.

Why dijon and not French Dijon despite the German pedigree. The Southern German home cook traditionally uses German medium-hot mustard (Löwensenf mittelscharf, Bautzner) rather than French dijon, but the substitution is essentially seamless: dijon is what most American kitchens have, the mucilage emulsifier function is identical, and the slightly sharper-less-sweet profile of dijon vs German medium-hot is a small adjustment the cook can ignore. The whole grain mustard is the textural-and-aromatic complement that approximates the German "Senf mit Körnern" register more authentically than dijon alone.

Why sugar. The white wine vinegar is acid-forward (6 to 7 percent acetic acid); the German tradition softens this with a measured pinch of sugar (5 to 8 g per 80 g vinegar, calibrated to leave the dressing reading tart-and-bright rather than sharp-and-aggressive). The sugar is not making the dressing sweet; it is masking the sharpest edge of the vinegar so the more aromatic notes (the mustard, the bacon, the parent wine aromatic) come through. Skipping the sugar produces a dressing that reads one-note acidic; the German cook will always taste the difference and reach for the sugar.

Why chives and parsley both. Chives provide the bright fresh allium note that the cooked sweated yellow onion does not; parsley provides the green herbal counterpoint that the alliums alone do not. Either one alone produces a thinner finish; both together produce the layered fresh-finish that distinguishes a well-made Kartoffelsalat from a competent one. Substituting curly parsley for flat-leaf is acceptable but the leaf texture is wrong (curly parsley is leathery and gets tangled in the slices; flat-leaf is supple and folds in cleanly).

How this salad is served. The dish is the structural starch under any German main course featuring sausage or roast pork: Bratwurst (Nuremberg, Thuringian, Weisswurst, Bockwurst — all match), Schweinebraten (roast pork), Sauerbraten (vinegar-marinated beef roast), Schnitzel (Wiener or Wiener Art), Leberkäse (Bavarian meatloaf). It also stands alone as a substantial lunch with rye bread and a beer (helles for Bavaria, weissbier for Swabia, Kölsch for Rheinland — match the regional axis). Leftover Kartoffelsalat is best the next day folded into a fresh batch of warm sliced potatoes with a fresh splash of vinegar and a small dab of mustard to refresh the dressing; do not microwave to re-warm, the dressing splits and the potatoes go gluey.
