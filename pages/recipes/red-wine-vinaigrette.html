Red wine vinaigrette

A canonical Mediterranean salad dressing: 1:3 red wine vinegar to extra-virgin olive oil, dijon as the emulsifier, mashed garlic and salt dissolved in the acid phase before the oil streams in. Holds 30 to 60 minutes after building. Stub.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 6 items ▾
  1. 30 g red wine vinegar
  2. 4 g dijon mustard
  3. 4 g garlic clove, mashed to a paste with a pinch of salt under the side of a chef knife — (1 small clove; raw, optional but canonical Italian-and-Provencal)
  4. 2 g fine sea salt
  5. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked
  6. 90 g extra-virgin olive oil

Execution

  1. 1
    Stub: combine the 30 g / 2 tbsp red wine vinegar, the 4 g / ¾ tsp dijon mustard, the 4 g / 1 small mashed garlic clove, the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt, and the 1 g / ½ tsp cracked black pepper in a small bowl. Whisk with a fork until the salt dissolves; the salt has to dissolve in the acid phase before the oil enters or it pockets in the dressing. Vinaigrette emulsion
  2. 2
    Stub: stream in the 90 g / 6 tbsp olive oil in a thin steady pour while whisking vigorously. The mucilage in the dijon and the surface-active proteins in the garlic do the emulsifier work; the slow oil addition gives the colloid time to form. The dressing should look opaque, slightly creamy, and uniformly thick when finished.
  3. 3
    Stub: taste and adjust. The dressing should read sharp from the vinegar, salt-forward, garlicky, and oil-rich on the finish. If the acid is too aggressive, add 5 to 10 g / 1 to 2 tsp more oil. Use within 30 to 60 minutes; the emulsion breaks past the hour mark and the garlic continues to bloom raw-pungent past 2 hours.

Notes

This is a stub. Flesh out: the 1:3 ratio reasoning (the classical French ratio that produces a salad reading bright but not sharp; 1:2 for sharper, 1:4 for milder), the role of dijon mucilage as a Pickering emulsifier (the mustard seed coat polysaccharides that surround oil droplets), the salt-dissolution-first sequence (so it does not pocket as gritty hot spots), the slow-stream oil technique (smaller droplets stay suspended longer, so the cook is shearing the oil into a fine colloid as it enters), the variants (sherry vinegar, white wine vinegar, lemon-juice substitution at 1:3), the additions (chopped shallot, anchovy, honey, herbs), and the storage failure mode (the emulsion breaks within hours, the dressing rebuilds with a fresh teaspoon of mustard whisked into 1 tbsp warm water if needed).

The recipe is the foundation for Tuscan panzanella, Provencal Niçoise, French green salad, and almost any composed Mediterranean salad. Cooks should build a small batch fresh per meal rather than holding a jar in the fridge; the difference between a 5-minute-old vinaigrette and a 2-day-old jar is dramatic.
