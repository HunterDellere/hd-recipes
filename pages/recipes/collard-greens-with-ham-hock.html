Recipe

Collard greens with ham hock

Long-braised collard greens in a smoky ham-hock-flavored broth: a smoked ham hock simmered alone for 90 minutes to build the pot likker (the broth that defines the dish), then collard greens stripped, washed, and torn in, braised down for 60 to 90 more minutes with onion, garlic, apple cider vinegar, and red pepper flakes. The greens emerge dark, silky, soft enough to twirl on a fork, with the pot likker reserved as the prized broth ladled over cornbread.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The total cook is 2.5 to 3 hours: 90 minutes for the ham hock to build the pot likker, then 60 to 90 minutes more for the collards to braise to silky-tender. Plan accordingly; greens cannot be rushed.
  • Smoked ham hock is the structural Southern flavor base. Available at any U.S. supermarket meat counter and most butchers; about $4 to $8 per hock. Substitute smoked turkey neck (popular for a less-pork version) or smoked turkey leg if preferred; the dish reads slightly different but acceptably close.
  • Collard greens have tough fibrous central stems that must be removed. Stripping the leaves from the stems (running thumb and forefinger down each stem from base to tip, peeling the leaf off) is the canonical Southern method; quicker is to fold each leaf in half along the stem and cut the stem out with a knife. Either way, discard the stems (or save for stock); they do not soften enough in the braise.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the pot likker (90 min simmer)

  1. 500 g smoked ham hock (or smoked turkey neck), 1 large hock, about 1 lb; rinsed under cold water
  2. 2500 ml cold water
  3. 200 g yellow onion, quartered (for the simmer) — (1 large)
  4. 20 g garlic, smashed — (5 cloves)
  5. 2 each bay leaves

Phase 2, the greens (added after 90 min hock simmer)

  1. 1400 g collard greens, stems removed, stems removed (about 800 g leaves after stripping), washed thoroughly in 3 changes of cold water (collards trap grit), torn into 8 cm pieces
  2. 3 g red pepper flakes
  3. 6 g kosher salt
  4. 3 g black pepper, cracked

Phase 3, the finish

  1. 30 ml apple cider vinegar
  2. 5 g light brown sugar (optional, to balance vinegar) optional
  3. 30 ml Texas Pete or Crystal hot sauce (at the table)

Execution

  1. 1
    In a large heavy stockpot, combine the 500 g ham hock with the 2500 ml / 10 cups cold water, 200 g quartered onion, 20 g smashed garlic, and 2 bay leaves. Bring to a hard boil, then reduce to a steady simmer. Skim any grey foam in the first 10 minutes (this is dissolved myoglobin from the ham; skimming clarifies the pot likker).
  2. 2
    Simmer the hock 90 minutes, partially covered. The hock should be fall-apart tender at the end; a fork inserted into the meat should slide in easily. The water will have reduced to about 1800 ml and turned a smoky tan color; this is the structural pot likker. The kitchen should smell aggressively of smoke and pork.
  3. 3
    While the hock simmers, prepare the collards. Strip the leaves from the stems (or fold and cut out the stems with a knife); discard stems. Wash the leaves thoroughly in 3 changes of cold water (greens trap grit and sand in their leaves; rushing the wash produces gritty finished greens). Tear or rough-chop into 8 cm pieces. You should have about 800 g of cleaned leaves.
  4. 4
    When the hock has cooked 90 minutes, lift it out with tongs and transfer to a plate; cool briefly. Pull the meat off the bone and skin (the meat should fall away easily); chop the meat into small chunks; return the meat to the pot. Discard the bone and any large pieces of skin.
  5. 5
    Add the prepared collard leaves to the pot in batches; press them down into the pot likker with a wooden spoon as they wilt to make room for the next batch. The full 800 g of leaves should reduce to fit in the pot within 3 to 4 minutes of stirring. Add the 3 g red pepper flakes, 6 g salt, and 3 g black pepper.
  6. 6
    Reduce heat to medium-low, partially cover, and simmer 60 to 90 minutes, stirring every 20 minutes, until the greens are dark, silky, and soft enough to twirl on a fork. The cook time depends on the toughness of the greens; younger spring collards may be tender at 60 minutes, while older winter collards may need a full 90 minutes. The greens should have shrunk to about 500 g and the pot likker should have reduced and concentrated to about 1 L of dark smoky broth.
  7. 7
    Off heat, stir in the 30 ml / 2 tbsp apple cider vinegar and 5 g / 1 tsp brown sugar if using. The vinegar brightens the dish; without it the greens read flat and one-note. Taste; adjust salt (rarely needed at this stage, the ham hock has been doing the work).
  8. 8
    Serve with a generous ladle of the pot likker poured into each bowl alongside the greens (or over a wedge of cornbread for sopping). Texas Pete or Crystal hot sauce at the table.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein pivot→as collards with smoked turkey neck (less pork)

    Replace the 500 g smoked ham hock with 500 g smoked turkey neck (one large neck). Same simmer time (90 min). The dish reads slightly less rich and slightly leaner; popular Southern alternative for households reducing pork. The structural smoky-meat broth is functionally the same.

  • Dietary protein pivot→as vegetarian collards

    Skip the ham hock entirely. Simmer the onion-garlic-bay aromatics in 2500 ml vegetable stock for 30 minutes (instead of the 90 min hock simmer). Add 15 ml soy sauce and 5 g miso to the broth for umami depth. Proceed with the greens. The dish reads cleaner and brighter but loses the structural smoky bass note; common Southern Lent or vegetarian-Sunday adaptation.

  • Regional flavor→as mixed greens (collards plus mustard greens plus turnip greens)

    Replace half the collards (700 g raw) with 400 g raw mustard greens and 300 g raw turnip greens. Add the mustard and turnip greens 15 minutes later than the collards (they cook faster and would over-soften). The dish reads more complex and slightly more bitter; the traditional Southern "mess of greens" rather than collards-alone.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • smoked ham hock → smoked turkey neck, smoked turkey leg, 200 g thick-cut bacon, or 200 g smoked sausage Smoked turkey neck is the most popular substitute (less pork, similar smoky depth). Smoked turkey leg is more meat per dollar but slightly less of the gelatin-pot-likker structure. Bacon works but produces a faster, less integrated dish; render at the start, simmer 30 min, then add greens.
  • apple cider vinegar → pepper vinegar (apple cider vinegar steeped 1 week with hot chiles) Pepper vinegar is the Southern-canonical condiment for greens; brings vinegar and heat in one splash. Many Southern households keep a bottle of homemade pepper vinegar on the table specifically for greens. White vinegar is too harsh; balsamic is too sweet.

Notes

Why pot likker is the point. The pot likker (the broth produced by the long ham hock and greens braise) is the structural prize of the dish; in many Southern traditions, the cook reserves the pot likker separately and serves it as a soup the next day, or insists the diner sop it up with cornbread on the same plate as the greens. The dissolved gelatin from the ham hock, the vitamins leached from the greens, the smoky pork-fat aromatics, and the slow-reduced concentration all combine into a broth that is more nutritious and more flavorful than any conventional soup base. Discarding the pot likker is the most common home-cook error.

Why the 90 minute hock simmer first. The ham hock needs the structural 90 minutes to release its gelatin, fat, and smoky aromatics into the water before the greens go in. Adding the hock and greens at the same time produces a dish where the pot likker tastes like greens-flavored water rather than smoky-pork-flavored broth, and the greens overcook before the hock is done. The sequence matters.

Why long braise the greens. Collard greens have thick fibrous cell walls (more lignin than most cooking greens) that take 60 to 90 minutes of slow simmer to fully break down into the silky texture that is structurally Southern. A 20 minute braise produces tough chewy greens that taste underdone; the cook time is the cook time. The greens cannot be rushed.

Why the vinegar at the finish. The structural Southern green has an acid lift at the very end of the cook. Without it the greens read flat and one-note (smoky pork bass note, no top note). Apple cider vinegar is the canonical Southern choice; pepper vinegar (apple cider vinegar steeped with hot chiles) is the upgraded version. Add off heat so the vinegar volatiles do not cook off.

Pairings. Canonical with cornbread (for sopping the pot likker), fried chicken, smoked pork ribs, or any Southern protein. A small bowl of pot likker on the side as a sipper. Texas Pete, Crystal, or pepper vinegar at the table. Sweet tea or a cold beer.

Storage. Improves dramatically overnight; flavors integrate and the greens get even softer. Keeps 5 days refrigerated. Reheat over low heat with a splash of water. Freezes well in 500 g portions; thaw overnight in fridge before reheating.

Sources
  • Southern (especially African American Southern) canon, descended from West African leaf-green cooking tradition — Collard greens with ham hock (also called "greens" or "Sunday greens") are structural to Southern home cooking, especially in the African American culinary tradition where the long-braise leaf-green technique traces back to West African cooking traditions adapted with American pork. The defining elements are: smoked ham hock as the structural flavor-and-fat source (descended from slavery-era cuts the enslaved cook had access to; transformed into a culinary virtue); the long simmer at low temperature that softens the tough collard leaves into silky greens; apple cider vinegar at the finish for the structural Southern acid lift; and the prized pot likker (the broth), traditionally served alongside or poured over cornbread.
