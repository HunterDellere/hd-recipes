Turkish lamb şiş kebap

Cubes of lamb leg marinated in grated-onion enzymes, olive oil, tomato paste, and pul biber, threaded on skewers and grilled hard over charcoal to a Maillard crust with a rosy medium interior. Salt goes on at the fire, not in the marinade, so the surface stays dry enough to brown. Served on warm lavash that catches the rendering juices, with a sumac-onion salad for acid and grilled peppers and tomato alongside. The canonical Anatolian mangal kebab, engineered for maximum char without overcooking the small cubes.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Marinate the lamb at least 4 hours ahead, ideally overnight. The grated-onion enzymes and the acid in the tomato paste need time to loosen the muscle fibers of the cubes; a 30-minute marinade seasons the surface but does not tenderize.
  • Do not salt the meat until it is on the skewer at the fire. Salt in the marinade draws moisture to the surface through osmosis, and a wet surface steams instead of charring. Salt goes on in the last minutes so the crust stays dry.
  • Skewer the lamb and the vegetables separately. Lamb cubes and onion-and-pepper chunks cook at different rates; threaded together, either the meat overcooks or the vegetables stay raw. Traditional mangal cooks run parallel skewers.
  • If you have a charcoal grill, use it. The smoke and the radiant char are structural to the dish, not optional flavor. A cast-iron grill pan or an oven broiler is the fallback (see the modifications), but the charcoal version is the target.

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, the marinade (4 to 24 hours ahead)

  1. 800 g boneless lamb leg or shoulder, trimmed of silverskin but not of fat, cut into 3.5 cm cubes
  2. 150 g yellow onion, grated on the coarse holes of a box grater, juice and pulp both used — (1 medium onion; the enzymes are the tenderizer)
  3. 40 g extra-virgin olive oil
  4. 1 × 6 oz / 170 g can tomato paste
  5. 4 g pul biber or Aleppo pepper
  6. 2 g dried oregano
  7. 3 g black pepper, freshly ground

Phase 2, the vegetable skewers

  1. 150 g red onion, peeled and cut into 3 cm squares, layers kept 2 to 3 deep — (1 medium onion)
  2. 150 g green pepper (Anatolian sivri or Italian frying pepper), cut into 3 cm squares — (2 long peppers; a thin-walled sweet pepper chars better than a thick bell)
  3. 150 g cherry or cocktail tomatoes — (about 8; kept whole so they steam inside their skins)

Phase 3, at the fire

  1. 9 g kosher salt
  2. 15 g neutral oil

Phase 4, the sumac onion salad

  1. 150 g red onion, halved and sliced pole-to-pole as thinly as possible — (1 medium onion)
  2. 5 g sumac
  3. 15 g flat-leaf parsley, leaves picked and roughly chopped — (½ bunch)
  4. 2 g kosher salt

Phase 5, to serve

  1. 4 each lavash or pita — (one flatbread per person; warmed at the edge of the fire)
  2. 1 each lemon, cut into wedges — (the finishing acid, squeezed over at the table)

Execution

  1. 1
    Marinate the lamb (4 to 24 hours ahead). In a bowl, combine the 150 g grated onion (juice and pulp), 40 g / 3 tbsp olive oil, 20 g / 1 tbsp tomato paste, 4 g / 2 tsp pul biber, 2 g / 1 tsp oregano, and 3 g / 1½ tsp black pepper. Add the 800 g lamb cubes and turn until every cube is coated. Do not add salt now. Cover and refrigerate at least 4 hours, ideally overnight. Pull the meat from the fridge 45 minutes before grilling so it comes toward room temperature and cooks evenly.
  2. 2
    Build the fire. Light a chimney of charcoal and, when the coals are ashed over and glowing, spread them for a hot direct zone and a cooler holding zone. You want a hot grate: a hand held 10 cm above the grate should force you to pull back within 2 to 3 seconds. Oil the grate with a rag dipped in the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil.
  3. 3
    Skewer. Thread the lamb cubes onto skewers, 5 to 6 per skewer, pushed snug but not crushed together (a hair of gap lets the sides char). On separate skewers, thread the 150 g red onion squares and 150 g green pepper squares, and skewer the 150 g cherry tomatoes on their own. Brush the vegetables lightly with any oil clinging to the marinade bowl. Now salt the lamb: sprinkle the 9 g / 1 tbsp kosher salt evenly over all the meat skewers just before they go on the fire.
  4. 4
    Grill the lamb. Lay the meat skewers over the hot zone. Grill 10 to 12 minutes total, turning every 2 to 3 minutes to build a Maillard crust on all four faces of each cube. The cubes should be deeply browned outside and reach 58 to 60°C / 136 to 140°F internal, a rosy medium that carries to about 63°C / 145°F over the rest; lamb leg dries out and toughens past 65°C. If flare-ups from dripping fat blacken the meat, slide the skewers to the cooler zone until the flames die, then return them. Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Grill the vegetables alongside. Put the onion and pepper skewers on the hot zone with the lamb; they want 8 to 10 minutes with turning until charred at the edges and softened. Add the tomato skewer for only the last 3 to 4 minutes, turning once, until the skins blister and split but the tomatoes still hold their shape. Pull each skewer as it is done.
  6. 6
    Rest the meat. Slide the grilled lamb skewers onto a platter and rest 5 minutes, loosely tented. Resting lets the muscle fibers reabsorb the juices driven to the center by the heat; cutting or serving immediately spills them onto the plate instead of keeping them in the cube.
  7. 7
    Make the sumac onion salad while the meat rests. Put the 150 g thinly sliced red onion in a bowl with the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt and scrunch it with your fingers for 20 seconds to soften and take the raw bite off. Add the 5 g / 1 tbsp sumac and 15 g chopped parsley and toss. The salad should look stained red-purple and smell tart.
  8. 8
    Warm the bread and assemble. Warm the 4 lavash or pita at the cooler edge of the fire for 20 to 30 seconds per side until pliable and spotted. Lay a flatbread on each plate, slide the lamb off the skewer onto the bread so the rendered juices soak in, and pile the grilled onion, pepper, and tomato alongside. Top with the sumac onions and serve with the lemon wedges to squeeze over. Eat by wrapping meat, salad, and a char of pepper in a torn piece of the juice-soaked bread.
Safety
  • Pulling the lamb cubes at 58 to 60°C / 136 to 140°F internal for a rosy medium Whole-muscle lamb (not ground) is safely served at 145°F internal per USDA; the 140°F pull plus a 5-minute rest carries the cubes to roughly 145°F. The interior of an uncut lamb cube is essentially sterile, and the hard sear on all four faces of each cube pasteurizes the contamination layer, which lives only on the surface. Vulnerable diners (pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, very old) should be served lamb cooked to 145°F internal with no carryover credit. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture no charcoal grill→grill pan or broiler şiş

    Preheat a cast-iron grill pan over high heat until smoking, or set an oven rack 10 cm below the broiler and preheat on high. Cook the salted, skewered lamb 3 to 4 minutes per face, turning, to the same 58 to 60°C internal. You lose the charcoal smoke but keep the Maillard crust. A pinch of smoked paprika in the marinade partly compensates for the missing wood smoke.

  • Protein chicken şiş (tavuk şiş)→as tavuk şiş with a yogurt marinade

    Swap the 800 g lamb for 800 g boneless chicken thigh in 3.5 cm pieces. Add 80 g / ⅓ cup whole-milk yogurt to the marinade; the lactic acid and calcium tenderize poultry the way onion enzymes tenderize lamb. Grill 10 to 12 minutes to 74°C / 165°F internal. The most common weeknight şiş in Turkey.

  • Texture a richer, fattier bite→kuyruk yağı (tail-fat) şiş

    Alternate the lamb cubes on the skewer with 1.5 cm cubes of lamb tail fat or fatty lamb belly, one fat cube every two meat cubes. The rendering fat bastes the lean cubes and drips onto the coals for extra smoke. This is the traditional way to keep lean leg meat from drying over the fire.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • boneless lamb leg or shoulder → beef sirloin or tri-tip Beef şiş (dana şiş) is common. Cut the same 3.5 cm cubes and grill to 54 to 57°C for medium-rare, since beef reads better slightly rarer than lamb. The dish loses the lamb aromatics but keeps the format.
  • pul biber or Aleppo pepper → a 3:1 blend of sweet paprika and a pinch of cayenne Pul biber is fruity and only mildly hot. Sweet paprika carries the color and fruit; the cayenne pinch supplies the gentle warmth. Do not substitute straight cayenne, which is far hotter and one-dimensional.
  • lavash or pita → any thin, pliable flatbread The structural job of the bread is to be warm and pliable enough to wrap the meat and soak the juice. Avoid thick, spongy bread that overwhelms the lamb.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → lavash or pita make it yourself A fresh, blistered home-baked Turkish flatbread pulled warm off the oven stone is a different food from bagged supermarket lavash, and it soaks the lamb juices without going to paste.

Notes

Why salt goes on late. Salt dissolved in a marinade pulls water out of the meat by osmosis and leaves the surface wet. A wet cube steams for the first minutes on the grill instead of browning, and you lose the Maillard crust that defines a good şiş. Salting on the skewer, in the last minute before the fire, seasons the surface while leaving it dry enough to char immediately. This is the opposite of a thick roast, where a long dry-brine is the right move; small fast-cooking cubes want late salt.

Why grated onion, not sliced. Onion contains protease-adjacent enzymes and sulfur compounds that break down surface proteins and loosen muscle fiber. Grating ruptures the cells and releases that juice directly onto the meat, where sliced onion mostly sits on top. The grated onion is a tenderizer first and a seasoning second; strain the pulp off before skewering so it does not scorch on the fire.

The doneness window is narrow. Lamb leg is lean. Below 55°C it is undercooked and chewy in the wrong way; above 65°C it dries out and the fibers seize. The 58 to 60°C target is the medium sweet spot where the fat has rendered and the meat is still rosy and juicy. Pull the skewers a couple of degrees early; carryover heat during the 5-minute rest finishes them.

Umami and acid, built in. The tomato paste in the marinade is the glutamate anchor that makes the lamb read savory-deep rather than merely grilled, and the sumac onions plus the lemon wedges are the structural acid that cuts the fat. Neither is optional garnish; a şiş without the tart onion and citrus eats heavy and flat.

Pairs across Turkish mangal cookery and the broader Anatolian, Cypriot, and diaspora grill tradition. Serve with rice pilav (şehriyeli pilav, with toasted vermicelli), grilled long green peppers, cacık (yogurt with cucumber and mint), and ayran to drink. The marinated cubes keep raw 2 days refrigerated and freeze well in the marinade for a month; thaw fully before grilling.

Sources
  • Anatolian mangal (charcoal-grill) tradition — Şiş kebap (from şiş, the Turkish word for skewer, the same root that gives English "shish kebab") is the oldest and most widespread Turkish kebab format: seasoned cubes of lamb or mutton grilled over charcoal. Unlike the hand-minced Adana and Urfa kebabs, şiş uses whole cubes, so the cut, the fat content, and the doneness of each cube are what the cook controls. This is the home mangal version served across Turkey with lavash, sumac onions, and grilled vegetables.
