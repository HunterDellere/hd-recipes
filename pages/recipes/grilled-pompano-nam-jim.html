Grilled whole pompano with green nam jim

A whole golden pompano scored to the bone, oiled, salted, and stuffed with lemongrass and cilantro stems, then grilled over high heat until the skin chars and crisps and the buttery flesh just sets at the spine. Served with nam jim talay, a raw pounded sauce of garlic, Thai chili, cilantro root, lime, fish sauce, and palm sugar. In the pla pao register: the fish carries the char, the cold green sauce carries the heat and acid. Pompano's high even fat makes it nearly impossible to dry out on the grill.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Preheat the grill HARD before the fish goes near it. Charcoal: build a full chimney and let it ash over to a medium-high bed, hand held 12 cm above the grate for only 3 to 4 seconds. Gas: all burners high, lid down, 15 minutes, then scrape and oil the grate. Stovetop or broiler fallback: a cast-iron grill pan smoking over medium-high, or the broiler element fully preheated with a rack 10 cm below it. A cold or half-heated surface sticks to the skin and tears it; the fish must hit a screaming grate so the skin proteins set and release before they bond to the metal.
  • Pound the nam jim raw and ahead. The sauce is made cold in a mortar and only improves sitting 15 to 30 minutes while the flavors marry; build it first so it is ready when the fish comes off the grill. Do not cook it, heat flattens the raw garlic bite and drives off the lime's top notes.
  • Start jasmine rice FIRST as a parallel track. The fish grills 12 to 16 minutes and is best eaten straight off the fire; the rice (12 minute simmer plus 10 minute rest) should be finished and resting by the time the second side chars.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, fish prep

  1. 450 g whole golden pompano, gutted and scaled, gills removed, rinsed inside and out, blood line scraped from the spine cavity, patted bone-dry inside and out; three diagonal slashes per side cut 1 cm deep to the bone, from gill plate to tail
  2. 15 g neutral oil (rice bran, peanut, or canola), rubbed over the skin on both sides and into the slashes
  3. 5 g kosher salt, rubbed over the skin both sides, into the slashes, and inside the cavity
  4. 20 g lemongrass, lower stalk, tough outer layers peeled, bruised hard with the flat of a knife, halved lengthwise — (1 stalk; stuffed in the cavity to perfume the flesh from inside as it steams)
  5. 10 g cilantro stems, the leafy tops reserved for the herb plate; tough stems bruised and stuffed in the cavity — (½ bunch of stems; the cavity aromatics, leaves go to the table)

Phase 2, green nam jim (raw, pounded)

  1. 12 g garlic, peeled, roughly chopped — (4 cloves)
  2. 10 g fresh Thai chilies (green and red), stemmed, roughly chopped; seeds left in for full heat, scrape some out to dial it back — (5 to 8 chilies; the heat axis, adjust to tolerance)
  3. 6 g cilantro root (or lower stem), scrubbed and chopped; the root is the deep, earthy backbone of nam jim
  4. 12 g palm sugar, shaved fine or grated so it dissolves in the cold sauce
  5. 30 g fish sauce
  6. 45 g lime juice, fresh, from 2 to 3 limes, squeezed just before serving

Phase 3, herb plate and service

  1. 8 g cilantro leaves, torn, the leafy tops reserved from Phase 1, torn loose
  2. 100 g cucumber, sliced, cut into thin half-moons; a cooling raw side against the chili — (½ cucumber; the cooling counterpoint on the plate)
  3. 30 g lime wedges, cut from 1 lime — (1 lime in wedges; for squeezing over the fish at the table)

Execution

  1. 1
    Build the nam jim first so it can sit. In a granite mortar, pound the 12 g / 4 cloves garlic, the 6 g / 2 cilantro roots, and a pinch of the salt to a coarse paste, 30 seconds. Add the 10 g / 5 to 8 Thai chilies and pound just until broken and bruised, not pureed, so you keep flecks of chili. Work in the 12 g / 1 tbsp shaved palm sugar and pound until it dissolves into the paste.
  2. 2
    Slack the sauce. Stir the 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce into the mortar to dissolve and loosen the paste, then add the 45 g / 3 tbsp fresh lime juice last and off any heat. Taste and balance: the sauce should hit salty, sour, and hot in that order with the palm sugar only rounding the edges, never reading sweet. Adjust with a few more drops of lime for acid or fish sauce for salt. Set aside at room temperature to marry, 15 to 30 minutes.
  3. 3
    Preheat the grill hard while you prep the fish: a medium-high charcoal bed, gas at full with the lid down 15 minutes, or a cast-iron grill pan smoking over medium-high. Scrape the grate clean and wipe it with an oiled paper towel held in tongs, twice, so it is slick; a clean oiled grate at full heat is the only reliable defense against torn skin.
  4. 4
    Prep the fish. Verify the 450 g / 1 whole pompano is gutted, scaled, and gilled. Scrape the dark blood line from the inside of the spine cavity with a spoon (it carries the strongest fishy note), rinse, and pat bone-dry inside and out; surface water steams instead of chars and makes the skin stick. With a sharp knife cut three diagonal slashes per side, 1 cm deep to the bone, gill plate to tail. The slashes open the thick shoulder to the heat so it cooks in step with the thin belly and give the salt and oil a path into the flesh.
  5. 5
    Season and stuff. Rub the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil over both sides and into the slashes, then rub the 5 g / 1¼ tsp kosher salt over the skin, into the slashes, and inside the cavity. Tuck the 20 g / 1 bruised lemongrass stalk and the 10 g / cilantro stems into the cavity; bruising ruptures the lemongrass cells so its citral perfumes the flesh from inside as the cavity steams. Let the salted fish stand 5 minutes while the grill finishes heating; the brief cure firms the surface and dries the skin further.
  6. 6
    Lay the fish on the screaming grate at a 45 degree angle to the bars and do not move it. Grill the first side 6 to 8 minutes over medium-high, lid down on a gas or kettle grill to drive heat through the shoulder. The skin must set and release on its own; if it grips when you test with a thin spatula, it is not ready, give it another 30 to 60 seconds. The slashes should char at their edges and the skin should blister and crisp.
  7. 7
    Flip once. Slide a thin fish spatula fully under the fish, support the top with tongs, and turn it in one committed motion. Grill the second side 6 to 8 minutes, until the skin chars and the flesh in the deepest slash near the spine is opaque and lifts cleanly from the bone. A pompano at 450 to 500 g runs about 3 cm at the shoulder and needs roughly 14 to 16 minutes total; the even fat means a minute long is still moist, so err toward fully set at the spine rather than translucent.
  8. 8
    Rest and plate. Transfer the fish to a warm platter and rest 2 minutes; the residual heat finishes the spine and the juices redistribute. Discard the spent lemongrass and cilantro stems from the cavity. Scatter the 8 g / ¼ cup torn cilantro leaves over the fish, fan the 100 g / ½ cucumber half-moons and the 30 g / lime wedges alongside, and set the herb plate by the fish.
  9. 9
    Serve with the nam jim on the side, not poured over: the dish is the contrast of hot charred fish against the cold raw sauce, and pooling the sauce on the fish steams away its sharpness. Each diner pulls flesh from the slashes, spoons a little of the 12 g / garlic-and-chili nam jim over each bite, and squeezes a lime wedge at the table. Eat with jasmine rice, which catches the sauce and the charred fat.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion cooking surface→broiler or stovetop grill pan (no live fire)

    No grill: set an oven rack 10 cm below a fully preheated broiler and broil the fish on an oiled wire rack over a foil-lined pan, 6 to 7 minutes per side, until the skin blisters. Or sear in a cast-iron grill pan smoking over medium-high, 6 to 8 minutes per side, pressing gently for grate contact. Both give char marks and crisp skin; the broiler version misses the smoke but keeps the texture.

  • Regional aromatics→banana-leaf wrapped (the night-market pla pao)

    Stuff the cavity with the lemongrass and cilantro stems plus 2 bruised kaffir lime leaves and a few slices of galangal, pack the slashes with a lemongrass-salt paste, then wrap the whole fish in a softened banana leaf before grilling 9 to 11 minutes per side over a medium bed. The leaf steams the flesh and chars to perfume it; unwrap at the table.

  • Protein fish species→with whole branzino or black sea bass

    Swap the pompano for a 450 to 500 g whole branzino or black sea bass. Both are leaner (2 to 5% fat) so they dry faster on the grill; oil the skin more heavily, hold to the lower end of the time (5 to 6 minutes per side), and pull the moment the spine opaques. Match the grill time to the thickest point, not the species.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • golden pompano → butterfish, small bluefish, branzino, or whole trevally Butterfish (Pacific harvestfish) is the closest in flat shape and buttery fat for a whole grill, just smaller, so cut the time. Trevally and other jacks share pompano fat character and grill well. Branzino works but is leaner and dries faster. Bluefish is oilier and stronger; lean harder on the lime.
  • palm sugar → light brown sugar or granulated sugar Palm sugar has a faint caramel-coconut depth the others lack. Light brown sugar is the closest stand-in; granulated sugar works but reads flatter. Shave or dissolve fully so no grit remains in the raw sauce.
  • Thai chilies → serrano chilies Serranos are milder and grassier than bird's-eye Thai chilies; use about double the weight to reach the same heat, and accept a slightly less floral, more vegetal burn. Drop the seeds for a gentler sauce.
  • cilantro root → extra cilantro lower stem Cilantro root is the authentic earthy backbone but is rarely sold attached in US markets. The lower 5 cm of stem, packed and finely chopped, carries most of the same flavor; use about 2 tbsp in place of the roots.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → fish sauce make it yourselfstub A long anchovy-and-salt ferment yields a fish sauce with cleaner umami depth and less harsh salinity than most commercial bottles, and it is the dominant savory note in a raw nam jim where there is nowhere to hide a flat sauce.

Notes

Why whole, and why pompano. Grilling the fish whole is structural, not decorative. The skin shields the flesh from the direct radiant heat and renders its subcutaneous fat into the meat as it crisps, the bone slows the heat into the shoulder so the thick part finishes near the moment the belly does, and the cavity, stuffed with lemongrass and cilantro stems, becomes a small steam chamber that perfumes the flesh from inside. Pompano is the canonical fish here for the same reason it suits the steamer and the parchment packet: at 8 to 12% evenly distributed, monounsaturated-leaning fat, the flesh self-bastes as its proteins contract, so the doneness window is wide and the aggressive grill heat never tips it into chalk. A lean branzino on the same fire dries the moment you look away; a pompano held a minute long is still moist. The fish is, in effect, nearly impossible to dry out on the grill, which is exactly what the high heat of live fire demands.

The temperature split is the whole dish. Hot, charred, fatty fish meets a cold, raw, sharp sauce, and the contrast is the point. Nam jim talay is built cold and stays cold: garlic, cilantro root, and chili pounded to a paste, dissolved palm sugar, then fish sauce for salt and umami and a generous hit of lime for acid, added last so its volatile top notes stay bright. Heating the sauce, or pooling it on the hot fish, flattens the raw garlic bite and steams off the lime, collapsing the contrast into something muddy. Serve it on the side and let each bite of char meet the cold sauce fresh.

The umami and acid axes. The glutamate source is the fish sauce, free glutamate and the nucleotides that multiply it, which is the savory spine of the raw sauce and the reason a flat or harsh bottle ruins the dish. The finishing acid is the lime juice, added at the very end and reinforced by the wedges at the table. Salt runs on two tracks: the kosher salt cured into the skin and slashes, and the fish sauce in the sauce. Season the fish itself properly, because the nam jim is a condiment and cannot rescue an underseasoned fish; it can only add the layer of heat, acid, and pungency that the char alone does not carry.

The grill mechanics. Sticking is the only real failure mode, and it is almost always a cold or dirty grate. Skin proteins bond to bare metal until they set, then release; a fully preheated, scraped, twice-oiled grate plus a fish whose skin is bone-dry and lightly oiled is the defense. Lay the fish down and leave it: every early nudge tears the skin you are trying to crisp. Test for release with a thin spatula, and if it grips, wait. The slashes do double duty, opening the shoulder to the heat and giving a visual doneness read, since the flesh inside them opaques first. A fish-shaped grill basket makes the flip foolproof if you are nervous, at the cost of slightly less direct char.

Sourcing the whole fish. Golden pompano (Trachinotus blochii, usually farmed) sits whole on ice at H Mart, 99 Ranch, and most Asian seafood counters in the 300 to 600 g range, a single fish for two. Look for clear domed eyes, bright silver flanks with the gold belly wash intact (it fades to gray as the fish ages, the species own freshness clock), tight scales, no gummy slime, and a clean ocean smell. Have the counter scale, gut, and de-gill it; verify all three at home and scrape the blood line yourself. Grill it the day of purchase. If only fillets are available the dish loses the cavity-aromatic step and the bone-buffered cook, so it is worth seeking the whole fish for this preparation specifically.
