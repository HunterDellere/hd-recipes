Shakshuka

The North African and Levantine one-pan dinner: eggs poached directly into a spiced tomato-pepper sauce, cumin and caraway and paprika built into a sofrito of charred bell pepper and onion, finished with crumbled feta and fresh herbs and served in the pan with grilled bread for sopping. Structurally a tomato-poach where the egg whites set into the sauce and the yolks stay liquid until broken at the table. The defining shared dish of Tunisian, Israeli, and broader eastern Mediterranean breakfast and dinner cookery.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Bread is the partner; the dish is built to be sopped, not eaten with a fork. Toast or grill pita, Tunisian flatbread, sourdough, or country loaf in the last 5 minutes of the egg poach so it hits the table warm at the same time as the pan.
  • Use a wide skillet (10 to 12 inch) so the eggs poach in distinct wells. A narrow pan crowds the yolks together; they overcook into each other instead of staying liquid and bright.
  • Crack each egg into a small bowl first, then slide into the well. Cracking directly into the sauce risks shell shards and uneven yolk placement, both of which the cook cannot fix once the egg starts to set.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the sofrito base

  1. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  2. 150 g yellow onion, small dice (about 5 mm cubes) — (1 medium onion)
  3. 150 g red bell pepper, small dice (about 5 mm cubes); seeds and ribs removed — (1 medium red bell pepper; the red color is structural for the canonical visual; yellow or orange peppers work)
  4. 12 g garlic clove, peeled, finely minced — (3 medium cloves)

Phase 2, the spice bloom

  1. 3 g cumin seeds, lightly crushed with the side of a knife or in a mortar (not fully ground; the crushed seeds bloom into the oil)
  2. 2 g caraway seeds, lightly crushed with the cumin
  3. 3 g smoked paprika (pimentón de la Vera, dulce)
  4. 15 g harissa (homemade or quality commercial)

Phase 3, the tomato base

  1. 1 × 28 oz / 794 g can whole peeled tomatoes (San Marzano, canned), crushed by hand into rough chunks (smaller than a walnut; keep texture) — , see substitutions)
  2. 4 g fine sea salt
  3. 3 g granulated sugar

Phase 4, the eggs and finish

  1. 200 g large eggs, cracked one at a time into a small bowl before sliding into the sauce (single-bowl check catches a broken yolk before it hits the pan) — (4 large eggs at room temperature; cold eggs from the refrigerator take longer to set and risk overcooking the sauce around them; bring to room temperature 30 minutes before cooking)
  2. 60 g feta cheese (Greek or Bulgarian, in brine), crumbled into small chunks (about 1 cm)
  3. 8 g fresh cilantro (or flat-leaf parsley), roughly chopped
  4. 10 g extra-virgin olive oil, for finishing

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil in a wide 25 to 30 cm (10 to 12 inch) heavy skillet (cast iron, carbon steel, or stainless; nonstick is acceptable but reads less Maillard-developed) over medium heat. Add the 150 g / 1 onion (diced) and 150 g / 1 bell pepper (diced); sweat 8 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the onion is translucent-gold and the pepper is fully softened but not browned. Do not rush this step; the slow sweat is where the sofrito base develops its structural sweetness.
  2. 2
    Push the vegetables to one side of the pan; in the cleared space, add the 12 g / 3 garlic cloves (minced), the 3 g / 1½ tsp crushed cumin seeds, and the 2 g / 1 tsp crushed caraway seeds. Toast 30 seconds until fragrant. Stir into the vegetables. Add the 3 g / 1½ tsp smoked paprika and the 15 g / 1 tbsp harissa; cook 60 seconds, stirring, until the paprika blooms into the fat (the oil will turn deep orange-red, structurally the visual signal that the spices have integrated).
  3. 3
    Add the 400 g / 1 can crushed tomatoes, 4 g / ¾ tsp salt, and 3 g / ½ tsp sugar; stir to combine and bring to a steady simmer. Reduce heat to medium-low; simmer uncovered 6 to 8 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the sauce has thickened noticeably (visibly reduced by about a third, the surface oils starting to separate at the edges, the sauce holding a spoon-drag mark briefly before flowing back together). Taste and adjust salt and harissa.
  4. 4
    Use the back of a spoon to create 4 shallow wells in the sauce (roughly evenly spaced). Crack the 200 g / 4 eggs one at a time into a small bowl, then slide each egg gently into its well. Season each yolk with a pinch (about 1 g total / a small pinch per egg) of fine sea salt. Reduce heat to low. Cover the pan with a tight-fitting lid; cook 4 to 6 minutes, checking at 4 minutes, until the whites are fully set (no visible translucent gel) and the yolks are still jammy-soft (a gentle nudge with a spoon shows the yolk still wobbling under a thin set film). The window between perfect and overcooked is short; pull immediately when the whites have just set.
  5. 5
    Off the heat, scatter the 60 g / ¼ cup crumbled feta over the sauce (between and around the eggs, not directly on the yolks); the residual pan heat warms the feta without melting it. Sprinkle the 8 g / 2 tbsp chopped cilantro over the top. Drizzle with the 10 g / 2 tsp finishing olive oil. Serve immediately in the pan, with grilled or toasted bread (Lebanese pita, Tunisian flatbread, sourdough, or country loaf) for sopping the sauce and breaking the yolks; each diner breaks their yolk into the sauce and scoops with bread.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as Turkish menemen (eggs scrambled into the sauce instead of poached on top)

    Skip the well-and-poach technique; instead, after the tomato base has thickened (end of step 3), reduce heat to medium-low and pour the beaten eggs (lightly beaten with a pinch of salt, not fully scrambled) directly into the sauce. Stir continuously with a wooden spoon for 90 seconds to 2 minutes until the eggs are softly set into curds throughout the sauce. The result is structurally menemen, the Turkish breakfast egg dish; reads softer and more integrated than shakshuka, with the eggs and sauce as a single texture rather than separate components.

  • Occasion green shakshuka→as green shakshuka (with leafy greens instead of tomato)

    Skip the tomato (and the sugar). Add 300 g of mixed tender greens (spinach, chard, dandelion, kale) in the place of the tomato; wilt 4 minutes after the spice bloom, then add 60 ml vegetable stock or water. Add a 30 g squeeze of lemon juice with the salt. Proceed with the eggs as in step 4. The result is a brilliant green shakshuka, a modern Israeli variant; reads brighter and more vegetal; pairs especially well with crumbled goat cheese instead of feta.

  • Protein as a more substantial dinner→with merguez sausage or chickpeas added to the sauce base

    For merguez: brown 200 g of casing-removed merguez sausage in the pan with the olive oil before the sofrito, breaking up with a wooden spoon; reserve the meat, sweat the vegetables in the rendered fat, and return the meat at the tomato addition. For chickpeas (vegetarian): add 200 g of drained cooked chickpeas with the tomato. Both make the shakshuka a complete dinner rather than a breakfast or light meal.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • harissa (homemade or quality commercial) → 5 g / 1 tsp red pepper flakes plus 5 g / 1 tsp tomato paste plus 1 g / ½ tsp ground caraway Harissa is the structural chili-and-depth base for shakshuka; the substitution above approximates the chili-and-caraway register but reads less complex. Worth making (or buying) real harissa for this dish; the depth difference is immediately obvious.
  • whole peeled tomatoes (San Marzano, canned) → 500 g ripe summer tomatoes, charred and peeled Char 4 to 5 ripe tomatoes (Roma, San Marzano, or a meaty heirloom) directly over a gas flame or under a broiler until the skins blister and blacken (6 to 8 minutes); cool slightly, peel, roughly chop. Skip the sugar. The summer-tomato version reads brighter and slightly less concentrated; the canned-tomato version reads deeper and more reliable across seasons. Use whichever is in season.
  • feta cheese (Greek or Bulgarian, in brine) → crumbled goat cheese or fresh ricotta salata Feta is the canonical Israeli-adoption finishing cheese; goat cheese reads slightly sharper and is acceptable; ricotta salata reads milder and saltier and is canonical in some Italian-influenced preparations. Pure ricotta (without the salata) reads too soft and watery for this application.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → harissa (homemade or quality commercial) make it yourself Home-pounded harissa from dried chilies, roasted red pepper, toasted caraway-coriander-cumin, garlic, and olive oil reads dramatically more aromatic than commercial paste; the spice depth and the integrated chili flavor carry directly into the shakshuka sauce.
  • → whole peeled tomatoes (San Marzano, canned) make it yourselfstub Out of season when fresh tomatoes are weak, slow-roasted concentrated tomato paste at home reads cleaner than canned tomatoes and pairs naturally with shakshuka; use 200 g paste plus 200 ml water in place of the canned tomatoes.

Notes

Shakshuka is one of the most structurally satisfying egg dishes in any cuisine because it integrates three separate techniques into a single pan: a slow sofrito (the structural sweetness base), a spice bloom (the aromatic depth), and a covered egg poach (the cooking technique that holds the eggs at the perfect texture). The dish is forgiving in many directions (the spice ratios are flexible, the harissa is adjustable, the cheese is optional) but precise in one: the egg cook is the variable that separates a great shakshuka from a disappointing one. Overcooked yolks turn the dish from a structural egg-poach into a baked egg casserole; undercooked whites read raw and unappealing. The 4 to 6 minute covered cook on low heat, with a check at 4 minutes, is the structural reference; trust the visual cues (set whites, wobbling yolks under a thin film) more than the timer.

The harissa is structural. The single defining ingredient that distinguishes Tunisian shakshuka from a generic North African egg-and-tomato dish is the harissa: the chili-and-caraway-and-coriander paste that brings the canonical heat and aromatic depth. Without harissa (or a careful substitution that includes caraway specifically) the dish is recognizable as a shakshuka-adjacent preparation but reads flatter and less identifiable. Homemade harissa is dramatically better; quality commercial harissa from a Tunisian or Israeli producer is the right next step; cheap harissa from a generic supermarket is often more red food coloring than chili paste.

Versatility. Shakshuka is structurally a breakfast-or-dinner one-pan egg dish, but the sauce base travels onto a wider range of applications. Use the same sofrito-and-spice base (without the eggs) as: a stew base for poaching white fish (cod, halibut, branzino; reduce the harissa to 8 g for fish); a sauce for grilled chicken thighs (brown the chicken first, then build the sauce around it); a flavor base for chickpea or white-bean stews; a side-sauce for grilled merguez or lamb kebabs; a topping for grain bowls (farro, freekeh, bulgur) with grilled vegetables; a stir-in for shakshuka pasta (toss the sauce with cooked pasta, then crack eggs on top and broil 3 minutes to set); a topping for crispy roasted potatoes (the canonical Israeli late-night dish); and as a flavor base for vegetable braises (zucchini, eggplant, fennel; add the vegetables with the tomato and braise 15 minutes).

Pairs across Tunisian (canonical), Israeli (canonical breakfast adoption), broader Levantine, Egyptian, Moroccan (variations on the same structural dish), contemporary American Middle Eastern, modern European Mediterranean cookery, and contemporary brunch cookery globally.

Storage. The sauce base (without eggs) keeps 4 days refrigerated and freezes 2 months; reheat the sauce in the same pan, create wells, and crack fresh eggs in for each service. Leftover finished shakshuka (with cooked eggs) reads acceptable cold or reheated at low heat 3 minutes, but the egg texture degrades meaningfully; the dish is best made fresh and served immediately from the pan.

Sources
  • The Tunisian and broader Maghreb canon; the modern dish carried by North African Jewish migration into Israel in the mid-20th century where it became a national breakfast and dinner staple; now structurally embedded in contemporary Middle Eastern, Israeli, and Mediterranean cookery worldwide. — Two structural schools: the Tunisian original uses the harissa-and-spice register (caraway, cumin, smoked paprika, harissa paste) with a tomato-and-pepper base; the Israeli adoption added feta as a finishing crumble and made the dish breakfast-coded rather than dinner-coded. The version here combines both: the Tunisian structural spice base with the Israeli feta finish. The defining technique is the egg poach (eggs cracked directly into wells in the simmering sauce; the whites set into the sauce and the yolks stay liquid). Distinct from huevos rancheros (egg-on-tortilla architecture rather than egg-in-sauce) and from menemen (Turkish eggs scrambled into the sauce rather than poached on top).
