Tacos al pastor

The Mexico City taco that married Lebanese shawarma to Mexican adobo: pork shoulder marinated in guajillo, ancho, achiote, pineapple juice, and warm spices, traditionally cooked on a vertical trompo with a crowning pineapple, reformatted here for the home kitchen as a thin-sliced skillet-seared preparation. Served on small warm corn tortillas with cilantro, white onion, salsa verde, and a charred pineapple finish.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The pork needs a 12-hour marinade for the adobo to penetrate and for the pineapple enzyme to soften the meat. Plan ahead by a day; the marinade is fast (15 minutes) but the rest is structural.
  • Slice the pork against the grain at 4 to 5 mm thickness. The vertical trompo version cooks shaved layers; the home-skillet version mimics that by searing thin slices to maximize Maillard surface area.
  • Have the garnish staged before searing: cilantro chopped, white onion diced fine, salsa verde in a small bowl, lime wedges cut. The pork sears fast (2 to 3 minutes total per batch) and the assembly needs to be immediate.
  • Warm the corn tortillas on a dry comal just before serving (15 to 20 seconds per side). Cold tortillas crack when folded around hot taco filling.

Phase 1, the adobo marinade

  2. 15 g dried ancho chilies, stems and seeds removed, toasted — (about 2 anchos; raisin-sweet depth)

Phase 3, the pineapple finish

Phase 4, the taco assembly

  1. 240 g corn tortillas (4 inch / 10 cm street-taco size) — (12 tortillas; 2 per taco if doubled in taquería style, or 1 per taco for home)
  2. 20 g fresh cilantro, leaves and tender stems, finely chopped
  3. 80 g white onion (for taco garnish), very finely diced (2 mm) — (about ⅓ medium onion; the structural raw bite)
  4. 60 g limes, cut into wedges — (2 limes, 8 wedges)

Execution

  1. 1
    Phase 1, build the adobo. Toast the 30 g guajillo and 15 g ancho chilies (stems and seeds removed) in a dry skillet over medium-low heat 30 seconds per side until fragrant. Cover with 250 ml boiling water; soak 20 minutes until pliable. Toast the 3 g cumin seeds and 0.5 g cloves in the same skillet 30 seconds; transfer. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  2. 2
    Blend the adobo. Drain the soaked chilies; transfer to a blender with 100 g of the soaking liquid. Add the 20 g chipotle in adobo, 25 g achiote paste, 120 g pineapple juice, 30 g white vinegar, 60 g orange juice, 20 g garlic, 80 g chopped onion, 2 g oregano, the toasted cumin and cloves, 12 g salt, and 30 g neutral oil. Blend 2 minutes to a smooth thick adobo. Pass through a fine-mesh sieve into a bowl, pressing with a spatula (structural for smooth coating on the pork).
  3. 3
    Marinate the pork. Place the 900 g sliced pork in a large bowl or zip-top bag; pour over the adobo. Mix to coat each slice. Refrigerate 12 hours minimum, up to 24 hours; the marinade penetrates over the long rest and the pineapple bromelain tenderizes the muscle surface. Do not exceed 24 hours; the bromelain over-tenderizes and the meat reads mushy.12 h
  4. 4
    Phase 2, sear the pork. Pull the pork from the refrigerator 30 minutes before cooking; bring to cool room temperature. Heat 15 g of the 30 g neutral oil in a large heavy skillet (cast iron is canonical) over high heat until smoking. Working in batches (do not crowd; crowding steams instead of sears), add a single layer of pork slices to the pan. Sear undisturbed 90 seconds until the surface is deeply charred. Flip; sear 60 to 90 seconds on the second side. Transfer to a cutting board; tent loosely with foil. Repeat with the remaining pork, adding more oil as needed (about 2 batches total). Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Phase 3, char the pineapple. In the same skillet (still hot, with the rendered fat from the pork), add the 200 g diced pineapple in a single layer; sear undisturbed 90 seconds until deeply charred on one side. Stir and continue 60 seconds until the pineapple is caramelized on multiple sides but still holds its shape. The pineapple should read sweet-smoky-charred, not stewed. Transfer to a small bowl. Maillard sear
  6. 6
    Chop the pork. While the pineapple chars, chop the seared pork into 1 cm dice on the cutting board. This mimics the trompo-shaved texture (small, charred-edged pieces with tender interior). The chopping is structural; whole-slice tacos do not eat as the al pastor experience.
  7. 7
    Phase 4, warm the tortillas and assemble. Warm the 240 g corn tortillas (12 tortillas) on a dry comal or in a clean skillet over medium-high heat 15 to 20 seconds per side; stack and cover with a clean kitchen towel to keep warm and pliable. Build each taco: lay a warm tortilla flat, mound about 50 g of chopped pork in the center, top with a small pinch of charred pineapple, a generous pinch of 20 g chopped cilantro and 80 g diced raw onion. Spoon a teaspoon of 150 g salsa verde over the top. Serve immediately with the 60 g lime wedges on the side. The diner squeezes lime over each taco at the table; the lime is the structural finishing acid that completes the dish.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→as al pastor chicken thighs

    Replace the pork with 900 g boneless skinless chicken thighs (left whole or sliced into strips). Marinate the same 12 hours. Sear on a hot skillet 4 to 5 minutes per side until the surface is deeply charred and the internal temperature is 74°C / 165°F. Chop and assemble as for the pork version. The result is structurally al pastor with chicken; lighter, faster, and slightly less fatty than the pork canon, popular in Mexico City for cooks who avoid pork.

  • Occasion cuisine pivot→as al pastor on the grill (the closest home approximation to the trompo)

    Skewer the marinated pork slices vertically on long metal or bamboo skewers (presoaked); intersperse with thick rings of pineapple every 4 to 5 slices. Grill over high direct heat 6 to 8 minutes, rotating every 60 seconds, until the exterior is deeply charred. Pull the skewers, slice off thin shavings horizontally from the outside (mimicking the trompo shave). The grilled version develops more structural smoke than the skillet and is the closest home approximation to the taquería trompo experience.

  • Occasion as a tortilla-free dish→as a taco bowl with rice and beans

    Serve the chopped al pastor pork over Mexican rice (cooked in tomato puree) with refried black beans, charred pineapple, cilantro, white onion, salsa verde, and lime. The structural flavors of al pastor work beautifully outside the tortilla format; the bowl version is the canonical post-tortilla shortage adaptation and is a brilliant lunch.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • achiote paste (annatto) → 5 g smoked paprika plus 5 g ground cumin plus 2 g ground turmeric Achiote paste (annatto) provides the structural orange-red color and earthy aromatic of al pastor. The smoked paprika and turmeric substitute approximates the color and adds smoke depth; the dish reads slightly less authentic but is mechanically functional. Look for achiote paste at any Mexican grocery (Yucatecan brand El Yucateco is widely distributed) before resorting to the substitute.
  • fresh pineapple juice → canned pineapple juice (unsweetened) Fresh pineapple juice contains active bromelain (the enzyme that tenderizes the pork) and produces a slightly more tender final product. Canned juice has been heat-pasteurized and the bromelain is denatured; the marinade still penetrates and the dish reads correctly, just slightly firmer. Use the same volume.
  • pork shoulder (Boston butt) → pork loin or pork tenderloin Loin and tenderloin are leaner and cook faster than shoulder; the dish reads slightly drier and less rich. The shoulder is the canonical cut for al pastor specifically because the fat self-bastes the meat through the sear. If using loin or tenderloin, do not exceed 6 hours of marinade (the leaner muscle takes the marinade faster and turns mushy beyond that).

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → salsa verde make it yourselfstub A fresh raw tomatillo salsa (tomatillos, serrano, cilantro, garlic, lime, salt, blended) takes 5 minutes and reads dramatically brighter than jarred salsa verde. For al pastor specifically, where the salsa is one of the structural flavors of the taco, the homemade version is worth the small time investment.
  • → corn tortillas (4 inch / 10 cm street-taco size) make it yourself Fresh corn tortillas pressed from masa harina dough produce a tortilla with structural integrity, deep corn flavor, and a soft pliable texture that holds the al pastor filling without cracking. For tacos specifically, where the tortilla is half the dish, the homemade version is dramatically better than the supermarket version.

Notes

Tacos al pastor are the most cross-cultural dish in the Mexican canon. The vertical-spit cooking technique comes from Lebanese shawarma (introduced to Mexico City by Lebanese immigrants in the 1930s); the adobo marinade is pure Mexican chili-and-citrus tradition; the pineapple crown is a Mexico City taqueria innovation that bridges the two. The resulting dish is a structural Lebanese-Mexican hybrid that has become one of the most recognized Mexican dishes globally, a useful reminder that authentic cuisine is always a living tradition rather than a museum piece.

The home version cannot replicate the trompo experience. The vertical spit cooks the pork in self-basting layers over 4 to 6 hours, with the outermost layer continuously crisping and being shaved off. The home skillet version compresses this into a fast high-heat sear of thin slices, which captures the Maillard char and the adobo flavor but misses the structural rotisserie character. The compromise is honest; tacos al pastor at home are not the same as tacos al pastor at El Tizoncito or El Vilsito in Mexico City, but the structural flavors transfer and the home version is dramatically better than skipping the dish entirely.

The pineapple does double work. The fresh pineapple juice in the marinade contributes bromelain, an enzyme that breaks down protein and tenderizes the pork surface; this is structural to the al pastor texture and is the main reason the trompo pineapple sits at the top of the spit (the juice drips down through the pork as it cooks). The charred pineapple garnish adds a structural sweet-smoky note that contrasts with the chili-rich pork. Both functions are non-negotiable for the canonical al pastor experience; skipping the pineapple in either form produces a recognizably different dish.

The slice-thin-and-sear-hot technique is the home-cook workaround for the missing trompo char. Slicing the pork at 4 to 5 mm against the grain maximizes the Maillard surface area; searing in a smoking-hot skillet for 90 seconds per side develops the structural char without overcooking the interior. Slicing thicker (1 cm and up) produces pork that cannot develop the al pastor surface character in the sear time; slicing too thin (under 3 mm) overcooks instantly and reads as jerky.

The chop-after-sear step is structural to the eating experience. Restaurant tacos al pastor consist of small irregular chunks of pork (the shaved trompo pieces) with charred edges and tender interiors. Whole-slice tacos do not eat the same way; the chopped texture allows the salsa, pineapple, cilantro, and onion to integrate into each bite and produces the characteristic flavor density of the dish. Chop the pork on the cutting board with a heavy chef knife into 1 cm pieces immediately after pulling from the skillet.

Versatility. The al pastor flavor architecture (achiote, chili adobo, pineapple, citrus) travels onto many other preparations. Use the same marinade and technique for chicken thighs, pork tenderloin, shrimp (marinate 30 minutes only, grill 2 minutes per side), or firm tofu (marinate 4 hours, pan-sear). The same flavor base works as a marinade for grilled vegetables (especially summer squash, eggplant, and bell pepper) and as the structural braising liquid for cochinita pibil (the Yucatecan whole-pork-shoulder banana-leaf braise).

Storage. The adobo marinade keeps 1 week refrigerated and freezes 3 months; make a double batch and freeze half for the next al pastor session. Marinated raw pork keeps 24 hours refrigerated (no longer, due to bromelain over-tenderization); freezes 1 month in the marinade. Cooked al pastor pork keeps 4 days refrigerated; reheat in a hot skillet (do not microwave, the texture turns rubbery) and reassemble fresh.
