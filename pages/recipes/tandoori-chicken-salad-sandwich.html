Tandoori chicken salad sandwich (Hunter's version)

Poached chicken thigh shredded fine, bound with a tandoori-masala-spiced mayo, layered with crisp celery, grated carrot, and toasted walnuts, and served on a buttered toasted croissant. The American chicken-salad-sandwich form re-engineered through a Punjabi spice base; the tandoori-mayo is the structural pivot that makes the dish.

Execution

  1. 1
    Poach the chicken. Combine the 2000 ml water, 20 g salt, 2 bay leaves, 3 g peppercorns, 15 g sliced ginger, and 12 g smashed garlic in a 4-quart saucepan. Bring to a low simmer (about 80 °C, surface tremor not bubbling). Add the 600 g chicken thigh in a single layer; submerge by 2 cm. Adjust heat to maintain the bare simmer; poach 18 to 22 minutes until the chicken reaches an internal temperature of 75 °C and the texture is just firm to the touch. Boiling rather than simmering toughens the proteins and produces dry, stringy chicken; the temperature window matters.
  2. 2
    Cool the chicken in the poaching liquid. Pull the saucepan off the heat. Let the chicken rest in the warm poaching liquid for 10 minutes; the carryover cook plus the slow cooling produces a more uniformly tender, more deeply seasoned final result than yanking the chicken out hot. Remove the chicken to a cutting board; cool 5 minutes more before shredding.
  3. 3
    Whisk the tandoori mayo. In a medium bowl, whisk the 200 g mayonnaise, 60 g yogurt, 12 g tandoori masala, 15 ml lemon juice, 1 g garlic powder, 3 g salt, 1 g black pepper, and 1 g optional cayenne until smooth and uniformly orange-pink in color. Let stand 10 minutes for the spices to bloom into the mayo (the dry spices need time to hydrate and integrate; freshly-mixed tandoori mayo reads dustier and less integrated than rested). Taste; the mayo should taste assertively spiced, slightly tangy, with the chili and cumin reading clearly.
  4. 4
    Toast the walnuts. Spread the 50 g walnuts on a small dry skillet over medium heat. Toast 4 minutes, shaking every 60 seconds, until the walnuts smell deeply nutty and have darkened a shade. Pull from heat immediately; walnuts go from toasted to bitter in a 90-second window. Transfer to a cutting board; cool 2 minutes; chop roughly into 5 mm pieces.
  5. 5
    Shred the chicken. Tear the cooled poached chicken into rough 1.5 cm pieces with two forks or with your hands. The torn-by-hand texture (irregular shapes, varying piece sizes) produces a better salad mouthfeel than the uniform fine dice some recipes specify; the salad reads more substantial and less industrially processed.
  6. 6
    Assemble the salad. In a large mixing bowl, combine the shredded chicken, 100 g diced celery, 80 g grated carrot, 50 g toasted chopped walnuts, 8 g chopped cilantro, and 30 g cold-water-soaked diced red onion (drained). Pour the rested tandoori mayo over the top. Fold gently with a rubber spatula until everything is uniformly coated; do not over-mix or the chicken will break down further and the salad will read as a paste rather than as bound shreds. Taste; adjust salt, lemon juice, and tandoori masala if needed. Refrigerate 15 minutes for the flavors to integrate; longer resting produces an even more cohesive salad and is recommended if time allows.
  7. 7
    Toast and butter the croissants. Slice each croissant horizontally; place cut-side up on a sheet pan. Toast under the broiler 90 seconds (watch closely; croissants burn fast) until the cut surfaces are golden and crisp. Spread the warm cut surface of each croissant half with about 7 g (1½ tsp) of softened butter; the heat melts the butter into the toasted surface and creates a moisture-and-flavor barrier between the bread and the salad.
  8. 8
    Build and serve. Place a large lettuce leaf on each croissant bottom. Mound about 150 g of the chicken salad onto each leaf-lined croissant bottom, distributing evenly. Cap with the croissant top. Slice diagonally if serving plated, or wrap in parchment for handheld eating. Serve immediately; toasted croissants soften within 15 minutes of assembly, and the textural contrast between crisp croissant and creamy salad is the structural eating experience.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→rotisserie chicken (the weeknight shortcut)

    Skip the poach entirely. Shred the meat from a 700 g whole rotisserie chicken (about 500 g of pulled meat); use directly. The salad reads slightly less seasoned (the poach contributes salt to the chicken interior); compensate by adding 1 g additional salt to the tandoori mayo. Total time drops from 75 minutes to 25.

  • Occasion bread→toasted brioche bun (the burger-form pivot)

    Substitute 4 brioche burger buns for the croissants. Toast cut-side-down in a hot dry skillet for 90 seconds. The dish reads as a burger-form chicken-salad sandwich; lighter than the croissant version, more substantial bread-to-salad ratio. Add a slice of red onion and a slice of tomato to each bun for textural variety.

  • Dietary occasion→lettuce wraps (the low-carb version)

    Replace the croissants with 8 large butter lettuce or romaine leaves (2 per serving). Mound about 75 g of the salad into each leaf and roll like a taco. The dish reads as a low-carb lunch and the lettuce contributes textural crunch the croissant version lacks. Pairs well with a small bowl of soup.

  • Regional flavor→tikka-masala-leaning version (with tomato)

    Add 2 tbsp tomato paste and 1 tbsp ground cashews (soaked 30 minutes and pureed smooth) to the tandoori mayo. The salad reads as a tikka-masala-flavored chicken salad rather than tandoori-flavored; deeper, sweeter, more complex but less direct. Pairs well with naan as the bread vehicle rather than croissant.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • tandoori masala → garam masala plus Kashmiri chili powder plus extra cumin Mix 6 g garam masala, 4 g Kashmiri chili powder, 2 g ground cumin to substitute 12 g tandoori masala. The flavor is distinctly different (more cinnamon-and-clove forward, less Kashmiri-chili dominant) but in the same Punjabi-spice family. Acceptable as a workaround; the structural identity of the dish leans toward tikka rather than tandoori.
  • walnuts → toasted cashews or sliced almonds Cashews lean the salad sweeter and richer; almonds lean cleaner and crispier. Both are functional 1:1 by weight. Cashews are the closer match to the South Asian flavor profile.
  • chicken thigh → chicken breast Reduce poach time to 14 minutes and pull at internal temperature 65 °C. The salad reads cleaner and lighter; loses some of the depth that thighs contribute. See ingredients/chicken-thigh for the full discussion.
  • croissants → sourdough bread or thick-cut country loaf Toast slices in a hot pan with 5 g butter per slice; assemble open-faced or as a closed sandwich. The dish reads as a more substantial deli-style sandwich; pair with a green salad or potato chips.

Notes

Why poach, not roast or sauté. The chicken-salad form depends on shredded chicken with no Maillard browning, the dish wants pure poached-chicken flavor as the canvas for the spiced mayo. Roasted or sautéed chicken brings caramelized notes that fight with the tandoori spice profile and produce a salad that reads as a roasted-chicken sandwich rather than a chicken salad. Poaching is the structural technique; the mild flavor of poached chicken is the point.

Why bloom the spices in the mayo. Tandoori masala is a dry spice blend that needs hydration time to develop its full flavor. Mixing the masala into the mayo and resting 10 minutes lets the dry chili powder, cumin, and other spices hydrate against the mayo''s water phase; the resulting mayo tastes more integrated, more deeply spiced, and less dustily-superficial than freshly-mixed mayo used immediately. The same rule applies to any dry spice in any cool emulsion (yogurt-based dressings, sour-cream dips, Greek-yogurt sauces). Ten minutes is the minimum; thirty produces an even better integration.

Why yogurt in the mayo. The tang from a small dose of yogurt (60 g into 200 g mayo) is structural to the dish identity. Pure mayo reads as flat and one-dimensional under the high spice load; yogurt''s lactic acid lifts the spice and brightens the overall flavor profile. The yogurt also references the tandoori-marinade tradition, where yogurt is the canonical spice-carrying medium. The 30 percent ratio (yogurt:mayo) is the kitchen-tested floor that gives the tang without going so far that the salad reads as yogurt-bound rather than mayo-bound.

The cold-water onion soak. Raw red onion has a sharp, sulfurous bite that can dominate a chicken salad. Soaking the diced onion in cold water for 5 minutes leaches out a meaningful fraction of the volatile sulfur compounds and produces a milder, sweeter onion that integrates with the salad rather than punctuating it. Drain thoroughly before adding to the salad. The same technique applies to any raw onion in any cold preparation, sandwiches, salads, tartines, ceviche.

Why day-old croissants. Fresh-from-the-bakery croissants are too tender and too laminated-flaky to hold up under the chicken salad, the salad''s moisture migrates into the layers and the croissant goes soggy in 5 minutes. Day-old croissants have firmed up, the layers have set, and the toasted-and-buttered preparation creates a structural crisp surface that holds the salad without falling apart. The freshness sweet spot is 24 to 48 hours after baking; older croissants lose flavor and start tasting stale.

The textural rule. Three textural elements define the dish: tender shredded chicken, crisp celery and carrot, and crunchy toasted walnut. Skip any one of the three and the salad reads as monotone. The celery and walnut both function structurally, celery provides cool crisp moisture, walnut provides hot crunch. Substituting both with cucumber or both with seeds compromises the textural architecture; substituting one with another acceptable crunch (sliced almonds for walnut, jicama for celery) is fine.

Hunter''s household standard ratio. The mayo:chicken ratio of 200 g mayo per 600 g chicken (roughly 1:3 by weight) is the lower end of what reads as "bound chicken salad" and the higher end of what reads as "lightly dressed shredded chicken." More mayo and the salad reads as soggy and heavy; less and the chicken reads as dry and underseasoned. Adjust by 10 g either direction based on whether you''re serving on bread (lower mayo, the bread holds moisture) or on lettuce wraps (higher mayo, no bread to absorb).

Make-ahead and storage. The chicken salad holds 3 days refrigerated in a sealed container and actually improves overnight as the spices fully integrate. Do not assemble the sandwiches more than 30 minutes before serving; the toasted croissants soften under the salad''s moisture and the sandwich loses its structural integrity. Pack the components separately for a brown-bag lunch and assemble at the table.
