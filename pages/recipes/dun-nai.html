Vanilla dun nai (steamed milk custard)

Cantonese steamed milk custard with vanilla bean — silken, barely set, and finished in a Dutch oven using residual heat to avoid honeycomb.

2 servings25 min total10 min activemediumChinesedessert
vegetarian

Mise en Place

Servings

Custard

  1. 240 ml whole milk — (1 cup; whole only — skim won't set silken)
  2. 2 each large eggs
  3. 5 g vanilla bean paste — (1 tsp)
  4. 25 g granulated sugar — (2 tbsp)
  5. 0.5 g fine sea salt — (pinch)

Execution

  1. 1
    Combine milk, sugar, salt, and vanilla paste in a small saucepan. Heat until hot to the touch (60°C / 140°F) and the sugar dissolves. Do not boil. You should smell the vanilla bloom and see the seeds suspend.3 min
  2. 2
    Whisk the eggs in a separate bowl, gently — no foam.1 min
  3. 3
    Slowly stream the warm milk into the eggs while whisking. This brings the eggs up gradually so they don't scramble on contact.2 min
  4. 4
    Strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a heatproof bowl. Cover tightly with foil and poke two small holes in the top.2 min
  5. 5
    Set a trivet or folded kitchen towel in the bottom of a Dutch oven (insulates the bowl from direct heat). Add water to about 1 inch — it should come halfway up the bowl once the bowl is in. Bring to a boil with the lid off.5 min
  6. 6
    Drop the heat to low. Place the foil-covered bowl on the trivet. Cover. Steam 8 minutes.8 min
  7. 7
    Cut the heat. Do not open the lid. Let it sit, undisturbed, 10 minutes. The cast iron radiates dying heat that finishes the center without overcooking the edges.10 min
  8. 8
    Lift the bowl out, peel the foil. The surface should be glossy and the custard should wobble like soft tofu. Eat warm, or chill 2 hours for a denser, pudding-like set.

Substitutions

  • whole milk → whole milk + 2 tbsp heavy cream Richer, slightly firmer set.
  • 2 whole eggs → 3 egg whites Traditional skin-on-top version. Don't cover with foil; the surface will wrinkle, not stay smooth.

Notes

The whole technique is one idea: keep the egg proteins below their fast-set temperature so they form a fine, even network instead of a coarse curd. Egg white starts to denature around 62°C; if you push past 90°C in the steaming environment you get bubbles and honeycomb.

Vanilla paste over extract: the suspended seeds give visible flecks and concentrated flavor without the alcohol burn-off of extract. Straining before steaming removes any stringy egg, but the seeds pass through.

A pinch of salt blocks the bitter receptors that cooked egg white can trigger. The custard tastes sweeter for it — you''re not just adding salinity.
