Furikake (homemade rice seasoning)

Spent kombu and katsuobushi from a fresh batch of dashi, dried in a low oven and chopped into a rice-grain-sized seasoning, then mixed with toasted sesame, shredded nori, and salt. The end-of-the-batch move that turns dashi byproducts into a pantry staple.

Phase 1, dashi byproducts

  1. 25 g spent kombu (from a fresh batch of dashi)
  2. 30 g spent katsuobushi (from a fresh batch of dashi)

Phase 2, glaze

  1. 30 ml light soy sauce
  2. 30 ml mirin (hon-mirin)
  3. 6 g granulated sugar
  4. 15 ml sake

Phase 3, finishing mix-ins

  1. 18 g toasted white sesame seeds
  2. 1 each nori sheet
  3. 2 g fine sea salt

Execution

  1. 1
    Strain the spent kombu and katsuobushi from a freshly-finished batch of dashi (see techniques/dashi). Squeeze the kombu gently to drain (do not wring; some water carries the flavor) and weigh; you want about 25 g rehydrated kombu and 30 g rehydrated katsuobushi. Lay the spent kombu flat on a cutting board and slice it into 2 mm × 2 mm dice with a sharp knife. The smaller the cuts, the more rice-grain-sized the finished furikake; oversized chunks will sit on top of rice rather than nestle in. Dashi (kombu and katsuobushi extraction)
  2. 2
    Build the glaze. In a wide nonstick or stainless skillet, combine the 30 ml / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, 30 ml / 2 tbsp mirin, 15 ml / 1 tbsp sake, and 6 g / 1½ tsp granulated sugar. Bring to a simmer over medium-low heat, swirling to dissolve the sugar. Hold for 30 seconds to burn off the alcohol from the sake and mirin (the nikiri step). The liquid should look slightly syrupy and smell sweet-savory.
  3. 3
    Cook out the moisture. Add the 25 g diced spent kombu and 30 g squeezed katsuobushi to the glaze. Stir continuously over medium-low heat, breaking up clumps of katsuobushi flakes with a spatula or wooden spoon. The mixture will look wet and shiny for the first 4 to 5 minutes, then begin to dry as the glaze reduces and the seaweed-and-fish proteins reabsorb the liquid. Cook 12 to 15 minutes total, stirring frequently in the last 5 to prevent scorching. The finish point: the mixture is no longer glossy, the bottom of the pan is dry between stirs, and the kitchen smells of roasted seaweed and toasted soy. The texture should be just-damp and crumbly, not gluey.
  4. 4
    Dry to crispness. Spread the cooked mixture in an even layer on a parchment-lined sheet pan. Bake at 110°C / 230°F for 20 to 25 minutes, stirring once at the halfway mark. The kombu-katsuobushi mixture should turn from damp to dry-and-crumbly with no visible moisture. Pull when the texture is sandy rather than tacky; oven-drying is what produces the shelf-stable, sprinkle-able final texture. (Alternative: dry in a dehydrator at 60°C / 140°F for 2 hours, or skip the oven step entirely and accept a softer, refrigerator-only product that keeps 1 week instead of 1 month.)
  5. 5
    Mix in the finishers. Cool the dried kombu-katsuobushi mixture to room temperature, then transfer to a bowl. Add the 18 g / 2 tbsp toasted sesame seeds, the nori cut into 5 mm flakes, and the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt. Toss thoroughly. Taste a pinch on a fingertip; the salt should be a faint background note (the soy and katsuobushi already carry most of the seasoning), the sesame should be detectable, the nori should add a sweet-marine top note. Adjust salt by ½ g pinches if needed.
  6. 6
    Store and serve. Transfer to a small airtight jar (a 4 oz / 120 ml mason jar is right). Sprinkle 1 to 2 tsp over plain steamed Japanese rice, into onigiri before forming, on top of chazuke, into bento boxes, over avocado toast, on grilled fish , anywhere a salty-savory-crunchy seasoning earns its place. The flavor sharpens after 24 hours as the seasonings cohere; the texture stays sandy-crumbly for the full storage window if the lid stays on.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional flavor→shiso furikake (mishima-style)

    Add 5 g / 2 tbsp dried red shiso leaves (yukari) to the finishing mix-ins. The shiso adds a bright, faintly minty-citrus note and shifts the color toward purple-pink. Yukari is sold in small bags at Japanese grocers; pre-mix or top a bowl of plain rice with it for the canonical purple sprinkle.

  • Protein flavor→salmon furikake (sake-fumi)

    Add 40 g flaked cooked salmon (broiled with a pinch of salt, then crumbled fine) during phase 3. The salmon must be fully cooked-through; the moisture content shortens the shelf life to 5 days refrigerated. The classic bento-box rice topping in Japan.

  • Heat spice→shichimi furikake

    Add 2 g / 1 tsp shichimi tōgarashi (Japanese seven-spice) to the finishing mix-ins. Adds gentle heat, citrus zest (yuzu peel), and a bright nasal note from sansho pepper. Right for adult palates; cuts through richer dishes like grilled fatty fish or fried tofu.

  • Occasion occasion→gohan-no-tomo bento style (vegetable-forward)

    Add 8 g dried wakame (crushed fine), 4 g toasted black sesame, and 2 g grated dried daikon to the finishing mix-ins. The vegetable load shifts the furikake toward a more savory-vegetable profile and pairs better with plain white rice in school-style bento boxes. Reduces the salt sensation slightly; bump fine sea salt to 3 g if needed.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • spent kombu and katsuobushi from a fresh dashi batch → fresh dried kombu (10 g) and dried katsuobushi flakes (25 g), softened with 2 tbsp water for 5 minutes Acceptable when not making dashi the same day, but the spent ingredients are flavor-loaded from the dashi extraction and produce a more complex furikake. The point of the recipe is the no-waste move; making it from fresh ingredients is a different (slightly worse) recipe.
  • mirin → sake plus 2 g / ½ tsp extra granulated sugar See ingredients/mirin substitutions. Workable if hon-mirin is unavailable; the glaze is slightly less complex.

Notes

Why this works. Dashi extracts a fraction of the flavor from kombu and katsuobushi , the ingredients still carry significant glutamate, inosinate, and aromatic compounds after the dashi is strained. Drying and salting them transforms what would otherwise be compost into a concentrated, shelf-stable seasoning that delivers umami in solid form. The same logic underpins okaka (the soy-and-sesame-tossed bonito flake filling for onigiri) and tsukudani (the longer-simmered, sweeter, soy-glazed kombu condiment); furikake sits between them on the dryness-and-density spectrum.

The mottainai principle. Mottainai (もったいない) is the Japanese cultural concept of avoiding waste, especially waste of resources that still have potential value. Furikake is the textbook mottainai dish: an ingredient stream (spent dashi solids) that the home cook would otherwise throw away, transformed into a pantry staple that ships in onigiri, dresses lunchboxes, and seasons rice for weeks. The cook who makes dashi 2 to 3 times a week and turns each batch's solids into furikake never buys commercial Gohan no Tomo, and the home version is fresher, less salty, and free of MSG and color additives that the commercial product carries.

Storage. Sealed jar, room-temperature pantry, 4 weeks. The texture stays crumbly-sandy for the full window if the lid is tight; humidity is the enemy. Refrigerator storage extends to 6 weeks but introduces a slight dampening of the snap; only refrigerate if the climate is humid or if the recipe variant includes salmon or other moist mix-ins. Freeze in a sealed container for 3+ months for long-term storage.

How to use. Sprinkle 1 to 2 tsp over a bowl of plain hot rice , the rice releases steam, the furikake absorbs a fraction of the moisture, the flavors bloom in the bowl. Mix 1 tbsp into the rice while shaping onigiri (this is the standard "yaki-onigiri furikake" filling pattern). Top a bowl of chazuke (rice with hot tea or dashi poured over) with 1 tbsp before pouring. Use as a finishing seasoning on grilled fish, avocado toast, soft-scrambled eggs, or buttered popcorn (the latter is a Japanese-American innovation worth trying). The seasoning is not a sauce, not a salt, not a spice , it is the textural-and-flavor finishing element that the cook reaches for last.
