Recipe

Olive oil-poached halibut

Two halibut fillets dry-brined for 30 minutes, then submerged in extra-virgin olive oil held at 55 to 60 °C with smashed garlic, lemon zest, and thyme until the dense flake just separates at the center, about 55 °C internal. The lean white flesh that turns chalky on a hard sear stays silky and translucent-just-set in the low oil bath, because the oil never spikes past the bath temperature the way a pan does. Finished with a brown-butter, caper, and lemon sauce that supplies the Maillard and the acid the gentle poach cannot.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Dry-brine the fillets 30 minutes ahead: salt both sides at 1 percent of weight and rest on a rack in the refrigerator. This firms the flake so it holds together in the oil and seasons the lean flesh through the cross-section, not just the surface. Skip it and the fish poaches mushy and tastes salted-on-the-outside only.
  • The poach is thermometer-driven, not clock-driven. Have an instant-read probe at the burner before you start; the bath must hold 55 to 60 °C for the full 12 to 15 minutes, and you adjust the flame every 2 minutes to keep it there. An induction burner with simmer control is ideal; a gas flame at its lowest setting plus a heat diffuser also works.
  • The oil bath drops 5 to 8 °C the moment the cold fish goes in, so preheat the oil to 62 to 64 °C and let it recover, rather than starting at 58 °C and watching it crash to 50 °C.
  • Build the brown-butter caper sauce in the last 3 minutes while the fish finishes, or hold the poached fish warm in the off-heat oil for up to 10 minutes while you make it. The sauce wants to hit the plate hot; the fish tolerates the wait, the brown butter does not.

Phase 1, dry-brine

  1. 400 g halibut fillets, 2.5 to 3 cm thick, skin off, 2 portions of about 200 g each, patted fully dry on both sides with paper towels
  2. 4 g kosher salt

Phase 2, the oil poach

  1. 600 g extra-virgin olive oil
  2. 16 g garlic cloves, smashed (skin on), side-smashed with the flat of a knife, skins left on so they infuse without scorching — (4 cloves; the oil is below frying temperature, so the garlic perfumes rather than toasts)
  3. 6 g lemon zest, in wide strips, peeled from the lemon in wide ribbons with a vegetable peeler, white pith avoided — (zest of 1 lemon; wide strips so you can fish them out, pith left behind because it turns the oil bitter)
  4. 4 g fresh thyme sprigs — (4 sprigs, left intact)
  5. 1 each bay leaf — (1 leaf; a quiet background aromatic in the bath)
  6. 2 g black peppercorns, cracked coarse

Phase 3, brown-butter caper finish

  1. 60 g unsalted butter
  2. 20 g capers, drained, drained and patted dry so they crisp rather than steam
  3. 25 g shallot, fine-minced, peeled and minced to 1 to 2 mm dice — (1 small shallot; softens in the brown butter in under a minute)
  4. 20 g lemon juice, fresh
  5. 6 g flat-leaf parsley, chopped, leaves chopped just before serving

Phase 4, finish

  1. 2 g flaky salt (Maldon or any large-flake finishing salt)

Execution

  1. 1
    Dry-brine, 30 minutes ahead. Pat the two 200 g halibut fillets fully dry on both sides with paper towels, lean white fish weeps if it goes into the oil wet. Sprinkle the 4 g / 1 tsp Diamond Crystal kosher salt evenly across both sides of both fillets, roughly 1 percent of the fish by weight. Set the fillets on a wire rack over a small tray and refrigerate uncovered for 30 minutes. The salt draws surface moisture in the first 10 minutes, then the salinity gradient flips and carries the salt into the flesh; the flake firms and seasons through the cross-section rather than sitting only on the surface. By the end the fillet feels slightly tackier and firmer than raw. Dry brining
  2. 2
    Build the bath and bring it to temperature. Choose a narrow saucepan or a small high-sided sauté pan, just wide enough to hold both fillets in a single layer; a narrow vessel means less oil to cover them. Add the 600 g / 2¾ cups extra-virgin olive oil, the 16 g / 4 smashed garlic cloves (skins on), the 6 g / zest of 1 lemon in wide strips, the 4 g / 4 thyme sprigs, the 1 bay leaf, and the 2 g / ½ tsp cracked black peppercorns. Set over the lowest heat with an instant-read probe in the oil and bring it slowly to 62 to 64 °C, which takes 8 to 12 minutes; the aromatics infuse during this climb. Do not rush it over high heat, garlic that fries at 140 °C turns the whole bath toasty-bitter and overshoots the target. Oil poaching
  3. 3
    Poach the fish. When the oil holds steady at 62 to 64 °C, lower both dry-brined fillets in gently so they sit fully submerged; the bath will drop 5 to 8 °C immediately, which is why you preheated above target. Adjust the flame to bring it back to and hold it at 55 to 60 °C. Poach 12 to 15 minutes for 2.5 to 3 cm fillets, checking the bath temperature every 2 minutes and nudging the flame to hold the window; the oil never boils or even shimmers, the surface stays still. The fish is done when an instant-read probe in the thickest part reads 52 to 55 °C and the dense raw block has just separated into thumb-sized flakes when prodded with the tip of a paring knife. Past 60 °C internal the lean flesh expels its water and turns chalky, so pull at 55 °C and let it carry. Oil poaching
  4. 4
    Hold the fish, start the brown butter. Pull the pan off the heat; the fillets can rest in the warm oil for up to 10 minutes while you finish the sauce. In a separate light-colored stainless skillet, melt the 60 g / 4 tbsp unsalted butter over medium heat. Cook it past the foaming stage, swirling, until the milk solids drop and turn deep amber and the butter smells like toasted hazelnut, about 3 to 4 minutes. The brown butter supplies the Maillard depth the gentle poach deliberately leaves off the fish. Brown butter (beurre noisette)
  5. 5
    Build the caper sauce. The moment the butter is amber, add the 25 g / 1 small shallot fine-minced and the 20 g / 2 tbsp drained, patted-dry capers. Cook 60 to 90 seconds: the shallot softens and turns translucent, and the capers crackle and crisp at their edges as their surface moisture flashes off. Keep the heat at medium, the milk solids are already browned and will scorch past gold if the pan runs too hot. Pull the skillet off the heat.
  6. 6
    Finish the sauce off-heat. Off the burner, whisk the 20 g / 1½ tbsp fresh lemon juice into the brown butter; it will sputter and the acid brightens the nutty fat into a loose pan sauce. The lemon goes in off-heat so its volatile aromatics survive rather than cooking flat. Stir in the 6 g / 2 tbsp chopped parsley. Taste: the sauce should read nutty, sharply lemony, and salty-savory from the capers; the poached fish carries its own salt from the dry-brine, so the sauce can run assertive.
  7. 7
    Lift, drain, and plate. Lift each fillet out of the oil with a fish spatula and a spoon to support it, the just-set flake is fragile, and rest it on a paper towel for 10 seconds to shed surface oil. Set each fillet on a warmed plate. Spoon the brown-butter caper sauce over and around the fish, about 35 to 40 g per plate, so the capers and shallot land on top. Scatter a pinch of the 2 g / ½ tsp flaky finishing salt across each fillet. Serve immediately. Strain the poaching oil through a fine-mesh sieve once it cools, discard the spent aromatics, and refrigerate the oil for vinaigrettes or the next poach.
Safety
  • halibut at 52 to 55°C / 126 to 131°F internal (below the USDA published-safe 145°F for fish) The low-temperature poaching canon pulls halibut at 52 to 55°C / 126 to 131°F so the lean flake just sets and stays moist; halibut is unforgiving and dries to cotton past 60°C. The published 145°F is the conservative line for the immunocompromised. Halibut from a reputable counter, dry-brined and held in a thermometer-controlled oil bath, follows established restaurant practice at the lower temperature, with the salt cure and the hold suppressing surface growth. Cooks who want the USDA-published-safe number should poach to 145°F / 63°C internal, accepting drier, more separated flake. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion a true confit hold (make-ahead, serve chilled or warm)→oil-poached halibut, cooled in the oil

    Poach to 52 °C as written, then pull the pan off the heat and let the fillets cool fully in the oil to room temperature, then refrigerate them submerged in the oil overnight. The fish keeps 3 days this way, flaked into salads or warmed gently back in the same oil at 50 °C for 5 minutes. This is the niçoise-tuna treatment applied to halibut; the oil seals the fish and the texture stays silky.

  • Dietary herb-oil finish instead of brown butter (dairy-free)→lemon-herb salsa verde

    Skip phase 3 entirely. Pound or finely chop 20 g / ½ cup parsley, 8 g / 2 tbsp capers, 4 g / 1 anchovy fillet, and 8 g / 2 cloves garlic, then loosen with 60 g / ¼ cup of the strained poaching oil and 20 g / 1½ tbsp lemon juice. Spoon over the poached fish. The salsa verde supplies the same acid-and-glutamate structure as the caper butter without the dairy, and reuses the infused poaching oil.

  • Regional Asian-leaning aromatics (ginger-scallion oil poach)→ginger-scallion oil-poached halibut

    Swap the olive oil for a neutral oil (grapeseed or light olive) and replace the bath aromatics with 30 g / 6 coins fresh ginger, 4 smashed scallion whites, and 2 strips orange zest. Poach the same way at 55 to 60 °C. Finish with a sizzling ginger-scallion oil and a few drops of light soy sauce instead of the brown butter. The neutral oil keeps the clean fish flavor forward where the olive oil would mask it.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • halibut → striped bass, black sea bass, cod, or sashimi-grade tuna Striped bass and black sea bass are the closest searing-grade white-fish substitutes and hold the flake in the oil; poach to the same 55 °C. Cod is more flaky and fragile, drop the poach to 52 °C and lift it with extra care. Tuna loin is the classic oil-poach protein; poach a 3 cm block to 52 °C for a niçoise-style rosy center.
  • extra-virgin olive oil → light olive oil, grapeseed, or refined avocado oil A medium-fruity extra-virgin gives the best flavor through the fish, but it is a lot of oil; a 50/50 blend of extra-virgin and light olive oil is the economical compromise. A neutral oil (grapeseed, avocado) reads cleaner and is the right base for the ginger-scallion modification. Avoid a peppery, high-polyphenol finishing oil, the bitterness intensifies when warmed.
  • capers → chopped nicoise olives or cornichons Capers bring brine, salt, and glutamate; chopped nicoise olives read in the same register with more fruit, and minced cornichons bring the brine-acid without the floral note. Use the same 20 g and add a pinch of kosher salt if you switch to a less salty option.
  • fresh thyme → fresh rosemary (less) or fresh oregano Rosemary is far more aggressive; use 1 small sprig instead of 4 in the bath. Fresh oregano is a 1:1 swap and leans the dish more Mediterranean. Dried herbs work in the oil bath but bring a dustier note; use half the quantity.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → capers, drained make it yourself Salt-packed capers, cured rather than brine-pickled, hold a firmer texture and a sharper, more floral note than the supermarket brine-packed product. The cure is a single-variable control the cook can dial for salt, and a home jar reads cleaner than the vinegar-muted commercial version.

Notes

Why oil poaching is the right call for halibut specifically. Halibut runs 1 to 2 percent intramuscular fat, which is very lean even by white-fish standards, and it has almost no buffer against overcooking. On a hard sear the surface overshoots 70 °C while the cook chases color, and by the time the center reaches a safe-feeling temperature the protein near the surface has expelled most of its water and reads chalky. The oil bath inverts that geometry: the heat medium is held at the target temperature with no evaporative swing, so the fish climbs gently and uniformly to 55 °C and stops there. There is no hot surface, no temperature gradient from edge to center, and no Maillard, which is exactly why the finish supplies the browning separately.

The protein-coagulation control is the whole technique. Fish myosin begins to set around 40 to 50 °C, the flesh turns opaque and the flake separates around 50 to 55 °C, and the actin contraction that squeezes out water peaks past 60 °C. A 55 to 60 °C bath sits the fish precisely in the window where myosin is set and actin is still relaxed, which is the silky, just-set, translucent-at-the-very-center texture that defines a restaurant halibut. The bath temperature is the entire lever; a probe in the oil and a flame you adjust every 2 minutes is the whole skill. A bath that drifts to 70 °C cooks the fish to chalk, a bath that sags to 50 °C leaves it raw and mushy and below the food-safety floor for surface contamination on a 12-to-15-minute hold.

Why the brown-butter caper finish, and the non-negotiable phases. A pure low-temperature poach gives a beautiful texture and a clean, mild flavor, but it gives no Maillard depth and no acid, and a savory dish needs both. The brown butter supplies the Maillard, the milk solids in the butter brown at the same chemistry the fish never gets to. The capers supply the glutamate-and-salt umami anchor, and the lemon juice added off-heat is the finishing acid that lifts the rich nutty butter and cuts the oil the fish was poached in. Skip the capers and the dish reads flat-rich; skip the lemon and it reads heavy. They are phases, not garnishes.

On the oil. Six hundred grams of olive oil sounds extravagant, but the oil is not consumed, it is a reusable cooking medium. Strain it through a fine-mesh sieve once it cools, discard the spent garlic and zest, and refrigerate it; it keeps 1 to 2 weeks and gets better with each poach as it deepens with infused character. Use it for vinaigrettes, to drizzle over the plated fish, or to start the next poach. Discard it only when the smell turns rancid, typically after four to six rounds for extra-virgin. A narrow saucepan is the economy move, less surface area to cover means less oil to buy.

Doneness and carryover. Pull the fish at 52 to 55 °C internal. Because the bath is so close to the target, carryover is gentle, 1 to 2 °C, far less than the 3 to 5 °C of a hard sear, so the window is forgiving in one direction and unforgiving in the other: a few extra minutes at 58 °C is fine, but a bath that creeps to 65 °C ruins the texture in five minutes. Trust the probe in the fish over the clock; thickness varies and a 3.5 cm block needs the full 15 minutes while a 2.5 cm block is done at 12.
