Roasted beet and citrus salad with whipped feta

Salt-roasted red beets, segmented blood orange, urfa-warmed pistachio, and a feta-yogurt cream whipped to spreading texture. Composed warm-on-cold; the dressing is the cheese itself.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The 75-minute beet roast is the long pole. Get the salt-bed beets into the oven before doing anything else; the whipped feta, supremes, and pistachio crumble all assemble in the last 20 minutes while the beets cool just enough to peel.
  • The dish is served warm-on-cold. Dress the beets while they are still warm so they absorb the vinaigrette into the cellular structure; compose and serve within 20 minutes of slicing. Refrigerating finished plates flattens the geosmin aromatics and turns the whipped feta gummy.
  • Source sheep's-milk Greek PDO feta in brine, not cow's-milk crumbles. Cow feta is too lactic and breaks when whipped instead of emulsifying smooth.

Phase 1, salt-roasted beets

  1. 700 g red beets, similarly sized, scrubbed, leaves and root tail trimmed, skins on
  2. 500 g kosher salt, for the roasting bed

Phase 2, beet finish

  2. 15 g extra-virgin olive oil
  3. 2 g flaky sea salt

Phase 3, whipped feta

  1. 200 g sheep's-milk feta (Greek PDO), drained, broken into rough chunks
  2. 100 g full-fat Greek yogurt, strained
  3. 30 g extra-virgin olive oil
  4. 10 g lemon juice, fresh
  5. 2 g lemon zest, microplaned — (zest of ½ lemon)

Phase 4, citrus

  1. 400 g blood oranges (or Cara Cara if blood are out of season), supremed (segments cut from membrane); juice from the trimmings reserved — (2 medium fruits)

Phase 5, pistachio crumble

  1. 60 g shelled raw pistachios
  2. 3 g urfa biber (Urfa pepper)
  3. 8 g extra-virgin olive oil
  4. 1 g flaky sea salt

Phase 6, finish

  1. 6 g fresh mint leaves — (small handful, torn at the last second)
  2. 10 g honey, runny

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the oven to 200 °C / 400 °F. Pour the 500 g / 2 cups kosher salt into a small heavy roasting pan or cast-iron skillet to make a 1 cm bed; nestle the 700 g / 1.5 lb beets in the salt skin-on, root-up, and tuck the 4 thyme sprigs around them. The salt bed conducts heat evenly to the underside of each beet, draws surface moisture out (which concentrates the geosmin and earthy-sweet beet sugars), and prevents the dripping beet juice from scorching the pan. Salt-bed roasting
  2. 2
    Roast 60 to 75 minutes until a paring knife slides into the centre of the largest beet with no resistance. Salt-bed roasting runs slightly slower than foil-wrapped roasting because the salt is a poor conductor of radiative heat; the trade is a more concentrated finished beet. Pull the pan, let the beets rest in the salt bed for 10 minutes; the residual heat completes the cook and the skins loosen.
  3. 3
    While the beets roast, build the pistachio crumble. Heat a dry skillet over medium and toast the 60 g / ½ cup pistachios for 4 to 5 minutes, shaking constantly, until the nuts smell intensely green-floral and the pale skin shows light gold edges. Pull off heat the moment the smell turns; pistachios go from toasted to scorched in 30 seconds and the chlorophyll-rich green will turn bitter brown if pushed.
  4. 4
    Cool the pistachios 5 minutes on a plate (they crisp as they cool), then chop coarsely on a board. Toss with the 3 g / 1 tsp urfa biber, the 8 g / 2 tsp olive oil, and the 1 g / ¼ tsp flaky salt. The oil binds the urfa flakes to the nut surfaces; the urfa is dried at low temperature and salt-cured, so its raisin-and-cocoa profile reads through warm fat without needing a separate bloom.
  5. 5
    Build the whipped feta in a food processor. Add the 200 g / 7 oz feta in chunks and pulse 8 to 10 times to break it down. Add the 100 g Greek yogurt, the 30 g / 2 tbsp olive oil, the 10 g / 2 tsp lemon juice, and the 2 g lemon zest. Process 60 to 90 seconds, stopping once to scrape, until the texture is smooth and spreading-soft like room-temperature ricotta. The yogurt is the lubricator; the olive oil makes it glossy; lemon brightens what is already very salty cheese. Taste, then adjust: feta varies, and if your batch is mild, add another 1 g / pinch of fine sea salt.
  6. 6
    When the beets are cool enough to handle but still warm, slip the skins off under a paper towel; they slide off in sheets. Cut each beet into wedges or 1 cm slabs. While still warm, dress them in a wide bowl with the 15 g / 1 tbsp red wine vinegar, the 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil, and the 2 g / ½ tsp flaky salt. Warm beets absorb the vinaigrette into their cellular structure rather than letting it pool; this is why the timing matters. Vinaigrette emulsion
  7. 7
    Supreme the 400 g / 2 blood oranges. Cut off both poles to expose the flesh, stand the fruit on one cut end, and slice the peel-and-pith away in curved strips down to the flesh. Hold the trimmed fruit over a bowl and slice between each membrane to release the segments; the segments fall into the bowl, leaving a fan of empty membranes. Squeeze the membranes over the bowl to capture the residual juice — this juice goes back into the salad as a finishing acid, not down the sink.
  8. 8
    Spread the whipped feta in a wide swoosh across a large platter or shallow bowl, leaving the lip clean. Use the back of a spoon to push the feta into a gentle craters-and-ridges landscape; flat whipped feta wastes the surface area that catches juices.
  9. 9
    Compose, do not toss. Lay the warm dressed beets across the feta. Tuck the blood-orange segments around the beets so the colors alternate (the deep crimson against the magenta-streaked orange is the visual point of the dish). Spoon a tablespoon of the reserved blood-orange juice over the whole platter.
  10. 10
    Scatter the urfa-pistachio crumble across the top in a wide arc, then drape the 6 g torn mint leaves over the platter. Drizzle the 10 g / 2 tsp honey from a spoon held high, in thin streams that catch on the beets and the cheese rather than pooling. Serve at room temperature within 20 minutes; the warm beet against the cool feta is the textural argument.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→Levantine mezze plate (no citrus)

    Drop Phase 4 entirely. Replace blood orange with 200 g / 1.5 cups pomegranate seeds and a 1 tsp / 5 g drizzle of pomegranate molasses. The dish shifts from California-Mediterranean to outright Levantine; the molasses provides the tart-sweet anchor the citrus was carrying.

  • Occasion protein→composed lunch with farro

    Add a base layer of 200 g / 1 cup cooked, cooled farro tossed with 1 tbsp / 15 g olive oil and 1 tbsp / 15 g lemon juice. Spread under the whipped feta. The grain absorbs the beet juice and the salad becomes a substantial lunch rather than a side.

  • Dietary dietary→vegan, no dairy

    Replace whipped feta with whipped silken-tofu base: 200 g silken tofu + 30 g olive oil + 10 g lemon juice + 8 g / 1 tbsp white miso + 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt processed to smooth. The miso provides the glutamate that the feta brought; the texture is close.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • blood oranges → Cara Cara oranges or pink grapefruit Cara Cara holds the pink-orange visual; grapefruit is more bitter and shifts the dish savory. Keep the segment count, drop honey by 25 percent if using grapefruit.
  • urfa biber → Aleppo pepper Brighter, sharper, less smoke. The dish reads more red-Italian and less Turkish; legitimate but a different finish.
  • pistachios → toasted hazelnuts, skinned Switches the dish toward Italian-Sicilian; the deep maple-y nut flavor reads against beets equally well, but loses the bright green visual.
  • red wine vinegar → pomegranate molasses, halved (so 8 g / 1 tsp) Sweeter and more concentrated; pulls the dish toward the Levantine modification above without committing to it fully.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → red wine vinegar make it yourself Live mother of vinegar in a jar of leftover red wine produces vinegar with floral and tannic complexity that pasteurized commercial vinegar lacks.
  • → full-fat Greek yogurt, strained make it yourselfstub Cultured at home from whole milk and strained 6 to 8 hours through cheesecloth, the yogurt reads tangier and richer than supermarket Greek yogurt, which is often thickened with milk solids rather than truly strained.
  • → honey, runny make it yourselfstub A jar of honey infused with thyme, rosemary, or chili pulls the sweet finish into deliberate savory territory; the dish picks up another dimension without adding ingredients to the mise.

Notes

Why salt-roast rather than foil-wrap: the salt bed pulls surface moisture out of the beet during the cook, which concentrates the cellular sugars and the geosmin (the compound responsible for the earthy beet character). Foil-wrapped beets steam as much as they roast and retain more water; the finished beet is paler in flavor. The same salt bed reuses across 8 to 10 roasts before the salt picks up enough beet juice to turn pink and flat-flavored; pour it back into a jar after each use.

Why whipped feta rather than crumbled: crumbled feta scattered over a beet salad makes every bite taste of feta-and-beet in alternation. Whipped feta as a base layer means every bite reads of feta + beet + citrus simultaneously; the cheese is the dressing, not a topping. The yogurt is structural — pure whipped feta is too dense and salt-aggressive; the dilution to a 2:1 cheese:yogurt ratio lands in the spreading texture window.

The umami source is the feta itself (salt-fermented sheep cheese, intensely glutamate-rich); the finishing acid is the blood orange juice and the lemon in the whipped feta. Both axes are baked in, not added at the end.

Compose at room temperature, not chilled. Cold beets read flat (the geosmin volatiles are temperature-dependent), cold whipped feta is gummy. Build the salad within 30 minutes of slicing the beets; the dish loses its warm-on-cool textural argument once the beets equilibrate downward.
