Sinigang na bangus

Filipino sour soup of milkfish poached in a clear tamarind broth with tomato and onion as the aromatic base, daikon, long beans, okra, eggplant, and water spinach simmered by hardness, seasoned with fish sauce. The bangus steaks slip in last for a gentle off-boil poach so they hold intact; the broth reads bright and clean-sour, the firm milkfish flesh and rich belly standing up to the acid the way the pork version stands up to it with fat.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Cook the rice in parallel and have it ready. Sinigang is eaten with white rice as a single composed dish: the sour broth is spooned over the rice, the fish and vegetables eaten alongside. Start 320 g / 1½ cups jasmine rice in the cooker (or stovetop, covered at a bare simmer 15 minutes then a 10 minute rest) so it lands hot when the fish comes out.
  • Build the sour broth and cook the hardier vegetables BEFORE the fish goes in. The milkfish poaches in a 5 to 6 minute window at a bare off-boil simmer (85 to 90 degrees C); a hard boil at that stage breaks the steaks into shreds and clouds the broth. Have the daikon, beans, okra, and eggplant cooked and the heat dialed down to a tremor before the fish slides in.
  • If using tamarind pulp (the traditional and superior souring agent), start the 15 minute hot-water soak while the broth base comes up, so the strained extract is ready when you need it.
  • Milkfish has fine Y-bones. Buy it deboned and butterflied or in cleaned steaks; if you use a whole fish for the bones and depth, tell diners to eat carefully and work the fine bones in the bowl.

Phase 1, broth base

  1. 1600 ml water
  2. 200 g ripe tomato, quartered, quartered through the stem end — (2 medium tomatoes; the aromatic-acid base, softens into the broth and adds glutamate and fruit-bright sourness alongside the tamarind)
  3. 120 g white onion, quartered, peeled, quartered through the root — (1 medium onion; goes in whole-quartered to sweeten the broth and softens into the soup)
  4. 15 g fresh ginger, sliced into coins, peeled, sliced into thin coins — (1-inch knob; not always in pork sinigang but standard in the fish version, where it deodorizes the fish and lifts the broth)

Phase 2, souring agent

  1. 70 g tamarind pulp (sour, seedless block), or wet tamarind paste, if using pulp: soaked in 240 ml / 1 cup hot water for 15 minutes, then mashed through a sieve to extract the pulp, seeds and fiber discarded — works at 35 g with the soak skipped; sinigang sa sampalok mix is the dusty shortcut, avoid it)

Phase 3, seasoning

  1. 30 g fish sauce (patis)
  2. 4 g kosher salt

Phase 4, vegetables (longest-cook to shortest)

  1. 250 g daikon radish, peeled, sliced into 1 cm half-moons — (about ½ medium daikon; 10 to 12 minute cook, softens to translucent and drinks the sour broth)
  2. 200 g eggplant (Chinese long or Japanese), sliced into 2 cm rounds — (about 2 small Chinese eggplants; 6 to 7 minute cook, holds shape but goes silky)
  3. 150 g yardlong bean (sitaw) or green bean, cut into 5 cm lengths
  4. 120 g okra, whole pods, trimmed of stem tip — (about 8 to 10 pods; 4 minute cook, whole so the mucilage stays inside the pod and the broth stays clear)
  5. 20 g long green chili (siling pangsigang), whole, lightly bruised — (2 to 3 long mild chilies; whole and bruised, infuses gentle heat and aroma without making the broth aggressively spicy. Use long mild chilies, not bird-eye)

Phase 5, the fish

  1. 600 g milkfish (bangus), cleaned and cut into thick steaks or deboned butterflied sections, thawed if frozen, rinsed, patted dry, cut into 3 to 4 cm cross-section steaks (or deboned butterflied, cut into 4 pieces) — (1 fish; buy it deboned and butterflied from the H Mart freezer case for the fine Y-bones, or cut a cleaned whole bangus into thick steaks and warn diners about bones)

Phase 6, finish

  1. 200 g water spinach (kangkong) or spinach, tough lower stems trimmed, cut into 8 cm lengths

Phase 7, serving

  1. 320 g steamed jasmine rice
  2. 20 g fish sauce (patis), extra for the table — ; diners drizzle over fish pieces for extra salt)

Execution

  1. 1
    Build the broth base. In a 4 L / 4 qt pot, combine the 1600 ml / 6½ cups water, the 200 g / 2 quartered tomatoes, the 120 g / 1 quartered white onion, and the 15 g / 1-inch sliced ginger. Bring to a simmer over medium-high heat and hold it there 8 to 10 minutes until the tomato slumps and gives up its juice and the onion turns translucent. Press the softened tomato against the side of the pot with a spoon to release its glutamate and pulp into the broth; this is the aromatic-acid foundation under the tamarind.
  2. 2
    Extract the tamarind. While the base simmers, place the 70 g tamarind pulp in a heatproof bowl with 240 ml / 1 cup hot water (ladled from the pot or from a kettle). Soak 15 minutes, then mash with a fork and push the slurry through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean bowl, scraping the underside of the sieve to capture the fruit purée and discarding the seeds and fiber. You should have roughly 200 ml of thick, deeply sour tamarind extract. (If using 35 g jarred concentrate, whisk it straight into 200 ml hot water and skip the soak.) Tamarind souring (sour-broth extraction and balance)
  3. 3
    Sour and season the broth. Stir the 200 ml tamarind extract into the pot. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce and the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt. Bring back to a simmer and taste. The broth should land clean-sour and forward, the tamarind bright, the patis salt deep behind it, no sweetness. Filipino sinigang is unapologetically sour; calibrate to where it reads sharp but not painful, then add more tamarind a tablespoon at a time if flat, or a cup of hot water if it overshoots. The broth is the dish; spend the 90 seconds to calibrate it now, before the fish dulls your read. Tamarind souring (sour-broth extraction and balance)
  4. 4
    Cook the hardier vegetables by hardness. With the broth at a steady simmer, add the 250 g daikon half-moons and cook 5 minutes. Add the 200 g eggplant rounds; cook 3 minutes. Add the 150 g yardlong beans and the 120 g whole okra; cook 3 more minutes. Drop in the 20 g bruised green chilies. The daikon should be nearly translucent, the eggplant silky, the beans crisp-tender, the okra just-set; total accumulated vegetable simmer is about 11 minutes, daikon first and the delicate greens still to come.
  5. 5
    Poach the bangus. Dial the heat down until the surface is at a bare off-boil tremor (85 to 90°C / 185 to 195°F), no rolling agitation. Slide the 600 g milkfish steaks gently into the broth in a single layer; do not stir, nudge with a spoon only to submerge. Poach 5 to 6 minutes for 3 to 4 cm steaks: the firm white flesh sets opaque through to the bone, the rich belly fat softens and renders a little gloss into the broth, and the steaks stay intact rather than flaking apart. A hard boil here shreds the fish and clouds the broth; hold the tremor. Oil poaching
  6. 6
    Taste and adjust once more. The vegetables and fish release water and dull the sour-salt edge slightly; correct with 1 tsp more fish sauce or a tablespoon more tamarind extract as needed. The broth should still read aggressively sour with a deep patis-salt backbone. This is not a subtle soup, and the milkfish belly fat will have rounded it, so push the acid back up if it has gone soft.
  7. 7
    Wilt the greens off the heat. Add the 200 g water spinach (kangkong), pushing the leaves and stems under the broth surface with a spoon. Cook 60 to 90 seconds only, just until wilted, then pull the pot from the heat immediately. Overcooking the kangkong turns it to mush and bleeds chlorophyll into the clear broth; pull it the moment the leaves go limp and glossy.
  8. 8
    Serve. Give each diner a wide shallow bowl with a piece of bangus, an assortment of vegetables, and a deep ladleful of the sour broth poured over. Set steamed jasmine rice in a separate bowl: the canonical eating method is a spoon of broth over the rice, then a bite of fish or vegetable alongside, alternating, the rice neutralizing the aggressive sour between bites. Pass a small dish of about 15 g / 1 tbsp extra fish sauce (sawsawan, spiked with chopped chili if you like) for diners to drizzle over their fish. Serve hot, while the fish is just-set and the kangkong still bright.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional souring agent→sinigang sa kamias (bilimbi sour)

    Skip the tamarind. Add 200 g of kamias (bilimbi), halved lengthwise, to the broth at the point the tamarind extract would go in. Simmer 8 minutes until the fruit collapses, then mash it against the side of the pot and leave it in or strain the skins. Kamias gives a sharper, greener, more vegetal sour than tamarind, lighter on the palate and very common in coastal fish sinigang.

  • Regional souring agent→sinigang sa miso (with miso)

    Add 30 g / 2 tbsp white miso to the broth along with the tamarind extract in step 3. The miso deepens the umami and adds a slight body and cloudiness; the bangus version with miso (and often a few mustard greens) is a Northern Luzon fixture and pairs especially well with the rich milkfish belly.

  • Protein protein→sinigang na hipon (shrimp instead of fish)

    Replace the milkfish with 500 g head-on shell-on shrimp. Add them after the vegetables (not before), simmering 2 minutes only until the shells turn brilliant orange and the flesh just opaques. Shrimp read sweeter than bangus, so tighten the tamarind by 5 g to keep the sour forward; the broth lightens and the shrimp heads enrich it.

  • Texture fish handling→whole bangus for depth (bones in)

    Instead of deboned steaks, use a cleaned whole bangus cut into thick cross-section steaks, head included. The bones and head enrich the broth with gelatin and a deeper fish flavor over the poach, at the cost of fine Y-bones in the bowl. Warn diners to eat carefully and work the bones; this is the traditional home version where the bones are simply navigated at the table.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • milkfish (bangus) → branzino, sea bass, snapper, or salmon belly, in thick cross-sections The firm flesh needs to survive the sour poach without disintegrating. Branzino, sea bass, and snapper all hold up cut into thick steaks rather than thin fillets. Salmon belly is the closest stand-in for the rich belly character milkfish brings to the bowl, though it reads as a different, more assertive fish. Avoid thin lean fillets, which break apart in the broth.
  • tamarind pulp (block, seedless) → tamarind concentrate (jarred, smooth) or sinigang mix Use 35 g jarred concentrate in place of 70 g block pulp; whisk it into 200 ml hot water and skip the soak. The concentrate reads slightly flatter than fresh-pulp extraction but works. Powdered sinigang sa sampalok mix is the last resort, dusty and one-note against real tamarind; use 1 sachet (about 30 g) and skip the pulp only if you must.
  • water spinach (kangkong) → regular spinach, baby bok choy, or mustard greens Spinach wilts faster (about 45 to 60 seconds) and is slightly more mineral than kangkong. Baby bok choy is a closer textural match for the crunchy stem. Mustard greens (mustasa) are the traditional pairing in the miso variant, adding a peppery bite.
  • long green chili (siling pangsigang) → Anaheim, Hatch, or a mild jalapeño, whole and bruised The chili is for aroma and gentle warmth, not heat; any long mild chili works whole and bruised. Avoid bird-eye chili in the pot, which makes the broth aggressively spicy and overrides the sour. Pass sliced hot chili at the table for diners who want fire.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → tamarind pulp (sour, seedless block), or wet tamarind paste make it yourselfstub Soaking and straining whole tamarind pods produces a paste with brighter top notes than the jarred concentrate, particularly noticeable in sinigang where tamarind is the dominant flavor. Takes 20 minutes and yields a month of paste.
  • → fish sauce (patis) make it yourselfstub Homemade patis is a months-long anchovy ferment but produces a fundamentally fresher, more complex salt-and-umami than commercial bottles. A weekend project for the dedicated cook; one batch covers a year of Filipino broths.

Notes

Why the fish goes in last, not first. This is the structural divergence from pork sinigang. Pork ribs need 45 to 60 minutes of plain-water simmer to render gelatin and turn tender, and the tamarind has to wait because strong acid toughens collagen-rich meat and slows that breakdown. Milkfish has the opposite timeline: it needs only 5 to 6 minutes of gentle poaching, and acid does it no harm. So the order inverts. You build the full sour broth first, cook the hardier vegetables in it, and slide the fish in at the very end for a brief off-boil poach. The fish is the last thing in the pot, not the first, and the broth is finished before it ever touches the steaks.

The umami and acid architecture. The savory depth comes from the milkfish itself (the firm flesh and the rich belly fat that renders gloss into the broth as it poaches) plus the fish sauce (patis), which is the salt-and-funk foundation, and the glutamate released by the simmered tomato. The acid is tamarind, and it is not a balancing accent here but the structural identity of the dish: Filipino sinigang is sour the way few other soups are sour, calibrated to read sharp and clean. Note there is no sugar in this broth, which separates it from the Vietnamese tamarind sour soups (canh chua) that balance the tamarind with palm sugar and pineapple. Sinigang leaves the sour unbuffered; the rice is the only sweetness, and it comes on the side.

Why a bare off-boil poach. Milkfish flesh is firm and holds together well, but a hard rolling boil will still flake the steaks apart, cloud the broth with suspended albumin, and toughen the surface. The target is a quiet tremor at 85 to 90°C / 185 to 195°F, steady steam with no turbulent bubbling. The flesh sets opaque to the bone and the steaks stay whole. Slide them in, do not stir, and let the gentle heat do the work; the belly fat softens and bastes the surrounding flesh from within.

The fine-bone problem. Bangus carries a famously dense network of fine intermuscular Y-bones threading through the muscle in addition to the rib cage, too numerous to pick out reliably at the table. The clean solution is to buy the fish deboned and butterflied from the freezer case and cut it into sections; the traditional home solution is to use a whole fish cut into thick steaks (the bones add gelatin and depth to the broth) and simply warn diners to eat carefully and work the bones in the bowl. Either is legitimate; just decide before you cook and tell the table which one you chose.

Why the vegetable order matters. The vegetables span a wide range of cook times, from daikon (10 to 12 minutes) to water spinach (60 to 90 seconds). Adding them all at once produces a soup of mush; staging them by hardness, daikon first and kangkong last, preserves the textural identity of each. The water spinach goes in off the heat or in the final seconds only; even three minutes in the broth and it bleeds chlorophyll, goes slimy, and dies. Whole okra (not sliced) keeps its mucilage inside the pod so the broth stays clear rather than turning gummy; the diner bites the soft interior and the soup stays clean.

Why fish sauce, not soy sauce. Sinigang is built on the salt-and-funk axis of patis, not the salt-and-fermented-soy axis of soy sauce. Substituting soy darkens the broth and pushes it toward a Chinese fish-soup register; the clean, pale, aggressively sour Filipino signature is lost. Use fish sauce throughout, and pass a little extra at the table (sawsawan), often spiked with chopped chili, for diners who want their fish saltier.

How it is eaten and how it keeps. Sinigang na bangus is a one-bowl meal eaten with rice: a spoon of sour broth over white rice, a bite of fish or vegetable alongside, the rice neutralizing the sour between bites. A first-time diner often finds the broth shockingly sour on its own; the rice integration is what makes it work. The soup holds a day in the fridge but is best fresh, the fish and especially the kangkong degrading on standing; if reheating, do it gently and never boil hard, or the greens disintegrate and the fish overcooks.

  • Adapted from the Filipino sinigang canon — Sinigang is a family of Filipino sour soups defined by the souring agent, not the protein; the fish version (sinigang na isda) and specifically the milkfish version (sinigang na bangus) is one of the lightest, fastest members of the family. Tamarind (sampalok) is the canonical modern souring agent and the one this recipe teaches; kamias, green mango, guava, and batuan are regional alternatives. Bangus carries a notorious network of fine intermuscular Y-bones, so the recipe assumes deboned, butterflied or steaked bangus from the H Mart freezer case; warn diners if you use a whole fish.
