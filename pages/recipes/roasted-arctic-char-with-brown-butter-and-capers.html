Recipe

Roasted arctic char with brown butter and capers

Two skin-on arctic char fillets scored, dry-salted, and high-roasted skin-side up at 230°C so the lean salmonid flesh sets to a just-translucent 49°C while the rendered skin crisps, then finished tableside with a foaming brown-butter sauce carrying capers, lemon, and dill. Arctic char sits between trout and salmon in fat: leaner than salmon, so it overcooks faster, which is why the roast is fast and hot and the fish comes out at the low end of the salmonid window. The brown butter supplies the Maillard-driven richness the lean flesh lacks, the capers and lemon cut it, and the crisp skin carries the textural contrast that a poached or steamed char never gets.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Dry-salt the fillets 30 to 45 minutes before roasting. Salt at 0.8% of weight, set skin-side up on a wire rack over a sheet tray, and refrigerate uncovered. The surface needs to be dry to the touch before it goes in the oven; salt without the rest seasons only the surface and the skin steams instead of crisping.
  • Get the oven and the sheet tray fully preheated to 230°C / 450°F before the fish goes in. A cold tray means the skin sits in its own released moisture and never crisps; a screaming-hot tray sears the skin from below the instant the fillet lands.
  • The brown butter is built in the last 3 minutes while the fish roasts and rests. Stage the rinsed capers, the lemon, the dill, and the cubed butter on the counter before the fish goes in; once the butter starts foaming you have a 30-second window before it scorches, with no time to start chopping.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, dry-salt (30 to 45 min ahead)

  1. 340 g skin-on arctic char fillets, 2.5 cm / 1 inch at the thickest, 2 fillets of about 170 g (6 oz) each, pin bones removed, patted dry on both sides
  2. 3 g kosher salt

Phase 2, roast

  1. 8 g neutral oil (high smoke point: refined avocado, rice bran, or grapeseed)
  2. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked, cracked coarse

Phase 3, brown-butter caper sauce

  1. 70 g unsalted butter, cold cubed, cut into 1 cm cubes so it melts evenly and browns in one wave
  2. 20 g capers, brined or salt-packed, drained; salt-packed soaked 10 minutes in cold water with one change and patted dry
  3. 30 g lemon, juiced; reserve the spent half plus extra wedges for the table
  4. 5 g fresh dill, fronds picked and roughly chopped
  5. 20 g shallot, fine-minced, minced to 1 to 2 mm dice — (1 small shallot; bloomed in the foaming butter for a soft aromatic backbone)

Phase 4, finish

  1. 1 g flaky salt (Maldon or any large-flake finishing salt)

Execution

  1. 1
    Score and dry-salt, 30 to 45 minutes ahead. Pat the 2 char fillets dry on both sides. Lay each skin-side up and, with a sharp paring knife held at a shallow 30-degree angle, cut 4 to 5 parallel scores across the skin 1 cm apart, just through the skin and 1 to 2 mm into the surface fat, never into the muscle (you should see the pale subcutaneous fat at the bottom of each cut, no exposed flesh). The scores release skin contraction tension and open a path for the subcutaneous fat to render. Sprinkle the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt evenly across the skin and flesh of both fillets, about 0.8% of their weight. Set skin-side up on a wire rack over a sheet tray and refrigerate uncovered. The salt draws surface moisture, then a dry pellicle forms in the cold air; that dry skin is what crisps in the oven instead of steaming. Fish skin scoring
  2. 2
    Preheat hard. Place a rimmed sheet tray on the middle rack and heat the oven to 230°C / 450°F with the tray inside. Give it a full 15 minutes so the tray itself is screaming hot; the fish will sear from below the instant it lands, which is half of how the skin crisps.
  3. 3
    Oil and pepper the fish. Pull the fillets from the fridge. Blot any beaded moisture off the skin with a paper towel (a dry surface is non-negotiable for crisp skin), then rub the 8 g / 2 tsp neutral oil over the skin and flesh of both fillets. Crack the 1 g / ½ tsp coarse black pepper over the flesh side. Do not skip the blot; surface water is the single most common reason fish skin steams limp instead of crisping.
  4. 4
    Roast skin-side up. Pull the hot tray out, drizzle a thread of the oiled-pan film across it if dry, and lay the fillets skin-side up directly on the hot surface, spaced apart. Roast at 230°C / 450°F for 7 to 9 minutes for 2.5 cm fillets. The high dry heat sets the lean flesh fast and crisps the scored skin from above and below at once. Pull when the thickest part reads 47 to 49°C / 117 to 120°F on an instant-read thermometer and the flesh just flakes when nudged with a fork but is still glossy and translucent at the center. Char is lean and overcooks in seconds; 49°C off the heat with carryover lands at a moist 52°C, where the fat that the fish does carry stays liquid and the flesh reads silky rather than chalky. Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Rest the fish, start the butter. Move the fillets to warmed plates, skin-side up so the crisp skin does not soften against a wet plate, and let them rest 3 to 4 minutes while you build the sauce; the interior climbs to about 52°C and the juices redistribute. Set a light-colored stainless skillet over medium and add the 70 g / 5 tbsp cold cubed butter. It will melt, then foam loudly as its water boils off.
  6. 6
    Brown the butter and bloom the shallot. Swirl the pan (do not whisk; whisking re-aerates and slows the milk solids from settling) and watch the foam. As it subsides, drop in the 20 g / 1 small minced shallot and stir; the shallot blooms in the fat and its water buys you a few seconds of margin. Keep swirling until the milk solids at the bottom turn deep amber and the butter smells of toasted hazelnut, about 90 seconds to 2 minutes total. The window from amber to burnt is roughly 30 seconds, so pull the pan the instant the nutty smell hits. Brown butter (beurre noisette)
  7. 7
    Add capers and the acid, off heat. Pull the pan from the burner. Add the 20 g / 2 tbsp drained capers; they will sputter and crisp slightly in the residual heat, releasing their mustard-pungent isothiocyanate as the brine hits the hot fat. Stir 20 seconds, then whisk in the 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice; it will hiss and emulsify with the browned butter into a glossy, slightly cloudy pan sauce. Taste: it should read nutty, sharply lemony, and briny-savory from the capers all at once. If it reads flat, more lemon; if too sharp, a splash of warm water rounds it. Stir in the 5 g / 2 tbsp chopped dill at the very end so the herb stays fresh and green rather than stewed.
  8. 8
    Plate. Spoon the brown-butter caper sauce around and partly over each rested fillet, keeping the crown of the crisp skin proud of the sauce so it stays crackling, about two-thirds of the sauce on the fish and the rest pooled on the plate. Scatter a pinch of the 1 g / ¼ tsp flaky salt over the skin and serve with the reserved lemon wedges. Eat immediately; the skin loses its crackle within a few minutes of meeting the sauce.
Safety
  • arctic char at 47 to 49°C / 117 to 120°F internal, carrying to 52°C (below the USDA published-safe 145°F for fish) Arctic char is a lean salmonid that overcooks in seconds and reads chalky past 52°C, so the northern European roasted-fish tradition pulls it at 47 to 49°C and lets carryover finish it at a moist 52°C. The published 145°F is the conservative line for the immunocompromised. Char from a reputable counter, dry-salted and high-roasted at 230°C so the surface and skin set hard, follows established practice at the lower temperature, with the hot dry heat pasteurizing the exterior where the bacterial load lives. Cooks who want the USDA-published-safe number should roast to 145°F / 63°C internal, accepting drier, chalkier flesh; previously-frozen char further lowers the parasite risk inherent to all salmonids. Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→rainbow trout, salmon, or steelhead

    Rainbow trout is the closest swap; it is even leaner, so drop the roast to 6 to 7 minutes and pull at 47°C. Salmon and steelhead carry more fat and tolerate a wider window; roast 9 to 11 minutes and pull at 50 to 52°C for medium. The brown-butter caper finish is unchanged; the only variable is the pull temperature, dictated by the fat content of the fish.

  • Texture pan-roast instead of oven→skin-side-down sear and baste

    Skip the oven. Heat the 8 g / 2 tsp neutral oil in a stainless skillet over medium-high, lay the fillets skin-side down, and press flat with a fish spatula for 10 seconds so the scored skin stays in full contact. Cook undisturbed 4 to 5 minutes until the skin is crisp and the flesh has opaqued two-thirds up the side, then flip for 60 to 90 seconds. The skin gets crisper this way but the flesh runs a touch more well-done at the edges; better for a single pair of fillets than for a crowd.

  • Regional New Nordic direction→brown butter with caraway and a lingonberry edge

    Bloom 1 g / ½ tsp lightly crushed caraway seeds in the foaming butter alongside the shallot, and swap half the lemon for 10 g / 2 tsp lingonberry preserves stirred in off heat. The sauce reads distinctly Scandinavian: nutty butter, toasted caraway, and a sweet-tart berry counterpoint against the briny capers. Keep the dill.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • arctic char fillets → rainbow trout, steelhead, salmon, or sea trout Trout and steelhead are the nearest in flavor and structure; salmon is fattier and more forgiving. All take the same scoring and finish; adjust only the roast time and pull temperature to the fat content (leaner fish, lower and faster).
  • capers, brined or salt-packed → chopped cornichons, green olives, or extra lemon Chopped cornichons bring the same briny acid with a vinegar edge; green olives read milder and rounder. With none of these, lean harder on the lemon and add a pinch of flaky salt to the sauce, accepting a less complex finish.
  • fresh dill → fresh parsley, chervil, or chives Dill is the canonical Nordic choice; flat-leaf parsley is the neutral default, chervil reads more French and delicate, chives add a mild allium note. Use the same 5 g / 2 tbsp chopped, always off the heat.
  • lemon → white wine vinegar or a splash of dry white wine plus a squeeze of lemon White wine vinegar gives a sharper, less aromatic acid; use about two-thirds the volume since it is more potent. A splash of dry white wine reduced briefly in the brown butter before the capers reads more bistro, but still wants a small finishing squeeze of lemon for brightness.

Notes

Why this fish and why fast and hot. Arctic char is a salmonid that eats like a leaner, more delicate salmon, with a fat content roughly between rainbow trout and farmed salmon. That leanness is the whole story: char carries less intramuscular fat to buffer overcooking, so the cooking window between perfect and dry is narrow. A long, gentle roast that suits salmon will leave char chalky. The 230°C oven with a preheated tray does two things at once, setting the lean flesh fast before it can dry and driving the scored skin to crisp from both sides, so the fish spends as little total time over the heat as possible. Pull at 47 to 49°C and trust carryover; char that reads 55°C at the table has been pushed past its sweet spot.

Why the skin gets scored and roasted skin-up. Char skin carries a thin subcutaneous fat layer that must render to crisp, and the scores cut just through the skin into that fat give it a path to escape and crisp the skin from underneath as it goes. Roasting skin-side up in a very hot oven over a preheated tray crisps the skin from above by direct radiant heat while the hot tray sears it from below; an unscored fillet on a cold tray steams the skin into a limp, fatty layer the diner peels off. The blot-dry step before oiling is not optional; surface water is the enemy of crisp skin, and a 5-second pass with a paper towel is the difference between crackling and rubber.

Why brown butter and not plain butter. A lean roasted fish needs a richness it cannot generate on its own; a poached or steamed char is clean but reads flat without help. Brown butter is the answer because the Maillard reaction on the milk solids produces furfurals and pyrazines, the same toasted, nutty compound families that make seared food taste of more than fat and salt. The browned butter supplies the savory depth, the capers contribute a briny, mustard-pungent counterpoint and act as a glutamate-adjacent savory anchor, and the lemon is the finishing acid that keeps the whole thing from reading heavy. Drop any one of the three and the sauce falls flat in a predictable direction: no brown means thin and bland, no capers means rich but monotone, no lemon means cloying.

Where the sauce breaks and how to save it. The brown butter scorches past amber in about 30 seconds, so the mise must be staged and the pan pulled the instant the nutty smell arrives; if it goes one shade too dark, it is still usable but pull immediately and accept a more aggressive roast note. The lemon must go in off the heat: added to a screaming pan it can split the emulsion and the acid flashes off harsh. If the sauce looks broken and oily rather than glossy, a teaspoon of warm water whisked in hard rebuilds the temporary emulsion. The dill goes in last and off heat every time; cooked dill turns grassy and drab, and the fresh green note is half of what makes the dish read Nordic rather than generically French.

Serving. The classic northern plate puts this fish over boiled or smashed new potatoes with the brown butter spilling over both, or alongside a sharp green (blanched haricots verts, wilted spinach, a dill-cucumber salad). A cold cultured-dairy element on the side, crème fraîche or skyr with a little lemon zest, plays well against the warm browned butter if you want a third texture on the plate.
