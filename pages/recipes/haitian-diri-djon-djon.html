Haitian diri djon djon

The black mushroom rice of northern Haiti: long-grain rice cooked in the inky steeping liquid of djon-djon mushrooms (a tiny dried black mushroom unique to Haiti), with green peas, scallion, thyme, and a whole intact Scotch bonnet. The dish reads dark grey, deeply earthy, faintly oceanic, and is the structural Haitian wedding-and-holiday rice.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Dried djon djon mushrooms need a 20 to 30 minute hot-water steep to release their black pigment and umami. Plan this first; the steeping liquid is the rice liquid, and the recipe falls apart if you steep at the wrong time. The mushrooms themselves are mostly discarded in the canonical version (they are tough and grainy); the steeping liquid is the prize.
  • The whole intact Scotch bonnet rule applies. The chili floats on top of the simmering rice for fragrance only; do not pierce or chop, or the rice becomes inedibly hot. Fish out before serving.

Mise en place by phase 2 phases ▾

Phase 1, the djon djon steep

  1. 20 g dried djon djon mushrooms (or dried black trumpet)
  2. 900 ml hot water (just off boil)

Phase 2, the rice cook

  1. 30 ml neutral oil
  2. 120 g yellow onion, fine dice — (1 small onion)
  3. 60 g scallions, finely sliced — (4 scallions, whites separated from greens, greens reserved for finish)
  4. 12 g garlic, minced — (3 cloves)
  5. 4 g fresh thyme sprigs — (6 sprigs whole)
  6. 1 g whole cloves — (2 whole cloves; a quiet warm-spice undertone)
  7. 400 g long-grain white rice (parboiled or jasmine), rinsed in cold water until water runs clear (about 3 changes)
  8. 100 g frozen green peas
  9. 12 g whole intact Scotch bonnet (do not pierce) — (1 chili; whole, unpierced)
  10. 8 g kosher salt

Execution

  1. 1
    Place the 20 g dried djon djon in a heatproof bowl. Cover with the 900 ml hot water. Steep 25 to 30 minutes; the water will turn from clear to deep inky black. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a measuring cup, pressing the mushrooms gently to release the dark liquid. Reserve the liquid (you should have about 800 ml). Discard the spent mushrooms (or reserve 30 g, rough chopped, to fold back into the rice for textural body if desired).
  2. 2
    Heat the 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy-bottomed pot or wide saucepan with a tight lid over medium heat. Add the 120 g diced onion and the white parts of the 60 g scallions; sweat 4 to 5 minutes until soft and translucent.
  3. 3
    Add the 12 g / 3 cloves garlic, 4 g / 6 sprigs thyme, and 1 g / 2 whole cloves. Cook 60 seconds until fragrant. Blooming aromatics
  4. 4
    Add the 400 g / 2 cups rinsed rice. Toast in the aromatic oil 90 seconds, stirring, until the grains turn from translucent to opaque white and smell faintly nutty.
  5. 5
    Add the reserved 800 ml djon djon liquid (the rice now turns deep inky grey on contact), the 100 g frozen peas, and the 8 g / 2 tsp salt. Stir once to distribute. Float the whole intact 12 g Scotch bonnet on top.
  6. 6
    Bring to a hard boil over high heat, uncovered. Once boiling, reduce heat to the lowest setting, cover tightly (use a kitchen towel between the pot and the lid if the seal is loose), and cook 18 minutes without lifting the lid.
  7. 7
    Off heat, keep covered and rest 10 minutes. Uncover; fish out and discard the Scotch bonnet (carefully, do not pierce now), the thyme sprigs, and the whole cloves. Fluff with a fork. Sprinkle the reserved scallion greens over the top. Serve hot.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein addition→as diri djon djon ak kribich (with shrimp)

    In a separate pan, sauté 250 g raw peeled shrimp seasoned with 4 g salt and 2 g black pepper in 15 ml olive oil for 90 seconds per side. Fold the cooked shrimp into the finished rice at the end of step 7. The shrimp version is the canonical wedding presentation.

  • Regional cuisine→as a Dominican substitute (moro de habichuelas negras)

    Replace the djon djon steep with the cooking liquid from a pot of cooked black beans (drained from 1 can or 200 g dried cooked beans). Add the drained beans (300 g) instead of the peas. The dish becomes a Dominican-style black-bean-and-rice version, structurally similar in color but with a different umami profile.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dried djon djon mushrooms → dried black trumpet mushrooms (15 g) plus 2 g squid ink, OR 20 g dried porcini plus 1 g activated charcoal for color Black trumpet mushrooms produce a similar inky steep with a slightly less earthy and more delicate flavor. Porcini reads more European; the charcoal restores the black color without flavor impact. Authentic djon djon is genuinely unique; substitutes approximate but do not match.
  • frozen green peas → lima beans, or pigeon peas Lima beans are the second-canonical Haitian choice. Pigeon peas pull the dish toward Jamaican rice and peas; acceptable but slightly different.
  • long-grain white rice (parboiled or jasmine) → basmati rice Basmati works; reduce liquid by 50 ml. Parboiled long-grain is the canonical Haitian choice (Mahatma or similar brands).

Notes

Why steep first. The djon djon mushroom''s value is entirely in the steeping liquid; the mushroom itself is tough and grainy after rehydration and contributes texture but not much flavor to the rice. The steep produces an inky umami-rich black liquid that replaces water as the rice''s cooking medium. Discarding the steep and trying to extract flavor from the mushrooms alone produces a pale rice with chewy mushroom bits, missing the point.

The 2:1 liquid ratio. 800 ml of djon djon liquid for 400 g of long-grain rice is the structural ratio for an absorption-style covered cook. The frozen peas contribute almost no extra water (they thaw rather than release liquid in the closed pot). Reduce the liquid by 50 ml if using basmati or a less-thirsty rice.

Why no aggressive browning. Diri djon djon is a quiet, elegant rice. The sofrito is a soft sweat (not a Maillard sear), the rice toasts only briefly, and the cook is gentle. Aggressive browning produces a heavier, smokier dish that misses the canonical clean black-mushroom-and-thyme balance.

Pairings. Canonical with grilled chicken (poul griye), poul en sos (Haitian chicken in tomato-pepper sauce), or grilled fish. Also paired with sliced avocado and pikliz (the Haitian fermented cabbage-and-Scotch-bonnet condiment) for a complete plate.

Storage. Keeps 3 days refrigerated. Reheat with a splash of water in a covered pan over low heat. The color remains; the flavor mellows slightly on day 2.

Sources
  • Haitian wedding and holiday canon (northern region origin) — Diri djon djon is the prestige rice of northern Haiti, reserved for weddings, Christmas, Independence Day, and special Sunday dinners. The djon-djon mushroom (Psathyrella sp.) is a small dried black mushroom that grows only in northern Haiti and parts of the Dominican Republic; the steeping liquid stains the rice deep grey-black, and the cooked mushrooms contribute a deep umami earthiness. Outside Haiti, dried djon djon is sold at Haitian groceries; the closest substitute is dried Polish or French black trumpet mushrooms (which produce a similar inky steep), or a combination of dried porcini and a small amount of squid ink for color.
