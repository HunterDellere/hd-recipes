Sichuan fish-fragrant eggplant (yu xiang qie zi)

Sichuan classic. Eggplant batons dry-fried until structurally collapsed, then tossed in a sweet-sour-spicy "fish-fragrant" sauce built on doubanjiang, ginger, garlic, scallions, and Chinkiang vinegar. Contains no fish; the name describes the flavor profile traditionally used for fish.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Steamed jasmine rice is the partner and must be on FIRST. Rinse 300 g / 1½ cups rice three times, start it with 360 ml water in the cooker (or stovetop covered at a bare simmer for 18 minutes plus 10 minute rest off heat). The 15 minute rest at the end of the cook overlaps the entire eggplant prep and cook so both finish together.
  • Pre-mix the fish-fragrant sauce in a single bowl before the wok heats. The dry-fry through final toss runs about 12 minutes nonstop with no time to whisk cornstarch into water once the doubanjiang hits the oil.
  • Cut all aromatics (ginger, garlic, scallion whites and greens separately) and have them in small dishes at the stove. The aromatic base in step 4 burns within 30 seconds of medium heat; you cannot stop to mince garlic.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, eggplant

  1. 600 g Chinese or Japanese eggplant — (2-3 long eggplants; cut into 1.5 cm × 6 cm batons)
  2. 30 ml neutral oil (rice bran, peanut, or canola)

Phase 2, fish-fragrant sauce

  1. 20 ml light soy sauce
  2. 20 ml Chinkiang vinegar
  3. 18 g granulated sugar
  4. 5 g cornstarch
  5. 60 ml water

Phase 3, aromatic base

  1. 30 ml neutral oil
  2. 25 g Pixian doubanjiang (chili bean paste)
  3. 15 g fresh ginger, finely minced — (1 inch knob)
  4. 20 g garlic, finely minced — (5-6 cloves)
  5. 30 g scallion whites, finely chopped — (2-3 scallions; reserve greens for finish)

Phase 4, finish

  1. 20 g reserved scallion greens, sliced — (reserved from Phase 3)
  2. 5 ml toasted sesame oil

Execution

  1. 1
    Cut the 600 g eggplant into 1.5 cm × 6 cm batons. Do not salt, Chinese and Japanese eggplant cultivars are already low in water and salting tightens the texture too much. The dry-fry technique handles moisture removal from the heat direction.
  2. 2
    Mix the fish-fragrant sauce. In a small bowl, whisk together the 20 ml / 1½ tbsp soy sauce, 20 ml / 1½ tbsp Chinkiang vinegar, 18 g / 1½ tbsp sugar, 5 g / ½ tbsp cornstarch, and 60 ml / ¼ cup water until the sugar and cornstarch dissolve. The sauce is going in as a single pour; pre-mixing prevents a scramble during the high-heat finish.
  3. 3
    Dry-fry the eggplant. Heat a wok over high heat until smoking lightly. Add the 30 ml / 2 tbsp oil and the eggplant batons. Stir-fry continuously for 4 to 5 minutes; the eggplant will progress through three stages, releasing steam, collapsing and shrinking by 30 to 40%, then browning at the cut faces. Pull when the cut faces are golden-brown, the structure has collapsed inward, and the surface is dry to the touch. Transfer to a plate. Dry-frying eggplant (gan bian)
  4. 4
    Render the aromatic base. Wipe the wok clean and return to medium heat (140 to 160°C / 285 to 320°F). Add the 30 ml / 2 tbsp oil and the 25 g doubanjiang. Cook 90 seconds, breaking up the paste with a spatula, until the oil turns brilliant red and smells distinctly of chili-bean paste. Add the 15 g minced ginger, 20 g minced garlic, and 30 g chopped scallion whites. Cook 30 seconds until fragrant; do not exceed medium heat or the aromatics scorch. Red oil rendering
  5. 5
    Build the dish. Return the dry-fried eggplant to the wok. Toss for 30 seconds in the rendered red oil to coat. Whisk the prepared sauce mixture once (the 5 g cornstarch settles) and pour around the edge of the wok. Toss continuously for 60 to 90 seconds; the sauce will boil, thicken, and coat the eggplant. Pull when the sauce has thickened to a glossy coat that clings to each piece, with no pooled liquid at the bottom of the wok.
  6. 6
    Finish. Pull off heat. Add the 20 g reserved scallion greens and 5 ml / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil. Toss once. Transfer to a serving plate and serve immediately with steamed jasmine rice.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion cultivar→globe eggplant (American / Mediterranean)

    Globe eggplant has 50% more water than Chinese eggplant and the cell structure is spongier. Cut into smaller batons (1 cm × 5 cm), salt at 1.5% by weight (9 g salt per 600 g eggplant), let stand 30 minutes, blot completely dry. Then proceed with dry-frying for 6 to 8 minutes (longer than Chinese eggplant). The salt-and-press is the move that lets globe eggplant approach the texture Chinese eggplant gets without it.

  • Occasion cultivar→Italian or fairy-tale eggplant

    Cut into 1 cm × 5 cm batons (smaller than Chinese cultivars to compensate for slightly higher water). Salting is optional; if you have time, salt at 1% by weight (6 g per 600 g) for 20 minutes and blot. Dry-fry 5 to 6 minutes. The end result is closest to Chinese-eggplant texture of any non-Asian cultivar.

  • Occasion cultivar→Thai green pea eggplant

    Different ingredient functionally; do not substitute in this recipe. Pea eggplant is for curries where the discrete bitter pop is the contribution. Use a Thai green curry recipe instead, where these eggplants belong.

  • Protein protein→with ground pork (yu xiang rou si or yu xiang qie zi rou)

    After rendering the aromatic base in step 4, add 200 g ground pork and stir-fry until the pork is no longer pink and starting to brown, about 3 minutes. Then proceed with the eggplant return and sauce as written. The dish becomes the pork-and-eggplant version, more substantial. Serves 4 instead of 3.

  • Heat heat level→milder for non-Sichuan-eaters

    Reduce doubanjiang to 15 g (1 tbsp). The dish loses some of its red color and savory depth but is approachable for diners who find the standard heat level intense. Add 1 tsp tomato paste to the aromatic base to recover some of the red color and umami. Or use erjingtiao chili powder (ground from dried chilies) at 2 g instead of doubanjiang for a different flavor angle.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • Pixian doubanjiang → no substitute See ingredients/doubanjiang. The fish-fragrant flavor profile is built on doubanjiang's aged fermentation; substituting another bean paste loses the dish.
  • Chinkiang vinegar → rice vinegar + a few drops dark soy Chinkiang is the traditional choice. Rice vinegar reads sharper; a few drops of dark soy add back some of the malty depth. Acceptable substitute.
  • cornstarch → potato starch 1:1 substitute. Potato starch produces a slightly glossier sauce that holds longer at table temperature.

Notes

The "fish-fragrant" name. Yu xiang (鱼香, "fish fragrance") is a Sichuan flavor category, not an ingredient list. The flavor profile, sweet, sour, hot, salty, with strong ginger-garlic-scallion aromatics, was historically used to season fish in inland Sichuan where fresh ocean fish was unavailable; the seasoning style took the name. The flavor profile is now applied to many ingredients (eggplant, pork shreds, tofu, even chicken), and the dish is named "yu xiang [main ingredient]." None of the dishes contain fish; "yu xiang qie zi" translates roughly as "fish-fragrant-style eggplant," not "eggplant with fish."

The sweet-sour-spicy balance is the spine. Yu xiang is one of the named "compound flavors" of Sichuan cooking (along with mala, lychee, strange-flavor, and others), and the balance is precise: roughly equal parts sugar and vinegar by weight, with the doubanjiang providing the chili and salt foundation, and the ginger-garlic-scallion providing the aromatic top. Adjust one variable at a time when troubleshooting: if the dish reads flat-sweet, more vinegar; if it reads harsh-sour, more sugar; if it reads salty-without-flavor, more aromatics. Restaurant versions sometimes go heavier on the sugar (up to 25 g) to make the dish more crowd-pleasing; the home version above hits a more balanced register.

Why not salt the Chinese eggplant. Salting drives water out osmotically; dry-frying drives water out through evaporation. Doing both produces dehydrated, chewy, tough eggplant. Chinese eggplant has low enough starting water that dry-frying alone produces the right structural collapse. Globe eggplant, in contrast, has so much water that dry-frying alone struggles to drive enough out before the surface burns; salt-and-press is the necessary preparation step for globe eggplant in this dish.

The dry-fry sequence vs the deep-fry shortcut. Many restaurant versions of yu xiang qie zi deep-fry the eggplant in 2 to 3 cm of oil at 175°C / 350°F for 90 seconds, which collapses the cells faster than dry-frying does. This is the easier-but-greasier version; the eggplant absorbs significantly more oil even with the deep-fry technique because the contact time is short but the oil contact is total. The dry-fry technique above produces less-greasy results and is the home-cooking standard. Choose deep-fry for restaurant-style results; choose dry-fry for the cleaner, more controllable outcome.

Eating mechanics. Like most Sichuan stir-fries, this is part of a multi-dish meal: serve with steamed white rice and at least one non-spicy side (a stir-fried green vegetable, a clear soup). Each diner takes pieces of eggplant onto their rice; the sauce-coated eggplant flavors the rice. The dish is best within 10 minutes of plating; sitting longer, the cornstarch-thickened sauce can break and the eggplant goes soggy from condensation. Reheats acceptably the next day for personal meals.
