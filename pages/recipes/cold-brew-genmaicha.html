Cold-brew genmaicha (overnight)

Genmaicha steeped overnight in cold water. The slow cold extraction pulls L-theanine and the toasted-rice aromatics without the tannic bite of hot brewing, produces a tea that drinks smoother than it has any right to.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is an overnight steep, 8 hours minimum in the refrigerator. Active hands-on time is 5 minutes; the rest is passive cold extraction. Start the night before you want to drink it.
  • Use cold water, not hot. Cold extraction pulls L-theanine and the toasted-rice aromatics while leaving most of the bitter tannins in the leaf; pouring hot water over genmaicha for a cold-brew result extracts the wrong fraction and the tea drinks astringent rather than smooth.

Execution

  1. 1
    Place the 12 g / 2 heaping tbsp genmaicha in a 1.5 L glass jar or carafe. Add the 1000 ml / 4 cups cold filtered water. The leaves and rice grains will float; press them down with a spoon so they hydrate evenly. Do not skip filtered water, chlorinated tap water pulls metallic notes from the toasted rice that ruin the brew.
  2. 2
    Cap the jar loosely (a tight lid is fine but a loose cover prevents pressure buildup if any fermentation occurs during a long steep). Refrigerate for 6 to 8 hours, or overnight. Cold water extracts L-theanine (the sweet umami amino acid) and most aromatic compounds slowly but extracts the bitter catechins very slowly, the result is a tea that tastes round and full-bodied without the dry-mouth tannin of hot brewing.8 h
  3. 3
    After steeping, strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean carafe. Press the wet leaves gently with the back of a spoon to extract the last 50 ml or so of tea trapped in the hydrated mass. The brew should be a pale gold-green with a faint suspended cloudiness from the rice flour, that cloudiness is correct; do not strain through a coffee filter or you remove the aromatic suspension.
  4. 4
    Pour over ice in tall glasses. The tea is perfectly drinkable straight out of the steep but reads brighter and more aromatic over ice, the dilution from melting ice opens the rice notes. Holds in the refrigerator 3 days; aroma slowly fades after 24 hours.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional flavor addition→with yuzu peel

    Add 5 strips of fresh yuzu peel (or lemon peel) to the jar at the start of the steep. The citrus oils infuse over the long cold brew without going bitter, adding a subtle perfume that lifts the rice notes.

  • Texture service→cold-brew genmaicha latte

    Build a glass with 120 ml / ½ cup cold whole milk (or oat milk), 1 tbsp honey or simple syrup, and ice. Pour 180 ml / ¾ cup cold-brew genmaicha over the top. Stir. The cold tea picks up creaminess without warming the milk; works particularly well with oat milk.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • genmaicha → sencha + ½ tsp toasted brown rice puffs Decent approximation. The rice puffs need to be unsweetened plain rice cereal, not flavored. Add to the cold brew alongside the sencha.
  • genmaicha → hojicha (cold brew, identical method) Different profile, hojicha is roasted leaf, not green leaf with roasted rice. Produces a warmer, nuttier cold brew that drinks more like an iced amber tea than green tea.

Notes

The 6-to-8-hour window is the key. Under 6 hours the rice notes have not fully extracted and the tea reads watery. Over 12 hours bitterness starts to creep in even at refrigerator temperatures, and the tea takes on a slight tannic edge. Overnight (8 to 10 hours) is the standard sweet spot.

Cold brew vs. iced brew. This recipe is the cold-brew method: cold water from the start, long slow extraction. The alternative iced brewing (hot water over ice into a chilled vessel) extracts faster, gives a brighter top note, but pulls more astringency. For genmaicha specifically the cold-brew method is significantly better because the rice flavor is delicate and slow to release; aggressive hot extraction overshoots and the rice gets lost behind the green tea.

Reusing the leaves. The wet leaves after one cold-brew steep have a second extraction in them: fill the jar again with cold water and steep another 6 hours. The second brew is paler and slightly more aromatic-forward; some prefer it. After two steeps the leaves are spent.
