Shrimp and grits (Lowcountry)

Stone-ground coarse white grits cooked slowly in milk-and-water until the cornstarch fully gelatinizes into a creamy porridge, finished with sharp white cheddar and butter. Topped with shrimp sautéed with andouille and the Trinity in a smoky tomato pan sauce, the shrimp residual heat melting into the grits at the bowl. The Charleston-Lowcountry breakfast dish that became a Southern dinner classic; the canonical Sean Brock direction with proper stone-ground grits, not the instant supermarket version.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Start the grits FIRST. Stone-ground grits need 45 to 60 minutes of slow cooking to fully gelatinize the cornstarch; instant grits cook in 5 minutes but produce a structurally different dish (grainy, thin, never creamy). The grits go on the back burner and simmer untended while the shrimp pan sauce builds; pull the shrimp the moment the grits are ready.
  • Source actual stone-ground coarse grits (Anson Mills, Geechie Boy, Marsh Hen Mill, Old Mill of Guilford). The white-corn variety is canonical Lowcountry; yellow stone-ground works and reads sweeter. Supermarket "quick grits" or "instant grits" produce a different dish entirely; substituting them is not recommended.
  • Dry-brine the shrimp 30 minutes ahead with kosher salt. Salt on the surface 30 minutes before searing produces shrimp that brown rather than steam in the pan; salt-as-you-cook produces gray, watery shrimp.

Phase 4, finish

  1. 30 g green onions, thin-sliced — (small bunch; white and green parts)

Execution

  1. 1
    Start the grits. Combine 500 ml / 2 cups whole milk, 500 ml / 2 cups water, and 8 g / 2 tsp kosher salt in a heavy 3 quart saucepan. Bring to a hard simmer over medium-high heat. Whisk in the 200 g stone-ground coarse grits in a steady stream, whisking constantly to prevent clumping. Reduce heat to low so the surface just bubbles gently. Cover with the lid offset (slight gap for steam release). Cook 45 to 55 minutes, stirring with a wooden spoon every 5 minutes to prevent the bottom from scorching, until the grits are creamy and the individual corn-grit kernels have softened from gritty to pleasantly tender (small textural bite is correct; total smoothness is overcooked). If the grits get too thick before the cook is done, splash in hot water 60 g at a time.
  2. 2
    Dry-brine the shrimp. While the grits cook, toss the 500 g shrimp with 4 g kosher salt. Let stand 30 minutes at room temperature (or up to 1 hour in the fridge if your kitchen is warm). The salt draws surface moisture out, then re-absorbs as a thin brine that seasons the flesh and primes the shrimp to sear rather than steam. Dry brining
  3. 3
    Render the andouille. About 20 minutes before the grits are done, heat 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil in a heavy 30 cm skillet over medium-high heat. Add the 150 g diced andouille; render 3 to 4 minutes, stirring, until the edges crisp and the fat renders out into the pan. Remove the andouille with a slotted spoon to a small plate; reserve. Keep the rendered fat in the pan.
  4. 4
    Sear the shrimp. Increase heat to high. Add the 500 g brined shrimp to the andouille fat in a single layer. Sear undisturbed 60 to 75 seconds per side until just opaque pink with browned edges. Do not fully cook through (the shrimp will finish in the pan sauce). Transfer the shrimp to a plate; reserve. Maillard sear
  5. 5
    Sweat the Trinity. Reduce heat to medium. Add the 120 g onion, 60 g celery, and 80 g bell pepper to the same skillet (still glossy with andouille and shrimp fat). Sweat 4 to 5 minutes until the onion turns translucent. Add the 15 g / 4 cloves minced garlic; stir 60 seconds.
  6. 6
    Toast the tomato paste; add tomato and spices. Push the vegetables to the edges of the skillet. Drop the 15 g / 1 tbsp tomato paste into the cleared center. Toast 60 seconds, stirring the paste alone in the hot fat until it darkens from bright red to brick-brown. Stir into the vegetables. Add the 200 g halved cherry tomatoes, 3 g / 1½ tsp smoked paprika, and 1 g / ¼ tsp cayenne. Stir 2 to 3 minutes; the tomatoes will burst and release their juice.
  7. 7
    Add stock; reduce. Pour in the 200 ml shrimp or chicken stock; scrape up any fond from the pan bottom. Bring to a simmer and reduce 3 to 4 minutes until the sauce reduces to a glossy spoon-coating consistency. Return the reserved 150 g andouille and the seared 500 g shrimp (with any accumulated juices) to the pan. Toss to coat and warm the shrimp through, 60 to 90 seconds; the shrimp finish cooking from the sauce heat and should curl tight and read fully opaque.
  8. 8
    Finish the grits. Pull the grits off the heat. Whisk in the 60 g cold cubed butter until fully integrated and glossy. Whisk in the 100 g grated sharp white cheddar in three additions, whisking between each until smooth. Taste; adjust salt with kosher salt by the pinch. The grits should be creamy, just thick enough to mound on a spoon, and visibly glossy from the butter.
  9. 9
    Finish the sauce. Pull the shrimp pan off the heat. Whisk in the 10 g / 2 tsp lemon juice, 5 g / 1 tsp hot sauce, and 30 g / 2 tbsp cold cubed butter. The butter mount glosses the sauce and stabilizes the emulsion of tomato and stock. Taste; adjust salt and hot sauce. Pan emulsion (mantecare)
  10. 10
    Plate and serve. Spoon 180 to 220 g of the cheesy grits into the center of each wide shallow bowl, spreading into a shallow well. Spoon the shrimp, andouille, and tomato pan sauce over the grits (3 to 5 shrimp per portion plus the sauce that pools into the grits well). Scatter the 30 g sliced green onions and 10 g chopped parsley over the top. Set a lemon wedge on the rim of each bowl. Serve immediately with hot sauce on the table; each diner squeezes lemon to taste at the bowl.
Safety
  • pulling shrimp at internal opaque-pink (about 60°C) Shrimp are done when they curl tight and the flesh turns from translucent gray to opaque pink, internal about 60°C. The USDA published-safe target is 63°C / 145°F; shrimp at 60°C in a 90°C pan sauce continue to carryover for 60 seconds and cleanly clear the published safety threshold. Pulling much past 65°C produces rubbery shrimp. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional occasion→Bill Neal Crook's Corner version (bacon, mushroom, lemon)

    Skip the andouille; render 6 strips of bacon (120 g) instead. Replace the cherry tomatoes with 200 g sliced cremini mushrooms sauteed in the bacon fat for 6 minutes until golden. Skip the tomato paste entirely. Skip the cayenne and hot sauce; the dish reads more refined-bistro and less Cajun-Creole. Lemon goes from 10 g to 15 g (more lemon-forward). This is the 1985 Crook's Corner direction that transformed shrimp and grits from a Carolina regional curiosity into a national restaurant standard.

  • Protein protein→crab and grits (Carolina coastal)

    Substitute 300 g picked lump blue crab for half the shrimp; fold the crab into the pan sauce off-heat at the end (lump crab is delicate and falls apart with stirring). The dish reads sweeter and more delicate.

  • Regional cuisine→Italian polenta direction (with sausage and rapini)

    Cook the grits identically (Italian coarse-stone polenta is structurally the same as stone-ground grits, just made from corn varietals selected for polenta). Skip the andouille and tomato pan sauce; saute 250 g hot Italian sausage with 200 g rapini (broccoli rabe) in olive oil and garlic. Spoon over the polenta with a drizzle of extra-virgin olive oil. The dish pivots from Lowcountry to Northern Italian cleanly.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • stone-ground coarse white grits → stone-ground coarse yellow grits, stone-ground polenta Yellow stone-ground grits are slightly sweeter than white; both are canonical. Italian polenta works identically (the cornmeal is just a different varietal). Quick grits and instant grits are not substitutes; they produce a thin, grainy dish.
  • whole milk → half-and-half (richer) or all water (lighter) Half-and-half makes a luxuriously rich grits, common at higher-end restaurants. All-water grits with extra butter at the end reads cleaner and more grain-forward, the Edna Lewis preference.
  • sharp white cheddar → aged gouda, gruyere, parmigiano-reggiano Sharp aged white cheddar is the canonical Lowcountry choice. Gruyere reads more French-bistro; parmigiano reads more Italian. Pre-shredded supermarket cheese works less well (anti-caking starch can go gummy).
  • andouille sausage → tasso, country ham, thick-cut bacon Tasso reads more aggressively spiced and more pork-forward. Country ham (Edwards, Benton's) is the heritage Lowcountry option, dice fine and render the same way. Thick-cut bacon is the Crook's Corner direction.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → shrimp stock or chicken stock make it yourselfstub A shrimp stock made from the reserved shrimp shells (sauteed 5 minutes, simmered 30 minutes in water with onion, celery, bay) is the structural upgrade. The pan sauce reads dramatically more shrimp-of-the-sea forward; takes 40 minutes total and uses an ingredient you would otherwise throw away.
  • → hot sauce (Crystal or Louisiana Gold) make it yourself Homemade fermented hot sauce in the pan sauce and at the table carries complexity bottled brands cannot match.

Notes

Stone-ground grits are the dish. The single biggest improvement in shrimp and grits from the 1990s through today was the rediscovery of proper stone-ground grits as a category of ingredient. Anson Mills (Glenn Roberts, Columbia SC, 1998) revived the South Carolina heritage corn varieties; Geechie Boy (Edisto Island), Marsh Hen Mill (Edisto), and Old Mill of Guilford (NC) followed. The stone-grinding leaves the germ and pericarp intact, producing a grit that requires 45 to 60 minutes of slow cooking to fully gelatinize the cornstarch and develop the creamy, dimensional, faintly grassy-corn flavor that defines proper grits. Instant grits cook in 5 minutes from steamed-and-rolled corn flake material and produce a thin, grainy, flavorless dish. The two are not interchangeable.

Why milk-and-water rather than all-water or all-milk. All-water grits read clean and corn-forward (the Lewis preference); all-milk grits can scorch on the pan bottom over a 50-minute cook and tend to taste over-rich. The 50/50 milk-and-water split is the modern compromise: creamy enough to read luxuriant, lean enough to let the corn flavor through. Adjust to preference.

The shrimp at the end is the move. Sean Brock''s Husk version (and most contemporary Charleston restaurant interpretations) puts the shrimp in the pan AT THE END, after the andouille has rendered, the Trinity has sweated, the tomato has reduced, and the sauce has come together. The shrimp cook from the sauce heat for 60 to 90 seconds and stay perfectly tender; cooking them at the start and reheating them in the sauce makes them rubbery. This is the structural sequencing change that separates contemporary shrimp and grits from the 1980s versions.

The lemon at the end is non-negotiable. The 10 g / 2 tsp lemon juice mounted into the pan sauce off-heat is the finishing acid that lifts the rich grits, cheese, butter, and andouille fat. Skipping the lemon (or the table lemon wedges) produces a dish that reads heavy and monotonal; the citrus brightness is what makes the dish work as dinner rather than as a breakfast bowl.

Lowcountry geography and lineage. The Lowcountry is the coastal plain of South Carolina and Georgia, from Pawleys Island down to Savannah, where Gullah Geechee people (descendants of enslaved West Africans, particularly from rice-cultivating Sierra Leone, Liberia, and Senegambia) maintained a distinct culinary tradition tied to the rice plantations, the tidal creeks, and the wild shrimp of the inlets. Shrimp and grits, gumbo z''herbes, hoppin'' john, red rice, benne wafers, and pilau are all Gullah-Geechee contributions to the broader Southern canon. The dish has been refined, restaurant-ified, and gentrified over the past 40 years; the heritage lineage is worth honoring.

Day-2 reheats fine for the grits, less well for the shrimp. The grits can be refrigerated 3 days and reheated with a splash of milk and butter (they thicken on the chill and need to be loosened back to creamy consistency). The shrimp are best fresh; reheated shrimp go slightly rubbery. If making ahead for service, prep everything but cook the shrimp at the last minute.

Plate the dish in a shallow well. Spreading the grits into a shallow well with the back of a spoon and ladling the sauce into the well is the canonical plating; the diner spoons each bite to include both the grits and the sauce. Mounding the grits high produces a dish where the shrimp roll off and the sauce pools at the bottom of the bowl.

Sources
  • Charleston Lowcountry tradition (Gullah Geechee, with the Sean Brock contemporary update) — Shrimp and grits originated as a Gullah Geechee breakfast dish along the Carolina Lowcountry coast: cooked grits topped with shrimp poached in their own juice and a knob of butter. The dish was a regional curiosity until Bill Neal at Crook's Corner (Chapel Hill, 1985) reinterpreted it as a dinner with mushrooms, bacon, and lemon. Sean Brock at Husk (Charleston, 2010s) returned the dish to its stone-ground roots while keeping the dinner-plate ambition. This recipe sits in the Brock direction with the andouille-and-Trinity tomato pan sauce that anchors the contemporary canonical version.
