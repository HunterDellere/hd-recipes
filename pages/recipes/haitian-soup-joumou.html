Haitian soup joumou

The Haitian Independence Day soup: kabocha or pumpkin pureed into a stock built from braised beef short rib, with cabbage, carrot, turnip, celery, leek, potato, vermicelli pasta, scallion, thyme, parsley, garlic, Scotch bonnet, and a finishing lime. The soup is rich, deeply orange, faintly sweet from the pumpkin, with the chunky cooked vegetables tucked into the puree. Served every January 1st in Haitian households worldwide.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The beef short rib needs to braise for 90 minutes before the pumpkin and vegetables go in. Plan a 2.5 hour total cook time, plus 12 hours of overnight marinade for the beef in epis (the standard Haitian preparation).
  • Kabocha squash is the canonical Haitian pumpkin (joumou) for this soup; its dense orange flesh purees smoother and is sweeter than American pie pumpkin. Butternut squash is the best Western substitute. Avoid sugar pumpkin (too watery) and canned pumpkin (texture wrong, picks up the can flavor).
  • The vermicelli is the structural finish that distinguishes soup joumou from a generic pumpkin soup. Use angel hair or thin spaghetti broken into 4 cm pieces; do not skip.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, the beef marinade (12 hours ahead)

  1. 800 g bone-in beef short rib, in 4 cm pieces, cut by butcher into 4 cm pieces, rinsed with white vinegar and patted dry
  2. 80 g fresh epis (Haitian green seasoning, see Poul en sos recipe for build)
  3. 8 g kosher salt
  4. 2 g black pepper, cracked

Phase 2, the beef braise base

  1. 30 ml neutral oil
  2. 200 g yellow onion, medium dice — (1 large)
  3. 100 g celery stalks, sliced, sliced 1 cm thick — (3 stalks)
  4. 20 g garlic, smashed — (5 cloves)
  5. 2500 ml cold water

Phase 3, the pumpkin and vegetables (added after 90 min beef braise)

  1. 700 g kabocha squash (or butternut), peeled, seeded, cut into 3 cm chunks — (about ½ small kabocha; kabocha is the canonical Haitian joumou, butternut is the substitute)
  2. 300 g waxy potato (Yukon Gold), in 2 cm chunks — (2 medium)
  3. 200 g carrots, in 2 cm chunks — (2 large)
  4. 150 g turnip, in 2 cm chunks — (1 medium; canonical Haitian addition, white turnip)
  5. 200 g green cabbage, sliced, sliced into ribbons 1.5 cm wide — (¼ small head)
  6. 100 g leek, white and light green parts, sliced, sliced 1 cm thick, rinsed of grit — (1 small leek)
  7. 4 g fresh thyme sprigs — (6 sprigs)
  8. 12 g whole intact Scotch bonnet — (1 chili, whole and unpierced)

Phase 4, the finish

  1. 60 g vermicelli or angel hair pasta, broken into 4 cm pieces
  2. 10 g flat-leaf parsley, chopped — (small handful)
  3. 30 ml fresh lime juice
  4. 30 g unsalted butter (optional, for body)

Execution

  1. 1
    The day before: combine the 800 g short rib with 80 g epis, 8 g salt, and 2 g pepper. Massage to coat. Cover; refrigerate 12 to 24 hours.
  2. 2
    Day of: heat the 30 ml / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a heavy-bottomed stockpot over medium-high heat. Sear the short rib in 2 batches, 4 to 5 minutes per side, until deeply browned on all surfaces. Transfer to a plate. Maillard sear
  3. 3
    Reduce heat to medium. Add the 200 g diced onion, 100 g sliced celery, and 20 g smashed garlic to the pot; cook 5 minutes in the rendered fat until softened.
  4. 4
    Add the 2500 ml / 10 cups cold water; scrape the fond from the pot bottom. Return the seared short rib (and any accumulated juices) to the pot. Bring to a hard boil; reduce to a steady simmer and cook, partially covered, 90 minutes. Skim any grey foam in the first 15 minutes. The beef should be approaching tender at the 90-minute mark.
  5. 5
    After 90 minutes, add the 700 g kabocha chunks. Continue to simmer 25 minutes, until the kabocha is fully tender (a knife slides through without resistance).
  6. 6
    Use tongs or a slotted spoon to fish out the kabocha chunks into a wide bowl; reserve. Add the 300 g potato, 200 g carrot, 150 g turnip, 200 g cabbage, 100 g leek, 4 g thyme, and the whole 12 g Scotch bonnet to the simmering broth. Cook 15 to 18 minutes until the potato and turnip are fork-tender.
  7. 7
    While the vegetables cook, puree the reserved kabocha. Use an immersion blender directly in the bowl, or transfer to a standing blender. Add about 500 ml of the hot broth from the pot to the kabocha; puree to a smooth thick orange cream. Stir the puree back into the soup pot. The broth should turn deep orange and gain noticeable body; this is the structural "pumpkin soup" body.
  8. 8
    Bring back to a simmer. Add the 60 g broken vermicelli; cook 4 to 5 minutes until the pasta is al dente. Fish out and discard the Scotch bonnet (do not pierce now).
  9. 9
    Off heat, stir in the 30 ml / 2 tbsp lime juice, 10 g chopped parsley, and the 30 g / 2 tbsp butter if using. Taste; adjust salt (the soup usually needs another 4 to 6 g at this stage to bring the seasoning forward through the pumpkin).
  10. 10
    Ladle into wide bowls, ensuring each serving gets a piece of beef short rib, a mix of vegetables, vermicelli, and the orange broth. Serve with crusty bread or a side of white rice for the day-after meal.
Safety
  • Bone-in beef short rib braised to fully tender (internal 90 to 95 °C / 195 to 205 °F) rather than the USDA published-safe 145°F target Soup joumou is a braise; the short rib cooks well past the USDA published-safe instantaneous target of 145°F for whole-muscle red meat. The braise endpoint is 90 to 95°C, the temperature at which collagen has fully converted to gelatin and the meat slides from the bone. The 2+ hour simmer above 80°C provides massive pasteurization equivalency. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Dietary protein pivot→as vegetarian soup joumou (the Lenten or year-round version)

    Skip the beef short rib entirely. Substitute 800 g vegetable stock plus 60 g additional epis at the start. Add 200 g extra kabocha to the pumpkin (1 kg total) to deepen the body. The soup loses the bone-broth richness; compensate with 30 g extra butter at the finish and 10 g miso (white) stirred in with the lime juice. Acceptable for vegetarian households; loses some of the structural depth.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • kabocha squash → butternut squash, or any hard winter squash Butternut is the most accessible substitute and works similarly. Avoid pie pumpkin (too watery) and acorn squash (less sweet, stringier). Canned pumpkin puree is the wrong texture and will produce a mealy soup.
  • bone-in beef short rib → boneless beef chuck (700 g, in 4 cm chunks), or bone-in beef shank Boneless chuck works but contributes less body; add 30 g extra butter at the finish. Bone-in beef shank is a closer substitute for the structural bone-marrow contribution.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → fresh epis (Haitian green seasoning, see Poul en sos recipe for build) make it yourself The poul en sos recipe builds a fresh batch of epis; use the same paste for this soup. Epis is the structural Haitian flavor base and homemade is meaningfully better than jarred.

Notes

Why the canonical vegetables. Each vegetable in soup joumou contributes a structural element: the kabocha for the orange body and sweetness; the beef short rib for the bass-note umami and bone-broth gelatin; the cabbage for textural crunch; the carrot and turnip for sweetness and structure; the leek and celery for the aromatic backbone; the vermicelli for the comforting starchy finish that distinguishes Haitian soup joumou from a generic pumpkin soup. The recipe survives small swaps but loses its identity if you skip more than one of the canonical components.

The puree-then-return technique. Pureeing all the kabocha and adding it back into the soup produces the canonical "creamy orange broth with chunky vegetables" texture. Leaving the kabocha as chunks (and not pureeing any of it) produces a clear-broth pumpkin soup that does not read like joumou; pureeing all the vegetables (not just the kabocha) produces a cream-of-pumpkin smoothie that also misses the point.

Why a 12-hour beef marinade. The epis-marinated beef is the Haitian standard for any meat preparation, and the soup tastes meaningfully different (better, deeper, more Haitian) with the marinated meat than with plain seared meat. Same-day soup joumou is acceptable but reads less Haitian.

Storage and remembrance. The soup is canonically made in massive quantities on December 31st evening (and overnight) for January 1st morning service, shared with extended family, neighbors, and anyone who comes by. Leftovers keep 4 days refrigerated and freeze well in 1 L containers. Reheat gently with a splash of water; the soup thickens overnight as the pasta absorbs broth, thin to consistency.

Sources
  • Haitian Independence Day canon (January 1, 1804) — Soup joumou is the dish of Haitian Independence Day, January 1, 1804, the day the Haitian Revolution succeeded and the colony of Saint-Domingue became the free Republic of Haiti. Under French colonial slavery, enslaved Haitians were forbidden to eat pumpkin soup; it was a delicacy reserved for the French. On Independence Day and every January 1st since, every Haitian household makes and shares soup joumou as an act of remembrance and celebration. UNESCO inscribed soup joumou as Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2021. The structural elements are: kabocha or pumpkin pureed into the broth, beef short rib for the body, the canonical vegetable list (cabbage, carrot, turnip, leek, celery, potato), vermicelli pasta, epis, Scotch bonnet, lime.
