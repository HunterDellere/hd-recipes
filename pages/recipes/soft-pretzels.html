Recipe

Soft pretzels

A lean bread-flour yeast dough, rolled into ropes and twisted into the lauge-pretzel knot, dipped in an alkaline bath (food-grade lye at 3% for the bakery-authentic mahogany skin, or a concentrated baked-baking-soda bath as the safe home alternative), slashed, salted with coarse pretzel salt, and baked hot. The alkaline surface is the entire dish: it raises the dough pH into the range where the Maillard reaction runs fast and dark, producing the glossy bronze-to-mahogany crust and the distinct pretzel flavor that no amount of ordinary baking delivers.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Lye safety, read before deciding which bath to use. Food-grade sodium hydroxide (lye) is caustic and will burn skin and eyes on contact. If you use the 3% lye bath: wear nitrile gloves and eye protection, work in a ventilated space, ALWAYS add the lye to the water and never water to lye (the reaction is exothermic and can spatter), use a stainless steel or glass bowl (never aluminum, which lye corrodes), and keep a bowl of vinegar nearby to neutralize any splash. Lye on raw dough is fully neutralized by the heat of baking and is food-safe once baked. If any of this gives you pause, use the baked-baking-soda bath in Phase 3b instead; it is genuinely good and carries none of the risk.
  • Total time is about 2 hours 15 minutes, most of it passive. Plan a 60-minute bulk proof and a short 15-minute bench rest after shaping. The dough is a stiff, low-hydration lean dough (about 55% hydration); it will feel firmer and less tacky than a sandwich dough, and that is correct.
  • If using the baked-soda bath, bake the baking soda first (Phase 3b), it takes an hour in the oven and must cool before you dissolve it. Start it during the bulk proof so it is ready when the pretzels are shaped, or bake it the day before.
  • Bring the bath ingredients and the shaped pretzels to room temperature before dipping, and have the salt, a slotted spoon or skimmer, and parchment-lined sheet pans staged. The dip is fast and you do not want to be searching for tools with a caustic or alkaline solution in front of you.

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the dough. Whisk the 7 g / 2¼ tsp instant yeast and the 12 g / 1 tbsp light brown sugar into the 275 ml / 1 cup plus 2 tbsp water warmed to 35 °C. Add the 500 g / 3¾ cups bread flour, the 9 g / 1½ tsp fine sea salt, and the 30 g / 2 tbsp softened butter. Mix to a shaggy mass, then knead 8 to 10 minutes by hand or 6 minutes in a stand mixer with the dough hook on medium. This is a stiff, low-hydration dough at about 55% hydration; it should come together smooth and firm, slightly tacky but not sticky. The strong gluten network is what lets you roll thin ropes that hold their twist without tearing. Enriched dough
  2. 2
    Bulk proof. Shape the dough into a ball, place it in a lightly oiled bowl, and cover with a damp towel. Let it rise at room temperature until roughly doubled, about 60 minutes. A stiff dough proofs a little slower than a wet one; judge by volume, not the clock. While it proofs, if you are using the baked-soda bath, start Phase 3b now.
  3. 3
    Shape the pretzels. Turn the dough onto an unfloured counter (a slightly tacky surface helps the rope grip and roll). Divide into 8 equal pieces of about 100 g each. Roll each piece into a rope about 50 cm long, thick in the middle and tapering to thin ends. To form the knot: lay the rope in a U, cross the two ends over each other twice to make a twist, then fold the twisted ends down onto the bottom of the U and press to seal. Set the shaped pretzels on parchment-lined sheet pans and let them rest uncovered 15 minutes; a brief skin-drying rest helps the bath cling evenly.
  4. 4
    Phase 3a, the lye bath (authentic). Read the safety note in Before You Start first. Put on nitrile gloves and eye protection. Pour the 1000 ml / 4¼ cups cold water into a stainless steel or glass bowl, then slowly add the 30 g / 2 tbsp food-grade lye to the water (never water to lye) and stir gently with a stainless spoon until dissolved; the solution will warm slightly. One at a time, lower each shaped pretzel into the bath with a stainless slotted spoon, submerge for 15 to 20 seconds, lift, let it drain, and return it to the parchment. Do not let the bath touch bare skin. This is the step that delivers the true Laugenbrezel skin and flavor. Skip to Phase 4. (When finished, neutralize leftover bath with a splash of vinegar before pouring it away.)
  5. 5
    Phase 3b, the baked-soda bath (safe alternative). First, bake the baking soda: spread the 80 g / ⅓ cup baking soda on a foil-lined sheet pan and bake at 120 °C / 250 °F for 1 hour, then cool completely. Baking drives off water and carbon dioxide, converting sodium bicarbonate into the more alkaline sodium carbonate (washing soda) and raising the bath pH from about 8 to about 11; the higher pH is what pushes the Maillard browning. Dissolve the cooled baked soda into the 1000 ml / 4¼ cups water just off the boil in a wide stainless or glass pot. Wearing gloves (the solution is mildly caustic), submerge each pretzel for 20 to 30 seconds with a slotted spoon, drain, and return to the parchment.
  6. 6
    Slash and salt. With a sharp paring knife or a razor, score one shallow slash along the thick belly of each pretzel (the fat part of the bottom curve). The slash splits open in the oven into the classic pretzel mouth and controls where the crust bursts. Immediately sprinkle the wet, dipped surface with the 15 g / 2 tbsp coarse pretzel salt; the alkaline film is tacky and grips the crystals. Salt now, while the surface is damp from the bath; a dried surface sheds the salt.
  7. 7
    Bake. Bake at 230 °C / 450 °F for 12 to 15 minutes, until the crust is deep mahogany brown and glossy. The dark color is the visual proof that the alkaline surface did its job: the raised pH accelerates the Maillard reaction between the dough sugars and amino acids, so the crust browns far faster and darker than ordinary bread at the same temperature, and the same reaction generates the roasted, faintly bitter pretzel aroma. Rotate the pans halfway through for even color. Pull when the tops read true mahogany, not merely golden; a pale pretzel is an under-developed pretzel.
  8. 8
    Cool and serve. Transfer to a rack and cool at least 10 minutes; the crust crisps and the crumb sets as it cools. Soft pretzels are best within a few hours of baking, while the skin still has its sheen and snap and the interior is tender. Serve warm with grainy or sweet-hot mustard, the alkaline-savory crust and the sharp acidic mustard are the canonical pairing, the mustard supplying the bright acid that the rich crust wants.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion shape and use→pretzel rolls or pretzel buns

    Skip the rope-and-twist. Divide the dough into 8 pieces, round each into a tight ball, rest 15 minutes, then dip and bake exactly as for pretzels. Slash a cross or a single line on top. Pretzel rolls at this hydration make an excellent burger or bratwurst bun; the alkaline crust holds up to sauce without going soggy. Bake 15 to 18 minutes since the mass is thicker.

  • Texture flavor→cheese-and-mustard pretzels (savory)

    After dipping and slashing, press a 1 cm cube of aged gruyere or sharp cheddar into the slash of each pretzel, or brush the surface with a thin film of grainy mustard before salting. The cheese melts into the crust mouth and browns into a frico-like edge; the glutamate in the aged cheese deepens the savory read of the alkaline crust. Reduce the finishing salt by half since the cheese brings its own.

  • Occasion flavor→cinnamon-sugar sweet pretzels

    Use the same dough and bath, but skip the coarse salt finish. Bake plain, then brush the hot pretzels with 40 g / 3 tbsp melted butter and toss in a mix of 50 g / ¼ cup granulated sugar and 6 g / 1 tbsp ground cinnamon. The alkaline crust still gives the mahogany color and pretzel base note under the sweet coating, which is what separates a real cinnamon-sugar pretzel from a glazed bun.

  • Regional grain→rye-laced pretzels

    Replace 100 g / ¾ cup of the bread flour with dark rye flour and add 4 g / 1½ tsp caraway seeds to the dough. The rye deepens the malty, earthy note and pushes the pretzel toward the Bavarian Brezn end of the spectrum. Rye lowers gluten strength, so the ropes are a touch more fragile; roll them slightly thicker.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • light brown sugar → barley malt syrup or diastatic malt powder Barley malt syrup (1 tbsp / 18 g) is the traditional sweetener and gives the most authentic malty pretzel flavor and a touch more crust color from its maltose. Diastatic malt powder (1 tsp / 4 g) adds enzymatic activity that boosts browning further. Honey or granulated sugar both work as plain substitutes if malt is unavailable; you lose a little of the malt depth.
  • food-grade sodium hydroxide (lye) → baked baking soda (sodium carbonate) The baked-soda bath in Phase 3b is the standard safe substitute and gives roughly 80% of the lye result: good color, good flavor, none of the caustic hazard. Plain unbaked baking soda dissolved in water (pH ~8) is the weakest option and gives a pale, faintly metallic crust; if you skip the baking step, at least use double the soda and the hottest water you can.
  • bread flour → all-purpose flour plus vital wheat gluten All-purpose flour alone makes a softer, less chewy pretzel that can tear when you twist thin ropes. Add 15 g / 2 tbsp vital wheat gluten per 500 g all-purpose flour to approximate bread flour's protein. See ingredients/bread-flour for the full conversion.
  • coarse pretzel salt → flaky finishing salt or pearl sugar Coarse pretzel salt is non-melting and holds its white crystal shape on the crust. Flaky salt (Maldon) works but dissolves a little into the damp surface. Pearl sugar is the move for the sweet variation. Avoid fine salt, which dissolves invisibly and over-salts.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → served with grainy or sweet-hot mustard make it yourself A house grainy mustard is the right partner for a fresh pretzel: the popped Brassica seeds give texture against the soft crumb and the vinegar acid cuts the rich alkaline crust. It is a five-minute grind plus a few days of mellowing, and a fresh batch reads sharper and brighter than any jarred mustard.
  • → served with grainy or sweet-hot mustard make it yourself Honey mustard is the sweet-hot direction for pretzel dipping: the honey rounds the mustard heat and the acid still cuts the crust. Homemade lets you dial the honey-to-mustard ratio and skip the cornstarch thickeners and stabilizers of the bottled version.

Notes

Why the alkaline bath is the whole dish. An ordinary bread dough sits around pH 5 to 6, slightly acidic from the yeast fermentation. At that pH the Maillard reaction (the browning between reducing sugars and amino acids) runs slowly, which is why ordinary bread browns to gold over a long bake. Raising the surface pH dramatically accelerates Maillard: the amino groups are deprotonated and far more reactive, so browning runs fast and dark even in a short bake. That is the entire reason a pretzel browns to mahogany in 12 minutes while a baguette takes much longer to reach a paler gold. The bath also gelatinizes the surface starch, which is what gives the skin its characteristic glossy sheen and chewy snap. The pretzel flavor itself, that roasted, faintly bitter, savory note, is a direct product of running Maillard hard at high pH.

Lye versus baked baking soda. Food-grade sodium hydroxide at 3% takes the surface to pH 13 to 14 and is the authentic Bavarian and Swabian method; nothing else matches its color, gloss, and flavor depth. Baked baking soda (sodium carbonate, pH ~11) is the safe home standard and gets you most of the way there. Plain unbaked baking soda (pH ~8) is the weakest and gives a pale, slightly soapy-metallic result. The hierarchy of result tracks the hierarchy of pH exactly: higher pH, darker crust, deeper flavor. The lye is fully neutralized by the heat of baking and the finished pretzel is completely food-safe; the hazard is entirely in the handling of the raw solution, which the safety note addresses.

Why bread flour and low hydration. The pretzel dough is deliberately stiff and lean (about 55% hydration, no enrichment beyond a little fat). The low water and high protein give a dough strong enough to roll into thin ropes that hold a tight twist without tearing or springing back, and a crumb that is chewy rather than fluffy. A wetter, softer dough makes a pretzel that sags out of its twist and bakes into something closer to a roll. The small amount of butter is the one concession to tenderness; leave it out entirely and the pretzel is more rustic and chewy, which is closer to a true Bavarian Brezn.

The malt question. Barley malt syrup is the traditional sweetener and contributes both flavor (the malty, roasted note that reads as quintessentially pretzel) and extra maltose for the crust to brown. Light brown sugar is the accessible stand-in used here; it feeds the yeast and adds a faint molasses note. If you want the most authentic result and can find it, use barley malt syrup, and if you want maximum crust color, add a pinch of diastatic malt powder for its browning enzymes.

Slashing and salt timing. The shallow slash on the belly is functional, not decorative: it gives the expanding crust a controlled place to burst, producing the open pretzel mouth instead of random cracking. Salt the moment the pretzel comes out of the bath, while the alkaline film is still tacky; that film is what glues the coarse crystals to the crust. A pretzel salted after the surface has dried sheds most of its salt in the oven.
