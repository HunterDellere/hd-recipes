Recipe

Brioche

A rich French enriched loaf built at roughly 55% butter to flour (baker's percent), 5 whole eggs, and cold milk, mixed in the strict order that makes brioche work: develop the gluten on flour, eggs, milk, and yeast first, then beat in cold butter cube by cube so the fat coats hydrated strands rather than coating dry flour. A long overnight cold proof firms the butter, slows the yeast to a clean ferment, and makes the slack dough handleable. Baked in a Pullman or fluted mold to a tender, tearable, pull-apart crumb under a deep mahogany crust.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a two-day bake. Day 1 is the mix, the bulk ferment (1.5 to 2 hours), and then a long overnight cold proof in the refrigerator (12 to 16 hours). Day 2 is shape, final proof (2 to 3 hours at room temperature), and bake. Do not try to compress it into one day; the cold proof is what firms the butter and makes the 55%-butter dough handleable, and it deepens flavor by slowing fermentation.
  • Eggs and milk go in cold, straight from the refrigerator, and the butter is used cold but pliable (15 to 16°C, bendable but not greasy). Brioche dough heats itself through long mixing; friction can drive it from 21°C to 28°C, and above 26°C the butter weeps out of the dough and the structure collapses. Starting cold buys you the mixing time you need. If your kitchen is warm, chill the mixer bowl and the dough hook for 20 minutes before you start.
  • A stand mixer with a dough hook is effectively required. Brioche needs 15 to 25 minutes of mixing to develop gluten and then emulsify the butter; doing this by hand is possible but punishing, and hand mixing tends to overheat the dough through body contact. Cube and re-chill the butter while the gluten phase runs so it is cold the moment you need it.
  • Weigh everything. Brioche is an emulsion held together by structure, and the ratios (hydration, fat, egg, salt, yeast) are tighter than in a lean bread. Volume measures introduce enough error to push the dough out of the workable window.

  3. 60 g whole milk, cold
  4. 45 g granulated sugar
  5. 7 g instant yeast
  6. 8 g fine sea salt

Phase 2, the butter (beat in last, cold)

  1. 200 g unsalted butter, cold but pliable, cut into 1 cm cubes, kept at 15 to 16°C (bendable, not greasy); re-chill if it softens while the gluten phase runs

Phase 3, egg wash and finish

  1. 50 g whole egg, for egg wash, beaten with a pinch of salt until fully liquid and strainable — (1 large egg; the pinch of salt breaks the egg structure so the wash brushes thin and bakes to an even mahogany gloss)
  2. 1 g flaky salt, optional for the crust optional — (a small pinch; optional, for a savory-leaning loaf)

Execution

  1. 1
    Day 1, build the dry and wet base. In the bowl of a stand mixer fitted with the dough hook, whisk together the 360 g / 3 cups bread flour, the 45 g / 3½ tbsp granulated sugar, and the 7 g / 2¼ tsp instant yeast. Keep the 8 g / 1¼ tsp fine sea salt to one side for now, direct contact between concentrated salt and yeast at this stage can slow the rise; it goes in once the flour buffers it. Add the 250 g / 5 cold eggs and the 60 g / ¼ cup cold whole milk.
  2. 2
    Develop the gluten BEFORE any butter. Mix on low (speed 2 on a KitchenAid) for 2 minutes to bring the shaggy mass together, then add the 8 g / 1¼ tsp fine sea salt. Raise to medium (speed 4) and mix for 8 to 10 minutes. The dough will climb from sticky and slack to a smooth, elastic, cohesive mass that wraps the hook and slaps the side of the bowl. This is the non-negotiable order: the gluten network must be fully built on the flour, egg, and milk while the dough is still lean, because once fat coats the flour the strands can no longer link. Check the window: stretch a walnut of dough thin between your fingers; it should pull to a translucent membrane without tearing. If it tears, mix 2 to 3 minutes more.
  3. 3
    Beat in the cold butter, cube by cube. With the mixer on medium-low (speed 3), add the 200 g / 14 tbsp cold cubed butter a few cubes at a time, waiting until each addition has disappeared into the dough before adding the next. This takes 8 to 12 minutes total and the dough will look alarming midway: it breaks, goes slack and greasy-looking, and seems ruined. Keep going. The yolk lecithin and the developed gluten are emulsifying the fat into the structure; the dough re-gathers into a glossy, satiny, cohesive mass that pulls cleanly off the bowl sides. Stop the moment it comes back together and looks smooth and shining. The dough temperature should still be at or below 26°C; if it climbs higher and the butter starts weeping, refrigerate the bowl 15 minutes, then finish.
  4. 4
    Bulk ferment at room temperature. Scrape the dough into a lightly buttered bowl, cover with a damp towel or a fitted lid, and let it rise at room temperature (21 to 23°C) until it has grown by about half again its size, 1.5 to 2 hours. It will not double like a lean dough; the fat weighs it down and the rise is slower and gentler. You are looking for a visibly puffed, domed mass that springs back slowly when pressed, not a full doubling.
  5. 5
    Deflate and cold-proof overnight. Gently deflate the dough by folding it over on itself three or four times in the bowl; this redistributes the yeast and degasses the large bubbles. Cover with a fitted lid or a damp towel and refrigerate 12 to 16 hours. This long cold proof does three jobs at once: it firms the butter so the slack dough becomes handleable, it slows fermentation to a clean low-acid profile that deepens flavor, and it relaxes the gluten so the dough shapes without fighting back. Do not skip this; warm 55%-butter dough is impossible to shape and bakes greasy.12 h
  6. 6
    Day 2, shape into the mold. Butter a 900 g Pullman loaf pan (or a large fluted brioche mold). Turn the cold dough onto a lightly floured surface; it should be firm and cool, like cold modeling clay. For a classic tearable pull-apart crumb, divide the dough into 4 equal pieces (about 200 g each), shape each into a tight ball by cupping and rolling on the bench until the surface is taut, and set them in a row in the buttered pan. For a fluted brioche à tête, shape a large ball and a small ball, set the large one in the mold and press the small one into a dimple on top. The tight surface tension is what gives the loaf a clean rise and an even crumb.
  7. 7
    Final proof at room temperature. Cover loosely with a damp towel and proof until the dough has risen to fill the pan and crests just over the rim, 2 to 3 hours at 21 to 23°C. Cold dough out of the fridge proofs slowly at first; do not rush it with extra warmth above 27°C or the butter softens and weeps before the structure sets. The dough is ready when a gentle fingertip press in a corner springs back slowly and leaves a faint dent, not when it springs back fast (underproofed) or collapses and stays dented (overproofed).
  8. 8
    Egg wash and bake. Heat the oven to 175°C / 350°F with a rack in the lower third. Strain the egg wash (the 50 g / 1 egg beaten with a pinch of salt) and brush a thin, even coat over the entire exposed surface, getting into the seams between the dough balls but not pooling at the base where it would scorch. If making a savory loaf, scatter the optional pinch of flaky salt now. Bake 35 to 45 minutes, rotating the pan halfway, until the crust is a deep mahogany and the internal temperature at the center reads 88 to 91°C / 190 to 195°F on an instant-read thermometer. Brioche is enriched and browns fast; if the top is dark by 25 minutes, tent loosely with parchment or foil and finish to temperature. Pull it at temperature, not by color alone, an underbaked center stays gummy. Maillard sear
  9. 9
    Cool fully before slicing. Turn the loaf out of the mold onto a wire rack within 5 minutes of leaving the oven so steam does not condense and soften the crust. Cool completely, at least 1.5 hours, before slicing. Cutting hot brioche tears the crumb and compresses the still-setting gluten into a dense streak; the structure needs to finish setting as it cools. The crumb should pull apart in tender, buttery, slightly stringy strands with an even fine grain, and the crust should be thin and lacquer-deep.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture crumb richness→brioche Nanterre vs brioche à tête

    The same dough shapes two classic ways. Nanterre (the four-ball-in-a-loaf-pan method above) gives even square slices ideal for toast and French toast. Brioche à tête (the topknot) is shaped one large ball plus a small ball pressed into a dimple, baked in a fluted mold for the iconic crowned shape; it reads more pastry, less sandwich loaf. The dough is identical; only the shaping and mold change.

  • Occasion savory direction→cheese-and-herb brioche, savory loaf

    Cut the sugar to 20 g / 1½ tbsp. After the butter is fully beaten in, knead in 90 g / 1 cup grated aged Gruyère or comté and 4 g / 2 tbsp chopped chives on the lowest speed for 30 seconds, just to distribute. The cheese adds glutamate depth and the dough bakes into a savory loaf for sandwiches, croque monsieur, or burger buns (divide into 8 for buns, bake 18 to 22 minutes to 88°C internal).

  • Occasion sweeter, more festive→tangerine-and-pearl-sugar mousseline

    Push the butter to 250 g / 17½ tbsp (a true mousseline, ~70% baker's percent), add the finely grated zest of 1 orange with the eggs, and press 60 g / ⅓ cup Belgian pearl sugar into the surface after the egg wash. This is the dessert-leaning brioche, richer and more delicate, used for tarte tropézienne and holiday loaves. The higher fat makes the dough slacker; keep it colder and expect a softer, more fragile crumb.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • bread flour → all-purpose flour All-purpose (10 to 11% protein) works but yields a slightly softer, less structured crumb that can sag under the butter. If using it, mix 2 to 3 minutes longer in the gluten phase to compensate, and expect a marginally denser loaf. Do not use cake or pastry flour; there is not enough protein to suspend the fat.
  • instant yeast → active dry yeast Active dry is fine but must be bloomed first: warm the 60 g / ¼ cup milk to 38°C, stir in the yeast and a pinch of the sugar, and wait 10 minutes for it to foam before adding to the flour. Use the same 7 g / 2¼ tsp weight. Note this warms the dough slightly, so chill the eggs and bowl harder to compensate.
  • whole milk → heavy cream or evaporated milk Heavy cream pushes the loaf richer and softer (it adds fat on top of the butter); use the same 60 g but expect a more delicate crumb. Evaporated milk reads slightly caramelized and gives a deeper crust color. Avoid skim or plant milks, the lower fat and protein produce a drier, paler crumb.
  • unsalted butter → cultured European-style butter High-fat cultured butter (82 to 86% fat, Échiré, Beurre d'Isigny, Plugrá) is the upgrade here and what the French tradition uses; the lower water content emulsifies cleaner and the lactic tang reads through the crumb. Use the same weight. If using a salted butter, cut the added fine sea salt to 5 g / ¾ tsp.

Notes

The one rule that makes brioche work: develop gluten first, add fat last. Gluten is a network of glutenin and gliadin proteins that cross-link only when flour is hydrated and worked. Butter is a waterproofing fat; if it coats the flour before the proteins hydrate and link, the gluten never develops and you get a dense, crumbly, cakey loaf that cannot hold the air the yeast produces. So the method is strict: mix flour, eggs, milk, and yeast to a fully developed, windowpane-stage dough while it is still lean, then beat the cold butter in cube by cube so the fat lubricates and tenderizes an already-built network. The dough breaking and going greasy halfway through the butter phase is normal and correct; it re-emulsifies as the yolk lecithin and the worked gluten pull the fat back into suspension.

Why cold, and why overnight. At 55% butter to flour, this dough is a fat-in-dough emulsion held together by structure and temperature. Above about 26°C the butter melts out of suspension and weeps, the dough goes slack and oily, and it bakes greasy and flat. Cold eggs and cold milk buy mixing time; the long mix generates friction heat, and starting near refrigerator temperature keeps the finished dough under the failure threshold. The overnight cold proof then does triple duty: it solidifies the butter so the otherwise-unmanageable slack dough firms to cold-modeling-clay consistency you can actually shape, it slows the yeast to a long, clean, low-acid ferment that builds flavor without the sour edge of a fast warm rise, and it relaxes the gluten so the dough shapes without snapping back. Skipping the cold proof is the single most common reason a home brioche comes out greasy and misshapen.

Hydration and the role of egg. The eggs are the main liquid in this dough, not the milk. Egg provides water (eggs are ~75% water), protein for structure, and yolk lecithin, the emulsifier that lets a fat load this high integrate without breaking. That is why a lean-dough baker's first brioche often fails: they treat it like bread with butter added, but it is closer to a structured emulsion that happens to also ferment. Respect the egg as both hydration and emulsifier and the rest follows.

Baking to temperature, not color. Enriched dough browns fast because sugar, egg, and milk proteins all Maillard and caramelize at lower temperatures than lean dough. The crust can read done while the center is still gummy. Pull the loaf at 88 to 91°C / 190 to 195°F internal, full stop; tent it with parchment if the top darkens before the center is set. An instant-read thermometer is the difference between a tender, fully-set crumb and a dense underbaked streak down the middle.

Storage and the best use of day-old brioche. Fresh brioche is best within a day; the high fat keeps it soft longer than a lean loaf but it still stales. Wrap the cooled loaf in a clean towel or beeswax wrap and keep at room temperature 2 to 3 days, or slice and freeze for up to 2 months. Day-two brioche is the ideal French toast and bread pudding base, the dense buttery crumb soaks custard without dissolving, and the slight staling actually improves the structure for soaking. Toasted with butter and jam is the simplest and arguably the best use of all.
