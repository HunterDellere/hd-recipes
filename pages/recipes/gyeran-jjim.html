Recipe

Gyeran-jjim

The Korean steamed savory egg custard: beaten eggs whisked with anchovy-and-kombu stock (or chicken stock), salt, scallion, and a small amount of sesame oil, then cooked gently in a clay pot or a heavy bowl over low heat until set into a soft trembling custard. The structural Korean banchan (side dish) that pairs with rice, soup, and grilled meat at every Korean table. Sometimes called a Korean chawanmushi cousin, though the architecture is meaningfully different: gyeran-jjim is denser, more egg-forward, and structurally always served in the cooking vessel rather than portioned.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a banchan side, not a standalone dish. Plan it alongside steamed rice, soup, and 2 to 4 other banchan; start the rice cooker first so the custard hits the table hot at the same time as everything else.
  • Pull the eggs to room temperature 30 minutes ahead. Cold eggs combined with the warm stock produce uneven setting and slow the gentle cook past the temperature window.
  • Make the kombu-anchovy stock first and cool it to room temperature before combining with the eggs. Hot stock cooks the eggs on contact and the custard sets streaky. The cool-down takes 5 minutes; budget for it before you start beating eggs.
  • The temperature window between perfect set (82 to 85 °C internal) and overcooked grainy weeping curdle (above 88 °C) is 5 °C wide. Pull the pot off the heat the moment the surface holds firm; the clay vessel continues cooking the custard for 2 to 3 more minutes after it leaves the burner. If you only have a thin metal pan, use a bain-marie to avoid scorching the bottom.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

The stock

  1. 5 g kombu (dried kelp), wiped clean with a damp cloth (do not rinse; the white powder is glutamate, structural for umami) — (about a 7 cm square piece; the kombu provides the structural amino acid umami base for the stock)
  2. 8 g dried anchovies (myeolchi, Korean dried anchovies, large), heads and dark intestinal tract removed (the head and gut read bitter); body broken in half — (about 8 to 10 medium dried anchovies; available at Korean and East Asian groceries; substitute katsuobushi or chicken stock, see substitutions)
  3. 200 g cold water

The custard

  1. 200 g large eggs — (4 large eggs at room temperature; the eggs are the primary ingredient and should be the freshest available)
  2. 6 g Korean fish sauce (or Japanese soy sauce, or Korean ganjang) — reads cleaner and more canonical, but Japanese shoyu is widely available and acceptable)
  3. 1 g fine sea salt

The finish

  1. 15 g scallion, thinly sliced into thin rings (1 to 2 mm); whites and greens used separately (whites go into the custard, greens are the finishing garnish)
  2. 3 g toasted sesame oil
  3. 2 g toasted white sesame seeds

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the stock: combine the 5 g / 7 cm square kombu, 8 g / 8 to 10 anchovies (heads removed), and 200 g / ¾ cup plus 2 tbsp cold water in a small saucepan. Bring to a gentle simmer over medium heat (small bubbles around the edge, no rolling boil; high heat extracts bitter compounds from the kombu). The moment small bubbles appear around the edge of the pan, reduce heat to low and simmer 4 to 5 minutes. The stock should be a pale amber color and smell of clean sea-and-fish umami. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a heatproof measuring cup; discard the kombu and anchovies. Let the stock cool to room temperature (about 5 minutes); using hot stock cooks the eggs prematurely when they are combined.
  2. 2
    In a medium bowl, beat the 200 g / 4 eggs with a fork or whisk for 30 seconds until uniform (no streaks of white; do not whip air into the eggs, which produces a foamy texture in the finished custard). Add the cooled stock (about 100 g, half the cooked amount; the rest will be added later), 6 g / 1 tsp fish sauce, 1 g / ¼ tsp salt, and 8 g / about 1 sliced scallion white. Stir to combine; pass the mixture through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean bowl to remove any chalazae (the white chord that anchors the yolk; removing it produces a smoother custard).
  3. 3
    Transfer the egg mixture to a small Korean clay pot (ttukbaegi, about 14 to 16 cm diameter, 5 to 7 cm deep) or a heavy ceramic bowl of similar size. The vessel should be about three-quarters full; the custard rises during cooking and a fuller vessel risks overflow. Add the remaining 100 g of cooled stock to the bowl just before cooking; the dilution at this point is what produces the soft trembling texture canonical to gyeran-jjim.
  4. 4
    Set the clay pot or heavy bowl directly over medium-low heat on the stove (if using a ttukbaegi, the clay can go directly on the burner; if using a ceramic bowl, set it in a wider pan with 2 cm of water around the bowl to create a bain-marie). Cook 10 to 12 minutes, stirring the surface gently with a chopstick or the tip of a spoon every 30 seconds for the first 3 minutes (this creates the small curd structure that defines gyeran-jjim and prevents a single thick skin from forming on top). After 3 minutes, stop stirring and let the custard finish cooking undisturbed; the surface will set into a soft cohesive layer with visible small curds throughout.
  5. 5
    The custard (built from the 200 g / 4 eggs) is done when the top is just-set (a gentle nudge with a chopstick shows the surface holding firm but with a slight tremble below; an instant-read thermometer in the center reads 82 to 85°C / 180 to 185°F). Pull off the heat the moment this happens; the clay vessel retains significant heat and continues cooking the custard for another 2 to 3 minutes. Overcooked gyeran-jjim is the most common failure mode; the 200 g / 4 eggs go from perfect to grainy and weeping in under a minute at the upper end of the cook.
  6. 6
    Sprinkle the 7 g / about 1 sliced scallion green over the top of the hot custard; drizzle with the 3 g / ½ tsp toasted sesame oil; scatter the 2 g / ¼ tsp toasted sesame seeds. Serve immediately, directly in the cooking vessel, with rice and other banchan as part of a Korean meal. The diners spoon the custard from the shared vessel onto their own bowls of rice; a small spoonful per bite balances the custard with the rice and the other banchan on the table.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine pivot→as Japanese chawanmushi (the steamed egg custard cousin)

    Increase the stock to 400 g (a 1:2 egg-to-stock ratio instead of the canonical 2:1 of gyeran-jjim); replace the Korean fish sauce with 6 g / 1 tsp Japanese light soy sauce; add 5 g / 1 tsp mirin. Portion into 4 small ceramic cups (chawan); add small garnishes to each cup (a thin slice of cooked shiitake, a small piece of cooked shrimp, a single ginkgo nut, a thread of yuzu peel); cover each cup with foil. Steam in a bamboo steamer (or in a covered pan over simmering water) for 12 to 15 minutes over medium-low heat. The result is structurally chawanmushi, the Japanese cousin; reads silkier, clearer, and more elegant than gyeran-jjim, with the garnishes visible suspended in the wobbling custard.

  • Protein protein addition→as gyeran-jjim with shrimp or mushroom

    Add 60 g of finely diced raw shrimp or 60 g of finely diced rehydrated dried shiitake (rehydrated 20 minutes in warm water, drained, diced) to the egg mixture in step 2. Cook as written; the protein addition reads as a more substantial side dish or as a light main with rice. Pairs especially well alongside Korean BBQ as a structural cooling counterpoint to the grilled meat.

  • Occasion as a steamed version (without a clay pot)→as a bain-marie steamed gyeran-jjim

    Pour the egg mixture into a heavy ceramic bowl. Set the bowl in a wider pot; pour boiling water around the bowl to come 2 cm up the sides. Cover the pot with a tight lid; steam over medium-low heat 15 to 18 minutes until the custard is just-set. The result is structurally a steamed gyeran-jjim; reads slightly less rustic than the direct-stovetop ttukbaegi version, with a more uniformly soft texture. Useful for cooks without a clay pot.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dried anchovies (myeolchi, Korean dried anchovies, large) → katsuobushi (Japanese dried bonito flakes) or chicken stock Dried anchovies are the canonical Korean choice and read as the structurally Korean umami base; katsuobushi (5 g, steeped with the kombu for 1 minute only, then strained) produces a Japanese-leaning stock that reads more delicate and slightly more elegant; chicken stock (200 g of light unsalted chicken stock, in place of the cold water and skipping the kombu-anchovy steep) reads heavier and richer and is the standard fallback when neither Korean nor Japanese stock ingredients are available. The chicken-stock version reads less authentically Korean but is acceptable as a weeknight shortcut.
  • Korean fish sauce (or Japanese soy sauce, or Korean ganjang) → light soy sauce (Japanese shoyu) Korean fish sauce reads cleaner and more canonical for gyeran-jjim and is the right choice when available; Japanese light soy sauce is widely available and produces an acceptable result. Avoid dark soy sauce (which is too dark and salty for this application).

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → dried anchovies (myeolchi, Korean dried anchovies, large) make it yourself When making the Japanese chawanmushi modification or when Korean dried anchovies are unavailable, the homemade ichiban dashi (the canonical Japanese first dashi from kombu and katsuobushi) is the structurally adjacent stock and the cleanest substitution; the kombu base is shared and only the protein source differs.

Notes

Gyeran-jjim is one of the most structurally precise egg preparations in any cuisine because the temperature window for perfect texture is narrow. Eggs begin to coagulate at 62°C (the yolk) and 65°C (the white); the custard reads structurally cohesive at 80 to 85°C; above 88°C the egg proteins over-coagulate and squeeze water out (the curdling reaction known as syneresis), and the smooth custard turns grainy and weeping. The 5°C window between perfect and overcooked is what makes gyeran-jjim a technique-driven dish; the cook must use visual cues (just-set top, slight tremble below) and the structural retained heat of the clay vessel rather than a strict timer to judge doneness.

The stock is structural. The single greatest difference between an excellent gyeran-jjim and a flat one is the quality of the stock. Plain water produces a custard that reads as scrambled eggs in a different format; the kombu-and-anchovy stock provides the structural amino acid umami base that integrates with the eggs and turns the dish from a simple egg preparation into a complete savory custard. The 4-to-5-minute simmer of kombu and anchovies in cold water is short but essential; the time extracts the glutamates from the kombu (the structural umami source) and the inosinate from the anchovies (which synergistically amplifies the glutamate umami by a factor of 8 in the human taste perception system). This umami synergy is the reason Korean and Japanese stocks combine kombu (or kelp) with a protein source; the combination is structural, not optional.

The ttukbaegi clay pot is canonical and structural. The thermal mass of the clay vessel performs two functions: it heats slowly and evenly during the cook (preventing localized overcooking on the bottom), and it retains heat after coming off the burner (continuing to cook the custard for 2 to 3 minutes after the pot is removed from the heat). This residual heat is the reason gyeran-jjim is pulled at 82 to 85°C rather than at the target final temperature of 85 to 87°C; the clay finishes the cook. A heavy ceramic bowl approximates the thermal mass and is the closest substitute; a thin metal pan reads wrong and risks overcooking the bottom before the top is set.

The stir-then-stop technique is the structural detail that produces the canonical small-curd texture. Continuous stirring throughout the cook would produce a scrambled-egg texture; no stirring would produce a single thick skin on top with uncooked egg below. The 30-second stirs for the first 3 minutes break the setting egg into small soft curds throughout the volume of the custard, while the subsequent undisturbed cook lets those curds set into a cohesive whole. The result is the characteristic gyeran-jjim texture: small visible curds suspended in a soft cohesive matrix, with a just-set top.

Versatility. Gyeran-jjim is structurally a Korean banchan and the canonical Korean steamed egg preparation. Use it as: the canonical egg-based side dish at any Korean meal (alongside rice, soup, kimchi, and 2 to 4 other banchan; the standard structural anchor of the Korean home table); a structural side dish alongside Korean BBQ (the soft cool custard balances the rich grilled meat); a structural side dish alongside Korean stews (gochujang jjigae, kimchi jjigae, sundubu); a light primary dish with rice (a portion of gyeran-jjim plus a bowl of rice plus a single banchan is a complete light Korean meal); a base for breakfast (with a fried egg on top, a drizzle of gochujang sauce, and a sprinkle of furikake; an inventive modern Korean breakfast preparation); a sauce-replacement for steamed vegetables (a small amount stirred into hot steamed vegetables binds them with structural umami); and as a Japanese-style chawanmushi (see modifications) for cross-cuisine Asian dinners.

Pairs across Korean (canonical), Korean-American (where gyeran-jjim has become a structural component of the diaspora table), broader East Asian (the Japanese chawanmushi cousin is structurally adjacent; Chinese steamed egg custards (zheng dan) share architectural DNA), and contemporary American Asian cookery.

Storage. Best eaten immediately while hot from the pot; the texture begins to degrade within 30 minutes as the custard cools and the egg structure firms. Refrigerator storage is possible but the texture changes significantly; cold gyeran-jjim reads dense and rubbery compared to the hot custard. If reheating, use a gentle 30-second steam in a bamboo steamer (not a microwave, which curdles the eggs). The dish is best made fresh for each meal; the cook time is short enough (15 minutes total active) to make this practical even on weeknights.

Sources
  • The Korean home and restaurant canon. The dish is one of the most universal Korean banchan; nearly every Korean meal at a restaurant includes a small bowl of gyeran-jjim, and most home cooks make it weekly. The dish exists in two structural preparations: a stove-top version cooked in a small heavy clay pot (ttukbaegi, the canonical vessel) and a steamed version cooked in a bain-marie. The stove-top ttukbaegi version is the more common restaurant preparation; the steamed version is more common at home where a clay pot is not available. — The dish is structurally a savory steamed custard with eggs as the primary ingredient (in contrast to chawanmushi, the Japanese steamed egg custard, where the eggs are diluted more heavily with dashi and the dish reads as a clear soup with garnishes set in a wobbling matrix). Gyeran-jjim reads more egg-forward, denser, and slightly more rustic; the texture should be soft and trembling but cohesive, not the silky-clear setting of chawanmushi. The defining elements are: the anchovy-and-kombu stock (which provides the structural umami base), the egg-to-stock ratio (canonically 2 to 1 by weight, denser than chawanmushi), the gentle heat (the egg coagulates above 80°C but tightens and curdles above 88°C; the cook must stay below the upper limit), and the finish with scallion and sesame oil. The clay pot (ttukbaegi) is canonical because it retains heat and continues cooking the custard after it comes off the burner, producing the structurally cohesive set; a heavy ceramic ramekin is the closest substitute.
