Cantonese clay-pot chicken with shiitake and taro

Bone-in chicken thighs and starchy taro red-cooked in a clay pot under a lid, the braising liquid built from light soy for salt, dark soy for color, Shaoxing for depth, and rock sugar for a clean gloss. The taro half-collapses into the sauce and thickens it without starch; rehydrated shiitake donate guanylate that multiplies the soy glutamate into something far larger than either alone. A finishing splash of black vinegar cuts the richness and lands the dish.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Optional but recommended brine: dissolve 30 g / 2 tbsp kosher salt in 1 L / 4 cups cold water and submerge the thighs 30 to 45 minutes, then pat fully dry. A short wet brine seasons the meat to the bone and buys insurance against the lean thigh edges drying during the braise. If you brine, cut the salt added during cooking to the soy alone and taste before any final salt.
  • Soak the dried shiitake in 300 ml / 1¼ cups just-boiled water for at least 30 minutes before you start cooking; the soaking liquid IS the braising stock and carries most of the mushroom glutamate. Start this first, or soak overnight in cold water for a cleaner, less bitter extraction.
  • Steam jasmine rice to finish 5 minutes before the braise; clay-pot chicken is served immediately, spooned over hot rice so the sauce soaks in.
  • Taro is mildly irritating to handle raw (calcium oxalate raphides); peel under running water or wear gloves, and do not rub your eyes. Cooking destroys the irritant completely.

Mise en place by phase 7 phases ▾

Phase 1, the soak

  1. 30 g dried shiitake mushrooms, soaked in 300 ml just-boiled water 30 min, stems removed, caps halved; reserve the soaking liquid — (about 8 medium caps; the reserved liquid is the braising stock)

Phase 2, the chicken

  1. 1000 g bone-in skin-on chicken thighs, patted very dry, salted with 5 g / 1 tsp kosher salt if not brined — (about 6 thighs; bone and skin give the sauce gelatin and rendered fat. Boneless thighs work but the braise is leaner)
  2. 15 g neutral oil

Phase 3, aromatics

  1. 30 g ginger, sliced into coins, lightly smashed — (a 5 cm / 2 inch knob)
  2. 25 g garlic, whole cloves, smashed — (about 6 cloves)
  3. 40 g scallion, whites cut into 4 cm batons, greens sliced thin and reserved for garnish — (about 4 scallions)
  4. 3 each dried chiliesoptional — (optional, for a gentle background warmth; left whole they perfume without dominating)

Phase 4, braising liquid

  1. 45 g light soy sauce
  2. 15 g dark soy sauce
  3. 60 g Shaoxing wine
  4. 20 g rock sugar, crushed if in large lumps
  5. 300 ml reserved shiitake soaking liquid

Phase 5, the vegetables

  1. 350 g taro, peeled, cut into 3 cm chunks — (about half a medium corm; the starchy thickener. The flexible slot, see notes for swaps)
  2. 150 g oyster mushrooms, torn into large pieces — (added late so they keep some bite)

Phase 6, finish

  1. 10 g Chinkiang black vinegar
  2. 5 g toasted sesame oil

Phase 6, serve

  1. 400 g steamed jasmine rice

Execution

  1. 1
    Confirm the 30 g dried shiitake have soaked at least 30 minutes in their 300 ml just-boiled water and gone fully pliable. Squeeze them out over the bowl, remove and discard the woody stems, halve the caps, and decant the 300 ml soaking liquid carefully off the grit that settles at the bottom. Reserve both.
  2. 2
    Heat the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil in a clay pot or heavy Dutch oven over medium-high until shimmering. Lay the 1000 g chicken thighs skin-side-down and sear undisturbed 5 to 6 minutes until the skin is deep golden and releases cleanly. This is the Maillard layer that anchors the braise; do not crowd or move them. Flip 1 minute, then lift the thighs to a plate. Pour off all but about 1 tbsp / 15 g of the rendered fat. Maillard sear
  3. 3
    Lower heat to medium. Add the 30 g smashed ginger coins, 25 g smashed garlic, the 40 g scallion whites, and the 3 dried chilies (if using). Sweat in the rendered fat 2 minutes until fragrant and the garlic edges turn pale gold. Do not let the garlic brown to bitter.
  4. 4
    Add the 60 g / ¼ cup Shaoxing wine and scrape the fond off the bottom as it sizzles; let it reduce by half, about 1 minute, to cook off the raw alcohol. Stir in the 45 g / 3 tbsp light soy, 15 g / 1 tbsp dark soy, and 20 g / 2 tbsp rock sugar until the sugar dissolves. Pour in the 300 ml / 1¼ cups reserved shiitake liquid.
  5. 5
    Return the chicken skin-side-up, nestling the thighs so they sit three-quarters submerged with the skin above the liquid line. Tuck in the halved shiitake caps and the 350 g taro chunks around them. Bring to a gentle simmer, then cover and reduce to low. Braise 30 minutes; the taro should be knife-tender and beginning to fray at the edges.
  6. 6
    Uncover. Scatter in the 150 g torn oyster mushrooms, pushing them into the liquid. Raise heat to medium and simmer uncovered 10 to 12 minutes to cook the mushrooms and reduce the sauce by roughly a third; it should turn glossy and coat a spoon. Tilt the pot and baste the chicken skin with the reducing liquid two or three times. The fraying taro will thicken the sauce on its own; resist adding starch.
  7. 7
    Pull off the heat. Stir the 10 g / 2 tsp Chinkiang black vinegar and 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil through the sauce, keeping them off direct heat so the vinegar stays bright and the sesame oil does not scorch. Taste: the braise should read salty-savory first, with a clean sweet middle and an acid lift on the finish. If flat, a few more drops of vinegar; if sharp, a pinch of sugar.
  8. 8
    Rest 5 minutes uncovered so the sauce tightens. Spoon the chicken, taro, and mushrooms over hot jasmine rice, pour the glossy sauce across, and shower with the reserved sliced scallion greens (from the 40 g scallions). Serve immediately.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture vegetable→kabocha or sweet potato instead of taro

    Swap the 350 g taro for 350 g peeled kabocha squash or sweet potato in 3 cm chunks. Both are sweeter and softer than taro, so add them 10 minutes later (at the 20-minute mark of the covered braise) to keep them from dissolving entirely, and cut the rock sugar to 12 g since the vegetable now contributes its own sweetness.

  • Texture vegetable→daikon and waxy potato

    Use 250 g daikon and 200 g waxy potato in 3 cm chunks in place of the taro. Daikon drinks up the braise and goes silky; waxy potato holds its shape and will not thicken the sauce the way taro does, so reduce the finished liquid 5 minutes longer or add a 1 tsp cornstarch slurry. A cleaner, less starchy braise.

  • Regional cuisine→three-cup chicken (san bei ji) lean

    Drop the taro and shiitake liquid. Braise with equal parts (about 60 g each) toasted sesame oil, Shaoxing, and dark soy as the "three cups," no added water, and finish with a large handful of Thai basil stirred in off heat. The dish reduces to a near-dry glaze rather than a saucy braise; this is the Taiwanese cousin of the clay-pot style.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • rock sugar → granulated sugar Use the same weight. Rock sugar gives a cleaner sweetness and slightly glossier braise; granulated is a fine everyday substitute.
  • Shaoxing wine → dry sherry The closest match in oxidized, nutty depth. Use the same volume. Dry white wine is not a substitute; it lacks the oxidized character and reads sour.
  • taro → sweet potato See modifications for timing. Sweeter and softer; add later and cut the rock sugar.
  • bone-in skin-on chicken thighs → boneless skinless chicken thighs Workable but leaner and less glossy; the sauce loses the gelatin from the bones. Reduce the covered braise to 22 minutes and add a tablespoon of neutral oil to compensate for the missing skin fat.

Notes

Why two soy sauces. Light soy (sheng chou) is the salt and the primary glutamate; dark soy (lao chou) is almost entirely about color and a faint molasses roundness, carrying very little salt. Using only light soy leaves the braise pale and thin-looking; using only dark soy makes it bitter and oversweet before it is salty enough. The split is structural to red-cooking, not optional seasoning.

Why the shiitake soaking liquid is the stock. Dried shiitake are dense in guanylate, a nucleotide that multiplies the perception of the glutamate from soy through umami synergy. The compound leaches into the soaking water, so the liquid carries more savory weight than the rehydrated caps themselves. Discarding it and braising in plain water throws away most of the dish's depth. Decant carefully; the grit that settles is sandy and gritty in the mouth.

Why taro is the thickener. Taro is high in fine starch that gelatinizes and partly dissolves over a 30-minute braise, thickening the sauce from within and giving it body without the gummy, translucent quality a cornstarch slurry leaves. It is the reason this braise needs no added starch. If you swap to a waxy potato that holds its shape, you lose that self-thickening and should reduce longer or add a small slurry.

Why the vinegar goes in off heat. Chinkiang black vinegar is fruity and malty, and its volatile aromatics are what lift a heavy soy braise. Boiling it drives those volatiles off and leaves only flat sourness, so it goes in at the very end, off direct heat. The same logic governs the finishing sesame oil, which turns bitter if cooked.

Why sear before braising. The skin-side sear builds a Maillard fond on the chicken and the pot bottom that dissolves into the braising liquid when you deglaze with Shaoxing, contributing roasted, savory depth a bare simmer never develops. Pour off most of the rendered fat afterward or the finished sauce turns greasy.

  • Adapted from Cantonese clay-pot (bou zai) tradition — The clay pot (sa bou) is a low-fired earthenware vessel that heats slowly and holds heat, producing a gentle even braise and, in the rice version, the prized crust at the bottom.
