Gochujang-glazed seared tuna

Sashimi-grade tuna blocks brushed with a thin gochujang glaze cut with rice vinegar and honey, seared fast and hot so the sugars in the glaze lacquer and lightly char while the center stays cool and ruby, then sliced and finished with toasted sesame and scallion. The Korean sweet-spicy-fermented register applied with restraint: the glaze is a thin coat that caramelizes on the surface, not a braising sauce, so the fish reads bright and clean rather than heavy.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The tuna is served rare, so it must be sashimi-grade or commercially flash-frozen for parasite kill (a home freezer cannot do this). Ask the counter "was this commercially flash-frozen," not "is this sushi-grade." If you cannot source it, sear the same glazed block through to a cooked tuna-steak doneness instead, see the modification.
  • The glaze carries sugar from both the honey and the gochujang rice base, and sugar burns fast in a smoking pan. Keep the coat thin, keep the sear short, and have the plate and garnishes ready, this is a sub-90-second sear with no time to fix a scorching glaze mid-cook.
  • Sear from cold. A fridge-cold (or briefly freezer-chilled) block lets the glaze lacquer and the surface set while the center stays under 50 °C; a room-temperature block overshoots the center in the same time. Do not temper the fish.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the glaze

  1. 30 g gochujang
  2. 15 g rice vinegar
  3. 12 g honey
  4. 10 g light soy sauce
  5. 3 g toasted sesame oil
  6. 4 g garlic, grated to a paste, microplaned — (1 clove; raw garlic pungency, Korean cooking is heavy on it)

Phase 2, the tuna

  1. 350 g sashimi-grade tuna loin, in two blocks, two rectangular blocks about 4 to 5 cm thick, patted completely dry; yellowfin or bigeye
  2. 3 g kosher salt
  3. 15 g neutral oil (high smoke point: refined avocado, rice bran, or grapeseed)

Phase 3, the finish

  1. 8 g white sesame seeds, toasted, dry-toasted to pale caramel-bronze
  2. 15 g scallion, sliced thin on the bias — (1 to 2 scallions, green and pale-green parts)
  3. 1 g gochugaru (Korean chili flakes) optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Mix the glaze. In a small bowl whisk the 30 g / 1½ tbsp gochujang, the 15 g / 1 tbsp rice vinegar, the 12 g / 2 tsp honey, the 10 g / 2 tsp light soy sauce, the 3 g / ½ tsp toasted sesame oil, and the 4 g / 1 clove grated garlic into a smooth, pourable glaze. It should be the consistency of thin ketchup; if it is paste-thick, loosen with another few drops of rice vinegar. Reserve about a third of the glaze in a separate clean bowl to brush on after searing (never reuse the brush that touched raw fish for the finishing glaze).
  2. 2
    Toast the sesame and prep the finish. Put the 8 g / 1 tbsp white sesame seeds in a dry pan over medium heat, agitating, until pale caramel-bronze and fragrant, 3 to 4 minutes; tip onto a cool plate. Slice the 15 g / 1 to 2 scallions thin on the bias. Set both aside. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  3. 3
    Salt and glaze the tuna. Pat both 350 g / 12 oz tuna blocks bone-dry, surface water steams and makes the glaze slide. Season all faces with the 3 g / ¾ tsp kosher salt. Using the brush you will discard, paint a thin even coat of glaze (from the non-reserved portion) over all four long faces and the two ends. Thin is the operative word: a thick coat scorches before the surface sets.
  4. 4
    Heat the pan. Place a heavy stainless or cast-iron skillet over high heat for 3 to 4 minutes until smoking. Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil and swirl; it should shimmer and just smoke within 5 seconds.
  5. 5
    Sear fast, on the clock. Lay the first block in the pan and sear 30 to 40 seconds per face, rolling around all four long sides plus a 15-second touch on each end. The glaze sugars will catch and lacquer to a glossy, slightly charred red-brown, watch for the color, and pull the block before the glaze goes past deep brown into black-bitter. Total time in the pan is about 3 minutes. The cooked-opaque band should creep in only 2 to 3 mm, leaving a wide cool ruby center. Sear the second block the same way. The glaze chars faster than a bare crust, so lean toward the shorter end of each window. Maillard sear
  6. 6
    Slice across the grain and finish. Rest the blocks 60 seconds to halt carryover, then slice each across the grain into 1 cm slabs. The cross-section: a thin lacquered-red rim, a 2 to 3 mm pale cooked band, a wide ruby center. Fan the slices on a plate, cut-face up. Brush or drizzle a little of the reserved (clean) glaze over the slices, scatter the 8 g / 1 tbsp toasted sesame, the 15 g / scallion, and the optional 1 g / ½ tsp gochugaru. Serve immediately while the lacquer is still glossy and the center cool.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein no sashimi-grade fish available→fully cooked glazed tuna steak

    Do not serve it rare from a regular fish counter. Use the same glaze, but sear each face about 90 seconds and cook the center through, pulling at an internal 55 to 60 °C. Because the longer sear risks scorching the glaze, brush most of it on in the last 30 seconds and again off-heat, so the sugars caramelize without burning. The fish reads firmer; the glaze carries it.

  • Regional heat and depth→doenjang-gochujang blend

    Replace 10 g of the gochujang with 10 g doenjang (Korean fermented soybean paste). The doenjang brings a funkier, earthier, less sweet backbone, so the glaze reads more savory-fermented and less sweet-spicy. Add 5 g more honey if you want to keep the lacquering sugar level constant. This is closer to a ssamjang register.

  • Occasion service as a rice bowl→gochujang tuna deopbap

    Slice the seared glazed tuna over a bowl of hot short-grain rice, add quick-pickled cucumber and shredded perilla or lettuce, top with the sesame, scallion, and a soft egg yolk. Mix the reserved glaze thinned with a little more sesame oil through the rice at the table. The dish becomes a one-bowl meal in the bibimbap-adjacent idiom.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • sashimi-grade tuna loin → sashimi-grade salmon or albacore loin Salmon takes the gochujang glaze beautifully (the oil-rich flesh balances the sweet-spicy lacquer) but reads richer; sear it slightly less. Albacore is softer and paler; handle identically.
  • gochujang → no true substitute Gochujang is structurally Korean and the identity of this dish. A blend of white miso, sambal, and honey approximates the sweet-spicy-fermented profile for a non-Korean fusion read but is not the same paste; if you do not have gochujang, make the sesame-crust or ginger-scallion seared tuna on this site instead.
  • honey → maple syrup, or sugar dissolved in the rice vinegar Maple reads slightly woodier but lacquers the same way; plain sugar (10 g) covers the caramelizing function without added flavor. Korean cooks often use oligodang (rice syrup) here, which is the most traditional and gives the glossiest lacquer.
  • rice vinegar → apple cider vinegar or a squeeze of lemon The acid keeps the glaze from reading flatly sweet. Cider vinegar is sharper and fruitier; lemon adds citrus brightness and tilts the dish slightly away from the Korean register.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → gochujang make it yourselfstub House gochujang lets you set the heat and the sweetness to your own palate and skip the corn syrup that loads many commercial tubs. It is a long ferment (weeks of aging in a warm spot) but a single batch lasts months and the difference, a cleaner sweetness and a deeper fermented backbone, is exactly what shows through on a thin glaze where the paste is brushed on rather than buried in a braise.
  • → light soy sauce make it yourselfstub A house light soy adds a fresher, less-pasteurized salt-and-glutamate note than bottled, and on a glaze this lean the soy is a load-bearing flavor rather than a background one. A single long-ferment batch seasons dozens of dishes.

Notes

Why thin glaze, not braising sauce. Gochujang's default home in Korean cooking is in quantity, 40 to 60 g braising a pan of dakgalbi, or stirred through a stew. Here it works in the opposite direction: a thin brushed coat whose job is to lacquer and lightly char in the sear, contributing color and a sweet-spicy-fermented edge while staying out of the way of the fish. Keep the coat thin and the sear short and the gochujang reads as a seasoning that frames the tuna; go thick and long and it reads as a sauce that smothers it. The user-facing rule: you should taste tuna first, glaze second.

Sugar and the scorch line. The glaze carries sugar from the honey and from gochujang's rice base, and sugar caramelizes then carbonizes fast in a smoking pan. That is a feature, the light char on the lacquer is most of the flavor, and a liability, a beat too long and the glaze goes acrid black. The mitigations stack: keep the coat thin, sear toward the short end of each window, and lean on the cold center to buy time (a cold block tolerates a slightly longer surface sear before the heat reaches the middle). Reserve a clean portion of glaze to brush on after searing so the fresh raw-garlic-and-vinegar brightness is on the plate alongside the caramelized version.

Why source still gates the dish. As with all seared-rare tuna, the center is functionally raw and the fish must be sashimi-grade or commercially flash-frozen for parasite kill. A home freezer does not reach the threshold. If the counter cannot confirm flash-freezing, cook the glazed block through (the modification above) rather than serving it rare; the glaze is forgiving enough that a fully-cooked version still eats well.

The finish does real work. The raw scallion, the toasted sesame, and the optional gochugaru are not garnish, they are the dish's fresh-and-textural counterpoint to the cooked lacquer. The scallion adds a sharp allium bite, the sesame a nutty crunch, the gochugaru a fruity-warm chili note that reinforces the gochujang without adding wet sweetness. Add them after slicing, off the heat, so they stay crisp and bright.
