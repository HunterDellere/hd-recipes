Homemade ketchup (from canned tomatoes)

Heinz-pattern ketchup built from San Marzano canned tomatoes, white wine vinegar, light brown sugar, and whole spices. No high-fructose corn syrup, no flavored vinegar shortcuts; the body comes from real reduction.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Make this at least a day before you want to use it. Fresh-off-the-stove ketchup tastes rough and disjointed; the spices, vinegar, and tomato need 24 hours refrigerated to integrate. The flavor difference between hour-1 and hour-24 is dramatic.
  • Active time is about 25 minutes; the rest of the hour is hands-off reduction. Stir every 8 to 10 minutes to prevent scorching at the pot bottom; once it sticks, the whole batch picks up a burnt note.
  • Have a clean 500 ml / pint jar with a tight lid ready before you start blending. The hot blended ketchup should go into the jar warm, sealed, and cooled in the fridge, fast cooling preserves the bright color and prevents oxidation darkening.

Execution

  1. 1
    Tie the spice sachet ingredients (3 cloves, 4 allspice, 1 cinnamon stick, 1 g mustard seeds, 1 g peppercorns, 1 bay leaf) in a small square of cheesecloth or muslin secured with kitchen twine. The sachet lets the spices infuse without leaving particulates in the smooth final sauce.
  2. 2
    Warm the 15 g / 1 tbsp oil in a heavy-bottomed saucepan over medium-low heat. Add the 100 g / ½ medium diced onion and a small pinch of salt. Sweat 8 minutes, stirring occasionally, until completely soft and translucent. Do not brown; color development here turns the final ketchup darker and slightly bitter. Add the 2 smashed garlic cloves; cook 1 minute more.
  3. 3
    Add the 800 g can of tomatoes with all juices, breaking the whole tomatoes apart with a wooden spoon as you stir. Add the 60 g / ¼ cup tomato paste, the 60 g / ¼ cup light brown sugar, the 80 ml / ⅓ cup white wine vinegar, the 5 g / ¾ tsp salt, the 1 g / ¼ tsp smoked paprika, and the spice sachet. Stir to combine.
  4. 4
    Bring to a simmer over medium heat, then drop to medium-low. Cook uncovered at a steady simmer (small lazy bubbles across the surface, not a vigorous boil) for 45 to 55 minutes, stirring every 8 to 10 minutes. The sauce will reduce by roughly 40 percent and deepen from bright red to a darker brick. A wooden spoon dragged across the bottom should leave a clear trail that fills in slowly.
  5. 5
    Pull off heat. Remove and discard the spice sachet, squeezing it gently against the pot side to extract residual infusion. Fish out the garlic cloves and bay leaf if still visible.
  6. 6
    Transfer the sauce to a blender or use an immersion blender directly in the pot. Blend on high for 90 seconds; long blending is the secret to the silky, smooth, no-pulp texture that distinguishes ketchup from tomato sauce. The mixture will lighten in color slightly as air incorporates; that is fine, it darkens again on cooling.
  7. 7
    For the smoothest commercial-style texture, pass the blended sauce through a fine-mesh sieve, pressing with a spatula to extract everything. Discard the small amount of fibrous solids left behind. This step is optional but produces a noticeably better mouthfeel.
  8. 8
    Taste. Adjust for salt, vinegar (more if it tastes flat), and sugar (more if it tastes too acidic). Good ketchup should hit a clear sweet-sour-salt-umami balance with the umami and acid arriving slightly ahead of the sweet. Cool to room temperature, then transfer to a sealed jar. Refrigerate at least 24 hours before using; the flavor integrates and deepens overnight in a way that fresh-off-the-stove ketchup does not.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional flavor→fancy ketchup (with sherry and fish sauce)

    Add 30 ml / 2 tbsp dry sherry and 5 ml / 1 tsp fish sauce in the first reduction step. The sherry adds yeast-y depth, the fish sauce adds glutamate without obvious fish flavor. Closer to traditional 19th-century English ketchups, before tomatoes became the standard base.

  • Heat flavor→spicy ketchup

    Add 1 tbsp finely minced chipotle in adobo to the simmer, plus an extra ¼ tsp smoked paprika. Smoky, hot, complex; excellent on burgers and breakfast sandwiches.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • canned San Marzano tomatoes any high-quality canned whole peeled tomato

    San Marzano is the gold standard for its balanced sweet-tart profile and low seed count. Cento, Bianco DiNapoli, or any DOP-stamped Italian whole tomato all work. Avoid pre-crushed or pre-diced cans; they include calcium chloride that holds the chunks together and resists smooth pureeing.

  • white wine vinegar apple cider vinegar

    Heinz uses distilled white vinegar; white wine is more refined; apple cider is fruitier and produces a ketchup with a slight orchard note. All valid. Distilled white vinegar is too one-note sharp for a thoughtful homemade version.

Notes

Homemade ketchup is meaningfully better than commercial because you control three things commercial cannot: tomato quality (canned San Marzanos vs. industrial tomato paste from concentrate), sweetness level (light brown sugar at restrained dose vs. high-fructose corn syrup at industrial dose), and freshness (refrigerator-fresh aromatic compounds vs. shelf-stable preservatives). The trade-off is shelf life; homemade ketchup lasts about 6 weeks refrigerated, versus a year for commercial.

The Heinz target is roughly 25 percent sugar by weight in the final sauce. This recipe runs at about 12 percent. Most palates that grew up on commercial ketchup will read homemade as undersweet on first taste; the recalibration happens around the third use, when the tomato flavor starts to register as the primary note rather than sweetness. Adjust if needed, but try the lower-sugar version first.

Long blending is the engineering. Ketchup is smoother than tomato sauce because of how thoroughly the fiber is broken down; restaurant suppliers run the puree through a colloid mill for several minutes. A high-power home blender for 90 seconds approximates this. The final sieve removes the last fibrous particles and produces a silky, ribbon-like ketchup that pours rather than plops.
