Recipe

Salmon rice bowl with quick cucumber

Roasted salmon flaked over a bed of short-grain rice, finished with a quick rice-vinegar-and-salt cucumber pickle (15-minute brine), a soft-boiled jammy egg, generous furikake, and a small drizzle of soy and sesame oil. The bowl is built on three parallel tracks (rice on the longest pole, salmon for 12 minutes, cucumber pickle in parallel, soft egg in the last 7 minutes) that converge at a single assembly. Asian-leaning, weeknight-feasible, the rice and salmon batch-cook for next-day lunches.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The rice is the longest pole. Start the Japanese short-grain rice first: rinse it three times until the water runs nearly clear, then cook in a rice cooker or absorption-method on the stovetop (1 cup rice + 1.1 cups water, simmer covered 18 minutes, rest 10 minutes off heat). The rest is critical; the carryover steam finishes the cook and lets the grains absorb the last of the water without going gummy.
  • While the rice cooks, do everything else in parallel. Quick-pickle the cucumber (15 minutes brine, runs while everything else happens), prep the salmon for the oven (3 minutes), bring the egg water to a boil (8 minutes). When the rice rests, the salmon is in the oven, the cucumber has pickled, and the eggs are being soft-boiled and ice-bathed.
  • Soft-boiled eggs need a 7-minute boil from a rolling start and a hard ice-bath stop. The 7-minute window gives a jammy yolk that still flows when cut; 6 minutes is too loose, 8 minutes is too set. Time exactly.
  • Get the oven to 220 °C with the rack in the upper third before the salmon goes in. The salmon roasts in 10 to 12 minutes at that temperature; a colder oven extends the cook time and dries the surface.

  2. 10 g neutral oil (refined avocado or grapeseed)

  2. 20 g light soy sauce
  3. 8 g toasted sesame oil

Execution

  1. 1
    Start the rice first. Rinse the 360 g / 2 cups Japanese short-grain rice in a fine-mesh sieve under cold running water, swirling with your hand, until the water runs nearly clear (about 3 rinses). Drain well. In a heavy small saucepan with a tight lid, combine the rinsed rice with the 400 ml / 1¾ cups water. Bring to a boil over high heat, immediately reduce to the lowest possible simmer, cover, and cook 18 minutes without lifting the lid. Pull off heat and rest covered another 10 minutes; the carryover steam finishes the cook.
  2. 2
    Heat the oven to 220 °C / 425 °F with the rack in the upper third while the rice cooks. Line a small sheet pan with parchment paper.
  3. 3
    Make the cucumber pickle. Slice the 300 g cucumber into 2 mm rounds. In a non-reactive bowl (glass or ceramic), combine the cucumber, the 30 g / 2 tbsp rice vinegar, the 8 g / 2 tsp granulated sugar, the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt, and the 3 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds. Toss to combine. Let pickle 15 to 20 minutes at room temperature, tossing once at the halfway point. The cucumber releases water as it pickles, the brine dilutes slightly, and the slices become slightly translucent at the edges.
  4. 4
    Roast the salmon. Pat the 600 g salmon fillets thoroughly dry. Place skin-side down on the parchment-lined pan. Brush the tops with the 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil and season with the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt. Roast 10 to 12 minutes at 220 °C / 425 °F until the salmon flakes when prodded gently at the thickest point. Internal temperature should be 49 to 52 °C / 120 to 125 °F for medium-rare; the fish carries to 54 °C / 130 °F off the heat.
  5. 5
    Soft-boil the eggs. While the salmon roasts, bring a small pot of water to a rolling boil. Using a slotted spoon, gently lower the 4 cold eggs (200 g) into the boiling water. Set a timer for exactly 7 minutes. Meanwhile, prepare an ice bath (a bowl of ice and cold water). When the timer ends, immediately transfer the eggs to the ice bath and let cool 3 minutes. Peel the eggs under running cold water (the membrane releases easier under water) and halve lengthwise; the yolks should be jammy and just set.
  6. 6
    Assemble the bowls. Divide the rested rice (about 220 g cooked per bowl) across four wide shallow bowls. Flake the roasted salmon into 3 to 4 cm pieces directly off the pan (the skin can be eaten or discarded by preference) and divide across the bowls. Drain the cucumber pickle of any excess liquid (or pour a tablespoon of the brine over the rice for extra acid) and divide across the bowls in a small pile next to the salmon.
  7. 7
    Finish each bowl. Place a halved soft-boiled egg yolk-side up on each bowl. Scatter generously with the 12 g furikake (3 g per bowl), drizzle each bowl with about 5 g / 1 tsp of the light soy sauce and 2 g / ½ tsp of the toasted sesame oil. Scatter the 25 g sliced scallions across the bowls. Tear the 4 g / 1 nori sheet into pieces and scatter on top (optional). Serve immediately; the bowl is at its best with the contrast of warm rice-and-salmon against cold cucumber and warm jammy egg.
Safety
  • Pulling salmon at 49 to 52°C / 120 to 125°F internal (translucent ruby, just-set) rather than the USDA-published 145°F target Fatty fish like salmon turn dry, chalky, and fall-apart past 130°F internal; the cooking tradition in Japan, France, and modern chef-driven American kitchens targets 120-125°F for medium-rare salmon, which is below USDA conservative published-safe. Source from a fishmonger handling sashimi-grade or properly parasite-cleared aquacultured Atlantic salmon. Vulnerable diners (pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, very old) should be served salmon cooked to 145°F (an additional 3 to 4 minutes in the oven), accepting the texture loss. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→with tuna, smoked salmon, or sliced tofu

    Tuna: sear 600 g sushi-grade tuna 30 seconds per side over very high heat, slice thin. Smoked salmon: skip the roast, lay 300 g cold smoked salmon (sliced) over the rice. Tofu: pan-fry 400 g extra-firm tofu cubes in 15 g neutral oil over medium-high heat 10 minutes until golden on all sides for a vegetarian version.

  • Texture rice type→with brown rice or barley

    Short-grain brown rice: rinse, soak 30 minutes, cook with 1.5 cups water per cup rice for 40 minutes covered. Barley: cook pasta-style in salted water 25 minutes, drain. Both substitute for the white rice with more whole-grain character and higher fiber.

  • Occasion service form→as a chirashi-style platter

    Spread the rice into one wide bowl rather than four. Arrange the salmon, cucumber pickle, halved eggs, and scallions in distinct sections on top, like a Japanese chirashi (scattered sushi) presentation. Serve family-style with chopsticks; each diner builds their own bites from the platter.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • Japanese short-grain rice → Korean short-grain rice, Calrose, or jasmine Korean short-grain rice and Calrose substitute 1:1 with no other adjustment. Jasmine rice works but is long-grain and the bowl reads less cohesive (the rice does not stick together).
  • salmon fillets, skin-on → arctic char, steelhead trout, or skinless salmon Arctic char and steelhead trout substitute 1:1 with similar fat content and cook time. Skinless salmon works but the cook time drops to 9 minutes.
  • furikake → a mix of toasted sesame seeds + crumbled nori + bonito flakes; or just toasted sesame seeds Commercial furikake is the easiest path. A DIY version of 10 g sesame seeds + 3 g crumbled nori + 2 g bonito flakes substitutes well. In a pinch, just toasted sesame seeds work but lose the seaweed-umami complexity.
  • rice vinegar (unseasoned) → seasoned rice vinegar (omit the sugar and reduce salt by half), or white wine vinegar Seasoned rice vinegar already contains sugar and salt; adjust the pickle accordingly. White wine vinegar substitutes 1:1 but reads sharper and less rounded.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → furikake make it yourself Homemade furikake is a 10-minute toast-and-mix project that produces a fresher, more aromatic product than commercial. The dried seaweed, sesame, and bonito flakes lose volatile compounds in commercial packaging.

Notes

Why the rest matters for the rice. The 10-minute off-heat rest after the simmer is where Japanese-style short-grain rice gets its texture. During the active cook, the surface starches gelatinize but the interior is still firm. The rest lets the residual steam (~100 °C) penetrate the grains and finish the cook from the inside, while the surface starches retrograde slightly into a tackier, more cohesive texture. Skip the rest and the rice reads gummy on the outside and slightly raw at the center. This is not optional; it is the technique.

Why 7 minutes for the egg. Soft-boiled egg doneness is a function of yolk temperature, which is a function of cooking time at 100 °C water from a cold-start. A 7-minute boil from a rolling-boil drop brings the yolk center to about 65 to 68 °C, the temperature at which the yolk proteins are partially set (jammy, flowing slightly when cut) but not fully cooked (which would happen at 70 °C+). The 7-minute window is reproducible across egg sizes within ±10 seconds. The hard ice-bath stop is critical; carryover cooking in a hot egg quickly pushes the yolk past jammy into chalky.

The quick pickle is the bowl''s structural acid. Without the cucumber pickle, the bowl is rich and savory throughout (rice, salmon, egg, soy, sesame oil) with no acid contrast. The cucumber pickle contributes three things: bright vinegar acid (which makes every other flavor read more cleanly), cold crunch (which contrasts with the warm soft components), and visual brightness (the pale green against the orange salmon and white rice). The 15-minute brine is non-negotiable for the texture; less than 15 minutes leaves the cucumber raw and crunchy without integrating the brine, more than 30 minutes turns the cucumber soft and waterlogged.

Why parallel-track timing. The bowl''s 30-minute total time only works if all four components run simultaneously. Rice (28 minutes total, mostly passive) runs the whole window. Cucumber pickle (15 minutes brine) starts at the 10-minute mark. Salmon (12 minutes) goes in at the 15-minute mark. Eggs (7-minute boil + 3-minute ice bath) start at the 18-minute mark. All four finish within 90 seconds of each other and assembly takes 5 minutes. Running them sequentially would push the total to 60+ minutes and break the weeknight format.

Why a single bowl works for batch lunches. The format batch-cooks beautifully: cook the rice and salmon once on Sunday, pickle the cucumber Sunday night (it improves for 24 hours), boil the eggs Sunday night. Each weekday morning, assemble a bowl in 4 minutes by warming the rice (microwave 90 seconds with a damp paper towel on top, or eat cold which is also good), flaking the cold salmon over it, adding the pickled cucumber straight from the jar, halving a fresh-from-the-fridge egg, and finishing with furikake and the two drizzles. The bowl eats just as well cold or barely-warm as it does fresh.
