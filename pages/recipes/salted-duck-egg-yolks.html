Salted duck egg yolks (home brine cure)

Raw duck eggs cured thirty-five days in saturated brine, then steamed and the yolks separated. Dense, fudgy, oil-bleeding red-orange yolks for congee, mooncakes, and salted-egg-yolk sauce.

Brine

  1. 1500 ml water
  2. 360 g kosher salt
  3. 30 ml Shaoxing wine
  4. 5 g Sichuan peppercorns, lightly toasted optional
  5. 2 each star aniseoptional

Eggs

  1. 12 each fresh raw duck eggs

Execution

  1. 1
    Bring the 1500 ml / 6 cups water to a boil with the 360 g / 1½ cups kosher salt, the 5 g / 2 tsp Sichuan peppercorns, and the 2 star anise. Stir to dissolve the salt completely. Boil 3 minutes, then pull off heat and cool to room temperature; the brine must be fully cool before the eggs go in or the heat will partially cook the whites and disrupt the cure.
  2. 2
    Once cool, stir in the 30 ml / 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine. Wash the 12 duck eggs gently in cool water and inspect each shell for cracks; discard any with even hairline cracks, as the cure will salt the inside catastrophically through any opening. Pat the eggs dry.
  3. 3
    Layer the eggs into a clean 2-liter glass jar. Pour the cooled brine over to cover; the eggs will float, which is fine. Weigh them down with a small clean plate or a brine-bag (a zip-top bag filled with extra brine, sealed, and pressed onto the eggs) so every egg stays submerged. Seal the jar.
  4. 4
    Hold at cool room temperature (18 to 22°C / 65 to 72°F), out of direct sunlight, for 35 days. Do not refrigerate; the cure runs slower at fridge temperature and the cure penetration becomes uneven. Check the jar weekly to confirm no eggs have surfaced; press them back under if they have. The brine may darken slightly, this is normal.35 d 0 h
  5. 5
    After 35 days, pull the jar. Test one egg before processing the rest: crack it raw into a small bowl. The white should be liquid, glassy, and pale yellow with visible saline graininess at the edges. The yolk should be intact, deep orange-red, and dense (it should hold a clear shape when slid out, not flow). If the yolk is still pale-yellow and runny like a fresh egg, return the rest to the brine for another 5 to 7 days.
  6. 6
    Steam the eggs to set them. Place the eggs in a steamer basket over actively boiling steam and cook 12 to 14 minutes. The whites firm to a slightly tough opaque set; the yolks firm into the dense, fudgy red-orange that is the entire point of the ingredient. Cool to room temperature, then refrigerate.
  7. 7
    Separate the yolks for use. Crack each cooled steamed egg, halve gently, and slip the yolk out with a small spoon. The whites are aggressively saline and most cooks discard them, though they can be diced and used as a saline-savory accent in fried rice or congee. Refrigerate the yolks in a sealed container; they keep 3 weeks and freeze well for 3 months.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion cure timing→a faster, lighter cure (28 days)

    Pull the eggs at day 28 instead of day 35. The yolks read less dense and less red, but the whites are noticeably less aggressive in salt. Better for a cook who wants to use the whole egg as a side dish rather than only the yolk; under-cured for mooncake-grade yolks.

  • Regional aromatic profile→a Filipino-leaning cure (itlog na maalat)

    Skip the Sichuan peppercorns and star anise. Add 30 ml / 2 tbsp white vinegar to the brine. Cure 18 to 21 days at warmer room temperature (24 to 26°C / 75 to 78°F) and dye the cooked shells red with food coloring before storage; this is the visual marker by which Filipino markets distinguish them from fresh eggs.

  • Occasion shortcut→salt-sugar dry cure (cured-egg-yolk style)

    Skip the brine entirely. Use only the yolks, cured in a 70:30 salt-sugar bed for 7 days plus 2 days drying as in the cured-egg-yolk recipe. The end product is a hard, gratable yolk rather than a fudgy whole-yolk preparation; different ingredient, faster timeline. Use the dry cure when you want grateable umami, the brine when you want fudgy lava-yolk.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • duck eggs → chicken eggs Chicken-egg yolks cure on the same timeline but read smaller, less red, and less dense. Acceptable when duck eggs are unavailable; the Cantonese ingredient is specifically duck for the larger yolk and richer fat fraction (~30% vs chicken's ~27%).
  • kosher salt (Diamond Crystal) → fine sea salt Use 250 g / 1 cup fine sea salt instead. Diamond Crystal's flake structure makes it lighter by volume; the brine percentage is what matters, and 360 g DC ≈ 250 g fine sea salt.
  • Shaoxing wine → dry sherry or vodka The wine is doing antimicrobial work, not flavor work. Any 14%+ ABV alcohol works.

Notes

Why duck, why thirty-five days. Duck eggs have a larger yolk and a higher fat fraction than chicken eggs, and the Cantonese ingredient evolved around that specific composition. The 35-day mark is where the yolk has lost enough free water to consolidate into the dense, oil-bleeding state that mooncake bakers and congee cooks reach for, without losing so much that it goes chalky and crumbles. Going past 45 days risks chalky yolks. Going under 30 days produces yolks that look right on the cut but read closer to a normal hard-boiled yolk in texture and color.

Why steam, not boil. Steaming the cured eggs sets the whites and yolks evenly with carryover heat; boiling pushes too much heat into the egg too fast and risks toughening the yolk into a rubbery state instead of fudgy. The Cantonese tradition is steaming.

How to use the yolks. Halved or quartered on top of a bowl of plain congee, where the salt-fat punch turns rice into a meal. Buried whole inside a Cantonese mooncake or a salted-egg zongzi. Steamed, sieved, and sautéed in butter into the salted-egg-yolk sauce that goes over crab, prawns, fried chicken, pasta, popcorn, or croissants. Grated raw over hot rice or noodles, where the heat half-melts the yolk into a sauce. Folded into a bao filling or dim-sum custard.

How to use the whites. Most cooks discard them; the salt content is too aggressive for direct eating. The defensible reuse is a 3 to 5 mm dice folded into fried rice or stirred into congee at the table as a salt-and-protein topping. Treat them as a salt source rather than as eggs.

Failure modes. Cracked shells let the brine into the egg directly and produce inedibly salty whites and chalky yolks; cull cracked eggs at jar time. Cure temperature too cold (refrigerator) makes the salt penetration uneven and the cure runs longer than 35 days; some yolks will be under-cured and others will be at target. Direct sunlight oxidizes the yolk lipids and produces an off, fishy character. The brine going visibly cloudy or growing surface mold means contamination; discard the batch and re-do with cleaner eggs and fresh brine.
