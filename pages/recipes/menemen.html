Recipe

Menemen

The Turkish breakfast eggs: long Anatolian green peppers (sivri biber) sweated in olive oil until silken, ripe tomato cooked down to a thick base, then beaten eggs poured in and stirred just until the curds set into the tomato-pepper sauce as a single soft mass. The defining technique is the egg integration: not poached on top as in shakshuka, not scrambled separately as in a Western breakfast, but folded continuously into the warm sauce until the eggs and the sauce become one cohesive texture. Served straight from the pan with grilled bread for dipping and a glass of Turkish tea.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Have the bread on the grill or in the toaster before the eggs hit the pan; menemen is built to be sopped warm and the bread should land at the same time as the pan.
  • Crack the eggs into a small bowl before you start so they pour in one motion when the moment comes. Cracking egg-by-egg into a hot tomato pan risks overcooking the early eggs while you fumble with the last shell.
  • Use a 25 cm (10 inch) skillet for 4 eggs. Smaller pans pile the eggs too deep and the lower layer overcooks before the upper layer sets; larger pans spread the sauce too thin and the eggs dry out.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the pepper-tomato base

  1. 45 g extra-virgin olive oil
  2. 180 g long thin green pepper (sivri biber, charliston, or Anaheim/Cubanelle as fallback), stemmed, seeded, cut into 1 cm rings — (3 to 4 long peppers; structural for the slightly grassy, mildly spicy character; bell pepper alone reads too sweet)
  3. 400 g ripe tomato, cored, grated on the large holes of a box grater (skin discarded as it catches in the grater); or peeled (X-cut, blanched 30 seconds, peeled) and diced small — (2 large ripe tomatoes; grating is the structural Turkish technique because it produces a near-instant sauce without skins; canned crushed tomato is a fallback when fresh tomatoes are pale)
  4. 1 × 6 oz / 170 g can tomato paste
  5. 2 g pul biber (Turkish red pepper flakes, or Aleppo pepper / red pepper flakes as fallback)
  6. 4 g kosher salt
  7. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Phase 2, the eggs

  1. 200 g large eggs, beaten lightly with a fork until the yolks and whites are just combined (5 to 6 strokes; do not whip) — (4 large eggs at room temperature; cold eggs cool the pan and disrupt the integration)

Phase 3, the finish

  1. 40 g feta cheese (or Turkish beyaz peynir if available), crumbled into small chunks — (optional; some regional versions add cheese at the finish; sheep-milk feta is the closest substitute for beyaz peynir)
  2. 4 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the 45 g / 3 tbsp olive oil in a 25 cm (10 inch) wide skillet over medium heat. Add the 180 g / 3 to 4 sliced peppers; sweat 6 to 8 minutes, stirring occasionally, until fully softened and slightly translucent at the edges but not browned. The slow sweat is structural; the peppers should taste cooked-through and silken, not crunchy.
  2. 2
    Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp tomato paste; stir into the peppers and cook 60 seconds, allowing the paste to deepen in color and lose its raw-tomato edge. Add the 2 g / 1 tsp pul biber and stir 15 seconds to bloom the flakes into the oil (the oil should turn faintly red).
  3. 3
    Add the 400 g grated tomato along with 2 g / ½ tsp of the salt and the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. Bring to a simmer; cook 5 to 7 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the tomato has reduced visibly (by about a third), the surface oils are starting to separate at the edges, and the sauce holds a brief spoon-drag mark before flowing back together. Taste: the base should read of cooked tomato and pepper with gentle warm spice; adjust salt if flat.
  4. 4
    Reduce heat to low. Add the remaining 2 g / ½ tsp salt to the beaten eggs and pour the eggs into the pan in a single continuous stream across the surface of the tomato-pepper base. Wait 20 seconds without stirring; the eggs will begin to set at the bottom in contact with the warm sauce.
  5. 5
    Now stir continuously with a silicone spatula or a wooden spoon, scraping the bottom and folding the setting egg curds back into the sauce in slow figure-eight motions. Cook 60 to 90 seconds until the eggs are softly set throughout the sauce (no visible liquid raw-egg pools, but the mixture still glistens and wobbles when the pan is shaken; the eggs are at the just-set stage, before they tighten into dry curds). Pull from heat immediately; residual pan heat finishes the cook on the way to the table.
  6. 6
    Scatter the 40 g / ⅓ cup crumbled feta over the top (if using); sprinkle the 4 g / 1 tbsp chopped parsley. Serve straight from the pan, with grilled or toasted bread (Turkish pide, simit, Lebanese pita, or country sourdough) for sopping. Black tea is the canonical pairing.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein menemen sucuklu (with Turkish sausage)→with sucuk (Turkish spiced beef sausage)

    Slice 100 g sucuk into 5 mm rounds. Before the olive oil, dry-render the sucuk in the empty pan over medium heat 3 to 4 minutes until the edges crisp and the fat releases. Push to one side of the pan; add the olive oil to the cleared space and proceed with the peppers. The sucuk fat carries through the dish; the result is the classic Turkish brunch heartier variant. If sucuk is unavailable, chorizo (Spanish, dry-cured) substitutes acceptably.

  • Regional with onion (the home variant)→with onion added to the base

    Add 100 g finely diced yellow onion to the olive oil before the peppers; sweat 5 minutes until translucent before adding the peppers. The dish reads sweeter and slightly more aromatic; many home cooks consider this the canonical version. Purists object; both are defensible.

  • Regional menemen kurutmalı (Aegean dried-tomato version)→with sun-dried tomato substituted for fresh in winter

    Skip the fresh tomato. Soak 100 g sun-dried tomatoes (oil-packed, drained) in 200 g hot water 10 minutes; chop fine and add with the soaking liquid in place of the grated fresh tomato. The dish reads deeper, more concentrated, and slightly chewy from the dried tomato; the Aegean winter variant when fresh tomatoes are pale.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • long thin green pepper (sivri biber, charliston, or Anaheim/Cubanelle as fallback) → 180 g green bell pepper plus 1 small jalapeño Sivri biber and charliston are the canonical Turkish peppers; both are mild, long, thin, and slightly grassy. Anaheim and Cubanelle are the closest US substitutes and work well. Bell pepper alone reads too sweet and lacks the slight grassy spice; pairing bell with a small jalapeño approximates the structural flavor.
  • pul biber → 2 g Aleppo pepper, or 1 g red pepper flakes plus a pinch of smoked paprika Pul biber is fruity, slightly raisiny, and gently warm; Aleppo pepper is the closest substitute (the two are nearly identical, the difference is regional production). Generic red pepper flakes are spicier and less fruity; use less and bolster with smoked paprika for fruit.
  • ripe tomato → 400 g canned crushed tomatoes (San Marzano) In winter when fresh tomatoes are pale, canned crushed tomato substitutes well. Reduce slightly longer (8 minutes vs 5 to 7) to drive off the canned-water flavor. The grating step is moot with canned tomato; pour directly.

Notes

The egg integration technique. Menemen is structurally distinct from both shakshuka (eggs poached on top of the sauce, yolks visible and runny) and Western scrambled eggs (eggs cooked separately as a single coherent texture). The menemen technique is a slow continuous fold of the beaten egg directly into the warm tomato-pepper base; the eggs set as soft curds dispersed throughout the sauce, and the finished dish has a single uniform texture in which egg, tomato, and pepper are indistinguishable as separate components. The 60 to 90 second window between liquid raw egg and tight overcooked curd is the structural skill of the dish; pull early (still wobbling when shaken) and let residual heat finish the cook on the way to the table.

Why grate the tomato. The Turkish technique of grating fresh tomato on a box grater (with the skin held facing the grater holes, so the skin acts as a barrier and the flesh extrudes through into the pan while the skin stays in your hand) is structural for the dish; it produces an instant smooth sauce without the texture jump of diced tomato and without the need for blanching and peeling. The technique is used across Turkish cookery for any application that wants tomato pulp without skin (menemen, gazpacho-adjacent dishes, simple tomato sauces). It is faster than peeling and produces a better result.

Versatility. The pepper-tomato base (without the eggs) is structurally a Turkish sofrito and travels onto: a sauce base for poached fish, a starter for any tomato stew, a topping for grilled bread (with crumbled cheese and herbs), and the foundation for menemen variations with merguez, lamb, or vegetables added.

Pairs across Turkish (canonical), Cypriot, Balkan, and broader Eastern Mediterranean breakfast contexts. The dish is also widely served as a light dinner in Turkey, not exclusively a breakfast item.

Storage. Menemen is best served immediately; the egg texture degrades meaningfully on storage. The pepper-tomato base (without eggs) keeps 4 days refrigerated and freezes 2 months; reheat the base in the same pan and crack fresh eggs in for each service. Leftover finished menemen reads acceptable cold but the warm soft-curd texture is the point.
