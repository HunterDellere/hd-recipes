Preserved lemon vinaigrette

Minced preserved lemon rind whisked into a dijon-stabilized emulsion of extra-virgin olive oil, fresh lemon juice, and a thread of honey. The fermented rind carries a salt-acid-floral depth that no fresh lemon delivers: weeks of salt-cure break the rind cell walls down and ferment the bitter pith into something savory and round, so the dressing reads citrus-bright on the front and slow-savory on the finish. Dijon does double duty as flavor and as the lecithin-and-mucilage that holds the oil suspended; honey blunts the rind salt without reading sweet.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This needs a jar of preserved lemons, lemons salted and fermented 3 to 4 weeks. If you do not have a jar curing, start one first (see the homemade link below) or buy a jar; there is no same-day shortcut that delivers the fermented-rind depth. A fresh-lemon-only version is in the substitutions, but it is a different, thinner dressing.
  • Use only the rind. Separate the rind from the soft pulp before you start, scrape the salty pulp off the inside of the rind with a spoon, and discard or reserve it. Pulp left on the rind makes the vinaigrette aggressively salty and faintly bitter.

Phase 1, the savory base

  1. 30 g preserved lemon rind, pulp scraped off, fine-minced, rind of about 1 medium preserved lemon, pulp removed, minced to 1 to 2 mm
  2. 10 g dijon mustard
  3. 3 g garlic clove, grated to a paste on a microplane, grated fine, or mashed to a paste with a pinch of salt — (1 small clove; grated so it disperses rather than biting in chunks)

Phase 2, the acid and sweetener

  1. 30 g fresh lemon juice, from about 1 lemon, strained of seeds
  2. 8 g honey

Phase 3, the fat

  1. 100 g extra-virgin olive oil

Phase 4, finish and adjust

  1. 4 g flat-leaf parsley, fine-chopped, leaves only, chopped just before serving optional
  2. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked
  3. 1 g fine sea salt, only if needed, the rind carries most of the salt — (a pinch; taste first, the preserved rind is already salty)

Execution

  1. 1
    Prep the rind. Pull the rind off about 1 preserved lemon and scrape the soft salty pulp off the inside with a spoon; discard or reserve the pulp. Mince the rind to a fine 1 to 2 mm dice, you want 30 g / 2 tbsp packed. The rind is where the fermented salt-acid-floral depth lives; the pulp is just concentrated salt and will overwhelm the dressing if it goes in.
  2. 2
    Build the savory base. In a small bowl or a jar, whisk the 30 g / 2 tbsp minced preserved lemon rind, the 10 g / 2 tsp dijon mustard, and the 3 g / 1 grated garlic clove together into a thick rough paste. Starting from the paste, rather than dumping everything at once, gives the dijon a chance to start hydrating, which is what lets it hold the oil later.
  3. 3
    Add the acid and sweetener. Whisk in the 30 g / 2 tbsp fresh lemon juice and the 8 g / 1 tsp honey until the honey dissolves and the base loosens to a pourable slurry. The fresh lemon juice is the bright front-of-palate acid; the rind already supplied the deep cured acid, so this is the lift on top of the base, not the only acid in the dressing.
  4. 4
    Emulsify the oil in. Whisking constantly and hard, drizzle the 100 g / ½ cup extra-virgin olive oil into the base in a thin steady stream, a teaspoon-width thread at first, then faster once the mixture turns opaque and pale. The dijon holds the oil droplets suspended in the lemon-juice water phase; add the oil too fast and you outrun the emulsifier and the dressing breaks into oil floating on liquid. If using a jar, add all the oil, cap with a fitted lid, and shake hard for 30 seconds instead. Reference the vinaigrette-emulsion technique for the full mechanism. Vinaigrette emulsion
  5. 5
    Finish and adjust. The dressing should be glossy, opaque, and thick enough to cling to the back of a spoon. Stir in the 4 g / 2 tbsp chopped parsley and crack in the 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper. Taste on a leaf of lettuce or a spoon, not from the air; preserved lemon reads very differently diluted on food than concentrated on a spoon. It should land bright and citrus-forward, then resolve into a savory salt-acid base. Adjust: too sharp, add a few more drops of honey; too flat, a squeeze more fresh lemon; too thick, a teaspoon of water. Add the pinch of 1 g fine sea salt only if it reads under-seasoned, the rind already carries most of the salt.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Heat heat→harissa preserved-lemon dressing

    Whisk 10 g / 2 tsp harissa paste into the savory base in step 2, before the acid. The Tunisian chili-caraway-coriander paste reads aromatic more than hot, and the caraway bridges into the fermented rind; cut the fresh lemon to 20 g / 4 tsp so the dressing does not go too thin once the harissa loosens it. This is the version for roast carrots, cauliflower, and lamb.

  • Texture body→creamy preserved-lemon dressing

    Whisk 60 g / ¼ cup full-fat yogurt or labneh into the finished vinaigrette. The yogurt turns the thin emulsion into a thick spoonable dressing for grain bowls, roast potatoes, and as a sauce under grilled fish; it also buffers the rind salt further. Thin with a tablespoon of water to pourable if needed.

  • Regional aromatic→cumin-and-coriander toasted-spice version

    Toast 2 g / 1 tsp whole cumin seed and 2 g / 1 tsp whole coriander seed in a dry skillet over medium until fragrant, 60 to 90 seconds, then crush coarse in a mortar and whisk into the base in step 2. The warm toasted spice pulls the dressing toward a Moroccan tagine register and pairs with chickpeas, couscous, and roast squash.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • preserved lemon rind → fresh lemon zest plus extra salt (the same-day shortcut) No fermented rind on hand: use the zest of 1 lemon plus 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt and a few drops more lemon juice. It will read bright and clean but flat by comparison; the fermentation depth and the rounded savory base are exactly what cannot be faked same-day. Make a real jar for next time.
  • dijon mustard → whole-grain mustard Whole-grain mustard emulsifies nearly as well (same seed mucilage) and adds visible seed texture and a milder bite. The dressing reads slightly more rustic. Avoid yellow ballpark mustard, too much vinegar and turmeric, it will skew the flavor.
  • honey → maple syrup or a pinch of granulated sugar Maple syrup works 1:1 and adds a faint caramel note; granulated sugar (½ tsp, dissolved into the lemon juice first) is the neutral option. The point of the sweetener is to round the rind salt, not to sweeten, so keep it restrained whichever you use. Use maple for a vegan dressing.
  • extra-virgin olive oil → a 50/50 blend of olive and a neutral oil A very robust, bitter, peppery olive oil can fight the preserved rind; cutting it half-and-half with a neutral oil (avocado, grapeseed) tames the polyphenol bitterness while keeping olive character. Use a milder fruity olive oil straight if you have one.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → preserved lemon rind, pulp scraped off, fine-minced make it yourself A jar of preserved lemons is the entire reason this dressing tastes the way it does, and it is almost nothing to make: lemons, salt, and 3 to 4 weeks of patience on the counter. The salt cure ferments and breaks down the rind, converting the bitter pith into the savory, floral, salt-acid note that no fresh lemon delivers. Store-bought jars work but vary widely in salt and bitterness; a home jar lets you control the cure and is dramatically cheaper per lemon. Start one jar and you have months of vinaigrette, tagine, and roast-chicken seasoning from a single afternoon of effort.

Notes

Why preserved lemon instead of fresh. A fresh lemon contributes citric acid and rind oils, full stop. A preserved lemon has spent weeks under salt, during which lactic fermentation and salt-driven cell breakdown transform it: the bitter pith softens and turns savory, the rind goes translucent and tender, the harsh raw-citrus edge mellows into a rounded sour, and a faintly floral, almost pickled note develops. The result is an ingredient that delivers salt, acid, and a deep savory base note all at once, the reason a teaspoon of minced rind can carry a whole dressing. Use fresh lemon juice on top of it for the bright lift the cured rind has lost, and you get a two-layer citrus structure: bright front, savory base.

Why dijon is structural, not optional. Vinaigrette is an oil-in-water emulsion, oil droplets suspended in the lemon-juice water phase, and oil and water do not stay mixed on their own. Dijon mustard is the emulsifier: ground mustard seed releases mucilage (a soluble fiber that thickens the water phase and physically slows droplet coalescence) plus surface-active compounds that coat the oil droplets. Whisking shears the oil into fine droplets; the dijon keeps them from merging back. Without it the dressing breaks within minutes. This is why the dijon goes into the base first and the oil goes in last and slow.

The salt math. Preserved lemon rind is salty, but how salty varies a lot by jar and by how thoroughly you scraped the pulp. That is why the recipe holds the added fine sea salt to a final taste-and-adjust rather than a fixed amount. Always taste the finished dressing on the actual food it will dress, a lettuce leaf, a forkful of grain, not from a spoon in the air; concentrated on a spoon it reads far saltier and sharper than it will once it is spread across a salad or a bowl of warm couscous.

What it dresses. This is a sturdy, assertive vinaigrette built for assertive food. It is at its best over warm grains (farro, couscous, freekeh), roast vegetables (carrots, cauliflower, squash, fennel), white fish and salmon, chickpea and lentil salads, and bitter greens (escarole, radicchio, arugula) that can stand up to the salt-acid punch. It is too forward for delicate butter lettuce alone; pair it with something that has body. Made ahead, it keeps in a fitted-lid jar in the refrigerator for up to a week; the emulsion will separate as it sits, which is normal, just shake or whisk it back together before each use, and let it come to room temperature so the olive oil un-clouds.
