Chia coconut pudding with mango

Chia seeds soaked overnight in full-fat coconut milk with maple syrup, vanilla, and a pinch of salt. The chia seeds absorb roughly 12 times their weight in liquid and gel into a soft tapioca-like pudding without cooking. Topped at serving with fresh or thawed-frozen mango (cut into 1 cm dice), toasted coconut flakes, and a small squeeze of lime that brightens the coconut-mango pairing. Vegan, gluten-free, no-cook for the pudding itself. Make-ahead breakfast or dessert; the texture continues to improve through 24 hours and holds 4 days refrigerated.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is an overnight recipe: 8 minutes of active work, then 6+ hours of refrigerator rest. The chia seeds need at least 6 hours to fully hydrate and gel; the texture is genuinely better after 12 hours and the format is calibrated for overnight rest.
  • Whisk the chia seeds into the liquid hard, and re-whisk at the 10-minute and 30-minute marks during the first hour of refrigeration. The seeds clump if they sit undisturbed in the early hydration phase, leaving pockets of un-gelled liquid and clumps of dry seeds. Three whisks in the first 30 minutes prevents clumping.
  • Toast the coconut flakes the night before. They cool while the pudding sets and stay crisp because they go on at serving rather than mixing in.
  • Use 4 small wide-mouth glass jars (6 to 8 oz) for individual portions. Glass holds the pudding cleanly without absorbing coconut aromatics; plastic absorbs vanilla and coconut over multiple uses.

Phase 3, the serving topping (per portion)

  2. 40 g lime wedges — (1 lime cut into 4 wedges; a small squeeze per portion at serving)
  3. 1 g flaky sea salt (optional, for finishing) optional — (a small pinch per portion if desired, amplifies the mango sweetness)

Execution

  1. 1
    Toast the coconut flakes the night before. Heat a dry small skillet (cast iron or stainless, not nonstick) over medium heat. Add the 30 g / ½ cup unsweetened coconut flakes. Toast 3 to 4 minutes, swirling the pan every 30 seconds, until the flakes are golden-brown at the edges and smell distinctly toasted. Tip onto a plate to cool fully (the residual pan heat over-toasts). Store airtight at room temperature. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  2. 2
    In a large bowl or wide jar, whisk together the 480 g coconut milk, the 120 g / ½ cup plant milk, the 45 g / 3 tbsp maple syrup, the 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla bean paste, and the 1 g / ¼ tsp fine sea salt until fully integrated. The coconut milk solids should dissolve into a smooth uniform liquid.
  3. 3
    Add the 80 g / ½ cup chia seeds to the liquid in a steady stream while whisking continuously for 30 seconds. The whisk action prevents the seeds from clumping in the initial contact with the liquid; clumps in this stage stay clumpy and ruin the texture.
  4. 4
    Divide the mixture across four 6 to 8 oz wide-mouth glass jars (about 180 g per jar). Cover with fitted lids. Refrigerate. Whisk or stir each jar at the 10-minute mark and again at the 30-minute mark to prevent the seeds from settling and clumping at the bottom.
  5. 5
    Refrigerate at least 6 hours, preferably overnight. The chia seeds absorb the liquid over the first 2 hours and gel into a soft pudding-like texture over 4 to 6 hours. After 12 hours the texture is at its best: smooth and uniform, with visible chia seeds suspended throughout but no liquid pooling at the bottom. The pudding holds 4 days refrigerated and the texture continues to firm slightly.6 h
  6. 6
    At serving (the next morning or any of the next 4 mornings): pull one jar from the refrigerator. Give the pudding a quick stir with a spoon to redistribute any settled seeds. If the pudding feels too thick, stir in 15 to 30 g / 1 to 2 tbsp of cold coconut milk or plant milk to loosen to your preferred consistency.
  7. 7
    Top each portion with about 75 g of the diced mango (one-quarter of the prepared 300 g), 7 g / 2 tbsp of the toasted coconut flakes (one-quarter of the toasted 30 g), a small pinch of flaky sea salt (optional), and a small squeeze of lime juice (about 10 g / 2 tsp from one wedge). Eat directly from the jar with a long-handled spoon.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion fruit→with berries in summer, pomegranate in autumn, citrus in winter

    Berries: 100 g raspberries or blueberries per portion. Pomegranate: 60 g pomegranate seeds per portion. Citrus: segments of 1 grapefruit or 2 mandarins per portion, with a bit of the juice. All substitute by season; the coconut-vanilla pudding base holds across fruit types.

  • Regional flavor direction→matcha coconut pudding with pineapple

    Whisk 6 g / 2 tsp culinary-grade matcha powder into the coconut milk in step 2 (sift it first to break up clumps). Replace the mango with 250 g diced fresh pineapple at serving. The pudding shifts from tropical-sweet to grassy-bittersweet; the pineapple acid contrasts with the matcha tannins.

  • Occasion flavor direction→chocolate chia pudding

    Add 25 g / 3 tbsp Dutch-process cocoa powder and an extra 15 g / 1 tbsp maple syrup to the pudding base. Top with raspberries and chopped dark chocolate instead of mango. The pudding reads more dessert than breakfast.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • full-fat coconut milk → light coconut milk plus 30 g coconut cream, or full-fat coconut cream diluted Light coconut milk alone produces a thinner runnier pudding. Coconut cream (the thick layer skimmed off the top of refrigerated full-fat coconut milk) substitutes for a richer denser pudding. Dairy milk substitutes 1:1 but the pudding loses its coconut character entirely.
  • chia seeds → basil seeds (sabja) or psyllium husk Basil seeds hydrate similarly and produce a comparable texture; they are the Southeast Asian counterpart and substitute 1:1. Psyllium husk gives a different mouthfeel (more uniformly gelled, less seed-textural) and uses 1:2 ratio (half the weight of chia).
  • maple syrup → honey (not vegan), agave, or date syrup Honey is not vegan but substitutes 1:1 for a more floral sweetness. Agave is the most neutral. Date syrup adds a caramel-fig character that pairs especially well with the coconut.
  • ripe mango → frozen mango chunks (defrosted), peach, papaya, or pineapple Frozen mango is the most accessible year-round option and substitutes 1:1. Peach and papaya are seasonal swaps with similar tropical sweet profiles. Pineapple is more acidic and contrasts more strongly with the coconut base.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → full-fat coconut milk make it yourselfstub Homemade coconut milk from fresh or desiccated coconut produces a fresher, more aromatic milk than canned, with no stabilizers (guar gum is common in canned). The blender-and-strain method takes 10 minutes and the milk keeps 4 days refrigerated.

Notes

Why chia hydrates the way it does. Chia seeds have a unique outer coat of polysaccharide mucilage that absorbs water aggressively. In contact with liquid, the mucilage swells to roughly 12 times the seed''s weight, forming a soft gel matrix that surrounds and suspends the seed. This is the structural mechanism: the seeds do not dissolve and they do not gelatinize like starch; they expand into individual gel-coated particles that collectively form a pudding-like texture. The mechanism explains why the seed-to-liquid ratio matters so precisely: too little liquid leaves dry seeds that scrape the throat, too much liquid leaves seeds suspended in unset liquid.

Why the early-whisk schedule. The chia mucilage swells fastest in the first 15 to 30 minutes of contact with liquid. If the seeds are left undisturbed during this window, they tend to settle in clumps at the bottom of the container, where the outer seeds gel into a barrier that prevents the inner seeds from accessing liquid. The result is a thin uneven pudding with dry clumps at the bottom. Three whisks in the first 30 minutes (initial mix, 10-minute, 30-minute) keep the seeds suspended in the liquid through the critical hydration phase, producing a uniform pudding texture throughout.

Why coconut milk specifically. Coconut milk fat (medium-chain triglycerides) is solid at refrigerator temperature and liquid at room temperature, which means the pudding gains slight structural body from the fat as it chills and the chia gel sets around the fat micelles. Dairy milk would give a similar fat contribution but read entirely differently flavor-wise. Almond milk and oat milk are too low in fat (1 to 3%) to provide structural body; the pudding using only nut milk reads watery and runny. The coconut milk plus a small dilution of plant milk hits the right total fat (about 14% by weight) for the desired pudding texture.

Why the salt and lime. A coconut chia pudding without salt reads as flat-sweet; the small pinch (1 g / ¼ tsp in the base, optional flaky salt at serving) amplifies the coconut and the vanilla and finishes the sweetness. The lime at serving brings a top-note acid that lifts the tropical-coconut register and prevents the pudding from reading as one-dimensionally sweet. Both seasonings are standard in Southeast Asian tropical-fruit desserts (Vietnamese che, Thai mango sticky rice) and translate directly to this format.

Why no plastic. Glass jars are the right tool for chia pudding: they do not absorb the coconut and vanilla aromatics, they do not impart any off-flavors over 4 days, and they go straight from refrigerator to spoon. Plastic containers absorb the coconut volatiles over multiple uses and will subtly off-flavor future contents. Wide-mouth jars are easier to eat from than narrow-mouth and easier to clean after.

Make-ahead and storage. The pudding holds 4 days refrigerated and continues to firm slightly through that window. After day 4, the texture begins to break down into a slightly looser gel and the coconut aromatics flatten. For longer storage, freeze in individual jars (leave 1 cm headspace for expansion); thaw overnight in the refrigerator before the morning of consumption. The texture after freeze-thaw is slightly different (the gel structure breaks and the pudding reads slightly grainier) but is still pleasant.
