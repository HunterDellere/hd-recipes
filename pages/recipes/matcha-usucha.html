Matcha usucha (thin matcha tea)

The everyday matcha preparation: 2 g of ceremonial matcha whisked with 60 ml of 80°C water into a frothed, brilliant-jade tea. Built for the proper aroma extraction without the bitter overdraw common in home preparations.

Tea

  1. 2 g ceremonial-grade matcha
  2. 60 ml soft filtered water

Execution

  1. 1
    Bring water to a full boil, then pull off the heat and let it sit for 90 seconds. This drops the temperature to roughly 80°C / 175°F, which is the working range for matcha. Boiling water (100°C) extracts the bitter catechins aggressively and the resulting tea reads grassy-harsh; below 70°C the L-theanine and chlorophyll do not extract fully and the tea reads pale and weak.
  2. 2
    Warm the matcha bowl (chawan, or any wide low ceramic bowl about 12 cm / 5 inches across) by rinsing with the just-boiled water you set aside, then drying with a clean cloth. Warming the bowl is functional: it stabilizes the brew temperature during whisking so the matcha does not flash-cool against cold ceramic.
  3. 3
    Sift the 2 g / 1 heaping ½ tsp matcha into the warm bowl through a small fine-mesh strainer. Sifting is non-optional, matcha powder agglomerates into tiny clumps that no amount of whisking will fully break apart. A sifted matcha whisks smooth in 15 seconds; an unsifted matcha leaves small green specks regardless of technique.
  4. 4
    Pour the 60 ml / ¼ cup of 80°C water onto the sifted matcha in a single steady stream. Pick up the chasen (bamboo whisk), or a small wire whisk as a substitute, and whisk vigorously in a tight Z-pattern, keeping the whisk tines just at the surface of the liquid. Whisk for 15 to 20 seconds. The matcha will go from a flat dark slurry to a bright jade liquid with a fine, even foam covering the surface. The foam should be small-bubbled and persistent, not large-bubbled and quickly collapsing.
  5. 5
    When the foam is even, lift the whisk straight up out of the center with a small twist, this leaves a peaked surface rather than a depressed pool. Drink immediately. Usucha is served at the temperature of brewing; reheating destroys the foam and dulls the color.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion concentration→koicha (thick matcha)

    Use 4 g / 1 tbsp matcha to only 30 ml / 2 tbsp water. Whisk slowly and gently, the goal is a smooth, paint-like thick liquid, not a frothed foam. Koicha is the tea of formal ceremony; it is sipped, not drunk, and tastes intensely umami-sweet rather than refreshing. Use only the highest grade matcha available.

  • Occasion service→matcha latte (cold)

    After whisking, add 180 ml / ¾ cup cold whole milk (or oat milk) and a teaspoon of simple syrup. Pour over a glass of ice. Stir. The latte format tolerates lower-grade matcha because dairy fat smooths bitterness.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • ceremonial-grade matcha → no substitute Culinary-grade matcha in this preparation produces a bitter, dull tea that obscures the technique. If only culinary is available, make a matcha latte (recipe matcha-latte) instead, the milk and sugar mask the bitterness.
  • chasen (bamboo whisk) → small balloon wire whisk or milk frother A wire whisk works but compacts the powder rather than aerating it, the foam will be less even. A handheld milk frother (the small battery-powered kind) gives the closest texture to a chasen.

Notes

Temperature is the single most consequential variable. Most home matcha tastes bad because the water was at a full rolling boil, the bitter catechin extraction outpaces the sweet L-theanine extraction at high temperatures, and the resulting tea reads as one-note grassy-harsh. 80°C is the temperature that lets both compound classes extract roughly together, producing the characteristic sweet-umami balance.

The chasen is purpose-built. The 80 to 120 fine bamboo tines aerate matcha in a way no metal whisk can match, they create a high-surface-area network that traps tiny stable bubbles instead of forcing larger ones. A good chasen lasts 6 months of daily use; replace when the tines start fraying or curling outward.

Quality is binary in matcha. A 2-week-old bag of ceremonial matcha tastes profoundly better than a 6-month-old bag of the same brand. Store in the refrigerator in the original tin, use within a month of opening, and accept that good matcha is a perishable. The price difference between ceremonial and culinary is real flavor difference, not marketing.
