Sourdough croutons

Day-old sourdough torn into chunks, tossed in garlic-infused olive oil, oven-toasted to a uniform shatter. The structural crouton of Caesar salad, panzanella, ribollita, and any salad that earns a starch component.

6servingsabout 4 cups, enough for 4 to 6 salads

Mise en place by phase 2 phases ▾

Phase 1, garlic oil

  1. 60 g extra-virgin olive oil
  2. 12 g garlic, smashed but whole — (3 cloves)

Phase 2, croutons

  1. 250 g day-old country sourdough, crusts trimmed if leathery, torn into 2 cm / ¾-inch chunks
  2. 2 g fine sea salt
  3. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the oven to 180 °C / 350 °F. While it preheats, warm the 60 g / ¼ cup extra-virgin olive oil with the 12 g / 3 smashed garlic cloves in a small saucepan over the lowest possible heat for 8 minutes. The oil should never come to a sizzle; keep it sub-Maillard so the garlic infuses without browning. A scorched garlic clove turns the oil bitter and contaminates the entire batch. Blooming aromatics
  2. 2
    Pull the oil off heat at 8 minutes. Fish out the garlic cloves and discard them; their job was the infusion, not the bake; cloves left in the oil at 180 °C for 14 minutes turn black and bitter. The strained oil should smell deeply garlicky and read pale gold.
  3. 3
    In a wide bowl, toss the 250 g / 9 oz torn sourdough chunks with the strained garlic oil, the 2 g / ⅓ tsp fine sea salt, and (if using) the 1 g cracked black pepper. Toss for a full 60 seconds; you want every surface of every chunk wet with oil. Day-old bread is dry enough that the oil absorbs into the crumb rather than pooling on the surface; if your bread is fresher than day-old, the oil will sit on the outside and the croutons will fry unevenly.
  4. 4
    Spread the chunks on a sheet tray in a single layer with no chunks touching. Bake for 12 to 14 minutes, tossing once at 7 minutes, until the chunks are uniformly golden and dry through to the centre when you bite into one. The exterior should shatter and the interior should be dry but slightly tender, not rock-hard like a melba toast. Pull and cool on the tray; they crisp further as they lose residual steam during the first 3 minutes off heat.
  5. 5
    Use immediately for best texture or hold in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 5 days. Recrisp in a 150 °C / 300 °F oven for 4 minutes if they soften; do not microwave (steams them into a leathery state).

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→parmigiano croutons (Italian-American)

    Add 30 g / ⅓ cup finely grated parmigiano reggiano to the bread-and-oil toss. The cheese coats the chunks and forms a Maillard-browned crust during the bake. Slightly less crisp shatter, more savory depth. Use for Caesar salad and ribollita.

  • Regional cuisine→butter-fried croutons (French bistro)

    Skip the garlic-oil infusion. Fry the bread chunks in 60 g / 4 tbsp brown butter in a wide skillet over medium heat for 3 to 4 minutes per side, tossing, until golden. Salt at the end. The brown-butter notes give a deeper flavor than oven-toasted; the texture is slightly less uniform.

  • Texture texture→finer crouton crumbs (for breading or garnish)

    Spread baked croutons on the tray to fully cool, then pulse in a food processor 5 to 8 times until coarse crumbs (3 to 5 mm pieces, not powder). Use as a topping for pasta, gratins, or as the structural crumb for a chicken Milanese-style breading.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • sourdough bread → rustic country bread, ciabatta, or pain de campagne Any open-crumb lean wheat bread works; the lactic tang of true sourdough is missed but the texture is similar.
  • extra-virgin olive oil → unsalted butter, melted Butter croutons are richer; reduce salt slightly because butter is salted to taste already in some kitchens. Add 5 g chopped thyme or rosemary to the butter for a herb-butter crouton.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → sourdough bread make it yourselfstub A natural-leavened sourdough has the open crumb and acetic-tang character that crisps into croutons with both shatter and chew; the lactic acidity also reads through after toasting.

Notes

Why day-old bread. Fresh sourdough has 35 to 38 percent moisture in the crumb; day-old has dropped to 22 to 25 percent. The difference shows up in the bake: fresh bread releases steam during oven time and the chunks come out leathery in the centre with hard exterior; day-old bread releases minimal steam and crisps uniformly. Bakers who plan ahead set aside the heel of yesterday's loaf for crouton duty rather than fighting fresh bread's water content.

Why garlic-oil infusion at sub-Maillard temperature. Garlic added directly to the bread before the bake at 180 °C burns within 6 minutes (the surface temperature exceeds the 150 °C / 300 °F threshold where allicin and the related sulfur compounds start to oxidize past pleasantness). Pre-infusing the oil at 80 to 90 °C for 8 minutes extracts the aromatics into the fat without burning, then the garlic is removed before the bake. The flavor lands on the croutons via the oil; the garlic itself never enters the oven.

Why oven, not pan. A pan fry produces unevenly crisped croutons (the side touching the pan crisps fast, the exposed sides take longer, the cook fights for uniformity). The oven produces uniform results because all surfaces of the chunk meet hot dry air at the same temperature. Pan-frying is right when the cook wants the deeper flavor of butter or fat-rendered croutons (see modifications); the oven is right for everyday salad croutons.

Salt timing. Salt with the oil during the toss, before the bake — this gives the salt time to dissolve into the surface oil and adhere uniformly. Salting after the bake produces croutons with visible salt crystals in some places and bland in others.
