Roasted delicata and farro salad with maple vinaigrette

Hard-roasted delicata half-moons (skin on) tossed with chewy farro, lacinato kale massaged in lemon-salt, toasted hazelnuts, and a maple-cider vinaigrette built on whole-grain mustard. The autumn North-American grain salad in its proper register: caramelized squash sweetness against bitter brassica, fruity acid carrying the dressing, hazelnut as the structural crunch.

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the oven to 230 °C / 450 °F with a heavy half-sheet pan inside on the middle rack. The pan needs to preheat for at least 10 minutes; the 700 g / 2 medium squash worth of half-moons must hiss on contact when added. Cold pan plus cold squash means 15 minutes spent climbing to roasting temperature and the squash steams before it browns. The thin edible skin of delicata is what differentiates this dish from a butternut version; the skin caramelizes into a savory chewy band around each half-moon and is the structural reason the dish reads as roasted-vegetable-salad rather than mashed-squash-salad. High-heat dry roasting
  2. 2
    While the oven heats, scrub the 700 g / 2 medium delicata squash, halve lengthwise, scoop the seeds with a spoon, and slice into 1 cm half-moons (skin on, no peeling). On a wide bowl or directly on a sheet of parchment, toss the half-moons with the 20 g / 1½ tbsp olive oil, the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt, and the 1 g / ¼ tsp cracked black pepper until every piece is glossed. Pooled oil at the bottom of the bowl means too much, drag the pieces through it.
  3. 3
    Pull the preheated sheet pan from the oven and tip the dressed squash onto it in a single layer, cut faces down where possible. The half-moons should sizzle audibly. Spread so each piece has 1 to 2 cm of clear pan around it; if it does not fit, use a second preheated pan. Crowded vegetables steam each other and the surface stays wet. Roast 22 to 28 minutes without disturbing for the first 18 minutes; at 18 minutes flip one piece to check the underside, which should be deep mahogany. Flip all pieces with a thin spatula and return for 4 to 8 minutes until the flesh is yielding at a fork and the second side has color too. Pull and rest on the pan; the residual heat finishes the cook. High-heat dry roasting
  4. 4
    While the squash roasts, build the farro pilaf. In a 3-quart heavy saucepan over medium heat, warm the 10 g / 2 tsp olive oil. Add the 200 g / 1 cup dry semi-pearled farro and toast in the dry oil, stirring constantly, for 90 to 120 seconds until the grains darken one shade and smell distinctly nutty. The dry-toast is what differentiates farro pilaf from farro boiled-in-water; the surface of each grain hits Maillard temperature and develops the popcorn-aromatic compounds that boiled farro lacks. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  5. 5
    Add the 800 ml / 3⅓ cups water and the 6 g / 1½ tsp kosher salt to the toasted farro. Bring to a boil, reduce to a bare simmer, cover loosely, and cook 25 to 30 minutes for semi-pearled (50+ minutes for whole; check the package). The grains should be tender with a slight chew at the center, not mushy. Drain off any excess water in a fine-mesh sieve and spread the farro on a wide plate or sheet pan to cool to warm-not-hot. Hot farro on the platter wilts the kale and breaks the dressing; warm farro picks up the vinaigrette without collapsing the structure.
  6. 6
    While the farro cooks, massage the kale. Place the 150 g / 1 medium bunch stripped-and-ribboned lacinato kale in a wide bowl. Add the 10 g / 2 tsp lemon juice and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. With clean hands, scrunch and rub the kale leaves between your palms for 60 to 90 seconds; the volume drops by half, the leaves turn a deeper green, and the texture goes from leathery-stiff to tender-pliant. The acid plus the salt plus mechanical bruising breaks down the cell walls (the same chemistry as a quick salt cure on a fish fillet); the kale becomes salad-edible rather than chewing-jaw-edible.
  7. 7
    Build the maple-cider vinaigrette. In a small bowl or jar, combine the 30 g / 2 tbsp apple cider vinegar, the 15 g / 1 tbsp maple syrup, the 12 g / 2 tsp whole-grain mustard, the 15 g / 1 small microplaned shallot, and the 2 g / ½ tsp kosher salt. Whisk vigorously to dissolve the salt and integrate the mustard. Stream in the 80 g / ¼ cup + 1 tbsp olive oil in a thin steady drizzle while whisking constantly; the vinaigrette tightens as the oil emulsifies into the acid base. Whisk for 20 to 30 seconds after the last of the oil; the dressing should ribbon off the whisk and slowly settle back into the surface. Taste; the dressing should read sweet-acid-mustard in that order, fully salted; if flat, more salt. Vinaigrette emulsion
  8. 8
    Toast the 60 g / ½ cup raw skin-on hazelnuts on a small sheet pan in the same 230 °C oven (the temperature is hotter than ideal for nuts but the squash was already pulled, drop the oven to 175 °C / 350 °F if you have time, or just watch closely) for 8 to 10 minutes until the skins crack and the flesh smells distinctly toasted. Wrap the hot nuts in a clean kitchen towel and rub vigorously for 30 seconds; most of the papery skins slough off (some residual is fine, the bitterness is in the bulk skin not the wisps). Cool 2 minutes and chop roughly. The skin-rub step is the single biggest difference between a hazelnut salad that reads sweet-and-toasted and one that reads bitter-and-tannic.
  9. 9
    Assemble. In a wide warm bowl or platter, spread the warm farro as the base layer. Drizzle one-third of the maple-cider vinaigrette (~50 g / 3 tbsp) over the farro and toss gently with tongs; the warm grains absorb the dressing into their structure. Add the massaged kale and toss to integrate; the kale should distribute evenly through the farro, not sit as a separate layer. Drizzle another third of the vinaigrette and toss again.
  10. 10
    Lay the warm roasted delicata half-moons across the top of the dressed grain-and-kale bed in a slightly overlapping pattern; do not toss the squash through the salad (the half-moons break apart and the dish reads as mush). The visible squash on top is the structural and visual centerpiece. Drizzle the remaining vinaigrette (~50 g / 3 tbsp) over the squash specifically; the dressing pools into the cupped half-moons and reads as direct seasoning rather than coating.
  11. 11
    Scatter the chopped toasted hazelnuts evenly across the top, then the shaved 30 g / ¼ cup pecorino curls, then the 10 g / ½ cup parsley leaves if using. The order matters; nuts first (heavy, settles), cheese second (visible curls), herbs last (lightest, reads green on top). Serve warm or at room temperature within 30 minutes of dressing; the kale and farro hold longer than the squash, which softens after about 20 minutes off heat. Leftovers refrigerate well overnight (the kale actually improves) but the squash texture is gone the next day; eat the squash first.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→with seared chicken thigh

    Sear 4 boneless skinless chicken thighs (about 600 g) seasoned with salt and pepper in 15 g / 1 tbsp olive oil for 6 minutes per side over medium-high heat; rest 5 minutes, slice into 1 cm strips, and lay across the assembled salad with the squash. The dish becomes a full main course; the seared chicken Maillard echoes the roasted squash.

  • Occasion season→spring version with asparagus and snap peas

    Replace the delicata with 400 g asparagus (woody ends snapped, cut into 4 cm pieces) and 200 g sugar snap peas (strings pulled). Roast at the same 230 °C for 8 to 10 minutes only; the green vegetables overcook past tender quickly. Replace the maple syrup with honey (cleaner, less autumnal). The dish reads spring rather than autumn but the structure holds.

  • Texture grain→with wheat berries or barley

    Replace the farro with 200 g wheat berries (cooks in 50 to 60 minutes, soak overnight if possible) or 200 g pearl barley (cooks in 30 to 35 minutes, the most forgiving substitute). Both grains hold their chew the same way; barley is slightly creamier and reads more rustic. Other whole grains (quinoa, freekeh) work but each shifts the dish character noticeably.

  • Regional cuisine→levantine-leaning with pomegranate molasses and sumac

    Replace the maple syrup with 15 g / 1 tbsp pomegranate molasses; replace the apple cider vinegar with 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice; replace the hazelnuts with 60 g / ½ cup toasted slivered almonds; finish with 2 g / 1 tsp sumac sprinkled across the platter. The dish drifts from American autumn to Levantine register; the structure holds.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • delicata squash → kabocha squash, peeled (skin too tough to leave on) and sliced 1 cm thick Closest flavor; the peeled half-moons brown the same way at 230 °C. The dish loses the textural contrast of the edible delicata skin but gains a slightly denser, sweeter flesh. Honeynut squash (peeled or skin-on if the squash is small enough) is the second-best swap.
  • lacinato kale → curly kale, or Swiss chard (with the stems treated as a separate vegetable) Curly kale needs an extra 30 seconds of massage to break down (tougher leaf structure). Swiss chard is more tender and needs less massage; the chard stems should be diced and quick-pickled in the dressing for 10 minutes before assembly. Spinach reads too soft and wilts under the warm components.
  • hazelnuts → pecans (toasted) or walnuts (toasted) Pecans are the most American autumn-coded swap; walnuts are slightly bitter and tannic which competes with the kale. Both toast on the same schedule. Almonds work but read less seasonal.
  • apple cider vinegar → sherry vinegar or white wine vinegar Sherry vinegar reads more European-savory and pulls the dish toward a wine-and-cheese register; white wine vinegar reads cleaner and less autumnal. The maple syrup pairs particularly well with apple cider vinegar (both fall-coded fermented-fruit acids); the dish loses some seasonal coherence with other vinegars.
  • maple syrup, Grade A dark → honey, or pomegranate molasses (different acid profile, see modification) Honey reads sweeter and less complex than dark maple syrup; the dish drifts toward generic vinaigrette. Use Grade A amber light only if dark is unavailable; pancake syrup (high-fructose corn syrup with maple flavoring) is not a substitute, the flavor is wrong and the viscosity is wrong.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → maple-cider vinaigrette (the assembled vinaigrette as a unit) make it yourself The vinaigrette doubles or triples easily and keeps a week refrigerated; making a batch as a standalone dressing yields enough for a week of grain salads, roasted-vegetable plates, or chicken-and-greens lunches. The recipe page documents the ratios and the rebuild-when-broken procedure.
  • → whole-grain mustard make it yourself Cracked yellow and brown mustard seeds soaked in white wine vinegar for 48 hours produce a fresher, more aromatic whole-grain mustard than commercial. The 5 minutes of soaking plus 5 minutes of pulse-blending is the entire technique.
  • → apple cider vinegar, unfiltered make it yourself Apple scraps (cores and peels) plus water plus a little sugar, fermented for 4 to 6 weeks under a cloth, produce a live unpasteurized cider vinegar with notably more complex apple character than commercial bottles. The work is two batches a year for a household supply.

Notes

Why semi-pearled farro and not whole. Whole-grain (unpearled) farro retains its full bran layer and needs an overnight soak plus 50 to 70 minutes of cooking; the result is chewier and more nutritious but the time commitment is wrong for a weeknight salad. Semi-pearled farro (the typical US supermarket product, often labeled "perlato" or "pearled") has had part of the bran scraped off; cooks in 25 to 30 minutes without soaking, holds its chew well, and is the practical choice for this application. Pearled farro (fully de-branned) cooks faster still but loses too much character; it reads as generic chewy grain rather than as farro specifically. Read the package; "semi-pearled" or "perlato" is the right register.

Why dry-toast the farro before boiling. The 90-second dry-toast in oil before adding water is what separates farro pilaf from farro boiled in water. The toasted grain develops Maillard compounds (popcorn-aromatic pyrazines and furans) on the surface; these compounds survive the boiling phase and read in the finished grain. The same technique drives Italian risotto (the toasting phase before stock addition) and Persian rice pilaf (the toasting phase in oil before water). Skipping the toast yields cooked-correctly-but-flat farro; including it yields farro with audible aromatic depth.

The umami source is the shaved pecorino curls; the finishing acid is the apple cider vinegar in the vinaigrette plus the lemon in the kale massage. The pecorino is non-negotiable; without it the dish reads sweet (squash + maple) and acidic (vinegar + lemon) without a savory anchor, and the salad does not satisfy as a main course. A vegan version that keeps the dish coherent needs nutritional yeast (15 g / 3 tbsp scattered with the hazelnuts) or a small handful of crumbled tempeh-bacon as the umami stand-in; substituting nothing leaves a hole.

Why warm-not-hot for assembly. The kale wilts irrevocably above 60 °C; the vinaigrette breaks above 70 °C; the squash continues to soften and lose structure if held above 80 °C. The right assembly window is 5 to 10 minutes after pulling each component from heat, when the squash is still hot enough to glisten and the farro is still warm to the touch. A salad assembled from refrigerator-cold components reads as different dish; the vinaigrette sits on top rather than absorbing into the warm grain, the squash is rubbery rather than caramelized-tender, and the contrast between hot-roasted and cool-fresh that drives the dish is gone.

The salad is structurally a meal. Farro (200 g dry, ~600 g cooked) plus 700 g squash plus 150 g kale plus 60 g hazelnuts plus 30 g cheese yields about 1.5 kg of finished salad serving 4 as a main course. As a side dish next to a roasted chicken or pan-seared fish, the same yield serves 6 to 8. The macronutrient profile (whole grain + roasted vegetable + nut fat + raw greens + small dairy) reads as a complete plate without further composition.
