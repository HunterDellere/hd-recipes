Recipe

Thai grilled beef salad (nam tok)

A whole sirloin steak grilled hard over high heat to a 54°C medium-rare, rested, then sliced thin against the grain and tossed warm with fish sauce, lime, palm sugar, toasted dried-chili powder, and toasted glutinous-rice powder, finished with shallot, scallion, mint, cilantro, and culantro. Nam tok is the sliced-grilled-meat sibling of larb; the name (waterfall) is the meat juice that falls from the steak as it is sliced, pooling into the dressing. The toasted rice powder is the structural element that turns the lime and meat juice into a clinging slurry rather than a wet salad.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Sticky rice is the partner and must start FIRST. Soak 200 g / 1 cup Thai glutinous rice in cold water at least 4 hours ahead (overnight is better), then steam in a bamboo basket while you grill the beef. A 25- to 30-minute steam finishes right as the steak rests.
  • Toast the raw glutinous rice for khao khua and the dried chilies for prik pon early; both must cool fully (about 15 minutes) before pounding to powder. Run both toasts in parallel while the grill heats and the steak tempers.
  • Pull the steak from the fridge 45 minutes before grilling so the interior comes up toward 18 to 20°C. A cold-from-fridge steak overshoots the surface char before the center reaches 54°C, and on a lean sirloin that gradient is the whole game.
  • Pull mint, cilantro, scallion, and culantro from the fridge and dry them before anything else. Cold wet herbs drop the dish temperature when they hit the warm sliced beef and dilute the dressing.
  • Nam tok does not hold. Once dressed, the toasted rice powder absorbs liquid every minute and the herbs wilt within 20 to 30 minutes. Slice, dress, and serve immediately.

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, beef

  1. 500 g beef sirloin steak (top sirloin or sirloin tip), 2.5 cm / 1 inch thick, one whole steak, patted fully dry on both sides with paper towels
  2. 4 g kosher salt
  3. 8 g neutral oil (high smoke point: refined avocado or grapeseed)

Phase 2, dressing

  1. 38 g fish sauce (premium, 30 to 40°N)
  2. 45 g lime juice, fresh, juiced just before using
  3. 8 g palm sugar, shaved or finely chopped from the disc

Phase 3, structural

  1. 15 g toasted glutinous-rice powder (khao khua), see steps 1 to 2; raw glutinous rice dry-toasted to amber, then pounded coarse in a mortar
  2. 3 g toasted dried-chili powder (prik pon), see step 1; dried red Thai chilies dry-toasted then pounded

Phase 4, herbs and aromatics

  1. 60 g shallot, sliced thin pole-to-pole, 1 to 2 mm — (2 to 3 medium shallots; thin pole-to-pole slicing minimizes onion-cell rupture and keeps the bite crisp rather than slumped)
  2. 40 g scallion, green and white parts, sliced 3 mm on a sharp bias — (4 to 5 scallions)
  3. 20 g mint leaves, fresh (spearmint), leaves pulled from stems, larger leaves torn in half
  4. 20 g cilantro, leaves and tender stems, rough chopped
  5. 5 g culantro (saw-tooth herb), if available, leaves, sliced 3 mm crosswise optional — (2 to 3 leaves; the canonical Lao herb, more pungent than cilantro and more heat-stable. Skip if unavailable; do not substitute more cilantro, the flavor is structurally different)

Phase 5, serving

  1. 400 g thai sticky rice, steamed (for the table)
  2. 200 g raw vegetables for the plate, cabbage wedges, long bean, cucumber spears, Thai eggplant if available — (a generous handful per diner; the cool raw vegetables are the foil to the salty-hot meat and the canonical Lao way to eat the dish)
  3. 4 g extra raw chilies, sliced optional — (2 to 3 fresh Thai bird chilies, sliced; for diners who want more heat at the table)

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the toasted chili powder (prik pon). Set a small dry skillet (cast iron or stainless, no oil) over medium heat. Add 6 to 8 dried red Thai chilies (about 5 g) and toast, shaking constantly, for 60 to 90 seconds until the chilies turn slightly darker, smell smoky-fruity, and become brittle. Pull at the first wisp of acrid smoke; past that the chilies turn bitter. Cool, then pound coarse in a mortar to yield 3 g / 1½ tsp prik pon. Set aside. Dry-toasting (grains, spices, seeds, dried chilies)
  2. 2
    Make the toasted glutinous-rice powder (khao khua). In the same dry skillet over medium-low heat, add 25 g / 2 tbsp raw glutinous rice. Toast 6 to 8 minutes, shaking the pan every 30 seconds, until the grains turn from chalk-white to even amber and smell like popcorn and toasted bread. The color cue is the whole grain reading the same medium-amber, not patchy white-and-brown. Cool 2 minutes, then pound in a stone mortar to a coarse powder, texture between sand and fine sea salt, never a fine flour. The pounded grain holds its texture and slurries the dressing; a spice-grinder flour goes dusty and loses the signature grit. Yields 15 g / 2 tbsp khao khua. Set aside. Mortar pounding (clay vs granite)
  3. 3
    Temper and season the steak. Pat the 500 g sirloin fully dry on both sides. Rub a thin film of the 8 g / ½ tbsp neutral oil over both faces, then season both sides evenly with the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt right before it goes on the grill. Salting at the grate (rather than a long dry-brine) keeps the surface dry enough to char fast on a lean cut while still seasoning the crust.
  4. 4
    Build a hot fire. Heat a charcoal grill to high (coals ashed-over and you can hold a hand 10 cm above the grate for only 2 to 3 seconds) or a gas grill to its highest setting with the lid down for 10 minutes. Lean sirloin needs a fast, hard char; a moderate fire gray-cooks the surface before any crust forms and the open grain dries out. Scrape the grate clean and wipe it with an oiled, folded paper towel held in tongs.
  5. 5
    Grill the steak. Lay the 500 g steak over the hottest part of the grate and grill undisturbed for 3 to 4 minutes per side, turning once, until each face carries a deep char and the interior reaches 50 to 52°C / 122 to 126°F on an instant-read thermometer (the temperature will climb 3 to 5°C during the rest into the 54°C medium-rare window). Total grill time for a 2.5 cm sirloin is 6 to 8 minutes. A traditional smoke note is welcome here; nam tok carries a faint grill-char aroma that distinguishes it from the cleaner poached larb. Maillard sear
  6. 6
    Rest, then slice thin against the grain. Move the steak to a cutting board and rest 5 minutes uncovered (covering traps steam and softens the char). Find the direction of the muscle fibers, then slice the steak across the grain into thin 3 to 4 mm strips, holding the knife at a slight bevel for wider faces. Cutting against the grain shortens the muscle fibers and reads dramatically more tender on a lean cut. The juice released onto the board is the waterfall the dish is named for; scrape it into the bowl with the slices in the next step. Cut while the meat is still warm, not hot.
  7. 7
    Dress the warm beef. Tip the sliced beef and every drop of board juice into a wide mixing bowl. Add the 38 g / 2½ tbsp fish sauce, the 8 g / 2 tsp shaved palm sugar, and the 3 g / 1½ tsp toasted chili powder. Toss for 30 seconds; the residual warmth of the meat dissolves the palm sugar and blooms the chili powder into the meat juice, the chili oils need warmth to release and read smoky rather than dusty. Let it sit 1 minute for the sugar to fully melt. Blooming aromatics
  8. 8
    Add the lime and the rice powder. Stir in the 45 g / 3 tbsp lime juice; adding lime to the now warm-not-hot beef keeps its bright top notes, which boil off if the meat is still searing-hot. Then add the 15 g / 2 tbsp toasted glutinous-rice powder and stir thoroughly. The starch hydrates in the lime-and-meat-juice mixture and thickens it to a clinging slurry that coats every slice; this is what makes nam tok structurally a salad rather than sliced steak in liquid. Taste: the dish should read salty-front (fish sauce), sour-prominent (lime), warmly-sweet but quiet (palm sugar), with chili heat building in the throat after the swallow. If flat, add 5 g / 1 tsp more fish sauce; if too sharp, 5 g / 1 tsp more palm sugar.
  9. 9
    Fold in the herbs. Add the 60 g sliced shallot, 40 g sliced scallion, 20 g mint leaves, 20 g chopped cilantro, and the 5 g culantro if using. Toss gently with two large spoons or clean hands until the herbs are evenly distributed, the herb-to-meat ratio in nam tok is high and the plate should look as much green as it does brown. Do not over-toss; the aromatic oils bruise out with excess handling and turn herbal-bitter.
  10. 10
    Plate and serve immediately. Pile the nam tok onto a flat plate (not a deep bowl, the dressing should not pool) and serve at once with a basket of warm sticky rice, a plate of raw vegetables (cabbage wedges, cucumber spears, long bean, Thai eggplant if available), and the optional 4 g sliced fresh chili on the side. Eat by pinching a small ball of sticky rice, pressing it into the salad to pick up meat and dressing, and chasing with a piece of raw cabbage or cucumber. The dish does not hold; the toasted rice powder keeps drinking the dressing and the herbs wilt within 20 to 30 minutes.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→nam tok moo (grilled pork)

    Substitute 500 g pork shoulder or pork neck (kor moo), grilled the same way to an internal 63°C / 145°F and rested. Pork neck has more intramuscular fat than sirloin and stays juicier; it is the most common nam tok in Isaan. Slice thin against the grain identically. The fattier pork carries the dressing differently, so taste and pull back the palm sugar a touch.

  • Texture method→pan-grilled (no outdoor grill)

    Sear the steak in a smoking-hot cast-iron skillet with 8 g / ½ tbsp neutral oil, 3 to 4 minutes per side to the same 54°C target. You lose the open-flame char aroma that defines nam tok against larb; reclaim part of it by toasting the prik pon a shade darker and, if you have one, passing a kitchen torch over the seared slices for 10 seconds before dressing.

  • Regional souring agent→tamarind-forward (eastern Isaan)

    Replace 15 g / 1 tbsp of the lime juice with 15 g / 1 tbsp tamarind concentrate loosened with a splash of water. Tamarind brings a deeper, date-like sour that reads more mainland-Lao; add it with the fish sauce in the warm-toss stage (it can take heat) and reserve the remaining fresh lime for the off-heat finish so the dish keeps its bright top.

  • Heat spice level→mild (farang) version

    Reduce the toasted chili powder to 1 g / ½ tsp and serve the extra fresh chilies on the side for diners who want to escalate. Do not eliminate the chili powder entirely; the smoky-fruit aromatic of toasted dried chili is structural to nam tok identity even at a low dose.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • beef sirloin steak → flank steak, skirt steak, or hanger steak Flank and skirt have a pronounced grain that suits the thin against-the-grain slice; grill hotter and faster (2 to 3 minutes per side) and pull at 52°C since they toughen past medium-rare. Hanger reads richest and most beefy. All three are leaner-flavored than ribeye, which is correct here, the dressing carries the dish and a heavily marbled cut reads greasy against the lime.
  • glutinous rice (for khao khua) → jasmine rice Long-grain jasmine works in a pinch but yields a less-sticky-feeling powder; the slight starchy chew glutinous rice contributes is what gives khao khua its signature mouthfeel. Buy a bag of glutinous rice if you cook Lao or Isaan food more than once a year, it keeps a year in the pantry.
  • culantro → increase cilantro by 5 g and add extra mint Culantro has a more pungent, longer-lasting herb note that fresh cilantro cannot fully replicate. The substitution reads close but not identical; the dish is not ruined by its absence.
  • fish sauce → pla ra (fermented fish sauce, opaque) for a third of the volume Isaan-canonical move: replace about 12 g / 1 tbsp of the regular fish sauce with pla ra, which adds funk and depth at the cost of the dish reading slightly cloudy. Sold in jars at Thai markets; not easily substituted otherwise.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → toasted glutinous-rice powder (khao khua) make it yourself Toasting and pounding khao khua takes 10 minutes start-to-finish and produces a powder dramatically more aromatic than commercial pre-toasted versions, which lose their popcorn-toasted top within weeks of grinding.
  • → fish sauce (premium, 30 to 40°N) make it yourselfstub Twelve-month enzymatic fermentation of fresh anchovies and salt produces a fish sauce with brighter top notes and less industrial salinity than commercial bottles. A genuine project, not a weeknight swap.

Notes

Nam tok versus larb. The two dishes share a dressing (fish sauce, lime, palm sugar, toasted chili powder, toasted rice powder, herbs) and a region (Isaan and Laos), and they are often confused. The dividing line is the protein and its handling. Larb is meat chopped fine and poached in its own moisture, a uniform mince. Nam tok is a whole cut grilled to medium-rare, rested, and sliced thin against the grain, so you get pink centers, grill char, and the meat juice (the "waterfall") that falls from the slices and joins the dressing. The same cook can make both from the same bowl of dressing; the protein decides which dish it is.

Why grill, and why pull at 54°C. The grill char is the flavor that separates nam tok from larb. A lean sirloin pulled at medium-rare keeps its juice and its tender bite; pushed past 60°C it dries chalky and the open grain reads stringy, and the dressing then has no meat juice to slurry the rice powder. Use an instant-read thermometer; the window on a cut this lean is narrow and finger-press doneness has too much variance.

Slice against the grain, every time. Sirloin, flank, and skirt all have a pronounced, visible grain. Slicing across it (perpendicular to the fiber direction) shortens the muscle fibers to bite size and is the single biggest lever on perceived tenderness. A with-the-grain slice on a lean grilled cut eats tough no matter how perfectly it was cooked.

The toasted rice powder is the structural element. Without khao khua, nam tok is grilled beef in a lime dressing, a wet plate. The pounded toasted rice hydrates in the warm meat juice and lime and thickens the liquid to a slurry that clings to every slice, and it contributes the nutty-popcorn aroma and faint grit that are the sensory signature of the larb-nam tok family. Pound it in a stone mortar, not a spice grinder; the mortar leaves the coarse grit that a grinder pulverizes into dust.

Balance and seasoning curve. Nam tok is salty-front and sour-prominent, with the sweet quiet and the heat building after the swallow. Season in two warmth windows: the fish sauce, palm sugar, and chili powder go in while the beef is warm so the sugar melts and the chili blooms, then the lime goes in once the meat has cooled to warm-not-hot so its bright top notes survive. Taste at the end and rebalance on the salt-acid axis (fish sauce up if flat, palm sugar up if too sharp), never by drowning the dish in sweet.

Timing reality. The whole dish comes together in the 5 minutes the steak rests, so the dressing components, the powders, and the herbs all need to be staged before the steak comes off the grill. And it does not hold: the rice powder keeps absorbing dressing and the herbs wilt within 20 to 30 minutes of the salt and warm meat. Slice, dress, and serve in one continuous motion.
