Mojo verde

The Canarian green herb-pepper-oil sauce: cilantro (or parsley), green bell pepper (or green chili for heat), garlic, cumin, sea salt, sherry vinegar (or white wine vinegar), and olive oil, pounded or processed into a vibrant green pourable sauce. The canonical pairing for papas arrugadas (Canarian wrinkled potatoes) and for grilled fish, chicken, vegetables, bread, and eggs across the Canary Islands and into mainland Spain. Structurally a pepper-thickened green sauce that bridges chimichurri (vinegar-and-herb logic) and salsa verde (paste-and-oil structure) with the singular Canarian cumin signature.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

The herbs and aromatics

  1. 50 g fresh cilantro, leaves and tender stems, washed and thoroughly dried; leaves and the upper tender stems used; tough lower stems discarded
  2. 60 g green bell pepper or Italian frying pepper, mild, seeds and ribs removed; roughly chopped — (about ½ medium green bell pepper, or 1 Italian frying pepper; the green pepper is structural for body and the canonical mild green-pepper flavor; for a spicier mojo, substitute a small green chili, see modifications)
  3. 12 g garlic clove, peeled, germ removed if visibly green — (3 medium cloves; raw garlic is canonical; the bite integrates with the cumin and pepper)

The spice and balance

  1. 3 g cumin seeds, toasted in a dry pan 60 seconds over medium heat, then ground to a fine powder in a spice grinder or mortar — is acceptable as a substitute, see substitutions)
  2. 4 g coarse sea salt or kosher salt
  3. 25 g sherry vinegar (or white wine vinegar)

The fat

  1. 120 g extra-virgin olive oil

Execution

  1. 1
    In a small dry skillet over medium heat, toast the 3 g / 1½ tsp cumin seeds for 60 to 90 seconds, shaking the pan continuously, until fragrant and slightly darker. Transfer to a spice grinder or mortar and grind to a fine even powder; the toast deepens the volatile aromatic compounds and is the structural difference between a vibrant mojo verde and a flat one.
  2. 2
    In the bowl of a food processor (4 to 7 cup capacity), combine the 50 g / 1¼ cups cilantro, 60 g / ½ medium green bell pepper (chopped), 12 g / 3 garlic cloves, the ground toasted cumin, and the 4 g / ¾ tsp salt. Pulse 8 to 10 times in 1-second bursts, scraping the sides between pulses, until the mixture is reduced to a coarse-medium paste with no visible whole leaves but with structural texture preserved.
  3. 3
    Add the 25 g / 5 tsp sherry vinegar; pulse 3 to 4 times to integrate. With the motor running on continuous low, drizzle in the 120 g / ½ cup plus 2 tbsp olive oil through the feed tube in a slow steady stream over about 20 seconds; the oil incorporates into the herb-and-pepper suspension and the sauce takes on a glossy unified appearance. Do not over-process; mojo verde is meant to retain visible herb and pepper structure, not become a smooth slurry.
  4. 4
    Taste and adjust: mojo verde should read herb-forward (cilantro), with structural body from the green pepper, a noticeable cumin-and-garlic warm-spice register, and a clean sherry-vinegar lift; if flat, an extra 1 g / pinch of coarse sea salt and 5 g / 1 tsp sherry vinegar; if too sharp, more olive oil; if the cumin is muted, the spice was insufficiently toasted (toast and grind another 1 g / ½ tsp cumin and re-fold). Let rest 15 minutes at room temperature for the flavors to integrate; the rest is structural for depth.
  5. 5
    Serve as the canonical sauce for papas arrugadas (Canarian wrinkled potatoes: small new potatoes boiled in heavily salted water until the skins wrinkle, served with mojo verde and mojo rojo for dipping); as a sauce for grilled or roasted fish (especially Canarian-style fish like vieja, sama, or any white fish); as a sauce for grilled chicken, pork, or lamb; as a sauce for grilled or roasted vegetables (especially potatoes, eggplant, sweet pepper, zucchini, asparagus); as a spread for grilled bread or a dip for crusty country bread; as a topping for eggs (fried, scrambled, soft-boiled); as a stir-in for soups and stews (a spoonful in a bowl of fish stew or vegetable soup at service brings structural herbal-and-cumin depth); as a marinade for grilled fish or chicken (mix 30 g mojo verde with 30 g olive oil and a squeeze of lemon as the marinade base, refrigerate the protein 1 to 4 hours); and as a finish for grain bowls and rice dishes.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Heat heat level→as a spicier mojo verde with green chili

    Add 1 small fresh green chili (jalapeño, serrano, or Italian peperoncino), seeds removed for moderate heat or left in for canonical Canarian heat, in addition to (or in place of half of) the green bell pepper. The result reads structurally Canarian with a deeper chili-forward heat; canonical for serving with rich oily fish, fried foods, and rice dishes where the heat cuts through the fat.

  • Regional herb pivot→as parsley mojo verde (the mainland Spanish and some Canarian preparations)

    Replace the cilantro with 50 g flat-leaf parsley (leaves and tender stems). The result reads less aromatic and more vegetal-bitter than the cilantro version; canonical for cooks with a cilantro aversion and for some Canarian and mainland Spanish households that prefer the parsley register. Either is correct; cilantro is the more international Canarian reference, parsley is the older mainland Spanish variant.

  • Occasion cuisine pivot→as mojo verde with avocado (a modern Spanish-Mexican fusion variant)

    Add ½ ripe avocado (about 100 g flesh) to the food processor with the herbs and pepper; reduce the olive oil to 90 g. The result is creamier and richer than canonical mojo verde, with the avocado fat replacing some of the olive oil; structurally a fusion sauce that reads as the bridge between mojo verde and Mexican guacamole salsa. Pairs especially well with grilled fish, fish tacos, and grain bowls.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • fresh cilantro → flat-leaf parsley (see modifications) or a 50/50 cilantro-and-parsley blend See modifications for the full parsley conversion. The 50/50 blend reads brighter and more balanced than either pure herb and is a useful middle ground for households with mixed cilantro tolerance.
  • green bell pepper or Italian frying pepper, mild → small green chili (jalapeño, serrano, or peperoncino) See modifications for the spicier version. The mild green pepper is the canonical structural component for body; replacing it entirely with green chili shifts the sauce toward a green chili sauce and loses some of the structural body.
  • sherry vinegar → white wine vinegar or apple cider vinegar Sherry vinegar is the canonical Spanish choice and reads more elegant and slightly nutty; white wine vinegar reads sharper and cleaner and is the standard supermarket substitute; apple cider vinegar reads slightly fruitier and is acceptable. All work; the sherry vinegar version reads most authentically Canarian.

Notes

Mojo verde occupies a useful position in the global green sauce repertoire as a structurally Spanish (and specifically Canarian) sauce that bridges the herb-acid logic of chimichurri and the cumin-and-pepper logic of North African chermoula. The Canary Islands at the crossroads of Europe, Africa, and the Americas produce a cuisine that integrates Spanish technique, North African spice (the cumin signature in mojo derives from this), and Latin American ingredients (the bell pepper itself is a New World ingredient adopted into the islands cookery); mojo verde is the structural sauce of this Atlantic crossroads cookery.

The cumin toast is the single defining technique. The Canarian cumin-and-green-pepper signature is what distinguishes mojo verde from every other green herb-and-oil sauce in the Mediterranean (chimichurri verde, salsa verde italiana, pesto alla genovese, zhoug). The cumin must be toasted whole and freshly ground; pre-ground cumin reads flatter and the sauce loses its characteristic Canarian warmth. The 60-to-90-second toast is short but structural; without it, the cumin reads dusty and the sauce reads incomplete.

The pepper choice is structural. Canonical mojo verde uses a mild green pepper for body (not heat), which gives the sauce its characteristic structural thickness and the vegetal-sweet green-pepper flavor that supports the cilantro and cumin. Substituting a hot green chili (jalapeño, serrano) produces a sauce that is recognizably mojo-verde-adjacent but reads as a different sauce (a green chili sauce with cumin, rather than a mojo); both are valid, but the mild-pepper version is the canonical Canarian reference. For a spicier version, the right move is to add a small green chili in addition to the mild pepper, not to replace it.

Versatility. Mojo verde is structurally a Canarian Spanish sauce, but its herb-and-cumin-and-vinegar profile travels naturally across a wider range of applications than its regional origin suggests. Use it as: the canonical pairing for papas arrugadas (Canarian wrinkled potatoes; one of the most famous regional Spanish dishes), grilled or roasted potatoes (sweet potato, fingerling, new potato, roasted wedges), and potato salad; a sauce for grilled and roasted fish (especially white fish like cod, halibut, sea bass, branzino, and oily fish like salmon, mackerel, sardines; the Canarian canonical pairing); a sauce for grilled chicken, pork, lamb, and beef; a sauce for grilled and roasted vegetables (asparagus, broccoli, cauliflower, sweet pepper, zucchini, eggplant, summer squash); a spread for grilled bread (pan tumaca-adjacent applications, where the mojo replaces the traditional tomato rub) or a dip for crusty country bread; a topping for eggs (fried, scrambled, soft-boiled, shakshuka-adjacent egg dishes); a stir-in for soups and stews (especially fish stews, vegetable soups, and the Canarian potaje canario chickpea stew; a spoonful in each bowl at service brings structural herbal-cumin depth); a marinade base for grilled fish or chicken (mix 30 g mojo verde with 30 g olive oil and a squeeze of lemon; refrigerate the protein 1 to 4 hours); a sauce for fried fish (especially small whole fried fish, fritto misto, calamari); a stir-in for grain and rice dishes (especially Spanish-style rice plates, paella-adjacent applications, and grain bowls); a condiment for cheese (especially Canarian palmero and majorero cheeses, and broader Spanish manchego and idiazabal; a tablespoon spooned next to a wedge of cheese with crusty bread); and as a cross-cuisine condiment for tacos (the cilantro-and-cumin signature transitions naturally onto Mexican applications, especially fish tacos and grilled vegetable tacos).

Pairs across Canarian (canonical), broader Spanish, Atlantic Portuguese (Madeira, Azores), modern Latin American cooking (where mojo has been adopted into the cookery of multiple Spanish-Caribbean and Atlantic countries), North African cooking (the cumin signature reads natural on Moroccan and Tunisian applications), and contemporary American cooking (especially modern Spanish-influenced kitchens and seafood-forward restaurants).

Storage. Keeps 5 to 7 days refrigerated in a sealed glass jar with a thin film of olive oil over the surface (the oil seal prevents oxidation; without it the sauce darkens visibly within 48 hours). Bring to room temperature 20 minutes before service; the cold sauce reads dull, the room-temperature sauce reads vibrant. The flavor deepens noticeably over the first 24 hours as the cumin and garlic fully integrate; mojo verde made a day ahead reads better than the same sauce made an hour ahead. Freezes acceptably for 1 month in small portions; thaw at room temperature and re-fold to restore the texture.

Sources
  • The Canarian canon (the Canary Islands, off the northwest coast of Africa, Spanish since the 15th century). Mojo verde is one of two structural mojos (the other being mojo rojo, with dried red peppers and paprika); both are documented across Canarian cookbooks since the 19th century and are structurally older. The Canarian cookery is shaped by the islands position at the crossroads of Spanish, Portuguese, North African, and Latin American influence; the cumin signature in mojo is thought to derive from North African (Berber) influence on the islands. — Two structural schools: cilantro-based mojo verde (mojo de cilantro, the most international form) and parsley-based mojo verde (mojo de perejil, more common in some Canarian households and on mainland Spain). The version here is cilantro-based with parsley as a substitution. Distinct from chimichurri (vinegar-and-herb suspension, Argentine register with oregano) and from salsa verde italiana (anchovy-caper-parsley paste, Italian register); mojo verde shares structural DNA with both but is identified by the cumin-and-green-pepper signature that is uniquely Canarian. The canonical green pepper component is a mild green pepper (Spanish pimiento verde italiano) rather than a hot chili; spicier versions use a small amount of fresh green chili.
