Autumn simmer pot (apple, orange, cinnamon, clove)

A non-edible house-fragrance preparation. Whole spices, orange and apple peels, and a few drops of vanilla simmered on the lowest setting all day. Replaces the chemical headache of plug-in air fresheners with actual volatile aromatics from food.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • This is a NON-EDIBLE preparation. The water is for evaporation and house fragrance; do not drink it. Extended simmering concentrates wax coatings, pesticide residue, and packaging compounds from the peels.
  • Plan to be home for the full 8 to 12 hour run. Set a timer to check water level every 2 hours and refill with hot water from a kettle, running the pot dry scorches the spices, produces an acrid smell, and can ruin the pan.
  • Use a stainless steel or enameled cast iron pan. Avoid non-stick at sustained low heat for many hours; the coating can degrade.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Citrus and fruit (use peels of fruit you have eaten)

  1. 1 each fresh orange, peel only; eat the flesh separately
  2. 1 each fresh lemon, peel only; juice the flesh into the pot, save the rest for another use
  3. 200 g apple cores and peels (from 2 to 3 apples)

Whole spices

  1. 3 each cinnamon sticks, broken in half
  2. 15 each whole cloves
  3. 2 each star anise
  4. 0.5 each whole nutmeg, cracked with the back of a knife
  5. 4 each green cardamom pods, cracked

Base

  1. 1500 ml water
  2. 5 g vanilla extract

Execution

  1. 1
    Choose the right pot. A small heavy saucepan (1.5 to 2 L / 6 to 8 cups) is ideal, small surface area means slower evaporation, longer run time. Avoid nonstick if you plan to run the pot for 8+ hours; the surface can degrade at sustained low heat. Stainless or enameled cast iron is the right choice.
  2. 2
    Combine the orange peel, lemon peel (and squeezed juice from the lemon), and 200 g / 1 cup apple cores and peels in the pot. Add the 3 broken cinnamon sticks, 15 cloves, 2 star anise, the half cracked nutmeg, and the 4 cracked cardamom pods.
  3. 3
    Add the 1500 ml / 6 cups water, enough to fully submerge everything with at least 5 cm / 2 inches of headspace. Stir in the 5 g / 1 tsp vanilla extract if using.
  4. 4
    Bring to a bare simmer over medium heat, then drop to the absolute lowest setting on your stove (or move to a small burner on its lowest setting). The water should produce occasional lazy bubbles, never a rolling boil, never quiet. The goal is sustained low evaporation that releases volatile aromatics into the air over hours rather than blasting everything out in 20 minutes.
  5. 5
    Hold at this low simmer for 8 to 12 hours. Check the water level every 2 hours; top up with hot water from a kettle when the level drops below half. The pot will smell strongest in the first 2 to 3 hours, then settle into a softer ambient register that scents the whole house through the rest of the day.8 h
  6. 6
    When done for the day, cool completely and either refrigerate (the pot will run again for one more day, weaker but functional) or compost the solids. The water itself can be poured down the drain, it is just citrus-spice tea.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion spice profile→baking pie (gingerbread-leaning)

    Add 30 g / 2 tbsp grated fresh ginger and 1 tbsp molasses to the pot. The dish smells distinctly like baking gingerbread cookies. Excellent for December evenings.

  • Occasion spice profile→mulled wine (without the wine)

    Add 5 black peppercorns, an extra star anise, and 30 g / 2 tbsp dried cranberries. Smells like a Glühwein market stall. Effective even without the actual wine, which would evaporate too fast to register.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • fresh orange and lemon peels → frozen citrus peels (kept in a bag in the freezer) Keep a freezer bag for peels from oranges, lemons, and grapefruits you eat through the week. Use them directly from frozen; the cell-wall damage from freezing releases aromatics more easily.
  • whole vanilla extract → a used vanilla bean pod (after the seeds have been scraped out for baking) Tossing a spent vanilla pod into the simmer pot recovers the residual vanilla aromatics that would otherwise go to waste. Even more rewarding than fresh extract.

Notes

This is a non-edible preparation. The water is for evaporation, not drinking, extended cooking concentrates whatever else has touched the citrus peels (wax coating, conventional pesticide residue, packaging adhesive). Treat as a fragrance preparation, not a tea.

Why this works better than scented candles or air fresheners. Commercial fragrance products release synthetic aromatic chemicals (phthalates, limonene derivatives, benzaldehyde isomers) that smell briefly intense but are uniform, flat, and chemically irritating to many people. A simmer pot releases dozens of natural volatile compounds (terpenes from citrus oils, eugenol from cloves, cinnamaldehyde from cinnamon, linalool from cardamom) at low sustained concentrations that the nose reads as complex and depth-layered. The smell also penetrates fabric and carpet less aggressively and dissipates within hours of pot-off, so the house does not retain residual fragrance the next morning.

Sustained low heat is the technique. The aromatic compounds in cinnamon, clove, and citrus peel are mostly volatile at temperatures well below boiling, they evaporate steadily at 70 to 80°C over hours, which is exactly what a barely simmering pot delivers. A hard boil drives everything out in the first 30 minutes and the pot goes flat for the rest of the day; the lazy low simmer is the entire engineering.

Never let the pot run dry. Even at the lowest setting, a 1.5 L pot evaporates roughly 200 ml per hour. The check-and-refill cadence keeps the spices from scorching and ruining the fragrance.
