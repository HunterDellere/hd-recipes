Dry-roasted peanuts

Raw skin-on or blanched peanuts dry-roasted in a 175°C / 350°F oven until evenly amber, fragrant, and shatter-crisp. The home-roasted kernel is fresher, more aromatic, and more textural than any pre-roasted bag.

Mise en Place

Servings
Units
Shopping list 2 items ▾
  1. 250 g raw peanuts, skin-on or blanched
  2. 2 g fine sea salt optional

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the oven to 175°C / 350°F. Spread the 250 g raw peanuts in a single layer on a heavy rimmed sheet pan. The single layer is non-negotiable, peanuts piled two-deep brown unevenly, with a scorched bottom layer and a pale top layer. Use a half-sheet pan or split between two sheets if needed.
  2. 2
    Roast 8 minutes undisturbed. Pull the pan out, give the peanuts a vigorous shake or stir with a heatproof spatula to redistribute, return to the oven.
  3. 3
    Roast 4 to 7 more minutes, checking every 90 seconds from minute 12 onward. Pull when the peanuts smell distinctly toasted (popcorn-and-roast-coffee aroma carrying out of the oven), the skins are slightly cracked and starting to flake, and a halved kernel shows uniform pale-amber to amber color through the cross-section. The window between toasted and burnt is narrow at this temperature, 60 seconds late and the peanuts shift from amber to scorched, with a bitter taste that no finishing seasoning can mask. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Tip the hot peanuts onto a cool sheet pan or cooling rack to arrest carryover. They continue to color for 2 to 3 minutes after coming out of the oven; pulling at "almost there" yields the right final color. If using salt, sprinkle the 2 g / ½ tsp fine sea salt over the peanuts while they are still hot, the surface oils released by roasting hold the salt; cold peanuts shed it. Cool completely (15 to 20 minutes) before storing.
  5. 5
    For skin-on peanuts: rub a handful of cooled peanuts between your palms over a wide bowl, the loosened skins will flake off and can be winnowed away by tossing in front of a fan or simply lifting the peanuts out and discarding the skin pile. Skin-on roasted peanuts are correct for most Southeast Asian and Chinese applications (the polyphenol-bitter skin contributes textural and flavor character); skin-removed roasted peanuts are correct for paler peanut sauces and confections.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Texture cuisine→oil-toasted peanuts (stovetop)

    Skip the oven. Heat 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil (rice bran or peanut) in a wide skillet over medium-low heat. Add the 250 g raw peanuts and cook, stirring constantly, for 9 to 11 minutes until amber. The kernels carry slight residual oil and read richer than oven-roasted; this is the technique behind soy-glazed peanuts and the peanut topping for Sichuan dan dan noodles.

  • Occasion cuisine→soy-glazed (Chinese banquet style)

    After roasting, while peanuts are still warm, toss with 30 g / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, 15 g / 1 tbsp sugar, and 1 g / ¼ tsp Chinese five-spice in a wide bowl. Spread on the sheet and return to a 95°C / 200°F oven for 8 to 10 minutes to dry the glaze. The glaze sets into a thin lacquer that delivers salty-sweet-spiced notes against the toasted kernel.

  • Occasion flavor→cumin-coriander spiced

    Toss hot peanuts with 4 g / 2 tsp ground cumin, 2 g / 1 tsp ground coriander, 2 g / ½ tsp salt, and 5 g / 1 tsp olive oil; spread back on sheet, return to a 175°C oven for 3 minutes to bloom the spices. The bloom brings the spices into the peanut oil and produces a deeper savory profile suitable for snacks, salad toppings, and mezze plates.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • raw peanuts → raw cashews or raw almonds Same technique, slightly different timing. Cashews are done at 8 to 10 minutes (lower fat oxidation point); almonds at 10 to 12 minutes. The skin-rub step does not apply to either. Useful for peanut allergies.

Notes

The single rule. Pull early. Carryover roasting is real and the window between toasted and burnt is roughly 60 seconds. A peanut that looks "almost done" coming out of the oven is the right one to keep; a peanut that looks "perfect" coming out will be over-toasted by the time it cools.

Why home-roasted is better than store-bought. Pre-roasted peanut bags are typically 3 to 9 months between roasting and shelf, even sealed, the fat content (around 50%) means oxidative rancidity has begun. Home-roasted peanuts that come out of the oven and into a sealed jar within 30 minutes deliver the kernel at peak. The flavor difference is most pronounced in dishes where the peanut is read directly (peanut topping on noodles, peanut sauce, peanut brittle) and less pronounced in dishes where the peanut is one of many ingredients.

Skin-on or blanched. Skin-on raw peanuts are the default at most Asian markets. The skins (testae) carry tannins and slight bitterness that distinguish skin-on roasted peanuts from blanched ones. For applications that want a clean peanut flavor (peanut sauce, peanut paste, peanut butter), blanched is correct. For applications that want the textural and flavor complexity of the skin (Southeast Asian salad topping, Chinese noodle topping, snack peanuts), skin-on is correct. Both are sold cheaply at Asian markets; the brand and freshness matter more than the form.

Salt timing. Salt the peanuts hot, immediately out of the oven, while the surface oils are still mobile and can act as a binder for the salt crystals. Cold peanuts shed salt to the bottom of the bowl. For larger salt grains (Maldon, flake), sprinkle and toss before peanuts cool below ~80°C. For fine sea salt, the same window applies but the salt distributes more uniformly.
