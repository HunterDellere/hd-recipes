Recipe

Beef and mushroom noodle soup with xiao long bao aspic

A homestyle improvisation built on a cup of leftover xiao long bao pork-skin aspic that melts into a 4-cup beef broth, doubling the broth body and turning a thin soup into something silken. 1 lb of ground beef hard-seared to fond, ginger and scallion and star anise bloomed in the rendered fat, a half teaspoon of Chinese five-spice for a hint of red-braise warmth, dried shiitake (rehydrated, soaking liquid kept) and a pinch of MSG for stacked glutamate plus guanylate, baby Yukon Gold potatoes simmered into the broth for starch and bite, a cluster of blue oyster mushrooms dry-seared and added at the end for layered umami and meaty pull, finished with thick Shanghai noodles, a drizzle of Chinkiang vinegar and toasted sesame oil, white pepper, and scallion greens. The technique trick is the aspic: a cup of chilled pork-skin gel concentrates more collagen than 4 hours of bone simmer, and it dissolves into the broth in two minutes of warming. A leftover-driven dish that eats like a planned one.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Rehydrate the 20 g dried shiitake in 250 ml cold water at least 1 hour ahead, ideally 4 to 8 hours or overnight. The cold soak is structurally non-negotiable: lentinionic acid (the compound responsible for the signature shiitake aroma) forms only at temperatures below 25 C, and a hot-water shortcut produces a flat-tasting mushroom and a flat-tasting soaking liquid. If you only have 30 minutes, use 60 C water and accept the loss; if you have 8 hours, use cold water and reap the upgrade.
  • The xiao long bao aspic should come straight from the fridge as a firm gel; you will cut it into 1 cm cubes for the soup pot. It melts back to liquid within 2 minutes of warming. If your aspic is too soft to cube cleanly, see the substitution at the bottom for the powdered-gelatin rescue.
  • Cook the Shanghai noodles in their own pot of unsalted water, not in the soup broth. Cooking thick wheat noodles directly in the soup releases enough starch to cloud and thicken the broth past the silky-clear texture the aspic was meant to give it. Boil in plain water, drain, and ladle the hot broth over the noodles bowl-by-bowl.
  • For tonight you will cook noodles for 2 servings; the broth holds 3 bowls. Reserve the broth, beef, potato, and mushroom for a leftover bowl tomorrow, and cook a single portion of fresh noodles when you reheat. Noodles bulk-cooked and stored in the broth turn waterlogged and gluey within an hour.

Mise en place by phase 7 phases ▾

Phase 1, shiitake rehydration (1+ hour ahead)

  1. 20 g dried shiitake mushroom (donko grade if available) — (about 8 medium caps; the guanylate nucleotide source that stacks with beef inosinate and MSG glutamate for synergistic umami)
  2. 250 ml cold water (for the shiitake soak)

Phase 2, sear the beef

  1. 454 g ground beef (80/20 chuck)
  2. 15 g neutral oil (refined avocado, grapeseed, or rice bran)
  3. 4 g kosher salt (for seasoning the beef during the sear)

Phase 3, aromatic bloom

  1. 25 g fresh ginger, peeled and sliced into 3 mm coins — (about a 1 inch knob; coined rather than minced so the cook can fish them out at service if desired)
  2. 16 g garlic cloves, smashed (skin removed) — (4 medium cloves; smash with the flat of a knife to expose the cut surface for allicin release)
  3. 30 g scallions, white and pale-green parts only (reserve the dark greens for garnish) — (whites from 5 scallions, cut into 5 cm batons and smashed; the dark greens, sliced thin, are the bowl garnish)
  4. 1 each star anise (whole pod) — (1 pod; reinforces the anethole note in the five-spice and bridges to the pork aspic, which has a faint sweet-spice memory of any red-braise lineage)
  5. 1.5 g Chinese five-spice powder

  1. 30 ml Shaoxing wine

  5. 5 g dark soy sauce (lao chou)
  6. 3 g MSG (Ajinomoto or equivalent)

Phase 5, potato simmer

  1. 340 g baby Yukon Gold potatoes (or any waxy baby potato)

Phase 6, sear the oyster mushroom cluster

  2. 10 g neutral oil (for the mushroom sear)

Phase 7, noodles and finish (per serving)

  2. 5 g toasted sesame oil (for finishing each bowl)
  3. 5 g Chinkiang black vinegar (for finishing each bowl)
  4. 0.5 g white pepper, freshly ground (for finishing each bowl)
  5. 15 g scallion greens, sliced thin (reserved from Phase 3) — (dark greens from the 5 scallions used in Phase 3; the green portion goes on the bowl raw for textural contrast and aromatic lift)

Execution

  1. 1
    At least 1 hour ahead, ideally 4 to 8 hours, drop the 20 g dried shiitake into a small bowl with the 250 ml cold water and cover with a small plate to keep the mushrooms submerged. The cold soak hydrolyzes the shiitake to its full umami potential and produces the aromatic soaking liquid that joins the broth in Phase 4. The minimum useful soak is 30 minutes in 60 C water; the maximum useful soak is overnight in cold water.
  2. 2
    Heat a heavy 4-quart Dutch oven or wide stainless pot over medium-high heat for 2 minutes until water flicked on the surface sizzles immediately. Add the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil and tilt to coat. Add the 454 g ground beef in a single mass without breaking it up; let it sear undisturbed for 90 seconds so the bottom face builds a hard mahogany crust. Then break the beef apart with a wooden spoon or potato masher into rough 1 cm pebbles and continue cooking 5 to 7 minutes, stirring occasionally. The beef releases its water in the first 2 minutes (the pan will look pale and steamy); keep going past that point until the water has fully evaporated and the meat starts to fry in its own rendered fat. Season with the 4 g / 1 tsp kosher salt once the meat is dry and browning, never before. The visual cue for "done" is grit-dark pebbles with deep brown fond stuck to the pot bottom. About 8 minutes total from when the beef hit the pan. Maillard sear
  3. 3
    Push the beef to one side of the pot. Tilt the pot slightly so the rendered beef fat pools on the empty side. Drop the 25 g sliced ginger, 16 g / 4 smashed garlic cloves, 30 g smashed scallion whites, 1 star anise pod, and 1.5 g / 1/2 tsp Chinese five-spice powder directly into the pooled fat. Bloom for 60 to 90 seconds, stirring constantly, until the aromatics smell sharply fragrant and the five-spice has darkened a shade. The bloom is the single most important flavor moment in the recipe; cold-five-spice added to broth tastes dusty, oil-bloomed five-spice tastes red-braise-warm. Blooming aromatics
  4. 4
    Pour in the 30 ml / 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine to deglaze, scraping the dark fond off the bottom with the wooden spoon. The wine will hiss and reduce by half in about 30 seconds. Add the 240 g cubed xiao long bao aspic, the 950 ml / 4 cups beef broth, the rehydrated shiitake plus its full soaking liquid (pour the liquid through a small sieve to catch any grit from the dried mushrooms, then add the strained liquid and the caps to the pot), the 30 g / 2 tbsp light soy sauce, the 5 g / 1 tsp dark soy sauce, and the 3 g / 1 tsp MSG. Bring to a simmer over medium-high heat. The aspic cubes will dissolve completely within 2 minutes of warming, contributing the collagen that turns the broth from thin to silky. Deglazing
  5. 5
    Once the broth reaches a steady simmer (small bubbles breaking the surface every second or two), add the 340 g halved-or-quartered baby Yukon Gold potatoes. Cover loosely with the lid askew and simmer 12 to 15 minutes, until a paring knife slides into the center of a potato with no resistance. The waxy cultivar holds its cube shape through the simmer; the cubes should look glassy-tender on the surface and uniformly soft to the knife. Taste the broth at this point: it should read deeply savory, salty, faintly sweet from the five-spice and aspic, with a noticeable body that coats the spoon. Adjust if needed with another 1/2 tsp light soy or another pinch of MSG; do not add more dark soy past the 5 g already in, the color is balanced.
  6. 6
    While the potatoes simmer, sear the oyster mushroom in a separate skillet. Heat a wide cast-iron or stainless skillet over medium-high heat for 2 minutes. Tear the 170 g blue oyster mushroom cluster by hand into 4 to 6 large fan-shaped pieces at the base (do not slice with a knife; tearing preserves the pulled-strand fiber direction that reads meaty). Place the torn pieces dry in the hot skillet, gill-side down for the largest fans, in a single non-crowded layer. Sear undisturbed 90 seconds; the mushroom releases water (the pan will look wet and steamy) and the gill surface in contact with the pan starts to color. Flip each piece, sear another 60 seconds, then drizzle in the 10 g / 2 tsp neutral oil and toss to coat. Continue cooking another 90 seconds, until the mushroom edges are deep gold and the cluster reads firm-meaty rather than wet-floppy. About 5 minutes total.
  7. 7
    When the potatoes are tender, transfer the seared oyster mushroom (with any browned bits from its skillet, scraped in with a spoon) into the soup pot. Stir gently to distribute. Hold the soup at a bare simmer (not boiling) for 2 to 3 minutes to let the mushroom absorb broth and the flavors merge. Fish out the 1 star anise pod, the 30 g smashed scallion whites, and the 16 g / 4 smashed garlic cloves if desired; they have done their aromatic work and can be discarded. Some cooks leave them in for visual rustic effect; either works.
  8. 8
    Meanwhile, bring a separate medium pot of unsalted water to a rolling boil for the noodles. Add the 200 g fresh Shanghai noodles to the boiling water and stir gently for the first 30 seconds to prevent clumping. Boil 3 to 4 minutes until the noodles read tender-chewy at the center; thick Shanghai noodles take longer than spaghetti. Drain and divide between two warmed bowls (about 100 g cooked noodle per bowl). Do not rinse the noodles; the residual surface starch helps the broth cling.
  9. 9
    Plate and finish. Ladle the broth, beef, potato, shiitake, and torn oyster mushroom pieces over the noodles in each bowl, distributing the solids roughly evenly between the two bowls (about 1.5 cups broth per bowl, plus a generous share of each component). Finish each bowl with 5 g / 1 tsp toasted sesame oil drizzled in a spiral, 5 g / 1 tsp Chinkiang vinegar drizzled the same way (the vinegar is structurally required, do not skip; the rich broth needs the acid lift to balance), a few twists of white pepper (about 0.5 g / 1/8 tsp), and a generous scatter of the reserved sliced scallion greens (about 7 to 8 g per bowl). Serve immediately. The remaining broth and solids hold in the fridge for 3 to 4 days; cook a fresh portion of Shanghai noodles per leftover bowl.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→braised beef shank or brisket (the Taiwanese beef noodle direction)

    Skip the ground beef phase entirely. Use 700 g beef shank or chuck cut into 3 cm chunks, browned hard in batches in the same Dutch oven (about 4 minutes per batch). Add 30 g doubanjiang to the aromatic bloom along with the five-spice for the Taiwanese hong shao niu rou mian profile. After the broth and aspic come together, simmer the beef chunks 90 minutes (covered, low heat) until fork-tender before adding the potatoes. The dish becomes a true 2-hour beef noodle soup; the aspic still does its body-building work. Expect to bump the broth by another 240 ml / 1 cup to account for the longer reduction.

  • Dietary noodle direction→rice noodle bowl (gluten-free pho-adjacent)

    Swap the Shanghai noodles for 200 g dried medium banh pho rice noodles, soaked in cold water 30 minutes then blanched 10 seconds in boiling water per bowl just before serving. Drop the dark soy from Phase 4 (it would stain the lighter rice noodle bowl) and bump the light soy by 1 tsp. The dish reads less Shanghainese and more pho-adjacent, with the aspic giving it a body that pho broth typically achieves only through 8 hours of simmer.

  • Heat heat→chili oil and Sichuan peppercorn finish

    Bloom 5 g toasted Sichuan peppercorn in the aromatic phase along with the star anise (remove before serving). Pass a jar of homemade or store-bought chili oil at the table for each diner to drizzle in to taste. The numbing from the peppercorn and the heat from the chili oil read more Sichuan than Shanghainese; the dish stays the same dish, just with the spice register dialed up.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • xiao long bao aspic (when no leftover is available) → 240 ml additional beef or pork broth plus 5 g powdered gelatin, bloomed and dissolved Bloom the gelatin in 60 ml cold water for 5 minutes, then dissolve into 180 ml warm broth. The mixture should set to a soft gel when chilled briefly; if it does not, add another 2 g gelatin. The flavor depth of real aspic is missing but the structural body-building function is preserved. The cleaner option, for the cook with no aspic and no time for gelatin work, is just to use 1200 ml beef broth and reduce the broth by 250 ml over hard heat before adding the aspic-replacement liquids; the reduction concentrates flavor and slightly improves body, though the silky aspic mouthfeel is unavailable without gelatin.
  • ground beef (80/20 chuck) → ground pork (80/20 shoulder) or ground lamb Ground pork makes the dish more conventionally Chinese (pork being the default Chinese ground meat) and adds a slightly sweeter character; the dish reads as more of a traditional pork-noodle soup. Ground lamb pushes the dish toward Xinjiang and Mongolian flavors, particularly when paired with cumin in the aromatic bloom. Both work at the same weight.
  • blue oyster mushroom → pearl oyster, king trumpet (Pleurotus eryngii), maitake, or fresh shiitake Any oyster-family mushroom works identically. King trumpet sliced into 1 cm rounds is the meatiest substitute. Maitake (hen of the woods) is the closest textural match and arguably better. Fresh shiitake works (use 200 g and slice into 5 mm thick wedges) but reads less meaty and more conventionally Chinese-soup.
  • Shanghai noodles (cu mian) → fresh udon, dried bucatini, or thick lo mein noodles Fresh udon is the closest substitute by texture (thick, chewy, non-alkaline wheat). Dried bucatini cooked to al dente works and adds the bonus of the hollow center holding broth. Fresh thick lo mein noodles are alkaline-treated and read springier; acceptable for the cook who likes the alkaline chew. Rice noodles work but shift the dish toward Vietnamese; see the modification for that direction.
  • baby Yukon Gold potatoes → baby red potatoes, fingerlings, or daikon radish Any waxy baby potato is structurally equivalent. Daikon (cut into 2 cm cubes) is the Chinese alternative; it adds a faint pleasant pepper note and matches Chinese homestyle soup tradition more closely than potato. Use 340 g daikon for an even swap.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → xiao long bao aspic (leftover, chilled and firm) make it yourselfstub The aspic is the structural body of this soup, and homemade aspic from pork skin and bones has 3 to 4 times the collagen density of any commercial chicken or pork broth on the market. A day-long aspic batch produces enough for one dumpling session plus 3 or 4 soup-fortification bowls; freeze the leftover aspic in 100 g portions and any future soup is a single thaw away from silky.
  • → beef broth (low-sodium, ideally homemade) make it yourself Homemade brown beef broth is the body baseline that the aspic builds on; a thin store-bought broth still works (the aspic will do most of the heavy lifting) but a roasted-bone homemade broth with its own gelatin pushes the soup from very good to restaurant-level. Make 2 L on a weekend and freeze in 240 ml / 1 cup portions; this recipe needs 4 cups, so a single batch supports 2 to 3 dinners of this soup.
  • → fresh Shanghai noodles (cu mian, thick round wheat noodles) make it yourselfstub Hand-rolled fresh Shanghai noodles take 90 minutes of mostly-passive time and produce a chewier, cleaner-tasting noodle than the store-bought vacuum-pack version. The chew is the entire reason to use a thick wheat noodle in this soup; the upgrade is real but optional, and the store-bought Twin Marquis version is genuinely good enough for a weeknight.

Notes

Why the aspic is the structural play. A cup of chilled xiao long bao aspic (the same pork-skin-and-bone gel that goes inside soup dumplings) contains roughly 25 g of dissolved gelatin: more collagen than 4 cups of normal beef broth simmered for 6 hours. When the aspic cubes drop into hot broth, they melt back to liquid within 2 minutes and distribute that gelatin across the whole pot. The functional consequence is that the broth gains noticeable body (the spoon coats, the liquid clings to the noodles and meat rather than running off them), and the mouthfeel reads as a long-simmered restaurant soup rather than a 40-minute home one. This is the single move that elevates the dish from competent weeknight soup to something that punches well above its time investment. Cooks without leftover aspic should consider the powdered-gelatin substitution; the gelatin alone replaces about 70 percent of the body-building effect without the flavor depth.

Umami stacking, in plain language. The dish is engineered around three free-glutamate-or-nucleotide sources that synergize: glutamate from the MSG, soy sauce, and the natural beef extract; inosinate from the seared ground beef (inosinate is the nucleotide that forms when meat is aged or cooked); and guanylate from the dried shiitake (the most concentrated guanylate source in any pantry mushroom). The chemistry behind umami synergy is well understood: glutamate plus inosinate, or glutamate plus guanylate, produces an umami response 5 to 10 times the additive intensity of either alone. By stacking all three sources in the same pot, the soup hits a savor depth that a single-source umami broth (say, an all-MSG broth, or a chicken-stock-only broth) cannot reach. This is why the recipe specifies all three rather than picking one; the chemistry is multiplicative, not additive.

The five-spice question. Five-spice in this dish is doing aromatic bridging, not foreground seasoning. The blend lives in the same flavor neighborhood as the master-stock and red-braise tradition (lu wei) that any Chinese cook would recognize from beef noodle soup, char siu, and braised soy-sauce eggs. A half teaspoon in 4 cups of broth is light by design; past 1 teaspoon, the clove and cinnamon take over and the dish reads medicinally heavy. The blend belongs in the aromatic bloom phase rather than the broth phase, because the volatile anethole (the licorice compound from the star anise and fennel) needs the hot oil bath to volatilize and integrate. Five-spice dropped directly into broth tastes dusty.

The MSG question. MSG is canonical in Chinese home and restaurant cooking and is specified here for the structural reason that it delivers pure glutamate without the body, color, or aroma of any other umami source. A pinch of MSG sharpens the savor without changing the broth profile; the same savor from added soy sauce would darken the broth past the desired bronze color, and the same savor from added fish sauce would push the dish toward Southeast Asia. The 1 teaspoon in this recipe is a moderate dose by Chinese standards (about 0.3 percent of the finished soup weight); cooks who want to start lower can add 1/2 tsp, taste, and add the rest if the broth reads under-savory. The American consumer panic around MSG was never substantiated by controlled study and has been broadly recognized as having had racial undertones; cook with it the way you cook with salt, deliberately and to taste.

Why dry-sear the mushrooms separately. Oyster mushrooms are 88 to 90 percent water by mass, and the structural cook rule (from the oyster-mushroom ingredient page) is dry-sear first, fat second. Adding oyster mushroom directly to wet broth produces flavorless, soggy fans; dry-searing in a hot skillet first drives off the water, builds Maillard color on the gill faces, and produces firm-meaty pulled-strand pieces that hold their texture in the broth. The 5-minute separate-skillet sear is non-negotiable for the texture this dish wants. Cooks who want a single-pot version can dry-sear the mushroom in the same Dutch oven after the beef comes out and before the aromatic bloom, but the timing gets tight and the cook is juggling two simultaneous high-heat events.

Leftover handling. The broth, beef, potato, and mushroom hold beautifully in the fridge for 3 to 4 days; the broth actually improves overnight as the flavors marry. The aspic is gone by then (dissolved into the broth permanently) but the gelatin it contributed still gives the cold-stored soup a noticeable jiggle. To reheat: warm a single bowl portion (about 1.5 cups broth plus solids) in a small saucepan over medium heat to a simmer, 4 to 5 minutes. Cook a single portion of fresh Shanghai noodles in a small pot of unsalted water during the broth warm-up. Ladle the broth over the fresh noodles in a clean bowl and finish with sesame oil, vinegar, white pepper, and scallion greens as in the original. Bulk-cooking noodles and storing them in the broth is the wrong move; the noodles waterlog and turn gluey within an hour.
