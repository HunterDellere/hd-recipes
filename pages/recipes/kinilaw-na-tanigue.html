Kinilaw na tanigue

Filipino vinegar-cured raw fish. Spanish mackerel diced and submerged briefly in cane vinegar with ginger, chili, red onion, and calamansi, then drained and served at the just-cured edge where the fish reads firm and opaque on the surface but still translucent at the center. Pre-dates Spanish ceviche by centuries; the vinegar is the defining cure, not the citrus, and the cure is short and aggressive, not slow and gentle.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Source sashimi-grade fish from a fishmonger you trust. Tanigue (Spanish mackerel) is the canonical kinilaw fish in the Philippines; in non-Filipino markets, look for sushi-grade tuna, kingfish, hamachi (yellowtail), or any firm white ocean fish that has been frozen to -20°C / -4°F for at least 24 hours (the parasite-kill threshold). Freshwater fish is never appropriate for kinilaw.
  • Chill all the curing components in the fridge for 30 minutes before assembly. The fish should stay cold through the entire cure to maintain texture and minimize bacterial risk. A warm cure produces a mushy, off-textured fish.
  • Time the cure aggressively. Kinilaw is a 5-to-10-minute submerge, not the 30-minute-to-overnight cure of Mexican or Peruvian ceviche. Past 10 minutes the vinegar starts to fully denature the surface proteins and the fish goes from raw-but-cured to fully-firm, losing the textural identity. Drain on time.

Mise en place by phase 4 phases ▾

Phase 1, fish prep

  1. 500 g tanigue (Spanish mackerel) or sushi-grade tuna fillet, skinless, pin-boned, trimmed of any bloodline (the dark red strip), and diced into 1.5 cm / ½ inch cubes — , kingfish, snapper, or albacore tuna. Frozen-to-spec is non-negotiable for safety)

Phase 2, cure

  1. 120 g cane vinegar (sukang iloko or Filipino cane vinegar)
  2. 30 g calamansi juice (or lime + orange juice 3:1)
  3. 6 g kosher salt
  4. 30 g fresh ginger, peeled, julienned into 3 cm matchsticks — (a 4 cm / 1½ inch knob; ginger is the canonical aromatic of kinilaw, contributes piney brightness and helps balance the fish bloodline notes)
  5. 8 g fresh bird-eye chili (siling labuyo), sliced thin crosswise into 2 mm rings, seeds in for heat — (2 to 3 chilies; canonical Filipino bird-eye chili. Use Thai bird chili as the closest substitute; jalapeno is too mild)

Phase 3, finish

  1. 80 g red onion, sliced thin pole-to-pole into 2 mm crescents — (½ small red onion; goes in only after the fish is drained, the raw bite is structural to the dish)
  2. 80 g cucumber, seeded and diced into 5 mm cubes — (about ½ small cucumber; contributes textural cooling crunch and helps absorb residual cure liquid)
  3. 10 g cilantro leaves and tender stems, leaves picked, stems chopped fine
  4. 2 g kosher salt, additional for finishing
  5. 2 g freshly cracked black pepper

Phase 4, serving

  1. 1 each lime or calamansi wedges, cut into 4 wedges — (extra citrus for diners who want more acid at the table)

Execution

  1. 1
    Prep the fish. Pat the 500 g fish fillet completely dry with paper towels. Trim any bloodline (the dark red strip running down the center) with a sharp knife; the bloodline tastes metallic and grainy in cured fish. Dice into even 1.5 cm cubes; the evenness is structural to even curing, irregular pieces cure unevenly. Keep the diced fish in a stainless or glass bowl in the fridge while you build the cure.
  2. 2
    Build the cure. Combine the 120 g / ½ cup cane vinegar, 30 g / 2 tbsp calamansi juice, 6 g / 1 tsp kosher salt, 30 g julienned ginger, and 8 g sliced chili in a wide non-reactive bowl (glass or stainless, never aluminum or reactive metals, the acid will leach metal flavors). Whisk to dissolve the salt.
  3. 3
    Submerge the fish. Add the diced fish to the cure; toss gently to ensure every piece is coated and submerged. The fish should be fully covered; if not, tilt the bowl or use a slightly smaller bowl. Refrigerate for 5 to 7 minutes only. Watch the color: the fish surface will turn from translucent-pink-to-translucent-white in 60 seconds and start to look opaque on the surface; by 5 minutes the exterior is fully opaque while the interior is still translucent. This is the kinilaw cure endpoint.
  4. 4
    Drain immediately. Pour the fish into a fine mesh strainer set over a bowl. Let the cure liquid drain off completely (about 60 seconds); do not press or squeeze the fish, you will damage the texture. Discard the drained cure liquid (or reserve, see notes on kilawin sauce). Return the drained fish to a clean bowl.
  5. 5
    Add the 80 g sliced red onion, 80 g diced cucumber, 10 g cilantro, 2 g / ¼ tsp salt, and 2 g / ½ tsp cracked black pepper to the drained fish. Toss gently to combine. The onion and cucumber should be raw and crisp; they go in after the cure specifically to retain texture. Taste, the kinilaw should read aggressively sour-front (vinegar and citrus), salt-balanced, with the chili heat building, ginger brightness in the background, and the fish itself reading clean and slightly firm.
  6. 6
    Serve immediately in small bowls or on a flat plate with lime wedges on the side. Kinilaw is structurally an appetizer or a starter for a Filipino meal, traditionally served as pulutan (drinking food) with cold beer. Eat with a small spoon; the dish should be at fridge-cold temperature, the warmth of the room will dull the textural sharpness within 10 minutes.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→kinilaw na isda sa gata (Bicolano coconut kinilaw)

    After draining the fish (step 4), add 120 ml / ½ cup full-fat coconut cream (the thick top from a can) along with the red onion, cucumber, and herbs. The coconut cream mellows the vinegar sharpness and produces a creamier, richer mouthfeel. The Bicol (southern Luzon) regional version, common in coastal communities where coconut and fish are both abundant.

  • Protein protein→kinilaw na tahong (mussel kinilaw)

    Substitute 500 g raw shucked mussel meat for the fish. The mussel needs slightly longer cure (8 to 10 minutes) because of denser texture. Discard any mussel that did not close fresh; bivalve safety is more stringent than fish safety. A coastal Visayan canonical version.

  • Regional cuisine→kinilaw na malasugue (with coconut vinegar and coconut milk)

    Substitute coconut vinegar (sukang tuba) for the cane vinegar, and add 60 ml / ¼ cup coconut cream after draining. Marlin (malasugue) is the canonical fish for this version, common in Cebu and Bohol. Substitute swordfish or tuna. The coconut vinegar is sweeter and softer than cane; the dish reads more rounded and tropical-leaning.

  • Occasion occasion→kinilaw na masa with green mango

    Add 100 g julienned green (unripe) mango to the post-drain mix along with the red onion and cucumber. The unripe mango contributes additional brightness and a tropical fruit-sour note that pairs especially well with tanigue and tuna. A common Filipino pulutan (drinking-food) version, brightens the dish further for hot weather eating.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • tanigue (Spanish mackerel) → sushi-grade tuna, hamachi, kingfish, or snapper Any sashimi-grade firm white-or-pink ocean fish works. Avoid soft-fleshed fish (cod, halibut) which break down too fast in the cure. Avoid salmon (the fat content reads off in kinilaw, and the cultural context is not Filipino).
  • cane vinegar (sukang iloko) → apple cider vinegar The closest globally-available substitute. Slightly sharper and less sweet than cane vinegar; reduce to 100 g and add 5 g / 1 tsp sugar to compensate.
  • calamansi juice → lime juice + orange juice (3:1) Approximates the floral-mandarin note of calamansi. Use 22 g lime plus 8 g orange juice for the 30 g total.

Notes

Why brief and aggressive vs slow and gentle. The structural identity of kinilaw is raw-but-cured-surface; the fish interior must remain translucent and tender while the exterior firms enough to read cooked. This only happens with a short, aggressive cure (5 to 10 minutes in concentrated vinegar). The longer, gentler citrus cures of Mexican or Peruvian ceviche produce a fully-firm, opaque-throughout fish, which is a structurally different dish. Kinilaw drained at 5 minutes reads as itself; kinilaw left in the cure for 30 minutes reads as a clumsy ceviche.

Why cane vinegar specifically. Cane vinegar (sukang iloko, sukang maasim, or sukang tuba) is fermented from sugarcane juice and has a particular sweet-floral undertone that other vinegars lack. White distilled vinegar is too sharp and one-noted; rice vinegar is too mild and Asian-leaning; balsamic is structurally wrong. Apple cider vinegar is the closest non-Filipino substitute (similar fruit-vinegar profile); use it before reaching for distilled vinegar. The vinegar choice is the difference between recognizably-Filipino kinilaw and a generic vinegar-cured fish dish.

Why ginger is structural. The ginger does two things: contributes piney aromatic brightness, and helps neutralize the metallic-bloodline notes that some oily fish carry. Without ginger the kinilaw reads flat and slightly off; with ginger it reads clean and bright. The julienned cut is important, finely-minced ginger blends into the cure and disappears; matchsticks remain distinct and contribute textural-aromatic moments throughout the dish.

Why drain (not just toss). Leaving the cure liquid with the finished fish would over-cure during the time it sits on the table. Draining at the 5-to-7-minute mark stops the cure precisely; the small amount of cure liquid that stays adhered to the fish (and that draws out during the rest with the onion-cucumber) is enough for the finishing flavor. The drained liquid (kilawin sauce) can be reserved for dipping or for drinking from a small cup (some Filipinos do this).

Why red onion goes in raw, after the cure. The structural crunch of raw red onion is part of the kinilaw textural architecture. Adding the onion to the cure produces softened, sour-pickled onion, which is a different mouthfeel and a different flavor. The raw onion contributes sharp allium bite and visible crunch; this is what makes kinilaw read as fresh and bright rather than soft and uniform.

Safety. Kinilaw is raw fish; the vinegar cure denatures surface proteins but does not kill parasites or significantly reduce bacterial load. Source sashimi-grade fish that has been flash-frozen to at least -20°C / -4°F for 24 hours (or -35°C / -31°F for 15 hours), which kills parasites per FDA and EU guidelines. Use within 24 hours of thaw. Pregnant or immune-compromised diners should avoid raw fish dishes; the cure does not make kinilaw safe in the way cooking would.

How it is eaten. Kinilaw is pulutan, drinking food, traditionally served with cold beer or local rum on Filipino coastal afternoons. It is a starter, not a main course; portion small, eat with a spoon, savor the textural moment of cool-firm-cured fish with raw-crunch onion and ginger heat. A full-meal serving with rice (kinilaw as ulam) is also legitimate but less canonical; the appetizer presentation is the dominant Filipino eating context.

Sources
  • Adapted from Visayan and Mindanao Filipino canon — Kinilaw is one of the oldest documented Filipino food preparations, predating Spanish colonization by at least 1000 years. Archaeological evidence places fish-vinegar curing on the Visayan and Mindanao coasts as early as 1000 CE. The technique is distinct from ceviche (Spanish-Mexican-Peruvian, citrus-based, longer cures) in three ways: the cure is vinegar-based (not citrus), the cure is brief (5 to 10 minutes vs 30 to overnight), and the structural texture is raw-with-firm-surface rather than fully-firm-throughout. Modern recipes often add coconut milk (kinilaw na isda sa gata, a Bicolano-Cebuano variant); this version is the unadorned tanigue canonical.
