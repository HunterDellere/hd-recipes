Chicken satay with peanut sauce

Indonesian-Malaysian skewers of marinated chicken thigh grilled fast over high heat for char and smoke, served with a peanut sauce built on roasted peanuts, fried bumbu, tamarind, palm sugar, and a thread of kecap manis. The marinade browns aggressively from the kecap manis sugar; the sauce balances roasted-peanut richness against acid and chili.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Marinate the chicken 2 hours minimum, 6 hours optimal. The kecap manis sugar needs time to penetrate the meat for both flavor depth and the lacquered char on the grill. Under 1 hour and the surface tastes coated but the interior is bland.
  • Soak bamboo skewers in cold water 30 minutes before threading. Dry bamboo burns through over a hot grill in 3 to 4 minutes and the skewers collapse before the chicken finishes cooking.
  • The peanut sauce is built in the same pan as a small bumbu fry. Build the sauce while the chicken marinates, it improves on standing as the peanuts hydrate and the flavors integrate. Hold at room temperature; refrigeration sets it too thick and the texture goes pasty.
  • Grill timing is fast and unforgiving. Have the rice, sambal, sliced cucumber, and any garnish plated before the chicken hits the grates. Each skewer cooks in 4 to 6 minutes total and demands constant attention.

  5. 300 ml water (hot)

Phase 4, serving

  1. 200 g sliced cucumber, sliced into 5 mm rounds — (1 English cucumber; the cooling foil to the grilled satay and rich peanut sauce)

Execution

  1. 1
    Marinate the chicken. Whisk together the 45 g / 3 tbsp kecap manis, 15 g / 1 tbsp light soy, 15 g garlic, 10 g ginger, 4 g / 1½ tsp coriander, 2 g / ¾ tsp cumin, 2 g / ¾ tsp turmeric, 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil, and 15 g / 1 tbsp lime juice in a large bowl. Add the 700 g cubed chicken thigh, toss aggressively to coat every piece, and refrigerate covered for 2 to 6 hours. Soak the bamboo skewers in cold water during the same window.
  2. 2
    Build the peanut sauce. Pound or blend the 60 g shallot, 15 g garlic, 15 g chili, and 3 g toasted terasi to a coarse wet paste. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a saucepan over medium heat. Add the paste and fry, stirring constantly, until the raw smell shifts to a deeper aromatic and the paste darkens, about 4 minutes. The oil should visibly separate at the edges.
  3. 3
    Add the 200 g coarse-ground peanuts to the bumbu. Toss to coat in the spiced oil; the peanuts will sizzle and toast slightly more in the residual fat for 60 seconds. Then add the 15 g / 1 tbsp tamarind paste, 20 g / 1½ tbsp palm sugar, 30 g / 2 tbsp kecap manis, and 300 ml / 1¼ cups hot water. Stir to combine. Reduce heat to low and simmer 8 to 10 minutes, stirring frequently, until the sauce thickens to a pourable but coating consistency (it should fall off a spoon in a thick ribbon, not a stream). The peanuts hydrate and the starches release from the ground peanut to body the sauce.
  4. 4
    Taste the sauce, adjust with the 3 g / ½ tsp kosher salt and adjust acid (more tamarind if flat, more palm sugar if too sharp). Hold at room temperature until serving. The sauce will tighten on standing; thin with hot water (1 tbsp at a time) before serving if needed.
  5. 5
    Thread the marinated chicken onto soaked skewers, 4 to 5 cubes per skewer, pressed close together (close-packed chicken produces a juicier center, spaced-out chicken dries out faster). About 16 skewers from 700 g chicken.
  6. 6
    Build a hot fire. Charcoal grill (the canonical method) over hardwood charcoal, heated until coals are glowing white with a thin gray ash and you cannot hold your hand 10 cm above the grate for more than 2 seconds. Gas grill on high heat (260°C / 500°F) is acceptable. Cast iron grill pan on a hot stove works for a smaller batch. The high heat is non-negotiable, low and slow produces dried-out chicken before the marinade browns.
  7. 7
    Grill the satay 90 seconds on the first side, flip with tongs, brush the cooked side with a thin coat of additional kecap manis (about 30 g / 2 tbsp total across all skewers), grill 90 seconds. Flip again, brush the second side, grill 60 seconds. Flip a final time, brush, grill 30 to 60 seconds until the surface is deeply lacquered, slightly charred at the corners, and the chicken interior is fully cooked (75°C / 165°F internal). Total grill time is 4 to 6 minutes per skewer; the constant flip-and-brush builds layers of caramelized kecap manis that produce the canonical satay surface.
  8. 8
    Plate immediately. Arrange 4 skewers per portion on the plate. Pour a generous pool of peanut sauce alongside (or in a small ramekin for dipping). Add a small pile of cucumber slices for cooling foil and a mound of jasmine rice. Scatter fried shallots over the peanut sauce. Serve with extra sambal on the side. Diners pull each skewer through the peanut sauce, eat the chicken off the stick with bites of cucumber and rice between.
Safety
  • Lamb satay modification: pulling lamb cubes at 60°C / 140°F internal for medium-rare Whole-muscle lamb (not ground) is safely served medium-rare at 145°F internal per USDA; the 140°F pull plus 3 to 5 minutes rest carries the meat to roughly 145°F. The interior of an uncut lamb shoulder cube is essentially sterile; surface searing handles the contamination layer. Vulnerable diners should be served lamb cooked to 145°F internal with no carryover credit. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→sate kambing (lamb satay, Indonesian street classic)

    Substitute 700 g lamb shoulder, cut into 2.5 cm cubes, for the chicken. Marinate the same way but extend to 6 to 8 hours; lamb takes longer to absorb the marinade. Grill 5 to 7 minutes total (lamb is denser); pull at 60°C / 140°F internal for medium-rare. Sate kambing is more common in Indonesia than chicken satay and is the late-night Jakarta street-food canonical.

  • Regional protein→sate maranggi (Sundanese beef satay, West Java)

    Substitute 700 g beef sirloin or tenderloin, cut into 2 cm cubes. The Sundanese version uses a marinade of kecap manis, palm sugar, garlic, ginger, ketumbar (coriander seed), and a small amount of pineapple juice (the bromelain tenderizes the lean cut). Serve with a sambal of chopped tomato, shallot, and chili (not peanut sauce). The maranggi style produces a sweeter-front, less peanut-driven satay.

  • Regional cuisine→satay celup (Malaysian Malacca lok-lok style)

    Skip the grill entirely. Instead, build a large pot of the peanut sauce (double the recipe) and bring to a gentle simmer at the table over a portable burner. Skewer raw chicken, vegetables, tofu, and other items; diners cook each skewer by dipping into the simmering peanut sauce for 2 to 3 minutes. The Malacca version (satay celup) is a hot-pot interpretation of satay; the sauce is the cooking medium and the dish.

  • Regional cuisine→satay padang (West Sumatran, yellow-rice-flour sauce)

    Skip the peanut sauce entirely. Build a yellow sauce instead: spice paste of turmeric, ginger, garlic, shallot, chili, lemongrass, fried in oil 5 minutes, then add 400 ml beef stock and 30 g rice flour slurried in cold water. Simmer until thickened to a velvety yellow-orange sauce; pour over the grilled satay. Satay padang is the West Sumatran take, no peanuts at all; the sauce is the defining feature.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • roasted unsalted peanuts → natural peanut butter (Skippy Natural or equivalent) Skip the grinding step; substitute 180 g natural peanut butter for the 200 g ground peanuts. The texture is smoother (more spreadable than slightly chunky), the flavor is close. Avoid sweetened or hydrogenated peanut butters, the sugar and oil profile is wrong.
  • chicken thigh → boneless skinless chicken breast Workable but produces a drier finished satay; the breast lacks the intramuscular fat that keeps thigh juicy under hard grill heat. If using breast, brine 30 minutes in 5 percent salt water before marinating, and pull from the grill at 70°C / 158°F internal (vs 75°C for thigh).
  • tamarind paste → lime juice plus 1 tsp brown sugar Tamarind has a deeper, more raisin-prune sour profile that lime does not replicate. Acceptable in a pinch; the peanut sauce reads brighter and less complex.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → tamarind paste make it yourselfstub Whole-tamarind-pod paste is dramatically brighter and more complex than the jarred concentrate. Takes 20 minutes and yields a month of sauce work.
  • → fried shallots (crispy) make it yourself Store-bought crispy shallots taste stale within weeks. Five-minute homemade version is dramatically sweeter and aromatic.

Notes

Why marinate at least 2 hours. The kecap manis is high-sugar and high-viscosity; it coats the surface of the chicken immediately but takes time to migrate into the muscle. Under 1 hour of marinade and the surface tastes coated while the interior reads bland; at 2 to 6 hours the sugar and soy salt have penetrated 3 to 5 mm into the meat, which is exactly the depth that the high-heat grill caramelizes during the brief 4-to-6-minute cook. Past 8 hours and the sugar starts to denature the surface proteins (a mild cure), producing a slightly mushy texture; do not marinate overnight unless reducing the kecap manis to half.

Why brush-and-flip during the grill. Each brush of kecap manis adds another thin layer of high-sugar liquid that caramelizes on the chicken surface in 30 to 60 seconds at hard grill temperatures (260°C and above). Four flips with brushing produces four distinct layers of lacquered surface, each progressively darker and more deeply flavored, which is the structural texture of canonical Indonesian satay. A single-pass grill (one flip, no brushing) produces a flat-tasting surface that is technically cooked but missing the layered char that defines the dish.

Why fry the bumbu before adding peanuts. The peanut sauce is structurally a bumbu (fried spice paste) with peanut as the body. Frying the shallot-garlic-chili-terasi paste first develops the same Maillard depth that any Indonesian curry depends on; adding the peanuts to the fried bumbu then frying them an extra 60 seconds blooms the peanut oil into the spiced fat and gives the sauce its deep, savory-front character. Skipping the bumbu fry and simply blending raw aromatics with peanut butter produces a flat, one-dimensional sauce, the difference between a real Indonesian satay sauce and the supermarket-jar version.

Why coarse-ground peanuts (not smooth). The slight visible texture of the ground peanut is the canonical sauce signature; smooth peanut butter sauces lack the toothful body that distinguishes Indonesian satay sauce from Thai satay sauce (which is smooth and curry-paste-driven). Pulse the peanuts in 2 to 3 second bursts until they look like coarse breadcrumbs with some larger pieces; do not process to smooth.

Why high-heat charcoal grill specifically. The kecap manis surface needs aggressive direct heat to caramelize the sugar without overcooking the chicken interior. A low-heat grill produces a dry, leathery satay because the chicken finishes cooking before the surface lacquers; gas grills work but lack the smoky aromatic that hardwood charcoal contributes (and that is part of the canonical satay flavor). If you only have a stovetop, a cast iron grill pan on the hottest burner setting works for small batches.

The peanut sauce holds and improves on standing. The flavors integrate over the first 2 hours at room temperature, and the texture sets to its final coating consistency. Refrigeration tightens the sauce too far (the peanut fat solidifies); if making ahead, refrigerate in a jar, then warm gently with a splash of hot water before serving. Lasts 5 days in the fridge.

Sources
  • Adapted from Indonesian-Malaysian street-food canon (satay ayam / sate ayam) — Satay exists across Southeast Asia as a family of skewered grilled meats. The Indonesian-Malaysian version (the one most non-Indonesians encounter) is built on kecap manis as the marinade base and a peanut sauce as the dipping sauce.
