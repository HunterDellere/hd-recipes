Chicken shawarma

The vertical-spit kebab reformatted for the home oven: boneless chicken thighs marinated 4 to 24 hours in a deep seven-spice blend (cumin, coriander, allspice, cardamom, paprika, cinnamon, turmeric), garlic, lemon, and yogurt, then layered on a sheet pan or roasted vertically on a skewered stack and finished under the broiler for the crisp edges that define shawarma. Sliced thin and served in warm pita with tahini-yogurt sauce, sliced tomato, cucumber, red onion, parsley, and toum (Lebanese garlic sauce) or pickled turnips. The structural defining feature is the seven-spice blend; the structural cooking feature is the layered roast that approximates the vertical spit.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • The marinade is a minimum 4-hour, ideally overnight, soak. Yogurt enzymes tenderize the chicken and the spices have time to penetrate; under 4 hours the surface flavors but the interior reads under-spiced.
  • For the canonical crisp-edge shawarma texture, the chicken is roasted on a sheet pan, then broiled briefly at the end; the broil is structural for the slightly charred edges that mimic the vertical spit. Some home cooks improvise a vertical spit by skewering the marinated chicken stacked on a single tall metal skewer in a cast-iron skillet and roasting upright; both methods produce excellent results.
  • Make the tahini-yogurt sauce (see recipes/tahini-yogurt-sauce) and warm the pita while the chicken broils. The assembly is fast once everything is at the table; the wrap is built and eaten within 90 seconds.
  • The seven-spice blend here is built from individual ground spices for accuracy. Pre-mixed "shawarma seasoning" varies dramatically by brand and is often heavy on paprika at the expense of the structural cumin, allspice, and cardamom.

Execution

  1. 1
    Make the marinade: in a large bowl, whisk together the 4 g cumin, 3 g coriander, 3 g allspice, 2 g cardamom, 3 g paprika, 2 g cinnamon, 2 g turmeric, 3 g black pepper, and 14 g salt. Add the 100 g / scant ½ cup yogurt, 45 g / 3 tbsp olive oil, 30 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice, and 16 g / 4 grated garlic cloves. Whisk to combine. The marinade should be a deep orange-amber paste, thick enough to cling to the chicken.
  2. 2
    Add the 900 g chicken thighs (butterflied or halved); turn to coat every piece thoroughly. Cover and refrigerate 4 to 24 hours (8 to 12 hours is the structural sweet spot).
  3. 3
    When ready to cook, preheat the oven to 220°C / 425°F. Brush a heavy sheet pan with the 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil. Arrange the marinated chicken in a single layer on the sheet pan, slightly overlapping is fine but do not pile (overlapping creates the structural layered shawarma look as the edges crisp differently from the centers).
  4. 4
    Roast 18 to 22 minutes, until the chicken is cooked through (internal temperature 71°C / 160°F at the thickest point) and the marinade has caramelized on the surface. The chicken should look deep amber-brown on top with darker char marks at the thinner edges.
  5. 5
    Turn the broiler to high. Move the sheet pan to the top rack, about 10 cm from the heating element. Broil 3 to 5 minutes, watching closely, until the edges are crisped and slightly charred and the surface is glossy with caramelized marinade. The broil is the structural step that mimics the vertical spit; do not skip it.
  6. 6
    Pull the chicken from the oven; let rest 5 minutes on the sheet pan (covered loosely with foil) to redistribute the juices.
  7. 7
    Slice the chicken thin (3 to 4 mm slices against the grain). Pile the slices on a serving platter with all the crisp edges showing. While the chicken rests, warm the 400 g pita briefly in the oven (the chicken is already out so the oven is empty and still hot; 2 minutes is plenty).
  8. 8
    Assemble each wrap: spread a generous 50 g of tahini-yogurt sauce across the warm pita. Pile 150 g of sliced chicken down the center. Add 2 slices tomato, 2 slices cucumber, a small handful of red onion (sprinkled with sumac), and a generous pinch of parsley leaves. Roll the pita tightly: tuck one end inward, then roll the long edge across the filling, tucking firmly as you go. Wrap the bottom half in parchment paper if eating on the go (the paper holds the wrap closed and keeps the bottom from getting drenched). Serve immediately; the wrap is built and eaten within 90 seconds. Alternatively, plate the chicken with the sauce and vegetables alongside as a shawarma platter, eaten with the warmed pita on the side.
Safety
  • Pulling chicken at 71°C / 160°F internal (just past the carryover threshold to a final 165°F) Pulled at 160°F internal, then rested for 3 minutes covered, the meat carries to roughly 165°F (USDA published-safe instantaneous target). Resting under foil drives the carryover; pulling at 165°F internal directly produces dry, chalky chicken. Vulnerable diners (pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, very old) should be served chicken cooked to 165°F internal with no carryover credit. Reference →

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion vertical-skewer roast (closest to the spit texture)→as a home vertical-spit shawarma

    Skewer the marinated chicken thighs vertically onto a single tall metal skewer (heavy 12-inch metal skewer or two stacked) standing upright in a cast-iron skillet (the skewer base wedged into a half potato or a small onion to keep it upright). Roast at 220°C / 425°F for 35 to 40 minutes, basting the visible edges every 10 minutes with rendered juices from the skillet bottom. Slice the chicken thin off the vertical stack with a sharp knife, working from top to bottom; the slices fall onto the skillet bottom and bathe in the juices. The result is structurally the closest home approximation to vertical-spit shawarma and is dramatically more crisp-edged than the sheet-pan method.

  • Protein lamb shawarma (the older traditional form)→with lamb shoulder instead of chicken

    Substitute 900 g of thin-sliced lamb shoulder or boneless lamb leg (3 to 4 mm slices, against the grain) for the chicken thighs. Increase the marinade time to 12 to 24 hours; lamb benefits from longer enzyme exposure. Roast at the same temperature for 25 to 30 minutes; broil 3 to 5 minutes for char. The dish reads more aromatic, more savory, and structurally closer to the original Ottoman shawarma. Lamb shawarma is the oldest and arguably most canonical form.

  • Occasion shawarma plate (not wrap)→as a shawarma plate over rice

    Skip the pita and serve the sliced chicken over Lebanese-style rice (basmati cooked with butter, vermicelli, and a pinch of cinnamon), with the tahini-yogurt sauce drizzled across, a side of toum (Lebanese garlic sauce), pickled turnips, and a small chopped salad of tomato, cucumber, parsley, and onion dressed with lemon-and-olive-oil. The plate format is the canonical sit-down service in Lebanese restaurants; the wrap is the street-food format.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • boneless skinless chicken thigh → boneless skinless chicken breast (with adjustments) Substitute 900 g chicken breast, sliced into 1 cm thick cutlets. Reduce the roast to 12 to 15 minutes and skip the broil (or broil very briefly, 2 minutes max); breast dries out quickly. The dish reads cleaner and leaner but less aromatic than the thigh version. Thigh is structurally the better choice; breast is the leaner-eater fallback.
  • ground allspice → ½ tsp ground cloves plus ½ tsp ground cinnamon A fallback only; allspice is structural for the Lebanese seven-spice profile and the substitution reads close but not identical. Worth seeking out ground allspice; it is also useful in many other Levantine preparations.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → tahini-yogurt sauce (or pure tahini sauce / tarator) make it yourself Home-made tahini-yogurt sauce is dramatically fresher and more lemon-forward than any bottled equivalent; the sauce is structural for the wrap.
  • → Lebanese pita or large flatbreads make it yourself Home-baked Lebanese pita that just came out of the oven (or 30 minutes ago) is the canonical bread base for shawarma; the difference between fresh pita and supermarket pita is meaningful.
  • → whole-milk plain yogurt make it yourselfstub Home-cultured yogurt has a brighter tang and stronger tenderizing action in the marinade.

Notes

Why seven-spice. The Lebanese baharat seven-spice blend (cumin, coriander, allspice, cardamom, paprika, cinnamon, turmeric, with variations) is what distinguishes Levantine shawarma from Turkish döner (which uses fewer spices, often just cumin and paprika) or Greek gyro (which uses oregano, thyme, and rosemary). The two structural Lebanese signatures are allspice and cardamom; without them, the blend reads as a different cuisine. Many recipes call for "shawarma seasoning" from a jar but supermarket blends vary wildly and often skimp on the structural spices in favor of cheaper paprika and salt; building from individual ground spices is worth the extra 2 minutes of measuring.

Why the broil is structural. The defining textural feature of shawarma is the crisp slightly-charred edges produced by the vertical spit (where the meat surface is constantly exposed to the heat source and the crisped outer layer is sliced off continuously). The home sheet-pan version cannot reproduce this continuous-crisp effect, but the broil step at the end approximates it by hitting the cooked chicken with direct high heat for the final 3 to 5 minutes; the marinade surface caramelizes deeply, the edges crisp, and the dish develops the structural shawarma texture. Skip the broil and the chicken reads as marinated baked chicken rather than as shawarma.

Versatility. The marinade transfers cleanly to: lamb cubes for grilled shawarma kebabs (skewered and grilled), shrimp shawarma (cut marinade time to 30 minutes; grill or roast 4 minutes), pork shawarma (in non-Muslim or non-Jewish contexts; cut from the pork shoulder, marinate 12 hours, roast same way). The seven-spice blend is the structural base for many Levantine preparations and can be made in larger batches and stored 6 months in an airtight jar.

Pairs across Lebanese (canonical), Palestinian, Syrian, Jordanian, Israeli (where shawarma is one of the most ubiquitous street foods), Egyptian, and broader Levantine diaspora cookery globally. Chicken shawarma is one of the most internationally exported Middle Eastern dishes and appears in food courts, casual restaurants, and home kitchens worldwide.

Storage. Marinated raw chicken keeps 48 hours refrigerated. Cooked sliced chicken keeps 4 days refrigerated; reheat briefly under the broiler (2 minutes) to restore the crisp edges, or eat cold in a wrap. The assembled wrap does not store well (the pita gets soggy and the vegetables release water); assemble at service from prepped components.

Sources
  • The Levantine street-food canon. Shawarma is documented across the eastern Mediterranean since at least the Ottoman period and is closely related to Turkish döner and Greek gyro (all three share the vertical spit and the thin-slice technique). — Two structural splits: (1) the meat. Chicken shawarma (the version here) is the most common modern shawarma globally; lamb shawarma is the older traditional form; beef shawarma is more common in some regional variants. (2) The cooking method. Commercial shawarma uses a vertical spit (the meat layered onto a vertical metal stake and rotated against a heat source); the home version reformats this as either a sheet-pan roast (the most common) or an improvised vertical-skewer roast (which gets closer to the spit texture). The version here is the sheet-pan-and-broil method; the vertical-skewer method is in modifications.
