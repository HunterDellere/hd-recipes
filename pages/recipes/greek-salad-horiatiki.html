Horiatiki (village Greek salad)

The canonical village salad of Greece: ripe tomato wedges, thick cucumber half-moons, sliced red onion, kalamata olives, green bell pepper, capers, and a single thick slab of real PDO sheep-milk feta laid whole on top, dressed with peak-quality Greek extra-virgin olive oil and dried Greek rigani (no vinegar, no lettuce, no crumbled cheese). Structurally a study in restraint: peak summer vegetables, real ingredients (real feta, real Greek oregano, real Greek olive oil), and the canonical Greek table-service form where the slab of feta sits intact on top of the salad and is broken apart with the diner's fork. The most commonly bastardized Greek dish in international cooking, and the simplest to do correctly.

The vegetables

  1. 500 g ripe summer tomatoes (heirloom, beefsteak, or vine-ripened, NOT winter tomatoes), cored, cut into 2 to 3 cm thick wedges (a medium beefsteak yields about 8 wedges) — (2 large or 3 medium tomatoes; the dish does not exist without good tomatoes; in winter, make a different salad)
  2. 300 g Persian or Greek cucumber (thin-skinned, low-seed), halved lengthwise, cut into 1 cm thick half-moons (do not peel, the thin Persian skin is structural; thick American slicing-cucumber skin should be peeled and the seeds scraped out) — (2 Persian cucumbers or 1 small English cucumber)
  3. 100 g red onion, halved through the root, sliced 3 mm thick into half-rings — (½ medium red onion; do not substitute yellow onion, the dish reads off-balance)
  4. 100 g green bell pepper, cored, seeded, sliced into 1 cm wide rings or strips — (1 small green bell pepper; the structural canonical Greek choice, do not substitute red or yellow; the slight bitterness of green pepper is part of the flavor balance)

The dressing and cheese

  1. 100 g kalamata olives (whole, with pits)
  2. 15 g capers, drained, drained, rinsed if salt-packed
  3. 200 g real PDO Greek feta cheese (sheep-milk or sheep-and-goat blend; in a thick rectangular slab), kept as a single thick rectangular slab, drained briefly on paper towels (do not crumble; the slab service is structural)
  4. 60 g extra-virgin Greek olive oil (peak quality)
  5. 2 g dried Greek oregano (rigani)
  6. 2 g fine sea salt
  7. 1 g black pepper, freshly cracked (optional)

Execution

  1. 1
    Toss the 500 g / 2 to 3 tomatoes (wedged) and 300 g / 2 cucumbers (half-mooned) together in a wide shallow serving bowl or platter (about 25 cm / 10 in diameter; the salad is served spread out, not mounded). Lightly salt with about 1 g / a pinch of the salt; the salt draws out a small amount of the tomato juices which become part of the dressing.
  2. 2
    Add the 100 g / ½ red onion (sliced) and 100 g / 1 green bell pepper (sliced) to the bowl. Toss gently with hands or large serving forks (do not over-stir; the tomato wedges should remain intact). Distribute the 100 g / ½ cup kalamata olives (whole) and the 15 g / 2 tbsp capers over the top.
  3. 3
    Place the 200 g / 7 oz slab of feta cheese whole on top of the salad in the center, like a thick rectangular tile resting on the vegetables. Do not crumble the feta into the salad; the slab service is structural and is how horiatiki is served across Greece. The diner breaks off pieces with the fork at the table.
  4. 4
    Drizzle the 60 g / ¼ cup olive oil evenly over the feta and the surrounding vegetables in a slow steady stream; the feta should be visibly oiled across its top surface and the oil should pool slightly in the bowl below.
  5. 5
    Rub the 2 g / 1½ tsp dried rigani between the palms of your hands directly over the salad (the friction releases the aromatic oils in the dried leaves; sprinkling without rubbing produces a much less aromatic finish). The rigani should fall in a generous shower over the top of the feta and the vegetables. Add the remaining 1 g / a small pinch of salt and the optional 1 g / ½ tsp black pepper.
  6. 6
    Serve immediately at room temperature with thick slices of crusty country bread (a sliced baguette, ciabatta, or country sourdough) on the side. The bread is structural: diners tear off pieces and use them to mop up the oil-and-tomato-juice pool that forms at the bottom of the bowl, often eating the soaked bread as the final and best bite of the meal.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional regional pivot→as a Cretan dakos (the Crete bread-base variant)

    Replace the cucumber and bell pepper with 200 g of crisp barley rusks (paximadia, the Greek twice-baked bread). Crush the rusks briefly and arrange in the bottom of the bowl; top with the tomato wedges, kalamata olives, and crumbled feta (Cretan style does crumble the feta over the rusks). Drizzle with olive oil and rigani. Let stand 5 minutes for the rusks to absorb the tomato juices and olive oil. The result is dakos, the canonical Cretan summer salad; the structural sibling of horiatiki served on a bread-rusk base.

  • Occasion occasion→as a meze platter component

    Divide the salad into 4 small bowls (about 200 g per bowl), placing a small chunk of feta on each. Serve as part of a larger Greek meze spread alongside tzatziki, hummus, taramasalata, dolmades, grilled octopus, and saganaki. The smaller portion size and the multi-dish context shifts the dish from a primary side to a single element in a tapas-style meal.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • real PDO Greek feta cheese (sheep-milk or sheep-and-goat blend; in a thick rectangular slab) → Bulgarian feta or French Valbreso feta (also sheep-milk) Real Greek PDO feta is the canonical choice and is structurally non-negotiable; the sheep-milk character (tangy, slightly funky, creamy-crumbly) is the entire flavor of the cheese. Bulgarian feta is sheep-milk and similar in profile; French Valbreso feta is sheep-milk and creamier. Do not substitute domestic cow-milk "feta-style" cheese (Athenos, Trader Joe's Athenos, generic supermarket "feta"); these are a structurally different product and the dish reads as a different salad entirely.
  • ripe summer tomatoes (heirloom, beefsteak, or vine-ripened, NOT winter tomatoes) → high-quality canned San Marzano tomatoes drained and roughly chopped Peak ripe summer tomatoes are the structural requirement; the dish does not exist without good tomatoes. In winter when good fresh tomatoes are unavailable, the canonical Greek response is to make a different salad (a cabbage-and-carrot salad, a beetroot salad, a fennel-and-orange salad). If you must approximate horiatiki out of tomato season, drained canned San Marzano tomatoes are an emergency workaround but the result reads notably worse than the seasonal version.

Notes

Horiatiki has no lettuce. This is the single most violated rule in international Greek-salad cookery, and the single most important rule to honor for a structurally correct dish. Adding romaine, iceberg, mesclun, or any leafy green transforms horiatiki into a different American restaurant salad commonly labeled "Greek salad" but which has no Greek precedent. The vegetable substrate of horiatiki is tomato and cucumber; the leafy elements that exist are the leaves on the dried rigani.

Horiatiki has no vinegar. Greek summer salad dressing is olive oil and rigani only; the acidity in the dish comes from the tomato juices, the kalamata olive brine, and the brine cured into the feta. Vinegar is structurally a Western addition and produces a sharper, more acidic dressing that conflicts with the salty feta and brined olives. A correctly dressed horiatiki tastes oily, salty, herbal, and tomato-sweet, not vinegary.

The feta serves whole, not crumbled. This is the canonical Greek table-service form: a thick rectangular slab of feta laid intact on top of the salad. The diner breaks off pieces with the fork at the table, combining each bite of feta with a bite of vegetables. Crumbled feta distributes the cheese across the salad and dilutes the impact of each bite; the slab service concentrates the cheese into a structural presence and lets the diner control the cheese-to-vegetable ratio.

Real Greek feta is essential and is increasingly available in the US. Real Greek PDO feta is sheep-milk (or sheep-and-goat blend) cheese brined in seawater; the texture is creamy-crumbly and the flavor is tangy, slightly funky, and richer than any cow-milk imitation. Domestic "feta-style" cheese sold in plastic tubs (Athenos, generic supermarket brands) is cow-milk cheese pressed and salted, structurally different from real feta. The price difference is meaningful (real PDO feta runs $10 to $14 per pound, cow-milk imitation runs $4 to $6), but for a dish whose entire structural complexity rests on three ingredients (tomato, olive oil, feta), the real cheese is the dish.

The dish does not exist out of tomato season. Peak ripe summer tomatoes (late June through September in the Northeast US, all of summer in California and the Mediterranean) are the structural requirement; winter tomatoes (the pale pink-green ones shipped from Mexico or Florida) produce a dramatically inferior salad that should not be served as horiatiki. Cooks craving horiatiki in winter should make a different salad and wait for July; the seasonality is part of the dish.

Storage. Does not store; eat within 30 minutes of dressing. The salt and olive oil pull moisture from the tomato and cucumber; after 30 minutes the vegetables release water and the salad turns watery. If you need to prep ahead, prep the vegetables (cut tomatoes, cut cucumber, slice onion, slice pepper) and refrigerate separately; combine, dress, and add feta at service.

  • Greek canon (the village salad, served across Greece and Cyprus) — Horiatiki (literally "village salad" from the word for village, χωριό) is the canonical Greek summer salad, served in every taverna and home across Greece from June to September. The structural rules are non-negotiable and the dish reads wrong if violated: NO LETTUCE (the addition of any leafy green, especially romaine or iceberg, makes the dish an "American Greek salad" and is not horiatiki), peak summer ripe tomatoes (not winter tomatoes; the dish does not exist without good tomatoes), thick cucumber slices (not the thin English slicing-cucumber rounds; Greek and Persian cucumbers cut into 1 cm half-moons), sliced red onion (not yellow), kalamata olives whole with pits (not pitted, the pits keep the brine intact in the olive), green bell pepper (a structural ingredient that is often skipped outside Greece but is canonical), capers (often added in northern Greece and the islands), and the canonical feta cheese service form: a thick rectangular slab of feta laid whole on top of the dressed salad, not crumbled.
