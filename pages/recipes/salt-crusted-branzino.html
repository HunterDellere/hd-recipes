Salt-crusted branzino

A whole gutted branzino sealed in an egg-white-bound coarse-salt shell and oven-baked until the crust hardens into a rigid clay case that steams the slim fish edge to spine in its own moisture. The salt-shell insulates and seals rather than salts; the intact skin and scales block migration, so the flesh comes out gently seasoned, not briny. Cracked open at the table, the fillets lift clean off the bone and get dressed with a warm salmoriglio, the southern-Italian oregano-lemon-oil emulsion carrying melted anchovy for umami and a sharp lemon edge for acid.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Three conditions must be met before the crust goes on, and all three are non-negotiable. Whip the egg whites to firm peaks so the coarse salt packs into a cohesive, moldable shell rather than a loose pile that sloughs and vents. Pat the branzino bone-dry inside and out, since surface water keeps the crust from sealing against the skin and lets the steam the technique depends on leak away. And bring the oven fully to 220 C / 425 F before the fish goes in, because a crust baked up from a cold start softens and slumps before it can harden into a rigid case.
  • Leave the fish whole and unscaled, gutted only. The intact skin and scales are the barrier that stops the crust from over-salting the flesh; a scaled branzino salt-baked in this crust comes out aggressively salty. Ask the counter to gut the fish and pull the gills but to leave it unscaled, and verify both before you leave.
  • Build the salmoriglio while the fish bakes, not before. The emulsion is whisked warm and used within a few minutes; the oregano and garlic go bitter and the lemon dulls if it sits. Have a serving platter, a heavy spoon or knife handle to crack the shell, and a dry pastry brush staged before the fish goes in, because the crust is cracked and brushed clean at the table while the fish is hot.

Mise en place by phase 3 phases ▾

Phase 1, the fish and cavity aromatics

  1. 550 g whole branzino, gutted, gills removed, left unscaled, rinsed inside and out, blood line scraped from the spine cavity with a small spoon, patted completely dry inside and out with paper towels; left unscaled so the crust does not over-salt the flesh
  2. 60 g lemon, half zested in wide strips with a peeler for the crust, that half then sliced into thin rounds for the cavity; the other half reserved for the salmoriglio and the table — (1 lemon; zest and rounds for the fish, the rest for the finish)
  3. 4 g fresh thyme, half the sprigs whole for the cavity, the leaves stripped from the rest for the crust — (about 6 sprigs)
  4. 9 g garlic cloves, 2 cloves, peeled and lightly smashed, left whole, for the cavity — (2 cloves)

Phase 2, the salt crust

  1. 1200 g coarse sea salt, coarse or medium-grain gray or white sea salt, not fine table salt and not flaky finishing salt; the large interlocking crystal is what builds a rigid shell
  2. 70 g egg whites, from about 2 large eggs, whipped to firm peaks; the binder that turns loose salt into a cohesive, moldable crust — (whites of 2 large eggs)
  3. 3 g fennel seed, lightly crushed, worked into the salt for aromatic lift through the crust
  4. 20 g water, cold

Phase 3, the warm salmoriglio

  1. 55 g extra-virgin olive oil
  2. 6 g anchovy fillets, 2 oil-packed fillets, blotted, melted into the warm oil as the umami anchor — (2 fillets; they dissolve invisibly and supply glutamate)
  3. 2 g dried oregano, preferably Greek or Sicilian rigani, crumbled between the fingers; the defining herb of salmoriglio
  4. 4 g garlic clove, for the sauce, 1 clove, grated to a paste on a microplane — (1 clove)
  5. 8 g capers, salt-packed if possible, rinsed and roughly chopped, for a briny savory pop
  6. 6 g flat-leaf parsley, leaves finely chopped, stirred in off heat
  7. 20 g lemon juice, for the sauce, from the reserved lemon half, whisked in warm to emulsify
  8. 1 g flaky finishing salt, optional — (a pinch, only if a portion tastes underseasoned at the plate)

Execution

  1. 1
    Heat the oven to 220 C / 425 F with a rack in the center and let it come fully up to temperature before the fish goes in. The crust has to meet a hot oven so it sets and hardens fast; a shell baked up from a cold or under-heated oven softens and slumps before it locks rigid. Line a sheet pan with parchment.
  2. 2
    Prep the branzino. Confirm the counter gutted and de-gilled the 550 g / 1 whole fish but left it unscaled; the scales are the barrier that keeps the 1200 g salt crust from over-salting the flesh. Rinse inside and out and scrape any dark blood line from the spine cavity with the back of a small spoon, since the blood line is the strongest source of fishy flavor. Pat the fish completely dry inside and out with paper towels. A wet fish keeps the crust from sealing against the skin and vents the steam, so dry is non-negotiable.
  3. 3
    Stuff the cavity. Tuck the thin lemon rounds (from half the 60 g lemon), the 3 whole thyme sprigs (half the 4 g), and the 2 smashed garlic cloves (9 g) into the cavity. These aromatics perfume the slim flesh gently from the inside as the fish steams; lay them along the cavity rather than packing them tight.
  4. 4
    Whip the egg whites to firm peaks. Beat the 70 g / 2 egg whites by hand or with a mixer until they hold a firm, upright peak, just short of dry. Branzino is slim and bakes in a shorter window than a deeper fish, so the crust gets less time to set; firmer whites pack a stiffer, more reliable shell that locks quickly. The whipped protein also lets you use less binder, keeping the salt rigid rather than wet.
  5. 5
    Build the salt mixture. In a large bowl, combine the 1200 g / 4 cups coarse sea salt, the 3 g / 1 tsp crushed fennel seed, the lemon zest strips (from the zested half of the 60 g lemon), and the thyme leaves stripped from the remaining 3 sprigs (from the 4 g thyme). Fold in the whipped egg whites until the salt has the texture of packable wet sand that clumps when squeezed. If it is still too dry to clump, work in the 20 g / 4 tsp cold water a little at a time until it just holds together.
  6. 6
    Pack the crust. Spread a 1 cm / ½-inch bed of the salt mixture on the parchment in the outline of the fish, and lay the stuffed branzino on top. Pack the remaining mixture firmly over and around the fish, mounding it about 1 cm / ½ inch thick everywhere and pressing it into a smooth, sealed dome; leave the tail fin poking out as a handle. Keep the shell on the thinner side here: a slim fish under a thick crust overshoots before the shell colors. Press out every gap, since a crack or thin spot vents the steam the method depends on.
  7. 7
    Bake the fish. Slide the sheet pan into the fully heated 220 C / 425 F oven and bake 20 to 26 minutes by size: about 20 minutes for a 500 g fish, 24 to 26 for a 550 to 600 g fish, adding 4 minutes per additional 100 g. The crust hardens, turns pale gold, and may craze with fine cracks. Inside the sealed shell the fish steams in its own moisture at a gentle, even heat, cooking edge to spine without the dry radiant blast of an open oven; this self-basting steam is the whole point of the salt crust, and on a fish this lean it is the difference between moist and cottony.
  8. 8
    Check doneness, then rest. Push a thin metal skewer or cake tester through the crust into the thickest part of the flesh behind the head and leave it 5 seconds; on withdrawal it should feel hot to the lip, or read 50 to 55 C / 122 to 131 F at the spine on an instant-read probe pushed through a small hole. Branzino is leaner and thinner than a sea bream and overshoots fast, so pull at the low end and let carryover finish it under the hot shell. Rest the fish in its crust 5 minutes out of the oven; the temperature evens and the flesh sets enough to lift clean.
  9. 9
    Build the warm salmoriglio while the fish rests. In a small saucepan over low heat, warm the 55 g / 4 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil with the 6 g / 2 anchovy fillets, mashing the anchovies into the oil until they sizzle and melt away in 30 to 45 seconds into a savory base; this melted anchovy is the glutamate anchor the lean fish needs. Pull off heat, stir in the 4 g / 1 grated garlic clove and the 2 g / 2 tsp crumbled dried oregano off the heat so they bloom in the warm oil without scorching, then whisk in the 20 g / 2 tbsp lemon juice in a thin stream; the oil and lemon emulsify into a loose, cloudy, bright dressing. Stir in the 8 g / 1 tbsp chopped capers and the 6 g / 3 tbsp chopped parsley.
  10. 10
    Crack the crust tableside. Carry the fish to the table on a platter. Tap firmly around the perimeter of the dome with the back of a heavy spoon or a knife handle to fracture the shell, then lift the top crust away in large slabs; it should come off cleanly, taking the skin and scales with it and exposing the flesh. Brush every loose salt crystal off the fish with a dry pastry brush before serving, since stray crystals are intensely salty and spike any bite they land in.
  11. 11
    Lift, dress, and serve. Run a thin knife or spoon along the spine and lift the top fillet off the bones in clean pieces; lift the skeleton away by the tail to free the bottom fillet, peeling off the bottom skin. Spoon the warm salmoriglio generously over the flesh; the oregano-anchovy oil supplies the umami and the lemon supplies the acid that lifts the gently seasoned fish. Taste a piece: the crust seasons only the surface faintly, so add a pinch of flaky finishing salt at the plate only if a portion reads underseasoned.
Safety
  • branzino at 50 to 55 C / 122 to 131 F (below USDA published-safe 145 F for fish) The salt-crust technique is built to pull the fish at just-set, roughly 50 to 55 C at the spine, below the USDA published-safe finfish target of 63 C / 145 F. This produces the moist, delicate flake that defines a well-cooked branzino, a leaner fish than a sea bream that dries fast past 58 C, and it relies on carryover under the hot crust plus the use of fresh, properly handled whole fish. Cooks who are pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, or elderly, or who are unsure of the fish freshness, should cook to the full 63 C / 145 F target instead, baking 4 to 6 minutes longer and accepting a firmer flake. Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional aromatic register→a Ligurian rosemary-and-bay crust

    Swap the fennel-and-thyme crust for the woodier Ligurian profile: work 6 g / 2 tbsp finely chopped rosemary into the salt with the lemon zest, and stuff the cavity with a rosemary sprig and a bay leaf in place of thyme. The dish shifts from the anise-leaning register toward the pine-resin herbs of the Italian Riviera; keep the salmoriglio finish unchanged.

  • Texture finish→a raw salsa verde instead of warm salmoriglio

    In place of the warm salmoriglio, spoon a cold salsa verde over the lifted flesh: blend 20 g / ¾ cup parsley, 8 g / 2 tbsp capers, 6 g / 2 anchovy fillets, 1 small garlic clove, 60 g / 4 tbsp olive oil, and a squeeze of lemon into a loose green sauce. The plate gains a sharper, herbier, fully raw finish that supplies both the umami and the acid in one spoonful.

  • Texture cooking vessel→a salt-crusted en papillote hybrid for a thicker fish

    For a deeper fish near 800 g, or to guard a slim branzino against overshoot, wrap the stuffed fish in a single layer of parchment before packing the salt around it. The paper adds a steam pocket and a thermal buffer, slows the heat slightly, and makes the cracked fish easier to lift intact; bake 3 to 4 minutes longer to account for the added insulation.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • whole branzino → whole orata (gilthead sea bream), porgy, black sea bass, or red snapper All are lean white round fish in the same 400 to 900 g whole-cooking class and salt-bake on the same logic; orata is the closest Mediterranean cousin and black sea bass the easiest US swap. A deeper-bodied fish (bream, porgy) wants a slightly thicker crust and 3 to 5 more minutes; keep any swap unscaled and time by size. Avoid oily fish like mackerel, which reads heavy under the sealed gentle heat.
  • coarse sea salt → coarse kosher salt Coarse sea salt (gros sel) is canonical for its large, irregular crystal that interlocks into a strong shell. Coarse kosher salt works and packs well with the egg white; avoid fine table salt, which sets into a dense slab and risks pushing salinity into the flesh, and avoid flaky finishing salt, which is too delicate to build a rigid crust.
  • egg whites → cold water alone The egg white is the structural binder; without it, dampen the salt with about 180 g / ¾ cup cold water until it packs like wet sand. The crust is workable but more fragile and prone to cracking, so pack it slightly thicker and move the pan gently. Whipped whites give the most reliable, rigid shell, which matters most on a slim fish baked in a short window.
  • dried oregano → fresh oregano or dried marjoram Salmoriglio is traditionally made with dried oregano, whose concentrated carvacrol is the defining note; fresh oregano works at roughly triple the volume (6 g / 2 tbsp chopped) with a greener, milder result. Dried marjoram is a softer, sweeter stand-in. Avoid the dusty pre-ground supermarket blend; crumble whole-leaf dried oregano for the real aroma.
  • anchovy fillets → colatura di alici or a few drops of fish sauce A teaspoon of colatura (Italian anchovy syrup) or a scant ½ teaspoon of fish sauce whisked into the warm oil delivers the same melted-anchovy glutamate without the fillets. To keep the finish free of small fish entirely, omit the anchovy and lean on the capers, a heavier hand of lemon, and a pinch of flaky salt; the salmoriglio reads cleaner and less savory.

Notes

Why the crust does not salt the fish. The intuition that packing a fish in over a kilo of salt must make it inedibly salty is wrong, and understanding why is the key to the dish. Salt migrates into flesh only through direct contact and a moisture gradient, and the whole unscaled fish presents neither: the intact scales and skin are a near-impermeable barrier, and the crust is bound and largely sealed by egg-white protein, so very little free brine ever reaches the surface. The seasoning is done at the plate by the salmoriglio, not the kilo of salt on the pan; the crust touches only the skin, which is lifted away whole when the shell cracks.

The crust is an oven, not a seasoning. Mechanically the salt shell does two things. It insulates: salt has low thermal conductivity, so heat arrives at the fish slowly and evenly rather than as the dry radiant blast of an open oven, which keeps the temperature difference between the thin belly and the thicker shoulder small so the whole fish reaches doneness together. And it seals: as the fish heats, the water in its flesh and cavity turns to steam that cannot escape the rigid shell, so the fish steam-cooks in a pocket of its own moisture, self-basting the entire bake. The combination is why salt-baked fish comes out so reliably moist and evenly set; it is the gentlest whole-fish method short of sous vide, with none of the equipment.

Why branzino changes the numbers from a deeper fish. Branzino is slim through the shoulder and runs lean, so the math shifts in three ways against a sea bream or porgy of the same weight. The crust goes on thinner, because a heavy shell on a thin fish overshoots the center before the crust colors and sets. The bake runs shorter, 20 to 26 minutes against the 25 to 32 a bream wants. And the pull comes lower, 50 to 55 C against 52 to 57, because branzino dries and turns cottony fast past 58 C at the spine where a fattier or thicker fish has more margin. The egg whites are whipped firmer here for the same reason: the shorter bake gives the shell less time to lock, so a stiffer pack sets faster and holds.

Salmoriglio is the seasoning, and it carries both jobs. The crust seasons the flesh only faintly, so the warm salmoriglio is doing the real work. It is the classic southern-Italian and Sicilian dressing for grilled and baked fish: olive oil and lemon whisked warm into a loose emulsion, heavy with dried oregano and garlic. Here it carries the dish two ways at once. The umami comes from two anchovy fillets melted into the warm oil, dissolving into a salty savory base, reinforced by the briny capers. The acid comes from the lemon juice whisked in to emulsify, sharp against the gently cooked sweet flesh. Build it warm and use it within a few minutes; sitting dulls the lemon and turns the raw garlic and oregano harsh. Without the anchovy-and-lemon finish the salt-baked branzino is clean but austere; with it the plate is complete.

Doneness and the lean-fish window. Branzino runs roughly 2 to 4% fat, leaner than a sea bream, which narrows the doneness window further: the connective tissue between muscle blocks gelatinizes around 55 C and the flesh dries and turns cottony fast past 58 C at the spine. The salt crust widens the margin by cooking gently and evenly, but it also hides the fish, so you cannot watch the flesh opaque the way you can in a steamer. Trust the probe. Pulling at 50 to 55 C and resting 5 minutes lands a moist, just-set fish after carryover; if you prefer or need the USDA-safe 63 C target, bake a few minutes longer and accept a firmer flake. Either way, rest before cracking so the flesh sets enough to lift clean off the bone.

Sourcing and the unscaled rule. The single sourcing instruction that matters is to leave the fish unscaled. Most counters scale by default, so say so explicitly: gutted, gills out, scales on. The scales are load-bearing here, the barrier that keeps the crust from over-salting the flesh, and a scaled branzino salt-baked in this crust does come out too salty. Otherwise judge the fish as any whole fish: clear convex eyes, bright red gills, tight scales with a clean sheen rather than gummy slime, firm springy flesh, and a clean ocean smell with no ammonia. Most US branzino is farmed in the Mediterranean and Aegean and arrives at a consistent 400 to 600 g, which is exactly the size salt-baking wants; one fish fits a sheet pan and serves two.

Sources
  • Adapted from the Italian whole-fish salt-crust tradition, the branzino al sale of the Tyrrhenian and Ligurian coast, finished in the Sicilian salmoriglio idiom. — Branzino (European sea bass, Dicentrarchus labrax) is the canonical restaurant whole fish of the northern Mediterranean: a slim, lean, fine-flaked round fish with mild sweet flesh that rewards gentle, even cooking and punishes overshoot. The salt crust is the gentlest practical method for it. A coarse-salt shell bound with whipped egg white is packed around the whole gutted fish and baked; the shell hardens into a rigid insulating case that traps the fish in a pocket of its own escaping steam. The branzino, slimmer through the shoulder than a porgy or sea bream, cooks faster and wants a thinner crust and a lower pull. The crust does not salt the fish: left unscaled, the intact skin and scales block salt migration, and the seasoning is done at the plate by a warm salmoriglio. Failure modes are a wet fish or wet crust that vents steam, an under-preheated oven that lets the shell slump before it locks, and overshooting the lean flesh past 58 C into cotton.
