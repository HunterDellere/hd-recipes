Chicken stock

Cold-water start with chicken bones, slow-skim, no-boil 4-hour simmer with mirepoix and bay. Clear, deeply gelatinous, neutral enough to use across French sauce work, Italian risotti, Chinese soups, and Mexican caldos.

Execution

  1. 1
    Combine the 1500 g chicken bones and 3000 ml / 12 cups cold water in an 8-quart stockpot. Cold-water start is the rule: cold water extracts proteins gradually as the temperature climbs, producing a clear stock; hot-water start sears the surface proteins and locks in compounds that should leach out, producing a cloudy stock with less gelatin.
  2. 2
    Bring slowly to the threshold of a simmer over medium-low heat (about 90°C / 195°F). This first stage is deliberate and slow, 30 to 40 minutes is right. As the 3000 ml / 12 cups water heats, gray protein scum will rise to the surface; skim with a flat spoon every 5 to 10 minutes for the first 30 minutes. The skimmed scum is denatured proteins and impurities; leaving them in the pot would cloud the stock and contribute off-flavors. Stop skimming once a thin, stable layer of clear fat appears on the surface (the fat is desirable; only skim the gray foam, not the clear fat).
  3. 3
    Add the aromatics. Once the stock is clear and skimmed, add the 1 onion (skin on), 2 carrots, 2 celery stalks, 2 bay leaves, 5 g / 1 tsp peppercorns, and the optional parsley stems and thyme. Adjust heat to maintain a bare tremor of a simmer (just below 90°C / 195°F, with no active bubbles breaking the surface). Cover loosely with the lid askew (full cover traps and emulsifies fat into the stock; no cover lets too much water evaporate).
  4. 4
    Simmer 3.5 to 4 hours. The stock will reduce by about 25%, deepen in color from pale yellow to amber, and develop body that you can feel when you tilt the spoon: finished stock has a light viscosity from the gelatin extracted out of the bone collagen. Do not boil. Boiling emulsifies fat into the broth and clouds it permanently; the entire technique of clear stock is keeping the heat below the boiling point throughout.
  5. 5
    Strain. Set a fine-mesh sieve over a second large pot. Lift out the bones and large vegetables with tongs first to reduce splash. Then ladle the stock through the sieve, letting it drip through naturally rather than pressing the solids (pressing extracts cloudy bits). For absolute clarity, strain a second time through cheesecloth set inside the sieve.
  6. 6
    Cool quickly. The stock must cool to fridge temperature within 2 hours to prevent bacterial growth (the danger zone is 4 to 60°C / 40 to 140°F). The fastest method: divide the stock between two wide shallow pans set in an ice bath, stirring every 5 minutes. Or pour into 1 L containers and place in the freezer for 30 minutes (no longer; freezing the whole batch loses the cold-storage flexibility). Refrigerate.
  7. 7
    Defat. Once cold, the fat layer that solidified on top is gold. Lift it off in chunks; reserve in a separate container for cooking (this rendered chicken fat, schmaltz, is a deeply flavored cooking fat for vegetables, eggs, and rice). The defatted stock below is what most recipes call for; some braised dishes benefit from leaving some of the fat in for richness.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional cuisine→Chinese chicken stock (high stock, gao tang)

    Replace the mirepoix with: 30 g smashed ginger, 4 scallion whites (smashed), 1 small onion (halved). Skip the bay, peppercorns, parsley, thyme. Add 30 ml / 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine in the last hour. The stock is the foundation of Chinese clear soups (egg drop, wonton, chicken-and-corn) and is structurally cleaner than Western stock for those uses.

  • Regional cuisine→Mexican caldo de pollo

    Replace the mirepoix with: 1 white onion (charred over open flame until skin blackens), 4 garlic cloves (charred), 1 jalapeño, 2 plum tomatoes (charred). Add 2 g / 1 tsp Mexican oregano (not Mediterranean) and a small bunch of cilantro stems. The stock is the foundation for Mexican rice, posole, and any caldo (clear soup); reads brighter and more aromatic than European stock.

  • Regional roasted version→brown chicken stock (fond brun de volaille)

    Roast the bones at 220°C / 425°F for 35 to 40 minutes until deeply browned before starting. After bones are out of the oven, deglaze the roasting pan with 200 ml / ¾ cup water, scraping up the fond, and add to the stockpot. Roast the mirepoix on a separate pan for 15 minutes until the edges char, and add. The stock comes out deeper amber, richer, with a pronounced roasted character; the right base for French sauces (espagnole, chasseur, demi-glace) and braised game.

  • Occasion time-shortcut→pressure-cooker version

    Use the same ingredients in an Instant Pot or pressure cooker. Cook at high pressure for 90 minutes, natural release. The stock will not be quite as clear as the long simmer (boiling is unavoidable in a pressure cooker), but the gelatin extraction is excellent and the flavor is 80 to 90% of the full version. Acceptable shortcut when 4 hours is not available.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • chicken bones (carcasses, backs) → whole chicken Use 1 whole 1.6 kg chicken cut into pieces. The flesh contributes to a stock that is more "broth" than "stock" (more meat flavor, less gelatin). Pull the meat from the bones at the 1-hour mark and reserve for soup; return the bones to the pot to finish the stock.
  • all chicken bones → mix of chicken and turkey bones Same technique. Turkey bones contribute deeper, more roast-leaning flavor and slightly more gelatin per gram. Excellent for fall and winter soups.

Notes

Why no salt. Stock is a foundation, not a finished dish. Salting it locks in a salt level that will be wrong for half the dishes downstream (a reduction-heavy sauce concentrates the salt; a seasoned soup adds more salt at the end). Always salt in the dish, not the stock. The exception is Chinese cooking, where stock is sometimes lightly salted (1 g / liter) for direct table consumption, but even then, downstream soups and braises are seasoned in the dish.

Why no boil. Boiling does three bad things to chicken stock. (1) The agitation emulsifies fat into the broth, producing a cloudy, greasy stock that cannot be clarified. (2) The high temperature breaks down delicate aromatic compounds (the volatiles in mirepoix, in herbs) faster than they can extract, producing a muted, cooked-down flavor. (3) The boil drives proteins into a tighter coagulated state that traps impurities rather than releasing them. The bare-tremor simmer is the entire technique.

Storage. Refrigerated, defatted: 5 days. Refrigerated with the fat cap intact (acts as an oxygen barrier): 7 days. Frozen in 500 ml or 1 L containers: 6 months. Frozen in ice cube trays for sauce-work portions (each cube is about 30 ml): 6 months, in zip-top bags after the cubes freeze solid. Reheated stock should be brought to a simmer briefly to kill any bacteria that grew during cooling; do not boil unless the recipe calls for reduction.

Bone sourcing. Save chicken carcasses from roasted whole chickens and freeze them in zip-top bags (a single carcass is about 400 to 500 g; three accumulated carcasses make this recipe). Buy chicken backs and necks specifically for stock at butcher counters and farmers' markets ($1 to $2 per pound is normal). Wings work but are the most expensive bone-per-gelatin trade. Feet, when you can find them at Asian groceries, are the gelatin gold standard: 200 g chicken feet added to this recipe doubles the gelatin yield and produces a stock that goes solid-jelly in the fridge.

Use cases. The neutral mirepoix-based version is the universal base. Use 750 ml / 3 cups for: any French sauce starting with stock; any Italian risotto; any soup that calls for "chicken stock" without further specification; the deglaze of any braising base; cooking liquid for grain (rice, farro, barley); the simmer liquid for any composed soup. The Chinese version is for Chinese soups, dim sum fillings, and any East Asian braise that calls for "chicken stock." The Mexican caldo version is for any Mexican rice, sopa, or pozole base. The roasted brown stock is for richer, deeper European sauces and game.
