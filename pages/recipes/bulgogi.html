Bulgogi (Asian pear-marinated grilled beef)

Paper-thin ribeye marinated in a paste of grated Asian pear, soy sauce, brown sugar, sesame oil, garlic, ginger, and scallion, the fruit enzymes tenderizing the meat while the sugars set up for Maillard browning. Seared hard in a smoking cast-iron pan or over a tabletop grill in 60 seconds per side, then served with rice, lettuce wraps, and ssamjang. The canonical Korean grilled-meat dish, the prototype for the whole Korean barbecue tradition.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Slice the beef AS THIN AS HUMANLY POSSIBLE. The canonical Korean bulgogi cut is 1 to 2 mm thick. At home this means freezing a ribeye or sirloin slab for 90 minutes (firm, not rock-hard), then slicing across the grain with the sharpest knife in the kitchen. Pre-sliced bulgogi meat from a Korean grocery is the easier path. Thick-cut beef does not cook to bulgogi texture.
  • Cook the short-grain rice as a PARALLEL TRACK during the marinade time. Korean meals are rice-based; bulgogi without rice and lettuce wraps is missing structural components. Plan rice timing to land hot at service.
  • Have the heat ripping. Bulgogi cooks in 60 to 90 seconds per side at 230°C+ pan temperature. Lower heat steams the meat in its own marinade liquid and produces gray, sad bulgogi. Cast-iron or carbon steel, smoking hot, single-layer pieces, work in batches.

Execution

  1. 1
    Slice the meat. If using a slab cut, freeze the 600 g ribeye for 90 minutes (firm, not solid-frozen). Slice across the grain into 5 cm × 7 cm pieces about 1 to 2 mm thick with your sharpest knife. The cuts should be paper-thin; you should be able to see light through them. If using pre-sliced bulgogi meat from a Korean grocery, skip this step.
  2. 2
    Make the marinade. In a large bowl, whisk together the 200 g grated Asian pear, 80 g soy sauce, 40 g brown sugar, 15 g sesame oil, 20 g grated garlic, 10 g grated ginger, 40 g sliced scallions, 100 g sliced onion, 15 g sake, 8 g sesame seeds (reserve 2 g for garnish), and 2 g black pepper. Whisk until uniform; the marinade should be thick and aromatic.
  3. 3
    Marinate. Add the sliced 600 g beef to the marinade. Toss with your hands to fully coat every piece (gloves are wise for the pear pulp). Cover and refrigerate 90 minutes to 4 hours. Do not marinate longer than 6 hours; the Asian pear enzymes (actinidin and related proteases) will over-tenderize the meat into a mealy mush past that window. 90 minutes to 2 hours is the canonical sweet spot.
  4. 4
    Heat the pan ripping hot. Place a 30 cm cast-iron or carbon-steel skillet over high heat. Give it 4 to 5 minutes to fully preheat; the pan should be visibly heat-shimmering above the surface and should make a flicked water droplet ball up and dance (Leidenfrost). Add 15 g / 1 tbsp neutral oil; tilt to coat the surface.
  5. 5
    Sear, batch 1 (one-third of the meat). Lift one-third of the marinated 600 g beef out of the marinade with tongs, letting excess liquid drip off. Lay the meat in a single layer in the smoking pan; do not crowd (any pieces touching steam each other rather than searing). The meat will sizzle violently. Sear 60 to 90 seconds on the first side until the edges are deeply browned and visibly caramelized. Flip with tongs; sear the second side 30 to 45 seconds. Transfer to a serving plate. Some of the onion from the marinade will end up in the pan and is welcome there; let it caramelize. Maillard sear
  6. 6
    Sear batches 2 and 3. Wipe the pan with a paper towel only if heavily caked (the residual caramelization is welcome flavor for subsequent batches). Add another 15 g oil per batch. Repeat the sear for the remaining two-thirds of meat in two more batches. Total sear time across 3 batches: about 8 to 10 minutes. Keep the plate of seared meat tented loosely with foil to hold heat. Maillard sear
  7. 7
    Plate and serve. Pile the seared bulgogi on a serving platter. Scatter the reserved 2 g toasted sesame seeds over the top. Set out a separate plate with the 200 g butter lettuce leaves (washed and dry), a small bowl of 60 g ssamjang, a small bowl of 200 g kimchi, a small bowl of 600 g cooked rice (or individual rice bowls), and the optional 10 g sliced chilies. Each diner constructs their own wraps at the table: take a lettuce leaf, place 1 spoonful rice, top with 2 to 3 pieces of bulgogi, dab with ssamjang, add kimchi and chili to taste, fold into a small bundle, and eat in one bite.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Occasion cooking method→tabletop grill (the canonical Korean BBQ table experience)

    Set up a portable tabletop charcoal or gas grill (a Korean BBQ-style butane burner with a domed cast-iron grill, or an electric induction setup with a cast-iron pan). The diners cook the meat themselves at the table, batch by batch, eating each batch directly off the grill into lettuce wraps. The marinade and rest of the meal is identical; the social cooking-and-eating-together dimension is the entire point of Korean BBQ.

  • Protein protein→galbi (LA-style cross-cut short rib)

    Substitute 1 kg LA-style cross-cut beef short ribs (thin strips of bone with meat attached, sliced perpendicular to the bones) for the ribeye. The same marinade works, but the marinade time extends to 4 to 8 hours (the meat is thicker and tougher). Grill or sear 2 to 3 minutes per side over high heat. Galbi reads slightly chewier and more deeply beefy than bulgogi; the bones add structural marrow flavor.

  • Protein protein→dak-bulgogi (chicken bulgogi)

    Substitute 700 g boneless skinless chicken thigh, sliced 1 cm thick (chicken needs more substance than 1 to 2 mm for the cook). Add 30 g / 2 tbsp gochujang to the marinade for the canonical spicy chicken bulgogi profile. Marinate 1 to 2 hours. Sear in batches as written; cook longer (2 to 3 minutes per side) since the chicken is thicker.

  • Protein protein→pork bulgogi (doejigogi bulgogi, often spicy)

    Substitute 700 g thin-sliced pork belly or pork shoulder for the beef. Add 30 g gochujang and 10 g gochugaru to the marinade. The dish is the canonical spicy pork bulgogi served at Korean home and restaurant; reads as a different category from beef bulgogi but uses the same construction.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • Asian pear → Bosc or Bartlett pear, kiwi, pineapple Asian pear (Korean nashi) is the canonical tenderizer; Bosc or Bartlett pear works with slightly less enzymatic action. Kiwi (actinidin is in higher concentration) over-tenderizes fast; use only 100 g grated kiwi and marinate no longer than 60 minutes. Pineapple (bromelain) over-tenderizes even faster; use sparingly. Skipping the fruit entirely produces tougher, less aromatically complex bulgogi.
  • soy sauce (Korean jin-ganjang) → Japanese koikuchi shoyu, generic soy sauce Korean jin-ganjang is the everyday Korean soy, slightly different in flavor profile from Japanese koikuchi (sweeter, slightly more fermented depth). Both work; the dish is recognizably Korean either way.
  • ribeye, sirloin, chuck eye → top sirloin, flat iron, brisket flat Top sirloin and flat iron are leaner; bulgogi reads less rich but still works. Brisket flat (sliced 1 mm thin against the grain) is the canonical Korean BBQ brisket cut for cha-dol-baegi-style; works for bulgogi too.
  • ssamjang → doenjang + gochujang + sesame oil + garlic (homemade approximation, 1:1:0.5:0.2) Ssamjang is the canonical Korean lettuce-wrap sauce; mix 30 g doenjang, 30 g gochujang, 15 g sesame oil, 6 g grated garlic, 3 g sugar, and a splash of water for a homemade version. Bottled ssamjang (CJ brand at Korean groceries) is the convenient option.

Notes

Asian pear is the structural tenderizer. The single ingredient that makes bulgogi recognizably itself (and that distinguishes Korean BBQ from other Asian grilled meats) is grated Asian pear. The pear contains actinidin and related proteases that partially digest the surface protein of the meat over the 90 minutes to 4 hours of marination, producing the canonical Korean BBQ tenderness without making the meat mealy. The pear also contributes subtle sweetness and an aromatic background. Kiwi and pineapple contain higher-concentration tenderizing enzymes and over-tenderize fast; Asian pear is the calibrated balance.

Why thin slicing matters. Bulgogi cooks in 60 to 90 seconds per side because the meat is paper-thin. Thicker cuts cannot cook to bulgogi texture; they sear on the outside but require longer cook time, by which point the meat is over-cooked and chewy rather than tender-soft. The thin slice plus the high heat sear is the entire technique. Pre-sliced bulgogi meat at Korean groceries (HMart, Galleria, Lotte) is labeled "bulgogi" or "bulgogi-yong" and is the practical path; home-slicing requires the 90-minute freeze and a very sharp knife.

The ssam (wrap) is the eating ritual. Korean BBQ is fundamentally a self-construction meal: each diner builds their own lettuce wraps at the table, choosing the meat-to-rice ratio, the amount of ssamjang, the kimchi addition, the chili level. This is the structural reason bulgogi is served family-style with all components separate rather than pre-plated. New eaters frequently miss the wrap dimension; experienced Korean eaters never serve bulgogi without lettuce and ssamjang.

Pan-sear versus tabletop grill. The canonical Korean BBQ restaurant experience is the tabletop grill: a domed cast-iron grill over a butane burner at the table, with the diners cooking their own meat. The home pan-sear approximates this with a hot cast-iron skillet; the flavor is the same, the social experience is different. For a true Korean home BBQ dinner, set up a portable burner and grill at the table; the dish translates to that format effortlessly.

Banchan are the structural accompaniment. A Korean meal is not bulgogi-and-rice; it is bulgogi, rice, kimchi (always), and 3 to 8 banchan (small side dishes: pickled radish, seasoned spinach, sigeumchi-namul, kongnamul bean sprouts, etc.). The banchan are eaten throughout the meal, the diner alternating bites of meat-and-wrap with spoonfuls of rice and pinches of various banchan. Serving bulgogi without banchan is technically possible but structurally incomplete.

Day-2 bulgogi reheats well. Refrigerate up to 4 days; reheat in a hot dry pan for 60 seconds. The marinade carries through reheating better than most meat dishes. Cold leftover bulgogi in a fried-rice form (bulgogi-bokkeumbap) is a Korean home favorite.

The Goguryeo origin and the modern form. Bulgogi traces to maekjeok, a Goguryeo-era skewer-grilled marinated meat. The modern form (thin-sliced, soy-pear marinade, pan or grill seared) is more recent, codified in 19th and 20th century Seoul. The dish exists at every level of Korean cuisine: home weeknight, lunch box (dosirak), restaurant chains, high-end (Hanwoo Korean beef bulgogi at fine-dining temples). The marinade ratio and the meat cut are the variables; the construction is constant.
