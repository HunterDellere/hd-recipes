Chè đậu trắng (black-eyed pea and sticky rice chè)

Warm Vietnamese chè of tender black-eyed peas and chewy glutinous rice in a barely-sweet coconut milk base. A central-Vietnamese workhorse dessert, comforting, structural, and unfashionable in a way that makes it good.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Soak the black-eyed peas 8 hours or overnight in cold water with a pinch of baking soda. Without the soak the dish does not work as a same-day recipe; the beans take over 2 hours to cook from dry. If you forgot, this is a tomorrow recipe.
  • Soak the glutinous rice at least 4 hours (overnight is better). Without the soak the rice steams unevenly and the center stays chalky.
  • Beans and rice can cook in parallel, start the beans first since they take longer (50 to 60 minutes), then start the rice at the 30-minute mark of the bean simmer. Both finish around the same time.

Coconut sauce

  1. 2 × 13.5 oz / 400 ml can full-fat coconut milk

  1. 5 g toasted white sesame seeds

Execution

  1. 1
    Drain the soaked 200 g / 1 cup black-eyed peas. Combine with the 1200 ml / 5 cups fresh water in a heavy saucepan. Bring to a hard boil, skim any gray foam that rises, then drop to a low simmer. Add 1 g of the fine sea salt (about ¼ tsp). Simmer uncovered 50 to 60 minutes until the peas are completely tender but still hold their shape, they should crush easily against the roof of the mouth but not have burst skins. Do not add sugar yet; sugar competes with starch for water and slows softening.
  2. 2
    While the peas cook, prepare the rice. Drain the soaked 150 g / ¾ cup glutinous rice. Combine with the 360 ml / 1½ cups water in a small saucepan. Bring to a boil, then drop to the lowest possible heat, cover, and cook 18 minutes. Pull off heat and let stand covered 10 more minutes, the residual steam finishes the centers. The rice should be tender and cohesive but not pasty; individual grains visible.
  3. 3
    When the peas are tender, stir in the 80 g / ½ cup chopped palm sugar, the remaining 2 g salt, and the 2 knotted pandan leaves. Simmer 5 minutes to dissolve the sugar and infuse the pandan. Stir in the cooked sticky rice. Hold over the lowest heat 5 minutes so the rice loosens into the bean liquid and the whole pot reads as a textured warm porridge. Add 60 to 120 ml / ¼ to ½ cup hot water if the consistency is tighter than a loose oatmeal, chè đậu trắng should pour from the ladle in a heavy stream, not flop in clumps.
  4. 4
    Coconut sauce. In a small saucepan combine the 480 ml coconut milk, the 15 g / 1 tbsp sugar, and a small pinch salt. Warm over low heat just until the sugar dissolves; do not boil. Whisk in the 5 g tapioca slurry; stir gently 1 minute as it thickens to a pourable cream.
  5. 5
    Taste the chè base. Black-eyed pea chè should read mostly bean-and-rice with sweetness as a backdrop, not a foreground. Adjust with a pinch more palm sugar or a small pinch of salt as needed. Fish out the pandan leaves.
  6. 6
    Serve warm in shallow bowls. Ladle the bean-and-rice base into each bowl, then pour 3 tbsp of the coconut sauce in a ribbon over the top. Finish with the 5 g toasted sesame and the 20 g chopped peanuts. The fat and crunch of the peanuts is structural; without them the dish reads sleepy.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional serving temperature→chilled chè đậu trắng

    After step 5, cool to room temperature, then refrigerate 3 hours. The rice tightens as it cools; thin with a splash of additional coconut milk to loosen back to spoonable. Cold version is more Saigon, warm is more Hue.

  • Texture protein addition→with mung bean, chè đậu trắng đậu xanh

    Add 80 g / ½ cup soaked split yellow mung beans to the black-eyed peas in step 1. Cook together; the mung beans break down into the liquid and thicken the body. A richer, more dessert-like version popular in central Vietnam.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • black-eyed peas → small white beans (navy or cannellini) Cooks similarly, slightly creamier. Loses the savory-earthy back-note of black-eyed pea that anchors chè đậu trắng.
  • long-grain glutinous rice → short-grain glutinous rice Cooks faster (12 minutes) and produces a more cohesive, mochi-leaning texture. The dish becomes closer to a Cantonese sweet rice porridge than the Hue version.

Notes

Black-eyed peas are functionally a savory bean in most of the world; their use as the base for a sweet pudding is a Vietnamese particularity, and the small amount of sugar relative to bean is the dish's signature. A Western cook tasting this for the first time often reads it as undersweet, that perception is the cultural calibration to dial back, not the recipe. The pleasure is in the textural play of soft bean against chewy rice in a coconut-warmed liquid, not in pure sweetness.

The baking soda in the soak (a pinch, 1 g / ¼ tsp per cup of beans) softens the bean skins and shortens cook time by about 15 minutes. Too much and the skins disintegrate and the beans turn mushy; the small pinch is the right balance. Some cooks skip it; the trade-off is longer cooking and slightly tougher skins.

Adding the sugar after the beans are fully tender, not during cooking, is essential. Sugar binds water and slows starch gelatinization; beans cooked in sweetened water never reach the proper soft texture. This is true for all bean-based desserts, adzuki, mung, black-eyed pea, and is the most common home mistake.
