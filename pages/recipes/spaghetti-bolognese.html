Spaghetti bolognese (ragù alla bolognese, the household version)

Long-cooked Emilia-Romagna meat ragù: pancetta rendered into the soffritto, ground beef and pork browned hard for Maillard depth, white wine deglaze, milk to tenderize the meat proteins, and a small dose of tomato to round the dish without dominating it. Served over spaghetti as the household-American convention; tagliatelle is the canonical Italian pairing.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Block out a 3 to 3½ hour window. The ragù simmers 2 to 3 hours and the long cook is structural, not optional; a 45 minute version is a different dish (the beef-mushroom-shallot ragu on this site). The ragù can be made a day ahead and refrigerated; flavor deepens overnight and a reheat at low heat is the better serving plan for guests.
  • Start the pasta water boil about 10 minutes before the ragù finishes its long simmer. A 5 L pot takes 10 to 12 minutes to reach a rolling boil; the spaghetti cooks 1 minute shy of al dente (about 8 minutes) and is then finished in the ragù, so the boil needs to be ready when the long simmer ends.
  • Reserve a full 250 ml of starchy pasta water before draining, in a heatproof measuring cup at the stove. Pasta drained without reserved water cannot be re-emulsified into the ragù and the dish dries on the plate.
  • Pull a parmigiano rind from the wedge before grating the finishing cheese. The rind is a structural glutamate addition to the long simmer; if you grate the wedge down too far, you lose the rind for the next batch.

Mise en place by phase 5 phases ▾

Phase 1, the soffritto and pancetta

  1. 100 g pancetta, finely diced (3 mm)
  2. 30 ml extra-virgin olive oil
  3. 30 g unsalted butter
  4. 200 g yellow onion, finely diced (3 mm) — (1 medium onion; the foundational soffritto vegetable)
  5. 100 g carrot, finely diced (3 mm) — (1 medium carrot; the sweetness counterweight to the savory meat)
  6. 100 g celery, finely diced (3 mm) — (2 stalks; the structural-aromatic third of the soffritto trinity)
  7. 12 g garlic, minced — (3 cloves; the modern household addition, classical Bolognese does not use garlic, modern household versions almost always do)

Phase 2, the meat

  1. 500 g ground beef, 80/20 lean-fat
  2. 250 g ground pork, 70/30 lean-fat
  3. 8 g kosher salt (for the meat browning)
  4. 2 g fresh-ground black pepper

Phase 3, the deglaze and tomato

  1. 240 ml dry white wine (Pinot Grigio, Trebbiano, or Verdicchio)
  2. 1 × 6 oz / 170 g can tomato paste
  3. 1 × 28 oz / 794 g can canned San Marzano tomatoes, hand-crushed (with their juice)

Phase 4, the long cook

  1. 250 ml whole milk
  2. 500 ml beef or chicken stock
  3. 2 each bay leaves — (2 bay leaves)
  4. 30 g parmigiano reggiano rind (the freezer treasure) — (a 4 cm × 8 cm rind; structural for glutamate depth, not optional if you have one, see ingredients/parmigiano-reggiano for the rind-as-flavor-base technique)

Phase 5, the pasta and finish

  1. 500 g dried spaghetti (or tagliatelle, the canonical pairing)
  2. 30 g kosher salt (for the pasta water)
  3. 60 g parmigiano reggiano, finely grated, for finishing
  4. 30 g unsalted butter (the finishing knob)

Execution

  1. 1
    Render the pancetta. Heat a heavy 6-quart Dutch oven or large saucier over medium-low heat. Add the 100 g pancetta dice and the 30 ml olive oil. Cook 8 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the pancetta is golden-crisp and has rendered roughly 60 ml of fat into the pan. The low heat and the long render are structural, high heat scorches the pancetta surface and leaves the interior fat unrendered.
  2. 2
    Build the soffritto. Add the 30 g butter to the pancetta fat. When the butter foams, add the 200 g diced onion, 100 g diced carrot, and 100 g diced celery. Cook 12 to 15 minutes over medium-low heat, stirring every 2 to 3 minutes, until the vegetables are completely soft, the onion is translucent-to-pale-gold, and the mixture has reduced visibly in volume. The soffritto is correct when the diced shapes have softened to the point of nearly dissolving but no Maillard browning has occurred, the goal at this stage is sweetness from low-heat caramelization, not browning. Add the 12 g minced garlic in the last 90 seconds; cook until fragrant. Blooming aromatics
  3. 3
    Brown the meat hard. Push the soffritto to the sides of the pot to clear the center. Increase heat to medium-high. Add the 500 g ground beef and 250 g ground pork in the cleared center; press flat with a wooden spoon to maximize contact with the pot bottom; sprinkle with the 8 g salt and 2 g black pepper. Do not stir for 4 to 5 minutes, let the meat develop a deep mahogany Maillard crust before breaking it up. After the first crust forms, break the meat into rough 1 cm chunks (not fine crumble, the chunks hold structure through the long cook); cook 6 to 8 more minutes, stirring occasionally, until no pink remains and the meat shows extensive browning on multiple surfaces. The Maillard layer here is the single most important flavor in the finished ragù; under-browning produces a flat, gray, sodden ragù that cannot be recovered later. Maillard sear
  4. 4
    Caramelize the tomato paste. Push the meat to one side. In the cleared spot, add the 60 g tomato paste and stir into the rendered fat. Cook 2 to 3 minutes, stirring constantly, until the paste deepens from bright brick to dark mahogany and a sweet-roasted-tomato aroma replaces the sharp-fresh smell. The fry-in-fat technique transforms tomato paste from raw-and-tinny to structurally savory; skipping this step is the second-most-common Bolognese mistake after under-browning the meat.
  5. 5
    Deglaze with white wine. Pour in the 240 ml dry white wine and scrape the pot bottom hard with a wooden spoon to lift any fond stuck during the meat browning. Bring to a vigorous simmer; cook 4 to 5 minutes until the wine has reduced by roughly two-thirds and the alcohol has cooked off (the sharp wine smell shifts to a softer, more integrated cooked-wine note). Deglazing
  6. 6
    Add the milk and tomato. Pour in the 250 ml whole milk and stir to combine; the milk will look strange in the moment (the residual acid from the wine and tomato will cause some curdling), this is correct and the curdling reverses over the long simmer. Add the 400 g hand-crushed San Marzano tomatoes with their juice, the 2 bay leaves, and the 30 g parmigiano rind. Bring to a bare simmer.
  7. 7
    The long cook. Reduce heat to the lowest setting that maintains a tremor on the surface (about 90 °C); cover with the lid set ajar. Simmer 2 to 3 hours, stirring every 20 minutes and adding 100 ml of the chicken stock when the ragù tightens against the pot bottom. The ragù is correct when the meat has fully integrated with the soffritto, the fat has separated and pooled at the surface, the color has deepened to a brick-mahogany, and the texture coats the back of a wooden spoon thickly without running. Total simmer time: 2 hours minimum, 3 hours preferred. Discard the bay leaves and parmigiano rind at the end. Taste; salt if needed (the parmigiano rind has contributed salt; the ragù may need none or only a finishing pinch).
  8. 8
    Cook the spaghetti. Bring 5 L of water to a rolling boil with the 30 g salt. Add the 500 g spaghetti; cook 1 minute shy of package al dente (typically 8 minutes for standard spaghetti). Reserve 250 ml of starchy pasta water before draining.
  9. 9
    Marry pasta and ragù. Transfer the drained spaghetti directly into the simmering ragù; add 60 ml of the reserved pasta water. Toss vigorously over medium heat for 90 seconds, lifting and folding the pasta through the ragù; add more pasta water 30 ml at a time if the sauce thickens beyond a glossy coating consistency. The pasta should finish cooking in the ragù (a "risottatura" technique), this lets the spaghetti absorb the ragù flavor rather than sitting on top of it. Pan emulsion (mantecare)
  10. 10
    Finish and serve. Pull the pot off the heat. Add the 30 g finishing butter and 30 g of the parmigiano (half the finishing portion); toss vigorously for 30 seconds, the butter and cheese emulsify into the ragù and add a glossy, creamy finish that defines the dish. Plate into wide bowls. Top each bowl with the remaining 30 g grated parmigiano (or pass at the table with a microplane and a wedge for guest-grating, the canonical Italian service). Serve immediately.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Regional pasta→tagliatelle (the Bolognese-correct pairing)

    Substitute 500 g fresh tagliatelle (or 400 g dried) for the spaghetti. Cook 2 to 3 minutes for fresh, 6 to 7 for dried; reserve pasta water as in the original. The fresh-egg tagliatelle absorbs ragù differently from spaghetti, more porous surface, faster integration. The Bolognese tradition specifies tagliatelle for this ragù; spaghetti is the global household-American convention but the regional dish is "tagliatelle al ragù alla bolognese."

  • Protein protein→beef-only ragù (the simplified weeknight version)

    Replace the 250 g ground pork with an additional 250 g ground beef (total 750 g beef). The ragù reads slightly leaner and less complex; acceptable when ground pork is unavailable or for a single-protein simplification. Add 1 tbsp extra olive oil at the meat-browning stage to compensate for the lost pork fat.

  • Occasion cuisine→lasagna alla bolognese (the structural use)

    Layer the ragù in a 9x13 pan with 12 sheets of lasagna noodles, 750 g béchamel sauce, and 200 g grated parmigiano. Bake at 200 °C for 35 minutes covered with foil, then 10 minutes uncovered. The ragù transitions from a pasta-tossing sauce to the structural layered-bake protein; the milk-tenderization in the ragù pays off in the bake by keeping the meat distinct from the béchamel.

  • Dietary protein→vegan mushroom-and-walnut ragù

    Replace the 750 g meat with 500 g cremini mushrooms (finely chopped, browned hard for 12 minutes) plus 100 g chopped walnuts plus 250 g cooked green lentils. Replace the milk with 250 ml unsweetened soy milk; replace the parmigiano rind with a teaspoon of white miso. The ragù reads structurally similar (deep, savory, glutamate-forward) and meaningfully different (cleaner, lighter, less caloric). The technique transfers; the dish is identifiably vegan and stands on its own as a different ragù rather than as a meat-imitator.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • dry white wine → dry vermouth (Dolin) or dry sherry (Lustau Manzanilla) Both are wine-based and acceptable. Vermouth adds an herbal aromatic; sherry adds a slight oxidation note. Use 60 percent of the white wine volume, 145 ml. Avoid red wine; it pulls the dish toward marinara-with-meat rather than Bolognese.
  • San Marzano tomatoes → quality whole peeled plum tomatoes plus 5 g sugar Cento, Muir Glen, or supermarket store-brand plum tomatoes; add 5 g sugar to round the harder edge of the bargain tomato. The 90+ minute simmer narrows the gap between San Marzano DOP and quality generic plum.
  • pancetta → guanciale (the upgrade) or thick-cut bacon simmered 5 minutes in water (the workaround) Guanciale renders richer and silkier; functional 1:1. Bacon-simmered-in-water strips the smoke and approximates pancetta; functional 1:1 by weight. See ingredients/pancetta for the full discussion.
  • whole milk → half-and-half Slightly richer ragù; the lactic-acid tenderization works similarly. Avoid heavy cream, too fat, the dish reads as cream-sauce rather than meat ragù.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → beef or chicken stock make it yourself Bolognese benefits substantially from real homemade stock, the gelatin and glutamate compound the ragù depth in ways that boxed stock does not approach. The freezer stash earns its place in this dish.
  • → dried spaghetti (or tagliatelle, the canonical pairing) make it yourself Hand-rolled fresh egg tagliatelle is the Bolognese-correct pairing and absorbs ragù differently from dried spaghetti, more porous surface, faster integration.

Notes

Why milk in a meat ragù. The lactic-acid component of milk (which is around 0.15 percent in fresh whole milk and 0.6 percent in cultured buttermilk) gently denatures the surface proteins of the ground meat and produces a more tender mouthfeel in the finished ragù. The technique is structural to ragù alla bolognese specifically and dates to the Accademia Italiana della Cucina's 1982 deposit, which lists milk as one of the canonical ingredients. The visible curdling on milk addition is the milk proteins reacting with the residual acid from the wine and tomato; this reverses during the long simmer and the milk integrates fully into the ragù body. Skipping the milk produces a tougher, drier ragù that reads as good but distinctly less complete than the milk-included version.

Why white wine, not red. Bolognese specifies white wine in the canonical formulation. The reasoning is two-fold: red wine's tannin is too aggressive against the long-cooked meat and produces a bitter, drying finish; white wine's softer acid temper the meat richness without competing. Northern Italian cooking generally uses white wine for meat ragùs and reserves red wine for game ragùs (cinghiale, lepre). The home-cook substitution of red wine pulls the dish toward marinara-with-meat-sauce, which is the Italian-American interpretation of Bolognese rather than the regional Italian dish.

Tomato is support, not lead. A defining feature of ragù alla bolognese is that the meat is the protagonist and the tomato is a supporting flavor, typically less than 200 g of tomato per 750 g of meat in the canonical proportions. The Italian-American "spaghetti and meat sauce" inverts this ratio, with 500 g or more of tomato per 500 g of meat, and the dish reads as a tomato sauce with meat folded in rather than a meat ragù with tomato as one of several flavors. Both dishes have their place; the recipe is named for the dish it intends to be, and Bolognese specifically wants the lower tomato ratio.

The parmigiano rind. Saving the rind from every wedge of parmigiano in a labeled freezer bag pays off here. The rind dropped into the simmering ragù slowly leaches glutamate and salt into the dish over 2 hours; the contribution is meaningful and distinct, and no other ingredient quite covers the role. If no rind is on hand, add a half-teaspoon of white miso and a quarter-teaspoon of fish sauce at the same stage; the substitute approximates the umami contribution.

The pasta-finishing technique (risottatura). Finishing the pasta in the sauce rather than tossing pre-drained pasta with sauce is structural to good Italian pasta cooking. The pasta absorbs sauce flavor through its still-active starch matrix; the starch released from the pasta thickens the sauce and helps it cling. Tossing fully-drained pasta with sauce in a separate bowl produces a pasta dish where the sauce sits on top of dry noodles, common in American home cooking and identifiably worse than the marry-in-the-pan technique used by every serious Italian kitchen.

The freezer life. Bolognese ragù freezes essentially perfectly. Portion into 500 ml deli containers, label, freeze for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator; reheat in a saucepan with a splash of stock or pasta water to loosen. The dish is identical to fresh after the freeze-thaw cycle, which makes Bolognese the canonical batch-cook freezer staple, make a triple recipe, freeze 8 portions, eat well for 8 weeks of weeknights.
