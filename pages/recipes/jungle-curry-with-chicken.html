Jungle curry with chicken (kaeng pa gai)

The coconut-free Central Thai forest curry built on chicken thigh: jungle curry paste bloomed hard in oil, slackened with stock, then simmered with thigh meat whose collagen lends the thin broth a little body it otherwise lacks. Thai eggplant, baby corn, and yardlong beans carry the bulk; fingerroot matchsticks and whole stems of fresh green peppercorn carry the identity; a finishing fistful of holy basil and sliced chili carries the heat. Bracingly hot, herbaceous, and clean.

Before you start

Read this first — the long-lead and coordination steps that need to be moving before mise.

  • Start the jasmine rice on a PARALLEL track so it lands when the curry does. Rinse 360 g / 1¾ cups rice three times, then cook covered at a bare simmer for 12 minutes and rest off heat 10 minutes; that timeline overlaps the whole curry build.
  • Use boneless skinless chicken thigh, not breast. The thin coconut-free broth has no fat to keep lean breast moist, and thigh collagen is the only source of body in the dish. Slice it about 1 cm thick so it cooks through in the simmer without going stringy.
  • Have the finishing handful of holy basil and the sliced fresh chilies within reach of the stove; they go in off heat at the very end and there is no time to fetch them once the chicken is cooked.

  4. 120 g yardlong beans, cut in 4 cm lengths — (1 small bunch; or green beans)
  5. 20 g fresh green peppercorns, on the stem — (3 to 4 stems; leave whole so diners strip the pods off)

Phase 4, finish

  2. 8 g fresh Thai bird chilies, sliced — (2 to 3 chilies, to taste)

Execution

  1. 1
    Bloom the paste. Heat the 30 g / 2 tbsp neutral oil in a wide pot or deep skillet over medium heat. Add the 75 g / 5 tbsp jungle curry paste and fry, pressing and folding it across the pan floor, for 3 to 4 minutes until it darkens one shade and the oil runs clear and red-tinged around the edges. With no coconut cream to crack, the oil is the bloom medium, and under-frying here is the single biggest reason a coconut-free curry tastes raw and flat. Blooming aromatics
  2. 2
    Sear the chicken in the paste. Add the 550 g sliced chicken thigh and stir to coat every piece in the bloomed paste, letting it cook 2 to 3 minutes until the exterior turns opaque. Searing the thigh in the paste before liquid goes in builds a layer of fond and seasons the meat from the surface in.
  3. 3
    Build the broth. Pour in the 1000 ml / 4 cups water (or light stock) and stir hard to lift the paste and any fond off the pan floor. Add the 30 g / 2 tbsp fish sauce, the 6 g / 1.5 tsp palm sugar, and the 3 g / 4 bruised makrut lime leaves. Bring to a gentle simmer and cook 8 to 10 minutes, until the chicken is cooked through to 74 °C and the thigh collagen has started to lend the broth a faint body. The broth should read salty, fierce, and barely sweet, a thin liquid rather than a sauce.
  4. 4
    Add the aromatics and vegetables. Drop in the 20 g fingerroot matchsticks, 200 g / 4 to 5 Thai eggplant quarters, 120 g / 1 cup baby corn, 120 g yardlong beans, and the 20 g / 3 to 4 stems of fresh green peppercorns. Simmer 6 to 8 minutes until the eggplant is tender and curry-stained and the beans are crisp-tender. The fingerroot perfumes the broth as it goes, and the whole peppercorn stems plump and release their resinous heat.
  5. 5
    Finish off heat. Pull the pot from the heat. Stir in the 12 g / 2 tsp lime juice, then fold in most of the 15 g / 1 cup holy basil and the 8 g / 2 to 3 sliced Thai chilies; the residual heat wilts the basil and releases its clove-peppery oils without bruising it. Taste the broth as a complete system: it should land salty, hot, herbaceous, and only faintly sweet and sour. Adjust with fish sauce in ½ tsp increments for salt and lime by the few drops for lift. Scatter the remaining basil over the top and serve in bowls over the 600 g / 3 cups cooked jasmine rice, or with rice alongside so the broth stays thin.
  6. 6
    The Adjustment: jungle curry should read fierce and clean, not rich. If it tastes flat, it is almost always under-salted or under-bloomed, so reach for fish sauce before anything else; if it tastes harsh or one-note hot, a touch more palm sugar (½ tsp) rounds it without making it a sweet curry. If the broth is thinner than you want, simmer it uncovered for 5 minutes before the vegetables go in, which also concentrates the thigh collagen for more body.

Modifications

Pivots that change the dish's character — protein swaps, regional variants, dietary recastings.

  • Protein protein→catfish or freshwater fish

    For the canonical kaeng pa pla, swap the thigh for 650 g cured catfish steaks: cure them with salt and rice vinegar, build the broth without the chicken-sear step, and poach the steaks at a bare simmer for 6 to 8 minutes at the end rather than simmering them through. See the dedicated catfish jungle curry on this site for the full method.

  • Protein protein→wild boar or pork shoulder, thin-sliced

    The most traditional jungle-curry protein. Slice 450 g pork shoulder thin against the grain, sear it in the paste in place of the chicken, and simmer 12 to 15 minutes until tender. Pork shoulder collagen gives the thin broth even more body than chicken thigh.

  • Heat heat & vegetables→fully traditional kaeng pa

    Push the heat up with more bird chilies and add the wilder vegetables the forest curry is named for: a handful of pea eggplants (they stay firm and pop), more fresh young peppercorn, snake gourd or chayote, and a few slices of fresh turmeric. Add krachai generously. The dish should be uncomfortably hot by Western standards.

  • Dietary dietary→vegetarian

    Swap the chicken for 400 g pressed firm tofu in 2 cm cubes (seared golden, then added with the vegetables) and 150 g oyster or straw mushrooms for body. Replace the fish sauce with light soy sauce (use 2 tbsp / 30 g) plus 1 tsp white miso for funk, use a shrimp-paste-free paste, and build the broth on vegetable stock.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • boneless skinless chicken thigh → bone-in thigh, or chicken breast Bone-in thigh (skin removed) deepens the broth further; add 5 minutes to the simmer and pull the meat off the bone before serving if you like. Chicken breast is a downgrade here: with no broth fat to protect it, slice it thin, velvet it, and add it for only the last 4 minutes to keep it from going chalky.
  • fingerroot (krachai) → frozen or brined krachai Frozen krachai is a true substitute; brined julienned krachai (drained and rinsed) works for the matchstick role. Do not use ginger, which loses the medicinal-peppery signature that defines this curry.
  • fresh green peppercorns, on the stem → brined green peppercorns, or cracked black pepper Drained and rinsed brined peppercorns are the closest stand-in. Failing that, a teaspoon of coarsely cracked black pepper supplies the heat but forfeits the juicy pop of the fresh pods.
  • Thai eggplant → Chinese eggplant or pea eggplant Chinese eggplant in oblique chunks braises softer and silkier; pea eggplants stay firm and pop, and are the most traditional choice. Either works in the thin broth.
  • holy basil → Thai basil The common Western substitute. Thai basil reads anise-sweet where holy basil reads peppery and clove-forward; the curry is still excellent but tastes slightly different. Add a few cracks of black pepper to nudge it back toward holy basil.

Make it from scratch

Store-bought is fine, but each of these has a homemade version that earns the time.

  • → jungle curry paste (store-bought, or red curry paste) make it yourself Jungle curry has no coconut richness to hide behind, so the paste does almost all the flavor work and the gap between jarred and fresh-pounded is wider here than in any coconut curry. A from-scratch prik gaeng pa carries the fingerroot and white-pepper backbone that jarred red pastes lack entirely, which is the whole identity of the style.

Notes

What makes it a jungle curry. Kaeng pa is defined by the absence of coconut. The forest cooks of inland Thailand had no coconut palms, so the curry is built on water or stock, which makes it thin, fierce, and clean where a coconut curry is rich and rounded. With no fat to carry flavor and no sugar to soften the edges, the dish leans entirely on a hot paste, a generous load of fresh aromatics, and a final hit of holy basil and chili.

Why thigh, not breast. The thin coconut-free broth has no fat to keep lean breast moist, and the only body the curry can develop comes from the chicken itself. Thigh, with its collagen and intramuscular fat, both stays succulent through the simmer and lends the broth a faint gelatin body as it cooks. Searing the sliced thigh in the bloomed paste before the liquid goes in also builds a layer of fond that deepens the whole pot.

Why bloom the paste in oil rather than coconut cream. A coconut curry begins by cracking thick coconut cream until its oil separates, then frying the paste in that oil. With no coconut here, plain neutral oil does the same job: the paste must fry in hot fat until it darkens and smells cooked before any liquid goes in, or it tastes raw in the finished broth. The bloom is the one rich step in an otherwise lean dish, and it cannot be skipped.

Why so little sugar and acid. Jungle curry sits at the hot, salty, herbaceous corner of the Thai four-flavor balance, with sweetness and sourness deliberately restrained. The small amounts of palm sugar and lime here are for rounding and lift, not to make a sweet-and-sour curry. Taste before adding more of either; the dish should read fierce first.

The role of krachai. Fingerroot is not a generic aromatic here; it is the ingredient that most distinguishes a jungle curry from a thin red curry. Its peppery-medicinal aroma is part of the dish's identity, and the matchstick cut keeps it present as both flavor and texture. If you can only find it frozen or brined, use it anyway; do not substitute ginger.
