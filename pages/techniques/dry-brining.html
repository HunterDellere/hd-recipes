Dry brining

Salt a protein surface and rest it long enough for osmosis to reverse — water and salt diffuse back into the meat, seasoning it throughout.

About

Dry brining is osmosis running in two phases. Salt on the surface initially pulls water out of the meat — visible as beading 30-60 minutes in. Then the gradient flips: the now-saltier surface water diffuses back in, carrying salt with it. End state is meat seasoned throughout, with surface proteins denatured into a structure that holds onto moisture during cooking. Wet brining does something similar but waterlogs the meat; dry brining concentrates flavor instead of diluting it.

Use kosher salt by weight at roughly 1% of the meat's mass. Table salt is too dense; the same volume oversalts and the fine grains dissolve too fast to season evenly. Diamond Crystal kosher and Morton's kosher have different bulk densities, which is why volume measurements lie. Weigh the salt.

Timing is the lever. Under 4 hours and the salt only seasons the surface; the equilibration phase hasn't started. The sweet spot for a chicken breast or a steak is 12-24 hours uncovered in the fridge. Uncovered matters: airflow dries the surface to a tacky pellicle, which crisps to glass-shatter skin on poultry and supports a faster Maillard sear on red meat. Past 36 hours on small cuts, you tip toward jerky-cure territory; large roasts (turkey, pork shoulder) tolerate 48-72 hours.

The sensory cue is dry, slightly tacky skin or surface: no visible weeping, no puddle in the tray. If the surface is wet at hour 12, the fridge airflow is poor; move the rack or run a fan. If the meat tastes salty only on the outside after cooking, you pulled it too early.
