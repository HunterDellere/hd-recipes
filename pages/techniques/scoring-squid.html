Scoring squid (cross-hatch)

Cut a shallow cross-hatch grid into the inside of a squid tube before slicing. The cuts increase surface area for char and dredge adhesion, force the tube to curl into pinecone shapes when it hits high heat, and prevent the tough collagen sheets from contracting into rubber strips.

About

Squid scoring is the controlled disruption of the dense collagen architecture of the cephalopod tube before high-heat cooking. Squid muscle is built from collagen sheets running in alternating perpendicular directions; under high heat the sheets contract aggressively and the tube curls hard, expels water, and toughens. Scoring the inside surface of the tube before cooking does three things at once: it severs the longitudinal collagen sheets so the contraction lifts the cut edges into pinecone shapes (the structural signature of well-scored squid), it dramatically increases the surface area for char and dredge adhesion, and it shortens the cooking time required to reach tender by reducing the diffusion distance from outside surface to center. The technique is the move that defines Cantonese salt-and-pepper squid and Korean ojingeo bokkeum; without it, both dishes read as flat strips of squid in sauce rather than the textured, sauce-clinging pieces the dishes are built around.

Method. Lay the cleaned squid tube flat on a cutting board with the inside (the side that was inside the body cavity) facing up. The inside surface is smoother and slightly more translucent than the outside; it is the correct side to score because the longitudinal collagen sheets are exposed there. Hold a sharp knife at a 30 to 45 degree angle to the surface — not perpendicular. Make shallow parallel cuts across the surface at 5 mm intervals, cutting only halfway through the wall thickness; the goal is to score the muscle, not to perforate it. Rotate the tube 90 degrees and make a second set of parallel cuts perpendicular to the first, again at 5 mm intervals and 30 to 45 degree angle, again cutting only halfway through. The result is a diamond-grid pattern of shallow cuts on the inside surface; the outside surface remains intact. Cut the scored sheet into 3 to 4 cm squares (or strips, for some preparations) and proceed with the recipe.

Why the angle. A perpendicular knife (90 degrees to the surface) cuts straight down through the muscle and produces square-cornered scoring; this works but is harder to do without cutting all the way through. The angled cut (30 to 45 degrees) is more forgiving — the knife glides along the muscle rather than driving down, and produces a longer cut surface for each pass, which translates to more surface area for char and a more dramatic pinecone curl. The angled cut is the traditional Cantonese style and is what produces the visual signature of restaurant-style squid.

Why the inside, not the outside. The inside surface of the squid tube is smoother and the collagen sheets are exposed in their native orientation. The outside surface has the spotted purple-grey membrane (which should be peeled off during cleaning), and even after the membrane comes off, the outside collagen runs slightly differently. Scoring the inside is the textbook approach and produces a cleaner pinecone curl; scoring the outside is acceptable in a pinch but produces less dramatic results.

Spacing matters. 5 mm grid spacing is the standard. Tighter spacing (3 mm grid) produces a finer texture and curls into smaller, more numerous bumps; this is sometimes preferred for very small or thin-walled squid. Wider spacing (8 to 10 mm) produces fewer, larger bumps and is faster to cut but loses some of the textural drama. The 5 mm default is what most Cantonese restaurants use and is the right starting point.

Failure modes. Cutting all the way through the wall: the squid falls apart in the pan into ragged strips and the structural integrity that holds the curl is gone. Solution: cut only halfway, with deliberate knife pressure. Scoring perpendicular to the long axis of the tube without rotating for the cross direction: the squid curls in only one dimension, producing tube rolls rather than pinecones. Solution: always score in two directions, perpendicular to each other. Skipping the score entirely on a recipe that calls for it: the squid pieces lay flat in the pan, the sauce slides off, and the cooking time stretches into the rubber-window. Solution: always score for high-heat applications regardless of how small the squid pieces seem.

Applications. Cantonese salt-and-pepper squid (jiao yan you yu), scored, dredged in cornstarch-rice flour mix, deep-fried 90 seconds at 175°C / 350°F, tossed with white-pepper salt. Korean ojingeo bokkeum, scored, stir-fried with gochujang sauce 2 to 3 minutes at high heat. Cantonese stir-fried squid with black bean sauce, scored, stir-fried with douchi and ginger 2 minutes. Greek/Turkish grilled squid (kalamarakia psita), sometimes scored, more often whole-tube grilled and cut after; both work. The technique is for the high-heat window of squid cookery; it is not used for long-braise applications (squid ink pasta, Galician squid stew) where the long simmer hydrolyzes the collagen regardless and the curling would not survive the braise.
