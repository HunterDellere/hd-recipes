Steeping tea

Extract polyphenols, theanine, and tannins from tea leaves on a time-and-temperature curve that the leaf grade dictates.

About

Tea extraction runs in two overlapping waves. First out are the soluble polyphenols and L-theanine: the compounds responsible for floral aroma, sweetness, and the smooth umami back-end of a good cup. They peak in the first 60-90 seconds in near-boiling water. Behind them come the catechins (EGCG and friends) and the larger tannins, which carry astringency and bitterness; their extraction accelerates after about two minutes and keeps climbing for the duration of the steep. The art is pulling between the waves: long enough to get the full aroma, short enough to leave most of the tannin in the leaf.

Leaf grade controls the curve. CTC (crush-tear-curl) tea — the small black pellets in chai blends and Thai iced tea — has its cell walls already ruptured, so everything extracts fast. Two minutes at a hard boil is a full steep; three is over-extracted. Orthodox whole-leaf tea (loose Darjeeling, oolong, sencha) keeps its cell structure intact, so extraction is slower and more sequential, so you can steep four minutes and stay on the right side of the tannin curve. For tea eggs, where the leaf sits in the braise for an hour, use orthodox black tea or the cooler liquid will have stripped every bitter compound out by the end.

The cue is color and aroma at the lid: a black tea in boiling water turns coppery in 60 seconds, deep red-brown by 90, opaque and slightly purple-edged at the over-extraction point. Smell the steam: once the floral top notes give way to a flat, drying smell, the tannins are winning. Pull the leaves; don't try to dilute. Equipment-wise, a mesh basket that lifts cleanly out beats a strainer-after-the-fact, because every extra second the leaves sit in hot water adds tannin you can't take back.
