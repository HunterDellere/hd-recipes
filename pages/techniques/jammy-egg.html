Technique

Jammy egg

Boil a fridge-cold large egg for 6 minutes 30 seconds, ice-shock immediately. The white sets fully (62 to 70 °C internal); the yolk holds at the chalky-cream threshold (about 68 °C): set enough to hold a dome at the cut, soft enough to weep slightly when broken.

About

A jammy egg is the doneness window between a runny soft-boiled egg (yolk still flowing) and a hard-boiled egg (yolk fully set, chalky and pale). The cooked yolk holds its shape when sliced (no flow) but breaks softly when chewed (no chalk). It is the canonical egg in ramen (ajitsuke tamago), the niçoise platter, the Vietnamese-banh-mi spread, the Israeli sabich, the British posh-cafe avocado-toast plate, and the modern restaurant-grade egg salad. The technique is one of the most reproducible operations in the kitchen if the inputs are controlled.

Egg-protein chemistry sets the doneness window. The white begins to coagulate at 62 °C as ovalbumin and ovotransferrin denature; it firms fully by 70 °C. The yolk stays liquid until about 65 °C, sets creamy at 68 to 70 °C, and turns chalky and grainy past 75 °C. The jammy window is the narrow band where the yolk has crossed into set-but-soft (around 68 °C) without tipping into chalky territory. The challenge is that the egg is heated from the outside in, so the white reaches the higher set-temperature first while the yolk lags; the cook is timing the yolk centre to the moment the white is fully set.

The reproducible method is the timer. With fixed inputs: fridge-cold large eggs (50 g each, 4 °C internal start), water at a hard rolling boil before the eggs go in, water mass roughly 1 L per 4 eggs, and a 6 minute 30 second timer: the result is reliably jammy. The hard-boil-water start matters: dropping eggs into cold water and bringing both up together gives a wider doneness gradient (the white near the shell is fully cooked while the centre yolk is barely set), which is the canonical method for hard-boiled eggs but produces inconsistent jammy results. Boiling water gives a sharper thermal front; the white reaches 70 °C before the yolk gets past 70 °C, and the timer holds.

Egg size and starting temperature shift the timer. Medium eggs (44 g) at the same fridge-cold start: 6 minutes. Extra-large eggs (56 g): 7 minutes. Room-temperature eggs (counter for 1 hour): subtract 30 seconds. The 6:30 standard assumes 50 g large eggs at 4 °C, water at hard rolling boil, sea level. Higher altitude cooks need to add 30 to 60 seconds (the lower boiling point of water at altitude reduces heat-transfer rate); test the first round and adjust.

The ice-shock is non-negotiable for two reasons. First, residual heat continues cooking the egg for 1 to 2 minutes after the boil; an egg pulled at 6:30 and left on the counter peels open at 7:30 doneness, past the jammy window. The cold shock arrests carryover. Second, the rapid cool contracts the white away from the shell membrane, which is what gives clean peeling. Hot eggs peel with the white sticking to the membrane and tearing; ice-shocked eggs peel cleanly. Hold the eggs in the ice bath at least 5 minutes before peeling.

Peel technique. Tap the wide end (where the air cell sits) on a hard surface to crack the shell, then roll the egg lightly to develop a network of cracks. Start peeling at the wide end, getting under the membrane; the shell peels in 2 to 3 large chunks if the technique is right. Pre-peeled jammy eggs hold in cold water in the fridge for 2 days; longer than that and the white starts to get rubbery from extended water contact. For storage, leave them in the shell.

Applications by category. Composed salad service (niçoise, salade Lyonnaise, panzanella with eggs, soba salad with jammy egg): the egg is halved at the plate and the soft yolk reads as part of the salad's sauce. Ramen (ajitsuke tamago): peel and marinate 4 to 8 hours in a soy-mirin-dashi mixture; the cure penetrates the white, dyes it brown-amber, and intensifies the savory profile. Banh mi and sandwich service: sliced jammy egg over the top of an open sandwich, the yolk breaks softly into the bread. Eaten on its own with a sprinkle of flaky salt and good olive oil: the simplest preparation and possibly the best.

Failure modes. Eggs cracking during the boil (eggs were not gently lowered, or the water was too aggressively boiling): fewer cracks if the cook lowers the eggs with a slotted spoon, and reduces to a brisk-but-not-violent boil. Yolk too set ("hard-boiled"): timer ran past 6:45 or the eggs were not fridge-cold; check the temperature of eggs before starting. Yolk too runny ("soft-boiled"): timer ran short of 6:15 or eggs were at room temperature; pull a 6:30 timer and adjust. Shell sticks during peel: eggs were not ice-shocked, or eggs are very fresh (super-fresh eggs peel less cleanly because the air cell has not had time to develop; use eggs at least a week old for boiled-egg applications).
