Maillard sear

Drive amino acids and reducing sugars on a dry protein surface past 140C to build a browned crust and several hundred new flavor compounds.

About

The Maillard reaction is amino acids reacting with reducing sugars on the protein's surface above ~140C. It generates the brown color, the crust texture, and several hundred flavor compounds — pyrazines (roasted, nutty), furans (caramel, meaty), melanoidins (the brown pigments themselves). It is not the same as caramelization, and it is not the same as "searing in juices" (which doesn't happen). It is a flavor-creation reaction that runs only when the surface is dry and hot.

Water is the enemy. While the surface is wet, evaporation pins the temperature at 100C and Maillard cannot start. Pat the protein bone-dry with paper towels: both sides, edges, every fold. Salt 40+ minutes ahead (dry brine) or right before the pan; salting in between pulls water back to the surface.

The cue is multisensory. Color drives toward deep mahogany (not blonde, not black). The sizzle is sustained and aggressive — a hiss-then-quiet pattern means the pan has dropped to steaming temperatures and you've crowded it. The protein releases from the pan when the crust forms; if you have to pry, it's not done. Don't move it before then.

Equipment constrains the technique. Use heavy carbon steel or cast iron, preheated until smoking. Thin pans lose heat the moment cold protein hits and the surface drops below the Maillard floor. Oil with a smoke point above 200C: refined avocado, grapeseed, ghee. Work in batches; a crowded pan steams its own contents and you get gray meat with no crust. The only fix is start over.
