Technique

Ice bath shock

Plunge blanched vegetables into ice water to arrest carry-over cooking and lock chlorophyll bright before it degrades to gray-green pheophytin.

About

Blanching and shocking is a two-step thermal move. The boil softens vegetables by gelatinizing pectin and sets chlorophyll bright — heat displaces magnesium-bound air pockets in the cell walls and the green reads as vivid rather than dull. The ice bath then arrests carry-over cooking and locks the color in place before chlorophyll degrades to pheophytin, the gray-green pigment that signals overcooked broccoli, beans, or peas.

Salt the boiling water aggressively: sea-salty, roughly 2-3% by weight. This is not for seasoning; it boosts flavor that survives into the finished dish, and the ionic strength helps protect chlorophyll from acid attack during the brief cook. Skimping on salt costs you both.

The sensory cue is vivid green on the way out of the boil, a bite that's just-tender (not raw, not collapsing), and full color hold after a minute in the ice. If the vegetable goes drab in the bath, the water wasn't cold enough — cooking continued and chlorophyll converted. If it's still tough, the boil was too short; you can't fix that in the ice.

Equipment matters more than it looks. Use a large bowl with a true 50/50 mix of ice and water, sized so the vegetables drop the bath temperature only briefly. A small bath warms instantly and becomes a tepid bath that doesn't shock. Pull the vegetables when they're cold to the core, not when they look cold; for thick stalks like broccoli that's 2-3 minutes. Drain on a towel; surface water dilutes any sauce that follows.
