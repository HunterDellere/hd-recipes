Acid-milk souring

Add a food acid (vinegar or lemon juice) to whole milk — casein micelles destabilize, viscosity rises, and the liquid is now functionally equivalent to buttermilk for leavening and batter behavior.

About

Buttermilk is acidified milk. Traditionally it was the liquid left after churning butter (low-fat, tangy from natural lactic-acid fermentation); commercially it is skim or low-fat milk inoculated with Lactococcus lactis and held warm until the bacteria produce enough lactic acid to drop the pH to roughly 4.5. That pH shift is the only property that matters for baking: it gives the liquid the acidity needed to react with baking soda (sodium bicarbonate, pH ~8.3) and produce CO2 for leavening.

You replicate this in 5 minutes by adding a measured food acid directly to whole milk. White vinegar (5% acetic acid) or freshly squeezed lemon juice (citric acid, roughly 5–7%) both drop the pH into the 4.5–5.0 range fast enough that the functional chemistry is identical for baking purposes. The ratio is 1 tablespoon (15 ml) of acid per 1 cup (240 ml) of whole milk.

What happens physically: the acid neutralizes the negative charge on casein micelles. Those micelles normally repel each other and stay suspended; once the charge is gone, they aggregate into small visible curds and the liquid turns slightly viscous. This is not spoilage. The curdled appearance is the signal that souring has occurred and the milk is ready.

Why whole milk performs better than lower-fat milks: fat carries fat-soluble flavor compounds (vanilla, browned butter) and contributes to a tender crumb by shortening gluten strands. Low-fat milk sours the same way but produces a leaner, slightly tougher result. Oat milk and soy milk contain enough protein to sour usefully; almond milk lacks protein and produces weak curdling with poor functional results.

The same technique applies to any baked good that calls for buttermilk: quick breads, waffles, muffins, cake layers that rely on baking soda for lift. Ratio stays constant at 1 tbsp acid per 1 cup milk.
