Pan emulsion (mantecare)

Bind starchy pasta water and fat into a glossy sauce by tossing pasta with fat over low heat, finishing off-heat with cheese.

About

Italian: mantecare — to whip or emulsify. The technique that turns pasta + fat + cheese into sauce instead of separate components.

Three forces are at play:

  1. Starch: pasta water carries solubilized amylose and amylopectin from the cooking pasta. These act as emulsifiers, binding water and fat into a stable suspension. The water must be starchy (cooked enough pasta in not-too-much water); plain water won't emulsify.

  2. Mechanical agitation: vigorous tossing in a wide pan creates the kinetic energy needed to break fat into droplets small enough to stay suspended. A weak toss yields a broken sauce.

  3. Temperature window: cheese needs to melt without separating. Above ~70°C the casein proteins denature and clump (the "seized" state); below ~60°C the cheese stays granular and won't hydrate. Pull the pan off direct heat before adding cheese — residual pan temperature is enough.

If the sauce breaks (looks oily and grainy): add more starchy pasta water and toss harder. The starch and motion will reform the emulsion. If the sauce is too thick: same fix, splash water, toss. If it's too thin: keep tossing over low heat to evaporate.

This is the technique behind cacio e pepe, carbonara, aglio e olio. The differences are which fats and proteins, not the binding mechanism.
