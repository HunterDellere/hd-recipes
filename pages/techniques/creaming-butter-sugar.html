Creaming butter and sugar

Shear sugar crystals into pliable butter to drag air into the fat phase — the bubbles leaven cookies and cakes alongside chemical leaveners.

About

Creaming is mechanical aeration. Sugar crystals act as tiny knives — they shear into softened butter and the cavities they leave behind trap air. Those air bubbles are real leavening; baking soda and powder expand the bubbles that creaming created, but they don't create them. Skip or shortcut the cream and the crumb collapses dense regardless of how much chemical leavener is in the recipe.

Butter temperature is the whole game. The window is 18-20C: cool but pliable, indents under firm finger pressure without smearing. Warmer than that and the fat moves toward melt; molten butter can't hold a bubble and the mixture goes soupy. Colder and the crystals can't penetrate; you get a sandy, broken mass that never comes together. Pull butter from the fridge 45-60 minutes before you start, depending on the room.

The sensory cue is visual: pale (nearly white-ivory), fluffy, and the mixture falls from the paddle in a thick ribbon at 3-4 minutes on medium speed. Stop when you hit ribbon; going longer at high speed warms the butter through friction and collapses what you just built.

Use a stand mixer with the paddle attachment, or a hand mixer. Whisks aerate too aggressively, cool the butter unevenly, and pack air into pockets the rest of the batter can't integrate. If the mixture looks greasy and sandy, the butter was too cold; let it sit 10 minutes and try again. If it looks soupy or separated, it's gone past; chill the bowl 5 minutes and bring it back with short low-speed pulses.
