Heavy Saucepan

2–3 quart pan with a thick conductive base. Thermal mass is the feature: stable low heat for tea, custards, and reductions that won't scorch.

About

A 2–3 quart saucepan with a thick aluminum or copper-clad base. Heavy bottom means thermal mass, and thermal mass means stability on low heat. That stability is what makes the pan right for tea concentrates that need slow infusion without scorch, custards that won't run away the moment the burner cycles, and reductions that simmer evenly instead of pulsing.

A thin-bottomed saucepan develops hot spots. The fond, the milk solids, or the sugar at those spots burns before the bulk of the liquid responds — and once that bitter note is in the pan, it's in the dish. Thermal mass smooths the burner's swings out into a flat plateau the food can survive.

Spec that matters: 2.5–3mm base minimum. Tri-ply stainless is the durable answer and will outlast the cook. Enameled cast iron also works, and gives even more mass, but heats up slower and rewards patience over responsiveness.

Substitutions

  • heavy saucepan → thin-bottomed saucepan Works only with attentive heat management — drop one rung on the burner setting, stir more often, pull at first hint of stuck solids. You can compensate with technique but can't replicate the steadiness.
