Equipment

Wok

High-walled carbon steel pan for stir-frying. The shape concentrates heat at the base and routes ingredients up the cooler walls between bursts of contact.

About

The shape is the tool. A high-walled, round- or flat-bottomed pan concentrates heat at the base for rapid Maillard contact, then reroutes ingredients up the cooler walls between bursts of searing — that vertical movement is what gives a stir-fry its texture, with proteins seared and aromatics on the edge of caramelization rather than steamed into uniform mush.

Carbon steel is the canonical material. It heats fast, holds a seasoning, and becomes effectively non-stick with use. The seasoning is the surface — never soap-scrub a wok; rinse hot, dry on the burner, wipe a film of oil.

Spec that matters: 14-inch carbon steel. Flat-bottom for Western induction and electric coil. Round-bottom needs a wok ring on a gas burner and is otherwise the better shape, because the walls do their geometry job all the way down.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • wok → wide skillet Handles some stir-fries (proteins that sit flat and brown) but loses the wall geometry that lets you push food up and out of the heat. Browns less, steams more; fakeable for 1–2 portions, not 4.
