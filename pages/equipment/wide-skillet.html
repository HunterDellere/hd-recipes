Wide Skillet

12-inch sauté pan with sloped sides. The width is the spec: surface area drives evaporation, browning, and the room you need to finish pasta in the pan.

About

A 12-inch (30cm) skillet with sloped sides. The width is the spec that matters — surface area drives evaporation rate, browning rate, and the elbow room you need to finish pasta in the pan without sloshing emulsion over the rim. A narrow pan crowds protein and aromatics; the moisture they release can't evaporate fast enough, the surface temperature drops, and you end up steaming food you meant to sear.

Material is a function of what you cook in it. Stainless tri-ply is the all-purpose answer: it builds fond, deglazes cleanly, and survives sauce work without reacting. Carbon steel is the high-heat answer — faster response, better sear, requires seasoning maintenance. Both have the sloped sides that let you toss and roll food without a spatula.

Substitutions

Workable alternatives when this tool is unavailable. The use case sets the bar each swap has to clear.

  • wide skillet (searing) → cast iron skillet Sears fine but loses the sloped-side ergonomics for tossing pasta and sauce.
  • wide skillet (pan emulsion) → non-stick pan Wrong tool: no fond develops, and the surface masks color cues you need to read doneness.
  • wide skillet (12-inch) → 10-inch skillet Cook in two batches. The surface area gap doesn't forgive crowding.
