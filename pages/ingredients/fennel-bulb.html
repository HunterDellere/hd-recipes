Ingredient

Fennel bulb

Florence or Sweet fennel, Foeniculum vulgare var. dulce. Crisp white-green basal bulb (swollen leaf bases, not a true bulb) with anise-perfumed fronds and feathery green stalks. Anethole-dominant, with parsley and tarragon undertones; the canonical bulb of Mediterranean fish cookery.

About

Florence fennel (Foeniculum vulgare var. dulce, also called sweet fennel or finocchio) is the bulb-forming cultivar of the herb fennel, a member of the Apiaceae (parsley) family. The "bulb" is not a true bulb; it is the swollen, overlapping leaf bases at the plant's stem base, forming a tight white-green head 8 to 15 cm across, topped by green hollow stalks that resemble celery and feathery dill-like fronds. All parts are edible and aromatically related: bulb (sweet, crisp, mild anise), stalks (more fibrous, can be braised or treated like celery), fronds (delicate, anise-forward, used as a finishing herb), seeds (concentrated anethole, dried as a spice). The plant is distinct from wild common fennel (F. vulgare var. vulgare), which does not form a bulb and is grown for the seed; the herb-only cultivar is the source of dried fennel seed in the spice rack.

Functionally, fennel bulb is an anethole vehicle. Trans-anethole, a phenylpropene, is the dominant aromatic compound and is structurally related to the anethole in anise, star anise, licorice root, and tarragon; the sensory profile shifts based on concentration and pairing. In fennel bulb, anethole concentration is ~5 to 10 mg per gram of dry matter (low compared to fennel seed at ~30 to 50 mg per gram, which is why the seed reads more aggressively anise). Secondary aromatic compounds include estragole (more prominent in tarragon, present in fennel as a supporting note), fenchone (the camphoraceous backnote that distinguishes fennel from anise), and a small amount of limonene (the bright citrus topnote). The combined effect is "sweet anise plus green herb plus faint cooling pine"; the bulb pairs cleanly with fish (whose mild flavor benefits from anethole's brightening effect), pork (the spice combination behind Italian sweet sausage), citrus (orange or lemon, which sharpens the anethole), olive oil and butter (both carry the lipid-soluble aromatics), saffron (the canonical Provençal pairing), and tomato. Raw, the bulb provides crunch and a mild anise sweetness suitable for shaved salads. Cooked, the anethole softens and the bulb caramelizes through its naturally high sugar content (~3 to 4 percent), turning silky and reading as a sweeter, more melting allium-adjacent vegetable.

Transformation. Raw fennel bulb is structurally similar to celery: parallel longitudinal fibers running from the stalk base into the bulb, cellulose walls that snap cleanly. Slicing across the fibers (perpendicular to the stalks) produces thin half-moon slices that read tender and shave-able for raw salad; slicing with the fibers (parallel to the stalks) produces long matchsticks that hold their texture in long-cooking applications like braises and stews. Cutting across the core forces the cook to remove the tough triangular core wedge at the base (woody, fibrous, inedible if left in raw applications, slow to soften in cooking). Heat transformations: 70 to 80 °C (gentle braise or steam) softens the leaf bases without browning; the bulb becomes silky and sweet in 20 to 30 minutes. 130 to 150 °C (high-heat roast) caramelizes the surface sugars and creates the deep nutty-anise notes that define a Provençal fennel-and-fish parchment-paper preparation. 170 °C and up produces the Maillard browning that pairs with sausage and slow-roasted pork; fennel sliced and roasted at 200 °C / 400 °F for 30 to 40 minutes turns deep golden and intensely sweet. The fronds and stalks tolerate less heat: fronds wilt at any cook temperature and should be added as a finishing herb (raw, off-heat); stalks can be briefly braised or added to fish stocks as an aromatic and then strained out.

Applications by category. In Italian cooking, raw shaved fennel is the canonical salad bulb (insalata di finocchio with citrus, oil, salt; or with orange and olive oil in Sicilian style), and braised fennel is a primary contorno (side dish) paired with pork or grilled fish; the spice from the seed defines Italian sweet sausage (the unstated reason the sausage tastes "Italian"); the bulb appears in fish stews, in pasta con le sarde (Sicilian sardine pasta with fennel fronds), and in baked-fish preparations across the coastal regions. In Provençal and Marseille cooking, fennel and saffron together define the broth flavor of bouillabaisse and aigo bouido; the bulb is sliced into the broth and the fronds are added at the end. In North-American Italian cooking, fennel is the structural sweet-aromatic vegetable in cioppino (alongside onion and garlic), where it softens and partly dissolves into the tomato-saffron broth and contributes the unmistakable anise note that distinguishes cioppino from generic seafood stew. In Greek and Levantine cooking, fennel substitutes for celery in lentil stews and white-bean preparations; in Egyptian and Iranian cooking, the seeds appear in spice blends but the bulb is less central. In modern American restaurant cooking, fennel pairs with oranges, blood orange, and olive oil in raw winter salads (the Sicilian template), with white fish in pan-seared preparations, and as a roasted side under porchetta. Across all categories, the same operational rule applies: choose slicing (across vs with the fibers) to match the cooking time.

Buying notes. A good fennel bulb is heavy, tight, pale white-green, with no brown spots, soft places, or splits. The stalks should be vivid green and crisp; the fronds should be feathery and fresh-smelling. Bulbs are sold with or without the stalks; with-stalks is preferable because the stalks are useful (for stock, or trimmed and braised) and the fronds are a free finishing herb. Size: a "medium" bulb is 300 to 400 g (small bulbs at the spring start of the season are smaller, 150 to 250 g; late-season winter bulbs can hit 500 g). Look for bulbs labeled "Florence" or "sweet fennel"; wild fennel and bronze fennel (an ornamental cultivar) are different plants. Available year-round in any well-stocked supermarket; peak season is October through April in the Northern hemisphere, when the bulbs are largest, sweetest, and most aromatic. Trader Joe's and Whole Foods carry good bulbs; Italian specialty markets like Eataly often have the best.

Storage

Refrigerator, in the crisper drawer, loosely wrapped in plastic or in a perforated bag: 5 to 7 days. The stalks dry out faster than the bulb; trim them off before storage and use within 2 to 3 days, or freeze (chopped) for stock. The fronds wilt fastest and should be used within 2 to 3 days. A bulb that has gone slightly soft at the cut base can be revived by trimming off the discolored slice; a bulb with extensive brown or yellow spots is past its window and should be discarded.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • fennel bulb (raw shaved salad) → celery plus a pinch of fennel seed or anise seed Celery provides the crisp-translucent structural element; a pinch of ground fennel seed or anise seed (¼ tsp per 200 g celery) adds the anise note. The dish reads as a celery salad with fennel undertones rather than a true fennel salad; acceptable for the crunch and texture, not equivalent in flavor.
  • fennel bulb (braised or roasted side) → celery root (celeriac) or kohlrabi plus 1 tsp fennel seed Celery root is structurally similar (dense, earthy, sweet when roasted); add 1 tsp of bloomed-in-oil fennel seed at the start of the cook to introduce the anethole note. The dish becomes a closely related root-vegetable preparation.
  • fennel bulb (cioppino, bouillabaisse, fish stew aromatic) → celery plus 2 tsp fennel seed (toasted, ground or whole) Acceptable substitute for the structural role; the broth carries fennel-seed flavor instead of the bulb sugar, so the finished dish reads slightly less sweet. Add a pinch of star anise (¼ pod) if the anise note still reads underdeveloped.
  • fennel bulb (Italian sausage flavor) → fennel seed (ground or whole, toasted) The seed is the flavor signature of Italian sausage and a higher-concentration source than the bulb. Substitute 1 tsp ground fennel seed per 100 g of expected bulb in any dish where the bulb is the aromatic carrier rather than the body.
