Ingredient

Mint

Aromatic herb in the Mentha genus; spearmint (Mentha spicata) is the cooking-canonical species, peppermint (M. × piperita) the candy-and-cocktail one. Defined aromatic comes from monoterpene compounds — carvone in spearmint, menthol in peppermint.

About

"Mint" in cooking covers a family of related species in the Mentha genus, but two dominate: spearmint (Mentha spicata) is the canonical cooking mint across the Mediterranean, Middle East, North Africa, and South and Southeast Asia, with a fresh, slightly sweet, vegetal aromatic profile. Peppermint (Mentha × piperita, a sterile hybrid of spearmint and water mint) is the cooler, sharper, more aggressive mint of British-American candy, gum, ice cream, and post-prandial chocolate; it is rarely the right mint for savory cooking despite being the more famous species in Anglo markets. The species choice matters: a Vietnamese summer roll wants spearmint (the soft, herbal note), not peppermint (which would taste medicinal in the same context).

Aromatic chemistry. Spearmint''s defining flavor compound is carvone (R-(−)-carvone specifically, the same compound that gives caraway seeds their character in a mirror-image enantiomer), present at 50 to 70 percent of the essential oil. Peppermint''s defining compound is menthol, at 30 to 50 percent of its essential oil, which produces the cool-mouth sensation by activating the TRPM8 cold-receptor channels in the mouth (the same receptors that fire when sensing cold temperatures, hence the cooling perception even at room temperature). Carvone does not have this cooling effect; spearmint is fresh and bright but not cold-tasting. The other notable Mentha species are: pennyroyal (M. pulegium, traditionally medicinal, mostly avoided in modern cooking due to liver toxicity at high doses); apple mint (M. suaveolens, a fuzzy-leaved spearmint relative with a softer, fruitier profile); chocolate mint (a peppermint cultivar with a faintly chocolate aromatic); Moroccan mint (M. spicata "Nana", the variety used for Moroccan tea, slightly sweeter and brighter than standard spearmint).

Heat sensitivity. The volatile aromatics in fresh mint are highly heat-sensitive; carvone and menthol both evaporate rapidly above 60 °C. This is why fresh mint is overwhelmingly a finishing herb (added off-heat or at the table) rather than a cooking herb; adding fresh mint to a hot pan or boiling broth volatilizes the aromatics within seconds and the dish loses most of the mint character. Dried mint, by contrast, has a different aromatic profile (the volatile compounds have largely evaporated during drying, leaving the more stable terpene compounds and the bitter chlorophyll-derived notes); dried mint reads more savory, more bitter, and cooked-mint-flavored. The Greek and Levantine canon uses dried mint in cooked applications (folded into yogurt for soups, into stuffed grape leaves) where fresh mint would be wrong; the Vietnamese and Thai canon uses fresh mint as a final scattered herb where dried would be wrong. The two are not interchangeable.

Cutting and bruising. Mint leaves bruise easily, and bruised mint releases the volatile aromatics into the air rather than the dish. Cooks who tear mint leaves at the last second (literally pulled off the stem and torn between fingers as the dish goes to the table) capture the most aromatic. Cooks who chop mint with a knife on a board lose 30 to 50 percent of the aromatic in the cutting; the cellular damage releases the oils onto the board and into the air. For most applications, tear at the last second; chop only when the recipe genuinely requires fine cuts and accept the aromatic loss. Mint stems are edible but tougher; in raw applications, strip the leaves; in long-cooked applications (stocks, marinades), the stems contribute aromatic and can be left in.

Applications by category. The Mediterranean and Levantine canon uses fresh mint in tabbouleh (the parsley-and-mint herb base), in fattoush, in Lebanese and Syrian rice and meat dishes, in Greek tzatziki and yogurt sauces, in Turkish cacık, and in the modern roasted-beet-and-citrus or watermelon-and-feta composed salads. The Moroccan canon uses fresh mint in the iconic Moroccan mint tea (a green tea infused with a generous bunch of fresh spearmint and sweetened with sugar). The Indian and South Asian canon uses fresh mint in raitas, in chutneys (mint-cilantro chutney with green chili and lime), in marinades for tandoori chicken, in biryani garnishes, and in lassis. The Vietnamese and Thai canon uses fresh mint as one of the herb-bouquet elements in summer rolls, in larb-style salads, in pho garnishes, and in chicken and noodle soups. The Italian canon uses mint relatively rarely (more in southern Italian and Sardinian cooking than northern), in dishes like trippa alla romana, in some pesto variants, and in salads with peas and pecorino. The American canon uses mint in mojitos, juleps, candy and chocolate, and in some seasonal pea-and-mint sides.

Buying. Look for vivid green leaves with no yellowing or black spotting, intact stems, and a strong aromatic when the leaves are gently rubbed (a mint that smells faintly of nothing is past its peak). Bunches should look freshly cut at the base, not slimy. The choice between varieties matters less for everyday cooking than for specialist applications: standard supermarket "mint" is almost always spearmint, which is what most savory cooking wants. For Moroccan tea or for the cleanest spearmint flavor, look for Moroccan mint or Nana mint at Middle Eastern groceries; for mojitos and juleps, peppermint is the traditional choice but spearmint is also legitimate and many bartenders prefer it. Mint grows aggressively in any garden (even a single plant in a pot will overrun the area within a season) and home-grown mint is significantly more aromatic than supermarket mint because the volatile compounds degrade in the supply chain.

Storage

Refrigerator drawer wrapped loosely in a slightly damp paper towel inside a sealed bag or container, 5 to 7 days at peak. Alternative: stand the stems in a jar of water like cut flowers, cover loosely with a plastic bag, refrigerate; this method holds for 1 to 2 weeks but takes refrigerator space. Failure modes: yellowing leaves (oxidation, the aromatic is diminishing, use immediately); slimy black-spotted leaves (microbial breakdown, discard); dehydrated/papery leaves (moisture loss, the leaves are still flavorful but reduced; use in cooked applications only). Mint freezes poorly fresh; the cellular damage from ice crystals destroys the aromatic. For preservation, dry whole sprigs for dried-mint applications, or pack chopped leaves in olive oil and freeze in ice-cube trays for finishing oils.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • mint (fresh, finishing herb in Mediterranean or Levantine application) → fresh basil + a small pinch of dried mint Basil shares the bright herbal volatility, the dried mint hints at the missing carvone. Imperfect; mint's specific cooling-vegetal note is not replaceable. Use when no fresh mint is available and the dish is forgiving.
  • mint (fresh, in Vietnamese or Thai herb-bouquet application) → fresh Thai basil or fresh shiso/perilla Both bring an aromatic herb to the bouquet. Thai basil is the closest substitute (anise-licorice notes); shiso is a different aromatic profile (more tea-like, slightly cumin-adjacent) but legitimate as the fresh-herb element. Both are common in the same Asian markets where Thai basil and mint are sold.
  • mint (dried, in Greek or Levantine cooked application) → dried oregano + small pinch of dried thyme Different flavor entirely; the substitution is for the dried-savory-herb function rather than the mint character specifically. Acceptable in tomato-based sauces and meat marinades; wrong for yogurt-based sauces where mint's specific cool note is the point.
