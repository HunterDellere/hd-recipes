Ingredient

Ghee (clarified butter, घी)

Butter simmered until the water boils off and the milk solids brown to deep amber, then strained. Pure butterfat with the toasted milk-solid aromatics carried in solution. Smoke point around 250°C / 480°F, distinctly nutty, shelf-stable. The structural cooking fat of North Indian and Pakistani cuisine.

About

Ghee is butter that has been simmered to drive off all the water and brown the milk solids past the brown-butter stage, then strained free of the now-toasted solids. The remaining liquid is pure butterfat (about 99.5%) carrying the dissolved aromatic compounds from the toasted casein and lactose. Cooled, it solidifies into a pale-yellow paste with a pronounced nutty-caramelized aroma. Indian ghee (made from cow''s milk) and Pakistani ghee are the structural cooking fat of those cuisines; clarified butter (the European/French version, made by skimming foam and pouring off the clear fat without browning the solids) is similar but milder and less aromatic.

Why ghee, not butter or oil. Three reasons. First, the smoke point: regular butter scorches around 175°C / 350°F because the milk solids burn; ghee with the solids removed and toasted has no scorch threshold below 250°C / 480°F, which is what tandoor-style cooking and aggressive tempering of whole spices require. Second, the flavor: the toasted-milk-solid aromatics are dissolved in the fat and carry into anything cooked in ghee. A dal tempered in ghee tastes nutty-caramelized in a way the same dal tempered in oil cannot. Third, the shelf life: ghee is shelf-stable at room temperature for months because the water and proteins (the parts that would spoil) have been removed.

Cooking applications. Tempering whole spices (cumin seeds, mustard seeds, dried chilies) — ghee handles the high heat of bloom temperatures (180 to 220°C) without breaking. Frying paneer cubes, finishing dal with a tarka (the spice-and-fat ladleful poured over a finished dal), enriching biryani, brushing onto naan straight from the tandoor or hot pan. Indian sweets (halwa, ladoo, jalebi) are typically made in ghee. Eggs scrambled in ghee taste structurally different than eggs in butter — more savory, more nutty, less dairy-forward.

Buying notes. Indian groceries sell ghee in glass or tin jars; the major brands (Amul, Nanak, Anchor, Pure Indian Foods) are reliable. Look for "ghee" with no other ingredients on the label, pure ghee is just clarified butterfat. The color should be deep gold to amber; pale-yellow ghee suggests under-cooking and lower aromatic depth. The aroma should be distinctly nutty-caramel, not just buttery. Cultured ghee (made from cultured butter, with a faintly tangy note from lactic-acid bacteria) is a higher-end style and worth seeking out for finishing applications. Plant-based "ghee" (Carrington Farms, Fourth & Heart) is generally a coconut-oil blend with butter flavoring; it works in a pinch but is recognizably different.

Homemade is straightforward. 500 g unsalted butter, simmer in a heavy saucepan over medium-low heat for 25 to 30 minutes, until the foam falls and the milk solids at the bottom turn deep amber and the kitchen smells of toasted hazelnut. Strain through cheesecloth into a clean glass jar. Yield is about 400 g ghee. The toasted solids are excellent, eat with rice, save in a jar for another use.

Storage

Sealed at room temperature in a cool dry pantry: 6 to 9 months unopened, 3 to 4 months opened. Refrigerated: 1 year or longer (the cold dramatically slows oxidation). Use a clean dry spoon every time; water introduced from a wet utensil will eventually mold. Off-cues are sour or rancid smell (oxidized fat, ghee with high turnover rarely goes off, but ghee that sits a year in a hot pantry can). The texture changes between cool (paste) and warm (clear liquid); both states are normal.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • ghee (Indian/Pakistani cooking, tempering, frying) → unsalted butter + neutral oil (50/50) Combine equal parts butter and a high-smoke-point neutral oil (peanut or rice bran). The butter contributes the dairy aromatic; the oil raises the effective smoke point. Less depth than real ghee but a defensible substitute. The classic French clarified-butter substitute also works (skim foam from melted butter, pour off the clear fat) but lacks the toasted-solid notes that distinguish ghee.
  • ghee (Indian sweets, halwa, finishing applications) → unsalted European-style butter, cooked into brown butter See techniques/brown-butter. For sweets where the ghee aromatic is the entire point, real ghee or homemade brown butter both work; the dish reads slightly different but acceptable.
  • ghee (vegan or dairy-free) → unrefined coconut oil + a few drops of toasted sesame oil Different fat profile but recovers some of the aromatic depth. The dish reads as a coconut-oil version, not as a butter-fat version; honest about the trade.
