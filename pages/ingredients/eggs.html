Ingredient

Eggs (large)

Roughly 50 g per large US egg. Whites set at 62 °C, yolks at 70 °C, and the gap between those temperatures is half of what cooks do with eggs.

About

A large US egg out of shell is roughly 50 g: 30 g white, 18 g yolk, 6 g shell. The white is mostly water (~88%) plus protein (~10%), dominated by ovalbumin, ovotransferrin, and ovomucoid. The yolk is denser nutritionally and structurally: ~52% water, ~27% fat (with phospholipids that emulsify), ~16% protein, and roughly 5% other (vitamins, minerals, carotenoids that color it). The two halves cook differently, foam differently, bind differently, and emulsify differently, which is why the cook gets so many distinct techniques out of one ingredient.

Heat thresholds explain most of egg cooking. Whites begin coagulating around 62 °C as ovalbumin denatures, firm up by 70 °C, and finish rubbery and tight by 80 °C. Yolks stay liquid until about 65 °C, set creamy at 70 °C, and turn chalky and grainy past 75 °C. The gap is the operating window: poach in water just below boiling and pull when the white has set and the yolk is still molten; bake a custard in a water bath at 90 °C and watch it thicken without curdling because the lower bath temperature ferries heat slowly enough that the proteins set evenly. Sous-vide eggs at 63 °C produce the canonical onsen-style soft custard yolk because that single temperature is high enough to denature the white and below the chalky threshold for the yolk.

Yolks are the canonical kitchen emulsifier. Phospholipids (primarily lecithin) plus apoproteins from the yolk membrane stabilize oil-in-water emulsions: mayonnaise, hollandaise, béarnaise, aioli, the egg-and-cream base of ice cream, and the tightly bound crumb of a butter cake. The mechanism is identical across these. Lecithin coats fat droplets and lets them suspend in a water phase that would otherwise reject them. Whole eggs work, yolks alone work better.

Whites are the canonical kitchen foam. Mechanical agitation unfolds the surface proteins and traps air; the foam is stabilized by globulins and ovomucin until heat sets it permanently in a soufflé, meringue, angel food cake, or a génoise. Older whites whip faster and to greater volume because the proteins have already partially denatured during storage; super-fresh whites whip slower but to a more stable foam. Adding acid (cream of tartar, lemon juice) or a copper bowl tightens the network by retarding overbeating; adding fat (a stray drop of yolk, an oily bowl) collapses it because fat blocks the protein-air interface.

Whole eggs are the binder in most baked goods. They contribute liquid (which gluten needs to develop), structure (the proteins set as the bake cooks), tenderness (yolk fat shortens gluten strands), color (the yolk pigments and the surface Maillard that gives an egg-washed crust its sheen), and lift (when whipped). Substituting them out reliably changes more than one variable at a time, which is why egg-replacement strategies tend to work in dense bakes (cookies, brownies, quick breads) and fail in structural ones (chiffon, soufflé, angel food).

Freshness shifts behavior. As an egg ages, the air cell at the wide end grows (water vapors out through the porous shell), the white thins as ovomucin breaks down, and pH rises from ~7.6 to ~9.2. Practical effects: fresh eggs poach with tighter whites, older eggs peel cleaner after hard-boiling because the higher pH loosens the inner membrane, and very old eggs whip more readily but give less stable meringues. The float test (egg in water, sinker is fresh, floater is old) exists because of the air cell expansion.

Buying notes are mostly stewardship. Pasture-raised yolks are deeper orange from carotenoids in green-pasture diets and read as richer in custards and pasta dough. Cage-free and organic certifications speak to welfare and feed inputs but not to flavor. Brown vs white shells are a breed marker, not a quality marker. Refrigeration is standard in the US, where eggs are washed (removing the protective bloom), and not the norm in Europe, where eggs are unwashed and stable on the counter for a week or so.

Storage

Refrigerator at 4 °C, in the original carton, on a middle shelf rather than the door. The carton blocks odor uptake and the middle shelf is the most temperature-stable. Last 4–5 weeks past pack date if undisturbed. Bring to room temperature before baking unless the recipe specifies cold (laminated dough wants cold; cake batters and meringues want temperate). Failure modes: a cracked shell admits bacteria, so discard. A rotten egg announces itself unmistakably when cracked into a small bowl, which is why every recipe that calls for multiple eggs benefits from a separate bowl per egg.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • whole egg (binding in cookies, quick breads, brownies) → flax or chia "egg" (1 tbsp ground seed + 3 tbsp water, rested 5 min) Vegan binder for dense bakes where structure is forgiving. Fails in cakes built around egg structure (sponge, angel food, soufflé) and in custards.
  • whole egg (custards, ice cream base, pastry cream) → no real substitute Custards depend on yolk lecithin and ovalbumin coagulation. Starches alone (cornstarch, gelatin) thicken but do not set with the same body. Pick a different dessert.
  • egg yolk (mayonnaise, hollandaise, aioli) → whole egg, blender method A whole egg at room temperature emulsifies in an immersion-blender mayonnaise as cleanly as yolks alone, with a slightly looser final texture. Common pasteurized-safety move.
  • egg white (meringue, soufflé, angel food cake) → aquafaba (chickpea-can liquid), whipped Whips to a similar foam structure thanks to dissolved proteins and saponins. Use 3 tbsp aquafaba per white. Wet meringues read slightly bean-sweet on the nose; works under flavored toppings, less so plain.
  • eggs scaled down by half (recipe halving) → beat one egg, weigh, use half by weight Each component (yolk, white) is needed in proportion, so a beaten whole egg measured by mass beats trying to split a single egg by structure.
