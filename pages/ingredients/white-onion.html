Ingredient

White onion

Allium cepa with white-papery skin and white flesh. Sharper and cleaner than yellow onion when raw; the canonical raw-application onion of central and northern Mexican cooking ; pico de gallo, salsa cruda, taco garnish, ensalada de nopales.

About

White onions are Allium cepa cultivars selected for white skin, white flesh, and a sharp, clean, mineral pungency that reads cleaner than yellow onion at the same maturity. The defining flavor difference is in the sulfur-volatile profile; white onions carry a higher proportion of allyl methyl sulfide and a lower proportion of the heavier allyl propyl disulfide that dominates yellow-storage-onion pungency. The result is an onion that reads bright and assertive raw without the throat-burn lingering bitter that aggressive yellow onions carry, and that has roughly 1 percent less residual sugar than yellow (5 to 6 percent vs 6 to 7), which keeps the raw-eating experience clean rather than cloying.

Where white onion is the right choice. White onion is the canonical raw-application onion of central and northern Mexican cooking. Pico de gallo, salsa cruda, salsa verde, taco garnish, ensalada de nopales, ensalada de pepino, and tostadas all use white onion by tradition and by flavor logic. The Mexican pantry default for raw onion is white; Cuban and Puerto Rican cooking tends toward yellow; Indian, Persian, and Levantine cooking tend toward red; Italian and French cooking tend toward yellow with red as the raw-color choice. The regional split reflects both flavor preference and the historical onion cultivars available in each cuisine's home territory.

Cell rupture is the flavor switch. Like all alliums, white onion's pungency-and-tear chemistry runs through alliinase ; the enzyme that converts s-alkyl cysteine sulfoxides (intact, odorless) into propanethial S-oxide (the lacrimator) and downstream sulfur volatiles when cells are cut. Sharper-cut onions release more; a duller knife crushes more cells and produces more pungency. Slicing pole-to-pole (along the grain, vertical cuts from root to tip) cuts fewer cells than slicing equator-to-equator (across the rings); pole-to-pole-cut white onion in pico de gallo or salsa reads cleaner and less aggressive than equator-cut. Diced onion is hardest to control; aim for a sharp knife and quick cuts. The Mexican home-cook standard for raw white onion is finely diced, 3 to 5 mm; the small dice produces a heat-fast, evenly distributed onion presence in the bite.

Cold-water soak. Aggressive raw white onion (older storage onions, or warm-stored onions where allyl propyl disulfide has accumulated) can be moderated by a 5 to 10 minute soak in cold water after cutting. The water-soluble sulfur compounds leach out and the lacrimator content drops by roughly 50 to 70%, but the textural crunch is preserved. Over-soaking past 15 minutes leaches sugar and texture as well, leaving washed-out onion. Soak only when the onion tastes sharper than the dish needs; fresh, in-season white onion typically does not require the soak.

Cooked behavior. White onion caramelizes more cleanly than yellow ; less surface browning at the same temperature because the lower residual sugar produces less burn-prone fructose, and a lighter final color (pale gold rather than mahogany) at full caramelization. The trade-off is that white onion takes about 20 percent longer to fully caramelize than yellow at the same temperature. For most savory cooking applications (sofrito, base for stew, sauteed onion), yellow onion is the canonical choice and white onion is an unusual pick; the white-onion advantage emerges only for raw or briefly-cooked applications.

Cultivar notes. The standard US-market white onion is the storage variety (typical supermarket product, sold by the pound); flavor is mid-pungency and stores 4 to 6 weeks at room temperature. Sweet white onion cultivars (Texas Sweet, Vidalia-derived whites) are seasonal (spring through early summer) and noticeably milder, suitable for raw eating with no soak ; these are the white onions most associated with high-quality Mexican home cooking and are worth seeking out in season. The pearl white onion (small, used for pickling) is a different cultivar bred for size and is not the same ingredient as raw eating-onion white.

Buying. Look for white onions that are heavy for their size with dry, papery skin and no green sprout from the top or soft brown spots near the root plate. Sprouting onions are usable but the green shoot has drawn sugar and moisture from the bulb, leaving a pithy, less-sweet onion. The skin should be unbroken and white-to-cream colored; yellow tinge in the skin indicates a mid-storage onion that has begun to dehydrate.

Storage

Whole, unpeeled, cool dark pantry: 4 to 6 weeks. White onions store slightly less well than yellow because the higher water content makes them more prone to soft-spot development at the root plate; check weekly for soft spots and remove any that develop. Refrigeration is unnecessary for whole onions and shortens shelf life. Cut onion: refrigerated in a sealed container, 4 to 5 days at peak; usable but increasingly sulfurous past day 5. The off-cue is a soft, moist outer layer that smells acridly sulfurous (vs the clean sharp note of a fresh onion); discard.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • white onion (raw applications, salsas, salads, tacos) → sweet onion (Vidalia, Walla Walla) Sweet onion is the closest substitute for white in raw Mexican applications when white is unavailable; flavor lands close, the sweetness is slightly higher but acceptable. Yellow onion in this role reads dull and overly sulfurous; red onion shifts the dish into a different cuisine register (kachumber, fattoush). Sweet onion is the right fallback.
  • white onion (pico de gallo, ensalada de nopales) → shallot, sliced fine Shallot has a mildness similar to white onion plus a faint garlic note from higher allicin content. The dish reads slightly more European than Mexican but the basic chemistry holds; acceptable.
  • white onion (cooked applications, sofrito, sauteed base) → yellow onion Yellow onion is the canonical cooking onion across most cuisines; white onion in cooked applications offers no advantage and takes longer to caramelize. Substitute 1:1 by weight; the dish stays the same.
