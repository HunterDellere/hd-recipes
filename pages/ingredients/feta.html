Ingredient

Feta

Greek brined sheep's-milk cheese (PDO since 2002), made from at least 70 percent sheep's milk with up to 30 percent goat. Crumbly, lactic-tangy, salt-cured in brine for 2 to 3 months. The defining cheese of Greek and broader Eastern Mediterranean cooking.

About

Feta is a brined white cheese made from sheep''s milk (at least 70 percent by EU regulation) with up to 30 percent goat''s milk, produced in defined regions of mainland Greece and the island of Lesbos. The cheese has held Protected Designation of Origin (PDO) status under EU law since 2002, meaning that within the EU, only cheese made in those regions to those specifications can legally be sold as "feta." Outside the EU, the term is generic and applied to a wide range of products including cow''s-milk imitations made in Denmark, Bulgaria, Wisconsin, and California; these are different products, not just lesser versions, and the substitution map needs to acknowledge this rather than treat all "feta" as the same ingredient.

The cheesemaking. Sheep and goat milk are heated to about 32 °C, inoculated with a lactic starter culture (Lactobacillus and Streptococcus species), and curdled with rennet. The curd is cut into 1 to 2 cm cubes, drained, salted, and pressed lightly into rectangular molds. After a few days of initial firming, the cheese blocks are transferred to brine (the brine is roughly 7 percent salt by weight, often the natural milk whey reused with added salt) and held in tanks for a minimum of 2 months at cool cellar temperatures (around 16 °C); high-quality feta cures 3 months or longer. During the brine cure the cheese develops its characteristic crumbly-but-cohesive texture (lactic acid bacteria continue to acidify the curd, which dissolves some of the protein bonds), its salty-tangy flavor, and the layered fermentation aromatic profile that distinguishes real feta from the rapid-cure cow''s-milk imitations.

Sheep versus cow milk. The 70-30 sheep-goat blend matters because sheep milk is roughly 6 percent fat (twice the cow figure of 3.5 percent) and the fat is composed of shorter-chain fatty acids (caproic, caprylic, capric) that contribute the distinctive sheep-cheese musk that reads as "this tastes like feta" to a Mediterranean palate. Cow''s milk lacks both the fat percentage and the short-chain fatty acid profile; cow''s-milk "feta-style" cheese is paler, milder, and more uniformly crumbly with none of the funky lactic depth of the real product. The texture difference is stark too: sheep-milk feta is creamier, holds its shape better when broken into chunks, and softens at room temperature into an almost buttery state that whipping can carry into a smooth spread. Cow''s-milk feta turns chalky-crumbly under the same handling.

Salt and texture variation. Greek feta varies by producer and aging time: shorter cures (2 months) are softer, milder, and creamier; longer cures (3 to 4 months) are firmer, sharper, and more aggressively salty. The salt content runs 3 to 4 percent of total weight (a substantial salt load, equivalent to a heavily seasoned cured meat). Some feta is sold "in brine" (cubes or blocks submerged in their cure liquid in plastic tubs or tins), which preserves the moist creamy texture; other feta is sold "drained" (vacuum-packed without the brine), which firms up over the storage period. For whipped-feta applications, in-brine feta is preferable because the moisture is part of what makes the whip smooth.

Applications by category. The Greek home canon uses feta in horiatiki (the village salad with tomato, cucumber, onion, olives, oregano, and a slab of feta on top — never crumbled, never with lettuce), in tiropita (filo and feta pastries), in spanakopita (feta and spinach in filo), and crumbled over grilled vegetables and fish. The broader Mediterranean canon uses feta in roasted-vegetable salads (the beet-and-citrus tradition), in pasta with tomato and herbs, baked whole in cast iron with tomatoes and olive oil (the viral "baked feta pasta" is a modern simplification of older Greek and Turkish baked-cheese dishes). The modern restaurant canon (US and UK from the 2010s onward) treats feta as a base for whipped dips and spreads (whipped feta, the labneh-and-feta hybrid), as the cheese in spanakorizo (spinach rice), and folded into dough for feta-and-herb biscuits. The Turkish and Eastern Mediterranean canon uses very similar brined sheep cheeses (beyaz peynir in Turkey, jibneh in the Levant) functionally identically; these are technically not feta under EU law but are the same product family.

Whipped feta technique. To whip feta into a spreading texture, drain the cheese from its brine, break into rough chunks (200 g feta is a standard portion), and process in a food processor with a lubricator (50 to 100 g full-fat Greek yogurt or labneh, or 50 to 75 g whole-milk ricotta) and a small amount of olive oil and lemon juice. Process 60 to 90 seconds, scraping once, until smooth and glossy. The yogurt is structural (pure feta is too dense and salt-aggressive to spread easily); the olive oil is for gloss and mouthfeel; the lemon brightens what is already very salty. The texture should land between thick yogurt and room-temperature ricotta — soft enough to spread with a spoon, firm enough to hold shape on a platter.

Buying. Real Greek PDO feta is sold in Greek and Mediterranean markets, in better supermarket cheese counters, and online (Whole Foods, Costco, Wegmans) for $10 to $18 per pound. Look for the PDO designation on the package (it will say "Greek feta" or "Φέτα" with the PDO logo). Brands to look for: Mt. Vikos, Dodoni, Krinos, Athenos (the imported real-Greek line; not the cow''s-milk Athenos sold in domestic supermarket plastic tubs, which is the imitation product). Avoid generic "feta cheese" in plastic tubs unless the label explicitly says "imported from Greece" with a producer name; most domestic supermarket feta is the cow''s-milk imitation.

Storage

Submerged in brine in original container, refrigerated, 4 to 6 weeks unopened, 2 to 3 weeks after opening. If the brine evaporates below the cheese, top off with a 1:10 salt-water solution; exposed feta dries out and develops a yellowed crust. Failure modes: pink or orange surface (mold, discard); strong fermented sour smell beyond the normal cure (over-cure, may still be usable for cooked applications but discard for raw); slimy texture on the surface (microbial breakdown, discard the surface and taste-test the rest); chalky-dry texture in feta that was creamy when bought (dehydration from low brine, soak in fresh brine for 24 hours to recover partial). Vacuum-packed (drained) feta keeps 6 weeks unopened; once opened, transfer to a brine of 1 tbsp salt per cup of water and use within 2 weeks.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • feta (composed salad, plate-display application) → Bulgarian sirene or Israeli white cheese Both are brined sheep-and-goat cheeses functionally identical to feta but legally not "feta" under EU law. Same texture, similar tang, often more accessible and cheaper. The closest substitution.
  • feta (whipped feta or smooth-spread application) → fresh chèvre (goat cheese) + 1 tbsp / 15 g lemon juice + 2 g / ½ tsp salt per 200 g Goat cheese is creamier and easier to whip but lacks feta's distinctive cured tang. The added lemon and salt push it toward feta's flavor profile. Workable for dips and spreads where the visual is plated and the cheese is the dressing rather than a discrete component.
  • feta (everyday cooking, not raw display) → cow's-milk "feta" (Athenos, Valbreso, Wisconsin domestic) A different product, not real feta. Milder, more uniformly crumbly, lacks the sheep-milk musk. Acceptable in cooked applications (baked feta pasta, feta in scrambled eggs) where the heat moderates the flavor anyway. Wrong for raw applications where feta's character is the point.
