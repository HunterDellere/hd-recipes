Espresso beans

Roasted Coffea arabica seeds (with occasional Robusta), ground fine and extracted under ~9 bar pressure. The roast level and grind define everything that happens in the cup.

About

Espresso is not a bean; it is a brew method. The beans are roasted seeds of two coffee species: Coffea arabica (the higher-acid, more aromatic, dominant species for specialty coffee, ~60% of world production) and Coffea canephora (Robusta, higher in caffeine and chlorogenic acids, lower in lipids, used in traditional Italian blends for crema body and price). What makes a bean "espresso" is the roast and the dose: a medium-to-dark development that pushes Maillard browning and partial caramelization of the bean's natural sucrose and chlorogenic acids, ground fine (just-finer than table salt, 200–300 µm particle size), and extracted with ~9 bar pressure of 92–96 °C water in 25–30 seconds.

Composition of the green bean: roughly 11% protein, 12% lipid (the fats that become crema), 50% carbohydrate (predominantly polysaccharides, with ~6–9% sucrose as the reducing-sugar fraction that drives Maillard browning during roast), 2% chlorogenic acids (the bitter-astringent compounds that break down during roast), and 1–2% caffeine. Roasting at 200–230 °C drives off ~12–18% water weight, generates ~700 identified volatile aroma compounds via Maillard and Strecker degradation, caramelizes the sucrose, and breaks chlorogenic acids into quinic and caffeic acids; the latter is the source of espresso's lingering, almost lactic acidity. Dark roasts (French, Italian) push past the second crack at ~225 °C, which volatilizes more of the acid fractions and pushes the flavor toward roasted, smoky, and bittersweet; light roasts pull at ~205 °C and retain the bean's origin-specific aromatic profile (jasmine and bergamot in Ethiopian Yirgacheffe, chocolate and nuts in Brazilian Cerrado, citrus and stone fruit in Kenyan AA).

The modern espresso ratio. Specialty espresso, since ~2010, has converged on a dose-to-yield ratio of 1:2 by weight: 18 g of ground beans extracted to 36 g of liquid in the cup over 25–30 seconds. The older Italian tradition of the espresso ristretto (restricted shot, 1:1.5) and the high-volume lungo (long shot, 1:3) bracket this. Below 1:2 the shot reads concentrated and oily but under-extracted (the bitter compounds extract last and are missing); above 1:2 the shot reads thin and over-extracted (the bitter and astringent late-extraction compounds dominate). 18 g is the dose for a modern double basket; single baskets run 7–9 g, triple baskets 21–22 g. The 18 g/36 g/28 s baseline is the calibration point most espresso recipes assume.

Grind size is the most sensitive variable. Espresso grind is fine but not powder; the particles need to pack densely enough to resist the 9 bar of pressure for the full 25–30 seconds (too coarse, water rushes through in 12 seconds and under-extracts; too fine, the puck chokes and the water can't pass at all). A test: rub the ground coffee between thumb and forefinger; it should feel like fine sand with a slight tackiness, not like talc. Burr grinders (conical or flat) produce a uniform particle distribution; blade grinders produce a bimodal distribution of dust and boulders that extracts unevenly and is unsuitable for espresso. The grind needs to be adjusted for ambient humidity, bean age, and roast level: darker roasts are more brittle and grind finer at the same setting; older beans have lost CO₂ and extract slower (so grind finer).

Freshness and the degassing window. Roasted coffee continuously releases CO₂ for weeks after roasting. The first 2–3 days post-roast, beans release CO₂ so vigorously that espresso shots blow apart (geysering, irregular flow); 7–14 days post-roast is the peak window where the bean has degassed enough to extract evenly but retains enough volatile aromatics for the shot to taste alive. Beyond 4 weeks, the volatile aromatics oxidize and the shot tastes flat and cardboardy. The roast date stamp on the bag is the most important indicator on the label, far more important than any flavor descriptor. Pre-ground supermarket espresso is almost always 6+ months past roast and produces shots that taste recognizably stale.

Crema and what it actually is. The hazelnut-colored foam on a fresh espresso is a stable emulsion of CO₂ (released from the bean under pressure), the bean's natural lipids (12% of roasted weight), and emulsifying proteins. A good crema sits ~3 mm thick, holds for 60–90 seconds, and shows a tiger-stripe pattern of lighter and darker streaks during the pull (the lighter streaks are the dissolved sugars; the darker streaks are the late-extraction compounds). Crema is not a flavor element on its own; it is a visual indicator of freshness, dose, and extraction. A thin or absent crema usually means stale beans, an under-dosed basket, or water too cool. A persistent thick foam that doesn't dissipate after 2 minutes usually means heavy Robusta content (Robusta produces more crema than Arabica due to its higher chlorogenic acid load).

Applications by category. In Italian tradition: straight espresso (single or double), macchiato (espresso "marked" with a teaspoon of milk foam), cortado (espresso cut with equal warm milk, 1:1), cappuccino (espresso with steamed milk and microfoam in a 1:1:1 ratio, classically a morning drink only), and caffè latte (espresso with proportionally more milk, 1:3 or higher). In specialty third-wave coffee: the flat white (espresso with velvety microfoam, no dry foam, lower volume than a latte), the cortado at the more rigid 1:1 ratio, and the espresso tonic (espresso poured over tonic water and ice for a bitter-bright cold drink). In dessert-leaning preparations: affogato (espresso poured over vanilla gelato), tiramisu (espresso-soaked savoiardi), and the broad category of espresso-based dessert drinks that build the bitter-roasted note against sweetened dairy (Filipino ube latte, Vietnamese cà phê sữa đá, Spanish carajillo, Mexican café de olla).

Buying notes. For home espresso the working tier is a small-batch local roastery with a clear roast date stamp, sold whole-bean in 250 g–340 g (12 oz) bags. Reputable national brands (Counter Culture, Intelligentsia, Verve, Onyx, Heart, Tim Wendelboe, Square Mile) ship roast-dated beans within a week of roast. Italian-style traditional blends (Lavazza, Illy) are the canonical heavier-roast espresso blends and produce shots that taste characteristically Italian (more roast-bitter, more crema, less origin-character). Decide between specialty-light-medium (origin-forward, brighter, more acid) and traditional-Italian-medium-dark (roast-forward, more body, more crema) based on what you want in the cup; both produce good espresso. Pre-ground espresso is almost never worth it; if you do not have a grinder, ask the roastery to grind for your machine on the day they ship, accept that the beans will be optimum for 3–4 days only, and freeze the remainder in 50 g portions.

Storage

Whole bean: airtight container at room temperature, away from light and heat, in 50–100 g portions broken from the master bag (each opening accelerates oxidation). Use within 4 weeks of roast date for espresso; the shot starts to taste flat past that window. Freezer for long-term storage: single-portion airtight bags, frozen within 5 days of roast, used directly from frozen (no thawing; frozen beans grind sharper and the cold water in the puck does not appear to harm extraction, and some baristas argue it improves it). Avoid the refrigerator; it cycles humidity and the beans absorb fridge odors. Failure mode: rancid or cardboardy smell, dull color (good espresso beans are glossy with surface oils on darker roasts; dull-matte beans have either oxidized or were never developed past first crack). Ground beans oxidize 50× faster than whole beans; grind to order.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • whole espresso beans (home espresso machine) → pre-ground espresso from a roastery, ground for your machine Acceptable for 3–4 days post-grind, then degrades fast. Ask the roastery to grind on the day they ship and freeze single-portion bags. The compromise on flavor is real but workable; this is the move for an espresso-machine owner without a grinder yet.
  • whole espresso beans (espresso machine, no specialty roastery available) → supermarket Italian-style espresso bag (Lavazza Super Crema, Illy Classico) within roast-date window Traditional Italian blends are formulated for crema and forgiveness. Check the roast date on the bag; if older than 3 months, the beans will produce flat shots. These blends lean roast-bitter and produce more crema than specialty single-origin; the cup tastes more "Italian café" and less "third-wave specialty."
  • espresso (any application, no espresso machine) → Aeropress with espresso-grind beans, inverted method, 18 g to 60 g water, 1-minute steep, press for 25 seconds Produces a coffee concentrate that approaches espresso strength but lacks the pressure-driven crema. Acceptable in latte applications where the milk will mask the missing emulsion; not acceptable where the espresso is consumed straight or where crema is structural (espresso martini, affogato).
  • espresso (any application, no equipment) → Moka pot with finely-ground espresso beans, fresh stovetop preparation The Bialetti tradition. Lower pressure (~1.5 bar vs 9 bar) so no crema and a thinner mouthfeel; the flavor leans more roasted and slightly more bitter from the longer extraction time at lower pressure. The canonical Italian home preparation and a genuine third path between espresso and drip; not a substitute for a real shot but a defensible drink on its own terms.
  • espresso (any application, decaffeinated) → Swiss Water or CO₂-process decaf espresso beans Swiss Water (water-process decaf) and CO₂-process beans retain the most flavor compounds of the decaffeination methods. Methylene chloride or ethyl acetate (the older solvent methods) strip more aromatics and produce flatter shots. Decaf espresso always reads slightly less complex than caffeinated due to caffeine's own bitter contribution; the gap is smaller than most cooks assume but real.
