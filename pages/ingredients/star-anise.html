Ingredient

Star anise

The dried fruit of Illicium verum, eight-pointed and intensely licorice-aromatic. The soul of Chinese five-spice and the unmistakable note in Thai iced tea.

About

Star anise gets its flavor from anethole — the same compound that drives anise seed, fennel, and licorice. There''s a lot of it in star anise (around 80–90% of the essential oil), which is why a single pod perfumes a whole pot.

Toast briefly in a dry pan before using to volatilize the anethole. Adds dimension to braises, broths (pho, master stock), tea infusions, and red-cooked dishes. Pair freely with cinnamon, clove, ginger, and citrus peel.

Buy whole pods, not ground. Whole keeps for a year; ground loses its aromatics in weeks. Crush pods between your fingers when you add them to release the inner seed.

Storage

Airtight jar, dark cool pantry. Whole pods last 1+ year. Discard when they no longer smell sharply of licorice when crushed.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • star anise (braises, broths, five-spice blends) → anise seed Use 1 tsp seed per pod. Similar flavor profile but more delicate — add late.
  • star anise (cold infusions, tea) → fennel seed + a clove Fennel for the licorice, a single clove for the warmth. Approximate but workable.
