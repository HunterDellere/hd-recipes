Ingredient

White pepper

The fully ripe drupe of Piper nigrum with the outer pericarp fermented and washed away, leaving only the inner seed. Floral, fermented, barnyard — a different spice from black pepper, not a milder version of it.

About

Black pepper is the unripe drupe dried whole; white pepper is the same plant taken to full ripeness, soaked for one to two weeks until microbial fermentation breaks down the outer fruit, then rubbed clean and dried. That fermentation is the whole story — it generates skatole and rotundone derivatives that give white pepper its hay-like, slightly funky aroma. It's not a polite stand-in for black pepper; it's a different spice.

Used heavily in Chinese cooking precisely because it disappears visually into pale dishes (egg drop soup, hot and sour soup, dun nai custard, white-cooked chicken) while contributing a low, persistent warmth. The piperine is the same as black pepper, so the heat is comparable, but the aromatic top notes that signal "black pepper" to a Western palate are gone.

Buy whole peppercorns and grind to order. Pre-ground white pepper loses the volatile fermentation aromatics within weeks and reads as dusty, urine-adjacent funk: the smell people complain about when they say they don't like white pepper. Sarawak (Malaysia) and Muntok (Indonesia) are the two reference origins.

Storage

Whole peppercorns, airtight, dark cupboard. 1–2 years. Pre-ground loses aroma in 2–3 months and develops an off note.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • white pepper → black pepper (half quantity) Different aromatic profile: adds visible specks and pine/citrus top notes the dish was avoiding. Acceptable in stir-fries; wrong for pale soups and custards.
  • white pepper → no real substitute in dun nai or egg drop soup The fermented funk is the point. Skip it before you sub black pepper into a delicate custard.
