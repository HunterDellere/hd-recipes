Ingredient

Fresh thyme

Thymus vulgaris. A perennial Mediterranean shrub-herb whose woody-stemmed sprigs carry the most heat-stable aromatic profile in the herb cabinet, thymol and carvacrol, the same phenols found in oregano, that survive long braises where soft herbs disintegrate.

About

Thyme is a low-growing perennial of the Lamiaceae family (the mint family, shared lineage with oregano, marjoram, rosemary, sage, basil, and mint), native to the rocky coastal hillsides of the western Mediterranean. The plant's defining feature is its essential oil, dominated by two structurally related phenolic compounds: thymol (typically 20 to 40% of the oil by mass) and carvacrol (5 to 30%). These same two molecules are the dominant aromatics in oregano; the difference between thyme and oregano lies in the smaller terpene fraction (linalool, geraniol, p-cymene) and in the relative thymol-to-carvacrol ratio. Thyme reads cleaner, more piney, and less aggressively medicinal than oregano; oregano reads sharper, hotter, and more straight-camphor.

Why thyme matters for cooking: heat tolerance. Most fresh culinary herbs are aromatic on the tongue because of volatile terpenes that flash off within 20 to 40 seconds of contact with hot fat or simmering liquid, this is why basil, parsley, cilantro, dill, mint, tarragon, and chives are added at the end of cooking, never at the start. Thyme is the exception. Thymol and carvacrol are phenolic compounds with much higher boiling points (thymol boils at 232 °C, carvacrol at 237 °C) and substantially lower vapor pressures than monoterpenes like linalool or limonene. They survive 3- to 4-hour braises with their character largely intact; they survive overnight stocks; they survive low oven roasts at 150 to 180 °C. This is why thyme is the soft herb of choice for the bouquet garni (the French aromatic bundle dropped into stocks, braises, and long-cooked sauces), why every French braise recipe specifies thyme rather than parsley as the cooking herb, and why thyme is the herb in cassoulet, coq au vin, daube provençale, and beef bourguignon. Parsley reads cleaner at the end of the cook because it's added at the end; thyme is the herb that did the long work in the pot.

Sprigs versus leaves. A "sprig" of thyme is a 4 to 8 cm length of woody stem with several pairs of small ovate leaves attached. The leaves carry most of the aromatic oil; the stems carry some but are tough and inedible. Two preparation modes: leave the sprig intact and remove the stripped stem at the end of cooking (the right move for long braises, stocks, and oil infusions where the leaves can fall off into the dish naturally and the stem is fished out at the end), or strip the leaves off the stem by running thumb and forefinger from the top of the sprig down toward the woody base (the right move for short cooks, pan sauces, dry rubs, and any application where the diner shouldn't encounter the stem on the plate). A bundle of 5 to 8 sprigs is the standard quantity for a 4-portion braise.

Heat behavior in detail. Thyme oil hits its peak aromatic release between 80 and 140 °C, hot enough to volatilize the phenolic and terpene fractions, cool enough to avoid burning them. Above 200 °C (high-temperature oil, the surface of a hot pan, the top of a high-heat roast) the leaves blacken within 30 to 60 seconds and the oil starts to break down into bitter degradation products. The technical implication: thyme can sit in a 150 °C oven all day and contribute steady aromatic depth; thyme thrown onto a smoking pan should be added in the last 30 seconds of the sear and not before. The hardier rosemary, with its more lignified leaf structure, tolerates higher direct-heat exposure than thyme.

The dried-versus-fresh question. Dried thyme retains 50 to 70% of its essential oil if dried correctly (low temperature, dark, airtight) and within 6 months of harvest. After 12 months in a transparent jar over the stovetop, that drops to 15 to 25%. For long-cooked applications (stocks, braises, stews, soups) dried thyme is functionally equivalent to fresh and is the more practical choice; 1 tsp dried = 3 to 4 tsp fresh-stripped leaves. For short-cooked applications (pan sauces, vinaigrettes, butter, finishing) fresh is meaningfully better, the chlorophyll-driven herbaceous notes that dried thyme has lost are still present in the fresh sprig, and the texture of the small fresh leaves reads cleaner than the brittle dried particle.

Applications by category. The French cooking canon, bouquet garni, every long braise, the classic mirepoix-based stock, beurre noisette with thyme as the finishing aromatic, is the deepest single use of the herb. Mediterranean roasting (lamb, chicken, root vegetables, tomatoes) lays thyme sprigs directly on the food before the oven; the leaves crisp and the oil drips onto the food. American steakhouse pan-finish (a hard-seared steak, butter melted in the pan with smashed garlic and 3 to 4 thyme sprigs, the steak basted in the bubbling herby butter) is the canonical finishing application. Sauce work (red-wine reductions, demi-glace, mushroom sauces, pan sauces) wants stripped thyme leaves added early in the reduction. Middle Eastern za'atar uses dried thyme as a structural component alongside sumac and sesame. Italian cooking uses thyme much more sparingly than oregano, mostly in northern braises and game dishes.

Buying. Fresh thyme bunches at the supermarket are usually 20 to 30 g, with 15 to 25 sprigs in the bunch. Look for sprigs with leaves that are uniformly green-to-gray-green, not yellow or wilted; the leaves should grip the stem tightly (loose-shedding leaves mean the bunch is past peak). Snap a stem, fresh thyme stems snap cleanly with a brittle crack and a strong herbaceous smell; old thyme stems bend without snapping and smell faintly of hay. Lemon thyme (Thymus citriodorus) is a closely-related cultivar with citral in the oil; it reads more lemon-zest and is good in fish, chicken, and dessert applications but is not interchangeable with common thyme for braises. Avoid hothouse thyme grown in plastic-clamshell hydroponics, the oil concentration is dramatically lower than field-grown thyme, and the price is higher.

Cross-cuisine reach. French (every cuisine-region application), Mediterranean (Provençal, Spanish, Greek, Lebanese, Italian-northern), American (steakhouse, Cajun cooking, BBQ rubs), Middle Eastern (za'atar, savory pastry). Less common in Asian cooking; Sichuan and Cantonese use the related phenolic profile through different herbs (cassia bark, star anise) but thyme itself is not part of the East Asian aromatic palette.

Storage

Refrigerator at 4 °C in a slightly-damp paper-towel wrap inside an open zip-top bag, sprigs lying flat. Keeps 10 to 14 days. Failure modes: yellowing leaves (oxidation, still usable but flavor reduced), black-spotted leaves on the underside (mold, discard the affected sprigs), mushy stem base (water damage from too-wet wrap, trim and use the upper sprigs). Do not wash thyme until just before using; pre-washed thyme rots within 3 to 4 days. To freeze, strip the leaves off the stems, spread on a parchment-lined tray, freeze flat, then bag, frozen leaves keep aromatic intensity 80 to 90% of fresh for 4 to 6 months and drop straight into hot dishes from frozen. Dried thyme keeps 6 to 12 months in an airtight container away from heat and light; replace annually.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • fresh thyme (long braise, stock, stew) → dried thyme Functionally equivalent for any cook longer than 30 minutes. Use 1 tsp dried = 3 to 4 tsp fresh-stripped leaves (roughly a 1:3 to 1:4 ratio by volume). Dried thyme should be reasonably fresh, within 12 months of opening, or the oil has degraded too far.
  • fresh thyme (finishing pan sauce, butter baste, vinaigrette) → fresh oregano, fresh marjoram, or fresh rosemary in smaller quantity Oregano shares the thymol-carvacrol backbone and reads close on the palate, slightly sharper. Marjoram is the closest aromatic cousin and reads almost identical, slightly sweeter and less piney. Rosemary is much more aggressive; use one-third the volume and add later in the cook. Lemon thyme is a 1:1 swap with a citrus-zest note that reads particularly well on fish and chicken.
  • fresh thyme (bouquet garni, French braise) → savory (Satureja hortensis) or marjoram Savory is the traditional French country-cooking herb for bean dishes and rustic braises before thyme became dominant in the modern canon; it carries a similar phenolic profile. Marjoram works in any bouquet garni context. Both are 1:1 swaps for fresh thyme in this role.
