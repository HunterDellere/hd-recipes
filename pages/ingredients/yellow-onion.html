Ingredient

Yellow onion

The Allium cepa workhorse cultivar with brown-yellow papery skin and high sulfur volatile load. The default cooking onion across Western and many Asian cuisines: sweetens dramatically on long heat, holds together in sautées, and forms the aromatic foundation of soffritto, mirepoix, and German Speckbraten.

About

The yellow onion is the most-produced cultivar group within Allium cepa worldwide and the default "onion" called for when a recipe says onion without qualification. The bulb runs 6 to 10 cm in diameter, weighs 150 to 250 g (medium ~ 170 g), has dry brown-yellow papery outer scales, and a firm white-to-cream interior arranged in concentric scales around a central growing point. The cultivar is bred for storage (the cured papery skin and the high dry-matter content allow 4 to 8 months of room-temperature storage) and for high sulfur volatile production (the pungency that distinguishes yellow onion from sweet or red).

The chemistry. Intact onion cells are odorless; the smell and tear-inducing pungency develop on cutting, when the enzyme alliinase (locked in the cell vacuoles) contacts S-alkenyl cysteine sulfoxide substrates (locked in the cytoplasm), releasing sulfenic acids that rearrange spontaneously into propanethial S-oxide (the lachrymator that makes the cook cry) and a cascade of pyruvate-and-disulfide compounds that produce the pungent aroma. The pyruvate concentration in yellow onion runs 6 to 10 µmol/g (vs 3 to 5 for sweet onion and 5 to 7 for red onion); the higher pyruvate the more pungent the raw onion and the more dramatic the sweetening on heat (the pyruvates and disulfides break down at 60 °C and above, while the residual sugars caramelize).

Cooking applications by category. Aromatic base sweat: fine-diced yellow onion (3 to 5 mm) sweated in butter or oil over medium-low heat for 8 to 12 minutes until translucent and just past raw is the foundation of soffritto (Italian), mirepoix (French), sofrito (Spanish), the trinity (Cajun), refogado (Portuguese), and the German Speckbraten base. The cook is breaking down the raw pungency without yet caramelizing the sugars; the onion reads sweet-and-aromatic but still recognizably onion. Caramelization: medium-dice yellow onion (5 to 10 mm) cooked in butter or oil over medium heat with the lid off for 35 to 75 minutes, stirring every 4 to 6 minutes until the onion collapses to roughly one-quarter its starting volume and reaches a deep mahogany color. The high pyruvate load is structural here; sweet onion caramelizes faster but produces less complex flavor. Pan-fried rings: thick-sliced yellow onion rings (8 to 10 mm), separated, dredged in seasoned flour or batter, fried to deep gold. Raw dice in dressings and salsas: small dice (3 to 4 mm) soaked briefly in acid (lime, vinegar) or rinsed under cold water for 1 minute to denature the harsher sulfur volatiles and shift the onion from sharp-and-aggressive to mild-and-bright while preserving the structural crunch. Whole roasted: medium yellow onion halved across the equator, roasted skin-on at 200 °C / 400 °F for 45 to 55 minutes until the interior collapses into a sweet jammy mass.

Yellow vs red vs sweet. Yellow onion is the default; the cooking-onion swap matrix is meaningful when raw onion is the application or when the cook wants caramelization characteristics other than the yellow profile. Red onion (lower sulfur load, anthocyanin pigment, structural crunch retained on heat) is the canonical raw-application onion (Mexican raw salsas, Greek salads, German Zwiebelringe). Sweet onion (Vidalia, Walla Walla, Maui — low sulfur, high sugar) is the canonical raw eating onion (sliced for burgers, onion rings, French onion soup as an alternative to caramelized yellow) and substitutes for yellow when the recipe wants sweetness without the pungent depth. Shallot (more concentrated allium aromatic, more delicate finish) substitutes for yellow at one-half the volume in dressings, pan sauces, and vinaigrettes where the cook wants the allium note without the volume of vegetable matter.

Buying. Yellow onion should be firm, weighty for its size, with intact dry papery skin and no soft spots, no sprouting green shoot from the top, no mold at the root plate. Avoid bagged supermarket onions with visible mold or wet papery skin; this is moisture damage in storage. The freshest onions are firmest and the heaviest; aging onions lose moisture and read lighter. Storage-cured yellow onions (Holland, Spain, the US Pacific Northwest) keep dramatically longer than fresh-harvest local yellow onions, which is why the supermarket onion is the storage cultivar.

Storage

Cool (10 to 15 °C), dark, dry, ventilated; never refrigerated (the cold converts starch to sugar, accelerates sprouting, and the high humidity inside a refrigerator causes the papery skin to mold). A wire basket or paper bag in a pantry cupboard works; storage life 4 to 8 weeks at proper temperature. Failure modes: green sprouts from the top (still usable, trim the green; the sprout signals the onion is entering dormancy break and the flavor sweetens), soft spot or weeping fluid (cut around it; if more than a quarter of the onion is soft, discard), surface mold on papery skin (peel away the affected scales; if the white interior is firm, the onion is fine), dark-and-mushy throughout (discard). Cut onion holds 5 to 7 days refrigerated in a tightly sealed container or zip bag; the cut surface dries and the onion picks up refrigerator odors past that point.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • yellow onion (aromatic base, soffritto, mirepoix, German Speckbraten) → white onion Slightly more aggressive raw pungency, similar cooking behavior. Acceptable across cooked applications; Latin American cuisines often use white onion where European cuisines use yellow, the substitution is essentially the same vegetable with regional preference.
  • yellow onion (caramelization) → sweet onion (Vidalia, Walla Walla, Maui) Caramelizes faster (15 to 25 minutes less time) due to higher sugar content; the finished flavor is sweeter and less complex (less of the deep umami that yellow onion develops). Acceptable shortcut; yellow is the canonical choice when the cook has time for the full caramelization curve.
  • yellow onion (raw dice in salsa, dressing) → red onion (rinsed or acid-soaked) Red onion is the canonical raw-application choice and reads brighter and less pungent than raw yellow. If only yellow is on hand, rinse the dice under cold water for 1 minute or soak in 1 tbsp lime juice or vinegar for 5 minutes to denature the sharper sulfur volatiles; the function approximates a raw red onion.
  • yellow onion (allium aromatic, when the volume of vegetable matter is not wanted) → shallot, at half the mass Shallot reads as a more concentrated allium with cleaner finish; substitute for yellow at 50 percent of the mass in vinaigrettes, pan sauces, and applications where the cook wants the aromatic without the bulk. Not a substitute in caramelization or soffritto; shallot burns at higher heat and the volume is wrong.
