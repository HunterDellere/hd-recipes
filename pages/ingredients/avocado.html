Ingredient

Avocado

Persea americana, fruit of the avocado tree, dominated commercially by the Hass cultivar. Cold-pressed monounsaturated fat in a creamy parenchyma; ripens off the tree, browns on cut surfaces from polyphenol oxidase activity, and provides structural fat in raw Mexican and Latin American cooking.

About

Avocado is the fruit of Persea americana, a tropical-and-subtropical evergreen tree native to south-central Mexico. The commercially dominant cultivar (worldwide, but especially in the United States) is Hass, a 1926 California-discovered seedling selection that produces a roughly 200 g pebbly-skinned fruit with a single large seed and 15 to 25 percent fat content by weight. Other commercial cultivars (Fuerte, Bacon, Pinkerton, Reed; the West Indian and Guatemalan races overall) are all minor; Hass dominates because it ripens predictably, ships well, and changes color from green to near-black as it ripens, providing a visual ripeness cue that the other cultivars lack.

The fat profile and culinary role. Avocado flesh is roughly 73 percent water, 15 to 25 percent fat, 8 percent fiber, and 2 percent protein, with the fat dominated by oleic acid (the monounsaturated fat that also dominates olive oil). The high fat-to-water ratio in a soft cellular matrix produces the avocado's defining mouthfeel ; creamy without being slick, structural without being dense. In Mexican and Latin American raw cooking, avocado plays the role that olive oil and butter play in Mediterranean cooking: it is the fat that enriches a dish without requiring heat. Guacamole is the canonical example (mashed avocado plus salt-and-acid plus aromatics is functionally a Mexican mayonnaise without the egg), but the same role appears in tortas, tostadas, ensalada de nopales, ceviche, and dozens of taco preparations where the avocado is the dish's structural fat source.

Climacteric ripening. Avocados are climacteric fruit ; they do not ripen on the tree but begin ripening only after harvest, in response to ethylene gas. A green-and-rock-hard avocado at the supermarket is fully developed but not yet ripe; over 3 to 7 days at room temperature it softens, the skin deepens in color, and the flesh transitions from starchy-and-bland to creamy-and-rich. The ripening can be accelerated by storage in a closed paper bag with a banana or apple (both of which produce ethylene) or slowed by refrigeration (which suppresses the ethylene response). Optimal eating ripeness lasts 24 to 72 hours; past that, the flesh develops brown patches around the pit and a slightly fermented note at the cut surface.

Ripeness testing. The single most reliable test is the stem-cap method: pop off the small dried stem cap at the top of the fruit; if the underlying tissue is bright green, the avocado is at or near peak ripeness; if the tissue is yellow-green, the avocado is firm-ripe (good for slicing into salads); if the tissue is brown, the avocado is overripe (still usable for guacamole but not for clean slicing). The squeeze test (gentle pressure with the palm of the hand) is less reliable because avocados can feel firm at the surface while being overripe inside; the stem-cap test cuts straight to the flesh state. Hass avocados are ripe when the skin is dark green-to-near-black; cultivars that stay green when ripe (Fuerte, Bacon, Reed) require the stem-cap test exclusively.

Polyphenol oxidase and browning. Cut avocado flesh begins browning within minutes of exposure to air; the responsible enzyme is polyphenol oxidase (PPO), which oxidizes phenolic compounds in the flesh to brown quinone derivatives. Lime or lemon juice (citric acid plus ascorbic acid) on the cut surface drops the local pH below the PPO activity range and inhibits browning for 1 to 2 hours; airtight wrapping (plastic film pressed directly against the cut surface, no air gap) prevents oxygen access and slows browning to 12 to 24 hours; the dual approach (acid plus tight wrap) buys 24 to 48 hours of acceptable cut avocado. The pit-in-the-bowl trick is folklore that doesn't work; the pit only protects the flesh directly under it from air contact.

Applications by category. Mexican raw cooking (guacamole, ensalada de nopales, taco garnish, tostadas, tortas, sopes, tamales). Latin American raw cooking (Brazilian avocado smoothies, Caribbean salads, Peruvian causa). Sushi and sashimi adapations (California roll, Hawaiian poke). North American adaptation (avocado toast, Cobb salad, BLT-A sandwiches, Tex-Mex everything). High-fat cold soups (gazpacho variants, chilled avocado soup). The fruit travels poorly into hot cooking ; once heated above 60 °C the flesh develops a faint metallic-bitter note that is not present in the raw form, which is why avocado is rarely cooked even in cuisines where it is foundational.

Buying. Look for Hass avocados that yield slightly to gentle pressure but are not soft (firm-ripe is the better choice for most applications because it slices cleanly); the skin should be deep green-to-black with no soft spots, breaks, or mold. Stem cap should be intact (a missing stem cap accelerates ethylene escape and uneven ripening); a green stem cap typically indicates more recent harvest. The fruit should feel heavy for its size, indicating fully developed flesh with full fat content. Underweight avocados are typically harvested early and develop poorly; overweight ones for their size may have an oversized seed.

Storage

Unripe avocado: room temperature, 3 to 7 days until ripe. Acceleration: paper bag with a banana, 1 to 2 days. Ripe avocado: refrigerated whole, 4 to 7 days; the cold temperature suppresses further ripening and slows the transition to overripe. Cut avocado: refrigerated with the cut surface coated in lime juice and pressed against plastic film, 24 to 36 hours; the flesh under the seed (or the half-with-seed) holds slightly better than the seedless half. Mashed avocado (guacamole): refrigerated in a sealed container with plastic film pressed directly on the surface, 24 to 36 hours; loses textural integrity past that point. Frozen avocado puree (with lime and salt added): 6 to 12 months, the texture changes (waterier, smoother) but the flavor holds; suitable for smoothies and dips, not for slicing. Failure modes: brown patches throughout the flesh (overripe; usable for mashing but not slicing); slimy skin (rot, discard); fermented or alcoholic smell at the cut surface (over-old, discard); rubbery flesh that won't soften (chill injury or cold-storage damage; usable but textural disappointment).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • avocado (raw applications, structural fat in salads and tacos) → no good substitute Avocado provides a specific combination of creamy fat texture, mild fruit flavor, and structural cling that no other ingredient duplicates. If avocado is unavailable, drop it from the dish rather than substituting; the salad or taco is fine without it. Mediterranean substitutes (olive oil, full-fat Greek yogurt) shift the dish into a different cuisine register without recovering the texture.
  • avocado (in guacamole when avocado is unripe and unyielding) → half avocado plus 50 g cooked frozen peas, mashed together A common restaurant trick to extend a too-firm avocado. The peas pick up the acid and salt of the dressing and read green-and-creamy enough to pass; the dish loses 20 percent of its avocado-richness but gains a usable texture. Acceptable rescue when the avocado is the issue, not the cook.
  • avocado (in poke bowls and California rolls) → firm mango or firm Asian pear Both cover the soft-fruit-in-rice role; mango brings sweetness, pear brings clean crunch. Different dish but recognizably the same family. Useful when the avocado at the store is rocks-or-mushy with no in-between option.
