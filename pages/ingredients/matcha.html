Ingredient

Matcha

Stone-ground powdered green tea from shade-grown tencha leaves. Bright vegetal-umami flavor, vivid jade color; grade and freshness determine whether it tastes sweet-creamy or grass-and-spinach bitter.

About

Matcha is made by shading Camellia sinensis plants for the final 20 to 30 days before harvest. Shading slows photosynthesis, increases chlorophyll, and dramatically raises L-theanine content (the amino acid responsible for the sweet, creamy, umami-savory edge in good matcha). After harvest the leaves are steamed (to halt oxidation), dried, deveined (the deveined leaf is called tencha), and stone-ground into a fine, talc-like powder. The grinding is slow, a traditional mill produces only ~30 g per hour, which keeps the powder from heating and oxidizing.

Grade is the most important variable a buyer controls. Ceremonial-grade matcha is from young, first-flush tencha; bright jade green, sweet and oceanic, intended for usucha (thin tea) and koicha (thick tea). Premium ceremonial or culinary-ceremonial sits one step down, still drinkable straight, suitable for matcha lattes where the powder is the dominant flavor. Culinary-grade is from later harvests with more oxidation; duller green, more bitter, intended for baking or blending where strong sweet/dairy notes mask the harshness. Buying culinary-grade for a latte produces a grassy, astringent drink; buying ceremonial for a cookie wastes money on aroma compounds that bake off.

Color is the visible quality signal. Vivid jade-green powder is fresh and chlorophyll-rich; olive, yellow-green, or brownish powder is oxidized and over the hill. Real ceremonial matcha smells faintly of fresh-cut grass and seaweed; if it smells of nothing, it is dead.

Whisking is the technique. Sift 2 g (1 scoop / chashaku) of matcha into a warmed bowl, add 60 ml water at 80°C (just below boil; boiling water makes it bitter), and whisk vigorously with a chasen (bamboo whisk) in a Z-pattern for 15 to 20 seconds. The foam should rise into a thick, even crema; if you see clumps, you skipped the sift. A metal whisk works in a pinch but compacts the powder rather than aerating it.

Storage

Airtight tin in the refrigerator, used within 4 to 6 weeks of opening. Light, heat, and oxygen destroy matcha fast. Unopened tins keep 6 months refrigerated; freezing extends to a year but condensation on opening reduces shelf life sharply.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • matcha (ceremonial, usucha, koicha) → no substitute Other green teas brewed and reduced will not provide the suspended powder and chlorophyll mouthfeel.
  • matcha (culinary, bakes, lattes with milk) → sencha powder Cheaper, more bitter, similar color. Acceptable where matcha is one note among several rather than the lead.
