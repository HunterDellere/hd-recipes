Ingredient

Red pepper flakes (crushed red pepper)

Coarse-crushed dried red chilies, typically a blend of cayenne and other thin-walled red varieties at 30,000 to 50,000 Scoville. The default Italian-American pizzeria heat shaker; capsaicin and capsanthin are both lipid-soluble, so the flakes bloom in oil rather than water.

About

Red pepper flakes ("crushed red pepper" in the U.S. spice aisle) is coarse-crushed dried red chili pepper, typically a blend dominated by cayenne (Capsicum annuum) with smaller amounts of other thin-walled red chilies (chiles de arbol, ancho, sometimes guajillo or paprika varieties for color). The crush includes both flesh fragments and the small pale-yellow seeds; the flesh is the source of capsanthin (the red pigment, a carotenoid) and capsaicin (the heat compound, an alkaloid), while the seeds carry additional capsaicin and a small amount of bitter compounds when stale or oxidized. Heat range: 30,000 to 50,000 Scoville Heat Units (SHU) is typical for commercial brands (McCormick, Penzeys, supermarket-aisle); the heat varies batch to batch because the underlying chili supply is agricultural and not controlled to a target SHU.

Functionally, red pepper flakes deliver lipid-soluble heat plus a small amount of caramel-fruit aromatic complexity. Capsaicin is the alkaloid that triggers the TRPV1 receptor (the same receptor that responds to heat); it is fat-soluble, water-insoluble, and survives high temperatures (the flakes can bloom in 175 °C / 350 °F oil without losing potency). Capsanthin is the orange-red carotenoid that colors the flakes and gives any oil they bloom in a deep red color; this is the same pigment that colors paprika and gochugaru. The aromatic compounds (esters and methoxy-pyrazines from the dried chili flesh) are minor compared to the heat but contribute a faint fruity-smoky backnote when the flakes are bloomed in oil; the same flakes added to water-based dishes (a brothy soup, a bivalve steam) read as pure heat with little aromatic complexity.

Transformation. The two operational modes are bloom-in-oil and sprinkle-on-finished. Bloomed in oil: add the flakes to oil heated to 175 to 200 °C (350 to 390 °F), agitate for 30 to 60 seconds, watch for the oil to turn deep red and a faint nutty-smoky aroma to rise; past 90 seconds the flakes scorch and the heat turns acrid with a bitter back-note. This is the technique for chili oil, arrabbiata sauce, and the structural heat layer in many Italian and Italian-American tomato sauces. Sprinkled on at the end: the flakes contribute texture and visual color plus a slow-blooming heat (capsaicin is fat-soluble, so it slowly partitions into the fat in the dish after sprinkling); this is the pizzeria heat-shaker mode, and it works because pizza is fatty enough to extract the capsaicin in the mouth. In a fat-free or water-heavy dish, sprinkling at the end is less effective; the flakes need oil to release their heat.

Applications by category. In Italian cooking, red pepper flakes (peperoncino) are the structural heat in pasta arrabbiata (with tomato, garlic, parsley), in aglio e olio (garlic-oil-pasta), in puttanesca (with anchovies, capers, olives), and in shellfish preparations like spaghetti alle vongole (a small pinch in the garlic-oil bloom); the flakes are aggressive enough that the average Italian preparation uses 0.5 to 1 g per serving. In North-American Italian cooking, the flakes are the pizzeria heat shaker (Italian-American restaurants and pizza joints put red pepper flake shakers on every table; the U.S. preparation is more aggressive than Italian, frequently 1 to 2 g per serving applied at the table) and a structural element in cioppino and Italian-American tomato sauces. In Sichuan and other Asian cuisines, the closer chili options (gochugaru, Kashmiri chili, dried chiles de arbol) typically replace red pepper flakes; the supermarket crushed red pepper reads "Italian" to an Asian palate and is rarely used in those traditions. In Mexican cooking, dried chiles toasted and ground (chile de arbol, guajillo, ancho) are the canonical heat source, not crushed red pepper. Across applications, the rule is consistent: bloom in oil for full heat release, sprinkle at the end for slow-blooming heat in a fatty dish.

Buying notes. Supermarket crushed red pepper (McCormick, Spice Islands) is acceptable for daily cooking and is the default. Penzeys, The Spice House, and Italian specialty markets carry slightly better-quality flakes with more aromatic complexity and a more consistent heat range; the price difference is small. Bulk supermarket bins are the cheapest option but the flakes vary in freshness; trust newer-stock spice shops. For single-variety dried chilies (Aleppo, Marash, Urfa, Calabrian chili, Korean gochugaru, Mexican chile de arbol), use those by name rather than crushed red pepper; the flavor profiles differ substantially. Aleppo pepper (specifically, urfa-biber and Aleppo are not interchangeable) is a Middle Eastern thin-skinned red pepper with a fruity, slow-blooming heat; substituting Aleppo for crushed red pepper changes the dish into a Middle Eastern register.

Western drift. The phrase "red pepper flakes" sometimes triggers confusion in cookbooks where the author means dried ground sweet paprika, not the spicy crushed red pepper aisle product. In an American cookbook, "red pepper flakes" or "crushed red pepper" universally means the spicy Italian-American shaker product. In a Middle Eastern or Mediterranean recipe, "red pepper flakes" sometimes means Aleppo or Marash flakes; check the context.

Storage

Airtight jar in a cool dark cabinet, 1 to 2 years before the heat noticeably declines and the color shifts from bright red to dull rust-brown. Old flakes (3+ years) lose 30 to 50 percent of their capsaicin potency and read flat. Freezer storage extends shelf life but is rarely worth the effort; cabinet storage is fine if the jar is sealed and the spices are turned over every 12 to 18 months.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • red pepper flakes (Italian or Italian-American, blooming in oil for heat) → cayenne pepper (ground) or chiles de arbol (broken) Cayenne is the dominant variety in commercial red pepper flakes anyway; ground cayenne is more concentrated, so use about ¼ to ⅓ the quantity by volume. Broken-up dried chiles de arbol (a Mexican thin-walled red chili) are the closest visual and flavor match.
  • red pepper flakes (Middle Eastern preparation labeled "red pepper flakes") → Aleppo pepper or Marash pepper For a Middle Eastern recipe that calls for "red pepper flakes" non-specifically, the intended ingredient is usually Aleppo or Marash; use those at 1.5x the volume of red pepper flakes (they are less hot and more fruity).
  • red pepper flakes (Korean dish) → gochugaru (Korean coarse chili flakes) Gochugaru is the canonical Korean ingredient; substitute at 2x the volume of red pepper flakes (gochugaru is milder and more fruity, with a longer-blooming heat profile).
  • red pepper flakes (Sichuan dish) → crushed Sichuan-dried er-jin-tiao chili or chiles de arbol Sichuan dried chilies (er-jin-tiao, facing-heaven, or chao tian jiao) are the canonical heat in Sichuan cooking; chiles de arbol are the closest Western-available substitute. Crushed red pepper reads "Italian" in a Sichuan dish.
