Ingredient

Pear

Pyrus communis (European) and Pyrus pyrifolia (Asian). Cultivar choice, Bartlett, Bosc, Anjou, Comice, Asian, drives texture, sugar, and cooking behavior more than ripeness alone.

About

Pears are a climacteric fruit (they ripen after picking) and the texture-sugar curve depends entirely on cultivar and stage. Unlike apples, which are eaten across a wide ripeness window, pears have a narrow ideal-eating window, often 3 to 5 days, and shift from rock-hard underripe to mealy overripe quickly once they start. Choosing the right cultivar for the right application is the entire technique of pear cookery.

The five cultivars to know:

  • Bartlett (Williams in Europe): bell-shaped, yellow when ripe, classic pear silhouette. Sweet, juicy, soft when fully ripe with a noticeable ripe-pear aroma. The default raw-eating pear and the standard for pear pies, poached pears in red wine, and canning. Holds shape moderately well in a brief cook (15 to 25 minutes); falls apart on long cooks. Press gently at the stem-end: ripe Bartletts give to thumb pressure.

  • Bosc: elongated, russet-brown skin, dense crisp flesh that softens slowly. Sweeter than Bartlett with a denser, less-juicy texture. The right pear for roasting, baking, poaching, and any cooked preparation where holding shape matters; the russet skin is edible and contributes a slightly nutty character to roasted preparations. Bosc is the canonical pear for poached-pear-in-red-wine and for the pear-and-blue-cheese tartine. Press at the neck (just below the stem): a ripe Bosc gives to firm thumb pressure but is still noticeably firm.

  • Anjou (red and green): egg-shaped, smooth pale-green or red skin. Crisper than Bartlett, sweeter than Bosc, with a clean white flesh. Versatile but excels at neither raw nor cooked exclusively. The right pear when the cultivar is "whatever's available" or for raw applications where slicing matters (salads, charcuterie boards, tartines). Press at the stem-end like Bartlett.

  • Comice: rounder, fatter, yellow-green with red blush. The sweetest pear cultivar, with an extremely tender flesh and intensely floral aroma. Premium pear; the canonical "buttery pear" of fruit-and-cheese plates. Too soft to cook; eat raw, sliced, or paired with cheese. Doesn't keep more than a week even refrigerated.

  • Asian pear (apple pear, nashi): round like an apple, golden-russet or yellow skin, dense crisp flesh that holds shape under any treatment. Crunchy, mildly sweet, juicy. Eaten raw and used in Korean cooking (galbi marinade, naengmyeon broth) for its enzymatic tenderizing effect on meat. Holds shape in cooked preparations but loses much of its charm; raw use is the rule.

Ripening control. Underripe pears (rock-hard, no give) are common in supermarket displays; pears are picked underripe deliberately for shipping and shelf life. To ripen at home, leave on the counter at room temperature 3 to 7 days; check daily by pressing the stem-end. To accelerate, place in a paper bag with a banana or apple (the ethylene speeds the climacteric ripening). To slow, refrigerate immediately on purchase (cold suppresses ripening; bring back to room temperature 1 to 2 days before eating). The ripening window is narrow: a pear that gives slightly at the stem today will be soft and starting to brown internally in 2 days. Plan accordingly.

Cooking behavior. Pears poached in liquid (wine, syrup, juice) maintain shape in the cultivars chosen for it (Bosc, Anjou) and absorb flavor deeply through their porous flesh. Pears roasted whole or halved at 200°C / 400°F caramelize the sugar at the cut faces and concentrate the flavor; Bosc is the canonical roasted-pear cultivar. Pears sliced and pan-seared in butter develop a thin caramelized crust and pair classically with pork, duck, and game. Pears baked into tarts and galettes need to be sliced thin enough to cook through (3 to 4 mm) but not so thin they collapse; Bosc and Anjou are the cultivars that hold up. Bartlett and Comice fall apart in any extended cook; eat them raw.

Pairing logic. Pears pair famously with blue cheese (gorgonzola, roquefort, stilton): the sweet, slightly grainy flesh contrasts the salty, sharp, fatty cheese, and the acidity of a ripe pear cuts the cheese's richness. The pairing extends across formats, raw slices on a cheeseboard, on a tartine with melted gorgonzola, in a salad with arugula and walnuts, and in pear-blue-cheese soup. Pears also pair with prosciutto and other cured pork (sweet-fat-salt logic), with walnuts and pecans (texture and roasted-nut sweetness against the soft flesh), and with red wine and aged balsamic (acid against sugar). The classic five-ingredient pear-and-cheese plate (pear + gorgonzola + walnut + balsamic + thyme or arugula) is essentially a complete dish in 5 minutes of assembly.

Buying notes. Look for pears with stems intact, no bruises, no soft spots, and a fresh-looking blossom-end (the bottom). Skin should be unblemished or evenly russetted (Bosc); spotty russet on a non-Bosc cultivar means damage. Avoid pears that are already very soft, the ripening window has likely passed. The neck (the narrow part below the stem) should give slightly to thumb pressure on a ripe pear; the body should still be firm. Buying pears 3 to 5 days ahead of when you plan to use them gives the cook control over ripeness; buying them already ripe is a gamble against the narrow window.

Storage

Underripe pears: countertop at room temperature, 3 to 7 days to ripen. Once ripe, refrigerated in a paper or perforated plastic bag: 5 to 7 days. Asian pears keep dramatically longer (refrigerated, 1 to 3 months from purchase) because they don't follow the same climacteric pattern. Cut pears, in lemon water or sealed in a container with a touch of acid: 1 to 2 days; cut pears brown rapidly from polyphenol oxidase. Off-cues are large brown patches on the skin (small bruises are fine and cuttable away), shriveled or wrinkled skin, and a fermented or vinegary smell.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • Bosc (cooked, holds shape) → Anjou or under-ripe Bartlett Anjou is closest in cooking behavior. Under-ripe Bartlett (still firm) holds shape better than ripe Bartlett but loses the buttery cooked texture.
  • Bartlett or Comice (raw, soft sweet pear) → ripe Anjou Less floral, less buttery, but covers the soft-sweet-raw function adequately.
  • Asian pear (raw, crunchy, Korean marinades) → Granny Smith apple Different fruit, similar crisp-juicy-tart profile and similar enzymatic activity. Acceptable for galbi marinade; the sweetness reads slightly sharper.
  • pear (cheeseboard, raw with cheese) → fresh fig (in season) or crisp apple Different fruit, similar sweet-soft-pairs-with-cheese function. Figs are closer in textural softness; apples are closer in seasonality and availability.
