Ingredient

Green chili (Indian hari mirch)

Fresh unripe Capsicum annuum or C. frutescens cultivars (hari mirch, finger chili, serrano-adjacent) used across South Asian and Latin American cooking as the bright capsaicin source ; sharp, vegetal, and hot at 30,000 to 50,000 Scoville. Different ingredient from dried red chili.

About

"Green chili" in South Asian cooking refers to fresh unripe chili pods of several Capsicum cultivars, most commonly hari mirch (a slim 6 to 10 cm green chili of the C. annuum species), used at the immature green stage before the pod ripens to red. Across Latin American kitchens, the equivalent role is filled by serrano (C. annuum, 5 to 8 cm) and jalapeño (C. annuum, 5 to 9 cm, milder); in Southeast Asian cooking, by Thai bird chili (C. frutescens, 2 to 3 cm, much hotter). Heat varies by cultivar but green chili in the South Asian context typically lands at 30,000 to 50,000 Scoville Heat Units, similar to serrano and noticeably hotter than jalapeño. The defining sensory profile is a bright vegetal-grassy top note from the unripe pyrazines and cis-3-hexenal, followed by a sharp throat-front capsaicin heat ; very different from the smoky-fruity profile of dried red chili.

Capsaicin is the active heat compound, a vanilloid alkaloid concentrated in the placental tissue (the white pithy ribs inside the pod) and to a lesser extent the seeds. Capsaicin binds to TRPV1 thermal-pain receptors and triggers a heat sensation without any actual temperature change. The compound is fat-and-alcohol-soluble but only weakly water-soluble, which is why drinking water does little for chili-burn while milk, yogurt, or beer help (casein and ethanol both displace capsaicin from the receptor). For the cook, the practical implication is that seeding a green chili (scraping out the placental ribs and seeds with a teaspoon) reduces the heat by 60 to 80% while preserving most of the vegetal aromatic ; the seeds-and-ribs are the heat, the flesh is the flavor.

Fresh green chili and dried red chili are different ingredients, not stages of the same one. Fresh green chili contributes the bright vegetal-capsaicin profile; dried red chili (the same pod ripened, dried, and either kept whole or ground to powder) contributes a smoky-fruity-tannic profile from the Maillard reaction during ripening and drying, plus the concentrated capsaicin of dehydration. Recipes that call for one and substitute the other read flat in opposite directions ; a kachumber made with red chili powder reads dusty and one-dimensional, a chili-laced stew built on raw green chili reads thin and grass-forward without backbone.

Heat applications. Raw, finely minced green chili in salads (kachumber, ceviche, salsa fresca, raita), tempered briefly in oil at the start of dal or stir-fry as a base aromatic before the cumin seeds, slit lengthwise and dropped whole into curry to release heat slowly during simmer, blended into chutney (cilantro chutney, mint chutney) for fresh-bright heat. Avoid in long braises; the bright top note is fragile and cooks out within 20 minutes of simmering, leaving only the bare capsaicin without the supporting aromatic ; long-cooked applications belong to dried red chili.

Buying notes. At Indian grocers, hari mirch is sold loose by the pound, deep glossy green and slim. At Latin grocers, serrano is the closest analog. Avoid pods with soft spots, brown streaks, or wrinkled skin (water loss past the freshness window). The capsaicin level varies pod-to-pod even within the same harvest; tasting a small slice before committing to the recipe quantity is a real practice in Indian home cooking. Heat sensitivity also varies dramatically by the individual cook and eater; first-time use of a new bag of green chili calls for a smaller dose and a build-up.

Storage

Refrigerator crisper, loose in a paper bag (not plastic ; the moisture trapped in plastic accelerates rot at the stem end): 1 to 2 weeks at peak. Whole green chilies can be frozen in a sealed bag for 3 to 6 months; freezing slightly softens the texture but capsaicin is heat-stable and the cooked applications (curry, stir-fry, chutney, dal tadka) work fine with frozen pods. Do not freeze for raw applications ; the cellular crunch is gone and the chili reads slumped and grassy. Off-cue is a soft, water-leaking pod with brown streaks at the stem; discard.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • green chili (raw applications, kachumber, ceviche, salsa) → serrano (1:1) or jalapeño (1.5:1, seeded) Serrano matches the heat profile and pod size of Indian hari mirch closely. Jalapeño is milder; use roughly 1.5 jalapeño per Indian green chili and seed if using whole, otherwise the dish reads underheated. Thai bird chili (1:3, much smaller and hotter) also works in raw applications; reduce quantity sharply.
  • green chili (cooked applications, dal tadka, stir-fry) → serrano or jalapeño, slit lengthwise For tempered or simmered applications, the substitution is straightforward; serrano gives the closer heat-and-vegetal profile. Skip the seeds if the cook wants a milder dish.
  • green chili (blended chutney, cilantro chutney) → fresh poblano (mild, larger), or jalapeño Poblano is much milder and adds a richer vegetal note; the chutney loses heat but gains body. Acceptable for non-Indian eaters or for kid-friendly versions.
