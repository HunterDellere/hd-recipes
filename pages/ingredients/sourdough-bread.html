Ingredient

Sourdough bread

A naturally-leavened wheat bread risen by a wild-yeast and lactic-acid bacteria culture rather than commercial yeast. Open crumb, chewy crust, characteristic acetic-and-lactic tang.

About

Sourdough bread is wheat bread leavened by a symbiotic culture of wild yeast (most commonly Saccharomyces or Candida species) and heterofermentative lactic-acid bacteria (Lactobacillus sanfranciscensis is the canonical species in the San Francisco tradition; many regional cultures host different strains). The starter (a perpetual culture of flour, water, and the live microbial community) is fed daily or weekly, and a portion is mixed into a fresh dough as the leavening agent. Commercial yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae as a single-strain monoculture) does not appear; the wild yeast does the gas production, and the lactic bacteria produce both lactic acid (the soft yogurt-tang character) and acetic acid (the sharper vinegar-tang character) as fermentation byproducts. The acid load is what gives sourdough its name and its keeping properties.

Flavor chemistry. The defining flavor difference between sourdough and commercial-yeast bread is the lactic-and-acetic acid character produced during the long fermentation. The two acids exist in different ratios depending on hydration, temperature, and starter age: cool, stiff doughs favor acetic acid (sharper, vinegar-like, the San Francisco profile); warm, wet doughs favor lactic acid (softer, yogurt-like, the European country bread profile). Beyond the acids, the long fermentation time (12 to 36 hours total versus 1 to 3 hours for commercial yeast) gives the wheat enzymes time to break down starch into maltose and glucose (which contribute sweetness and Maillard substrate), to hydrolyze gluten proteins into smaller fragments and free amino acids (which contribute umami and Maillard substrate), and to release vitamins and minerals bound in phytic-acid complexes (which improves nutrition). The crust character (deep mahogany, blistered, with intense Maillard browning) reflects this elevated free-sugar and free-amino-acid load.

Crumb and crust structure. A well-made country sourdough has an open, irregular crumb with visible large holes (the result of high hydration plus strong gluten development plus long bulk fermentation), a chewy crust (formed during a hot bake on a stone or in a cast-iron Dutch oven), and a soft pliable interior that holds together under butter and slicing. Hydration ranges from 70 percent (a lean country loaf) to 85 percent or higher (a more open ciabatta-style loaf). Higher hydration produces more open crumb but is harder to shape and bake. Bakers regulate hydration and fermentation length to target a specific texture: a high-hydration, long-fermented loaf for sandwich and dipping bread; a stiffer, shorter-fermented loaf for slicing bread.

Regional variants. San Francisco sourdough is the canonical American style, sharper and more acetic than European sourdoughs, traditionally credited to the cool, foggy climate of the Bay Area which favors acetic-acid-producing Lactobacillus strains; modern research suggests the original microbe combination is achievable elsewhere with cool fermentation. European country sourdoughs (French pain au levain, Italian pane di Altamura, German bauernbrot) are typically gentler and more lactic, with regional flour varieties (durum in Altamura, rye in Bavarian and Eastern European traditions) shaping the crumb. Russian and Eastern European borodinsky is a dark rye sourdough with coriander and molasses; Estonian and Finnish ruisleipä is a dense rye-only sourdough fermented for several days. American whole-wheat country sourdough is a recent (1990s onward) tradition descended from Tassajara Bread Book and Chad Robertson's Tartine bakery: dense, deeply fermented, often 30 to 40 percent whole-grain.

Applications by category. Daily bread: sliced for toast, sandwich, or dipping in olive oil and salt; the canonical European-American everyday loaf. Croutons: torn day-old sourdough, fried in oil with garlic and salt, the structural crouton of Caesar salad and panzanella. Bruschetta and crostini: sliced, grilled, rubbed with garlic, topped with tomato or other; works because the dense crumb and chewy crust hold up to wet toppings. Bread soup (panzanella, ribollita, pappa al pomodoro): stale sourdough soaked in tomato juice, in vinaigrette, or in vegetable broth as the structural starch. Bread pudding: stale sourdough soaked in custard, baked into a sweet or savory pudding. Tartines: thick-sliced toasted sourdough as the platform for any topping (avocado, pâté, smoked fish, cheese). Stuffing: cubed and dried sourdough as the base for Thanksgiving-style bread stuffing. Bread crumbs: dried and ground; the lactic acidity gives the crumbs more flavor than commercial breadcrumbs.

Day-old versus fresh. Fresh sourdough is for eating with butter, with olive oil, with cured meat, with cheese: the soft pliable crumb and warm interior are the point. Day-old to three-day-old sourdough is the right bread for croutons, panzanella, ribollita, French toast, bread pudding, and any oven-crisping application. The staling does two things: it dries the crumb to roughly 22 percent moisture (from 35 to 38 percent in fresh bread), which means the bread crisps cleanly in the oven without going leathery, and the staling also recrystallizes the starch (retrogradation), which gives day-old sourdough its characteristic firmer chew when toasted. Sourdough stales more slowly than commercial-yeast bread because the acetic acid inhibits mold and bacteria; a country sourdough stays edible at room temperature for 4 to 6 days where a commercial-yeast loaf would need to be eaten or frozen by day 2.

Buying. The reliable signal at a bakery or grocer is the loaf weight and the crust character: a real country sourdough at 800 g to 1 kg per loaf, a deeply browned mahogany crust with visible blistering, and a slightly tangy aroma when sniffed. The "sourdough" label is unregulated in the United States and the United Kingdom: supermarket "sourdough" sandwich loaves are typically commercial-yeast breads with added vinegar or fermented-flour flavor packets to fake the tang. The honest test is to look at the cut crumb (open and irregular, not uniform), to feel the weight (denser per volume than commercial yeast), and to taste for the lactic-and-acetic depth that no flavor packet replicates. Real bakeries (neighborhood bakeries, farmers' markets, dedicated bakery counters) are the reliable source.

Storage

Cut-side down on a cutting board at room temperature for 2 days, then in a paper bag (never plastic, which traps moisture and softens the crust) for up to 4 days. Refrigeration accelerates staling and makes the crumb leathery; never refrigerate sourdough. For longer storage, slice and freeze in a zip-top bag for up to 3 months; toast directly from frozen. Failure modes: visible mold (rare in true sourdough, discard the loaf entirely if seen); leathery crumb (refrigerated by accident; only useful for croutons or breadcrumbs now); fully dry stale bread (perfect for breadcrumbs or panzanella, useless for sandwich).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • sourdough bread (for croutons and panzanella) → rustic country bread, ciabatta, or pain de campagne Any open-crumb, lean wheat bread with a chewy crust works mechanically; the lactic tang of a real sourdough is missed but the structural role is filled.
  • sourdough bread (for sandwich and toast) → commercial-yeast country bread or pain de mie A good commercial-yeast country bread is a clean substitute for everyday sandwich and toast applications; sandwich loaves are softer and less open but functional.
  • sourdough bread (for bread pudding and stuffing) → brioche, challah, or any dense egg-enriched bread Egg-enriched breads soak custard differently (denser and more uniformly than the open-crumb sourdough) but produce a legitimately different and often richer pudding or stuffing.
