Ingredient

Scallion (green onion)

Allium fistulosum or young A. cepa. Mild oniony bite raw, sweet and almost leek-like when seared.

About

Scallions are two ingredients in one stalk. The white and pale-green base behaves like a mild onion — pungent allyl compounds when raw, softens and sweetens with heat. The dark green tops are more delicate, more grassy, and largely wilt rather than caramelize; they're best raw or added at the very end as a garnish for color and a clean herbal lift.

The conventional move is to slice both parts and use them for different jobs in the same dish: whites with the aromatics at the start of cooking, greens scattered raw at the finish. For stir-fries, slicing on a sharp bias maximizes surface area and visual contrast; for braises and slow-cooks, leaving them in 5 cm batons is fine.

Don't conflate scallions with leeks (much larger, woodier base, hold up to long braising), chives (much thinner, herb-tier flavor), or spring onions in the British sense (which are sometimes thicker scallions, sometimes young bulb onions). The American "green onion" is the standard A. fistulosum here.

Choose stalks with crisp, dark green tops and bright white bases. Slimy tops or yellowing bases mean past prime.

Storage

Refrigerator crisper, loose in a paper bag or upright in a glass with the roots in 1 cm of water. 7–10 days. Roots regrow if you replant the white base in soil.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • scallion (raw garnish, finishing herb-tier lift) → chives, finely sliced Closest visual and aromatic match for the green tops. Lacks the white-base body, so wrong for any cooked application.
  • scallion white parts (cooked aromatic base, stir-fry start) → shallot, thinly sliced Stronger, sweeter, more onion-forward when cooked. Use about 60% the amount; brings more sugar to the Maillard reaction.
  • scallion (general all-purpose, when only one form is on hand) → spring onion (true bulb-form young onion) Bulb is meatier and slightly stronger; greens behave the same. Most British "spring onions" are actually scallions — check what you have.
