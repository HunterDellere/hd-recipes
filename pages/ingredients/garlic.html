Ingredient

Garlic

Allium sativum. Pungency comes from allicin, a sulfur compound that only forms when cells are damaged. How you cut a clove decides how it tastes.

About

A single clove sitting on the counter has almost no smell. The bite arrives only after damage. Inside intact cells, sulfur-containing alliin sits in one compartment and the enzyme alliinase sits in another. Cut, crush, or grate the clove and the two meet, and within seconds alliinase converts alliin into allicin, the volatile sulfur compound responsible for the sharp, hot, sulfurous bite. The more cell walls you break, the more allicin you make. This is the single chemistry rule that drives everything cooks do with garlic.

The cut matters more than the cook. A whole clove poached in milk or olive oil is sweet, mild, and almost nutty because the alliinase never gets to do its work. A clove smashed once with the side of a knife and tossed into a braise contributes background warmth without sharpness. A finely minced clove gives moderate punch. A microplane-grated clove or one pressed through a garlic press hits hardest, because the cell damage is total and surface area is maximized. Two cloves prepared three different ways produce three different ingredients, all called "garlic" on a recipe card.

Heat tames it fast. Allicin starts breaking down within seconds in hot oil and is mostly gone after thirty seconds of sweating. Past golden, the residual sugars caramelize and the dish gains a sweet, toasty depth. Past golden-brown, the sugars and remaining sulfur compounds tip into bitter, acrid territory; this is the failure mode in stir-fries where garlic hits the pan first and overcooks while the rest of the mise is still being added. Adding garlic later, or to slightly cooler oil, is the fix.

Whole-clove cooking is its own technique tree. Roasted heads turn into a sweet spreadable paste because slow gentle heat caramelizes sugars without ever generating much allicin. Confit cloves poached in oil at 80–90 °C take on a nutty, almost candied character; the oil itself becomes a finishing flavor. Black garlic, fermented at low heat for weeks, is the same enzyme system run backwards into Maillard territory: balsamic, savory, slightly fruity, with no harshness left.

Pre-minced jarred garlic is a different ingredient. The grinding happens at the factory, the allicin breakdown stalls in acid (citric or phosphoric, used as preservatives), and what reaches the kitchen is the residue of degraded sulfur compounds. The flavor reads flat, slightly metallic, and one-note. The same charge applies to garlic powder, which is dehydrated post-cut and missing the volatile fraction; granulated garlic is fine for spice rubs and pickle brines but not a stand-in for fresh in anything where garlic is supposed to ride above the dish.

At the store, look for hard, tight heads with papery dry skins and no green sprouts. A green core inside the clove is fine to eat but bitter when raw; halve and remove it for raw applications (aioli, dressings, salsa verde) and leave it in for braises and roasts. Hardneck varieties (Music, German Red, Chesnok) carry more complex flavor but smaller clove counts; softneck (California Early, Inchelium Red) is what most supermarkets stock and stores longer.

Storage

Cool, dark, dry, with airflow. Whole heads keep 1–2 months in a mesh bag or open ceramic crock at room temperature. Refrigerator turns cloves rubbery and accelerates sprouting; only use the fridge for already-broken heads. Once a head is broken open, individual cloves last 1–2 weeks. Failure mode is sprouting (still usable, mildly bitter), then softening, then dark spots that signal mold. Peeled cloves submerged in oil in the fridge are a botulism risk past a week unless acidified; freeze instead.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • fresh garlic (raw applications: aioli, dressings, salsa verde) → no real substitute The point of raw garlic is the active allicin. Powder, granulated, and jarred all start without it. Skip the dish or use a different aromatic.
  • fresh garlic (cooked applications: braises, sauces, sautés) → granulated garlic, in the same dish Use about 1/4 tsp granulated per fresh clove. Adds the cooked-garlic depth but none of the bite. Acceptable when fresh is unavailable.
  • fresh garlic (long braises, slow-cooked sauces) → roasted garlic puree Sweeter, mellower, no harshness. Use 1 tsp per clove called for. Shifts the dish toward background sweetness; fine in tomato sauces, soups, and braises that already have other aromatic punch.
  • minced fresh garlic → crushed and chopped fresh garlic Same clove, milder finish. Crushing once and rough-chopping breaks fewer cells than mincing, so less allicin. Useful when the recipe written for "minced" reads too sharp at the table.
