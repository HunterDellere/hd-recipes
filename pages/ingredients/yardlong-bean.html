Ingredient

Yardlong bean (long bean)

Long, thin, slightly tough green beans (Vigna unguiculata subsp. sesquipedalis), 30 to 60 cm long, in the cowpea family rather than the green-bean (Phaseolus vulgaris) family. Used across Southeast Asian, southern Chinese, Bangladeshi, and West African cooking where their dense walls and slightly nutty flavor hold up to high-heat stir-frying, pounding, and long-simmer braising.

About

Yardlong beans (also called long beans, snake beans, asparagus beans, or by the Mandarin chang dou and the Thai thua fak yao) are immature pods of Vigna unguiculata subspecies sesquipedalis, the same species as the southern-US cowpea and African black-eyed pea. They are not closely related to common green beans (Phaseolus vulgaris) despite the visual similarity, and the difference matters in cooking. Yardlong bean walls are denser, slightly fibrous, and more resistant to overcooking than common green beans, which is why Thai cooks pound them in the mortar for som tum (where common green beans would mush) and Sichuan cooks dry-fry them at high heat for 8 to 10 minutes (where common beans would scorch on the outside before cooking through). The pods reach 30 to 60 cm in length and are harvested when slim and tender, before the seeds inside have fully developed.

Functional role across cuisines. Thai/Lao/Cambodian: pounded into som tum dressing alongside the green papaya, where the bruise releases the bean's slight grassy-nutty character into the fish-sauce-lime-palm-sugar liquid; also stir-fried in Thai pad pak ruam (mixed vegetable stir-fry), wrapped raw alongside herbs in Lao tum mak hoong, and added to red and green curries for the last 5 minutes. Southern Chinese (Cantonese, Sichuan, Hokkien): dry-fried in Sichuan gan bian si ji dou with pork and preserved vegetable, the high-heat dry-fry technique blistering the bean walls and concentrating the flavor; stir-fried with fermented black beans (douchi); braised with pork. Filipino: in adobong sitaw (long-bean adobo) and in pinakbet (mixed vegetable stew). Bengali, Bangladeshi, Indian (eastern): in begun bori, in sukto, in stir-fried preparations with mustard seed and turmeric; the bean's slight nuttiness suits the panch phoron and mustard-oil pantry. West African (Nigerian, Cameroonian): in stews and porridges where the closely-related cowpeas are also used; the long-bean form is less common than the dried bean.

Dense walls, why they matter. Common green beans (Phaseolus vulgaris) have relatively thin cell walls and a high water content (~90%), they cook quickly (3 to 5 minutes blanched) and turn floppy when overcooked. Yardlong beans have noticeably thicker, more pectin-rich cell walls (closer to 88% water), they need 6 to 8 minutes blanched, hold their shape under longer cooking, and brown rather than steam in dry-heat applications. This is what makes them the right structural bean for som tum: a bruised long bean splits along its length, releases interior gel and seeds into the dressing, and absorbs the liquid into the fractured walls. A bruised common green bean disintegrates instead.

Color and visible cues. Yardlong beans range from pale-green ("white" varieties, the Thai default) to deep dark green to red-purple ("red snake bean" varieties popular in Indonesia). The pale-green and dark-green varieties cook indistinguishably; the red-purple variety bleeds purple into pickling liquids and stir-fry sauces (visually striking but flavor-equivalent). Choose beans that are firm, cool to the touch, and snap when bent, soft, rubbery, or fully limp beans are old. The seeds visible through the wall should be small and just-formed; large, prominent seeds mean the bean is past its prime tenderness window.

Buying notes. Sold by the bunch (typically 8 to 12 beans tied together with twist tie or rubber band) at Asian markets, especially Chinese and Southeast Asian. The bunch typically weighs 200 to 400 g. Many supermarkets stock Chinese long beans seasonally; Thai markets stock them year-round. Choose bunches where the beans are uniform in color and firmness, with no soft brown patches at the cut ends. Long beans wilt visibly within a day or two off-store, so the bunch should look fresh and the cut ends should not be browning.

Substitution. Common green beans (haricots verts or standard string beans) are the closest substitute and work in most applications, but with caveats: in som tum, the bean is pounded raw and needs the long-bean wall density to survive without disintegrating, common green beans can be used but need shorter pounding (3 to 4 strikes versus 4 to 6) and reads slightly less structural. In dry-fried Sichuan applications, common beans scorch before they char-blister; lower the heat and shorten the cook time. In curries and braises, common green beans work but cook 30% faster.

Storage

Refrigerated in a sealed plastic bag in the crisper drawer: 5 to 7 days from purchase, with quality declining steadily. The beans wilt and soften before they spoil; once they bend rather than snap, they are still usable for cooked applications but no longer right for raw-pounded or pickle uses. Frozen blanched (2 minutes in salted boiling water, ice-bath shocked, dried, frozen flat on a sheet pan, transferred to bags): 6 months, acceptable for cooked applications, suboptimal for raw. Spoilage cues: soft slimy patches (especially at cut ends), visible mold, fermented or sour smell. Yellowing along the bean is a chill-damage indicator from over-cold refrigeration; the bean is still usable but the flavor is muted.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • yardlong bean (Thai som tum, raw pounded applications) → haricots verts (slim French-style green beans) Closest fresh substitute in Western markets. Pound 30% less aggressively (3 to 4 strikes vs 4 to 6) to avoid mushing the thinner walls. The dish reads slightly less structural and slightly more sweet-grassy.
  • yardlong bean (Thai/Sichuan stir-fry, curry) → standard green beans (string beans) Cook 30% faster. The flavor is approximately right but slightly more sweet-grassy and less nutty. Acceptable workhorse substitute when long beans are unavailable; the dish is recognizably present.
  • yardlong bean (Sichuan dry-fried) → asparagus, trimmed and cut into 4 cm pieces Different vegetable but produces a similarly textured Sichuan dry-fried dish, the asparagus walls blister and char similarly under dry heat. Loses the distinctly bean-y flavor; gains a slight grassy-asparagus note. Acceptable cross-substitution.
