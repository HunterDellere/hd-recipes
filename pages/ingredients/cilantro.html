Ingredient

Cilantro (coriander leaves)

The leaves and stems of Coriandrum sativum, used fresh as a finishing herb across Latin American, South Asian, Southeast Asian, Middle Eastern, and Chinese cooking. Stems carry more flavor than leaves and should not be discarded.

About

Cilantro is the leaf-and-stem form of Coriandrum sativum; the seed of the same plant is sold dried as coriander seed and is a structurally different ingredient (different aroma compounds dominate the seed vs the leaf). British and Commonwealth English calls the fresh herb "coriander" or "fresh coriander"; American English uses "cilantro" (the Spanish name) for the leaf and reserves "coriander" for the seed. The same green-leafed plant is reached for from Mexico to India, Vietnam, China, Lebanon, and Iran, which is rare for a single herb.

The soapy-gene problem. About 4 to 14% of the global population (varying by ancestry, higher in East Asian and European populations, lower in Latin American and South Asian) carries a variant of the OR6A2 olfactory receptor gene that makes the aldehyde compounds in cilantro (especially trans-2-decenal, an unsaturated aldehyde) smell soapy or metallic rather than fresh and citrusy. This is real, genetic, and not a preference; cilantro tastes fundamentally different to soap-tasters than to non-soap-tasters. The mitigations are limited: bruising, chopping, and crushing the leaves volatilizes some of the offending aldehydes; long cooking degrades them; salt-and-acid treatment in chimichurri-style preparations also helps. Some soap-tasters find their reaction diminishes with repeated exposure, others do not. If a recipe is being cooked for a known soap-taster, substitute parsley.

Stems carry more flavor than leaves. The stems of cilantro contain a higher concentration of the citrusy aroma compounds than the leaves do, and chopping stems-and-leaves together rather than picking off only leaves produces a more aromatic dish. Thai cooking goes further, treating cilantro stems as a discrete ingredient (rakphakchee) ground into curry pastes and used in stocks; the stems contribute body and depth that the leaves alone do not. Discarding the stems is wasteful; the only stem-free preparation is the small-leaf garnish on top of a finished dish, where the visual matters.

Coriander root. Even more aromatic than the stems are the roots, which Thai cooking specifies in green and red curry pastes (always sold with cilantro on the stem at Asian groceries; cut off and reserve them when you buy a bunch with roots attached). The roots have a deep earthy-citrus flavor that is structurally important to Thai curries; substituting cilantro stems-only for cilantro-with-roots loses some depth.

Cooking behavior. Fresh cilantro is heat-sensitive: above 60°C / 140°F the bright top-notes degrade quickly. The herb is added at the very end of cooking, off-heat or in the bowl, almost universally. The exception is when stems-only or roots-only are used as part of an aromatic base (Thai curry pastes, some Sichuan stir-fries that use chopped stems as a frying ingredient); those are tolerant of heat because they lack the volatile leaf-only aromatics that degrade. Salsa, chutney, and chimichurri-style raw preparations let cilantro carry its full aromatic load.

Cross-cuisine reach. Mexican: salsa verde, tacos, pozole, mole verde, guacamole. Indian: chutneys, dal tadka, biryani, chaat. Thai: green and red curry pastes (root and stem), pad thai (leaf), tom yum (leaf), nahm jim sauces. Vietnamese: pho garnish, bun bo Hue, bahn mi (leaf and stem). Chinese: a major Sichuan, Hunan, and Northern garnish (xiang cai, "fragrant vegetable"); pork-and-cilantro dumplings; lamb-and-cilantro skewers. Lebanese: chermoula, parsley-substitute in some Levantine salads. Persian: sabzi (a mixed-herb plate that always includes cilantro), ash reshteh.

Buying notes. Look for vibrantly green leaves, no yellowing, no slimy patches at the cut ends. The stems should be crisp, not wilted. Cilantro sold with roots intact (a cluster of small white roots at the bottom of the bunch) is the highest-quality form and is what to look for at Asian and Latin American groceries; Western supermarkets typically trim the roots off, which slightly reduces the herb's shelf life and removes a useful ingredient. A bunch should smell distinctly citrusy-fresh when you bring it to your nose; weak or grassy smell means past prime.

Storage

Cilantro is fragile and decays fast. The best storage method: trim 1 cm off the cut ends, place the bunch upright in a glass with 3 cm of water (like cut flowers), drape a plastic bag loosely over the top, refrigerate. Replace the water every 2 days. This keeps cilantro fresh 2 to 3 weeks, dramatically longer than other methods. Loose in a plastic bag in the crisper drawer: 5 to 7 days, with leaves yellowing and stems wilting toward the end. Pre-washed cilantro in clamshells: 5 to 7 days but the texture is already compromised by the wash. Frozen chopped cilantro (in olive oil cubes or sealed bags): 6 months but only acceptable for cooked preparations; the freeze destroys the texture for raw use. Off-cues are yellowing leaves, slimy stems, sour smell; discard.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • cilantro (Latin American applications) → flat-leaf parsley + lime zest Cilantro is genuinely irreplaceable in Mexican and Latin American cooking, but parsley plus lime zest covers part of the function: parsley for the herbaceous green note, lime zest for the citrusy top-notes. The dish reads less Mexican; for soap-tasters in mixed company this is the standard compromise.
  • cilantro (Vietnamese pho garnish, where multiple herbs are already on the table) → Thai basil and mint, doubled The Vietnamese herb plate (rau thom) traditionally includes cilantro, Thai basil, mint, and Vietnamese coriander (rau ram). For soap-tasters, doubling the other herbs covers the aromatic gap.
  • cilantro stems and roots (Thai curry pastes) → no good substitute The roots and lower stems are structural to Thai green and red curry pastes. If unavailable, the curry will be thinner and less complex; consider using a different curry that doesn't lean on coriander root.
