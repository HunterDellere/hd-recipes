Ingredient

Pineapple

Tropical bromeliad fruit (Ananas comosus), 11 to 15% sugars by weight, 0.6 to 1.0% citric and malic acid, carrying the proteolytic enzyme bromelain in the raw flesh. The structural sweet-acid fruit of Southeast Asian sour soups, Filipino sweet-and-sour braises, Mexican al pastor marinades, and the global tropical pantry.

About

Pineapple is the fruit of Ananas comosus, the cultivated bromeliad domesticated in tropical South America (the Amazon and Paraguay basins) and dispersed globally through Spanish and Portuguese trade. The fruit is a multiple-fruit (a coalescence of many small berries fused around a central core) rather than a single fruit, which is why each "eye" on the rind corresponds to one of the original flowers. Commercial varieties in the US market are dominated by the "MD-2" (Costa Rican, the bright-yellow extra-sweet pineapple that Dole and Del Monte popularized in the 1990s) and the smaller "sugarloaf" or "Queen" varieties; tropical-Asian markets stock a wider range including the Thai Phulae and the Filipino Formosa.

Composition and chemistry. Ripe pineapple is 85 to 87% water, 11 to 15% sugars (a mix of sucrose, glucose, and fructose, with the sucrose fraction higher in extra-sweet MD-2 varieties), and 0.6 to 1.0% organic acid (citric primarily, with secondary malic and traces of others). The acid-sugar ratio at ripeness gives the fruit its characteristic balance; under-ripe pineapple reads sharply acidic with a green-vegetal back-note, over-ripe reads cloyingly sweet with fermented top notes. The signature feature of raw pineapple is bromelain, a complex of proteolytic enzymes (cysteine proteases) that breaks down protein peptide bonds. Bromelain is concentrated in the stem and core, lower in the flesh, and is responsible for the slight tongue-tingling sensation when eating raw pineapple (the enzyme is digesting the surface mucosal proteins of the mouth in real time) and for the meat-tenderizing function in marinades.

Bromelain behavior under heat. Bromelain is denatured (irreversibly deactivated) above 60°C / 140°F over several minutes; the enzyme is mostly inactive after 5 minutes at 70°C / 158°F and entirely inactive after a brief boil. This is why canned pineapple (which has been pasteurized at >85°C / 185°F) does not tenderize meat or set off the mouth-tingle, and why bromelain-using marinades (Filipino tocino, Hawaiian-Polynesian preparations) use raw fresh pineapple. In cooked applications (canh chua, Filipino sweet-and-sour, Cantonese sweet-and-sour pork, al pastor marinade after the marinating step), the bromelain has been deactivated by the cook, and the pineapple contributes only its sugars and acids, not its enzymatic effect. This also means raw pineapple in a gelatin dessert prevents the gelatin from setting (the bromelain digests the gelatin protein), but canned pineapple in the same dessert sets normally.

Cooking applications by category. Vietnamese: canh chua cá (the southern Vietnamese sour soup, where pineapple's sugars and acids round the tamarind broth); some Mekong-delta stir-fries and braises. Filipino: sinigang variations (sometimes pineapple-soured rather than tamarind-soured); sweet-and-sour preparations; tocino (the bromelain-marinade pork). Cantonese / Chinese-American: sweet-and-sour pork (the canonical Cantonese version uses fresh pineapple chunks added late in the stir-fry; the Chinese-American version uses canned). Mexican (al pastor): the pineapple-bromelain marinade on the trompo-grilled pork; the pineapple slices grilled and chopped onto the taco. Thai: pineapple-fried rice (khao phad sapparod, in a hollowed pineapple shell); some southern Thai curries. Hawaiian / Polynesian: grilled, with lime and chili; on the famous pizza (whose Canadian origin is documented); in poi-adjacent preparations.

Buying notes. Ripe pineapple cues: a slightly fragrant sweet smell at the base of the fruit (no smell or fermented-sour smell are both bad signs), evenly golden-yellow skin (not pale green) with no soft brown patches, intact green crown leaves (some browning at the leaf tips is normal but limp brown leaves are over-ripe), slight give when pressed at the base but no soft mushy spots, weight that feels heavy for the size (full water content). The "pull a leaf from the center" test is folk-lore and unreliable; some leaves pull cleanly from ripe fruit, others from under-ripe. Picked-ripe whole pineapple does not continue to ripen significantly after harvest (unlike bananas, climacteric fruit), so a green-and-firm pineapple on the supermarket shelf will not sweeten on the counter; it will only soften. Costa Rican and Filipino imports dominate the US market; the MD-2 variety is the default. Trader Joe's, Whole Foods, and most Asian markets stock acceptable fruit; H Mart and 99 Ranch stock a wider variety in season.

Common Western drift. (1) US home cooks substituting canned pineapple for fresh in dishes that use the bromelain or the bright-fresh acid (al pastor, canh chua, fresh-pineapple stir-fries); canned is heat-pasteurized and reads sweeter, softer, and without the enzymatic effect. (2) Using under-ripe fresh pineapple (because cooks pick it off the supermarket shelf early), producing dishes that read sharply acidic and green-vegetal; the fruit must be picked at full ripeness for the cooking application to work.

Storage

Whole ripe pineapple at room temperature: 2 to 4 days, with the fruit slowly softening and the sugars converting; no further sweetening occurs after picking. Whole ripe pineapple refrigerated: 5 to 7 days, the cold slows softening but the flavor flattens slightly. Cut pineapple in a sealed container in the refrigerator: 3 to 5 days; the cut surfaces oxidize and brown slightly, which does not affect flavor but reads visually. Frozen cut pineapple: 6 months at peak quality; the freeze-thaw breaks down the cell structure, so thawed pineapple is suitable for cooked applications, blended drinks, and smoothies, not for raw snacking. Canned pineapple in syrup or juice: 1 to 2 years unopened; once opened, transfer from the can to a glass or plastic container and refrigerate for 5 to 7 days. Failure modes: fermented-sour smell at the base of a whole fruit (over-ripe or starting to ferment internally, discard or use immediately in a cooked application), soft brown patches on the cut flesh (oxidation; trim away if minor, discard if extensive), visible mold (discard).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • pineapple (Vietnamese canh chua, Filipino sinigang variations) → green mango or unripe papaya Green mango is the Mekong-delta variation when pineapple is out of season; it reads sharper, more grassy, and more acidic, so reduce the tamarind by 5 to 10 g to keep the total acid in balance. Unripe papaya is a closer textural substitute but reads less acid; add a small splash of lime to compensate.
  • pineapple (Cantonese sweet-and-sour pork, al pastor marinade) → canned pineapple chunks in juice (drained) Canned pineapple is the workable substitute when fresh is out of season or poor quality, reduce any added sugar in the dish by 10 to 20% because the canned fruit is sweeter. The bromelain enzymatic effect is lost, which matters for raw marinades (al pastor pre-cook) but does not matter in cooked applications.
  • pineapple (raw, salsa or fresh sweet-sour applications) → mango (ripe) or papaya (ripe) For raw fresh applications, ripe mango is the closest sweet-tropical substitute; reads sweeter and less acid, add a squeeze of lime to compensate. Papaya is a textural substitute but reads more delicate and less assertively tropical.
