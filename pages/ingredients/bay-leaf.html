Ingredient

Bay leaf (tej patta, तेज पत्ता)

Dried leaves of Laurus nobilis (European bay) or Cinnamomum tamala (Indian bay, also called tej patta or malabar leaf). Used whole as an aromatic in long-simmered dishes. Indian bay is structurally different from European bay: closer to cinnamon-clove than to camphor-pine.

About

Two different plants are commonly sold as "bay leaf" and the distinction matters. European bay leaf (Laurus nobilis, also called Mediterranean bay or sweet laurel) is a dried leaf of the bay laurel tree, native to the Mediterranean. The aroma is camphorous, slightly pine-like, with eucalyptol (1,8-cineole) as the dominant volatile compound; this is the bay leaf of French, Italian, Spanish, and broader Mediterranean cooking. Indian bay leaf (Cinnamomum tamala, also called tej patta or malabar leaf) is the dried leaf of an entirely different tree, in the cinnamon family rather than the laurel family. The aroma is closer to cinnamon-clove than to camphor-pine, with cinnamaldehyde and eugenol as the dominant compounds; this is the structurally different bay leaf of Indian, Pakistani, Nepali, and Bhutanese cooking.

Why this distinction matters. A North Indian biryani made with European bay leaf reads recognizably wrong because the camphor-pine character is foreign to Indian cuisine; conversely, a French bouquet garni made with Indian tej patta reads as cinnamon-spiced rather than as a clean savory base. The two leaves are not interchangeable, and most "Indian recipe" Western cookbooks that say "bay leaf" without specifying are unfortunately calling for European bay leaf in dishes that should have tej patta. Indian groceries reliably sell tej patta separately; Western groceries almost universally sell European bay leaf when they say "bay leaf."

Cooking applications, European bay (Laurus nobilis). Long-simmered French and Italian stocks (chicken, vegetable, court bouillon), bouquet garni (the herb bundle in stews and braises), Italian bolognese, Spanish bean stews (fabada, garbanzo dishes), pickling brines, mulled wines. Standard quantity: 1 to 2 leaves per pot of stock or stew, infused throughout cooking and removed before serving. The leaf does not soften appreciably under cooking; do not eat directly.

Cooking applications, Indian bay (tej patta). North Indian biryani and pilaf (1 to 2 leaves added with the whole spices), Pakistani nihari and haleem (3 to 4 leaves), Mughlai korma and rogan josh, garam masala blends (sometimes — varies by recipe), Bhutanese ema datshi. Standard quantity: 1 to 3 leaves per dish. The leaves do not break down; do not eat directly.

Fresh bay leaves. Fresh European bay (sometimes available in farmers'' markets or as a cultivated houseplant) is more aromatic and slightly more complex than dried; use at the same quantity as dried. Fresh tej patta is rare outside South Asia. Both are bitter when used as primary leafy greens (do not substitute for any leafy herb), but excellent as long-infusion aromatics.

Buying notes. Indian groceries sell tej patta in 25 g and 50 g bags; the leaves are usually broken into 5 to 10 cm pieces and have a noticeable cinnamon smell when handled. European bay (sold as "bay leaves" or "laurel") at any supermarket is reliable; the leaves should be unbroken, gray-green, and aromatic when crushed. Both keep well; avoid the small rectangular spice-aisle bottles that have sat for years.

Storage

Sealed at room temperature in a cool dry pantry, 1 to 2 years for both varieties. The leaves can mold under humidity; use a dry storage container. Off-cues are loss of aroma when crushed, mottled spots on the leaf surface, or musty smell; discard.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • Indian bay leaf (tej patta) in biryani, nihari, Mughlai cooking → a small piece of cinnamon stick (about 1 cm) Tej patta is in the cinnamon family; a small piece of cinnamon stick approximates the aromatic direction better than European bay does. Acceptable substitute when tej patta is unavailable; the dish reads close to authentic.
  • European bay leaf (Laurus nobilis) in stocks, stews, French cooking → no good substitute for the camphor-pine note European bay has no clean substitute. Thyme + a pinch of dried oregano approximates a herbal bouquet but lacks the bay character. Most stocks and stews are acceptable without bay leaf; better to omit than to substitute incorrectly.
