Ingredient

Preserved radish (chai poh, 菜脯)

Daikon radish dry-cured in salt then sun-dried into a deeply savory, chewy, salt-and-umami concentrate. Sold whole, sliced, or diced; reaches across Chinese, Taiwanese, and Southeast Asian cooking as a small-volume high-impact ingredient.

About

Preserved radish is daikon (Raphanus sativus) cured by a centuries-old two-stage process: the fresh radish is salted heavily (typically 8 to 15% by weight) and pressed under weight for 1 to 3 days to expel water, then sun-dried for 3 to 7 days until it loses 70 to 80% of its starting weight. The result is a leathery, dark amber, intensely savory radish that keeps for years at room temperature in a sealed jar. The cure shifts the radish from a watery, faintly bitter raw vegetable into a concentrated salt-umami ingredient that behaves more like an aged cured meat in cooking than a vegetable.

Two grades to know. Salt-cured (xian chai poh, 咸菜脯) is the savory grade: aggressive salt, no sugar, deep amber-brown, used as a small-volume seasoning ingredient in fried rice, omelets, and congee toppings. Sweet-cured (tian chai poh, 甜菜脯) adds 5 to 10% sugar to the cure: amber-gold, milder salt, faintly sweet, used in larger volumes as a side dish, in chai poh omelet (a Teochew classic), and as a textural component in the Taiwanese rice-roll fan tuan. Most Asian groceries stock both; recipes that want one and get the other will either taste cloying (salt recipe with sweet cure) or harshly saline (sweet recipe with salt cure). Read the label.

Functional role. The cure has driven off the radish's sulfur-volatiles and concentrated its glutamate to the point that a tablespoon of finely chopped preserved radish carries the same umami impact as several whole fresh radishes. Cooks reach for it as a small-volume seasoning that contributes salt, savor, and a chewy textural bite simultaneously. In a Teochew chai poh dan omelet, two tablespoons of finely diced preserved radish stirred into beaten eggs become the entire seasoning of the omelet; no soy, no salt, just the radish doing the work. In Cantonese congee, a teaspoon of finely chopped chai poh on the table beside the bowl is the salt-savory contrast that makes plain rice porridge into a meal. In Sichuan dan dan noodles, the dished-out portion of fried preserved radish is the small dark fleck that sits on top of the noodles and contributes both crunch and salt-umami punch.

Buying notes. Sold whole (long radish lengths, sometimes still in their original twine bundles), pre-sliced into matchsticks, or pre-diced. Whole-form keeps best and the cook controls the cut to suit the dish; diced is convenient but oxidizes faster once the bag is open. Look for radish that is dark amber to mahogany, leathery but not brittle, and moist to the touch (not sticky); brittle, pale, or chalky pieces are over-dried or old. Lee Kum Kee, Mae Pranom, and various Hong Kong / Taiwanese / Thai brands offer reliable products at North American Asian groceries. The Thai Thep brand sells a particularly good sweet-cured grade marketed as "sweet radish" in English.

Soaking. Salt-cured preserved radish often benefits from a brief 5-minute soak in cold water before use: it softens the chew slightly and pulls a fraction of the salt back out, producing a more balanced final dish. The exception is fried rice and stir-fry, where the dry texture is desirable; do not soak for those. Sweet-cured radish almost never needs soaking, as its salt level is already balanced by sugar.

Storage

Sealed in an airtight glass jar at room temperature, away from light: 1 to 2 years. Refrigerated: indefinite, but the cold can dry the radish further into brittleness; only refrigerate after opening if humidity in your storage area is high. Once opened, condense the contents into the smallest possible airtight container to minimize headspace; the salt level is high enough to inhibit spoilage organisms, but oxidation will dull the flavor over months. The cue for past-prime is the smell: fresh preserved radish smells salty and faintly funky; old preserved radish smells flat and dusty.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each card names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • preserved radish (savory, small-volume seasoning) preserved mustard (sui mi ya cai or zha cai), finely chopped

    Both are Sichuan preserved-vegetable preparations with similar salt-umami functional profiles. Sui mi ya cai is closest in cut and color (finely chopped, dark); zha cai is firmer and spicier. Use 1:1 by weight; the dish will read more aromatic and slightly hotter.

  • preserved radish (savory) capers, rinsed and chopped

    Mediterranean cousin in functional role: small-volume salt-umami seasoning with a chewy bite. The flavor profile is different (briny rather than sweet-savory) but the structural job is the same. Reduce volume by 30% as capers are sharper.

  • preserved radish (sweet, larger-volume side ingredient) kimchi-style pickled radish (kkakdugi), drained

    Korean ferment with sweet-salt-spice profile. Acceptable when sweet preserved radish is unavailable; the dish will read more sour and spicier. Drain off the brine before use.

  • preserved radish (any application, fresh approximation) fresh daikon, salted and squeezed

    Toss 200 g julienned daikon with 8 g salt, let stand 1 hour, squeeze out all the water. Dry-toast in a skillet for 3 minutes to drive off remaining moisture and concentrate flavor. Closer to fresh-pickled territory than true preserved radish, but the savory function is approximately covered.
