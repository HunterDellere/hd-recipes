Ingredient

Ground pork

Pork muscle ground through a fixed plate, typically from shoulder (butt) at roughly 75/25 lean-to-fat. The structural protein of Italian polpette, Sichuan zha jiang sauces, Thai larb, Filipino lumpia, and the meatball traditions of nearly every cuisine that keeps pigs. Sweeter, more emulsifiable, and more forgiving than ground beef.

About

Ground pork is whole-muscle pork passed through a meat grinder with a plate of specified hole diameter (most commonly 4.5 mm for "medium grind" supermarket ground pork, 3 mm for "fine grind" used in sausage and dumpling filling, 6 mm for "coarse grind" used in ragu and chili). Unlike ground beef, where the cut origin is often labeled (chuck, sirloin, round), supermarket ground pork is almost always from the shoulder (Boston butt) without further specification; it runs roughly 75 to 80% lean and 20 to 25% fat, the inherent ratio of the cut. The exception is "ground pork loin" which is sometimes sold as a leaner option (90/10) and behaves more like ground turkey than like real ground pork.

Fat percentage and cut origin. The 75/25 shoulder default is what nearly every ground-pork recipe in the global canon assumes; the 25% fat creates the emulsion structure that makes pork sausages, dumplings, and meatballs hold together without binders. The shoulder also has more connective collagen than lean cuts, and the collagen melts during cooking to produce the slightly sticky, juicy interior characteristic of well-made pork meatballs. Lean ground pork (90/10 loin) lacks both the fat and the collagen, browns to a pebbly grey, and is generally a cooking liability outside of fitness-targeted dishes. Butcher-ground pork from a specified cut (pork belly added for 70/30 sausage, shoulder-and-jowl blend for terrine) is available at good butchers and is dramatically better than supermarket ground for any application where the pork is the focal protein.

Emulsion behavior. Pork is the canonical sausage and meatball protein because the muscle fibers and the fat phase emulsify cleanly when worked with salt. The mechanism is myosin extraction: when ground pork is mixed with salt (and optionally pounded, kneaded, or paddled in a stand mixer), the myosin protein extracted from the muscle dissolves in the salt solution and forms a sticky matrix that traps the fat globules and water. This is what makes a pork meatball spring back at the bite (the "snap" or "bounce" texture in Asian meatballs) and what keeps Italian polpette tender without falling apart. The salt-and-work step is non-negotiable for any pork-based meatball or sausage; under-worked ground pork falls apart, over-worked ground pork (past the point of full myosin extraction) reads dense and rubbery. The window is typically 60 to 120 seconds of vigorous mixing with salt added at the start.

Cooking applications by category. Italian-American: meatballs (polpette in tomato), sausage (sweet and hot Italian sausage), bolognese (pork-and-beef blend), pâtés. Chinese (Cantonese, Sichuan, Shanghai): lion's head meatballs (large braised meatballs from Yangzhou), wonton and dumpling fillings, mapo tofu (the Cantonese version), zha jiang noodle sauces, char siu bao filling. Thai, Lao, Burmese: larb (the toasted-rice-powder pork salad, also called laab or laap), sai oua (Lao sausage), nam tok, dipping-sauce bases. Filipino: lumpia Shanghai (the small spring rolls), pork tocino marinade base, embutido. Mexican: chorizo (the bright-red fresh sausage with chili and vinegar), picadillo (pork-and-fruit hash), albondigas (meatballs in tomato-chipotle broth). Korean: mandu (dumpling) filling, jeon (pancake) batters, bulgogi-style stir-fries. Eastern European: kielbasa, kotlety (pan-fried patties), gołąbki (cabbage rolls). The breadth here reflects the simple fact that pork has been the universal pig-keeping cuisine's protein for centuries.

Cooking behavior. Ground pork releases water in the first 30 to 60 seconds of high-heat cooking, similarly to ground beef; the cook's choice is the same (drain or cook off). For meatballs, where the protein is shaped before cooking, the water release is internal and contributes to juiciness rather than to pan splatter; the meatball's surface browns once the surface water evaporates, and the interior remains moist. For loose-grind stir-fries (mapo, larb, zha jiang), the dry-fry-until-the-water-cooks-off step is structural; the meat browns to deep mahogany only after the water has gone, and this browning is the flavor anchor of the dish. The internal-safe temperature for ground pork is 71°C / 160°F (USDA), which corresponds to the firm-but-juicy texture that pork meatballs should hit; an instant-read thermometer in the meatball center is the right tool.

Buying notes. Supermarket ground pork in plastic-wrapped trays is acceptable for everyday cooking; aim for label dates within 2 days of purchase. Butcher-ground pork is meaningfully better in flavor (the cut is fresher, the grind is same-day) for any focal-protein application. Asian markets (H Mart, 99 Ranch, Patel Brothers) often carry ground pork that is fresher and slightly fattier than supermarket ground; this is the right pick for dumplings and Chinese meatballs. The color of fresh ground pork is pale pink to rose; bright red color suggests added preservatives (sodium nitrite, common in commercial sausage but unusual in plain ground pork) and may indicate a brand to avoid for clean cooking applications.

Storage

Refrigerated in original packaging at the back of the fridge: 1 to 2 days from grind date. Once opened, transfer to a non-reactive container and use within 24 hours. Frozen in airtight packaging or vacuum-sealed: 3 to 4 months at 0°F / -18°C. Defrost overnight in the fridge, never at room temperature or in warm water. Cooked ground pork refrigerates 3 to 4 days and freezes 2 to 3 months. Off-cues are sour or ammonia smell, slimy or tacky surface, grey-green discoloration past normal age-grey, or a sticky-sheen on the package interior; discard.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • ground pork (Italian meatballs, polpette, sausage) → ground pork-and-beef blend (50/50), or ground veal-pork-beef blend The traditional Italian meatball is actually a blend, not pure pork; the beef adds savor and the pork adds tenderness. 1:1 substitute by weight; the dish reads more traditionally Italian-American. The veal-pork-beef blend (terzina) is the upscale restaurant version.
  • ground pork (Thai larb, Lao laab) → ground chicken, ground turkey, or finely chopped firm tofu Ground chicken (preferably with thigh meat) makes laab gai, a recognized regional variant. Ground turkey is a defensible US-supermarket substitute. Chopped firm tofu makes a vegan version that takes the dressing well but lacks the protein chew.
  • ground pork (Chinese lion's head, dumpling filling) → ground pork-and-shrimp blend (70/30), or ground pork-and-water-chestnut The pork-and-shrimp blend is the Cantonese refinement of the lion's head and the canonical dumpling filling; the shrimp adds glutamate, sweetness, and a slight snap to the texture. Substitute 30% of the pork weight with finely chopped peeled shrimp.
  • ground pork (general protein, dietary swap) → ground chicken thigh, ground turkey thigh, or Beyond Meat plant-based ground Ground chicken thigh is the closest in fat profile and behavior. Beyond Meat is the best plant-based substitute for browning behavior but produces a dish that reads as Beyond-Meat-flavored rather than pork-flavored.
