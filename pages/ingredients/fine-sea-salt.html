Ingredient

Fine sea salt

Evaporated seawater, ground fine. Small crystals dissolve fast and distribute evenly — the right salt for baking and for anything that needs to disappear.

About

Sea salt is sodium chloride with trace minerals (magnesium, calcium, potassium) left over from evaporation. Those traces sit below the threshold of taste in most dishes — the marketing about "complex minerality" is largely overstated for fine grades. What matters is crystal size.

Fine grain dissolves on contact with water. That's why it's the right salt for bread dough, cookie dough, custards, and anywhere even distribution beats pinch-control. Kosher salt's coarse flakes can leave undissolved pockets in a dough that mixes briefly; fine sea salt won't.

Iodized vs non-iodized matters at the edges. Potassium iodide adds a faint metallic, slightly bitter note that's invisible in a stew but audible in a pastry cream, a panna cotta, or an egg-yolk-heavy sauce. Use non-iodized for delicate dishes. Iodized is fine for general cooking and is genuinely useful as a dietary iodine source.

Fine sea salt is not a stand-in for kosher salt by volume. It's roughly 1.5–2× as dense as Diamond Crystal: a teaspoon weighs ~6g vs ~3g. Salt by weight when crossing between them.

Storage

Airtight container, dry shelf. Indefinite. Failure mode is clumping from kitchen humidity; keep the lid on, store away from the stove and dishwasher.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • fine sea salt (baking) → table salt By weight, 1:1. Watch for iodized off-notes in delicate baked goods (shortbread, custards).
  • fine sea salt → kosher salt By weight, 1:1. By volume, use ~1.5–2× the measure. Coarse flakes may not fully dissolve in brief mixes; grind first or hydrate before adding.
  • fine sea salt (finishing) → flaky sea salt (Maldon) Different job: flake salt is for crunch and visible sparkle. Fine sea salt finishes invisibly; Maldon finishes texturally.
