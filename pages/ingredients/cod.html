Ingredient

Cod

Atlantic or Pacific cod (Gadus morhua, G. macrocephalus). Mild, flaky, low-fat (0.7 percent) white-fleshed groundfish; the canonical lean white fish of the North Atlantic. Cooks in a narrow window because of the very low fat content. The default firm-white-fish substitute when halibut is unavailable, and the structural protein of fish-and-chips, brandade, bacalao, and Iberian salt-cod traditions.

About

Cod is a mild, white-fleshed bottom-dwelling groundfish in the family Gadidae. Two species dominate US markets: Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua, the historically dominant species, severely depleted in the 1990s by overfishing and now under tight Northeast US and Canadian Maritime quota management) and Pacific cod (Gadus macrocephalus, the larger and more abundant species, fished sustainably in Alaskan waters and the dominant species in US supermarket fillets today). Adult fish reach 60 to 120 cm and 5 to 25 kg; supermarket fillets are boneless rectangular blocks 1.5 to 3 cm thick, sold fresh on ice or frozen-at-sea. Related gadid species sold under "cod" or near-equivalents include haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus, slightly drier and milder than cod, the canonical British fish-and-chips fish), pollock (Pollachius virens and walleye pollock, milder and slightly oilier, the dominant species in fish sticks and imitation crab), and hake (Merluccius species, similar lean-flaky structure, common in Iberian cooking).

The flesh is bright white when raw, opaque white when cooked, with a notably large, distinct flake that separates cleanly along the muscle blocks. Composition: ~82 percent water, ~17 percent protein, ~0.7 percent fat (one of the leanest commonly-eaten fish; halibut is 1 to 2 percent, salmon is 13 percent for comparison). The connective tissue is delicate and short-fiber; the flake structure is wider and more separated than halibut, which makes cod prone to falling apart in stews and broths if overhandled but ideal for the canonical "thumb-pressed flake" texture of fish-and-chips. The flavor is mild, sweet, and clean, with a faint mineral backnote and minimal "fishy" character when fresh; cod is the white fish that reads as the neutral baseline against which other species are compared.

Transformation. The very low fat content is the operational constraint: cod has essentially no buffer for overcooking, and the dry-out cliff is sharp and unforgiving. Internal temperature targets: 50 °C (122 °F) for very-rare (translucent center, possible only for sashimi-grade fish, never for supermarket cod); 54 to 57 °C (130 to 135 °F) for medium (just-flaky, juicy, moist, the chef target); 60 °C (140 °F) is the upper limit for keeping the fish moist; past 63 °C (145 °F, the USDA target) the protein has expelled most of its water and the flesh reads chalky and dry. Pacific cod at 145 °F internal is the standard supermarket-recipe target and is famously chalky; the dish redeems itself only with sauce or moist heat.

The flake structure begins to separate at 50 °C as the collagen between muscle blocks softens; by 60 °C the flakes pull apart with the touch of a fork and the fish is at peak doneness. Optimal cooking methods: pan sear with a 30-second flip rhythm (target 130 to 135 °F, finish in pan or under a broiler); oven-roast at 200 °C / 400 °F for 8 to 10 minutes by 2.5 cm thickness; poach in court bouillon at 65 to 70 °C for 6 to 8 minutes; submerge in a barely-simmering tomato-saffron broth (cioppino or bouillabaisse) for 4 to 6 minutes by 3 cm chunk thickness; deep-fry at 180 °C / 360 °F in beer batter for 5 to 7 minutes (the canonical British technique). Sous vide at 51 to 54 °C for 30 to 40 minutes is the precision-restaurant approach. Salt-curing transforms cod into bacalao or salt cod (saltado), the Iberian and Portuguese preservation that draws out water through osmosis, concentrates the protein, and produces a firmer, more flavorful product that requires 24 to 36 hours of soaking in cold water (3 changes) to rehydrate before cooking.

Applications by category. In British and Anglo-American cooking, cod is the canonical fish for fish-and-chips (beer-battered, deep-fried, with malt vinegar and tartar sauce), for fish pie (white sauce, mashed potato top), and for fish cakes (cod, potato, parsley). In French classical cooking, cod (fresh) is poached for fish dumplings (quenelles) and as a base for fumet; salt cod is the canonical body of brandade de morue (Provençal pounded salt-cod-and-potato puree with garlic and olive oil), an emulsion that pairs with toasted bread. In Mediterranean cooking, cod is the firm white fish of bouillabaisse and bourride (Provençal); in Italian cooking, baccalà mantecato is the Venetian whipped salt cod with olive oil, and baccalà alla Vicentina is the milk-poached salt cod of the Veneto. In Iberian cooking, salt cod (bacalhau in Portugal, bacalao in Spain) anchors hundreds of regional dishes, including Portuguese bacalhau à brás (with shoestring potato and scrambled egg) and Spanish bacalao al pil-pil (with garlic and olive oil emulsion). In modern American cooking, cod is the default supermarket "white fish" substitute for halibut in stews, the protein in baked-fish-with-vegetables sheet-pan dinners, and the lean-fish option for lower-calorie preparations. Across all categories, the same operational rule applies: pull at 130 to 135 °F internal, never trust 145 °F unless the dish is sauced or moist enough to hide the result.

Buying notes. Pacific cod (Alaskan-managed) is the more sustainable and more available choice in US markets; available year-round, with peak quality fresh in winter and spring. Frozen-at-sea Pacific cod (Costco, Whole Foods, Asian markets like H Mart) is excellent and often a better choice than "fresh" supermarket cod that has been thawed and sat on ice for 2 to 4 days. Atlantic cod is largely depleted in the wild; what is sold as Atlantic cod in US markets is typically farmed (Norwegian or Canadian) and reads slightly oilier and softer than wild Pacific. Look for fillets with bright white flesh (not gray, yellow, or chalky), tight intact flake structure, and a clean ocean smell with no ammonia. Salt cod is sold in Portuguese, Spanish, and Italian specialty markets in pre-packaged dried fillets (boneless or with bones) and at H Mart in some Korean-influenced stores; soak 24 to 36 hours in cold water with 3 to 4 water changes before cooking. Avoid cod labeled with no species qualifier in budget-supermarket counters; the fish may be a different gadid sold as a cheap substitute.

Common Western drift. The single biggest mistake in home cod cooking is the same as halibut: cooking to USDA 145 °F target instead of pulling at 130 to 135 °F. Cod is even less forgiving than halibut because of the lower fat content. The second mistake is treating cod as fragile and underhandling it; cod can hold up in a stew or broth if the chunks are 3 cm or larger and the broth is at a bare simmer (90 °C / 195 °F), but smaller chunks or a hard simmer will produce broken flakes throughout the dish. The third is using thawed, days-old supermarket cod and getting an off-flavor result; frozen-at-sea Pacific cod is the better default for any stew or sear application.

Storage

Refrigerator: 1 to 2 days for fresh fillets, kept on ice in a perforated container over a drain pan, paper towel between the flesh and the ice. Frozen-at-sea fillets in vacuum bags: 4 to 6 months at -18 °C; defrost in the refrigerator overnight, never at room temperature. Cooked cod: 2 days refrigerated, reheated gently in sauce or soup or eaten cold (the dense flake does not microwave well). Salt cod (dried): pantry storage, dark cool place, 12 to 18 months sealed; once opened, refrigerate in an airtight container and use within 6 months.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • cod (fish-and-chips, beer-battered) → haddock or pollock Haddock is the canonical British alternative and reads slightly drier and milder; pollock (Alaskan) is the most common substitute and reads slightly softer and oilier. Both work; haddock is preferred by British purists.
  • cod (cioppino, bouillabaisse, fish stew) → halibut, lingcod, black sea bass, Pacific rockfish, or monkfish Halibut is denser and holds chunks better; rockfish and lingcod are the Pacific Coast canonical choices; monkfish reads firmer with a lobster-like texture. All work as substitutes in stew applications.
  • cod (pan-seared or roasted fillet) → haddock, hake, pollock, or striped bass Haddock and hake are the closest substitutes (similar lean-flaky structure); striped bass reads firmer and holds together better in the pan; pollock works but is softer.
  • salt cod (brandade, bacalao, baccalà) → no good fresh-fish substitute; the cure is structural Salt cod is structurally different from fresh cod; the cure changes the protein structure and concentrates the flavor in a way that fresh cod cannot reproduce. If salt cod is unavailable, use the fresh-cod recipe canon (poached cod, white sauce) rather than attempting a salt-cod dish.
