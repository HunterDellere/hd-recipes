Ingredient

Dark soy sauce (老抽)

Aged longer than light soy and cut with molasses or caramel. Used in small amounts for color and depth, not for primary salting.

About

Dark soy (lao chou / 老抽) is aged longer than light soy and finished with molasses or caramel. The result is thicker, less salty, and dramatically darker — the color of strong coffee. Use it for color (red-cooked pork, tea eggs, lo mein, hong shao anything) and a touch of caramelized depth. It is not a 1:1 swap for light soy — using dark soy as your primary seasoning will make a dish taste flat and look like used motor oil. A typical recipe uses 1–2 tbsp dark soy alongside 3–4 tbsp light soy. Dark for the look, light for the salt and umami.

Storage

Pantry. Refrigerate after opening to preserve the molasses notes. The bottle can sit for a year — you use it slowly.

Substitutions

  • dark soy sauce → light soy + 1/4 tsp molasses per tbsp Approximate but workable. Color won't be as deep.
  • dark soy sauce → kecap manis (Indonesian sweet soy) Much sweeter — only works in dishes that can take the extra sugar.
