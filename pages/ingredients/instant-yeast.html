Ingredient

Instant yeast

Saccharomyces cerevisiae, dried into fine granules small enough to mix directly into flour without a separate proofing step. The faster, more forgiving evolution of active dry yeast.

About

Instant yeast is the same organism as fresh, cake, and active dry yeast, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, a single-celled fungus, processed differently. Manufacturers grow the yeast on a sugar-based medium, drum-dry the resulting biomass at moderate temperatures (around 35 to 40 °C, low enough to keep most of the cells alive), and grind the dried product into fine granules. Instant yeast (also sold as "rapid-rise" or "bread-machine" yeast) is the finest of these grinds; the small particle size lets the yeast hydrate fast enough to skip the warm-water proofing step that active dry yeast requires.

Functionally, instant yeast is the convenience-and-margin choice for nearly every bread baker who is not running a sourdough culture. Mixed straight into flour, it begins fermenting within 5 to 10 minutes of hydration; the dough rises predictably; the cook does not need to verify the yeast is alive before committing the rest of the ingredients. Active dry yeast (the older granular product) needs a 5-minute warm-water bloom to verify viability and to rehydrate the larger granules, skip the bloom and the rise is sluggish and uneven. Fresh yeast (cake yeast, sold refrigerated in 2 oz / 56 g blocks) is the legacy professional product with the highest cell-count-per-gram and the fastest rise; it spoils in 2 weeks and is rarely worth sourcing for home use unless a specific recipe demands it.

Substitution math by mass: 1 g instant yeast = 1.25 g active dry yeast = 3 g fresh yeast. By teaspoon, 1 tsp / 3 g instant ≈ 1 tsp / 3 g active dry (the volumes are close even though the active mass differs slightly) ≈ 9 g fresh. American recipes that call for one packet of yeast assume 7 g (¼ oz / 2¼ tsp), which is the size of a single Fleischmann's or Red Star envelope. Rapid-rise yeast and instant yeast are interchangeable; they are the same product under different brand labels.

Temperature and the rise. Yeast metabolism is fastest around 35 °C and is fully active from 20 to 40 °C; below 4 °C the yeast goes dormant; above 50 °C the cells start dying; at 60 °C and above the yeast is dead and the dough will not rise from there. The cook controls the rise with the dough temperature: a warm kitchen at 26 °C produces a 90-minute first rise on a basic bread dough; a cold refrigerator at 4 °C produces a 12-hour overnight rise. The cold-rise route produces deeper flavor (longer fermentation = more enzymatic activity = more sugar-to-acid byproducts and more flavor compounds) and is the canonical route for pizza dough, croissants, and any enriched dough where the cook wants flavor over speed.

Salt and sugar are inhibitors. Salt at 2 percent of flour mass slows the rise by about 30 percent; salt above 3 percent kills yeast outright (which is why bread recipes top out around 2 to 2.5 percent salt). Sugar at low doses (under 5 percent of flour mass) feeds the yeast and accelerates the rise; sugar above 10 percent dehydrates the yeast osmotically and slows the rise; sweet doughs (brioche, panettone, doughnuts) at 15 to 25 percent sugar by flour mass need either extra yeast or specialized osmotolerant yeast (SAF Gold) to rise in reasonable time.

Buying. SAF Red Instant Yeast (the 1 lb / 454 g vacuum-pack pouch) is the bench standard for serious home bakers; lasts 2 years in the freezer and works out to roughly $0.05 per loaf. Fleischmann's RapidRise envelopes are the supermarket staple at 7 g per packet; convenient but expensive per gram. Avoid pre-2020 pouches that have sat at room temperature for over a year, viable but slow.

Storage

Unopened pouches keep at room temperature for the printed expiry, typically 2 years. Once opened, transfer to an airtight container and store in the freezer; lasts 2 to 3 years frozen with no measurable activity loss. Refrigerator is acceptable but humidity penetration is faster and the yeast loses 10 to 20 percent activity per year in the fridge versus 1 to 2 percent in the freezer. Failure mode: the dough does not rise; verify viability by mixing 5 g yeast into 50 ml warm water (35 °C) with a teaspoon of sugar, visible foam and bubbling within 10 minutes confirms live yeast.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • instant yeast (most recipes) → active dry yeast Multiply the instant-yeast mass by 1.25 to get the active-dry-yeast mass. Bloom the active dry yeast in 5 times its mass of warm water (35 °C) for 5 minutes before adding to the dough. Functional but adds 5 minutes and an extra step.
  • instant yeast (most recipes, professional setting) → fresh cake yeast Multiply the instant-yeast mass by 3 to get the fresh-yeast mass. Crumble the fresh yeast directly into the flour or dissolve in the recipe water. Higher cell count and faster initial rise; more challenging to source and to keep alive in a home kitchen.
  • instant yeast (sweet doughs above 15 percent sugar by flour mass) → osmotolerant yeast (SAF Gold) Engineered to tolerate high-sugar environments where standard instant yeast slows. The right tool for brioche, panettone, hot-cross buns, and very-sweet doughs. Substitute 1:1 by mass.
  • instant yeast (sourdough-leaning flavor, no commercial yeast) → active sourdough starter Different leavening organism (wild yeast plus Lactobacillus bacteria). Replaces 100 percent of the commercial yeast with 20 percent of the flour mass as 100-percent-hydration starter, with corresponding adjustment to dough hydration. Produces a slower rise (8 to 12 hours bulk vs 90 minutes) and a tangier, more complex bread.
