Ingredient

Bread flour

Wheat flour at 12.5 to 14 percent protein. The strongest of the common Western flours; the protein lever for chewy, holding doughs.

About

Bread flour is milled from harder wheat varieties (predominantly hard red spring wheat in the US, durum-blend or hard red winter elsewhere) and standardized to 12.5 to 14 percent protein. King Arthur Bread Flour sits at 12.7 percent, Gold Medal Better for Bread at 12.5, and specialty bakery flours can run higher. The grind is slightly coarser than cake or pastry flour, which gives the flour a marginally greater starch-damage profile and absorbs water more aggressively.

Protein is the single variable that decides what flour will do. Glutenin and gliadin in dry flour are dormant; add water and mechanical work and they cross-link into gluten, the viscoelastic network that traps fermentation gas and gives bread its chew. Bread flour has more of these proteins per gram than all-purpose, which means more cross-links per unit hydration, which means a stronger, more elastic dough that holds gas longer and produces a chewier, more open crumb. The same mass of bread flour can absorb 5 to 8 percent more water than AP without going slack, the flour pulls more hydration into the gluten matrix because it has more proteins to do the pulling.

Functionally, bread flour is the canonical choice across yeast-leavened doughs that depend on chew and structure. Pizza dough at hydrations 60 to 70 percent develops the rim oven-spring and chewy mouthfeel that is the point of pizza; bread flour at the same hydration as AP yields a noticeably better crumb. Bagels and pretzels at 50 to 55 percent hydration need bread flour to handle the dense kneading and to survive a boil-bath without falling apart. Enriched doughs (brioche, milk bread, breadsticks, pull-aparts) want bread flour because the eggs and butter weaken gluten, and the higher protein margin keeps the dough strong enough to rise. Sourdough and naturally-leavened breads benefit from bread flour at the higher end of the protein band (13 to 14 percent) because the long fermentation continues to relax the gluten over hours and the structural margin pays off in the final crumb.

Hydration changes the dough behavior more than the flour does. Bread flour at 60 percent hydration produces a tight, chewy, sliceable loaf; at 75 percent the same flour produces an open, holey ciabatta-style crumb; at 85 percent (high-hydration sourdough) the dough is essentially a pourable batter and the bread reads as a glassy, irregular crumb with a thin, crackly crust. The recipe controls the structure; the flour is the base material that allows the recipe to reach that structure.

Substitution math. Bread flour and AP are interchangeable by mass for most recipes that do not specifically depend on chew, but the result will be a softer, less chewy bake. Going the other way, using AP where bread flour is called for, produces a noticeable structural deficit in any high-rise yeast bread; the dough will rise but collapse during oven spring, and the crumb will be tighter and softer than intended. The single recipe-level fix is to add 1 tablespoon (10 g) of vital wheat gluten per cup (130 g) of AP flour to approximate bread-flour protein levels.

00 flour is a separate Italian milling category, not a protein category. 00 refers to the fineness of the grind (the finest commercial grind available), and within 00 you can get cake-strength flour, pasta-strength flour, and pizza-bread-strength flour. "Caputo Pizzeria 00" is a 12.5-percent-protein bread-flour-equivalent ground extra-fine; it produces a more delicate, blistered Neapolitan crust than American bread flour at the same protein. Reading the protein on the bag matters more than the grind designation.

Storage

Airtight container in a cool dry pantry. Six to nine months at room temperature. Whole-grain bread flours (containing germ) go rancid in 2 to 3 months at room temperature; refrigerate or freeze. Failure modes: musty smell from oxidation, or live insects (Indian meal moth, flour weevil, visible webbing or moths on opening). Freeze new bags for 48 hours before pantry storage to kill any pest eggs that traveled in the bag.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • bread flour (yeast bread, pizza, bagels) → all-purpose flour plus vital wheat gluten Add 1 tbsp (10 g) of vital wheat gluten per 130 g (1 cup) of AP flour to approximate the protein content of bread flour. Functional 1:1 by mass. The bread will be very close but slightly less chewy than true bread flour.
  • bread flour (enriched doughs: brioche, milk bread, breadsticks) → all-purpose flour AP at 11 to 12 percent protein produces a softer, less chewy enriched bread. Acceptable when the dish does not depend on chew. The crumb will read more cake-tender; the structural rise will be slightly compromised at high enrichment levels (more than 25 percent butter or egg by flour mass).
  • bread flour (Neapolitan-style pizza) → 00 pizza flour (Caputo Pizzeria, Antimo Caputo Cuoco) The finer grind produces a more delicate, blistered crust at the same hydration. Functional 1:1 by mass. The single largest upgrade for serious Neapolitan pizza work; bread flour is the everyday choice that produces a still-excellent crust.
  • bread flour (whole-grain swap, partial) → whole-wheat bread flour, 25 to 50 percent replacement Whole-wheat bread flour adds bran and germ that physically cut into the gluten matrix and reduce rise; partial replacement adds nutty depth without crippling structure. Going beyond 50 percent without recipe adjustment produces a dense bake.
