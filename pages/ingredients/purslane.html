Ingredient

Purslane

Portulaca oleracea. A succulent leafy summer green with a faintly lemony, slightly mucilaginous bite — eaten across the Mediterranean, the Levant, Mexico, and Central Asia, dismissed as a weed across most of North America.

About

Purslane (also called pigweed in North America, verdolagas in Mexico, baqle in Persian, farfaha in Arabic, semizotu in Turkish, tribulus in Greek-Cypriot tradition) is the leaf and stem of Portulaca oleracea, a low-growing succulent annual found everywhere on Earth where summers are warm and soil is disturbed. The plant is genuinely cosmopolitan: it grows wild in sidewalk cracks across North America, in Mediterranean garden margins, on Cretan hillsides where it has been foraged for thousands of years, in Mexican cornfields where it is a beloved summer green, and in Indian and Levantine kitchen gardens where it is intentionally cultivated. The leaf is small (1 to 2 cm), thick-walled, succulent, and faintly sour-lemony; the stem is reddish-pink, tender when young and stringy when older; the seeds are produced in tiny capsules and are themselves a forgotten Greco-Egyptian grain.

The flavor profile and the mucilage. Purslane reads as a mild green with a distinctive faint lemony tartness (from oxalic acid) and a slight slippery-mucilaginous mouthfeel (from the same polysaccharide gum that gives okra its slime, present in much smaller quantity). The lemony note is gentle — nothing like sorrel's aggressive sour ; but it is the flavor signature: a salad with purslane has a faint citric register that the same salad without purslane lacks. The mucilage is barely perceptible in a fresh raw leaf, more noticeable in a chopped older stem, and quite present in a long-cooked preparation; this is why purslane is used raw in salads more often than cooked, and why the Mexican verdolagas dishes (purslane stewed with pork in green or red sauce) build the slight viscosity into the sauce structure.

Nutritional note. Purslane is the highest-known plant source of omega-3 fatty acids (alpha-linolenic acid, ALA), at roughly 4 mg of ALA per gram of fresh weight ; several times the concentration in spinach or arugula. The plant also carries meaningful magnesium, potassium, vitamins A and C, and the same carotenoid profile as many leafy greens. None of this changes how the plant is used in the kitchen, but it explains some of the historical persistence of purslane as a foraged vegetable across cultures: pre-modern populations needed the omega-3 and the micronutrients, and a weed that grows everywhere is functionally a free crop. The oxalic acid content is moderate (similar to spinach); cooks who avoid spinach for kidney-stone reasons should treat purslane similarly.

Identification. The leaves are small (less than 2 cm), thick, glossy, paddle-shaped, succulent (will feel firm and full of water when squeezed); the stems are smooth, thick, reddish or pink, jointed at the leaves; the plant grows low to the ground in a sprawling mat. The most common confusion is with spurge (Euphorbia maculata or similar), which superficially resembles purslane but has thinner leaves with a central seam, exudes a milky white sap when broken, and is mildly toxic. Purslane has clear sap, not milky, and the leaves are thick-succulent, not flat. If you forage rather than buy, learn to break a stem and check the sap; the rule is absolute. Cultivated purslane sold at farmer's markets and specialty grocers is unambiguously P. oleracea and the foraging concern is moot.

Applications by category. Levantine: fattoush (the optional-but-traditional summer green), tabbouleh (in some regional variants), salata khadra (mixed-greens salad). Mexican and Central American: verdolagas con puerco (pork stewed with purslane in green tomatillo or red ancho sauce); verdolagas in tacos and tortas as a cooked filling; raw in summer salads. Greek and Cypriot: salads, particularly in summer when the plant is in full season; folded into horta (cooked-greens preparations) along with dandelion, chard, and amaranth leaves. Turkish and Persian: in summer salads (semizotu salatası in Turkey); in mast-o-khiar-style yogurt salads (Persian); dressed with olive oil and lemon as a side dish. Indian: in dal preparations, as a cooked green alongside chana dal and toor dal. Modern Western: a niche restaurant-pantry green increasingly stocked at farmer's markets and specialty grocers in summer; used the way the cook would use watercress.

Buying. Look at farmer's markets in summer (June through September in most of the temperate Northern Hemisphere); sometimes available at Mexican grocers (verdolagas) and Middle Eastern markets year-round when imported. The bunch should look fresh, vibrant green-and-pink, with no yellowing or wilting; the stems should snap when bent rather than fold. Do not foraged purslane from a sprayed lawn or a roadside (pesticide and lead concerns); only forage from known-safe gardens or wild areas.

Storage

Refrigerated in a paper-towel-lined container, leaves only if separated from stems, 4 to 6 days. Whole bunches with stems and leaves together hold 2 to 4 days. The succulent leaves do not wilt the way tender greens do; instead they yellow and turn translucent when past prime. Failure modes: yellowing leaves (chlorophyll breakdown, discard); slimy texture in the bag (bacterial breakdown, discard the bunch); excessive flowering and going-to-seed in the bunch (older plant, the leaves are smaller and more bitter ; still usable but the texture is compromised).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • purslane (in fattoush, tabbouleh, and Levantine salads) → watercress, or omit and add 25 percent more parsley and mint Watercress brings a peppery character that purslane lacks but the structural role (extra leafy green for body) reads similar. Omitting purslane is a fully legitimate option ; it is a traditional inclusion, not a structural one.
  • purslane (in cooked applications ; verdolagas con puerco, dal preparations) → spinach with a squeeze of lemon, or amaranth greens Spinach has similar oxalic-acid content and a related leaf structure but lacks purslane's mucilage; the lemon compensates for the missing tartness. Amaranth greens are the closer cooking analogue if available.
  • purslane (when foraged at home) → cultivated purslane from a farmer's market Foraging risk (spurge confusion, pesticide exposure on lawns) is real for the inexperienced; the cultivated product is the same plant grown for kitchen use and is the safer first-time option.
