Ingredient

Galangal

Pale rhizome of Alpinia galanga, related to ginger but flavorally distinct. Piney, citrusy, with a faint medicinal eucalyptus note. Not substitutable with ginger.

About

Galangal (Alpinia galanga, called kha in Thai, laos in Indonesian, lengkuas in Malay) shares the ginger family but is flavorally a different animal. Where ginger is warm, pungent, and slightly sweet from gingerols and shogaols, galangal is piney, citrusy, almost camphor-like, driven by 1,8-cineole (eucalyptus aroma) and methyl cinnamate (warm-cinnamon note). Substituting one for the other produces an audibly wrong dish; this is the most common Western drift in Thai and Indonesian cooking.

The rhizome is paler, harder, and more fibrous than ginger; cutting it feels like slicing into a piece of dense wood, and a sharp knife is non-negotiable. The skin is thinner and lighter beige, often striped with translucent rings.

Two preparations. For broths (tom kha gai, soto ayam, rendang), slice into coins 2 to 3 mm thick and add whole; the slices flavor the liquid without disintegrating, and diners eat around them or fish them out. For spice pastes (Thai curry pastes, Indonesian bumbu, Malaysian rempah), peel and slice paper-thin against the grain, then pound or blend smooth. The fiber load is significant; pounded properly it integrates into the paste, blended in a food processor it often leaves stringy bits that need to be sieved.

Fresh galangal is the standard; frozen retains 85 percent of the aromatic load and is structurally identical for paste-making. Dried galangal slices are useful for broths but lose the citrusy top note within months; galangal powder is largely a waste.

Storage

Fresh: wrap in paper towel, refrigerate 3 to 4 weeks, or freeze whole rhizome in a sealed bag for a year. Frozen galangal is easier to slice than fresh because the fibers stiffen with cold.

Substitutions

  • galangal (in broth or paste) → half the weight in ginger plus a strip of lemon zest and a pinch of black peppercorn A genuine substitution emergency only. The flavor profile is structurally different; ginger contributes pungent warmth where galangal contributes piney-camphor brightness. Acceptable for a single weeknight meal, not for a Thai curry intended to read authentic.
