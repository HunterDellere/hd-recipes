Ingredient

Yellow split peas

Dried, hulled, mechanically split field peas. They cook down to a sweet, earthy purée in dals and soups, and fry into a hard, nutty crunch in Burmese tea-leaf salad.

About

Yellow split peas are the dried, hulled, and split seeds of the field pea (Pisum sativum), the same species as the fresh green garden pea but a starchy, drying cultivar harvested fully mature rather than green and tender. Splitting is purely mechanical: the dried pea is passed through rollers that remove the outer hull and cleave the seed along its natural seam into two cotyledon halves. Removing the hull and exposing the interior is what lets split peas cook without soaking and break down readily, where a whole dried pea would need hours and stay intact. The seed is roughly 25% protein and 60% carbohydrate (mostly starch, with a meaningful soluble-fiber fraction), which is why split peas both thicken a soup as they disintegrate and deliver real protein.

Two opposite roles, governed by water. Hydrated and simmered, yellow split peas collapse into a sweet, mild, earthy purée, the body of pea-and-ham soup, of many South Asian dals (where the closely related chana dal, split brown chickpea, is often interchanged), and of thick legume porridges. They need no pre-soak; 40 to 60 minutes at a simmer takes them from hard to spoonable, faster if the cooking water is soft (hard water and acid both slow the softening, so salt and tomato go in late). Dried and deep-fried straight from the bag without hydrating, the same pea becomes a hard, nutty, intensely crunchy nugget, the defining textural element scattered over Burmese lahpet thoke (pickled tea-leaf salad) alongside fried beans, peanuts, and sesame. The two uses share an ingredient and nothing else: one is about full starch gelatinization in water, the other about driving off the seed's residual moisture in hot oil until it shatters.

For the fried crunch, the pea is usually given a brief soak (20 to 30 minutes, not the full hydration of a dal) and dried thoroughly before frying, so the interior cooks through to a clean crunch rather than staying raw-hard in the center while the outside browns. Frying medium-low and long, rather than hot and fast, is the technique: the pea must dehydrate and cook through before it colors, or it burns bitter outside and stays chalky inside.

Seasonality

A dried pantry staple, available and stable year-round. No seasonal variation that matters to the cook.

Storage

Stored airtight in a cool, dark, dry place, yellow split peas keep 1 to 2 years, though older peas (over a year) take noticeably longer to soften and may never fully break down in a soup, a sign the seed has dried past the point of easy rehydration. They do not go rancid the way oily nuts and seeds do, but they will absorb moisture and odors, and very old peas harbor pantry pests; keep them sealed in glass or a tight tub. Discard if you see webbing, tiny moths, or any musty smell. Fried split peas, once cooled, keep their crunch a week or two in an airtight jar but soften with humidity; re-crisp briefly in a dry pan if they go stale.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • yellow split peas (dal, soup body) → chana dal (split brown chickpea) or red lentils Chana dal is the closest cousin, slightly firmer and nuttier, holds its shape a touch more. Red lentils cook faster and break down softer; reduce simmer time by half and expect a looser purée.
  • yellow split peas (the fried crunch on lahpet thoke) → fried split chickpeas (chana dal) or roasted chana Split chickpeas fry to a very similar hard crunch and are traditional in some lahpet thoke versions. Pre-roasted chana (sold ready in South Asian shops) skips the frying entirely with a comparable result.
