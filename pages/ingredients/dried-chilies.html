Ingredient

Dried Chinese chilies

Sun-dried whole chilies sold by varietal: erjingtiao, chao tian jiao, da hong pao, tian tian, deng long jiao. Each cultivar has a distinct heat-aroma split that drives Sichuan, Hunan, and Northwestern Chinese cooking.

About

Dried Chinese chilies are the foundational red-spice ingredient across Sichuan, Hunan, Yunnan, and Northwestern Chinese cooking. The cuisine treats heat and aroma as separable variables, and the cultivar choice for any given dish is the cook's first decision: a Sichuan dry-pot built on the wrong chili reads either flat (insufficient aroma) or punishingly hot (insufficient body). Western pantries that stock "red pepper flakes" or "dried red chiles" as a single SKU lose this entire dimension of the cooking; the named cultivars are not interchangeable.

The five cultivars to know:

  • Erjingtiao (二荆条): the canonical Sichuan chili. Long (10 to 14 cm), slender, deep brick-red when dried, with a fragrant, complex aroma and moderate heat (15,000 to 35,000 Scoville). The default chili for Pixian doubanjiang production, mapo tofu, twice-cooked pork, and most Sichuan stir-fries. Use whole (with stem) for visual presence in dry-pot or break into 2 cm pieces for stir-fry. The cultivar's signature is aroma-forward heat: the kitchen smells distinctly Sichuan when these chilies hit hot oil.

  • Chao tian jiao (朝天椒, "facing-heaven chili"): small (2 to 4 cm), squat, deep red, points upright on the plant (hence the name). Hotter than erjingtiao (50,000 to 75,000 Scoville) with a sharper, less complex aroma. The right chili for adding heat without dominating the flavor profile, often used together with erjingtiao to balance heat-and-aroma in dry-pot and Sichuan boiled-fish (shui zhu yu). Use whole for dry-pot, broken in half for stir-fry.

  • Da hong pao (大红袍, "big red robe"): large (5 to 7 cm), wide, deep red, named for its visually striking color rather than heat (it is moderate, 30,000 to 40,000 Scoville). The cultivar is grown specifically for color contribution to chili oil and red-braised dishes. Aroma is rounded and fruity. The chili behind the brightest red Sichuan chili oils.

  • Tian tian / Tien tsin (天鹰椒): small (3 to 5 cm), thin-walled, very hot (50,000 to 90,000 Scoville). Originally from Tianjin (天津 → "tien tsin" in older Cantonese romanization, hence "Tien Tsin chili" on Chinese-American restaurant menus). Aggressively hot with a sharp, slightly grassy aroma. The default for Hunan cooking and the chili-rich Sichuan dishes that prioritize heat over fragrance. Use broken or whole; cooks often crush them coarsely for stir-fry.

  • Deng long jiao (灯笼椒, "lantern chili"): medium-large (4 to 6 cm), bulbous lantern-shape, deep red. Mild heat (10,000 to 20,000 Scoville) with a sweet, slightly fruity aroma. Used in Sichuan and Yunnan for color and aroma without aggressive heat; sometimes substituted for da hong pao in chili oil production.

Whole vs broken vs ground. Whole chilies in hot oil release aroma slowly and contribute color and flavor without dispersing pieces of chili into the dish; this is the right form for dry-pot, where the chilies sit alongside the proteins as a visual and aromatic component but are not eaten directly. Broken chilies (cut or torn into 2 cm pieces with seeds released or partly retained) release more heat and more aroma, faster; this is the right form for stir-fry where the chili pieces become part of the dish. Ground chilies (pulse-ground in a spice grinder until coarse-flake texture) become "Sichuan chili flakes" (la jiao mian) used in chili oil production and as a finishing seasoning.

Heat-from-seeds note. The seeds of Chinese chilies carry significant heat and a slightly bitter, hay-like aroma. Cooks shake them out for "milder" preparations and retain them for "hotter" ones; Sichuan chefs typically retain seeds in their primary cooking and shake them out only when making chili oil for delicate applications. The seeds also age faster than the flesh and contribute to off-flavors in old chilies; if you can shake the seeds out and the chilies still smell vibrant, the chilies are still good. If shaken-out seeds smell stale, the whole batch is past prime.

Buying notes. Look for chilies that are deep red (faded brick or brown means old), pliable rather than brittle (very brittle chilies have dried beyond ideal), and aromatic when the bag is opened (no aroma means they are well past prime). Chinese groceries stock named cultivars in the dried-spice aisle; specialty Chinese spice purveyors (Mala Market, The Mala Project, Chengdu Mala Market) sell single-cultivar chilies at higher quality than supermarket bulk bins. The cultivar should be on the label in pinyin or Chinese; if the label only says "dried red chilies," the product is mixed-variety and the cook cannot predict the heat-aroma split.

Toasting before use. Dry-toasting whole chilies in a dry wok over medium heat for 60 to 90 seconds, until the color deepens and the chilies smell intensely fragrant, dramatically improves their contribution to the finished dish. The aromatic oils volatilize and re-deposit on the chili surface; subsequent cooking in oil extracts them more efficiently. Skip toasting if the chilies are going into a long-simmered braise (the long heat does the same work) but do not skip for stir-fries, dry-pot, or chili-oil production.

Heat scaling. The cook controls heat by chili choice and quantity, not by adjusting other variables. A dry-pot that calls for 30 g erjingtiao + 10 g chao tian jiao at standard heat can be scaled to mild by halving the chao tian jiao while keeping the erjingtiao constant; the dish stays Sichuan-aromatic without becoming punishingly hot. Conversely, doubling the chao tian jiao while keeping erjingtiao constant produces a hotter dish without losing aromatic complexity. The two cultivars do not substitute for each other.

Storage

Sealed in a glass jar (not the original plastic bag, which is permeable to aroma over months) in a cool dark pantry: 6 to 9 months at full quality, 12 months acceptable. Refrigerated: 18 months. Frozen: 2 years (the freezer preserves the volatile aromatics). The flavor degrades faster than visual appearance; chilies that look red and fine but smell weak in the bag will produce a flat dish. Replace the stock every 6 to 9 months for the best Sichuan cooking.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • erjingtiao (Sichuan stir-fry, dry-pot, chili oil) → Mexican guajillo or California chiles Both are mild, fruity, and produce a similar aromatic-forward heat profile. The dish reads less authentically Sichuan but the structural function is covered; this is the closest Western-pantry stand-in.
  • chao tian jiao or tien tsin (heat-forward dishes) → Mexican chiles de árbol Similar small, hot, sharp-aromatic profile. Acceptable substitute when Chinese cultivars are unavailable.
  • da hong pao (red color in chili oil) → sweet Hungarian or Spanish paprika, added in addition to other dried chilies Paprika contributes the deep red color without significant heat; combine 1 tbsp paprika per 30 g hot dried chilies to approximate da hong pao's color contribution.
  • unspecified "dried red chilies" in old recipes → erjingtiao When a recipe is vague, default to erjingtiao. It is the canonical chili and most recipes are tuned to its heat-aroma profile by tradition even when the cultivar is not named.
