Ingredient

Ají amarillo paste

The blended paste of the Peruvian yellow chili (Capsicum baccatum). Fruity, moderately hot, the defining flavor of Peruvian cooking.

About

Ají amarillo is Capsicum baccatum, a chili species distinct from the more common Capsicum annuum (jalapeño, bell, ancho) and Capsicum chinense (habanero, scotch bonnet). The fresh pod is bright orange-yellow, finger-shaped, 8 to 12 cm long, with thin walls and a fruity-floral aroma that is the structural signature of Peruvian cooking. Heat level is moderate (30,000 to 50,000 SHU, between jalapeño and serrano) but the flavor is the point, not the burn.

The paste form is the practical kitchen product outside Peru where fresh pods are nearly impossible to find. Manufacturers (Doña Isabel, Inca's Food, Belmont) blanch the pods to mellow the heat, blend with water and a small amount of oil and salt, and jar at roughly 1 kg per jar. The paste reads concentrated, brightly orange, and slightly oily; one tablespoon of paste roughly equals two fresh pods.

Functional category: aromatic chili base. Ají amarillo paste forms the structural flavor of ají de gallina (chicken stewed in ají-cream sauce thickened with bread), papa a la huancaína (potatoes in ají-cheese sauce), lomo saltado (where it adds a faint background heat to the Chinese-Peruvian stir-fry), causa rellena (cold layered potato cake), and ocopa (the ají-and-huacatay sauce of Arequipa).

Without ají amarillo, none of these dishes is recognizably itself; the fruity-floral chili profile is not substitutable from any other widely available chili. Frozen pulp (sold at some Latin American groceries) is the closest substitute to fresh; a 50/50 blend of habanero (heat) and roasted yellow bell pepper (sweetness, color, body) is the desperate-substitute hack that captures rough color and rough heat without the floral note.

Storage

Refrigerated after opening, 3 to 4 months in the original jar with a clean spoon (cross-contamination from a dirty spoon is the failure mode; the paste itself is acidic and salty enough to resist spoilage if kept clean). Freeze in 1 tbsp portions in ice cube trays for 12 months; thawed paste loses some textural integrity but retains flavor. Unopened jars: pantry, by the printed date (typically 2 years).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • ají amarillo paste → frozen ají amarillo pulp (50% more by weight) Frozen pulp is less concentrated than paste; use 1.5 times the weight. Thaw and drain excess water before measuring. Available at well-stocked Latin American groceries.
  • ají amarillo paste → a blend of 1 part habanero paste and 2 parts roasted yellow bell pepper puree The desperate substitute when no Peruvian chili is available. Provides rough color and rough heat without the floral note; the dish reads recognizably wrong but functional. Use the same weight as the paste would be.
