Ingredient

Greek yogurt

Whole-milk yogurt strained through cloth to remove a substantial fraction of the whey. Concentrated protein (10 percent), thicker texture, tangier flavor than American-style yogurt. Structural ingredient in dips, sauces, and Eastern Mediterranean cooking.

About

Real Greek yogurt is whole-milk yogurt that has been cultured (whole milk plus a starter of Lactobacillus bulgaricus and Streptococcus thermophilus, held warm at 42 to 45 °C for 4 to 8 hours until it sets), then strained through cheesecloth or a fine fabric for 4 to 8 hours to remove a substantial fraction of the whey. The result is a yogurt with roughly twice the protein concentration (10 percent versus 4 to 5 percent for unstrained yogurt) and a markedly thicker, almost cream-cheese-like texture. The straining is the entire technical difference; without straining, the same cultured milk is just yogurt.

The American supermarket "Greek yogurt" problem. Most yogurt sold in the US under the "Greek" label is not strained yogurt; it is American-style yogurt thickened with added milk solids, milk protein concentrate, gelatin, or modified starches to mimic the texture of strained yogurt at lower cost. The protein concentration is comparable on the nutrition label, but the flavor and the cooking behavior differ significantly. Real strained Greek yogurt has the concentrated lactic tang of the original yogurt amplified by the removal of the diluting whey; thickened-American product is mild and often slightly sweet. In cooking, real strained yogurt holds its texture under heat (it can be folded into a sauce without weeping or breaking); thickened-American product weeps water and breaks at the slightest temperature shift. The difference is most noticeable in tzatziki, in cucumber-yogurt sauces, in marinades for grilled meat, and in any application where the yogurt is a structural ingredient rather than a topping.

Distinguishing real from thickened. Read the ingredient label: real Greek yogurt has milk and live cultures, full stop (sometimes cream is added for richness in the "full-fat strained" line). Thickened-American product lists milk, cultures, and then milk solids, milk protein concentrate, pectin, gelatin, or modified food starch. Brands that produce real strained Greek yogurt sold in US markets: Fage Total (the Greek brand, the benchmark), Stonyfield Greek (US-made but genuinely strained), Cabot (US-made strained), Wallaby Organic (Australian-style strained). Brands that thicken with additives: Chobani standard line, Yoplait Greek, most domestic store-brand "Greek yogurts" sold in plastic tubs in the dairy aisle. The premium for real strained yogurt is roughly 30 to 50 percent higher; for cooking applications, it''s the only product worth using.

Fat content. Greek yogurt is sold in 0 percent (nonfat), 2 percent (low-fat), and 5 to 10 percent (full-fat) variants. The fat percentage matters cooks should default to full-fat (typically 5 to 10 percent) for structural cooking applications: full-fat strained yogurt holds its texture under heat, lubricates the palate, and provides the mouthfeel that the dish was built to deliver. Nonfat strained yogurt is for cooks managing fat intake; it works in dips and dressings but reads thin and chalky in the dishes that depend on full-fat texture (tzatziki, the lubricating element in whipped feta, the base for marinated lamb).

Cooking transformation. Yogurt''s milk proteins (caseins primarily) are heat-stable up to about 80 °C; above that, the proteins denature and the yogurt curdles into ricotta-like solids weeping clear whey. This is why yogurt is rarely added to a hot pan in the way cream is; the cook either tempers the yogurt by stirring a little hot liquid into the cold yogurt before combining (raises the temperature gradually), or stabilizes the yogurt with a starch (a teaspoon of cornstarch or flour whisked into the cold yogurt prevents curdling at higher temperatures), or uses the yogurt off-heat as a finishing element. The Indian and Persian canon has yogurt-stabilization techniques baked into the cuisine (e.g. khoresh-e fesenjan, the long-simmered Persian stew with yogurt; raita, which is served cold; biryani, where yogurt is layered into the cold rice but never boiled).

Applications by category. The Greek and Mediterranean canon uses Greek yogurt in tzatziki (with grated cucumber, garlic, dill, olive oil), in tirokafteri-style spiced cheese dips, in marinades for grilled lamb and chicken (the lactic acid tenderizes the meat), and as a finishing dollop on stuffed peppers and roast vegetables. The Levantine canon uses very similar strained yogurts (labneh is Levantine strained yogurt, often strained further to a thick spreadable cheese-like consistency) in mezze plates with za''atar and olive oil, in shakshuka-adjacent dishes, and as the dairy element in many cooked dishes. The Indian canon uses both strained and unstrained yogurt in raita, in marinades for tandoori-style cooking, and in curries (where stabilization technique matters). The American restaurant canon uses Greek yogurt as a sour-cream replacement in dressings and dips, as the lubricator in whipped-feta-style spreads, and as a marinade base for grilled chicken.

Buying. For cooking, default to Fage Total 5% (the full-fat strained line; the 0% and 2% are also genuinely strained but the texture is thinner) or another label-verified strained yogurt. The container should say "strained" somewhere on it (some labels are direct, others bury it in marketing copy); the ingredient list should be milk, cream (in full-fat), and live cultures only. Avoid plastic tubs of "Greek yogurt" that list milk solids, gums, gelatin, or starches; these are the thickened-American product and behave differently in cooking. Greek and Middle Eastern grocery stores often carry imported Greek yogurt at lower prices than US supermarkets; Fage and Total brand from Greek markets are identical to the US-label Fage product but cheaper.

Storage

Refrigerator in original sealed container 2 to 3 weeks past the printed sell-by date for unopened tubs; 1 to 2 weeks after opening. The whey will continue to separate to the surface as a clear or yellowish liquid; this is normal and the yogurt is fine, just stir back in or pour off as desired. Failure modes: pink, orange, or fuzzy mold on the surface (discard); sour-bitter smell well beyond the normal lactic tang (over-cultured, discard); separated into watery whey on top of paste-like solids that won't recombine (broken, discard); fizzy or carbonated texture (gas-producing wild fermentation, discard). Frozen Greek yogurt holds 2 months but the texture breaks irreversibly on thaw; thawed yogurt is for smoothies and baking only, not for raw applications.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • Greek yogurt (cooking and structural application) → labneh (Levantine strained yogurt, often goat) Functionally identical, often thicker and tangier. The Lebanese and Israeli grocery option; pricier and more concentrated. Excellent in any application where Greek yogurt is called for; reduce the quantity by 10 percent or thin with a tablespoon of water if the recipe targets a specific looseness.
  • Greek yogurt (raw dip or topping application) → sour cream (for richness) or crème fraîche (for elegance) Both lack the lactic-tang depth of strained yogurt and shift the dish toward American-Mediterranean rather than authentic Greek. The richness and mouthfeel are similar; the flavor profile is meaningfully different. Use when authenticity is not the goal.
  • Greek yogurt (in marinades for grilled meat) → whole-milk plain yogurt + 30-minute cheesecloth strain A 30-minute strain through cheesecloth concentrates regular yogurt enough for marinade applications without the full 4-to-8-hour Greek-yogurt strain. The texture is looser than commercial Greek yogurt but the lactic-acid tenderizing function is intact.
  • Greek yogurt (whipped or processed application) → whole-milk ricotta + 1 tbsp / 15 g lemon juice per cup Ricotta provides the creamy structure; the lemon adds the tang. Different cheese family entirely but the texture works for whipped-feta-style dips when no Greek yogurt is available.
