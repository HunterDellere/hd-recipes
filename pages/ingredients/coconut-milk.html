Ingredient

Coconut milk (canned, full-fat)

Pressed coconut emulsion, ~17–22% fat. The lipid phase that carries fat-soluble aromatics in Thai and Southeast Asian curries.

About

Canned full-fat coconut milk is an oil-in-water emulsion of pressed coconut endosperm. The fat range is 17–22% by weight; the rest is water plus trace protein and carbohydrate. It separates on standing: the cream layer (denser fat fraction stabilized by coconut protein) rises and thickens, and the watery serum sinks. Shaking re-homogenizes; not shaking lets you skim the cream off, which is what most Thai curries call for.

Density runs close to 1.00 g/ml. The fat (~0.92 g/ml) and water (1.00 g/ml) components average to near unity, with full-fat brands trending slightly above water. A standard 13.5 fl oz / 400 ml can weighs ~395–405 g depending on fat content and brand fill. Treating 1 can = 400 g for scaling math is accurate to within a percent.

Two-ingredient brands (coconut, water) are the working standard. Aroy-D and Chaokoh are canonical. Brands that add gums (guar, xanthan, carrageenan) prevent the cream from separating, which defeats the cracking-the-cream technique that builds Thai curries: the homogenized version refuses to fry the paste; it just steams it.

Heat behavior: at a hard boil the emulsion permanently breaks into greasy pools. Hold curries at a low simmer (~190°F / 88°C) and the emulsion stays intact and silky. A reserved spoon of cold cream stirred in off-heat at the end reseats any thinning the simmer caused.

Storage

Unopened cans keep 2+ years at room temperature. Once opened, transfer to a non-reactive container and refrigerate; use within 4 days. Do not freeze in the can (the contents expand). Frozen and thawed coconut milk separates irreversibly; only use thawed coconut milk where breakage is acceptable (rice, rice porridge).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • canned full-fat coconut milk (curries, sauces) → canned coconut cream + water (3:1 by weight) Coconut cream runs 24–30% fat. Diluting 3 parts cream to 1 part water lands close to full-fat coconut milk. Useful when a store carries cream but not full-fat milk.
  • canned full-fat coconut milk (lighter dishes) → unsweetened cashew cream Different flavor profile: cashew is neutral-sweet where coconut is tropical. Density and emulsion behavior are similar; works in Indian-style sauces. Wrong for Thai green/red curry, where the coconut aroma is part of the dish.
  • canned full-fat coconut milk (broth-style soups) → lite coconut milk + 1 tbsp / 14g coconut oil per cup Reconstructs the fat ratio of full-fat from a lite (≤9% fat) base. For tom kha specifically, this matches the texture closely enough that a side-by-side is hard to call.
