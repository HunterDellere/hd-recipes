Ingredient

Halibut

Large Pacific or Atlantic flatfish (Hippoglossus stenolepis or H. hippoglossus). Dense, mild, low-fat white flesh; cooks in a narrow window because of the low collagen and low intramuscular fat. The premium "firm white fish" for chunks in stews and seared fillets.

About

Halibut is a large bottom-dwelling flatfish in the right-eye flounder family (Pleuronectidae). Two species are sold under the name: Pacific halibut (Hippoglossus stenolepis, the dominant species in U.S. markets, harvested from the Bering Sea to the California coast under tight Alaskan and Canadian management), and Atlantic halibut (Hippoglossus hippoglossus, the larger and now mostly farmed species, with wild populations depleted; the species traditionally associated with British "halibut and chips"). Adult fish reach 1 to 2 meters and 30 to 200 kg; supermarket fillets are cross-sections from very large fish, sold as steaks (bone-in cross-cuts of the body) or boneless fillets (rectangular blocks cut from the four "quarters" of the flatfish, two dorsal and two ventral).

The flesh is bright white when raw, opaque white when cooked, with a notably dense, large-flaked structure. Composition: ~80 percent water, ~19 percent protein, ~1 to 2 percent fat (very lean by white-fish standards; cod is 0.7 percent fat, salmon is 13 percent for comparison). The connective tissue is short-fiber collagen between muscle blocks, similar to cod and black sea bass but slightly tighter; the flake is larger and more dense than cod. The flavor is mild, sweet, and clean, with a faint mineral backnote; halibut is the white fish that reads "premium" in a Western register, fetching 2 to 3 times the price of cod.

Transformation. The low fat content is the critical operational fact: halibut has very little intramuscular fat to buffer overcooking, and the dry-out cliff is sharp. Internal temperature targets: 50 °C (122 °F) for rare-medium-rare (sashimi-adjacent, still translucent at the center, possible for sashimi-grade fish only); 54 to 57 °C (130 to 135 °F) for medium (opaque, just-flaky, juicy, the restaurant target); 60 °C (140 °F) is the upper limit for keeping the fish moist; past 63 °C (145 °F, the USDA "fully cooked" target) the protein has expelled most of its water and the flesh reads chalky. Pacific halibut at 145 °F internal is a famously dry preparation; the dish redeems itself with butter sauces or moist-cooking methods, but the fish is unforgiving on a sear past 130 °F.

The flake structure transforms at 50 °C: collagen between muscle blocks softens and the dense raw block separates into the canonical thumb-sized flakes when prodded. The protein contraction that expels water peaks between 60 and 75 °C; halibut held at 65 °C for 5 minutes will weep substantially. Optimal cooking methods are therefore those that allow precise control: pan sear with a 30-second flip rhythm (target 130 to 135 °F at the center), oven-roast at 200 °C / 400 °F for 8 to 10 minutes by 2.5 cm thickness (pull at 130 °F, carryover to 135 °F), poach in court bouillon at 65 to 70 °C for 8 minutes, or submerge in a barely-simmering tomato-saffron broth (cioppino) for 4 to 6 minutes by 3 cm chunk thickness. Steam works but the chunks toughen at the edges if the steam is too aggressive. Sous vide at 51 to 54 °C for 30 to 45 minutes is the chef-test temperature window for restaurant precision.

Applications by category. In North-American Italian cooking, halibut is the firm-white-fish chunk in San Francisco cioppino (the structural white-fish element, alongside clams, mussels, crab, and shrimp); the dense flake holds its shape in a tomato-saffron broth where softer fish would fall apart. In French and Mediterranean cooking, halibut substitutes for the rockfish-and-conger of Provençal bouillabaisse when those are unavailable, and pairs canonically with brown butter, lemon, and capers (sole meuniere translated to a thicker fillet). In Pacific Northwest American cooking, halibut is grilled with butter and herbs, en papillote with leeks and white wine, or beer-battered for fish and chips (the canonical Alaskan preparation). In Japanese cooking, hirame (which translates as "flatfish" but specifically means right-eye flounders like halibut) is sliced thin for sashimi (when sashimi-grade) or salt-grilled (shio-yaki); the firm flesh holds the salt cure well. In Korean cooking, halibut substitutes for the local gwangeo in raw hweh preparations. In all preparations, the operational concern is the same: pull the fish at 130 to 135 °F internal, never trust a 145 °F target unless the dish is sauced enough to hide the result.

Buying notes. Pacific halibut (the Alaskan-managed species) is the more sustainable and more available choice in U.S. markets; the season runs March through November, with fresh-caught quality peaking in spring and early summer. Frozen-at-sea Pacific halibut (Costco, Whole Foods, specialty Asian markets like H Mart) is excellent and often a better choice than "fresh" supermarket fillets that have been thawed and sat on ice for 2 to 4 days. Look for fillets with bright white, translucent-edged flesh (not gray, yellow, or chalky); a clean ocean smell with no ammonia; tight, intact flake structure (large-flaked, not falling apart); and ideally skin-on for one side, which carries the visual cue of freshness and protects the flesh during cooking. Atlantic halibut is mostly farmed now and reads softer and slightly oilier than wild Pacific; acceptable but distinct. Avoid "halibut" labeled with no species qualifier in budget-supermarket fish counters; the fish may be cheaper greenland halibut (Reinhardtius hippoglossoides, a different species marketed as "lesser halibut," softer and oilier). Size: a 200 to 250 g fillet section, 2.5 to 3 cm thick, is the right per-person portion for a sear or roast; for stews, 150 to 175 g of 3 cm chunks per person.

Western drift. The single biggest mistake in home halibut cooking is to treat it like salmon and cook to USDA temperature; the resulting fish is chalky, dry, and disappoints. The chef-level rule is pull at 130 to 135 °F internal, rest 2 minutes, and serve. Halibut also reads as a "premium" fish that should be served plainly; this is half right (the fish has restraint built in) but undersells the dish where halibut shines structurally, which is a stew with bivalves and crab where the firm flake is the foil for everything else.

Storage

Refrigerator: 1 to 2 days for fresh fillets, kept on ice in a perforated container over a drain pan, paper towel between the flesh and the ice. Frozen-at-sea fillets in vacuum bags: 4 to 6 months at -18 °C; defrost in the refrigerator overnight, never at room temperature. Cooked halibut: 2 days refrigerated, reheated gently or eaten cold. The fish does not hold well as leftovers because of the dense flake; better to undercook the original portion and reheat in sauce than to fully cook then reheat.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • halibut (cioppino or fish stew, chunks) → firm white fish: cod, lingcod, black sea bass, Pacific rockfish, or monkfish Cod is the most available and pairs well with the tomato-saffron broth (slightly more flaky than halibut, reads similar). Lingcod, black sea bass, and Pacific rockfish are the West Coast canonical substitutes for cioppino and read structurally identical. Monkfish has a lobster-like texture and works well but reads as a different dish.
  • halibut (pan-seared or roasted fillet) → cod, black sea bass, striped bass, or branzino fillet For a sear at 130 to 135 °F internal: cod is too flaky and falls apart in the pan, prefer striped bass or black sea bass for the searing case. Branzino fillets are excellent but smaller; use two per person.
  • halibut (sashimi or hweh) → fluke (summer flounder) or sashimi-grade striped bass Fluke is the closest in texture and flavor for sashimi specifically; the fish must be sashimi-grade (commercially flash-frozen at -20 °C for 7 days to kill parasites per FDA guidance).
  • halibut (English fish and chips) → cod or haddock The British canon uses cod or haddock; halibut is the Alaskan and Pacific-Northwest American version. Same beer-batter and frying technique; cod reads slightly more flaky in the batter, haddock slightly drier and milder. All three work.
