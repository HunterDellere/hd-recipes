Ingredient

Kaffir lime leaves (makrut lime leaves)

Glossy, double-lobed leaves of Citrus hystrix. Floral citrus aroma from citronellal, bruised whole in broths or shredded fine in salads. No real substitute.

About

Citrus hystrix, the makrut lime tree, produces a knobby fruit with almost no juice but two intensely aromatic parts: the bumpy zest and the leaves. The leaves grow in distinctive double lobes (figure-eight shape), are deep glossy green, and carry citronellal as the dominant aromatic, the same compound that gives lemongrass its green-citrus top note. The aroma is floral-citrus, brighter and more perfumed than any lime zest, with a faint pine backbone.

Note on the name. "Kaffir" is a slur in southern African contexts and most contemporary Thai cooking writers have moved to "makrut lime leaves" (from the Thai bai magrood). Recipes still use both names interchangeably; this page uses kaffir lime leaves as the more searchable term while acknowledging the issue.

Three preparations. For broths and curries, bruise whole leaves between palms or with the back of a knife and add 4 to 6 leaves per pot; they perfume the liquid over 10 to 15 minutes and get fished out before serving. For salads and stir-fries (larb, pad kee mao, Indonesian rendang finish), remove the tough central rib of each leaf, stack the leaves, and shred into the finest possible ribbons (chiffonade); the leaves are eaten directly so the cut must be hair-thin or the texture turns leathery. For spice pastes (Thai red and green curry paste), pound shredded leaves into the paste, the volatile oils release as the paste fries.

Fresh leaves are the gold standard; frozen retains 90 percent of the aromatic load and is functionally equivalent. Dried leaves are largely flavorless within a month of drying; avoid unless desperate.

Storage

Fresh: wrap in paper towel inside a sealed bag, refrigerate 2 to 3 weeks, or freeze whole in a sealed bag for a year. Frozen leaves shred slightly less cleanly than fresh but flavor identically. Buy in bulk when you find them; freeze immediately.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • kaffir lime leaves (in broth) → three strips lime zest plus a bay leaf Approximates the citrus brightness and a faint herbal backbone. Will not hit the floral-pine perfume that defines kaffir lime; the broth reads cleaner-citrus than complex.
  • kaffir lime leaves (in salad) → finely grated lime zest, ½ tsp per leaf called for Top-note approximation only. The textural-aromatic of shredded fresh leaf is structurally absent. Add finely chopped lemongrass tender heart to compensate for the missing herbal-citrus depth.
