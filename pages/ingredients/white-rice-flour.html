Ingredient

White rice flour

Finely milled long- or medium-grain white rice. Pure starch and trace protein with no gluten; behaves nothing like wheat flour even in matched ratios.

About

White rice flour is finely milled polished rice, with the bran and germ removed. It is approximately 80% starch (about 20% amylose, 80% amylopectin in long-grain types; closer to 1:99 in glutinous-rice flour, which is sold separately under the names mochiko, sweet rice flour, or shiratamako depending on origin), 7% protein in the form of glutelin, and almost no fat. Critically, it contains no gluten: the wheat protein complex of glutenin and gliadin that builds elastic dough networks. Rice protein does not form an extensible network in water; this is why a rice-flour batter is fluid where a wheat batter would be viscous, why rice-flour breads need binders (xanthan gum, psyllium husk, eggs) to mimic structure, and why rice-flour fritters fry into a shatter-crisp shell rather than a chewy one.

The defining functional property across cuisines is shatter-crisp frying. When rice-flour batter contacts 175 °C oil, the surface starch gelatinizes, dehydrates, and locks into a glassy matrix within seconds. This is the texture of Filipino ukoy, Vietnamese bánh xèo and bánh khọt, Korean haemul pajeon (when wheat is partly replaced with rice flour), Japanese karaage when dredged in rice flour, and South Indian dosa to a lesser degree. The shell does not get soggy as quickly as a wheat-flour shell because there is no gluten network to absorb steam back into chewiness. This is also why pure rice-flour shells go from crisp to brittle if held too long; they cannot recover the way wheat-based shells can.

Beyond frying, rice flour shows up as a thickener in rice noodles, as the structural starch in steamed cakes (Vietnamese bánh bò, Filipino puto), as a coating that prevents sticking in mochi and dumpling work, and as a gluten-free baking flour where it is almost always blended with starches (tapioca, potato) and gums to compensate for the missing structure. The protein-light, starch-heavy profile is also why rice-flour roux thickens with less of a flour taste than a wheat roux, useful in clear sauces.

Glutinous rice flour (mochiko / shiratamako / nuomifen) is a different ingredient despite the shared name. It is milled from short-grain glutinous rice (also called sweet rice or sticky rice), which is essentially pure amylopectin. This produces a chewy, stretchy, even gummy texture when hydrated and cooked: the texture of mochi, daifuku, tang yuan, and Korean tteok. Substituting glutinous rice flour for plain white rice flour will turn a fritter batter into a chewy mass that does not crisp; substituting plain for glutinous will produce mochi that is grainy and falls apart. They are not interchangeable.

Stone-ground vs. hammer-milled rice flour also matters at the margin. Traditional stone-ground rice flour (still common in South Indian and Vietnamese kitchens) has a coarser, more variable particle size and absorbs water more slowly, producing a slightly more textured fried shell. Hammer-milled commercial flour (Bob''s Red Mill, Erawan, Three Ladies) is uniform and very fine, producing a smoother, more glassy fry. Both are correct; they fry differently.

Storage

Sealed container, cool dry pantry, 6 months at full quality and 12 months acceptably. Failure mode is rancidity from any residual germ oil (fatty notes turning to crayon) and pantry-moth infestation. Refrigerator or freezer storage extends shelf life to 2 years and is worth it in humid climates. Failure to seal in humidity also causes the flour to clump, which is recoverable with sieving but a sign that storage discipline is slipping.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • white rice flour (fritter batter, ukoy, bánh xèo) → cornstarch + all-purpose flour (1:1) Mimics the shatter-crisp shell reasonably well. Drops 10–15% in crispness against pure rice flour and the shell goes soggy faster. Acceptable in a pinch; not the right ingredient if the cuisine's identity depends on the rice-flour signature.
  • white rice flour (gluten-free thickener) → cornstarch (half the volume) Pure starch, much more thickening power per gram. Use roughly half by mass; the resulting sauce reads cleaner and less floury but lacks the body that rice-flour starch brings.
  • white rice flour (steamed cakes, Asian dim sum applications) → no real substitute The specific gel structure of long-grain rice flour at steam temperatures defines the texture of these dishes. Wheat substitutions read as bread; tapioca substitutions read as gummy. Source it.
  • white rice flour (vs. glutinous rice flour) → they are different ingredients Sweet rice flour, mochiko, shiratamako, and glutinous rice flour are pure-amylopectin sticky-rice products. They produce chewy, stretchy textures and cannot substitute for the shatter-crisp behavior of plain white rice flour.
