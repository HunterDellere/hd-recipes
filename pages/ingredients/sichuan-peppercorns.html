Ingredient

Sichuan peppercorns

The husks of Zanthoxylum berries. Not actually pepper — they create má, the buzzing tingling numbness that defines Sichuan cooking.

About

The active compound is hydroxy-alpha-sanshool, which interacts with touch receptors (specifically RA1 mechanoreceptors) to produce what Sichuan cooks call má — a buzzing, tingling numbness on the lips and tongue distinct from chili heat (là). Together, má-là is the defining signature of Sichuan food.

Buy the red husks (Hanyuan or Maoxian if you can find them) and discard the black inner seeds, which are gritty and contribute nothing. Toast briefly in a dry pan before grinding to volatilize the aromatic limonene and citronellal notes.

Quality matters here more than for almost any other spice. Stale or low-grade peppercorns just taste woody. Fresh ones make your mouth vibrate.

Storage

Whole husks, airtight, dark cupboard. 6–12 months at peak. The numbing potency fades faster than the aroma; buy small quantities.
