Ingredient

Mirin (hon-mirin)

Sweet rice cooking wine — fermented, alcoholic, glossy. The gloss on teriyaki, the sweetness in dashimaki tamago, the round backnote in nimono.

About

Real mirin (hon-mirin, 本みりん) is a fermented rice product, ~14% ABV, made from glutinous rice, koji, and shōchū. The sweetness is not added sugar — it's glucose generated by koji enzymes converting rice starch during a 60-day-plus fermentation. Hon-mirin contributes glucose for browning and gloss, free amino acids for umami, and ethanol that flashes off and carries volatile aromatics into the food.

What's commonly sold in Western supermarkets as "mirin" is usually mirin-fu chōmiryō (a sweetened cooking liquid, sub-1% alcohol, made with glucose syrup, rice flavoring, and salt to dodge alcohol-tax classification). It works as a sweetener but lacks the depth and the alcohol-driven aromatic carry. Look for "hon-mirin" or "hon-mirin / mirin" with no added sugar or salt and an ABV near 14%. Eden, Takara, and Kikkori Wadakyu are reliable brands.

Standard use: glazes, teriyaki, simmered dishes, dressings, and as a counter-sweet to soy and miso. Pairs with sake (additional aromatic carry), dashi (umami stack), and rice vinegar (acid balance).

Storage

Pantry, sealed, 6–12 months. After opening, refrigerate hon-mirin for longest aroma retention.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • hon-mirin (glazes, teriyaki, simmered nimono) → sake + sugar (3 parts sake : 1 part sugar by weight) Best emergency substitute. Covers the alcohol + sweetness pair; lacks koji-derived complexity but the difference is mostly invisible under heavier glazes.
  • hon-mirin (dressings, sushi rice, raw or barely-cooked applications) → rice vinegar + sugar (4 parts vinegar : 1 part sugar) When the alcohol carry is not the point and you need sweet-tart balance instead. Wrong for glazes; no body or gloss.
  • hon-mirin (Western kitchens with no Japanese pantry) → dry sherry + sugar to taste Wider flavor mismatch but workable as a last resort. Use 1:1 by volume sherry to mirin, then add sugar; the oxidative sherry note is detectable but recedes under soy.
