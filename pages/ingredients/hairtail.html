Ingredient

Hairtail (beltfish, galchi)

Long ribbon-shaped silver marine fish, no scales, with delicate fine-grained white meat that lifts cleanly off a single central spine. The defining fish of Korean galchi jorim and galchi gui, Chinese braised daiyu, and Japanese tachiuo shioyaki.

About

Hairtail is the common market name for Trichiurus lepturus, the largehead hairtail or beltfish, a long ribbon-bodied marine fish in the family Trichiuridae and the cutlassfish lineage. It is sold under its regional names at most Asian grocers: galchi in Korean, tachiuo in Japanese, daiyu (帶魚, literally "belt fish") in Chinese. The body is exactly what those names describe, a flattened silver ribbon up to a meter long and only a few centimeters tall, tapering to a fine whip at the tail with no caudal fin. There are no scales. Instead the fish carries a thin coating of guanine crystals, the same purine pigment that silvers a mirror, deposited in the skin as a smooth metallic film. That guanine layer is the visual freshness gauge for the whole fish: bright reflective silver on a fresh specimen, dulling to a chalky gray as the surface lipids oxidize.

Structurally the fish is built for one cooking logic. A single central spine runs the length of the ribbon with thin pin bones radiating off it, and the white muscle sits in two long fillets on either side. The flesh is fine-grained, low in connective tissue, and moderately lean to medium-fatty depending on season, with a clean mild flavor far less assertive than mackerel or sardine. Because the muscle blocks are short and the binding collagen is sparse, cooked hairtail flesh lifts off the spine in clean flakes with almost no effort, which is the property that defines how it is handled at the table and also its central weakness: it falls apart under any aggressive stirring, flipping, or turning. The standard market format follows directly from the anatomy. The whole ribbon is gutted, the dorsal fin trimmed, and the body cross-cut into segments 6 to 10 cm long, each a cross-section of spine plus the two flanking fillets. You braise, fry, or grill the segments; you do not fillet hairtail the way you fillet a round fish.

Heat behavior tracks the delicate structure. The flesh sets to opaque white quickly, the short muscle fibers separating into flakes by the time the segment is cooked through, which means hairtail rewards cooking methods that hold the segment still. Braising is ideal: the segments rest on a bed in the pot, simmer in liquid without being moved, and absorb the surrounding sauce while the lean flesh stays moist in the steam-and-liquid environment. Pan-frying and grilling are the other canonical routes; here a light dredge or a salt cure firms the surface, and the cook flips the segment exactly once, because a second turn shreds it. The same fragility that ruins a stir-fried hairtail makes a braised or salt-grilled one luxurious, the flesh yielding in soft sheets off the bone.

Applications span several traditions, all built around the cross-cut segment. In Korea, galchi jorim braises the segments over thick radish coins in a gochugaru and gochujang sauce, while galchi gui is the salt-grilled version, segments salted and seared until the skin blisters and the flesh flakes. In China, daiyu is most often shallow-fried in a light dredge then braised in a soy, scallion, and ginger sauce, or dry-fried and finished with a vinegar-soy dip; the braised Shanghai and Cantonese versions are weeknight staples. In Japan, tachiuo appears as shioyaki (salt-grilled), as tempura, and occasionally as sashimi from very fresh specimens, where the soft flesh and clean flavor are prized. Across all three the fish plays the same role: a mild, tender white fish that carries a strongly seasoned braising or grilling sauce rather than competing with it.

Sourcing. Hairtail is a counter and ice-bed fish, sold at H Mart, 99 Ranch, Hannam, and most Korean, Chinese, and Japanese grocers, frequently frozen-then-thawed because the fish oxidizes quickly and rarely travels fresh. Judge it by the silver: a good specimen has a bright, even, reflective metallic coat with no yellow or rust-brown patches, firm flesh that springs back, intact unbroken skin, and a clean marine smell. Yellowing of the silver coat, a dull chalky surface, or any sour or ammoniated note means the surface lipids have oxidized and the fish is past its window. Buy it whole or, more practically, have the counter gut it, trim the fin, and cross-cut it into 6 to 10 cm segments on the spot; the segments are what every recipe calls for and the cut is tedious to do at home with a fish this long and slippery.

Storage

Hairtail oxidizes faster than most white fish because the guanine-coated skin and the thin subcutaneous fat are exposed across the whole ribbon surface. Fresh or thawed segments, on ice in the coldest part of the refrigerator: same day, 24 hours at the outside. Set the segments on a rack over a rimmed pan under a bag of crushed ice so meltwater drains away from the flesh. Frozen segments, vacuum-sealed: 2 to 3 months at peak quality, thawed overnight in the refrigerator in the bag, never at room temperature. Failure modes read on the silver first: a bright reflective coat dulling to chalky gray or going yellow-to-rust at the edges is surface lipid oxidation, the fish is past peak and should go only into a heavily seasoned braise that masks it, or be discarded. A sour, sharp, or ammoniated smell is microbial breakdown, discard. Soft mushy flesh that does not spring back, or skin that has gone slimy and gummy rather than smoothly silvered, means the segment is spent.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • hairtail (braised, jorim) → Spanish mackerel steaks, small whole croaker cross-cut, or cutlassfish (the same family) Cutlassfish is the nearest relative and behaves identically. Spanish mackerel is oilier and more assertive but holds a braise well and is widely available cross-cut into steaks; croaker is a milder, slightly firmer white fish that takes the gochugaru-gochujang sauce cleanly. Cut any substitute into bone-in cross sections so it sits in the sauce the way segments do.
  • hairtail (salt-grilled or pan-fried) → butterfish, Spanish mackerel, or small whole croaker Butterfish (pomfret) is the closest match for the salt-grilled register: mild, tender, fine-grained, and built to flake off a central bone. Spanish mackerel grills well but reads richer and fishier. Croaker pan-fries cleanly in a light dredge. Keep the skin on so it crisps the way hairtail skin does.
