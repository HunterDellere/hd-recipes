Ingredient

Taro stem (bạc hà, elephant ear stem)

Spongy aerial stem of certain taro varieties (Colocasia esculenta and related), used as a textural vegetable in Vietnamese, Bangladeshi, Filipino, and West African cooking. Pale-green to slightly purple, mildly grassy in flavor, with a porous cellular structure that absorbs broth like an edible sponge.

About

Taro stem, called bạc hà in Vietnamese, kachu in Bengali, and gabi or saluyot stem in other Southeast Asian contexts, is the spongy aerial stem (technically the petiole, the structure connecting the leaf to the underground corm) of certain taro varieties, primarily Colocasia esculenta cultivars selected for non-bitter stems. Not every taro plant produces an edible stem; the cultivated edible varieties are distinct from the ornamental "elephant ear" plants common in Western landscaping (those produce stems too high in calcium oxalate crystals to eat). The stems are sold fresh at Vietnamese and Bangladeshi markets, typically in bundles of 4 to 6 stems each 30 to 60 cm long, or frozen in pre-cut 4 cm pieces; the frozen form is more widely available in US Asian groceries.

Cellular structure, why it matters. The stem's defining feature is its aerenchyma, a tissue type consisting of large air-filled cells separated by thin cell walls. This structure (which evolved in taro to allow gas exchange in waterlogged paddy environments) gives the cut stem a honeycomb-like appearance and a sponge-like ability to absorb liquid. In cooking, this means a piece of taro stem dropped into a broth swells slightly as it soaks up the surrounding liquid; on the spoon, each piece functions as an edible broth-delivery vehicle, holding sour-savory liquid that releases when bitten. This is the structural role taro stem plays in canh chua: without it, every spoonful is liquid; with it, each bite has a soft-spongy textural contrast holding the broth.

Calcium oxalate handling. Raw taro stem contains calcium oxalate crystals, particularly in the outer skin layer, which cause skin irritation on contact (an itch or slight burn) and mouth irritation if eaten raw. The crystals are degraded by two mechanisms: peeling off the outer skin (the bulk of the irritant load is in the thin outer layer) and cooking (the crystals dissolve in hot acidic liquid above 80°C / 176°F over a few minutes). Vietnamese cooks typically peel the outer skin with a paring knife, slice into 4 cm angled batons, and salt-rub briefly to draw moisture (which also flushes residual oxalate) before adding to the broth. The cooked stem is entirely safe and tastes mildly grassy with no irritation; raw nibbling is not advised. The frozen pre-cut form is sold pre-peeled and is the most foolproof for home use.

Flavor profile. Taro stem itself is mildly flavored: a clean, grassy, faintly nutty note with a slight earthy back-note. The cooking role is far more about texture than flavor; the stem's job is to be the spongy carrier for the dressing or broth it's cooked in. This is why taro stem in canh chua reads as "the bite that holds the broth" rather than as a distinct vegetal flavor; it is the structural complement to the more flavorful pineapple, tomato, and herbs around it.

Cooking applications by category. Vietnamese: canh chua cá (the central textural vegetable in the southern Vietnamese sour soup); some Mekong-delta stir-fries; occasionally in light coconut-based soups. Filipino: sinigang na hipon and other sour-soup variations (often called gabi or laing depending on region); pinakbet variations. Bangladeshi / Bengali Indian: kachu shaak (a stem stew with garlic and turmeric); kachur loti (a long-stem preparation with mustard and panch phoron). West African (Nigerian, Cameroonian): in cocoyam-leaf and stem stews, sometimes alongside dried fish.

Buying notes. Fresh taro stem at Vietnamese, Filipino, or Bangladeshi markets: pale-green to slightly purple stems, firm to the touch (a soft, limp stem is past peak), no soft brown patches, intact outer skin, cut ends not browning or drying out. Sold in bundles, typically 200 to 400 g per bunch. Frozen pre-cut taro stem in plastic bags at H Mart, 99 Ranch, and similar groceries: 400 to 500 g bags of pre-peeled 4 cm pieces in a slightly icy state, the most foolproof source for home cooks. The frozen version is what most Vietnamese-American home cooks use and produces an indistinguishable result in canh chua.

Common Western drift. The substitute of "celery" is widespread in US-published canh chua recipes; celery is structurally different (firm cellulose tubes, not aerenchyma) and does not absorb broth the same way. The substitution is acceptable but the dish reads crunchier and less coherently. The most honest substitute is "leave it out and acknowledge the texture loss"; the second-best is celery with the understanding that the broth-sponging role is gone.

Storage

Fresh whole stems, wrapped in damp paper towel inside a plastic bag, refrigerated: 5 to 7 days; the stems wilt slowly before they spoil. Pre-cut frozen stems in their original bag: 6 to 12 months at peak quality; do not thaw before cooking, add directly to the simmering broth. Peeled and pre-cut fresh stems, sealed in a container in the refrigerator: 2 to 3 days; the cut surfaces brown slightly which is cosmetic. Failure modes: soft mushy patches (advanced cellular breakdown, trim or discard), strong fermented smell (spoilage, discard), visible mold (discard), pronounced itching when touched (excessive surface oxalate, peel more aggressively or discard if unmanageable).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • taro stem (Vietnamese canh chua, textural broth-sponge role) → celery, cut into 4 cm batons The most common substitute and the workable workhorse, but structurally different. Celery has cellulose tubes that hold their crunch and do not absorb the broth the same way. Add at the same point as the tomato (not at the pineapple-stage) so it stays crisp rather than overcooking. The dish reads crunchier and less coherently but is recognizably canh chua.
  • taro stem (Filipino sinigang or Bengali kachu) → omit, or substitute lotus root for textural interest No close textural substitute exists for the spongy stem; lotus root (a different vegetable with a porous structure) provides some textural complexity but reads as a different dish. Omitting and acknowledging the texture loss is more honest than poor substitution.
