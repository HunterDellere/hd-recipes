Ingredient

Black beans (frijoles negros)

Small to medium black-skinned legumes (Phaseolus vulgaris cv. Black Turtle) with a creamy off-white interior. The structural legume of central Mexican, Cuban, and Brazilian cooking; rich in soluble fiber, anthocyanin pigments in the seed coat, and the highest free-glutamate of any common bean.

About

Black beans are the seeds of Phaseolus vulgaris, the same species as kidney, pinto, navy, and cannellini beans, in the Black Turtle cultivar group. The seed coat is shiny black from anthocyanin pigments (cyanidin-3-glucoside dominant); the interior is creamy white-to-off-white. Mature dried beans are roughly 9% water, 22% protein, 62% carbohydrate (mostly starch with significant soluble fiber as raffinose-family oligosaccharides), and 1.5% fat. The carbohydrate profile is what makes the bean digestively notorious (the oligosaccharides are fermented by colonic bacteria to gas) and what makes the cooking water so flavorful (those same compounds plus free amino acids leach into the water and concentrate as the broth reduces).

Black beans carry the highest free-glutamate concentration of any common dried bean, roughly 30 mg per 100 g cooked, against pinto at 18 and navy at 12. This is why a pot of black beans cooked in just water with onion and salt tastes substantially more savory than the same treatment of pinto or navy; the glutamate is structural, not added. The bean liquor that comes off a long simmer (typically called caldo de frijol in Mexican cooking, or potlikker in the African-American tradition with collards) is itself a usable cooking ingredient, valuable as a soup base, a sauce thinner, or the cooking liquid for rice.

Soak vs no-soak. The traditional 8-to-12-hour overnight soak shortens cooking time by roughly 30% (from 90 to 60 minutes for soaked vs unsoaked at a steady simmer), reduces the gas-causing oligosaccharides by 25 to 40% if the soak water is discarded, and produces a more uniformly cooked bean (the soak hydrates the interior so the cooking heat does not have to travel as far). The no-soak method (start dried beans in cold water, bring to a simmer, cook 90 to 120 minutes) is faster on the day-of and produces a more flavorful broth (oligosaccharides stay in the pot rather than going down the drain) but takes longer total active and stovetop time. Both methods produce excellent beans; the soak is preferred when the broth is the point (bean soup, frijoles de la olla as a standalone dish), the no-soak is fine when the beans are headed into a salad or composed dish where the broth role is secondary.

Salt timing. The old kitchen lore says salt makes beans tough; this is false at typical home-cooking salt levels (1 to 2% by weight of cooking water). What is true is that high-acid additions (tomato, vinegar, citrus) early in cooking can prevent the bean skin from softening fully, and very-hard water (high in calcium) can produce the same effect. The practical rule is salt the beans at the start (1 g salt per 100 g dry beans dissolved in the cooking water) and add tomato or other acid in the last 30 minutes of cooking; the resulting beans are seasoned through and not tough.

Cooking method. Cover dried beans (rinsed and picked over for stones) with water by 5 cm in a heavy pot. Add salt (1 g per 100 g dry beans), 1 quartered onion, 1 bay leaf, optionally a 5 cm strip of kombu (the alginate softens the bean skins faster and adds glutamate). Bring to a vigorous boil for 5 minutes, drop to a bare simmer (small bubbles breaking the surface), partially cover, and cook 60 minutes (soaked) or 90 to 120 minutes (unsoaked), checking water level and topping up with hot water as needed. The beans are done when one bean smashed between thumb and forefinger crushes easily; uneven doneness across the pot means the beans are old (more than 18 months from harvest) and need another 15 minutes plus a check. Cool in the cooking liquid; the beans absorb additional broth as they cool and the cooked-bean texture is meaningfully better than straight-from-the-pot.

Canned black beans are a fully acceptable shortcut for composed dishes (salads, fillings, dips); the texture is slightly mushier and the broth is thinner-flavored than home-cooked, but for applications where the bean is one of several components rather than the main act, the difference is small. Drain and rinse canned beans in hot water before use; the brining liquid is salty, slightly viscous, and dulls the bean flavor.

Applications by category. Mexican: frijoles de la olla (the foundational pot of beans, eaten as a side or as a soup with crumbled queso fresco), frijoles refritos (mashed and fried in lard), enfrijoladas (tortillas dipped in pureed black bean sauce), tamales de frijol, tlayudas. Cuban: Moros y Cristianos (rice and black beans cooked together), black bean soup (sopa de frijoles negros, slowly reduced to thick richness with sherry and sofrito), congrí Oriental. Brazilian: feijoada (the national dish, black beans simmered for hours with multiple cuts of pork). Caribbean: rice and beans across Puerto Rico, Dominican Republic, and Jamaica with regional variations. African-American: hoppin' John variants, black bean soup. The reach across the African diaspora and the Latin American world is structural, not coincidental; black beans are one of the most-cooked legumes globally.

Buying. Dried beans at Mexican groceries, Latin American sections of supermarkets, or natural-food bulk bins (Rancho Gordo is a US specialty source for heirloom varieties at premium prices). Look for plump shiny black beans with no broken seed coats and no white powdery dust at the bottom of the bag (signs of age). Canned beans: low-sodium (or no-salt-added) cans give the cook control over the final salt level; Goya, Bush's, Eden, and 365 are widely distributed. Avoid cans with BPA-lined liners if it matters to you; Eden lines its cans with a bisphenol-free coating.

Storage

Dried beans: sealed glass or food-safe plastic at room temperature, 18 months for peak hydration behavior; usable beyond 2 years but cooks longer and never quite reaches the same creaminess (the seed coat hardens with age). Cool dry pantry; refrigeration is unnecessary and introduces moisture risk. Cooked beans: refrigerated in the cooking liquid in a sealed container, 5 days; freeze in 250 g portions in zip-top bags or deli containers for up to 6 months; the texture holds well through one freeze-thaw cycle. Canned beans (unopened): pantry temperature, by the can date (typically 3 years from production); once opened, transfer leftover beans to a sealed container with the brining liquid, refrigerated 4 days. Failure modes: dried beans with white powdery dust or visible insect activity (discard, contaminated); cooked beans with a sour smell or visible film on the broth (spoiled, discard); canned beans with a swollen or dented can (botulism risk, discard immediately without opening).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • black beans (composed salad and filling applications) → pinto beans, kidney beans, or small red beans All same species; pinto is the most common Mexican alternative and works without adjustment, reads slightly creamier and less savory. Kidney beans are bigger and firmer; small red beans (frijoles colorados) are similar in size and bite. The dish loses the distinctive black-bean color contrast in the bowl but the cooking behavior is interchangeable.
  • black beans (Cuban or Brazilian deep-flavor applications) → no good substitute; the dish is built on the bean Cuban black bean soup, feijoada, and similar dishes are built around the specific savory-glutamate profile and inky-black broth that black beans produce. Substituting another bean produces a recognizably different dish that is not the same dish. If black beans are unavailable, cook the alternative bean and call the dish what it is (Cuban pinto soup, feijoada de feijão branco) rather than presenting it as the original.
  • black beans (canned, drained and rinsed) → cooked-from-dried black beans (250 g cooked from 100 g dry, drained) Home-cooked beans are markedly better in flavor and texture; canned are markedly faster. The substitution is a quality-vs-time tradeoff and works mechanically without adjustment. If using home-cooked, season the cooking water with salt (1 g per 100 g dry beans) so the resulting drained beans are pre-seasoned to roughly the same level as canned.
