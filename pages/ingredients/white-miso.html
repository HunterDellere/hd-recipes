Ingredient

White miso (shiro)

Short-fermented soybean-rice paste, pale beige, mellow and sweet. The miso to default to when a recipe just says "miso."

About

Shiro miso ("white miso") is fermented for a comparatively short stretch; typically 2–6 months; using a higher proportion of rice koji than soybean. The result is pale, sweet, and gently salty, with the mellow-savory backbone of all miso but none of the aggressive funk you get in red (aka) or 3-year-aged hatchō miso. Salt content sits around 5–6% by weight, less than half the salt of dark miso.

Treat it as a flavor amplifier, not just a salt source: it carries free glutamates, peptides from soy hydrolysis, and ester aromatics from koji fermentation. Never boil it; sustained boiling drives off the volatile aromatics and dulls the umami. Whisk into hot liquids off-heat, or stir in at the end of cooking.

Pairs naturally with butter (the lipids carry the volatile esters), citrus (the acid sharpens the sweet edge), and dashi (a glutamate-on-glutamate stack). Miso butter, miso vinaigrette, and miso glazes are pantry-foundational moves.

Buy refrigerated, in a sealed tub. Marukome and Hikari are widely available reliable brands. Check ingredients; real miso is just soybeans, rice (or barley), salt, and koji. "White miso paste" with added sweeteners or alcohol preservatives is a different product.

Storage

Refrigerated in its tub, surface covered with parchment to prevent oxidation darkening, 6–12 months. The color deepens over time; this is fine; it just means continuing fermentation.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • white miso (delicate finishes, dressings, miso butter, light glazes) → awase miso (white-red blend) Closest functional swap. Slightly more savory and less sweet, but holds the same delicate-finish role without going funky.
  • white miso (braises, marinades, robust glazes for fatty fish or pork) → red miso (aka), reduced by 30% by weight Saltier, funkier, longer-fermented. Right for applications where depth beats restraint; wrong for anything where the sweet-mellow edge is the point.
  • white miso (rustic stews, hearty Korean-leaning dishes) → doenjang, reduced by 40% by weight Korean fermented soybean paste. Earthier and saltier with a sharper funk. Pulls washoku off-profile; right for cross-pollinated dishes only.
