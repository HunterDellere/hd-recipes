Ingredient

Pomegranate molasses

Pomegranate juice reduced 4:1 to a thick, sour-sweet syrup. The defining sour of Persian and Levantine sauces, marinades, and dressings — not grenadine, which is a sugar syrup with red dye.

About

Pomegranate molasses (Persian rob-e anar; Arabic dibs rumman; Turkish nar ekşisi) is reduced pomegranate juice — fresh pomegranate juice cooked down to roughly one-quarter to one-fifth of its original volume until it thickens into a deep-burgundy syrup with a sour-sweet flavor profile that reads pomegranate-concentrated. The reduction is the entire chemistry: pomegranate juice is naturally about 12 to 16 percent sugar (fructose and glucose), 1.5 to 2.0 percent citric acid, 0.5 to 1.0 percent tartaric acid, and 0.5 percent malic acid; reducing 1 liter of juice to 250 ml concentrates everything fourfold, producing a syrup that is roughly 50 percent sugar, 6 to 8 percent acid, and viscous from the dissolved sugars. The flavor is the star: deeply sour-sweet, fruity but savory, with a slightly tannic finish from the polyphenols (anthocyanins, ellagitannins) that are also concentrated by the reduction.

The grenadine confusion. The single most common Western kitchen mistake is substituting grenadine for pomegranate molasses. Grenadine is a sugar syrup originally flavored with pomegranate (true grenadine, like Small Hand Foods or homemade) but more commonly today a sugar syrup with red dye and artificial flavor (Rose's, the standard supermarket grenadine, contains no pomegranate at all). Even true grenadine is much sweeter than pomegranate molasses (the syrup is mostly sugar, with pomegranate as a flavoring rather than the structural ingredient). Pomegranate molasses, by contrast, is fundamentally pomegranate juice ; the reduction has not added sugar, it has only concentrated what was already in the fruit. Substituting one for the other ruins any Levantine, Persian, or Turkish recipe that calls for the molasses; a fattoush dressing made with grenadine reads as a cocktail, not a salad.

The home reduction question. Pomegranate molasses is straightforward to make at home from pomegranate juice (POM Wonderful or freshly pressed): bring 1 liter / 4 cups of juice to a simmer in a wide non-reactive saucepan, reduce 30 to 45 minutes uncovered until the liquid coats the back of a spoon and the volume is roughly 250 ml / 1 cup. Some recipes add 50 to 100 g of sugar and 2 tbsp / 30 g of lemon juice during the reduction; the sugar reinforces the sweetness and the lemon brightens the acid. The home version is fresher and less cooked-tasting than commercial bottles, which often add citric acid and sugar to standardize the product across batches. The trade is shelf life ; home-reduced molasses keeps 2 to 3 months refrigerated; commercial bottles keep 2 years on the shelf.

Applications by category. Levantine canonical: fattoush dressing (the structural sour alongside sumac and lemon), muhammara (Syrian roasted-red-pepper-and-walnut dip ; the molasses is a defining ingredient), kibbeh dipping sauces, Aleppine eggplant preparations. Persian canonical: fesenjan (Persian walnut-and-pomegranate stew over chicken or duck ; the molasses is the cooking liquid and the structural sour); dressings for fresh-herb plates (sabzi khordan); salads dressed with molasses-and-walnut vinaigrettes. Turkish: nar ekşili meze tradition (molasses-dressed salads, dips); marinades for grilled meats; dressings for shepherd's salad (çoban salatası) variants. Modern Western: glazes for roasted vegetables (carrots, beets, eggplant); marinades for chicken thighs and lamb; vinaigrettes for grain salads; finishing drizzle over labneh or burrata; cocktails (the genuine pomegranate-cocktail use case, where the molasses replaces grenadine for a more complex, less sweet drink).

Buying. Cortas (Lebanese, widely available) is the workhorse supermarket-tier pomegranate molasses; reliable and authentic, slightly more sweet than tart. Al Wadi (Lebanese), Mymouné (Lebanese, premium), and various Iranian and Turkish brands carry slightly different sweet-tart balances. Look for an ingredient list that begins with "pomegranate juice" or "concentrated pomegranate juice" ; anything that begins with "sugar," "high-fructose corn syrup," or any flavoring or dye is the wrong product. Some American brands (especially shelf-stable ones with very long ingredient lists) are essentially grenadine relabeled; check the label.

Storage

Sealed bottle at room temperature 2 years; once opened, refrigerated 6 to 12 months. The high sugar concentration makes pomegranate molasses shelf-stable in the closed bottle and resistant to spoilage in the refrigerator. Crystallization on the bottle threads is normal ; the dissolved sugars come out of solution as the syrup ages ; and does not indicate spoilage. Failure modes: visible mold on the surface (rare but possible if water has contaminated the bottle, discard); fermented or off-yeasty smell (similar; discard); thickening to nearly solid in the bottle (over-concentration during manufacturing or evaporation through a poorly sealed cap; thin with a tablespoon of water and stir to restore).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • pomegranate molasses (in dressings and vinaigrettes) → pomegranate juice reduced 4:1 plus 1 tsp / 5 g lemon juice The home-reduction approach is the canonical fallback. Cooks 1 cup pomegranate juice down to ¼ cup over 30 minutes; brighten with lemon. Effectively identical to commercial molasses for any cooking application.
  • pomegranate molasses (when no pomegranate product is available) → tamarind concentrate plus a pinch of sugar Tamarind brings the tart-fruity-savory register; the dish drifts South Asian. Acceptable for marinades and glazes; not interchangeable in canonical Levantine fattoush or fesenjan, where the pomegranate flavor is structural.
  • pomegranate molasses (for sweet applications ; drizzles over yogurt, desserts) → good-quality balsamic glaze or saba Balsamic glaze brings concentrated sour-sweet character with a similar viscosity. The dish becomes Italian-leaning rather than Levantine; works for fusion contexts.
