Ingredient

Light brown sugar

Granulated sugar coated with ~3.5% molasses. The molasses adds moisture, acid, and color — and changes how a cookie spreads.

About

Most commercial light brown sugar is white granulated sugar with refinery molasses sprayed back on — roughly 3.5% by weight. It is not an "unrefined" sugar in any meaningful sense. The molasses is the active ingredient.

That molasses changes three things at once. It adds water (~10% of the molasses by weight), so a brown-sugar cookie has more free moisture than a granulated one, meaning more steam in the oven, more spread, a chewier set. It adds acid (pH ~5), which weakens gluten and accelerates Maillard browning, deepening color and crust flavor. And it adds the molasses itself (toffee, raisin, faint smokiness), which reads as "caramel" even though no caramelization has happened yet.

Pack-by-volume is the crime scene of brown-sugar baking. A loose cup and a packed cup can differ by 30–40%. American recipes usually mean "firmly packed" but rarely say so. The fix, again, is grams: 1 cup packed is ~213g.

Storage

Airtight container at room temperature. Failure mode is moisture loss: exposed to air, the molasses dries and the sugar hardens into a brick. Revive by sealing it overnight with a slice of bread or a damp paper towel; the sugar pulls moisture back from the bread.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • light brown sugar → granulated sugar + molasses For 1 cup (213g): 200g granulated + 13g (~1 tbsp) molasses. Mix until evenly coated. Behaves identically.
  • light brown sugar → dark brown sugar 1:1 by weight. Cookie will spread slightly more, brown deeper, taste more strongly of molasses. Often an upgrade in chocolate-chip cookies; less so in delicate cakes.
  • light brown sugar → granulated sugar Loses the moisture, acid, and color: cookies set crisper and pale, with a flatter sucrose-only sweetness. Not a quiet swap.
