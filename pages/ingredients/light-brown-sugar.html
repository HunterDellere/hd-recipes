Ingredient

Light brown sugar

Granulated sucrose coated with ~3.5% refinery molasses. The molasses adds water, acid, and a toffee-leaning flavor that changes spread, browning, and crumb in everything it touches.

About

Most commercial light brown sugar in the US is white granulated sucrose with refinery molasses sprayed and tumbled back on at roughly 3.5% by weight. It is not an unrefined sugar in any meaningful sense; the brand-side language about "natural" obscures what is really a re-blended product. Genuinely unrefined sugars (muscovado, panela, jaggery, gur) are different ingredients with their own behavior. The 3.5% molasses share is what does all the actual work in a recipe; the rest is sucrose chemistry identical to white sugar.

Functional role: light brown sugar contributes four things simultaneously, which is why it cannot be cleanly traded for white sugar in baking. First, water. Molasses is roughly 10–15% water by weight, so a brown-sugar dough carries more free moisture than a granulated one, meaning more steam pressure in the oven, more spread, and a chewier final crumb. Second, acid. Molasses sits around pH 5, which weakens gluten development (giving a tenderer set) and accelerates Maillard browning by lowering the pH of the surrounding dough, deepening color and crust flavor. Third, the residual sugars in molasses (mostly fructose and glucose) caramelize and Maillard-react at lower temperatures than sucrose alone, so the surface browns sooner. Fourth, flavor itself. Molasses delivers toffee, raisin, and faint smoky-bitter notes that read as caramel even before any caramelization has happened.

This combination is why light brown sugar is the working sugar in chocolate chip cookies, butterscotch pudding, blondies, banana bread, oatmeal cookies, sticky toffee pudding, BBQ rubs and sauces, baked beans, sweet pickle brines, gingerbread (paired with dark for more depth), and the canonical American spice cakes. Substituting white sugar in any of these gives a noticeably crisper, paler, less complex result; substituting dark brown gives a darker, more spread-prone, more aggressively molasses-flavored one.

Across other cuisines, light-brown-sugar-equivalent ingredients show up in different forms. Latin American panela and piloncillo (pure unrefined cane sugar with all the original molasses) play a similar functional role in flan, dulce de leche, mole sauces, and Caribbean rum punches; British demerara is closer to a coarse turbinado but used like brown sugar in tea and oatmeal toppings; Indian gur and jaggery anchor sweet preparations from kheer to chai.

Pack-by-volume is the central measurement trap. A loose cup of brown sugar weighs ~165 g; the same cup firmly packed weighs ~213 g, a 30% difference. American baking recipes usually mean "firmly packed" but rarely say so explicitly. The reliable fix is to weigh: 1 cup packed light brown ≈ 213 g. Recipes that produce dramatically different results in different kitchens almost always have an unstated packing convention as the variable.

Storage failure mode is a consequence of how brown sugar is built. The thin molasses coating around each crystal holds moisture; when air circulates and that moisture evaporates, the molasses dries and the crystals fuse into a brick. The fix exploits the same chemistry: seal the dried sugar overnight with a damp paper towel or a slice of bread, and the sugar pulls moisture back from the towel until it loosens. Specialty terra-cotta brown-sugar disks (soaked in water before storing) work on the same principle.

Storage

Airtight container at room temperature, away from heat. Indefinitely shelf-stable as long as moisture is preserved. Failure mode is hardening into a brick from moisture loss; revive by sealing the container overnight with a slice of bread or a damp paper towel. The sugar pulls water back from the bread and softens. Avoid storing anywhere humid (above sinks, near dishwashers); excess moisture can drive a slow secondary caramelization at room temperature, deepening flavor and color over years (mostly cosmetic; still safe).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • light brown sugar (any baking application) → granulated sugar + molasses (rebuilt) For 1 cup packed (213 g): 200 g granulated + 13 g (~1 tbsp) molasses. Mix until evenly coated. Behaves identically because it is the same construction.
  • light brown sugar (chocolate chip cookies, blondies, chewy bakes) → dark brown sugar 1:1 by weight. Cookie spreads slightly more, browns deeper, reads more strongly of molasses. Often an upgrade in chocolate-chip cookies; can overwhelm delicate cakes and white-flour bakes.
  • light brown sugar (delicate cake or crumb, neutral sweetness desired) → granulated sugar Loses the moisture, acid, and toffee notes; cake sets crisper and paler with flat sucrose-only sweetness. Acceptable when those changes are wanted; not a silent swap.
  • light brown sugar (Latin American or Caribbean preparations) → piloncillo or panela Unrefined cane sugar with all the original molasses intact. Sold in cones or solid blocks; grate or dissolve in liquid. Slightly more mineral and complex than refined brown sugar; closer to muscovado in spirit. Use 1:1 by weight.
  • light brown sugar (Indian and South Asian preparations) → jaggery (gur) Unrefined sugarcane or palm-sugar block, soft to crumbly depending on style. Carries deeper caramel, faint mineral, and slightly smoky notes. Use 1:1 by weight; expect a more pronounced flavor.
  • light brown sugar (lower-glycemic alternative, baked goods) → coconut sugar Less sweet (~75% the perceived sweetness of granulated), with caramel notes from inulin and trace molasses-equivalents. Use 1:1 by weight; cookie spread will be less because the sugar is less hygroscopic.
