Ingredient

Chipotle in adobo

Smoke-cured ripe jalapeños (chipotle morita or chipotle meco) rehydrated in a tomato-vinegar-aromatic sauce. The most concentrated form of Mexican smoke heat in a single pantry can; carries pectin and tomato emulsifier body that makes it function as a vinaigrette base, not just a flavoring.

About

Chipotle is a fully-ripened red jalapeño (Capsicum annuum) that has been smoke-cured to dehydration over wood (traditionally pecan, mesquite, or oak in northern and central Mexico). The smoking process removes about 80% of the pepper's water content (from ~80% to ~10%) and infuses the flesh with phenolic smoke compounds (guaiacol, syringol, 4-methylguaiacol) that produce the characteristic deep wood-smoke aromatic. Two main grades exist: chipotle morita (smaller, darker, more delicate, fruitier; smoked for less time, 2 to 4 days) and chipotle meco (larger, lighter brown, more pronounced wood-smoke and almost cigar-leaf aromatic; smoked longer, 5 to 7 days). Morita is the more common Mexican-supermarket variety and is what is canned in adobo for export markets; meco is preferred by some traditional cooks for moles and adobos but is harder to find outside Mexico.

Adobo is the sauce, not the pepper. The canned product (most familiarly La Costeña, San Marcos, or Embasa in 7 oz / 198 g cans) suspends rehydrated chipotle peppers in a sauce of tomato puree, vinegar, garlic, onion, salt, sugar, and aromatic spices (typically oregano, bay, and either cumin or thyme). The peppers absorb the adobo over the canning process and become both pepper and sauce; the peppers themselves can be minced and used directly, the surrounding sauce can be spooned out separately, or both can be used together. The proportions matter: 1 chipotle pepper alone is roughly 4 g of dried pepper plus the absorbed sauce, which gives a more pepper-forward, structured heat; 1 tbsp of just the surrounding adobo sauce is roughly 15 g and gives a smoke-and-vinegar background without the discrete pepper bite. Most recipes use both together, minced into a paste.

Heat level. Chipotle scores 2,500 to 8,000 Scoville heat units, in the moderate range; significantly hotter than poblano (1,000 to 2,000) but well below habanero (100,000 to 350,000). The heat reads as a slow build rather than a sharp front-of-tongue hit (the oils dissolve in the sauce fat and distribute over the palate gradually); 1 chipotle pepper plus 1 tbsp adobo produces moderate-to-pronounced heat in a 4-serving dish, 2 chipotles produce aggressive heat. The smoke component does not scale with the heat; even a small amount of chipotle contributes pronounced smoke without much capsaicin load.

Functional role beyond flavor. Chipotle in adobo carries pectin and hemicellulose from the rehydrated pepper pulp, plus tomato pectin and vinegar acid from the adobo sauce. Together these contribute substantial emulsifier function; a vinaigrette built on chipotle in adobo paste plus lime and oil emulsifies tighter than a comparable lime-and-honey vinaigrette without an adobo base, and holds the emulsion for 4 to 6 hours at room temperature. This is why chipotle in adobo recurs across Mexican and Mexican-American cooking as the base of marinades, sauces, mayonnaises (chipotle mayo), and dressings; it is doing structural emulsifier work in addition to flavor work.

Applications by category. Mexican and Mexican-American: tinga de pollo (shredded chicken in chipotle-tomato sauce, the classic taco filling), enchiladas chipotle, chipotle mayo for sandwiches and burgers, marinades for grilled meat (especially chicken and skirt steak), barbacoa-style braises, salsa de chipotle (a smooth fiery dipping sauce), chipotle mac and cheese (American adaptation), chipotle aioli, chipotle bean dip. American adoptions: chipotle ketchup, chipotle in chili, chipotle vinaigrette for grain bowls. The ingredient travels well into non-Mexican cooking and is one of the few Mexican pantry items that has fully integrated into American restaurant cooking.

Buying. The canned product is widely distributed through US supermarkets, especially in the Hispanic foods section. La Costeña (red label) is the most common brand and produces a balanced adobo with moderate heat. San Marcos is similar; Embasa has a slightly sweeter adobo. Premium small-producer brands (Tía Rosa, El Yucateco) are available at Mexican groceries and tend to use higher-quality chipotle and less filler. Whole dried chipotle peppers (sold separately, not in adobo) are also available at Mexican groceries and can be reconstituted at home into a fresher-tasting adobo; the canned version is a substantial time-saver for everyday cooking.

Storage

Unopened can: pantry temperature, by the can date (typically 3 years from production). Once opened, transfer the entire can contents (peppers and sauce) to a sealed glass or food-safe plastic container; refrigerated, the adobo keeps 1 month with no quality loss. For longer storage, freeze: spread individual chipotle peppers (with a small puddle of adobo each) on a parchment-lined tray, freeze solid (2 hours), transfer to a zip-top freezer bag. Frozen peppers keep 6 months and can be minced from frozen with a sharp knife. Failure modes: surface mold on refrigerated adobo (discard the entire container); off-cue smell (sour-fermented rather than smoke-and-vinegar; discard); canned product with swollen or dented can (botulism risk, discard immediately without opening).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • chipotle in adobo (paste, vinaigrette base, marinade base) → smoked paprika plus a dab of sriracha or other vinegar hot sauce (use 4 g smoked paprika plus 8 g sriracha to replace 24 g chipotle paste) Preserves the smoke (paprika) and the heat (sriracha) but loses the chipotle pepper-pulp body and the adobo tomato-vinegar emulsifier. The dish reads close in flavor; the texture and emulsifier function are weaker. For vinaigrette applications, add ½ tsp dijon mustard to compensate for the missing emulsifier load.
  • chipotle in adobo (whole peppers, braising or stewing) → dried chipotle morita peppers, rehydrated in hot water 20 minutes Whole dried chipotle peppers (sold at Mexican groceries) are the unprocessed source; rehydrated in hot water and minced, they provide the chipotle pepper component without the adobo sauce. Add a splash of cider vinegar and a squeeze of tomato paste to approximate the adobo. More work, fresher result.
  • chipotle in adobo (smoke-and-heat profile) → guajillo or pasilla chili paste plus 1 tsp smoked paprika and a dab of cayenne Different chili family (the dried red chiles), different smoke profile, but produces a recognizable Mexican-chili-paste base with smoke layered on top. Acceptable when chipotle is unavailable; the dish reads in a different chili register but stays Mexican.
