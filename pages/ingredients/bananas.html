Ingredient

Bananas

Fruit of *Musa acuminata* / *M. balbisiana* hybrids, dominated by Cavendish in global trade. The starch-to-sugar conversion curve over a few days at room temperature is the single most important variable for cooking.

About

The dessert banana of grocery aisles is almost entirely Cavendish, a single Musa acuminata cultivar grown clonally and shipped green so it ripens in transit. Plantains and red bananas are different cultivars with different starch profiles; their cooking behavior is not the same, and recipes that specify "banana" mean Cavendish unless they say otherwise. The fruit is roughly 75% water, 22% carbohydrate, and 1% protein, with the carbohydrate fraction shifting dramatically between green and ripe. A green Cavendish carries 70–80% of its dry mass as resistant starch; a fully spotted one has converted nearly all of that starch to fructose, glucose, and sucrose, and reads as 14–18% sugar by weight.

This conversion is the cooking lever. Amylase enzymes in the fruit hydrolyze starch chains into simple sugars over the post-harvest window, and the same window concentrates volatile esters — primarily isoamyl acetate, the compound responsible for the artificial-banana smell of Laffy Taffy and of overripe fruit. A green-shouldered, pale-yellow banana is firm and starchy, suited to slicing into oatmeal or frying as a chip. A solid yellow banana with no spots is the eating standard, balanced between sweet and tannic. Heavily speckled brown-on-yellow is the baking grade: the fruit is mash-soft, maximally aromatic, and sweet enough to let the cook drop refined sugar by 25–30% without anyone noticing. Black-skinned with the flesh still intact and the skin not weeping is the Maillard-favored stage for banana bread, where the long-cooked esters carry through a 50-minute bake.

Heat treatment changes the fruit further. Sliced and pan-roasted in butter and brown sugar, the surface fructose caramelizes between 160 and 170 °C and the cell walls collapse into a syrup; this is the bananas Foster trajectory. Whole and unpeeled on a grill or in a 200 °C oven, the skin acts as a steam jacket and the inside cooks to a custard-like texture without browning. Whisked into a batter, the pectin in the fruit substitutes partially for egg or oil, which is why classic banana bread tolerates being lower in both than other quick breads. The banana is also acidic enough (pH ~4.5–5.2) to activate baking soda directly without needing additional buttermilk or vinegar.

Cavendish bananas oxidize fast once cut. Polyphenol oxidase reacts with cell sap, browning the surface within minutes; a quick toss in lemon juice, ascorbic acid, or a saline solution drops pH and saturates oxygen-binding sites enough to slow the reaction by an order of magnitude. For a fruit salad served immediately, plain water rinses are enough. For anything held longer than thirty minutes, acidify.

The variety has a known fragility: the global Cavendish monoculture is currently being eroded by Tropical Race 4 of Fusarium oxysporum, the same lineage of soil fungus that wiped out the previous standard, the Gros Michel, in the 1950s. This is not a cooking variable today, but it is why the supply chain may shift cultivars in the next decade and why a banana that tastes "different" in a few years is not your imagination.

Storage

Counter, away from other fruit, until ripeness reaches the cooking grade you want; the ethylene the banana itself releases accelerates further ripening of anything nearby. Once at the desired stage, refrigerate to slow further conversion — the skin will blacken from cold injury within hours but the flesh stays held at that ripeness for 5–7 days. Frozen, peeled, in a ziploc: keeps 6 months for smoothies and baking; the texture turns to a slurry on thaw, which is correct for both. Failure mode is fermentation: if the fruit goes from sweet to alcoholic and the skin weeps, microbes are now competing with the amylases and the flesh is past baking grade.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • bananas (banana bread, baking, mash applications) → no real substitute The combination of acid, pectin, sugar, and water content is what makes banana bread work as a low-fat low-egg quick bread. Apple sauce comes closest on water and pectin but lacks the acid and flavor; the bread reads as plain rather than banana.
  • bananas (smoothies, frozen mash) → frozen mango or peach Different flavor, similar viscosity-thickening behavior from frozen fruit pulp. Drop sweetness by 20% to compensate for lower sugar load.
  • bananas (sliced raw, fresh applications) → plantain, ripe (black-skinned), pan-fried Plantain ripens to higher sugar but stays starchier, so it needs heat to read sweet. Not raw-substitutable.
