Ingredient

Broccoli

Brassica oleracea var. italica. Sulfur-forward when overcooked; nutty and sweet with a hard, dry sear.

About

Broccoli's vegetal-sweet flavor lives in the glucosinolate-myrosinase system: chop the head, the enzyme reacts with the precursors, and you get sulforaphane and other volatile sulfur compounds. Heat them gently and they read nutty and brassicaceous in a pleasant way. Overcook (15+ minutes simmering, or boiling at full pot temp), and they break down into hydrogen sulfide and dimethyl sulfide; that's the cafeteria-broccoli smell.

The cook's directive: fast and dry, or short and steamed, never boiled. Roasting at 230°C / 450°F for 12–15 minutes, or a hard sear in a screaming dry pan for 3 minutes followed by a 30-second steam-finish under a lid, gets you the deepest flavor. Stir-frying at proper wok-hei heat with no crowding works equally well.

The stems are not waste; peel off the fibrous outer layer with a vegetable peeler and the core is sweeter, denser, and more vegetable-like than the florets. Slice on a bias and treat them like a different vegetable in the same dish.

Look for tight, dark-green-to-purplish heads with crisp stems. Yellowing florets mean it's past prime; flavor will be flat and the sulfur-cabbage notes start dominating.

Storage

Refrigerator crisper drawer, loosely wrapped in a damp paper towel, 5–7 days. Do not store in airtight bags; broccoli respires and goes off-flavor in trapped ethylene.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • broccoli → broccolini (sprouting broccoli) Sweeter, more tender stems; cooks faster. Reduce sear time by 1 minute.
  • broccoli → Chinese broccoli (gai lan) More bitter, more leafy. Excellent for stir-fries with oyster sauce; less ideal for roasting.
