Ingredient

Haricots verts

Phaseolus vulgaris bred for slim pods, picked young at 3-4 mm cross-section. Thinner walls, faster cook, more sugar than commodity green beans.

About

Haricots verts are the same species as standard green beans (Phaseolus vulgaris) bred and harvested young, with cultivars selected for slim pods (4 to 5 inches long, 3 to 4 mm thick) and tender walls. The French market expects them picked before the seeds inside the pod begin to plump; once seeds bulge, the pod texture turns starchy and the variety loses its identity. The narrower wall is the entire point: thinner cell-wall structure means less pectin and cellulose to break down, so the cook hits crisp-tender at a 90-second blanch instead of the 4-minute window standard green beans need.

Chlorophyll chemistry decides cooked color. The bright green of a fresh bean comes from chlorophyll a and b in the chloroplast membranes; under heat, the membranes rupture and acidic cellular contents (vacuoles holding malic and citric acid at pH 5.5) reach the chlorophyll. A magnesium ion at the centre of each chlorophyll molecule swaps for hydrogen, converting bright green chlorophyll into olive-gray pheophytin. This conversion happens slowly at room temperature, fast at boil, and irreversibly within 7 to 8 minutes of sustained heat. The blanch-and-shock technique exists to set the chlorophyll before the magnesium-hydrogen swap completes; the ice bath halts residual heat and locks the bright green state.

Salt-saturated water matters. Blanching in heavily salted water (3 percent salt by weight, or 30 g per liter) raises the water's effective ionic strength, which protects the chlorophyll's magnesium core during the brief cook by shifting the equilibrium against the swap. The same blanching done in unsalted water produces visibly grayer beans at the same time-out. The salt is not for flavor here; it is a chlorophyll preservative.

Applications by category. The salade niçoise role (composed salad, vinaigrette dressing, room-temperature plating) is the canonical Western European use. In Provençal and Italian cooking they appear in summer vegetable braises (haricots verts à la provençale, the long-simmered tomato-and-bean stew at the opposite end of the doneness spectrum, where the gray-olive color is the deliberate finish). In French bistro service they go alongside roast lamb or duck, blanched and tossed in butter and shallots. The Lyonnaise gastrique and the Parisian almondine are both finishing techniques on top of the same blanched-and-shocked base.

Buying. Look for pencil-thin pods (3 to 4 mm cross-section), uniform green color with no yellowing, snap when bent (not flex). Skip beans with visible seed bulges in the pods; these are old harvests, the texture turns mealy. Whole Foods and most farmers' markets stock haricots verts as a separate SKU from green beans; the price runs 30 to 50 percent higher per pound and the texture difference is real. Trim only the stem ends; the tail end (the flower remnant) is tender and fine to eat. Pre-trimmed beans in plastic clamshells lose freshness fast; buy whole-pod and trim at home.

Storage

Refrigerator drawer in a paper bag or vented container, 4 to 5 days at peak. Failure modes: limp/flexible (residual moisture loss, blanch and use); rubbery/translucent (dehydrated past the recovery point, discard); slimy/black-spotted (microbial breakdown, discard). Do not freeze raw; freezing ruptures cell walls and the thawed bean is mush. Blanched and shocked beans freeze well in vacuum bags for 2 months.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • haricots verts (composed salad, niçoise role) → slim Asian long beans, cut to length Different texture (denser, slightly fibrous), and the cooking time runs longer (3 to 4 minutes blanch). Visual presentation is similar.
  • haricots verts (general blanched-vegetable role) → standard green beans, blanched 3 to 4 minutes Same plant, older harvest. Blanch longer to set the texture, accept the slightly thicker wall and chewier finish. The 30 percent price difference is an honest tradeoff for everyday cooking.
  • haricots verts (Provençal long-braise application) → standard green beans, no compensation In a long-simmered tomato braise, both pod types collapse to the same olive-gray finish; the wall thickness difference washes out at the 45-minute simmer mark.
