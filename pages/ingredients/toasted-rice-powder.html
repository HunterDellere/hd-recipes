Ingredient

Toasted rice powder (khao khua, thinh)

Raw glutinous (sticky) rice toasted in a dry pan to a deep amber color, then ground to a coarse powder. The signature flavor and textural element of Lao laab, Thai larb, and northeastern Thai-Lao salads, providing a toasted, nutty, slightly bitter character and a faintly sandy mouthfeel that thickens dressings and grips the meat. The same ingredient appears in Vietnamese cooking as thinh (a finer grind, used in nem chua and bì).

About

Toasted rice powder is raw glutinous rice (sticky rice, the same short-grain rice used to make sticky rice for eating) dry-toasted in a heavy pan or wok over medium heat until the grains turn from chalky white to deep amber-brown, then cooled and ground to a coarse, sand-like powder using a mortar and pestle or a spice grinder. The toasting takes 10 to 15 minutes of continuous stirring, and the color transition is gradual; the right end-point is a uniform deep amber with a strong nutty, popcorn-adjacent aroma, just before the rice begins to scorch. Past peak, the powder reads bitter rather than nutty.

The toasting reaction. Rice contains a small amount of free amino acids and reducing sugars in the bran and endosperm; dry toasting drives a Maillard-like reaction on the grain surface as moisture evaporates, producing the characteristic toasted aroma compounds (pyrazines, furans, pyrroles) that give khao khua its distinct character. Glutinous rice is preferred over jasmine or long-grain rice for the toasting because its higher amylopectin content produces a slightly chewier grind that holds together when bitten and contributes more body to dressings; jasmine rice can be substituted but the powder reads thinner and lighter.

The role in larb and laab. In northeastern Thai (Isan) larb and Lao laab (the canonical minced-meat salad dish, the national dish of Laos), toasted rice powder is one of the four structural elements alongside the meat, the fish sauce dressing, and the fresh herbs (mint, cilantro, scallion). It serves three functions: (1) flavor, the toasted, nutty character is the dish''s signature aroma, and a laab without khao khua reads incomplete to a Lao cook regardless of how well the other elements are balanced. (2) Texture, the coarse grind provides a faintly sandy mouthfeel that contrasts with the soft minced meat and the crisp fresh herbs. (3) Body, the powder absorbs the fish sauce dressing and grips the meat, preventing the dressing from pooling at the bottom of the bowl and giving each forkful an even seasoning. The standard ratio is about 1 to 2 tablespoons of toasted rice powder per 500 g of minced meat; less than this and the dish feels under-seasoned, more and the texture turns gritty.

Vietnamese thinh. Vietnamese cooking uses the same toasted-and-ground rice powder, called thinh, in nem chua (the fermented pork rolls of central Vietnam) and in bì (the shredded pork-skin dressing used in cơm tấm broken-rice plates). Thinh is typically ground finer than Thai-Lao khao khua, more like a coarse flour than a sand, and its role is more about flavor and dressing-body than about texture contrast. The toasting is similar but often pulled slightly earlier, producing a paler powder with a more delicate toasted character.

Cooking applications by category. Lao: laab (the national dish, the minced-meat salad), nam tok (a related sliced-meat salad with the same dressing), naem (fermented pork-and-rice sausage). Thai (Isan, northeast): larb (the Thai spelling, same dish as Lao laab; can be made with pork, chicken, beef, duck, or fish), nam tok, koi soi (a similar dish from Kalasin province). Vietnamese: nem chua, bì (shredded pork skin and toasted rice powder mixed with seasoned shredded pork), various central Vietnamese rolls and salads. Cambodian: pleah (a related minced-meat salad with toasted rice powder), various Khmer salad dressings. Burmese (limited): in some Shan-state minced-meat dishes that show Tai-Kadai linguistic and culinary kinship with northern Thailand. Western adoption (limited): in modern Thai-American and Lao-American restaurant cooking; rarely used outside this culinary lineage.

Make-ahead vs commercial. Toasted rice powder is dramatically better when made fresh; the aromatic peak fades within a week even in a sealed container, and the dish is meaningfully improved by toasting and grinding just before assembly. The whole process takes 15 to 20 minutes for enough powder to dress 4 portions of larb. Commercial pre-toasted rice powder (sold in plastic packets at Thai and Lao groceries, brands like Cock Brand and Lobo) is convenient but has lost most of its aromatic complexity in the time between toasting and packaging; use as a fallback when toasting fresh is impractical, and accept a meaningful flavor loss. Some better-quality commercial brands toast the rice with kaffir lime leaves or galangal in the pan to perfume the powder; these are recognizable on the package and worth seeking out.

Buying and toasting notes. Glutinous rice (sticky rice) is available at any Thai, Lao, Vietnamese, or pan-Asian grocery, typically sold in 1 kg bags labeled "sweet rice" or "sticky rice" or "Thai sticky rice." The same rice used to make sticky rice for eating is correct for toasting; specialty "toasting rice" is not a separate product. For toasting, use a heavy skillet or wok (cast iron, carbon steel, or stainless; nonstick masks the visual color cues), heat over medium, add the rice in a single layer, and stir continuously with a wooden spoon for 10 to 15 minutes. The first 5 minutes the rice stays white and the moisture cooks off; the next 5 minutes the rice begins to color (pale gold to medium amber); the final 3 to 5 minutes the rice reaches deep amber with a strong aromatic peak. Pull immediately at the amber stage; the rice will continue to darken in residual pan heat. Cool fully (5 to 10 minutes on a plate) before grinding, or the residual moisture will gum up the grinder. A small mortar-and-pestle produces the right coarse-sand grind in 1 to 2 minutes of pounding; a spice grinder or coffee grinder dedicated to spices does the same in 10 seconds (pulse to avoid over-grinding to flour). The right grind is coarse enough that you can see individual grains, fine enough that no single grain feels gritty between the teeth.

Storage

Freshly toasted and ground rice powder, sealed in an airtight glass jar at room temperature: peak quality for 5 to 7 days, then begins fading in aromatic intensity. Refrigerated in a sealed container: 2 to 3 weeks with slower aromatic decline. Frozen: 2 to 3 months with minimal loss; bring to room temperature before using. Commercial pre-toasted rice powder, unopened: check the printed date, but expect significantly diminished aromatic quality even within the stated shelf life. Failure modes are loss of aroma (the powder is still usable but the dish suffers; re-toast in a dry pan for 2 to 3 minutes to refresh) and rancid smell from oxidized rice bran oil (discard; the toasting concentrates the small amount of fat in the rice and that fat can go rancid).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • toasted rice powder (Thai larb, Lao laab) → crushed toasted unsalted peanuts plus a pinch of finely toasted breadcrumbs A 70/30 blend of crushed toasted peanuts and toasted breadcrumbs approximates the texture and toasted character; the dish reads more peanut-flavored than rice-flavored but is recognizable as larb. Use 1:1 by volume. This is the right substitute for cooks who cannot find glutinous rice and need to make larb today.
  • toasted rice powder (Vietnamese thinh, dressing body) → toasted jasmine rice ground fine, or rice flour briefly dry-toasted Jasmine rice toasted and ground works at 1:1 with a slightly less chewy character. Pre-ground rice flour (white rice flour, available at any Asian market) toasted in a dry skillet for 4 to 5 minutes until pale gold approximates thinh's texture and works for nem chua and bì applications.
  • toasted rice powder (general toasted-grain element) → toasted sesame seeds (ground coarse), or toasted oatmeal ground Toasted sesame seeds ground coarsely produce a similar nutty toasted character with a different aromatic profile; works as a non-traditional flavor in larb-adjacent dishes. Toasted oatmeal ground works for body but tastes distinctly different. These are non-canonical substitutes for cooks experimenting outside the tradition.
