Ingredient

Glutinous rice (sticky rice, sweet rice)

Short- to medium-grain rice (Oryza sativa, glutinous cultivars) whose endosperm is essentially pure amylopectin. Cooks to a translucent, chewy, fully cohesive mass with no individual-grain separation. The table rice of Laos, northeast Thailand, and parts of Yunnan; the structural rice of mochi, zongzi, sticky-rice cakes, and toasted-rice powder.

About

Glutinous rice (also called sticky rice or sweet rice; Thai khao niao, Lao khao niaw, Chinese nuo mi, Japanese mochigome) is a cultivar group within Oryza sativa whose endosperm is composed almost entirely of amylopectin, the highly-branched starch polymer, with essentially no amylose, the linear starch polymer that gives long-grain rice its grain-separation behavior. The name "glutinous" refers to the sticky, glue-like cohesion of the cooked grain and is unrelated to gluten; glutinous rice is gluten-free. Two main grain shapes exist: a short-medium grain typical of mainland Southeast Asia and Yunnan (the variety used in Lao and Isaan sticky rice and in Chinese sticky-rice dumplings), and a medium-short grain typical of Japan and Korea (mochigome, used for mochi and dduk). Both are essentially the same starch chemistry; the cultivar selections produce slight differences in grain shape and milling yield.

The chemistry of stickiness. Standard long-grain rice (jasmine, basmati, Carolina) contains 18 to 25% amylose and 75 to 82% amylopectin in its endosperm. Amylose is a linear molecule that crystallizes (retrogrades) on cooling, producing the firm, separate grain texture of cooled long-grain rice. Glutinous rice has 0 to 2% amylose; the starch is essentially pure amylopectin, a highly branched molecule that does not retrograde on cooling and stays soft, sticky, and cohesive even when cold. The practical consequence: glutinous rice is the only rice that retains its cohesion after cooling, which is why it is used for hand-pinched eating (Lao and Isaan canon), for filled rice cakes (zongzi, mochi, lo mai gai), and for any application where the rice needs to be molded or shaped. Long-grain rice cooled and re-shaped reads dry and fractured; glutinous rice cooled holds its form indefinitely.

The cooking method is steaming, not boiling. Glutinous rice is the only major rice variety that is canonically not boiled; the standard preparation is to soak the dry grain in cold water for 4 to 8 hours (or overnight), drain, and steam in a bamboo basket lined with cheesecloth over simmering water for 20 to 30 minutes. Boiling glutinous rice produces a gluey single mass; steaming produces grains that are individually translucent, slightly chewy, and cohesive enough to be pinched into a ball but not bonded into a paste. The 4-to-8-hour soak is non-negotiable; an unsoaked grain steams unevenly, with hard chalky centers. Microwave and rice-cooker glutinous rice are not equivalent; both produce a wetter, looser texture than the bamboo-steamed version. The difference is most pronounced in Lao and Isaan eating contexts where the rice is hand-pinched as the primary utensil; a properly steamed sticky rice forms a tight ball that picks up larb or som tum cleanly, while a wet rice-cooker glutinous rice falls apart in the hand.

Functional role across cuisines. In Laos and northeast Thailand (Isaan), glutinous rice is the table rice and the eating implement; it is served in covered bamboo baskets and pinched into balls by hand at every meal. In southern Thailand and central Thailand, jasmine rice is the table rice and glutinous rice appears mainly in desserts (mango sticky rice, sweet rice with custard) and in toasted-rice-powder applications. In Chinese cooking, glutinous rice is used in zongzi (leaf-wrapped sticky-rice dumplings), lo mai gai (sticky-rice-and-chicken steamed bundle), Eight Treasures rice pudding, and Northern-Chinese sweet sticky-rice cakes; it does not appear as a table rice. In Japanese cooking, mochigome is pounded into mochi and used in red-bean-and-rice festival dishes (sekihan); not a table rice. In Korean cooking, glutinous rice flour is the structural starch in dduk (rice cakes) and tteokbokki. In Filipino cooking, malagkit is the rice for kakanin (sticky-rice desserts) including bibingka and biko.

Khao khua (toasted glutinous-rice powder). The Lao and Isaan technique of dry-toasting raw glutinous rice and grinding it to a coarse powder yields khao khua, the structural element of larb, nam tok, and many Lao dipping sauces. The toasting (175 to 200 °C in a dry skillet for 6 to 8 minutes) develops Maillard products that contribute a popcorn-and-toasted-bread aromatic; the resulting powder hydrates in the dish dressing and thickens it to a clinging slurry. Khao khua made from jasmine rice is acceptable but does not produce the same slightly-sticky mouthfeel as glutinous-rice khao khua; the binding behavior is what distinguishes the two. The same technique appears in Vietnamese cooking as thinh, used in nem chua (fermented pork rolls), bi cuon (pork-skin rolls), and bun cha thit nuong.

Buying signals. Glutinous rice at Asian groceries is sold under several names; look for "glutinous rice", "sticky rice", "sweet rice", or "khao niao" on the bag. The grains should be uniformly chalk-white (an opaque pearly white, not the translucent clear-white of jasmine), indicating they are unmilled or lightly milled glutinous rice. The Three Ladies brand and the Golden Pyramid brand from Thailand are reference standards for Lao and Isaan applications; for Chinese sticky-rice cooking the Erawan brand is widely used. Mochigome (Japanese sweet rice) is sold separately and is shorter-grained; Tamaki Gold and Koshihikari sweet rice are reference standards. Avoid "sweet rice" labels at North American supermarkets that turn out to be a long-grain variety; the only correct buying signal is opaque chalk-white grain. Glutinous rice flour (mochiko, sweet rice flour) is the milled form, used in dduk and mochi-flour applications; not interchangeable with whole grain.

Common Western drift to avoid. Substituting jasmine rice in Lao or Isaan dishes that call for sticky rice, the cohesion is structural, not preference, and a jasmine substitution changes the eating method. Boiling glutinous rice in a saucepan in the manner of jasmine, the result is gluey and unevenly cooked; the rice must be steamed. Skipping the 4-hour soak; the rice steams hard and dry without it. Using glutinous rice flour in a recipe that calls for whole glutinous rice; the milled form behaves as a starch thickener, not a grain.

Storage

Airtight container, cool dark cupboard, indefinite at room temperature. The opaque grain is well-protected; aromatic compounds in the bran (lower in glutinous rice than in jasmine, since most glutinous rice is sold polished) fade only slightly over a year. Refrigerator or freezer storage is unnecessary except in hot humid climates where rice weevils are a risk. Cooked sticky rice should be eaten the day of cooking; refrigerated sticky rice loses its softness as the amylopectin begins (slowly) to retrograde, and re-steaming for 5 minutes restores most of the original texture. Frozen cooked sticky rice (in a sealed bag, with a teaspoon of water, microwaved 90 seconds or steamed 5 minutes) revives well and is the canonical Lao home practice when sticky rice is made in larger batches. Failure modes: weevils as small dark specks (freeze the bag for 48 hours to kill them, then return to the cupboard); rancid smell from extremely old rice (rare, but discard if present); fermented or off-smell from improperly stored cooked rice (Bacillus cereus risk; never leave cooked sticky rice at room temperature for more than 4 hours).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • glutinous rice (Lao/Isaan steamed sticky rice for the table) → no good substitute; jasmine rice is a different dish The hand-pinch eating method depends on the cohesion of cooked glutinous rice. A jasmine substitute means the diner needs to use a fork; the dish becomes structurally different. Buy a bag of glutinous rice if you are cooking Lao or Isaan food; it keeps a year and is inexpensive.
  • glutinous rice (toasted-rice powder for larb, nam tok) → jasmine rice Long-grain jasmine works in a pinch and yields a workable khao khua, but the slightly-sticky mouthfeel that pure-amylopectin starch contributes is missing. Acceptable substitute for the powder; not equivalent.
  • glutinous rice (Chinese zongzi, lo mai gai) → glutinous rice (only); no substitute The cohesion of the steamed sticky rice in a leaf-wrapped dumpling is what holds the filling together. Long-grain rice produces a fragmented, fall-apart dumpling. Mandatory ingredient.
  • glutinous rice (mochi, Japanese sweet-rice applications) → mochigome (Japanese sweet rice) Mochigome is glutinous rice in a slightly different cultivar selection; mainland Southeast Asian glutinous rice will work for mochi but produces a slightly less elastic final texture. Both are interchangeable for most home applications.
