Ingredient

Waxy potato

Low-starch, high-moisture potato cultivars (fingerling, new potato, La Ratte, red bliss, Yukon Gold). Cell walls hold their structure through boiling: the right potato for salads, gratins, and any dish that needs the cube to stay a cube.

About

Waxy potatoes are cultivars with low total starch (15 to 17 percent of fresh weight versus 22 percent for russets) and a starch composition tilted toward amylopectin over amylose. Amylopectin is a heavily branched polymer that swells in hot water but does not gel out of the cell as readily as the linear amylose chains in starchier potatoes. The functional consequence is that waxy potatoes hold their cellular structure through boiling: a cube cooked through stays a cube: while floury potatoes (russet, Idaho, King Edward) collapse into a fluffy mash. The cell wall structure is also more pectin-rich, contributing to the slightly translucent, glassy interior of a fully cooked waxy potato.

Common cultivars by region. North American: Yukon Gold (the workhorse all-purpose-leaning-waxy variety, available year-round, 18 percent starch: slightly higher than true waxy but functionally waxy in cooking), red bliss / red norland (the round red-skinned new potato, classic potato salad cultivar), fingerling (Russian banana, French fingerling, La Ratte's American cousin), new potato (any small immature potato of any cultivar harvested before reaching maturity, with thin skin and high moisture). European: La Ratte (the elite French waxy fingerling, tight nutty flesh, used in Joël Robuchon's purée), Charlotte (French oval-shaped, holds shape exceptionally), Nicola (German, used in salads and gratins), Linzer Delikatess (Austrian fingerling). The differences are real but the category behavior is consistent: any waxy cultivar can swap for any other within reason.

Cooking applications by category. Boiled and salad service is the canonical waxy role. Boiled in heavily salted water (at least 1 percent salt by weight, ideally closer to 2 percent: the seasoning has to penetrate through the skin) at a tremor (not a hard boil: boiling agitates the potatoes against each other and chips the surface), the cubes hold their shape, take on the salt seasoning, and provide a glassy-firm bite that holds dressing without breaking down. Niçoise, German potato salad, French pommes vinaigrette, Greek patatosalata all rely on this property. Roasting at high heat (220 °C / 425 °F): the surface dehydrates and crisps to gold while the interior holds tender; cubes of waxy potato roasted with olive oil and rosemary are the bistro side. Gratins: thinly sliced waxy potatoes layered with cream and cheese hold their shape through the bake without dissolving into the dairy: gratin dauphinois, scalloped potatoes, Hasselback. Hash and pan-fries: parboiled then crisped in fat, the dense waxy structure holds together against the spatula.

Where waxy fails: mash, baked potato, gnocchi, latkes: anything that wants the potato to break down or release starch. A mashed waxy potato reads gluey and pasty because the high-pectin cell walls grind into a starchy paste rather than fluff. A baked waxy potato reads gummy, not fluffy. Gnocchi made from waxy potato falls apart in boiling water (or compensates with so much added flour that it is no longer potato gnocchi). For these applications the cook wants a russet or king edward.

Heat behavior. Waxy potatoes set their starch around 60 to 70 °C; below this the potato reads raw and crisp, above this the cellular structure has gelatinized and the potato is fully cooked. The "cooked through" sensory cue is a paring knife sliding into the centre with no resistance; visual cues alone are unreliable. A boil starting from cold water gives the most uniform cook (the potato heats evenly with the water); a drop into already-boiling water cooks the surface fast and the centre slow, producing a textural gradient that some recipes (potato gratin) want and some (potato salad) do not.

Buying. Look for firm, smooth-skinned potatoes with no green tinge (green skin signals solanine production from light exposure; pare deeply or discard). Skin condition matters more than potato variety; thin-skinned new potatoes degrade fast and need to be used within a week. Avoid bags of pre-washed potatoes; the wash strips the natural protective layer and accelerates decay. Farmers' market and specialty stores often carry fingerlings and heirloom varieties that the supermarket does not.

Storage

Cool (10 to 15 °C), dark, dry, ventilated. A paper bag in a kitchen cupboard works; refrigeration converts starch to sugar (the potato turns sweet and browns aggressively when cooked, sometimes called the "cold sweet" effect), so avoid the fridge except for already-cooked potatoes. Storage life: 2 to 3 weeks for fresh waxy potatoes at proper temperature; new potatoes 1 week. Failure modes: green skin / sprouts (still usable; pare deeply); soft, wrinkled, weeping fluid (water loss past the recovery point, cook only); dark spots, soft rot, off smell (discard the affected potato, others usually fine).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • waxy potato (potato salad, niçoise, boiled-and-dressed) → Yukon Gold or new potato The closest available substitutes if a specific cultivar (La Ratte, fingerling) is missing. The shape-holding property is the same; only the texture nuance shifts.
  • waxy potato (gratin, scalloped) → a 50/50 mix of Yukon Gold and russet The mix lets some starch release into the cream for body while preserving structural slices. Pure russet collapses into the cream; pure waxy reads dry.
  • waxy potato (mashed potatoes: wrong category) → russet or king edward Waxy potatoes do not mash well. If forced to use them, rice rather than mash, fold in butter and cream off-heat, accept the slightly denser texture.
