Ingredient

Tomato paste

Tomatoes cooked down to roughly one-sixth their volume, a concentrated glutamate-and-fructose paste used to deepen sauces, braises, and stews.

About

Tomato paste is whole tomatoes broken down, strained of seeds and skin, and reduced under low heat until the water is mostly driven off. Domestic American paste runs around 28 to 30 percent solids; Italian "doppio concentrato" (double-concentrate) is closer to 36 to 40 percent and reads denser, sweeter, and more glutamate-forward. The paste carries the entire flavor of the tomato compressed into a sixth of the volume: the natural sugars caramelize on the spoon, the glutamate (~600 mg per 100 g, the highest of any culinary tomato product) saturates, and the lycopene concentrates into the deep brick-red color that defines the spoonful.

Functionally, tomato paste is a savory amplifier rather than a stand-alone sauce. A tablespoon of paste fried in fat for two minutes will deepen a Bolognese, a chili, a French onion soup, or a curry base in ways that no equivalent volume of canned tomatoes or fresh tomato can. The mechanism is two-fold: the residual sugars Maillard-brown against the hot pan and develop the same caramelized aromatics as roasted onions or seared meat, and the concentrated glutamate amplifies the savory perception of every other ingredient in the dish. Skipping the fry-in-fat step and dumping the paste straight into liquid leaves the paste tasting raw and tinny, which is the single most common mistake home cooks make with it.

Heat behavior. The paste begins to caramelize around 130 °C in a thin layer of fat. The visual cue is color shift from bright brick-red to a darker brick-mahogany; the aromatic cue is a sweet-roasted-tomato smell that replaces the sharp-fresh smell of the raw paste. Most recipes call for two to three minutes in fat over medium heat; some classic French and Italian preparations push it longer for a pincage that approaches the color of dried fig and reads structurally similar to a deeply browned mirepoix.

Forms and packaging. Cans (6 oz / 170 g, the American standard) are economical but commit the cook to the whole can, leftovers pressed into a small jar with a thin film of olive oil keep about two weeks refrigerated. Tubes (the Italian and European standard, usually doppio concentrato) cost more per gram but let the cook dose by tablespoons over months, refrigerate after opening, and the tube will keep three to six months. Tubes are the ergonomically correct choice for any kitchen that uses paste in tablespoons rather than half-cans.

Buying. Look for short ingredient lists: tomatoes, salt, sometimes citric acid. Avoid pastes with added high-fructose corn syrup, calcium chloride beyond a token amount, or "tomato concentrate" labels that signal double-strain or seed inclusion that reads as bitter. Italian DOP and "doppio concentrato" labels are reliable depth markers; Cento Organic, Mutti, and Bianco DiNapoli are the brand-bench standards stateside. Color matters: a deep brick-red paste is fresher than an orange-tinged or brown-tinged one (the brown shift signals oxidation in the can headspace).

Storage

Unopened cans and tubes hold at room temperature for the printed expiry, typically 18 to 24 months. Opened cans transfer to a small jar, top with a thin film of olive oil to seal the surface, and refrigerate; use within 2 weeks. Opened tubes refrigerate and keep 3 to 6 months. Failure mode: surface mold (visible white-green colonies) means the whole jar goes; off-color browning at the surface alone is oxidation and the lower paste is fine if salvaged within a week. Freezing is the long-term answer: spoon tablespoons onto a parchment-lined sheet, freeze flat, and bag the frozen disks for up to 6 months.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • tomato paste (sauce-deepening, fry-in-fat) → no real substitute No other ingredient combines the concentrated sugar, glutamate, and lycopene of paste in caramelize-in-fat applications. Sun-dried tomatoes (rehydrated and pureed) approach the flavor but lack the smooth-paste texture and read more rustic.
  • tomato paste (volume filler, thickening) → reduced canned crushed tomatoes For dishes where the paste is bulking the sauce rather than fortifying it, simmer canned crushed tomatoes 15 minutes to halve the volume. Lacks the depth-from-caramelization angle but covers the body.
  • tomato paste (last 5 g in a recipe) → ketchup In a pinch only. Ketchup is paste plus sugar, vinegar, and onion-garlic spice; works for a quick weeknight meatloaf or BBQ-style application but throws off any classic Italian or French preparation. Reduce added sugar in the recipe by ~5 g if substituting one tablespoon of ketchup for one tablespoon of paste.
  • tomato paste (vegan umami support without the tomato) → mushroom paste or a teaspoon of miso Different flavor entirely, but covers the glutamate role. Use only when the dish does not need tomato-color or tomato-acidity, works in mushroom-forward bolognese variants, fails in any dish that reads as red-sauce.
