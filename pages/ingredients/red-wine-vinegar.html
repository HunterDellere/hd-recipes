Ingredient

Red wine vinegar

Acetic-acid solution (5 to 7 percent) produced by acetobacter fermentation of red wine. The default Mediterranean vinaigrette acid, with structural notes from the underlying wine.

About

Red wine vinegar is the product of a two-stage fermentation. Stage one is alcoholic: yeast converts grape sugars into ethanol, the wine. Stage two is acetic: acetobacter (a genus of aerobic bacteria) oxidizes ethanol into acetic acid in the presence of oxygen, with a fine layer of bacterial biofilm called the "mother of vinegar" floating on top. The cure runs to 5 to 7 percent acetic acid; commercial vinegars are standardized to a fixed percentage (typically 6 percent) and pasteurized to halt the bacteria.

The acetic acid is the same compound across all vinegars (white wine, apple cider, distilled, rice). What differentiates red wine vinegar is the residual chemistry of the wine before fermentation: anthocyanin pigments (the deep red color), tannins from grape skins (a slight astringency), and trace fermentation byproducts (esters, glycerol). A well-made red wine vinegar tastes like vinegar with a complex tannic-fruity backbone; a cheap one tastes like neutral acid with red food color and not much else. The price gap is meaningful and audible in finished dishes; a $4 bottle and a $20 bottle are different ingredients.

Vinaigrette role and emulsion. Red wine vinegar is the canonical acid in French and broader Mediterranean salad dressings. The acid component (5 to 7 percent acetic acid) provides the bright pH-driven contrast against the oil and the salt; it also lowers the pH below 4.5, which kills any surface bacteria on the salad components and extends practical shelf life of the dressed salad. In a vinaigrette emulsion, the vinegar is the continuous phase (water) into which the oil is dispersed; without an emulsifier (mustard, egg yolk, honey), the emulsion is temporary and breaks within 20 minutes, which is fine for a freshly tossed salad and not fine for a dressing held overnight. Standard vinaigrette ratio is 1 part vinegar to 3 parts oil; cooks who want a sharper salad pull this to 1:2, and cooks who want a softer plate push to 1:4.

Other applications. Quick-pickling (red wine vinegar plus salt and sugar at 60-30-10 ratio, 30-minute cold soak on shallots or onions, classical French pickled-shallot garnish). Deglazing a hot pan (lower acidic boil, scrapes Maillard fond, builds pan sauces in the French bistro tradition). Soup finishing (a teaspoon stirred into bean and lentil soups at the table, raises the brightness without changing the underlying flavor). Marinades for tougher cuts (acid plus salt softens collagen and seasons; works in beef bourguignon and lamb braises).

Buying. Mass-market US red wine vinegar (Heinz, Pompeian, supermarket house brands) is industrial-tank-fermented, fast-cycle (10 to 15 days from wine to vinegar), and sold pasteurized. The flavor reads as acid plus generic red note. Italian and Spanish craft vinegars (Forvm, Volpaia, La Vieja Fabrica) are barrel-aged for 6 months to several years, sometimes from specific grape varieties (Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot), and develop complex layered notes with woody and fruity depth. The "barrel-aged" distinction is the biggest quality marker; look for it on the label. Spain's Jerez/sherry vinegar is a separate product (sherry vinegar is fermented from sherry, with the saline-nutty notes of the underlying sherry); excellent in Mediterranean cooking but not interchangeable for the bright fruit-acid role of standard red wine vinegar.

Avoid the salty bottom-shelf "cooking vinegar" with added salt. Real vinegar has no added salt; the cook controls salt at the dish level.

Storage

Cool dark pantry, indefinitely. Acetic acid is its own preservative. Failure modes: cloudy bottle with floating sediment (formation of a new mother of vinegar from any residual sugar; harmless, strain or use as starter); flat-tasting (loss of volatile esters over years; still functional, less aromatic). Refrigeration is unnecessary and slows residual acetobacter activity to no benefit.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • red wine vinegar (vinaigrette, salad dressing) → sherry vinegar Slightly nutty and saline; works one-for-one in French and Spanish dressings, marginally less floral, more savory. A half-step closer to Iberian than French sensibility.
  • red wine vinegar (pan deglaze, sauce reduction) → white wine vinegar No tannin or color contribution but the brightness and acid level land identically. Use white wine vinegar when red color would clash with the dish (cream sauce, white-fish pan sauce).
  • red wine vinegar (general acid finishing) → lemon juice, half quantity by volume Different acid (citric rather than acetic), different aromatic profile (citrus vs fermented fruit), slightly more acidic by molarity so use less. Works in soups and on grilled vegetables; fails as a vinaigrette emulsion partner if no other emulsifier is present.
