Ingredient

Canned San Marzano tomatoes

Plum tomatoes from the volcanic soil south of Naples, hand-peeled, packed whole in puree. Lower acid, denser flesh, sweeter finish than supermarket plum tomatoes.

About

San Marzano is a long, narrow plum tomato cultivar grown traditionally in the volcanic-soil triangle around Sarno, San Marzano sul Sarno, and the slopes of Vesuvius south of Naples. The DOP designation (Pomodoro San Marzano dell'Agro Sarnese-Nocerino DOP) restricts the appellation to fruit grown in this specific zone under specific cultural practices, and the legitimate cans carry both the DOP seal and the consortium ID number. The varietal itself can grow anywhere, California, Mexico, the East Coast, and those tomatoes are sometimes labeled "San Marzano-style" or "San Marzano variety" without the DOP, and they are not the same product. Genuine DOP runs lower acid (about pH 4.4 to 4.6 against the 4.1 to 4.3 of generic plum), higher Brix, and lower seed-and-skin ratio because the volcanic soil is mineral-rich and produces fruit with denser pulp and thinner skin.

Functionally, canned tomatoes in any form are the textural and structural backbone of red-sauce cooking. Whole packed in puree (the canonical San Marzano format) gives the cook control: crushed by hand, the tomatoes break into ragged, irregular pieces that read as rustic and homestyle in marinara and pizza sauce; passed through a food mill, they yield a smooth pomarola; chopped on the cutting board, they make a chunky pasta sauce. Pre-crushed and pre-puréed cans skip these decisions and lock the cook into one texture; the puree-of-puree formulations are also more cooked at the cannery, which costs you flavor brightness over the long simmer.

The acid-and-sweetness profile is what makes San Marzano DOP distinctive across applications. In a 90-minute Bolognese where the tomato is one of many flavors, the sweeter San Marzano disappears slightly into the meat and aromatics; the harder-edged generic plum sometimes reads more clearly. In a 20-minute marinara or a Neapolitan pizza sauce where the tomato is the main flavor, the San Marzano sweetness and low acid let the cook skip the half-teaspoon of sugar that home recipes call for to round bargain-can pucker. In a slow-roasted or braised application like pizza al taglio toppings or a long ragù, the San Marzano holds shape better because the dense flesh resists collapse.

Texture by application: hand-crushed for rustic Neapolitan-style sauces and pizza, run through a food mill for a smooth marinara without seeds and skin, blended in a food processor for a uniform pizza sauce, sliced lengthwise for layered baked dishes (lasagna, eggplant parmigiana). Always reserve the canning liquid; it carries glutamate, lycopene, and natural fruit-sugar that the recipe usually wants. Recipes that say "drain the tomatoes" are throwing away flavor, divert the liquid into the pan as the simmer base instead.

Buying and identification. The DOP can shows the pomodoro red-and-yellow consortium logo, a CIPA or ANICAV identifier number, the words "Pomodoro San Marzano dell'Agro Sarnese-Nocerino DOP," and lot codes traceable to the farm and year. Reliable importers stateside include Cento Certified DOP, Bianco DiNapoli (technically a California cultivar grown San Marzano-style; superb but not DOP), Strianese, and Gustarosso. Prices around $6 to $9 per 28 oz / 794 g can in 2026 reflect the DOP designation; cans labeled "San Marzano" at $3 are essentially marketing.

Storage

Unopened cans hold 24 to 36 months at room temperature. Once opened, transfer the unused tomatoes (with their canning liquid) to a glass container, top with olive oil to seal, and refrigerate; use within 5 to 7 days. Failure modes: a swollen or dented can must be discarded unopened (botulism risk in low-acid commercial canning is real, though rare). Surface mold or off-smells in opened tomatoes mean the whole container goes. Freezing is the long-term answer: portion into 1-cup containers and freeze for up to 6 months. The thawed product reads slightly softer than fresh-from-the-can but is functionally identical for cooked sauces.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • canned San Marzano tomatoes (raw or short-cook applications) → Bianco DiNapoli California canned tomatoes Single-source California tomatoes packed whole in puree, comparable depth and acidity to San Marzano DOP, often slightly sweeter. The closest non-DOP equivalent and frequently the best practical choice in the US market.
  • canned San Marzano tomatoes (long-cook braises, ragùs) → whole peeled plum tomatoes (Cento, Muir Glen, supermarket store-brand) In a sauce that simmers 60+ minutes with browning aromatics and meat, the difference between San Marzano DOP and a quality generic plum tomato narrows. Add a pinch of sugar (2 g per can) and a small splash of red wine vinegar to round out the harder edge of the bargain tomato.
  • canned San Marzano tomatoes (pizza sauce, marinara) → high-quality whole peeled plum tomatoes, hand-crushed For pizza sauce and short-cook marinara, the tomato is the main flavor and the substitution shows. If San Marzano DOP is unavailable, Bianco DiNapoli is the upgrade; quality generic plum is the acceptable everyday choice with a half-teaspoon of sugar to compensate.
  • canned San Marzano tomatoes (when out of canned) → oven-roasted ripe Roma tomatoes plus tomato paste Halve and roast 1 kg ripe Roma tomatoes at 200 °C for 35 minutes; pulse with 30 g tomato paste for body. Approximates a single 794 g can. Worth the trouble only when the season is right and the fresh tomatoes are demonstrably better than canned.
