Ingredient

Eggplant (aubergine, brinjal)

Solanum melongena. A sponge-textured nightshade with a cell structure that absorbs oil and water aggressively. Cultivar choice (globe, Italian, Japanese, Chinese, Thai green, Indian, fairy-tale) is the first decision in any eggplant recipe; everything else flows from it.

About

Eggplant is a nightshade fruit with a cell structure that defines the entire culinary problem of the ingredient. The flesh is built of large, water-filled cells separated by thin walls and large air pockets, closer to a stiff foam than to dense vegetable tissue. Raw, those air pockets are full of air; cut into the eggplant and they collapse on contact with oil or water and the eggplant drinks whatever liquid is on hand, which is why poorly-handled eggplant tastes greasy or watery (or both). The behavior is not a defect; it is the entire reason eggplant is the canonical vehicle for fat-and-flavor across Mediterranean, Levantine, South Asian, and East Asian cooking. The art is controlling what gets absorbed and when.

Bitterness. Eggplant contains glycoalkaloids (chiefly solanine and solasodine) and chlorogenic acid that read as bitterness when concentrated. Modern commercial cultivars have been bred to lower these compounds dramatically, and a fresh, in-season eggplant from any major variety today is rarely bitter; the older recipe instruction to "salt it to draw out bitterness" is mostly obsolete. Salting still has value, but the function is moisture removal (and corresponding oil-absorption reduction), not bitterness extraction. Older or larger globe eggplants, particularly seedy specimens stored too long, can still read bitter; salt-and-press handles them.

The major cultivars and when to use which:

  • Globe (American / Mediterranean): large, dark purple, oval. Spongy, seedy, and the most thirsty for oil. Best for braised dishes (caponata, ratatouille, baba ghanoush, eggplant parmigiana) where extended cooking time and an oil-and-tomato bath integrate the sponge texture, and for grilled or roasted preparations where direct fire collapses the structure before oil ever enters. Salt and press for 30 to 45 minutes before frying to limit oil absorption. The default Western eggplant.

  • Italian (graffiti / striped): medium, purple-and-white striped. Slightly less spongy than globe, fewer seeds, sweeter. The default for grilled eggplant, eggplant rollatini, melanzane alla parmigiana when you can find them. Salt-and-press optional.

  • Japanese: long and slender, deep purple. The thinnest skin of any cultivar, the smallest seeds, and the lowest sponginess. Cooks quickly without absorbing much oil. The default for stir-fries (Sichuan yu xiang qie zi works perfectly with Japanese eggplant), miso-glazed nasu dengaku, and tempura. Do not salt, the moisture is already low and the flesh tightens too much.

  • Chinese: longer than Japanese (often 25 to 35 cm), pale lavender to deep violet. Thin skin, small seeds, holds shape during high-heat stir-fry better than any other cultivar. The right choice for Sichuan, Cantonese, and Hunan stir-fries and for stuffed preparations (qing zheng qie zi, steamed eggplant with garlic-soy). Do not salt.

  • Thai green pea: tiny, pale green, the size of a pea. Eaten whole in green and red curries, where they keep their structure through the long simmer and contribute a faintly bitter pop against the curry's sweetness. Different ingredient functionally; do not substitute for other cultivars.

  • Indian (small purple round / round green): small, round, sometimes 4 to 6 cm across. Used whole or quartered for stuffed bharta, baingan masala, and stuffed Gujarati preparations. Closer in behavior to globe (spongy, seedy) but in a smaller package; salt-and-press for stuffed dishes.

  • Fairy-tale (baby Italian): tiny pale-purple-and-white striped, 6 to 8 cm long. Sweetest of all cultivars, almost no seeds, holds shape under any cooking method. The right choice for grilled or pan-roasted whole as a side, halved for crostini, or quick-pickled.

The salt-and-press question. The function is moisture removal, which directly reduces oil absorption: less internal water means less hydraulic suction pulling oil into the cell network when the eggplant hits hot fat. Salt 1.5% by weight (15 g per 1 kg eggplant), let stand 30 to 45 minutes, blot or rinse, and the eggplant fries with about 30 to 40% less oil than unsalted. The trade-off is texture (salted eggplant cooks denser, less spongy) and time. Use it for: globe and Italian eggplant going into pan-fried or shallow-fried preparations. Skip it for: Japanese and Chinese eggplant (low moisture to start), stir-fried preparations where the dry-fry technique handles the same problem, grilled or roasted preparations where direct heat collapses the structure first, and any preparation where soft spongy texture is the point.

Dry-frying (gan bian). Sichuan technique that solves the soggy-eggplant problem without salting. Eggplant is cut into batons or oblique chunks, then dry-fried (no oil, or a film of oil) over high heat in a wok until the surface dehydrates, browns, and the structure collapses inward. Once the eggplant has reduced visibly and the cut faces are golden-brown, oil and aromatics enter and the eggplant takes on flavor without continuing to absorb. The technique is the structural answer to "eggplant that isn't soggy and has rich flavor"; salting solves the same problem from the other direction (water out before cooking) and the two are alternatives, not complements.

Buying notes. Look for tight, glossy, taut skin with no bruises, no soft spots, no shriveling at the stem. The stem cap should look fresh and green. Press gently with a thumb: a fresh eggplant springs back; an old one holds the indent. Heavy-for-size means dense flesh and few seeds; light-for-size often means hollow or seedy. Smaller specimens of any cultivar are sweeter and less seedy than larger ones; if you have the choice, go smaller.

Storage

Eggplant is chill-sensitive; refrigeration below 10°C / 50°F damages the cell membranes and the eggplant develops soft, water-soaked patches within 3 to 5 days. The best storage is cool room temperature (15 to 18°C / 59 to 65°F) in a single layer, not stacked, away from ethylene producers (apples, bananas) which speed ripening past usable. Refrigerator-stored eggplant lasts 5 to 7 days but reads softer and starts to brown on the cut faster. Cut eggplant goes brown within minutes from polyphenol oxidase activity; cook within 30 minutes of cutting, or hold in acid water (1% lemon juice or vinegar) for up to an hour. Off-cues are visible mold (any color), large brown internal patches when cut, or a ammoniated smell. Discard.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • globe eggplant (Mediterranean braises, stuffed, parm) → Italian eggplant Same family, less spongy, often sweeter. Salt-and-press optional. The closest 1:1 substitute.
  • Chinese or Japanese eggplant (stir-fries) → globe eggplant, cut small and salt-pressed first Acceptable but the texture will read denser and the skin will be tougher. Cut into 1.5 cm batons (smaller than you would for Japanese), salt 1% by weight 30 minutes, blot dry, and proceed. Globe eggplant skin in stir-fry sometimes turns leathery; peel half the skin in stripes if it bothers you.
  • Thai pea eggplant → no good substitute The dish (Thai green or red curry) needs the discrete bitter pop of pea eggplant; substitute and the curry tastes flatter. Skip the eggplant entirely or pick a different curry.
  • eggplant (vegan, non-nightshade, for those avoiding the family) → cremini or portobello mushrooms Different ingredient, similar role: high water, sponge texture, strong umami after cooking. Salt-and-press the mushrooms with 1% salt 15 minutes, blot, and proceed. The dish is no longer eggplant-anything but the structural and flavor function is approximated.
