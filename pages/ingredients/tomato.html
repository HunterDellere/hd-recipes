Ingredient

Tomato (fresh)

Solanum lycopersicum fruit, eaten ripe. Free-glutamate concentration runs 100 to 250 mg per 100 g: the second-highest natural source after parmigiano. The umami driver of Mediterranean cooking when ripe; a watery footnote out of season.

About

Tomatoes are the ripe berries of Solanum lycopersicum, a New World nightshade introduced to Mediterranean Europe in the 16th century and absorbed into Italian, Spanish, Greek, and Provencal cooking by the 18th. The fruit is botanically simple: water (94 percent), sugars (2 to 3 percent), acids (0.4 percent citric and malic), free amino acids (notably glutamate at 100 to 250 mg per 100 g), and trace volatiles: but the proportions and the volatile profile vary so dramatically between cultivars and ripening conditions that the difference between a peak-summer farmstand tomato and a January supermarket tomato is one of the largest gaps in the produce aisle.

Glutamate is what makes ripe tomatoes umami. The free-glutamate concentration in a fully ripened tomato (100 to 250 mg per 100 g) is second only to parmigiano-reggiano (1200 mg) and aged hams among everyday ingredients; this is why a great Italian salad of just tomato, salt, and olive oil reads as savory-deep rather than thin and watery. The glutamate compound rises sharply in the last days of vine ripening, which is why a tomato picked green and gas-ripened in transit (the mass-market commodity model) carries 30 to 50 percent of the glutamate of a vine-ripened tomato. The salt-cure technique used in niçoise, panzanella, and caprese (slicing ripe tomato, salting, draining 15 minutes) concentrates the existing glutamate by reducing water; it cannot create glutamate where there is none. A bad tomato cured does not become a good tomato.

Acid and sugar balance the umami. Citric and malic acid (about 0.4 percent of the fruit) keep the tomato bright and counter the savory depth of the glutamate; sugars (2 to 3 percent, mostly glucose and fructose) sweeten the finish. The pH runs 4.2 to 4.6, low enough to read sour without acid addition. Cooking concentrates all three (water cooks off, glutamate, acid, and sugar concentrate proportionally), which is why a long-simmered tomato sauce reads richer than the raw fruit. Adding a pinch of sugar to a tomato sauce that reads thin or sour is a stop-gap; the real fix is to start with better tomatoes, salt aggressively, and cook longer.

Volatile profile is the second flavor axis. Beyond glutamate-driven umami, ripe tomatoes contain a complex aromatic blend: cis-3-hexenal (the green, freshly cut grass note), beta-ionone (floral, violet-like), and 2-isobutylthiazole (the characteristic tomato-skin smell). These volatiles develop in the final days of vine ripening and are sharply diminished in commodity gas-ripened tomatoes. Cold storage (below 12 °C) destroys the volatile fraction within 48 hours; this is why supermarket tomatoes that lived in a refrigerated truck and a refrigerated grocery display read as bland and watery: the chemistry is gone, not just the texture.

Cultivar by application. Slicing tomatoes (Beefsteak, Brandywine, Cherokee Purple, Mortgage Lifter) for raw applications: salads, sandwiches, caprese. Plum and Roma tomatoes (San Marzano, Roma, Amish Paste) for sauces: denser flesh, less seed cavity, less water, concentrate well in cooking. Cherry and grape tomatoes (Sungold, Sweet 100, Black Cherry) for snacking, blistered roasts, or quick blends: high sugar, intense flavor in small bites. Heirloom (any open-pollinated traditional variety) for peak summer flavor with imperfect shapes and short shelf life: never refrigerate, never buy in winter.

Heat behavior in cooking. Brief contact with high heat (charring on a grill or under a broiler) develops Maillard products in the surface sugars and browns the volatile fraction toward roasted-tomato territory. Long simmering (40 minutes to 2 hours) drives off water, concentrates glutamate and acid, and softens the cellular structure so the sauce develops body. Skin removal: scoring an X in the bottom of a ripe tomato, dunking 10 seconds in boiling water, then ice-shocking, slips the skin off cleanly: the technique for any fine-textured tomato sauce or for a chilled gazpacho.

Buying. Peak season is July through September in the Northern Hemisphere temperate zones. Out of season, the highest-quality tomatoes are usually canned San Marzano DOP (peeled whole tomatoes, no citric acid, no salt): the canned product made from peak-ripe fruit beats fresh winter tomatoes for any cooked application. For raw applications out of season, the cherry and grape tomato categories survive cold-chain handling better than slicing tomatoes; supermarket Campari and Sungold are usable in winter. Hothouse "vine-ripened" tomatoes from Mexican and Canadian greenhouse operations are the middle tier: better than gas-ripened but well below summer farmstand. Look for fragrant stem ends (the volatile compounds carry through the calyx), heavy weight for size (denser flesh, less hollow), and slight give when pressed (fully ripe should yield slightly).

Storage

Counter at room temperature, 4 to 7 days at peak. Stem-side down to slow water loss. Never refrigerate ripe tomatoes: fridge temperatures (below 12 °C) irreversibly destroy the volatile aromatic compounds within 48 hours, leaving a bland, mealy tomato. Refrigerate only cut or fully overripe tomatoes that need to last another day. Failure modes: wrinkled skin and weeping fluid (water loss past the recovery point, cook only); dark spots or surface mold (cut around small spots, discard heavily affected fruits); fermented sour smell (microbial breakdown, discard).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • fresh tomato (raw salad, plate role: peak summer) → no real substitute The peak-summer ripe tomato is the dish in caprese, panzanella, and niçoise tomato role. Out-of-season fresh tomatoes do not work; cancel the dish or wait for the season.
  • fresh tomato (cooked sauce, soup, braise) → canned San Marzano DOP whole peeled tomatoes For any cooked tomato application out of season, the canned product is meaningfully better than fresh winter tomatoes. The cure-by-canning preserves the peak-ripe glutamate and volatile profile; the fresh winter alternative does not. 400 g can equals roughly 350 g drained tomato weight.
  • fresh tomato (sandwich, raw application out of season) → cherry or grape tomatoes, halved The smaller fruit categories survive cold-chain handling better than slicing tomatoes. Higher sugar fraction, often more flavor; smaller per-bite size shifts the sandwich texture but the dish lands.
