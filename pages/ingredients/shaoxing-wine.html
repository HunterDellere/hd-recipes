Ingredient

Shaoxing wine (绍兴酒)

Amber fermented-grain wine from Zhejiang, brewed from glutinous rice and wheat-koji starter. The aromatic backbone of Chinese marinades, braises, and stir-fries.

About

Brewed from glutinous rice and a wheat-based qū starter, then aged in earthenware for one to ten years. The fermentation produces ethanol around 14–18% along with a wide pool of esters, higher alcohols, and amino acids. Those amino acids — especially glutamate and proline from broken-down wheat protein — are why a splash of Shaoxing carries weight a dry sherry can't quite reach. In cooking, the wine does two distinct jobs. Splashed against a hot wok during a stir-fry, the ethanol flashes off and the volatile esters carry through the rim of the pan, perfuming the dish. In a marinade or braise, the alcohol denatures surface proteins on meat and dissolves fat-soluble off-aromas — the trimethylamine compounds that read as "fishy" on poultry and seafood. Buy "hua diao" (花雕) grade for cooking — aged longer, deeper amber, more complex. Avoid bottles labeled "Shaoxing cooking wine" sold next to soy sauce in American supermarkets; those are salted to dodge alcohol-tax rules and will throw off the seasoning. A Chinese liquor store or grocery will carry the unsalted bottles in the wine aisle.

Storage

Pantry, capped tight. Stable for years unopened; once opened, oxidation flattens the aromatics within 6–12 months — refrigerate after opening to slow it.

Substitutions

  • shaoxing wine → dry pale sherry (fino or amontillado) Closest western analog. Lacks the wheat-koji glutamate depth but the oxidative ester profile is similar. Avoid cream sherry.
  • shaoxing wine → dry sake Cleaner and less complex. Works in a pinch but the dish will read flatter — bump aromatics or add a few drops of soy.
  • shaoxing wine → salted "shaoxing cooking wine" Reduce added salt by ~1g per tablespoon swapped. Quality is markedly worse — use only if no real bottle is reachable.
