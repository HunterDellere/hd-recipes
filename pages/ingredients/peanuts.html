Ingredient

Peanuts

A legume sold raw, blanched, dry-roasted, oil-roasted, or salted. Eaten across global cooking as a textural and protein topping, sauce thickener, oil source, and confection base.

About

The peanut (Arachis hypogaea) is a legume, not a true nut: the plant flowers above ground and pushes the developing pod down into the soil to mature. The kernel is roughly 25% protein, 50% fat (heavily monounsaturated, with linoleic and oleic as the dominant fatty acids), and 16% carbohydrate, plus a thin reddish skin (the testa) that carries polyphenols and the slight bitterness that distinguishes a skin-on roasted peanut from a blanched one. Peanut oil expressed from the kernel has a high smoke point (~225°C / 440°F) and a faintly nutty character that suits high-heat applications across Cantonese stir-fry, Sichuan chili-oil rendering, and Senegalese stew.

Roles across cuisines. As a textural topping in Chinese, Vietnamese, and Thai cooking: scattered over noodle bowls, congee, cold cucumber salads, and rice plates for the salt-fat-crunch contrast against soft starchy bases. As a sauce base in Indonesian gado-gado, Thai satay, West African groundnut stew, and Sichuan dan dan noodles, where the kernel is ground with chilies, aromatics, and a liquid into a smooth or coarse paste that is then thinned and balanced with acid. As a protein component in West African mafe and Indonesian peanut sambal, where the legume is the central ingredient rather than a topping. As an oil source for stir-fry and deep-fry across East Asia. And as a confection base in American peanut butter, Chinese tang and ya-ya peanut brittle, Indian chikki, and innumerable bar cookies.

Forms and when to use. Raw skin-on peanuts are the default for cooks who want to control the toast: the skins contribute color and polyphenol bitterness, and roasting at home produces a fresher, more aromatic peanut than any store-bought roasted version. Raw blanched peanuts (skin removed) are right when a recipe wants a paler peanut or a smoother peanut sauce; the skins introduce specks into a ground sauce. Dry-roasted peanuts are oil-free and slightly drier; oil-roasted peanuts (most American snack peanuts) carry residual oil that reads richer but cuts shelf life. Salted peanuts are a finishing snack rather than a cooking ingredient; the salt level is tuned for direct eating and overwhelms most cooked applications.

Toasting. Raw peanuts toast in three modes. Oven dry-roast at 175°C / 350°F for 12 to 15 minutes, stirred at minute 8, produces an even toast with the cleanest peanut flavor. Oil-toast on the stovetop in a wide skillet (cold-oil start with raw peanuts) for 9 to 11 minutes produces a slightly richer kernel that takes a glaze well, and is the right approach for soy-glazed peanuts and chili-oil peanut work. Wok-fry in 3 cm of hot oil at 150°C / 300°F for 4 to 5 minutes is faster but riskier; the temperature window between toasted and burnt is narrow.

Allergen note. Peanut allergy is among the most severe and prevalent food allergies in the West and demands strict cross-contamination control in any household where it matters. Peanut oil specifically: highly refined peanut oil is generally considered safe for peanut-allergic individuals because the protein component (the allergenic fraction) is removed during refining, but cold-pressed or unrefined peanut oils retain protein and are not safe. The conservative rule is to assume any peanut-derived product carries allergen risk unless the cook has cleared the specific product with the affected diner. Peanuts are not the same allergen as tree nuts (almonds, cashews, walnuts), and tree-nut-allergic individuals are not necessarily peanut-allergic.

Aflatoxin note. Peanuts grown in warm humid conditions can develop aflatoxin contamination from Aspergillus flavus mold during storage. Reputable suppliers test for and reject contaminated lots; home cooks should buy peanuts from sources with high turnover, store sealed and dry, and discard any peanut that shows visible mold or smells musty. The risk is real but well-managed by major suppliers in regulated markets.

Storage

Raw and roasted peanuts stay best refrigerated or frozen, sealed in airtight glass or zip-top bags. Refrigerated: 6 months. Frozen: 1 year. Room temperature in a sealed jar: 2 to 3 months in cool dry storage; the high oil content makes them go rancid faster than nut-shaped expectations suggest. Salted peanuts in their original sealed bag: as marked, typically 6 to 9 months from packaging. The cue for rancidity is smell: fresh peanuts smell sweet and faintly grassy; rancid peanuts smell of old paint or stale crayons. Discard any with off-smell or visible mold (suspect aflatoxin).

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • peanuts (textural topping on congee, salads, noodles) → cashews or almonds, chopped Cashews are sweeter and softer; almonds are crunchier and read more confection-leaning. Either works as a textural topping when peanut allergy is a concern. Toast them at 175°C for 8 to 10 minutes to develop equivalent depth.
  • peanuts (peanut sauce base) → sunflower seed butter (sunbutter) The classic peanut-allergy substitute. Sunbutter is leaner, slightly more bitter, and the satay or gado-gado sauce will read greener and less rounded. Adjust acid and sugar up by ~10% to compensate.
  • peanuts (peanut sauce base) → cashew butter Sweeter and richer than peanut butter. The sauce reads silkier and less savory; reduce added sugar by 25%. Better for Thai-style satays than for West African groundnut stew.
  • peanut oil → rice bran oil or refined avocado oil For high-heat applications. Both have higher smoke points than peanut oil, similar neutrality, and no peanut-allergen risk. Acceptable substitute when frying anything where peanut flavor is not the point.
