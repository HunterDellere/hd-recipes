Ingredient

Sencha

The everyday Japanese green tea: needle-shaped, steamed leaf that brews into a grassy, marine, gently astringent cup. The steaming (rather than pan-firing) is what gives Japanese green tea its vivid color and oceanic note.

About

Sencha is the default green tea of Japan, roughly three-quarters of all tea grown there, and the benchmark against which other Japanese greens are read. It is whole-leaf Camellia sinensis processed by the Japanese method: fresh leaf is steamed within hours of plucking to halt oxidation, then rolled and dried into the characteristic dark-green needles. Steaming (rather than the pan-firing used for most Chinese greens) is the single defining choice. It produces a more vivid green color, a higher chlorophyll retention, and a distinctly marine, vegetal, umami-leaning flavor where pan-fired greens read nuttier and toastier.

Steam depth is the main quality axis. Standard asamushi (light-steamed) sencha keeps the leaf intact and brews a clearer, more delicate cup; fukamushi (deep-steamed) sencha is steamed longer, which breaks the leaf down into smaller particles, clouds the brew green, and yields a richer, rounder, less astringent infusion that forgives slightly hotter water. First-flush spring sencha (shincha) is the prized, sweetest, most aromatic harvest.

Brewing is temperature-critical, like all green tea. Use 4 to 5 g per 240 ml of water cooled to 70 to 80°C, never boiling, for 1 to 1.5 minutes. Boiling water strips out the catechins and caffeine faster than the sweet L-theanine, giving a harsh, drying cup; gentle water lets the savory-sweet amino-acid character come forward. Sencha gives a good second and even third steep, each progressively lighter and sweeter; raise the temperature slightly on later steeps to keep pulling flavor.

Beyond the cup, sencha is the canonical brew for ochazuke, where hot tea (or tea-spiked dashi) is poured over rice as a fast, restorative one-bowl meal; the tea is the broth, not a garnish. It is also the green-tea base of genmaicha (blended with roasted rice) and the practical from-scratch substrate for Burmese-style fermented tea leaf, since sencha is already steamed (kill-green complete) and rehydrates into a tender, fermentable leaf.

Seasonality

Shincha (first-flush spring sencha) appears in late spring and is the most aromatic; standard sencha is a year-round pantry tea.

Storage

Sencha is perishable. Its retained chlorophyll and catechins oxidize on contact with air, heat, light, and moisture, and stale sencha goes flat and hay-yellow, losing the marine brightness first. Store airtight in an opaque tin, away from heat and light, and use within 4 to 6 months of opening; refrigerate unopened premium tins. Fresh sencha smells of cut grass and seaweed; if a sniff of the dry leaf gives you nothing, the tea is spent.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • sencha (the cup or ochazuke broth) → genmaicha or bancha Genmaicha adds a toasted-rice sweetness and is more forgiving of hot water; bancha is a milder, coarser everyday green. Both make a fine ochazuke broth with a different character.
  • sencha (fermentation substrate for lahpet) → any whole-leaf steamed green tea The steaming matters more than the grade here; a mid-grade sencha rehydrates and ferments as well as a premium one for pickled tea.
  • sencha (powdered, in bakes or lattes) → culinary matcha Where you want suspended powder and color rather than a brewed infusion, culinary matcha is the move. Sencha is a leaf for steeping, not a powder.
