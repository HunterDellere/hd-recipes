Ingredient

Sweetened condensed milk

Milk reduced by ~60% with ~45% added sugar. The sugar is what makes it shelf-stable in the can and what gives it its emulsifying body.

About

Cow''s milk reduced to about 40% of its original volume, then dosed with sugar to roughly 45% by weight. The sugar is doing structural work — it raises osmotic pressure high enough to inhibit microbial growth, which is why an unopened can keeps indefinitely without refrigeration. Evaporated milk needs sterilization in the can to do the same job; condensed milk does it with sugar chemistry.

In Thai iced tea it works as both sweetener and emulsifier. The casein and whey proteins, concentrated by the reduction, help suspend the fat from any added evaporated milk and bind the tannins in the strong-brewed tea: same mechanism as milk-in-tea, amplified.

Boil the can (submerged, 2–3 hours) and the lactose and added sugar Maillard with the milk proteins to make dulce de leche. The reaction needs the proteins; pure sugar syrup won''t do this.

It is not interchangeable with evaporated milk. Evaporated has no sugar — swapping one for the other will wreck either the sweetness or the body of whatever you''re making.

Storage

Pantry indefinitely unopened. Once opened, transfer to a sealed jar and refrigerate; keeps ~2 weeks. A skin forms on the surface as it loses moisture; stir it back in.

Substitutions

1:1 ingredient swaps where the dish stays the same dish.

  • sweetened condensed milk (Thai iced tea, coffee) → dulce de leche thinned with hot milk Carries more browned-sugar flavor and reads darker. Works when caramel notes are welcome; wrong if you want clean dairy sweetness.
  • sweetened condensed milk (baking, rough sub) → honey + heavy cream (1:1 by weight, warmed to dissolve) Approximates the sweetness and viscosity but lacks the protein body and the cooked-milk flavor. Won't set the same in no-bake pies.
