Ingredient

Butter (unsalted)

Cow's-milk fat with ~16% water and ~2% milk solids. The solids are the flavor lever; the water and the melt curve are why it behaves so differently in different roles.

About

Cow''s-milk butter is roughly 80% fat, 16% water, and 2% milk solids (caseins plus lactose), with a trace of salt in salted versions and naturally fermented lactic-acid notes in cultured ones. The fat is a mix of saturated, monounsaturated, and short-chain fatty acids; the short chains are what give butter its distinctive aroma. The milk solids do the visible flavor work, and the water content controls how the fat behaves under heat.

Butter sits across more functional categories than almost any other fat. It is the conduction medium for gentle sautéing, low enough in smoke point that you can hear when the heat is right. It is the structural fat in cake (creamed with sugar to trap air) and in pastry (kept cold to laminate or to cut into pea-sized pieces that steam open in the oven). It is the binder in finished sauces, where cold cubes whisked into a warm reduction emulsify the liquid into something glossy and viscous. It is the flavor in compound butters that finish steaks and roast vegetables. The same brick does all of these jobs because each one exploits a different fraction of what is in the brick.

Heat behavior splits the brick into stages. At 32–35 °C the fat melts. From there to about 100 °C the water boils off as foam. Past that, the milk solids cling to the bottom of the pan and Maillard-toast — caseins reach the browning threshold around 150 °C and develop the amber color and nutty aroma (furfurals and pyrazines) that define beurre noisette. Push past about 175 °C and the solids burn rather than brown; at that point clarified butter or ghee is the right tool, since both have had the solids removed and tolerate higher heat.

Cold behavior splits it again. Plastic-cold butter (around 16 °C, still pliable) creams against sugar to aerate a cake batter. Hard-cold butter (4 °C) stays in distinct chunks when cut into flour, which is what flake-laminates a pie crust or biscuit when those chunks steam in the oven. Soft butter (room-temperature) blends into a dough without trapping air and produces a denser, finer crumb. The same ingredient at three different temperatures gives three different textures.

Buy unsalted for cooking so you control sodium independently across the dish. Salted butter is finished, not raw material. European-style (≥82% fat, often cultured) browns slower because there is less water to boil off, and the cultured tang reads through in finishing applications where the butter is the point. Grass-fed and seasonal lots run yellower from beta-carotene in the milk and carry a slightly grassier note, which is mostly a finishing detail rather than a cooking variable.

Storage

Refrigerator, wrapped tightly, in the coldest part. Keeps 4–6 weeks at 4 °C. Freezes well for 6 months without texture loss; thaw in the wrapper. Failure mode is rancidity from oxidation of the unsaturated fraction, which reads as paint or crayon on the nose long before the texture shifts. Butter absorbs odors aggressively; keep it away from onions, garlic, and any strong-smelling cheese.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • unsalted butter (sautéing, low–medium heat) → olive oil or refined neutral oil Higher smoke point and no milk solids to scorch, but you lose the toasted-solids aroma that pushes a sauté past plain. Acceptable when the dish carries its own dominant flavor.
  • unsalted butter (browning to noisette) → ghee or clarified butter Already has the milk solids removed. Cannot brown the way whole butter does — different ingredient, not a swap. Use only when the recipe wants high-heat butter flavor without the foaming and Maillard stage.
  • unsalted butter (sauce mounting, beurre blanc, hollandaise) → no real substitute The emulsification depends on butter's native casein and water phase. Margarine and oil won't set the same emulsion. If butter is unavailable, pick a different sauce.
  • unsalted butter (laminated dough, pie crust) → leaf lard, kept ice-cold Higher melting point gives flakier laminations than butter. Loses butter flavor; gains structural lift. Common in old-school American pie traditions.
  • unsalted butter (creamed cake, cookie dough) → shortening, partial swap Replacing 25–50% of the butter with shortening gives a taller rise and softer crumb because shortening has no water to steam. Pure shortening loses the butter flavor entirely; use only when texture is the priority.
  • unsalted butter → salted butter Functional 1:1 swap. Reduce added salt by ~1 g per 30 g butter swapped, then taste and adjust.
