Ingredient

Calcium lactate

Calcium salt of lactic acid. Supplies free calcium ions for spherification without the metallic bitterness of calcium chloride; the preferred cross-linker for reverse spherification where the calcium ends up in what you eat.

About

Calcium lactate (and its variant calcium lactate gluconate) is the food-grade calcium source of choice for reverse spherification. Calcium chloride works chemically but carries a sharp, metallic, faintly bitter taste that survives even thorough rinsing, fine for basic spherification (where the calcium stays in the discard bath) but unworkable for reverse spherification (where the calcium is in the liquid being encapsulated and eaten).

Standard reverse-spherification ratio: 2% calcium lactate by weight in the flavored liquid (juice, milk tea concentrate, fruit purée). Whisk to dissolve. The liquid is then dropped or piped into a 0.5% sodium alginate bath; the calcium leaches outward, crossing the alginate boundary and forming a flexible membrane that contains the still-liquid center.

Solubility is fast at room temperature. No heating required. If the liquid is high in sodium or other competing cations, increase calcium lactate by 25% to compensate.

Calcium lactate gluconate (a 1:1 blend with calcium gluconate) is functionally interchangeable and slightly more soluble; brands like Cuisine Tech sell both. Recipes specifying one work with the other at 1:1.

Storage

Airtight container, cool dry pantry. Two years dry. Hygroscopic, keep moisture out or it cakes.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • calcium lactate (reverse spherification) → calcium lactate gluconate, 1:1 Functionally identical. Slightly more soluble; whisks in cleaner.
  • calcium lactate (reverse spherification) → calcium chloride, 0.5% by weight Last resort only. Will carry a metallic bitter note into the finished sphere; partially masked by sweet liquids, obvious in delicate flavors.
