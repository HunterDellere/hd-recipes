Ingredient

Samna (clarified butter)

Butter cooked past its water phase and strained free of milk solids — pure butterfat, around 99% lipid, with the toasted-solid aroma already baked in. The Levantine and North African analog of Indian ghee.

About

Samna (سمنة) is the Levantine and Egyptian name for clarified butter: cow, sheep, or goat butter cooked past the water-boiling stage until the milk solids drop out, then strained off so what remains is essentially pure butterfat at 99%+ lipid by mass. The cooking-and-straining step is what distinguishes it from whole butter; what distinguishes it from a French clarified butter is that samna is typically taken further, with the milk solids browned slightly before they are strained, leaving the fat with the toasted-casein aroma of beurre noisette already integrated. Samna baladi (سمنة بلدي, "country samna") is the traditional version made from sheep or goat butter, often aged or scented with qamar el din, mahleb, fenugreek seeds, or local herbs during cooking, and is the canonical fat in mansaf, in Bedouin cooking, and in older Egyptian and North African pastry traditions. The commercial supermarket product (Faraon, Crystal, store-brand) is cow-milk samna without the herb infusion and reads cleaner and milder than baladi.

Functionally, samna is what a cook reaches for when butter flavor is wanted at temperatures or holding times that would scorch whole butter. The water has already been boiled off, so the fat does not foam or splatter when it hits a hot pan. The milk solids have been removed, so there is nothing to burn at high heat; smoke point sits around 230 °C (450 °F), well above any sauté or shallow-fry the cook is likely to do. And because the casein and lactose have already been Maillard-browned during clarification, the fat carries nutty-toasted volatiles forward into whatever it cooks, without the cook having to brown butter to noisette in the moment.

Where it fits in dishes. The fat in mansaf rice, where it bonds with turmeric and warm spices and lays a savory underlayer that cleaner oils cannot. The frying medium for the toasted pine nuts and slivered almonds that crown mansaf and Persian polo. The fat in kibbeh, kunafa, and many Levantine and North African pastries, where the high smoke point lets the dough crisp without the fat turning bitter. A standard sauté fat in Egyptian and Bedouin home cooking when butter flavor is wanted at chicken-roasting heat. Samna also melts cleanly into hot stews as a finishing fat, contributing aroma without watering the broth as whole butter would. Indian ghee is functionally identical and substitutes 1:1 in any of these applications; the only audible difference is the herb-infused aroma of samna baladi, which ghee does not carry.

Heat behavior. Stable. The smoke point is high enough that the fat tolerates a hard sear, the absence of water means no splattering, and the absence of milk solids means no scorching. The fat is solid at room temperature in cool climates (~20 °C) and liquid above ~32 °C, so a jar shipped through summer heat may arrive liquid; this is normal and reversible. Cooks who want the fat liquid for measuring can warm the jar in a bowl of warm water for a minute, or scoop the solid version directly into the pan and let the heat melt it.

Buying notes. The label list should read butter or clarified butter, full stop, with possible additions of natural herb extracts on a baladi product. Vegetable-oil "samna" exists in some Egyptian markets as a butter-substitute product (samna nabati, "vegetable samna") and is essentially shelf-stable shortening; it is a different ingredient and should not be substituted into a recipe that calls for true samna or ghee. The fat should be golden-yellow to deep amber depending on the extent of solid browning, with no greasy or rancid edge to the smell. A faintly cheesy or grassy note is correct on baladi versions and signals quality.

How it differs from butter. Whole butter is roughly 80% fat / 16% water / 2% milk solids. Samna is 99%+ fat. The water phase is gone (so no foaming, no splattering), the milk solids are gone (so a higher smoke point and no scorching), and the Maillard browning is permanent (so the toasted aroma carries forward). Substituting whole butter for samna in a hot-fat application like rice frying loses the smoke-point margin and risks burning the milk solids; substituting samna for butter in an emulsified sauce (beurre blanc, hollandaise) misses the casein and water that the emulsion depends on. The two ingredients overlap on aroma but not on behavior.

Storage

Sealed jar at room temperature, away from heat and light. Unopened, samna keeps 12 months or more; the absence of water suppresses microbial growth almost entirely. Opened, it keeps 6+ months on the shelf or 12 months refrigerated. Failure mode is rancidity from oxidation of the unsaturated fatty acid fraction, which reads as paint, crayon, or sharply waxy on the nose long before any visual change. The fat absorbs ambient odors aggressively; keep it lidded and away from cut alliums.

Substitutions

What to reach for when this ingredient is unavailable. Each row names the use case so the swap is judged in context.

  • samna (high-heat sauté, fried garnishes, rice fat) → Indian ghee Functionally identical clarified butter from cow milk. Brands like Pure Indian Foods or Tin Star are clean. Substitutes 1:1 in any cooking application; misses only the herb-infused aroma of baladi versions.
  • samna (low to medium heat sauté or finishing) → unsalted butter + a few extra minutes of cook time to brown the milk solids Whole butter taken to the noisette stage approximates the toasted-aroma side of samna. Loses the high-heat margin and the cleaner mouthfeel. Acceptable when a quick noisette is built into the recipe step itself.
  • samna (when dairy must be omitted) → a neutral high-smoke-point oil + a small splash of toasted sesame oil or roasted hazelnut oil No real equivalent for the dairy aroma; the toasted-nut oils contribute a faint roasted-volatiles note in the same direction. Use only when the dish must be dairy-free; the result is a different dish.
