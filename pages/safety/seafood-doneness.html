Seafood Doneness, Parasites, and Live Handling

Internal-temperature targets for fin fish and shellfish, parasite-freezing rules for raw applications, bivalve live-handling, scombroid and ciguatera basics, and what "sushi-grade" actually means.

Reference

Seafood safety splits into three distinct problems and a temperature target only solves one of them. The first is bacterial contamination, addressed the same way as meat — heat to a kill threshold, hold long enough, surface-vs-mass logic for ground products. The second is parasite contamination (anisakid worms, tapeworms, flukes), addressed by either cooking through or by deep-freezing per FDA spec. The third is biotoxin contamination — scombroid histamines, ciguatera, paralytic shellfish toxins — which heat does not destroy and which is managed by sourcing and handling alone. Conflating these is how good cooks get into trouble.

Cooking targets for fin fish

USDA published-safe instantaneous target: 145°F (63°C). This is the conservative line and produces opaque, flake-apart fish. Many traditions and most chefs cook to lower temperatures for texture: salmon at 120-125°F (translucent ruby), tuna at 110-115°F (still red in the center), cod at 130°F (just-set, glassy). These lower targets are categorically not USDA-published-safe for the general public; they are texture-driven calls that depend on sourcing and on parasite-protocol-followed fish.

The texture cue most cooks rely on — flesh turns from translucent to opaque, just-resists-flake — corresponds to roughly 130-135°F internal for most species. Salmon, tuna, mackerel, and other fatty fish overshoot quickly and the carryover window is short. Pull at 5°F below target.

Shrimp, scallops, squid, octopus: cooking targets are texture-driven, not pasteurization-driven, since their bacterial loads are typically lower than groundfish and they are usually fully cooked to opacity well above 145°F regardless. Octopus benefits from extended low-temperature cooking (180°F sous vide, 4-5 hours) for collagen breakdown, well past any safety question.

Parasites — when raw fish needs to be frozen first

FDA Food Code 3-402.11 specifies the parasite-destruction protocol for fish intended to be served raw or undercooked:

  • -4°F (-20°C) or colder for 7 days (total time, including the freezing-down period), or
  • -31°F (-35°C) or colder for 15 hours (faster, requires a blast freezer), or
  • -31°F (-35°C) until solid, then held at -4°F for 24 hours (blast then standard freezer)

Home freezers run at roughly 0°F. Most home chest freezers run -10°F to 0°F. The standard upright freezer attached to a refrigerator does not reliably hit -4°F throughout the volume — that is the published threshold, and a -2°F freezer for 7 days does not satisfy the rule.

Exemptions in the FDA code: large species of tuna (bluefin, yellowfin, bigeye, albacore over 20 lb) are exempt because the parasite-load data shows essentially no anisakid burden. Aquacultured fish raised on pelleted feed in parasite-controlled environments (most commercial Atlantic salmon) are exempt under specific paperwork from the supplier. "Sushi-grade" or "sashimi-grade" is not a regulated term in the US; what makes a fish actually safe for raw service is documented compliance with one of the protocols above. A reputable Japanese-market fishmonger or a restaurant supply house can produce that documentation; a typical grocery-store seafood counter usually cannot.

Practical rule for the home cook making crudo or poke at home: source from a counter that explicitly markets parasite-cleared sashimi-grade product (often labelled as such with a per-piece price premium), or buy bluefin/yellowfin tuna which is exempt by species. A standard grocery-store salmon fillet is not raw-safe regardless of how fresh it is.

Bivalves — oysters, clams, mussels

Different problem entirely: bivalves filter-feed and concentrate whatever is in the water column. The two operative risks are Vibrio bacteria (warm-water growing season, raw consumption — the source of the "only eat oysters in months with an R" lore, which is a rough stand-in for "avoid summer when Vibrio counts spike") and norovirus from sewage-contaminated harvest waters (cold-water risk, also raw).

Cooking kills both. Bivalves opening their shells in steam or boiling water indicates the abductor muscle has cooked through; an oyster baked to 140°F internal or steamed in a covered pot for 4-6 minutes at full boil is in the clear.

Live handling for raw service: shells must be tightly closed when raw (or close when tapped — a slight gap is alive-and-relaxed; a permanently open shell is dead, discard); shells already broken or muddy go in the bin; harvested-on-date and harvest-tag from the supplier should be checked. Refrigerate at 40°F or below in a single layer with a damp cloth on top, never sealed in a plastic bag (they suffocate).

Scombroid (histamine) — the toxin no temperature fixes

Mahi-mahi, tuna, mackerel, bonito, sardines, anchovies, bluefish, amberjack, and other "scombroid" species accumulate histamine when stored above 40°F. Bacteria on the fish convert the naturally-high histidine in the muscle into histamine, which is heat-stable and not destroyed by cooking, freezing, or canning. Symptoms in the eater are an allergic-reaction profile — flushing, headache, hives, sometimes severe — within 30-60 minutes of eating.

The only control is the cold chain. Buy these species from a counter that clearly handles ice well, refrigerate immediately, and cook or freeze within 24-36 hours of purchase. A fish that has been temperature-abused will still cook normally and look normal; it is the histamine, not the bacteria, that has done the damage and that damage cannot be undone.

Ciguatera — also not a cooking problem

Tropical reef fish (barracuda, large grouper, snapper, amberjack, kingfish, moray eel) can accumulate ciguatoxin from algae lower in the food chain. Heat-stable. Freezing-stable. The only control is sourcing — avoid the affected species from known endemic regions (Caribbean reef, parts of the Pacific). For most US home cooks this is a non-issue because the affected species are not commonly sold; for cooks in the Caribbean, Hawaii, or Pacific island contexts, local guidance is the input.

Pre-cooked seafood and storage

Cooked shrimp, crab, smoked salmon, surimi, and similar products: refrigerate at 40°F or below and treat as ready-to-eat. Hot-smoked salmon is fully cooked and treated as such; cold-smoked salmon (lox, gravlax) is cured but not heat-cooked, and follows the parasite-freezing rule for safety from parasites and a 14-day refrigerated shelf life from a Listeria standpoint.
