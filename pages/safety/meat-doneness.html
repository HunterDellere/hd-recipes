Meat Doneness and Pasteurization

USDA published-safe internal temperatures for poultry, pork, beef, and lamb, plus the full time-temperature pasteurization table that explains why thin-sliced and sous-vide approaches can sit below the instantaneous number and still clear safety.

Reference

Meat safety is a microbiology problem, not a temperature number. Pathogens of concern (Salmonella, Campylobacter, Listeria, pathogenic E. coli, Toxoplasma) are killed by heat as a function of both temperature and time. The USDA's headline numbers — 165°F poultry, 145°F pork, 145°F whole-muscle beef — are instantaneous targets: temperatures at which the lethal log-reduction is achieved in roughly the time it takes to insert a probe and read the display. Hold meat at a lower temperature for long enough and you reach the same log reduction. This is the entire intellectual foundation of velveting, sous vide, slow-roasting, and the 2011 USDA pork revision. The cook who understands the table is not gambling — they are reading the published equivalency curve.

The other axis is where the pathogens live. Whole-muscle cuts (a steak, a pork loin, a chicken breast) carry pathogens almost exclusively on the exterior surface, which means a properly seared surface plus an interior that hits the right temperature is sufficient. Ground meats (burgers, sausage, meatballs) and tenderized or needle-injected cuts (most "enhanced" pork, mechanically tenderized steaks) have surface contamination redistributed throughout the mass, so the entire interior must reach the kill temperature. This is why a 130°F medium-rare burger from random ground beef is a different risk profile than a 130°F medium-rare ribeye from the same animal. Different rules apply because the bacterial geography is different.

Poultry

USDA published-safe instantaneous target: 165°F (74°C) anywhere in the bird, including the deepest part of the breast and the joint between thigh and body. This is the number to use when you want one rule and no math.

The USDA-FSIS pasteurization table (Appendix A of the 1999 Performance Standards for the Production of Certain Meat and Poultry Products) publishes the equivalent holds for chicken meat. A 7-log reduction of Salmonella is achieved at:

  • 165°F (74°C) — instantaneous
  • 160°F (71°C) — held 14 seconds
  • 155°F (68°C) — held 49 seconds
  • 150°F (66°C) — held 2 minutes 48 seconds
  • 145°F (63°C) — held 9 minutes 51 seconds
  • 140°F (60°C) — held 30 minutes 17 seconds
  • 136°F (58°C) — held 1 hour 3 minutes
  • 130°F (54°C) — held over 2 hours

Two practical consequences. First, a chicken breast pulled at 150°F and rested under foil — where the interior continues to drift between 150°F and 155°F for 5 minutes — has cleared the table by a comfortable margin. Second, velveted chicken sliced 4-5 mm thin and held in a 190°F (88°C) Thai curry simmer for 4 minutes has spent more than 2 minutes 48 seconds above 150°F at every point in the cross-section, and far longer at the surface. Pull at 145°F internal, carryover to 150-152°F, with continuing exposure to a 190°F medium puts the slice well past pasteurization equivalency. The technique is not a shortcut around the rule; it is the rule, applied with thermometer literacy.

Two cautions. The pasteurization table assumes meat held continuously at the stated temperature; a probe reading 150°F for an instant on the way through the curve is not the same as 150°F held 2:48. And the table is for chicken meat only — turkey breast pasteurizes at slightly different rates and is more often cooked as a large whole-muscle roast where carryover is the dominant factor. For turkey, 150°F pull → 20-minute rest → 160-165°F final via carryover is the conservative line on a roast. Stuffing inside the bird must hit 165°F regardless; it is a denser, slower-heating mass with more surface area for cross-contamination.

Pork (whole muscle)

USDA published-safe instantaneous target since 2011: 145°F (63°C) followed by a 3-minute rest. This was a deliberate revision; the previous 160°F target dated from a Trichinella-prevention era that no longer reflects US commercial pork (Trichinella in commercial herds is now vanishingly rare, and the parasite is killed at 137°F regardless). The 145°F + 3-minute rest rule is the modern, published-safe number for pork loin, tenderloin, chops, and roasts.

Pork tenderloin pulled at 140°F, rested 5-10 minutes, and finishing at 145°F is squarely within the rule and produces meat that is faintly pink, juicy, and not the gray cardboard of the previous era. The 145°F target in the green-curry-pork recipe (where tenderloin slices simmer briefly at 190°F) follows this directly: pull at 145°F, the carryover and continuing simmer exposure clears any residual time-temperature concern.

Cured pork (bacon, ham, pancetta, prosciutto) is a separate category. Pre-cooked cured products are ready to eat; raw-cured products like prosciutto rely on salt and time, not heat, for safety.

Beef and lamb (whole muscle)

USDA published-safe instantaneous target: 145°F (63°C) with a 3-minute rest for steaks and roasts. This is conservative; whole-muscle beef carries pathogens only on the seared exterior, and a properly seared 130°F medium-rare interior is the long-standing restaurant standard with an excellent safety record. The published USDA number exists for the most cautious case (immunocompromised, very young, very old).

Time-temperature equivalencies for whole-muscle beef (FSIS Appendix A, 6.5-log Salmonella reduction):

  • 145°F (63°C) — held 4 minutes
  • 140°F (60°C) — held 12 minutes
  • 135°F (57°C) — held 36 minutes
  • 130°F (54°C) — held 112 minutes (1 hour 52 minutes)
  • 126°F (52°C) — held over 4 hours

This is the table that underwrites sous-vide steak: 130°F for 90 minutes is cleanly inside the equivalency, and the seared exterior handles the surface bacterial load. Lamb follows the same physical rules and the same temperature targets.

Ground meat and mechanically tenderized cuts

Different rules. Surface contamination is now distributed through the mass, so the entire interior must clear the kill threshold.

  • Ground beef, lamb, pork, veal: 160°F (71°C) instantaneous
  • Ground poultry (chicken, turkey): 165°F (74°C) instantaneous
  • Mechanically tenderized or needle-injected steaks: same rules as ground meat — treat as 160°F target, not 145°F whole-muscle

The pasteurization equivalency for ground beef (E. coli O157:H7 6.5-log reduction) is more aggressive than whole-muscle:

  • 155°F (68°C) — held 17 seconds
  • 150°F (66°C) — held 1 minute
  • 145°F (63°C) — held 3 minutes
  • 140°F (60°C) — held 12 minutes
  • 135°F (57°C) — held 37 minutes

A burger held at 135°F sous-vide for 40 minutes is medium-rare in color and clears E. coli pasteurization; a burger pulled instantly at 135°F off the grill does not. This distinction is what separates a competent low-temperature burger program from a dangerous one. Most home cooks should grind their own from a single primal (the contamination geography reverts to whole-muscle once you are the only contact point) or stick to 160°F for store-ground product.

Carryover and resting

Every internal-temperature target is a pull number, not a serve number. The thermal mass of the meat continues to drive heat inward after the pan or oven is gone. Rough rules of thumb:

  • Thin cuts (steaks, chops, breast): 5°F carryover
  • Medium roasts (pork loin, chicken whole-roasted): 5-10°F carryover
  • Large roasts (turkey, prime rib, leg of lamb): 10-15°F carryover

The rest also matters mechanically. Contracted muscle fibers reabsorb expelled juice as they relax; sliced too soon, the juice ends up on the cutting board. Five minutes for a steak, fifteen for a roast, twenty for a turkey breast or whole bird.

Residual-risk language

The USDA published-safe numbers are calibrated for the general population. If you are cooking for someone who is pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, or very old, the conservative line is the instantaneous number, not the time-temperature equivalency. A 130°F medium-rare steak is a fine call for healthy adults; it is the wrong call for a 70-year-old on chemotherapy. The cook decides; the table is the input.
