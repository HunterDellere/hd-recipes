Egg Doneness and Salmonella

Shell-egg pathogen risk math, when raw or partly cooked yolks are reasonable, and when to reach for pasteurized eggs. Covers carbonara, mayo, hollandaise, mousse, custards, and meringue.

Reference

The egg-safety problem is essentially one organism: Salmonella enteritidis, which can colonize the hen's reproductive tract and end up inside the shell before it is laid. US shell-egg contamination rates are low but nonzero — FDA modelling estimates roughly 1 in every 20,000 shell eggs from clean commercial supply contains viable Salmonella. That number is small enough that an adult eating a runny yolk is at low absolute risk and large enough that a pregnant cook making homemade mayonnaise for twelve people is making a different bet than they may realize.

Salmonella kills like any other pathogen — as a function of temperature and time. The egg-pasteurization curve is gentler than the meat curve because eggs proteins coagulate close to the kill range, which is the entire reason commercial pasteurized eggs exist (the industrial process holds eggs at 134-140°F long enough to clear Salmonella without setting the white).

The pasteurization curve for whole egg

USDA published equivalencies for in-shell pasteurization (5-log Salmonella reduction):

  • 140°F (60°C) — held 3 minutes 30 seconds
  • 138°F (59°C) — held 5 minutes
  • 136°F (58°C) — held 7 minutes
  • 134°F (57°C) — held 11 minutes
  • 132°F (56°C) — held 17 minutes
  • 130°F (54°C) — held 27 minutes

This is the curve for in-shell eggs in a temperature-controlled water bath. Once cracked into a sauce or mixture, the heat-transfer math is different (the egg is in much more direct thermal contact with the surrounding liquid) and applications like custards, hollandaise, and tempered sabayon reach the kill window quickly if held above 140°F for the requisite time.

Egg whites coagulate at ~144°F, yolks at ~150°F (the yolk has more lipid, which raises the set point). That gap is the reason a 135°F sous-vide poached egg has a fully runny yolk and a not-quite-set white; it is also why home pasteurization in shell tops out around 140°F before the white starts to firm.

Categorically safe (no special handling)

Anything fully cooked. Hard-boiled eggs, scrambled eggs cooked through, baked goods past their internal-temperature mark (cakes, cookies, breads), egg-bound batters that reach 160°F+ in the oven. The kill is comfortably done.

Conditionally safe — runny yolk, set white

Soft-boiled, sunny-side-up, basted, fried-with-runny-yolk, soft-poached, eggs benedict, ramen eggs, pasta carbonara at the moment of plating. The white has set (>144°F, ~3 minutes simmer) but the yolk is still liquid and below pasteurization.

Risk assessment for healthy adults: low. The Salmonella prevalence math means most cooks will go a lifetime of eating runny yolks without incident. For the conservative case (pregnancy, immunocompromise, very young, very old), reach for pasteurized in-shell eggs (Davidson's, Safest Choice — ~1 in 50,000-100,000 grocery stores carry them) or treat the egg through a custard-style temper that holds 140°F+ for 3 minutes 30 seconds.

Specifically requires pasteurized eggs (or a temperature temper)

Preparations where raw or barely-warmed egg is the structural element and never reaches the kill curve:

  • Caesar dressing (raw yolk + lemon + anchovy emulsion)
  • Aioli and homemade mayonnaise (raw yolk + oil emulsion)
  • Mousse au chocolat (raw yolk folded into ganache, raw white whipped and folded in)
  • Tiramisu (raw yolk + sugar zabaglione, sometimes raw white as well)
  • Eggnog (traditional recipes — modern published recipes generally include a 160°F custard step)
  • Italian buttercream (cooked syrup poured into raw whites — the syrup heat does pasteurize, but only if it is actually 240°F when it hits the white; a thermometer is non-negotiable)
  • Steak tartare with raw yolk; carpaccio garnished with raw yolk
  • Hollandaise and béarnaise made cold-emulsion style (the warm-bath method is fine; the immersion-blender cold method is not unless eggs are pasteurized)
  • Bavarian cream and pastry cream variations that fold raw yolk into a partially cooked base

For these, two valid paths. Either source pasteurized in-shell eggs (Davidson's Safest Choice is the most common US retail brand) or use the French custard-temper method: whisk yolks with a portion of sugar, slowly stream in the hot dairy or syrup, return to heat, and bring the entire mixture to 165°F (74°C) on a thermometer or 160°F held 1 minute. The temper method is what underwrites a properly made commercial-recipe eggnog or a "safe" Caesar built on a yolk-and-oil emulsion warmed in a bain-marie before the rest of the dressing is built around it.

Carbonara

Worth its own paragraph because it is the most-asked question on the curve. Real carbonara is finished off-heat by tossing hot drained pasta and rendered-guanciale fat with a raw yolk and pecorino mixture; the residual pasta heat warms the egg into a creamy emulsion without scrambling it. Internal temperature of the finished sauce is 140-160°F, depending on how aggressively the cook tosses and how hot the pasta was. This is at or above the egg-pasteurization curve for a few minutes of contact, which is at the borderline-safe end of the spectrum. For the conservative case, use pasteurized yolks; for healthy adults, real carbonara is a low-risk preparation eaten constantly across Italy and the diaspora.

Meringue and whipped white applications

Italian meringue (cooked syrup at 240°F poured into whipping whites) pasteurizes the white reliably. French meringue (raw white whipped with sugar, baked into shells) pasteurizes through the bake. Swiss meringue (whites and sugar warmed over a bain-marie to 160°F, then whipped) pasteurizes through the warm step. Raw-white folds (as in mousse au chocolat) do not.

Eggshell handling

Surface contamination is the other failure mode. Salmonella can also live on the shell exterior from the laying environment. Wash hands after cracking; don't crack on the rim of the bowl that the egg goes into; don't wipe the shell residue back into the mixture. Refrigerate eggs at 40°F or below — US-washed commercial eggs have had their cuticle stripped and need refrigeration, where European unwashed eggs can sit at room temperature.
